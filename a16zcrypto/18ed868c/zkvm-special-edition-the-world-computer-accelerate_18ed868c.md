⚡ Feature: Understanding the most performant zkVM to datewith Justin Thaler, Sam Ragsdale, and Michael Zhu a16z crypto’s engineering and research teams recently released the most performant zkVM to date: an open source implementation of While the ideas behind Jolt, and its theoretical foundation, Why zkVMs matterIf blockchains can be considered a “world computer” — a machine anyone in the world can help operate and secure — then zkVMs are SNARKs that let the prover prove that it correctly ran a specified computer program, where the program is written in the low-level assembly language of some simple CPU. The trivial way of proving the computation would be simply to send the data to the verifier, which can then run the computation on the data (“re-execution”); or to send an entire transcript of the computation to the verifier (a record of what happened at each and every step). But SNARKS speed up the verification. Many SNARKs already have very low verification costs — proofs as small as a few hundred bytes, and verified in milliseconds. The bottleneckWhile verification is fast, the cost to the prover has been the Without more performant SNARKs, each operation done by the world computer will be millions of times more expensive than any other computing environment. In this case, “performant” means faster, which means more throughput — without more hardware — leading to lower cost, which in turn means a more useful computing environment. Introducing JoltFor the blockchain world computer to succeed, we need much more efficient proof generation methods. And that’s where Lasso and Jolt come into play. Related resources, watch: ▶️📹 See tweets from 📝 Bonus: Field notes from zkSummit 2024with Joseph Bonneau The 11th Zero Knowledge Summit ( ZK hardwareHardware support for proof generation has long been a goal of the community. The first two talks on the main stage outlined developments in that direction. [Justin Drake](https://substack.com/redirect/7d2afa5f-3ef6-49b2-b493-9739e490b439?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o), researcher at the[Ethereum Foundation](https://substack.com/redirect/1ce137e6-c24e-49ee-ad6f-223323807465?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o), gave an overview of ZK hardware, including a taxonomy of companies in the space. The list included companies using general hardware (like[Ulvetanna](https://substack.com/redirect/a17276ec-aeec-4ec6-90b6-27f287a4afaf?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o)), companies making custom hardware (including[Accseal](https://substack.com/redirect/de4410ee-4638-4d8a-bad1-c03cd7959f05?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o),[Cysic](https://substack.com/redirect/1e41e519-43ce-4adf-9fb7-757f47d0377e?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o), and[Fabric](https://substack.com/redirect/646f8c35-ef39-4cb7-90dd-a40d9cc0daaa?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o)), and companies running decentralized prover networks (like[Aleo](https://substack.com/redirect/d13afe40-6234-4c92-bbb6-d038837acd1b?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o)). He predicted that an “end game” of a zkVM, such as[Jolt](https://substack.com/redirect/070be09d-31c2-4fb0-badc-428787c0c84e?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o), augmented by[Binius](https://substack.com/redirect/503a58ea-e9ac-47ca-863d-d4312add5bb2?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o)(a hardware-optimized SNARK proving system) and other upcoming optimizations, plus dedicated hardware, could achieve a 1000x overhead over computation and could factor into the final, fully battle-tested version of Ethereum. He also predicted hardware will mostly focus on non-ZK succinct proofs, and most proofs will be Groth16-wrapped onchain. He also mentioned the Ethereum Foundation will be announcing a contest for formal verification of provers and verifiers, with $20 million in prizes.
[Jim Posen](https://substack.com/redirect/e9ff2b48-8e23-4c41-9b62-6768c379a498?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o), cofounder of Ulvetanna, talked about Binius, and the general concept of co-designing proof systems and hardware at the same time. Binius uses binary tower fields and the[sumcheck protocol](https://substack.com/redirect/77e80115-6ce7-47e7-a67a-fa927526386b?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o), which Jolt is also based on. An interesting takeaway from early testing of Binius is that performance was significantly better for the hash function Groestl (a SHA-3 runner up) than Keccak (the official SHA-3 standard), so it might be beneficial to use Groestl in some applications.
Decentralized prover networksMany in the space envision a future where proof generation for large statements (e.g. correctness of a bundle of transactions in a rollup) is done by a competitive, decentralized marketplace of specialized provers. [Uma Roy](https://substack.com/redirect/614eb7c5-3a59-4b7e-8c11-6b8598f9fbfa?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o), cofounder of[Succinct](https://substack.com/redirect/ba1bf7e4-80ca-44e1-82f0-898aa0f492a6?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o), talked about Succinct's upcoming prover network. She covered a variety of potential mechanism designs for decentralized prover networks, and predicted that designs based on racing (first prover wins) or mining (first prover wins, modulo some randomness) would not lead to good outcomes. She said design goals should be: Minimal cost, maximum latency, and censorship resistance, in that order. She predicted that an issuance/staking model could maybe work but an auction model is mostly likely to win out and may end up looking like block building does today. Succinct is building a general auction network for proofs that will support multiple zkVMs, not just Succinct’s own[SP1](https://substack.com/redirect/09b70454-54f5-425d-bc52-50124c62373d?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o), such as[Jolt/Lasso](https://substack.com/redirect/a2a1bd04-ba29-4e56-b9e2-3aad7ba3f214?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o), she said.
[Wenhao Wang](https://substack.com/redirect/4b357513-fa76-4ee0-94a7-1493008ae270?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o), PhD student at Yale, talked about a[new paper](https://substack.com/redirect/b097db7a-d0b4-41e9-9d78-6f5b54b78f58?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o)on the economics of prover networks that was released the morning of the talk, written in collaboration with[Ben Fisch](https://substack.com/redirect/daa85d80-55b2-4429-8c1d-7b4648243979?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o)([Espresso Systems](https://substack.com/redirect/d8af62c3-892d-460d-b2fb-707bf9da611f?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o)) and[Ben Livshits](https://substack.com/redirect/586a57da-370a-476e-990b-ac41d8dc67a4?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o)([Matter Labs](https://substack.com/redirect/5c52e899-f759-4fb0-b896-5fd2ba125af2?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o)). Wenhao mentioned that a two-sided auction is vulnerable to collusion between provers and bidders, and they introduce an alternative mechanism called Proo-phi that involves greedily matching transactions and provers. Proof-phi requires setting a capacity parameter, which appears to be a key open design question.
[Daniel Kales](https://substack.com/redirect/d00c99dd-d8bb-4708-8223-668024c17e5d?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o), cofounder and chief technology officer of[TACEO](https://substack.com/redirect/aea04034-3f66-45e8-8f35-191d69b7a5d4?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o), talked about[multiparty computation](https://substack.com/redirect/91c5457e-c8c6-45b2-a94d-dfc02f6fa347?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o)(MPC)-enabled proof markets, specifically using MPC to maintain privacy between a small client with a secret witness and a large untrusted prover. He talked about how we can choose combinations of proof systems to mostly do linear operations (like the Fast Fourier Transform algorithm) which are relatively cheap in MPC and minimize cost.
ZK credentialsThree different talks discussed efforts to build zero-knowledge credentials out of existing identity systems. Each relied on a different existing identity system. [Aayush Gupta](https://substack.com/redirect/5500e88b-f81c-46f9-9046-b1c43de4971c?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o)and[Sora Suegami](https://substack.com/redirect/f0433f0e-1fdc-41bf-adf9-95c12c598b5a?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o), cofounders of[ZK Email](https://substack.com/redirect/6f43afb1-5e04-4665-a1aa-c269726926eb?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o), talked about ZK proofs of email address ownership. These rely on proving knowledge of a DKIM signature of an email sent to a specific address, and DKIM is already widely deployed by major email providers (albeit primarily as an anti-spam measure). Many applications are possible with a ZK proof that a user controls an email address, including sending money to an email address but also applications like anonymous whistleblowing.
[Alin Tomescu](https://substack.com/redirect/c577bf21-1881-404a-b267-45c798e30ab8?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o), research scientist at[Aptos Labs](https://substack.com/redirect/ea3d19d7-03e9-43ad-8563-d766a7be455f?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o), talked about[Aptos Keyless](https://substack.com/redirect/8dcb8fdd-73b7-405f-8e81-204f84bee862?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o), which uses[OpenID connect](https://substack.com/redirect/10054df6-d22e-4cb4-aabe-c20c2cca2f2e?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o)to interact with traditional web2 identities. OpenID connect is the technology that enables “log in with Facebook, Google, etc.” to third-party websites. Aptos Keyless interacts with existing OpenID providers and proves that a user controls a given address, enabling applications like sending money to a Google or Facebook account.
[Michael Elliot](https://substack.com/redirect/d9a2d31f-539a-48e1-b459-a16f0ed46c0b?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o)and[Derya Karli](https://substack.com/redirect/13c17e9d-91c9-4035-88e2-27b3ea11bdbb?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o)of[zkPassport](https://substack.com/redirect/7572b93f-ab4c-43e6-b71c-ecf1f036f913?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o)discussed building anonymous credentials from existing ePassports. For example, users might prove that they hold a passport from the United States and are above 25 years of age, without revealing their passport number or exact age.

Strata
On their own, the most powerful archetypes are useless or worse. They have to be paired with another pattern, animated by specific content.
The archetype of rebirth, for example, has to answer a question: reborn as what?
That’s true for the whole family of rebirth-adjacent patterns: evolution, development, transformation, unfoldment, emergence,,,
Evolve into what? Transform into what? Emerge as what?
It’s one thing to be transformed into a Warrior, or to develop into a Sage, or to emerge as a Tribe. It’s another thing to non-specifically just unfold. To deny the dynamic tension of the evolving path, in favor of continuing to just evolve without settling into a form.
A ton of energy can be raised by invoking rebirth, but if that energy doesn’t get channeled somewhere specific, it either fades away or ends up following the path of least resistance, like lightning or floodwaters.
Archetypes are form, not content. The forms are at our disposal, but the content we animate them with is a deciding factor. The energy they raise can do almost anything—which sounds promising right up until you see what almost anything looks like up close.
Encounter
The Humanities are the study of consciousness coded in culture.
~ Jeffrey Kripal, The Flip
This quote strikes me as resoundingly world-shifting—but only for certain ideas of what consciousness is.
If you believe that consciousness is generated by the brain, or that it’s an accident of evolution or an illusion, then the way it gets coded in culture might be an interesting curiosity, but nothing really crucial to learn about.
If, though, you believe consciousness is a more mysterious and fundamental aspect of reality, not generated by the human body but rather filtered through it, then its encoding in culture and history suddenly becomes a very interesting way of exploring the nature of reality.

The Scoop launched as a regular series in 2022. Most Thursdays, I bring you interesting events, trends and patterns I detect, based on conversations with software engineers and engineering managers. Many scoops come from [people proactively contacting me](https://substack.com/redirect/dc928624-9c2e-4e9b-aaee-4d4b2d0e9a26?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o) and I treat these sources as confidential and anonymous by default.
The Scoop is my way of “keeping my ear on the ground” and sharing with you what’s going on inside tech businesses, as it happens. This means The Scoop reported some potentially industry-wide shifts in their very early stages, during 2022.
For the second year in a row, the mainstream media regularly confirmed what I observed and reported, months after it was covered in The Scoop. For example, at the end of October, I gathered enough evidence to make the call that a [Big Tech hiring slowdown was starting](https://substack.com/redirect/675e8632-eb28-4387-b4fd-777d2109e68c?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o), which has had industry-wide implications, including new grads having a harder time getting a foothold in the industry, especially Big Tech. The New York Times confirmed this observation six weeks later, reporting that [Computer science students face a shrinking Big Tech Market](https://substack.com/redirect/79e8ab43-b939-40b5-b793-a256f8791db0?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o).
This was similar to what happened during the fall of 2021, when I covered [The perfect storm causing an insane hiring market](https://substack.com/redirect/24b8b68d-7795-436e-912e-fdd95bb9c815?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o). Six months later, the New York Times [covered the same topic](https://substack.com/redirect/c63dccfd-a615-4d0f-99e6-acf9614e9dfa?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o).
During 2022, I covered the following trends in The Scoop:
[A chilling and boiling hiring market](https://substack.com/redirect/bf34daad-09ba-4186-8039-73b6a1d1ce54?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o)in February. This was a strange month, when much of the market was very hot, but layoffs had already begun at companies like Peloton and Hopin. I was unsure what to make of this blend of contrasting forces.[The start of the Great Reshuffle?](https://substack.com/redirect/e6b0b8d2-1ecb-4b5a-9205-c22231d2c015?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o)By March, unease was spreading in Big Tech about the size and composition of compensation packages, and I expected more people to switch jobs. Back then, it was still an employee’s market, as private companies like Cruise were introducing programs to buy secondary shares.[Fast unexpectedly goes bankrupt](https://substack.com/redirect/a4324c8b-3e0f-4081-9baa-56f22691aa68?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o). In an exclusive in early April, I brought you details about what happened inside the company which had raised $100M just a year earlier. But the business failed to raise its next round of funding. At the time, Fast looked like it could be an isolated case. But it turned out to be a canary in the coalmine: VC funding was drying up, fast.[A tech market slowdown looking likely](https://substack.com/redirect/7c4c03ac-8959-49dc-ad24-469d3438c9b0?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o). In late April I was wondering if late-stage companies like Stripe, Klarna and Databricks were overvalued. Sure enough, by the end of 2022, all have cut their former valuations, and Databricks did so recently. I wrote that I expected layoffs to conserve cash; sadly, this prediction came true.[The tech boom is over](https://substack.com/redirect/f7e9d402-2afe-4e2d-a327-230fdee75155?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o)in May. A stream of hiring freezes or layoffs started at places like Meta, Wayfair, Redfin and Bolt. Later, all these companies did layoffs and rescinded offers.[The crypto bust](https://substack.com/redirect/86e734b1-7c6f-4325-9056-add6a3a54aa4?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o)in June. Coinbase, Gemini, Crypto.com, BlockFi, Bitso and BitMex announced layoffs and it seemed like a golden age for Crypto was rapidly ending. Looking back, it certainly was.[The start of a tech winter?](https://substack.com/redirect/c5a899d2-4b36-467c-a444-b0c8ae75e21e?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o)in July. Most of Big Tech froze hiring and Apple joined this group.[Return to the office at Apple](https://substack.com/redirect/dc7d115e-b337-4044-918b-b3afe7b7f6d3?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o)in September. Apple became the first major Big Tech to mandate that employees return to the office for 3 days per week, and it seems it suffered no major attrition as a result. Perhaps because by this time, the Big Tech hiring market was cool enough.[Meta’s historic growth challenge](https://substack.com/redirect/67533cea-b17f-4602-a94a-9dc10146ce44?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o)in October. A month before Meta announced its historic first layoffs, I analyzed why the company faced a bigger growth challenge than ever before. Talking with engineering managers and directors, most were hopeful Meta would avoid layoffs, but everyone was clear the decision rested with Zuck. In November, Meta let go 13% of staff.[Cruel changes at Twitter](https://substack.com/redirect/607c7d63-c558-4619-915a-afa9258cb272?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o)early November. As the market was cooling across Big Tech, Elon Musk bought Twitter. Almost overnight, the then richest man in the world turned the workplace into a theater of cruelty; the most toxic environment in Big Tech, especially for software engineers. Week after week, Musk enforced unnecessarily cruel methods like firing people then calling them back the next day, giving employees 24 hours to return to the office, and firing a software engineer in public on Twitter who had corrected him, then[mocking](https://substack.com/redirect/3ac67428-fd06-4f8f-b05f-b3e050731e62?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o)those who’d just lost their jobs. Even now, when most tech workers are off for two weeks, Twitter employees are expected to work through the holidays.[Companies still hiring](https://substack.com/redirect/52a4fa53-2d49-455e-8f8c-7f37ddf63046?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o)and “The Good Scoop,” mid-November. To counterbalance the volume of negative news this year, I started to seek out positive stories, the ones that rarely get told. In what was a somewhat unexpected development, the major news outlet Bloomberg noticed this newsletter, and recently [published a profile of The Pragmatic Engineer and me](https://substack.com/redirect/7b93ebed-2b5f-4bf7-8f2a-73fb5ab648ec?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o).
Looking ahead to 2023, my main goal is to ensure you get interesting, relevant and actionable details and insights about software engineering, engineering management, career development, Big Tech, startups – and everything in between.
I continue to keep my ear to the ground, to report on the hottest topics in software engineering. Looking ahead, expect more articles on topics like security engineering, disciplines like developer advocacy, and technologies like machine learning. I’m also planning to delve into cases of engineering teams operating in “pragmatic” ways.
As budgets shrink across tech, I’m also looking to widen the scope of these newsletters by covering how “non-high growth” startups do more with less, in addition to covering Big Tech and high-growth startups, as usual.

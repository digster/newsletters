The Tech Downturn

A guest post from newsletter writer Eric Newcomer.

Gergely Orosz
May 11

I’ve been covering the increasingly chilly tech market in The Scoop #10 (Another chill hitting the tech market), The Scoop #9 (Big changes at Twitter, Netflix, Apple Robinhood, and predicting companies that might lay off next), and The Scoop #8 (A tech market slowdown starting?).

It is increasingly looking like the start of a tech market downturn. But how did we get here?

Fellow Substack writer Eric Newcomer covers the intersection of venture capital and technology in his deeply-reported newsletter. 

In his latest issue, he caught up with top investors, sketched out what happened in the last 18 months, where we are today, and put together out how things might play out from here. He generously agreed to share the second part of his subscriber-only issue with The Pragmatic Engineer readers.

Fall 2021

The peak came on November 17. The NASDAQ composite hit its all-time high. That same day, I published an ominous article about private market valuations, titled, “Raising More With Less.”

I documented nine companies that had raised at unicorn valuations with less than $10 million in annual recurring revenue. One of the CEOs emailed me to ask me about the story, “Is your take that the investors are crazy or that these companies must have something really exciting going on? :)”

In that same article, I broke the news that white board company Miro — a company that had benefited from the work-from-home world of the pandemic — was raising at a decacorn valuation. That deal, which ultimately valued Miro at $17.5 billion, could end up ranking as the frothiest in a frothy time for a SaaS startups.

I wrote that founders were playing with fire,

“Investors are logically shooting for the company that could be the next Snowflake, the next decacorn,” says Nick Mehta, the CEO of SaaS company Gainsight. “But for some of those companies that never make it to that status but are still good businesses — they’ll be stuck with a high valuation and high 409a and won’t be able to get back to that valuation for 5, 7, 10 years.”

That could make it hard to hire and retain employees. And employees who join after an astronomical valuation could eventually find their options underwater.

For an unlucky subset of these companies, Mehta said, “It will be a little bit like a zombie existence where you feel like you’re treading water.”

It would be downhill from there.

Winter, Late 2021 / Early 2022

We entered a period where the private markets tried to gauge whether the downturn would be permanent.

I wrote in January after public equities had started to fall, Here's What Investors Think the Treacherous Public Markets Mean for Private Startups.

Many of those predictions hold up well with the benefit of hindsight:

  • Many investors feel that companies were truly overvalued.

  • Crossover investors like Tiger Global and Coatue could shift even more of their private market investing to Series A and Series B companies.

  • Early stage rounds will be the last to adjust.

  • It only takes a few crazy investors to ignore the public market correction.

Today and Looking Forward

With the NASDAQ composite down 26% since the start of the year, we’ve moved closer toward a consensuses that the tech downturn is here to stay.

Here are a few tech stocks’ performance since the start of the year:

  • Facebook is down 42%

  • Asana is off 70%

  • Snowflake is down 57%

  • Uber is down 46%

  • Netflix has fallen 70%

  • Coinbase is down 71%

  • Robinhood is down 50%

Firms like Dragoneer, D1, and Coatue have slowed or halted their late stage private investing. (One notable exception to that has been Insight, which announced a $20 billion fund in February and has continued to make investments, sources tell me.)

Tiger is doing some later stage investing but has largely shifted its focus toward Series A funding rounds. Tiger — either through the fund or its partners’ private money — has funded a bunch of seed stage investors, giving them visibility and deal flow into up-and-coming Series A deals, sources tell me.

The closer a company is to going public, the more pain that it’s feeling. Even though Instacart cut its valuation, there seems to be plenty of room to fall.

But from what I can tell, seed and Series A deals remain frothy.

Crypto investing has been the rare bright spot over the past six months. But there are a few worrying signs: Bitcoin is down 35% since the start of the year and the NFT market is imploding.

The private market pullback narrative has been complicated by how much money funds have raised. So that’s given room for venture capital firms to swoop in where Tiger would have last year.

For instance, I know of one buzzy SaaS company that has investors committed to raise at unicorn valuation that hasn’t been announced. The deal is still in the works but it looks to be a sign that up rounds are still getting done.

But anecdotally it feels like there are more layoffs than funding rounds. Many companies are going to try to weather the storm, shed employees, and lower cash burn as they pray the market corrects before they need to raise a down round.

I’ve been pounding the pavement for verifiable down rounds but haven’t been able to ferret out any notable ones. Investors expect founders to “extend” their last rounds, trying to raise money at the same price they did months ago.

Layoffs are becoming common.

Wednesday, 4th of May 2022, the B2B finance startup MainStreet announced that it was laying off 30% of employees.

Also on Wednesday, 4th of May 2022, The Information reported that the celebrity video app Cameo is laying off 80 employees — about a quarter of its workforce.

Meanwhile, this week, Insider reported that Facebook had imposed a hiring freeze. At the same time, The Pragmatic Engineer has reported on employees having their offers rescinded.

The biggest question now is whether we’re simply going to witness startup valuation multiples contract or if we will also see revenue growth/fundamentals falter across the board.

  • Startups that sell to other startups could be in trouble.

  • We haven’t really seen how SaaS businesses perform in a major correction. How sticky will their services be? Or will companies come to regret swapping large upfront payments for easier-to-cancel subscriptions?

  • On the valuation side, the collapse of Lyft’s stock price — down by a third after it reported earnings this week — has to be worrying for private online-to-offline businesses. Everyone expects to see consolidation among GoPuff’s competitors. Companies that are valued based on their gross merchandise volume could see those valuations crash as investors chase profits.

In March 2020, the pandemic gave Silicon Valley a head-fake downturn. Investors who pulled back missed a great moment to make money. How long will this pullback last? How high will interest rates go? How far does the stock market have to fall?

“Public market prices are mostly based on a consensus with thousands of people and institutions agreeing on price every day,” Redpoint Ventures investor Logan Bartlett messaged me. “The private market really only requires a founder and a single VC to agree to a price. Over the last few months, we’ve seen most firms adjust to a new normal on price for growth stage companies, but all it takes is one to bid at a valuation similar to December’s valuations for that to be the ‘market.’”

He concluded, “Right now there are fewer people drunk at the bar that need to go home. But it’s like New Orleans — as long as someone’s drinking, the bar stays open.”

Gergely’s thoughts

Gergely here. Venture capitalists have their own outlook of the market, and they are usually focused on returns, business growth opportunities, and want to get good deals - investing in growing companies, which will end up growing, and, one day, exiting, making a profit for their fund.

Venture capital plays a key role in the tech market. The more venture capital, the more startups get funding, and the more people they hire - often trying to hire away from other companies, and even from Big Tech, as I wrote in The Scoop #2 about startups openly competing with Big Tech salaries.

From Newcomer’s analysis, what looks increasingly certain is we will see a pullback in VC funding. The only question is whether this will just be like a “brief blip” as in 2008, or a longer one, like in 2000.

In either case, this is not good news for many people working in tech - and it’s especially bad news for people working at companies that will need more VC funding. If VC funding pulls back, this further cools the tech job market, as growing VC funding was one of the causes for the Perfect storm causing an insane tech hiring market in September 2021. 

For now: exercise caution when considering job offers from VC-funded companies. Do your due diligence, and ask about numbers: what’s the revenue? How many customers are there? How are these numbers growing? What is the runway?

More VC-funded companies are putting hiring freezes in place, and I have reports of some smaller companies pulling offers - even signed ones. It can be risky to join a company that is in dire need of VC funding, but cannot raise it. In the worst case, they could end up going bankrupt like Fast rapidly did. In the best case, they ride up this time by reducing their costs.

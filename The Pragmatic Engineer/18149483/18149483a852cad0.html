The Scoop: Business as Usual in Parts of the Market

Tech layoffs are continuing, but how reflective are these cuts of what’s happening in the broader industry?

Gergely Orosz
Jun 9

👋 Hi, this is Gergely with a bonus, free issue of the Pragmatic Engineer Newsletter.

In the latest issue of The Scoop, sent to full subscribers I analyzed what is really happening across the tech job market. Below is a part relevant for anyone working in tech, to help understand how indicative the stream of layoffs news is for the whole market.

Data on the tech hiring market

Reading tech news, it’s the stream of reporting on layoffs which stands out. But how bad are these cuts, in context of the whole tech market? The site layoffs.fyi offers a handy comparison, starting with data from 2020. Looking at this, it seems like we’re seeing fewer layoffs than in Spring 2020, when COVID-19 struck, global lockdowns kicked off, and many businesses decided to take this drastic measure:

Looking at this chart, it feels we’re seeing a similar layoffs trend as at the beginning of the pandemic.

Could we be seeing a “COVID-19 reversed” reaction on the job market? The newsletter Robinhood Snacks reported the service sector is struggling to hire fast enough, while in tech jobs are being cut. This is the exact opposite of what happened when COVID started, where service jobs were cut rapidly and tech was hiring rapidly. From Robinhood Snacks:

“A tale of two markets... Unemployment stayed at a pandemic low of 3.6%, and rebounding sectors like hospitality can't hire fast enough. The paradox: sectors that thrived mid-pandemic are slowing hiring and cutting jobs to shave costs.

We're hiring: Airlines, restaurants, hotels, and retailers are scrambling to boost headcount to meet demand — and have cut growth plans because they can’t. American Airlines has hired 16K employees this year and is still struggling to fill positions. Small businesses have been forced to slash hours and services.

We're un-hiring: As inflation and slowing demand hit tech profits, Peloton, Netflix, Carvana, and others have laid off workers (Tesla reportedly plans to as well.) Meanwhile: Microsoft, Meta, Salesforce, Twitter, Uber, Wayfair, and Snap say they’ll rein in hiring.”

The number of jobs posted on the monthly Hacker News “who is hiring” does not suggest a worrying trend. Hacker News is one of the most popular sites among software engineers looking for interesting, tech-related things to read. The site is operated by startup accelerator Y Combinator and has a monthly open thread called “Who is Hiring.” 

Job posts on this thread are overwhelmingly from startups and venture-funded companies, and some are from publicly traded tech companies. Using this script, I grabbed the data and visualized how the number of postings on the site has changed over the years:

Looking at the job postings from 2012, these went steadily up until 2018, with a slowdown to 2020, and then a peak in May 2020. Zooming in to the years 2018-2022:

In May 2020, the number of jobs posted hit a bottom of 540 vacancies. In June 2021, it hit a peak of 1,041 jobs posted. In May 2022, the number of jobs posted was almost identical to in January 2021.

Based on this data source, the jobs being posted now are indeed down, the decline does not look dramatic.

The number of tech jobs posted shows no visible drop over the past month, globally. I got in touch with DQ Market, a matchmaking platform connecting developers with fast-scaling engineering teams. Their team started a project to track the number of jobs posted for software engineers, worldwide. They do this by scraping the major job boards and aggregators like LinkedIn, Indeed and other job boards. I got access to some early results and here is the trend of global and US jobs posted per day:

Note that their current methodology does not eliminate duplicate jobs, but as this is consistent, it doesn’t distort the statistics much. This data source will prove more interesting as they gather details on a longer period of time.

You can browse the data on this site - it’s still a work in progress and the team behind it is improving it as we speak.

I hope this information was useful. The latest issue of The Scoop covers more details observed on the tech market among others, like how some tech companies are retaining staff, layoffs at Trade Republic, patterns of companies laying off, Coinbase rescinding offers, and others.

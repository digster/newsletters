Many Big Pre-Sputnik Earth-Orbit Glints

Robin Hanson
Aug 17
 
READ IN APP
 

IMHO, the strongest evidence so far that (some) UFOs are aliens just dropped. This evidence is of many brief bright glints of sunlight reflecting off of big surfaces in high orbit around Earth, before humans had put anything up there, and correlated in time with both UFO reports and nuclear tests.

Glints are brief flashes of the Sun (or other bright sources) off of flat reflecting surfaces. On Earth, and in the rest of the solar system, natural glints come mainly from flat surfaces of water or ice. (Rarely, wind polished rocks glint.) On Earth today, correcting for water, glints are strong indicators of human spaces, due to our many flat reflecting surfaces.

We humans have recently made the space around Earth glinty. Modern automated telescope sky surveys see ~1800 glints per hour per sky near the equator (as of ~2020), typically of momentary brightness magnitude 9 to 11, mostly due to satellites and their debris. Making a glint this bright requires (I think) an ideal ~1-2cm diameter circle at geosynchronous orbit (35.8K km up). Real glinting objects are smaller than this if closer to Earth, but larger if not perfectly flat, aligned, or reflective.

No one is known to have predicted big glints like this in Earth orbit before humans launched the first satellite, Sputnik, on October 4, 1957. Yet there seem to have in fact been ∼120 big glints per hour per sky before Sputnik, ~1/15 of the current rate!

Says who, you ask? The First Palomar Sky Survey, using the 48-inch Schmidt telescope at Mount Palomar 1949-1958, was the first comprehensive photo survey of the entire northern sky. It took almost 2,000 square photos, each covering about 6° of sky on a side. Each region was photographed twice in a row, once using a plate sensitive to red light for ~50min, and then one sensitive to blue for ~10min.

These plates were later digitized, and recently the VASCO project has looked for “transients”, i.e., objects seen in the red but not blue plates. For example, three close transients appear in the middle of the left plate but don’t appear in the right:

Due to telescope imprecision and atmospheric fluctuations during photo exposure, each space source shows up on the plates as a circular blob about two arc-seconds wide. Blobs of the transients seem narrower, sharper, and more round than others, suggesting that their sources were visible for shorter durations than a whole plate exposure.

We have good reasons to think most of these transients are brief glints of sunlight off of flat reflective objects in the rough ballpark of geosynchronous Earth orbit, as opposed to being defects in the photo plates, self-illuminating sources like stars, or sources much closer or further.

What reasons? If one assumes that sources are either at 42K km or 80K km from Earth, then one can calculate for each source whether the Earth blocks its view of the Sun. If most of these transients are glints reflecting sunlight, and if their sources are about this far away, then we should see more glints in places that are in sunlight, relative to in shadow.

And in fact, in a dataset of 106,339 transients, using the rate at which we see transients in sunlight regions, at 42K km we’d expect to see 1223 transients in the shadows, but we actually see only 349. And at 80K km, we’d expect to see 339 transients in the shadows, but see only 79. These differences are significant at 21.9 and 12.7 sigma levels respectively! Thus at least ~2/3 of these ~100K 1950s transients are in fact brief glints off of flat shiny objects in the roughly geosynchronous orbit!

How brief? As objects in geosynchronous orbit move about 15 arc-seconds per second, then these had to be <~0.01 sec duration glints to not noticeably stretch their photo blobs. Also, ~80% of the glints have magnitude 17-19, while those three close transients shown above were magnitude 15-16. At geosynchronous orbit it would take an ideal ~44-70 cm diameter circle, perfectly flat, aligned, and reflective, to create a magnitude 15-16 image in these photos from a 0.01 sec glint. (Or so says ChatGPT5.) For magnitude 17-19, that changes to an ideal 11-28 cm diameter. Objects less than perfectly flat, aligned, or reflective, or with shorter duration glints, would have to be larger.

Thus while today Earth sees ~1800 glints per hour off of the roughly ~1-2cm equivalent diameter ideal circle sized human satellites and debris, in the decade before Sputnik, the space around Earth generated ~120 glints per hour mostly from brief (<0.01s) glints of sunlight off of at least roughly 11-28cm equivalent ideal circles. Which looks larger than today, though there may be big errors in these estimates.

As far as we can tell, there was never a regular sequence of glints, with at least three in a row equally spaced in a line across the sky. But there are many clusters of glints, more than you’d predict at random, like the three in the picture above.

The distribution of magnitudes of these glints seems to follow the same power law as the distribution of stellar magnitudes. At first that seemed puzzling, but then I realized that in both cases the power seems set by how source count and brightness change with distance.

These glints also seem to have a significant date correlations with nuclear tests and UFO reports. Glints were 45% more likely (p = 0.008) on dates within one day of nuclear tests, and there was a significant (p<.001) correlation between the number of UFOs reported and number of glints on each date. In fact the cluster of three glints above appeared on July 27, 1952, during one of the most famous UFO events of the era at Washington DC.

If water and ice are the main sources of glints in the solar system, could big flat ice crystals be the source of these glints? I’m told that by volume most ice in near Earth space is in ~1-2mm diameter flakes, and most of the count is in sub-micron specks, both of which seem way too small to cause these big glints. It seems hard to imagine that they’ve missed many huge 10+ cm flat ice crystals. I guess someone should check.

But as that does seem a long shot, it seems we must take seriously the claim that these were glints off of big flat reflective parts of advanced devices from an alien civilization. That kinda hurts to type, knowing that many of you will respect me less for saying so. But I gotta go where the evidence leads.

Now obviously the authors behind these studies might have made big mistakes, and the world should want to see confirmations using other analysis and datasets. And we should worry a bit that they, or those who supplied them data, might be fronting for an expensive psych-op that is faking evidence to push some narrative.

My guess, however, is that this isn’t a psych-op, and they didn’t make big mistakes in the above analysis. Making this pretty big news.

Several LLMs find this thesis plausible and well supported by evidence. It predicts more travel and leisure, lower discount rates, investing more in In this post, I want to focus on the moral consequences of this process, as those have an out-sized impact on the key process of In the ancient world, people tried to live morally, and helped to define and enforce moral norms for their immediate associates. But the tasks of defining and enforcing morality beyond one’s immediate associates was mostly left to elites: gods, kings, priests, lords, etc. Such elites attended to the moral health of the communities to which they had strong ties, but not so much to more distant communities. And elites rarely had much reason to try to make big changes to how their community defined morality. Strongly interacting communities, when large like cities, sometimes broke into competing factions, usually defined in terms of material factors like ethnicity, family, age, gender, profession, region, and wealth. In such cases, individuals might see themselves as having identities defined by their relevant group memberships. Sometimes destructive factional conflict would induce norms of tolerance, wherein choices re shared communal spaces were not to be made overtly to favor disputed group identities. At some point universalizing religions arose, and sought to convert outsiders, often recruiting the entire population to such conversion efforts. This made ordinary people move involved in defining and enforcing the morals of larger communities, and inducing more frequent changes to such things. But only regarding the entire cultural package which was their religion. When such religions sometimes fragmented into competing factions, ordinary people were often recruited into promoting factions, but again as entire communities promoting entire religion packages. In Europe, conflicts between religious factions became so fierce and destructive as to induce a new norm of tolerance of religion. This added to prior European norms discouraging family clans as an overt basis for choices in shared communal spaces. These helped Europe to have unusually productive economies. In our modern world, trade, travel and talk happen on much wider social scales, and religion has declined, being replaced by many diverse unpackaged more frequently changing opinions on moral norms. Furthermore, we are all now rich, and so plausibly all see ourselves as like ancient high status elites, for whom it makes more sense to get involved in larger scale issues. So in addition to moving to democracy, so we can all get more involved in wider governance decisions, we all also get more involved in how best to define and enforce these many specific changing moral norms.

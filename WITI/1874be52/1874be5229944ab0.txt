Guan Yang (GY) is a data scientist based in New York. His favorite content set in Venice is the Brunetti Mysteries [ https://substack.com/redirect/08eb077a-754c-4782-bbcd-cd5265cbf06d?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ].
Guan here. The Most Serene Republic of Venice (726-1797) was a major commercial and maritime power. In addition to the famous lagoon city in Northeastern Italy, Venice ruled territories and islands stretching all the way down to Crete.
Map by Reincanarietheorie [ https://substack.com/redirect/d374d376-1a82-43fa-8a6a-902045518408?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ]
Venice’s head of state was the Doge, or Doxe in Venetian [ https://substack.com/redirect/1d6fe6ac-5010-42d9-a611-229c6cde25c7?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] (not the shiba inu based [ https://substack.com/redirect/a3d7aeaa-5a00-4474-ba95-ca037f2fdba0?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] meme [ https://substack.com/redirect/7c567f7a-0bb4-43b1-81d2-0d9c68bf2ec3?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ], or the related cryptocurrency). The doge was chosen with rules first adopted in 1268 [ https://substack.com/redirect/8df5e2fb-4cf8-4ed3-a3ff-ad17e95fb511?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] and used, with few changes, until the last election in 1789:
Thirty members of the Great Council, chosen by lot, were reduced by lot to nine; the nine chose forty and the forty were reduced by lot to twelve, who chose twenty-five. The twenty-five were reduced by lot to nine, and the nine elected forty-five. These forty-five were once more reduced by lot to eleven, and the eleven finally chose the forty-one who elected the doge. Election required at least twenty-five votes out of forty-one, nine votes out of eleven or twelve, or seven votes out of nine electors.
On the chance you found that hard to follow, Miranda Mowbray and Dieter Gollman of HP Labs summarized it with this handy table in a paper analyzing the procedure [ https://substack.com/redirect/5f614dc0-b35a-4a66-b0e6-ecccb8ea02a6?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ]:
In addition to the hereditary Great Council (round 1), we get nine additional bodies, some chosen randomly by lot—a process known as “sortition [ https://substack.com/redirect/eca4a5c5-2e6e-4c55-8a23-6709cc9d446a?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ]”—and some elected under various supermajority rules (meaning they require a greater than 50 percent vote), culminating in a 41-person college that elects the doge with at least 25 votes. There were also restrictions on the number of members of each college from each family and on the electors’ communications with the outside world [ https://substack.com/redirect/69bc3194-2691-4e4f-b72c-c22faeb3fbe1?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ]. Campaigning was forbidden.
Neither papal conclave [ https://substack.com/redirect/c24be02b-0a26-4447-bebc-a6a1bdf01d70?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] nor the system used to select [ https://substack.com/redirect/3562a910-3cf0-45a8-a188-274cb233ebb3?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] the [ https://substack.com/redirect/17222b35-d33a-49f3-bde1-19fcbc54cd76?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] president of the United States can hold a candle to this harebrained scheme. So why did the Venetians make it so complicated, and why did the system survive so long?
Gabriele Bella: Election of the Doge by the Forty-one [ https://substack.com/redirect/3e012667-5329-4fa6-b2f0-9434cd16efce?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ]
The city was nominally governed by the doge and the hundreds of noble families [ https://substack.com/redirect/6064a799-ff27-4248-b376-a33c5af984ff?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] represented in the Great Council, but politics was dominated by a handful of powerful families. The standard explanation [ https://substack.com/redirect/4f44e907-ce30-4319-954d-7ac810d5178e?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] for the complex selection procedure is that it prevented powerful families from applying undue pressure to elect their preferred candidates without majority support. (Being doge was quite costly, and the powers of the office were later curtailed, so some powerful men tried to avoid election, using the same tricks used to get elected.)
Sortition, combined with isolation for the electoral college, can make the system less vulnerable to manipulation [ https://substack.com/redirect/f331d587-e922-4177-b00d-b6c43c27eb2d?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ], and allow the randomly selected electors more careful consideration and deliberation. Adding a supermajority rule to sortition makes it less likely that a minority candidate can be elected by chance [ https://substack.com/redirect/17d9511e-5af7-42e4-854b-84dc0f90e625?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] because they happen to be supported by members of the randomly selected group.
Mathematical analysis of the protocol shows that having many rounds of sortition could lead to a doge being elected with only minority support, so using too many rounds would be unwise [ https://substack.com/redirect/5f614dc0-b35a-4a66-b0e6-ecccb8ea02a6?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ]. But having multiple rounds reduces the probability that a powerful minority candidate could unduly influence a small number of electors with bribes or threats, and makes it harder to figure out exactly who should be bribed or threatened.
Why is this interesting?
The way societies make decisions is important. There is a growing understanding that different systems can lead to quite different outcomes. Ireland rejected the British first-past-the-post system after independence and adopted the single transferable vote [ https://substack.com/redirect/d0a2e56a-663d-4ee0-90a2-4eb9c20b41a1?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] in 1921. New York City started using ranked-choice voting this summer, with some hiccups [ https://substack.com/redirect/e89b858a-7e19-48cc-921e-a23f0f4e1cba?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ]. Other countries have moved to full proportional representation where seats are allocated to parties more or less [ https://substack.com/redirect/88b43f02-9e4b-48bb-a464-a3aba633401e?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] based on national vote share.
There’s also the question of the best level of representation. Should city councils be elected at-large for the whole city (like in Cambridge, Mass. [ https://substack.com/redirect/1658d152-2691-4536-8423-a25c0975c8cb?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ]) or in single-member districts, and how would that affect outcomes such as diversity [ https://substack.com/redirect/c71bf1d2-0a26-4694-9a19-f47cdb258ecc?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] and zoning [ https://substack.com/redirect/f94847e1-fb18-4959-9b2f-8b1c72fb6fc5?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ]? Perhaps some decisions should be taken away from the city council, and either moved down to the neighborhood level or up to the regional level? And should some decisions, such as monetary policy, be taken out of democratic control altogether and left to technocrats [ https://substack.com/redirect/4d610dd5-aa26-425a-8054-6d0d94a0b126?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ]?
Using sortition to choose government officials, as Venice and Ancient Athens did [ https://substack.com/redirect/93b72cac-dce6-4860-8ff4-c5e97e25581f?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ], is a niche idea these days, but in common-law countries, juries deciding legal cases are (supposed to be [ https://substack.com/redirect/1611c485-abfd-454a-967f-8e238bb2c87f?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ]) chosen randomly from the population. Nobel laureate Daniel McFadden [ https://substack.com/redirect/50eeedb8-e860-4c32-87bc-5c0930e0011e?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] wants to use “economic juries [ https://substack.com/redirect/5c19b9dc-18fc-4647-b865-91f607a34729?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ]” of randomly selected people to decide on big public projects, arguing that this can better reflect public opinion than a referendum.
Since these political design choices affect policy outcomes, it would be naive to think this is only about high-minded notions of the “quality” of decisions. But that doesn’t make the question of how societies should make decisions any less interesting. (GY)

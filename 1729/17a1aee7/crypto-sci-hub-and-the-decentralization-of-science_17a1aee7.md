How academic paywalls prevent progressAnalysts estimate that Journals form a core part of the process of scholarly communication and are an integral part of scientific research itself. Journals do not just disseminate information, they also provide a mechanism for the registration of the author’s precedence; maintain quality through peer review and provide a fixed archival version for future reference. They also provide an important way for scientists to navigate the ever-increasing volume of published material. But in reality, our current practice of keeping the most high-quality scientific papers in closed repositories works against all four of these benefits. 1) Dissemination of research: It usually costs Individual articles are usually available a-la-carte, but in most of the publications we reviewed they ranged in price from $35 to over $100. If your work requires you to read multiple articles per week (or even per day), these rates are prohibitive. So the process of sharing and discussing research papers online is sufficiently complex, even for scientific insiders, that too often they just don't do it. Or, they learn to rely on mainstream media outlets and other proxies. 2) Registration of precedence: The scientific paywall doesn't really make it difficult to determine who published a novel idea or result first, but it does make it a lot more expensive and painful to look for prior art so that you don't end up repeating someone else's work by accident. It also extracts a lot of money to fulfill a function that's fairly trivial to do on the blockchain. 3) Providing a fixed archival version for future reference: When privately-owned journals go under, their archives can simply vanish from the internet. What would a crypto Sci-Hub look like?Now you understand the academic publishing problem in some detail, and the inside-the-system approaches (like PLoS or ArXiv) and the outside-the-system approaches (like Sci-Hub and LibGen). One possible resolution that people have discussed is a new "streaming" publisher, similar to iTunes or Spotify, which offers a catalog of all papers for a flat fee. But a more radical approach would be a crypto Sci-Hub, one that might even offer enough financial incentive to legacy publishers to flip them over to open access for their entire archive.

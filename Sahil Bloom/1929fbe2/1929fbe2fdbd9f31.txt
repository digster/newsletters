Question to avoid self-rejection:
---------------------------------

Are you allowing self-protection to become self-rejection?

Here's a tough truth to face:

Sometimes the reason you didn't give your best effort is because
you're afraid of what will happen if you give it your all and
still fail.

Your self-protection can quickly become self-rejection.

I've been there. I've avoided giving 100% effort to something
because I wanted an out in case it didn't work. I wanted to be
able to say, "Well, I didn't really try that hard" if I failed.

It's a flimsy self-protection mechanism: To protect your ego, you
sacrifice your growth and progress. You self-reject as you
self-protect.

So, this weekend, take a close look at the challenges you are
taking on:

Where are you allowing your self-protection to become self
rejection?

Hedgehogs vs. Foxes
-------------------

In the 8th century B.C., a Greek poet named Archilochus wrote a
parable that has stood the test of time:

"The fox knows many things; the hedgehog knows one big thing."
The idea gained prominence in the modern world when philosopher
Isaiah Berlin wrote his 1953 essay, ​The Hedgehog and the Fox​ (
https://click.convertkit-mail4.com/wvu37nnrx6agh5dr3lxcefnx4ozxxc8/z2hghnh32merzesp/aHR0cHM6Ly93d3cuYW1hem9uLmNvbS9IZWRnZWhvZy1Gb3gtVG9sc3RveXMtSGlzdG9yeS1TZWNvbmQvZHAvMDY5MTE1NjAwWA==
), which explored the topic in greater depth.

In Berlin's framework, you could categorize thinkers and writers
into two distinct groups:

* Hedgehogs: Relate everything to a single, central vision; see
the world through the lens of this one overarching principle.
* Foxes: Embrace multiple, sometimes conflicting visions or
perspectives; see the world as too complex to reduce to a single
principle.

In the context of deep thought, philosophy, and writing, Berlin
offered a favorable view of the Fox: The ability to navigate
complexity, avoid oversimplification, and maintain mental agility
are all pro-Fox.

But when author Jim Collins expanded on the idea to apply it to
the business world in his best-selling book, ​Good to Great​ (
https://click.convertkit-mail4.com/wvu37nnrx6agh5dr3lxcefnx4ozxxc8/p8heh9hzwp45egtq/aHR0cHM6Ly93d3cuYW1hem9uLmNvbS9Hb29kLUdyZWF0LVNvbWUtQ29tcGFuaWVzLU90aGVycy9kcC8wMDY2NjIwOTk2L3JlZj10bW1faHJkX3N3YXRjaF8wP19lbmNvZGluZz1VVEY4JmRpYj1leUoySWpvaU1TSjkuem9xN29mSVN3eHNCd3ZWM3NzLVZPbkM2Z2pRQ0VqMS15SGNTX2VuY0NOZjI0SWQxQV94Y29uTHJyS25rRGV1Q0g5Y0RZSkhCdTU2OE94TWtvbUVzY1Zsdkctc3Zlb1h5MTU4SklqbG5hQjNqUE84ZnQ1NDg3a3puSjNseWt6Z2liQXk0aDVDUHV5YjRuTnhUZWpwN2JRTGltREhtanlsdHBjRTZCN1FmWHdzblhMTDVxZ3Vic25rUUxHT0x5cUhWM013bHhXbk5Cbi1nQnkwWWp1Ni1iYXoyRV92MnlDRkJ4MTlYY291bmZwcy5McmJ3bDZieWFWbVNzR3lJbHpNMlVONlhtZjBSMGhaQy1Hcy0tVG53Mk9rJmRpYl90YWc9c2UmcWlkPTE3Mjg5MDI5OTgmc3I9MS0x
), he leaned into the metaphor of the Hedgehog as a powerful
business framework.

His so-called Hedgehog Concept is based on three central
principles:

* Focus: The best companies have a singular focus and simplify
complex markets down into this unifying idea.
* Discipline: The best companies have radical self-awareness and
honesty about competencies and limitations.
* Economic Clarity: The best companies know the single metric
that best captures their economic performance.

I love this framework from Berlin and Collins, but I don't think
it's as simple as taking a pro-Fox or pro-Hedgehog view...

To me, the real gold in life is found in balancing the Hedgehog
and the Fox that exist within you:

* Your inner Hedgehog maintains a grounding focus and clarity on
your True North, your vision for the future.
* Your inner Fox remains adaptable, capable of navigating the
chaos that inevitably enters your world.

It's not about being a Hedgehog or a Fox. You need both in order
to build a thriving life.

Study that I found fascinating:
-------------------------------

There's a common saying that you become the average of the five
people you spend the most time with. This study (
https://click.convertkit-mail4.com/wvu37nnrx6agh5dr3lxcefnx4ozxxc8/x0hph6hwv4edm3u5/aHR0cHM6Ly93d3cuaGJzLmVkdS9yaXMvUHVibGljYXRpb24lMjBGaWxlcy9qb2huJTIwbm9ydG9uJTIwMjAxM184ZDE0MjdhYS0xMDA5LTQ1NWItOGI0Yi05NzdlZjI1OWU2MDcucGRm
) may beg to differ...

Researchers gave treadmill desks to a group of employees and then
provided them with data on their and their coworkers' activity
levels on the treadmills over a six month period.

They found that there was a tendency to converge to the lowest
common denominator—meaning participants slowly aligned their
walking behavior with their least active coworker.

While I hesitate to apply the insights of a single study too
broadly, it should serve as a reminder to pay close attention to
the people you surround yourself with.

You may be getting dragged down without knowing it...

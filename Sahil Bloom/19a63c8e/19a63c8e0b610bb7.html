Monday: The "Rules" That Hold You Back In Life

There is a concept in cognitive science called the Illusory Truth Effect:

It's the tendency to believe false information after consistent, repeated exposures. In other words, if you're told something over and over again, it takes root in your mind as a truth.

This is how suggestions slowly, silently start to feel like rules.

If you're not careful, over time, you'll stop questioning them and start organizing your entire life around them.

But here's the truth: Most of the "rules" shaping your life are anything but.

This week I challenged readers to ask the question:

What rules am I following that are really just suggestions?

Wednesday: The Logical Fallacy Field Guide

Last week, I shared ​the story​ of the Donkey and the Tiger, who argued over the color of the grass.

The Donkey insisted it was blue, while the Tiger maintained it was green. To settle the argument, they brought their case to the Lion, who agreed with the Donkey and sentenced the Tiger to a year of silence. Before walking away, the Tiger asked why he was punished, when the Lion knew the grass was green.

"Because someone as wise as you should never waste time proving anything to a fool."

The lesson of the story is simple: Never argue with a fool. Because if you argue with a fool, you become one yourself.

Friday: Chesterton's Fence: The Importance of the Origin

Never remove a fence until you know why it was there in the first place.

In our rush to criticize, it's important to first understand why something exists in its current form––to understand the origin.

Fences typically aren't built for no reason at all. If something exists, there was generally a reason. Tear it down without understanding that reason, and you may open yourself up to the wolves it was keeping at bay.

Most discussions of Chesterton's Fence focus on change within societies, culture, or business. It's a call-to-action to think deeply before making sweeping change.

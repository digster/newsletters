Monday: A Scene That Changed How I Think About Success
In a recent visit with University of Missouri head coach Eliah Drinkwitz, I heard a story that stuck with me—a single scene from Yellowstone that completely changed how he thought about success.
In the scene, a seasoned veteran tells a young rider: “To be any good at this game...you gotta do it for the horse.”
That line cuts to the truth. Most people do it for the lights, the crowd, the applause. But real greatness isn’t found there. It’s found in the quiet service. Between you and your craft, you and your people, you and your mission.
If you focus on the horse, you’ll always find a way to win.

Friday: The Water Glass Effect
I recently came across an [old video](https://click.kit-mail6.com/p9uv6rrdq4a9h2x4x5gfqhpd0l033hrhgdv6g/58hvh7hgkd737gh6/aHR0cHM6Ly93d3cueW91dHViZS5jb20vd2F0Y2g_dj1SR1RmRWZWY01xQQ==) of a professor teaching a powerful lesson with a simple glass of water.
He explained that the absolute weight of the glass doesn’t matter—it depends on how long you hold onto it. Hold it for a minute, nothing happens. Hold it for an hour, and your arm starts to ache. Hold it all day, and it becomes unbearable.
The stresses and worries of life are like that glass of water. The longer you hold onto them, the heavier they become.
Most of the burden we feel in life comes not from the weight of what we’re holding, but from how long we choose to hold it.
Put the glass down.

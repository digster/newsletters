The 80-Year-Old Life Decathlon
******************************

Photo by Matt Bennett
In his best-selling book, Outlive: The Science & Art of Longevity
(
https://click.convertkit-mail4.com/27uo6kkm80hohvd7k4ot8urxep344/kkhmh6hl3new5pal/aHR0cHM6Ly93d3cuYW1hem9uLmNvbS9PdXRsaXZlLUxvbmdldml0eS1QZXRlci1BdHRpYS1NRC9kcC8wNTkzMjM2NTk5L3JlZj10bW1faHJkX3N3YXRjaF8wP19lbmNvZGluZz1VVEY4JnFpZD0mc3I9
), Dr. Peter Attia proposes a novel framework to establish and
organize physical aspirations for lifeŌĆÖs later years.

He calls it the Centenarian Decathlon, a reference to the Olympic
event that combines ten track and field events held across two
consecutive days.

In the book, Dr. Attia describes the Centenarian Decathlon as
ŌĆ£the ten most important physical tasks you will want to be able
to do for the rest of your life.ŌĆØ

The items on the list should be specific, but can include actual
athletic feats (AttiaŌĆÖs personal list has ŌĆ£Do five pull-upsŌĆØ and
ŌĆ£Swim half a mile in twenty minutesŌĆØ), personal interests (ŌĆ£hike
1.5 miles on a hilly trailŌĆØ), or simple, important activities of
a high-functioning daily life (ŌĆ£pick up a young child from the
floorŌĆØ or ŌĆ£carry two five-pound bags of groceries for five
blocksŌĆØ).

My list looks something like this:

* Walk for an hour in the neighborhood.
* Pick up a small child off the floor.
* Perform 30 minutes of resistance training.
* Perform 5 pull-ups, 5 push-ups, and 5 body squats in 5 minutes.
* Chase grandchildren around yard for 15 minutes.
* Carry heavy suitcases or groceries into the house from the car.
* Walk up flights of stairs without assistance.
* Toss a small child up in the air in a swimming pool.
* Hike 3 miles on a moderate difficulty trail.
* Get up off the floor without assistance.

Dr. Attia uses the list as a visual stake in the ground to inform
the training and lifestyle actions necessary in the present. To
do these things at 100 (or 80 or 90), you need to be working on
them today.

Imagining the ideal future implies a clear set of necessary
actions in the present.

While Dr. Attia's Centenarian Decathlon is grounded entirely in
the physical, I think it's even more informative to go through a
similar exercise that covers the full breadth of our lives.

Your Ideal 80-Year-Old Life
---------------------------

What does your ideal life look like at age 80?

* Who are you with?
* Where are you?
* What are you doing?
* How do you feel?

Here was what I imagined as my ideal life at 80-years old (
https://click.convertkit-mail4.com/27uo6kkm80hohvd7k4ot8urxep344/25h2hoh2e3g9gru3/aHR0cHM6Ly93d3cuaW5zdGFncmFtLmNvbS9yZWVsL0N3NUNSVXhnTHJiLz91dG1fc291cmNlPWlnX3dlYl9jb3B5X2xpbmsmaWdzaGlkPU16UmxPREJpTldGbFpBPT0=
):

* Healthy mind and body
* Relaxing on a sunny porch
* Smiling wife at my side
* Children chatting with us
* Grandchildren playing in yard
* Friends walking over for a big dinner

Note that most of the items on my list have very little to do
with money or financial wealth. They are things that cannot be
bought, things that are earned through consistent behaviors and
actions.

Beginning with the ideal future end in mind allows you to reverse
engineer what is important to focus on in the present:

* Want a healthy mind and body at 80? Exercise your mind and body
daily.
* Want a smiling wife at your side at 80? Be a loving partner
daily.
* Want kids who choose to be around you? Be a supportive and
caring parent daily.
* Want good friends who you share laughs with? Be a loyal friend
daily.

This simple exercise can have a powerful impact on how you live
your life in the present.

It forces you to begin with the ideal end in mind.

Sit down with a journal or piece of paper and sketch it out:

* What does that ideal future look like?
* What does that imply about how you need to live today?

Write it down and place it somewhere that you can reference itŌĆöa
compass to guide you to the beautiful shores at the end of your
journey.

Remember: Your daily actions, habits, and behaviors shape your
future. Small things become big things.

The Curiosity Counsel
---------------------

I'm at the beginning of my career and I try to keep up with the
productivity suggestions but I can never stick to them. So it
would be my question:
ŌĆŗ
How did you first start sticking to a schedule, to a routine?
What were the very initial steps you took to ensure that you did
that?
ŌĆŗ
- Aditi Katoch

This is a common (and very good) question. We live in a world
where information is abundant but action is scarce. There are
millions of productivity ideas being thrown at you daily, new
systems, frameworks, mental models, etc.

The key is to lower the barrier to action, to never let the
optimal get in the way of the beneficial.

My personal process: When I encounter a new idea, system, or
model, the first thing I do is spend 5 minutes thinking about the
minimum viable version of that idea. The version that has been
shared is usually the most optimal version, but I don't have the
headspace to take that on, so I need to distill it down to the
essence.

Example: If someone shares their deep note-taking method that
allows them to retain 100% of everything they encounter (note:
impossible!), rather than feeling intimidated by it, focus on
distilling it down to the essence. What are the most simple
basics of what the person is suggesting? In my case, this led to
me buying a pocket notebook and pen to use for simple note
taking. I didn't go overboard, but focused on the essence and
built up from there. That will let you get moving and avoid
paralysis from information overload.

I hope this helps you get started, Aditi!

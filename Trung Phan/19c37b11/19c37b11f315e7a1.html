Economics of Super Bowl Ads

 

It's Super Bowl weekend (AKA the Super Bowl for Domino's Pizza Tracker).

 

To be honest, I haven't really followed the NFL this year. Just wasn't the same without Taylor and Travis in contention.

 

I will obviously be watching the game because the Super Bowl is one of the last true monoculture events in North America and the real-time meme-age is insane...especially for the ads. Very much looking forward to the ads. 

 

So far, three Super Bowl ads have caught my eye.

 

First, Anthropic went absolutely scorched earth with a Super Bowl ad campaign trolling ChatGPT running ads. It is four ads that are each a minute-long showing how ad incentives can compromise an AI chatbot ("Ads are coming to AI. But not Claude"). Each ad ends with a creepy smile courtesy of the horror film Pearl. Anthropic gets 80% of its sales from enterprise, so it doesn't have to lean on the consumer as OpenAI is doing. It doesn't cost them much to run the ad. Sam Altman replied that OpenAI is trying to serve a much larger customer base and to do so freely, while Anthropic serves "rich people". We are officially in the "Drake vs. Kendrick" phase of the LLMs. 

Second, we need to talk about Jurassic Park. The most unrealistic part of Steven Spielberg's 1993 classic film — even more unrealistic than scientists using DNA from a mosquito to make dinosaurs — is that the Park's billionaire owner Dr. John Hammond spent $1B on the Park but only $12.99 on IT and security. Thankfully, a new Xfinity Super Bowl ad brings back all of the main actors and rectifies this plothole. 

 

Third, we have an ad campaign from Ro. The $5B+ direct-to-consumer healthcare startup that has been riding the GLP-1/Ozempic wave. Ro will run its first Super Bowl ad starting tennis star Serena Williams. I don't actually have a link to that ad. But I do have a great write-up by Ro CEO Zachariah Reitano on the economics of a Super Bowl ad.

 

Reitano confirms that the Super Bowl is an unparalleled advertising opportunity: 

 

The Super Bowl is not just another media buy. It is a uniquely concentrated moment where attention, scale, and cultural relevance align in a way that doesn’t exist anywhere else in the media landscape. That alone changes the calculus.

 

There is exactly one moment each year when the incentives of advertisers and viewers are perfectly aligned. For a few hours, on a Sunday night in February, more than 100 million people sit down and are excited to watch an ad. People rank Super Bowl ads. They rewatch them. They critique them. They talk about them at work the next day. The Today Show plays them…during the show as content, not as ads! [...]

 

Last but not least, the Super Bowl is the only moment where you can speak to ~100 million people at the same time. In 30 seconds, you can reach an audience that would otherwise take years — this is what it means to compress time.

 

He then runs down the all-in cost of a Super Bowl ad: 

In addition to buying the ad itself, for every 30 seconds slot you purchase, you generally are required to commit to spending the equivalent amount on other programs broadcast by that network. So if you’re purchasing a $7-$10M 30-second Super Bowl ad, you also need to spend another $7-$10M on media on that same broadcast network (e.g., in conjunction with our Super Bowl buy, we’ll also have an advertising presence within the 2026 Winter Olympics on NBC).

    • 30-Second Ad (Media Cost): $7-$10M
    • Production: $1-$4M
    • Talent: $1-$5M
    • Total Superbowl: $9-$19M
    • Additional Media Spend: $7-$10M
    • Total Committed Media + Production Spend: $16-29M

Apart from any immediate customer acquisition, the expected long-term benefit from a Super Bowl ad is brand awareness that can “drive efficiency in future advertising" spend. For companies that drop $100m-$1B (or more) on ads a year, the Super Bowl is a very calculated risk and Reitano frames it as a portfolio decision:

 

When you put all of this together, the risk profile of a Super Bowl ad becomes much clearer. On the downside, the financial risk is meaningfully capped. Even in a scenario where the ad underperforms and only recovers roughly half of its cost, the impact on the overall marketing program is relatively modest. For most large advertisers, this would translate into approximately a 0.5% to 2.5% decrease in annual marketing efficiency, depending on the size of the company’s total marketing budget. In absolute terms, that downside might look like a $5–7 million loss over the course of a year — not insignificant, but also not existential for companies operating at this scale. [...]

 

The upside, however, is far less bounded. If the ad succeeds in improving brand awareness, trust, and understanding in a way that meaningfully increases the efficiency of future marketing spend, those gains can compound quickly. Because the value scales with total marketing budget, even small percentage improvements can translate into tens of millions of dollars in incremental value. In stronger scenarios — where efficiency gains persist and reinforce future campaigns — the upside can reach $50 million, $75 million, or even $100 million or more over time.

 

This asymmetry is why we think about a Super Bowl ad not as a moonshot, but as a portfolio decision. The downside is known and capped. The upside is uncertain but potentially very large. When evaluated this way — and when paired with a product and story that can genuinely resonate at scale — the investment starts to look less like a gamble and more like a calculated bet with a highly favorable risk-reward profile.

 

Give the whole piece a read. It's very good and puts everything in context. Kinda makes sense that Anthropic is spending $25-50m (it'll run a 30-second spot during the game and a 60-second spot pre-game) to body OpenAI. 

 

Also, Dr. John Hammond clearly could have used a similar risk-reward matrix for his Park.

 

SpaceX Acquires xAI 

 

The inevitable merger of all of Elon's companies is moving apace.

 

Last week, Elon's space company (SpaceX) acquired Elon's frontier AI lab (xAI), —which had previously acquired Elon's social media site (X aka Twitter) — in a deal that values the combined entity at $1.25 trillion. 

 

This caps off one of the wildest 3.5-year timelines in recent corporate history:

  • October 2022: Elon acquires Twitter for $44B.
  • March 2023: xAI founded.
  • July 2023: Twitter rebrands to X.
  • May 2024: xAI raises $6B at $24B.
  • July 2024: xAI completes the Memphis Supercluster (100,000 GPUs up in 122 days).
  • December 2024: xAI raises $5B at $50B.
  • March 2025: xAI acquires X in all-stock deal, which values xAI at $80B and X at $33B ($45B less $12B debt) and starts building Colossus 2 (>500,000 GPUs).
  • June 2025: xAI raises $5B at a $136B valuation.
  • January 2026: xAI raises $20B at a $230B valuation.

  • February 2026: SpaceX acquires xAI in a stock swap (investors in xAI will get 0.1433 shares of SpaceX). The deal values SpaceX at $1T and xAI at $250B.

  • June 2026: SpaceX IPO at $1.5T (?????).

When Elon took Twitter private at $44B, it was a pretty clear overpay. Twitter's ad business really never had a chance against the Meta/Google/Amazon digital ad money machines and the deal was done near the COVID market top. Fidelity marked it down to $10-15B within a year.

 

But then Elon financial engineered a win by riding the AI wave and Twitter shareholders own SpaceX now (meanwhile, Twitter sh*tposters that are part of the creator revenue program also now technically work for SpaceX...ipso facto, I work at SpaceX). 

 

It's the same AI wave is the rationale for the deal per a SpaceX blog.

 

Current advances in AI are dependent on large terrestrial data centers, which require immense amounts of power and cooling. Global electricity demand for AI simply cannot be met with terrestrial solutions, even in the near term, without imposing hardship on communities and the environment.

 

In the long term, space-based AI is obviously the only way to scale. To harness even a millionth of our Sun’s energy would require over a million times more energy than our civilization currently uses!

 

The only logical solution therefore is to transport these resource-intensive efforts to a location with vast power and space. I mean, space is called “space” for a reason. 😂

 

So, the vision is for space data centres. Maybe up to 1 million satellites, which is 100x the number that are already up there. There's a long way from here to there and Elon has a track record of overly ambitious timelines. 

 

But the timing of everything makes a lot of sense when you consider that Anthropic and OpenAI are in talks to go public in 2026. 

 

Retails would have shat its pants to get a taste of SpaceX on its own. A $1T valuation on $16B of revenue is expensive, except SpaceX is a monopoly provider of launching skyscrapers into space and landing them on drone barges in the middle of the ocean. Starlink is most of that revenue and has a lot more room to run...with long-term goals of funding Mars. 

 

Now, add in an "AI data centre chip space laser beams satellite" story and SpaceX is going to raise an insane amount of money at its IPO, before OpenAI and Anthropic can get there. 

 

All of these AI labs need fundraising. Especially with Google in its Empire Strikes Back phase, jamming Gemini down every crevice and Workspace screen it can find, while being funded by the $400B search ad machine.

 

OpenAI ($20B revenue, $800B valuation) and Anthropic ($10B, $350B) have found much more traction than xAI ($250-300m), which is burning $1B a month. Well, they are all burning money but OpenAI and Anthropic have clearer roads to profitability. 

 

Matt Levine writes:

 

The combination here is not so much “use SpaceX’s revenue to fund xAI’s capital needs” as it is “use SpaceX’s fundraising ability to fund xAI’s capital needs.” There are a lot of money-hungry AI companies these days, but only one money-hungry rocket company that also does AI. 

 

A very relevant example is Tesla buying Solar City in 2016, which many felt was Elon's car manufacturer bailing out Elon's founding investment in a solar energy firm ran by his cousins.

 

It's a risky play but Elon and I'm sure some SpaceX shareholders would have preferred only a focus on SpaceX. But SpaceX and xAI are both private and majority controlled by Elon (he is largest owner with 42% stake, followed by 10% for Fidelity, 10% for Founders Fund and 7% from Google)...and the $1.25 trillion valuation set mostly by his team. 

 

Eric Berger says of the bet: 

 

Musk’s plan for the merged companies is predicated on several assumptions, including that AI is not a bubble, but rather a technology that will be fully embraced in the future; that orbital data centers are cost-competitive compared to ground-based data centers; and that compute is the essential roadblock that must be solved for widespread adoption of AI by society.

 

If these assumptions are true, the merged SpaceX-xAI company holds a powerful position. It could potentially own a full stack of capabilities, from launch to orbital bandwidth to frontier AI models, and with Starlink Internet, it could provide AI on demand, anywhere in the world, to any mobile device.

 

The eventual merger with publicly-listed Tesla will be much more complicated. But Elon was on the shot clock with OpenAI and Anthropic...a the potential prize is so big, he clearly thinks it's time to go all in with SpaceX and xAI is trying to change the convo to "will space data centres be cheaper than terrestrial data centrers in 2-3 years?"

The DeepMind Documentary

 

Google acquired AI lab DeepMind in 2014 for $400m (this was a year before Google and Fidelity invested $1B in SpaceX at a $10B valuation, insane run).

 

The Big Tech firm recently released a documentary (The Thinking Game) about DeepMind and its co-founder Demis Hassabis. It currently has 380m** views on YouTube and I can't recommend it enough. 

 

There's a massive spoonful of corporate PR but the project is very well done.

 

Ben Cohen from the WSJ tells the interesting backstory on the project.

 

A former NFL Films director (Greg Kohs) had done some commercials for Google and they asked him to make a documentary for the famous 2016 AlphaGo match with Lee Sedol.

After that project, Kohs realized he should just do a documentary on DeepMind (Google would own rights but he had full editorial final cut).

He spent a ton of time with Demis and Co from 2018-2024, and that’s how he behind-the-scene footage...including this one truly incredible scene when Demis realizes the potential of AlphaFold, the project that would later land him a Nobel Prize. 

 

The team is sitting in a meeting. Demis is on his phone and someone says that AlphaFold can “easily” predict all known (1-2B) protein sequences “in a month”.

He looks up from his phone and says “Why don’t we just do that? That’s a great idea. We should just run every protein in existence and then release that.” 

**Google is probably artificially boosting the view numbers on this video...but, for real, IT'S WORTH THE WATCH.

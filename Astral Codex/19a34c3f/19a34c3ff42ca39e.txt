Everyone who studied biochem has asked themselves at some point “Why do cells need so many second messengers?” - proteins whose only point is to activate other proteins, and so on in a chain, until the last protein in the chain makes something happen. If I understand this paper [ https://substack.com/redirect/30157a38-9583-4eef-98e2-bc66422cfbdd?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] right, it’s claiming that if each protein has enough positive and negative inputs, this is analogous to a neural network, capable of making primitive “decisions” about cellular behavior. I asked some real biologists, who were not nearly as impressed with this thesis as I was and said that the analogy between levels of a messenger and the activation function of a neuron was too weak to hold so much weight. I still wonder whether insights from mechanistic interpretability could help us understand networks like these.

27: Also Fatima-related: in the comments highlights post, I linked FWLAB’s criticism of David Hume’s argument against ever believing miracles. [ https://substack.com/redirect/4adb449d-7829-4aeb-8cd3-02ce600c0b2a?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] Joe James argues that FWLAB, myself, and other critics are misunderstanding Hume’s argument [ https://substack.com/redirect/0b3e1ac9-6bd0-4f16-8dd5-ef8fe0e0623a?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ]. FWLAB says no he isn’t [ https://substack.com/redirect/9d7a8556-cb9c-40d0-a1ac-1cc0b4cc1e94?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ]. They continue the “discussion” in the comments [ https://substack.com/redirect/9d7a8556-cb9c-40d0-a1ac-1cc0b4cc1e94?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ], but both sides kind of embarrass themselves and don’t get anywhere. I am unfortunately still confused - there are many cases where something that never happened before happens for the first time. For example, nobody had ever seen a grizzly-polar bear hybrid until recently, so “the universal testimony of mankind” was that this didn’t happen. But when a reliable person did see it, we had little trouble imagining that we were wrong and it was simply very rare, or a new thing happening now because of climate change. If nobody has ever seen a sea part before, but then many people say they saw Moses part the Red Sea, what is different about this such that “the universal testimony of mankind” suddenly becomes a disqualifier? Hume seems to be trying to make this same distinction in his eight days of darkness example [ https://substack.com/redirect/c6aa86c3-2775-467d-b9f0-a73208381bbf?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ], but there it seems like he is only saying he will accept non-religious anomalies, but rule out religious ones, because religious people often lie. But then what happened to the “universal testimony of mankind” argument? I kind of get the impression that he’s groping towards Bayes’ Theorem, but hard-coding in a belief that the prior probability of lots of religious people lying is higher than the probability of a miracle. If that’s his belief, then fair enough, but I guess I expected the much-vaunted Hume’s Argument Against Miracles to be something more than this.

36: Wang, Visscher, et al [ https://substack.com/redirect/de0eb71b-6906-41e8-bae6-5e2f716fbb2b?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] is a step up in studying the genetics of racial differences. It looks at a sample of Mexican families of mixed white-native heritage. By coincidence, some of their children will inherit more genes from the white side, and others more genes from the native side. These children will have identical social situations (since they’re from the same families) but different proportional ancestry, so we should expect any racial differences among them to come from the genetic rather than the social aspect of race (except that we can’t rule out “colorism”, ie genes making people look different and then causing discrimination). The paper finds that racial genetic differences directly affect height, diabetes risk, and other medical traits, but not educational attainment. Twitter discussion here [ https://substack.com/redirect/acea7dbc-97c8-490e-82e1-6738d3f50124?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ].

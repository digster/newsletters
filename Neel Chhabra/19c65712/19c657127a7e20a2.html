Meesho: The Discovery Engine for India's Missing Middle

How Meesho accidentally discovered that India's informal economy wasn't a bug to fix but a feature to formalize and what it reveals about asymmetric competition in fragmented markets

Neel Chhabra
Feb 16
 
READ IN APP
 

There’s a particular kind of investment insight that’s hard to monetize in real-time but becomes obvious in retrospect: the recognition that what looks like market fragmentation is actually market structure, and what looks like inefficiency is actually optimization for different constraints. The gap between “this is broken” and “this is how it actually works” is where asymmetric returns live.

Meesho is a $7 billion case study in that gap.

The Illegibility Premium

Here’s the setup: In 2015, Indian e-commerce was a two-player game. Flipkart and Amazon were burning billions of dollars fighting over 50 million affluent urban consumers who wanted branded products delivered fast. The entire narrative infrastructure—VCs, tech media, analyst reports, was focused on this market because it was legible. It looked like e-commerce markets everywhere else. You could model it. You could benchmark it against China or the US. You could build DCFs that venture partners would nod at.

Meanwhile, 85% of Indian retail was happening in a completely different fitness landscape: unbranded products, sold through layers of wholesalers and traders, purchased by consumers who’d never used an e-commerce app, in cities that nobody writing tech analysis had ever visited.

The illegibility wasn’t incidental. It was structural. And structure creates moats.

When Vidit Aatrey and Sanjeev Barnwal started watching how retail actually worked in India, not how the PowerPoints said it worked, but how actual transactions happened… they noticed something the consensus had priced at zero: women in Gujarat and Rajasthan were running sophisticated commerce operations through WhatsApp groups. They’d curate product catalogs, share photos with their social networks, take orders, and collect cash on delivery. No app. No brand partnerships. No logistics infrastructure. Just trust, deployed at scale through personal networks.

This is the critical insight: these weren’t primitive versions of “real” e-commerce waiting to graduate to the Amazon model. They were optimized solutions to a different problem with different constraints. The argument wasn’t “how do we bring these people up to the standard of metro consumers?” The argument was “what constraints are they solving for that we’re not seeing?”

The Dimensional Problem

Let me frame this through a concept from topology: when you’re optimizing in different dimensions, you’re not competing, you’re operating in orthogonal spaces.

Amazon India was optimizing on these dimensions:

  • Speed (1-2 day delivery)

  • Selection depth in branded categories

  • Trust in platform (reviews, guarantees, easy returns)

  • Search efficiency (I know what I want, help me find it)

The WhatsApp boutique economy was optimizing on completely different dimensions:

  • Price (absolute rock-bottom)

  • Social proof through personal relationships

  • Discovery without intent (I don’t know what I want until I see it)

  • Trust in people, not institutions

  • Willingness to wait if the price is right

These aren’t points on the same spectrum. They’re different axes entirely. And when you’re operating in orthogonal dimensions, the competitor’s moat doesn’t protect them… it limits them.

Amazon’s sophisticated logistics network, optimized for fast delivery of branded products to metro pincodes, is a liability when trying to profitably ship a ₹200 unbranded kurta to Jaunpur with a customer who’s happy to wait 20 days. The infrastructure that creates dominance in one dimensional space creates blindness in another.

This is the innovator’s dilemma in its purest form: not “they couldn’t see the threat” but “the threat was invisible in the dimensions they were measuring.”

The Illegibility Moat

Here’s what makes this particularly interesting from a competitive dynamics perspective: Meesho’s market wasn’t hidden because it was small or hard to access. It was hidden because it was unmeasurable by the tools the incumbents used.

Think about how e-commerce companies assess markets:

  • Total Addressable Market (branded product sales)

  • Serviceable Addressable Market (areas with logistics coverage)

  • Average Order Value (high enough to support unit economics)

  • Customer Acquisition Cost (below certain thresholds)

Every one of these metrics would tell you Meesho’s market didn’t exist. Unbranded products don’t show up in brand tracking studies. Tier 2/3 city consumers don’t respond to performance marketing. ₹265 average order values look like a death sentence for logistics economics. And women running WhatsApp boutiques don’t show up in any employment or entrepreneurship database.

The market was invisible not because the data didn’t exist but because it existed in a form that existing analytical frameworks couldn’t capture. This is what I call the illegibility premium: the return to seeing what’s already there but unmeasured.

Essentially, the illegibility was self-reinforcing. Because the incumbents couldn’t measure it, they didn’t build for it. Because they didn’t build for it, it remained informal. Because it remained informal, it stayed illegible. The loop created a permanent moat around a massive market that was hiding in plain sight.

The Architecture of Intermediation

Let’s talk about what Meesho actually discovered when they started serving the WhatsApp boutique economy. The standard narrative is “they cut out middlemen and created efficiency.” The reality is more interesting: they discovered that the middlemen… the women running these boutiques, weren’t extracting rents. They were providing value that the platform couldn’t.

In low-trust economies, institutions are weak and personal relationships are strong. This isn’t a development stage that markets grow out of. It’s an equilibrium—stable, self-reinforcing, and optimized for the actual constraints people face.

When a woman in Surat shares a product in her WhatsApp group, she’s not just distributing information. She’s staking her reputation. Her neighbors know where she lives. They see her at the temple. They went to school with her kids. The transaction isn’t happening in an anonymous marketplace, it’s happening in a dense social network with long memory and real consequences for defection.

That’s a trust infrastructure. And trust infrastructure, once established, is very hard to displace.

Meesho’s insight was that they couldn’t replace this infrastructure, they could only augment it. The reseller model didn’t eliminate intermediation; it made intermediation more efficient by solving the constraints that limited it: unreliable supply, payment friction, logistics gaps.

This is a profound observation about platform strategy in fragmented markets: sometimes the winning move isn’t to consolidate the network… it’s to make the fragmented network work better.

The Four Pivots and the Rugged Landscape

I want to zoom in on Meesho’s pivot history because it’s a masterclass in navigating uncertainty without compass bearings.

Pivot 1: Fashnear (hyperlocal fashion discovery) failed because it tried to average two value propositions—the selection of online and the immediacy of offline—and ended up in the valley between them. This is a common failure mode: when you can’t decide between two strategies, you split the difference and end up with neither strategy’s strengths.

Pivot 2: Digital storefronts failed for a subtler reason: the value proposition was “convenience” rather than “revenue growth.” In developed markets, merchants pay for convenience because their opportunity cost of time is high. In India’s long-tail economy, merchants have low opportunity costs and high price sensitivity. They’ll only pay for tools that directly increase sales. Convenience is a luxury good.

Pivot 3: The reseller marketplace succeeded because they followed the anomaly. When they noticed their users weren’t the SMB owners they’d built for but women in WhatsApp groups, they had three choices:

  1. Ignore the anomalous users (most common response)

  2. Try to force them into the original product vision (second most common)

  3. Rebuild the product around what the anomalous users were actually doing (rare)

They did the third thing. This is harder than it sounds because it requires admitting your original theory was wrong. But in complex adaptive systems, the surprising signals are the most important ones. They’re telling you where the actual fitness landscape is versus where you thought it was.

Pivot 4: Direct-to-consumer marketplace preserved the attributes that made resellers successful (discovery-led browsing, ultra-low prices, social trust) while removing their constraint (network size caps at ~100 people). This is elegant: keep the mechanism, remove the bottleneck.

Four pivots, same mission, each one getting closer to the true peak. This is what disciplined search under uncertainty looks like: you’re not random-walking, you’re following gradients. But you have to be willing to follow gradients even when they point away from your original hypothesis.

The Economics of Orchestration

Let’s talk about Valmo, Meesho’s logistics platform, because it reveals something fundamental about where value accrues in fragmented markets.

The standard playbook for e-commerce logistics in emerging markets is: build owned infrastructure. Warehouses, fulfillment centers, delivery fleets. This is what Amazon did in the US, what Alibaba did in China, what Flipkart tried to do in India. The theory is vertical integration creates control, which creates reliability, which creates competitive advantage.

Meesho went the opposite direction: zero-asset orchestration.

Valmo doesn’t own trucks or warehouses. It coordinates thousands of independent logistics operators—two-wheeler delivery pilots earning ₹15,000-30,000 per month, hub operators running small sorting centers, linehaul partners moving goods between cities. It’s a conductor for a massively distributed orchestra.

The question is: why does this work? In theory, fragmented logistics should be more expensive and less reliable than integrated logistics. But in practice, at ₹265 average order values, integrated logistics doesn’t work at all… the physics of moving cheap parcels across long distances makes it unprofitable.

Here’s the key: Valmo isn’t competing on the same cost curve as traditional 3PL providers. Traditional providers have fixed costs (warehouses, fleets) that need to be amortized across high-volume, high-value routes. Valmo’s partners have variable costs and can profitably serve low-volume, low-value routes because their alternative isn’t “operate at scale or shut down”—it’s “earn ₹15,000 per month or earn ₹0.”

This is a different optimization problem. And different optimization problems have different solution sets.

The orchestration model creates two compounding advantages:

  1. Density economics: As order volume increases, Valmo can fill trucks more efficiently, optimize routes better, and reduce per-unit costs. But unlike owned infrastructure, the capital to expand comes from the entrepreneurs joining the network, not from Meesho’s balance sheet.

  2. Coverage economics: Valmo reaches 15,000 pincodes not because Meesho built 15,000 delivery stations but because they enabled 15,000 local entrepreneurs to build profitable micro-businesses. The expansion is self-funded by the people doing the expanding.

This is the opposite of Uber’s model. Uber needed to own the demand side (riders) to force suppliers (drivers) to accept low unit economics. Meesho needed to make the supply side (logistics entrepreneurs) profitable enough that they’d voluntarily expand into marginal areas. Different constraint = different architecture.

The Browse-First Interface and the Attention Arbitrage

Now let’s talk about why Meesho’s product architecture is fundamentally different from Amazon’s, and why that difference compounds into defensibility.

Amazon is built on intent-based search:

User has need → formulates query → sees results → evaluates → purchases

This optimizes for:

  • Matching efficiency (show right product fast)

  • Functional fulfillment (solve immediate need)

  • Transaction completion (minimize friction between intent and purchase)

Meesho is built on serendipity-based discovery:

User opens app → scrolls feed → sees interesting products → impulse purchase

This optimizes for:

  • Engagement time (keep user browsing)

  • Discovery quality (show things they didn’t know they wanted)

  • Habit formation (make opening the app a daily behavior)

This sounds like a small UX difference. It’s actually a fundamental difference in the economic model.

Search-first economics:

Value creation: Match efficiency (intent → product)
Engagement: Low (come with purpose, leave when fulfilled)
Frequency: Needs-based (shop when you run out)
Monetization: Sponsored search (pay for top placement)
Data moat: Query-to-product matching

Browse-first economics:

Value creation: Curation quality (show interesting things)
Engagement: High (scroll like Instagram, not like Google)
Frequency: Habitual (open app daily, even without need)
Monetization: Feed placement + higher order frequency
Data moat: Behavioral preference learning

The critical difference: Browse-first platforms compete for attention-time. Search-first platforms compete for intent-fulfillment.

And attention-time has radically different economics:

Meesho’s 9.78 average annual purchase frequency means users open the app ~40-50 times per year. That’s not intent-driven. That’s habitual browsing behavior, the same psychological pattern as Instagram or TikTok.

This creates different moat mechanics:

Search moat depth:

  • Knowing what products match which queries (trainable from search logs)

  • Catalog breadth in popular categories (addressable through capital)

  • UI optimization for transaction completion (copyable)

Browse moat depth:

  • Knowing individual user preferences from behavioral signals (requires massive transaction volume)

  • Understanding what triggers impulse purchases (requires engagement data at scale)

  • Optimizing for discovery delight not transaction efficiency (different objective function)

  • Habit formation and muscle memory (psychological switching costs)

The browse moat is dimensionally orthogonal to the search moat. You can’t build browse-first on search-first infrastructure because:

Catalog organization:

  • Search: Hierarchical categories, brand/specification filters

  • Browse: Affinity clusters, trending patterns, creator associations

Algorithm optimization:

  • Search: Query → product matching accuracy

  • Browse: User → unexpected product affinity prediction

Data requirements:

  • Search: Query logs, click-through rates, conversion by category

  • Browse: Scroll patterns, dwell time, engagement signals, impulse trigger patterns

Product architecture:

  • Search: Minimize friction from query to purchase

  • Browse: Maximize engagement, discovery delight, session time

These are different systems solving different optimization problems. You cannot bolt a browse feed onto a search platform and expect it to work… the underlying data, algorithms, and product philosophy are incompatible.

This is why Amazon’s “Inspire” (TikTok clone inside Amazon) has failed to gain traction. It’s not a feature deficit… it’s an architectural impossibility given how the rest of the platform is optimized.

The Creator Flywheel and the Zero-CAC Distribution Engine

Let me articulate one of Meesho’s most underappreciated strategic weapons: they’ve built a creator economy that generates negative customer acquisition cost.

Traditional e-commerce CAC model:

Spend on ads (Google/Facebook/Instagram) → User clicks → Maybe converts
CAC = Marketing spend / Acquired customers
Typical CAC: ₹300-800
Required LTV/CAC ratio: 3x-5x for unit economics
Challenge: CAC increases with scale (exhaust best channels first)

Meesho’s creator model:

Enable creators to share product catalogs → Creator earns commission on sales → User discovers through trusted source → Higher conversion
CAC = ₹0 (creators do marketing, platform pays commission only on conversion)
Effective CAC = Negative (commission < saved marketing spend, plus creators generate organic traffic)

The economics are inverted:

Paid acquisition:

  • Pay upfront (before knowing conversion)

  • Generic targeting (demographics/interests)

  • No social proof

  • CAC scales with competition (auction dynamics)

  • Rented attention (ad expires)

Creator acquisition:

  • Pay on performance (commission on actual sales)

  • Precise targeting (creator’s audience matches products)

  • Built-in endorsement (creator social proof)

  • CAC declines with scale (more creators = more competition = lower commissions)

  • Owned attention (creator relationship persists)

This creates a flywheel that inverts traditional CAC dynamics:

More products → More creator earning opportunities → More creators join
↓
More creators → More content created → More organic traffic → More users
↓  
More users → More sales → More creator earnings → Attracts more creators
↓
More creators → Competition for audiences → Better content quality → More engagement

Traditional e-commerce: CAC ↑ with scale (exhaust channels) Meesho: CAC ↓ with scale (creator competition drives efficiency)

And there’s a second-order effect: Creators optimize for engagement, not just conversion.

Performance marketing ad: Optimized purely for clicks/conversions (boring, transactional)

Creator content: Optimized for entertainment/education with commerce embedded (engaging, shareable)

This means Meesho gets:

  • Content value (entertainment attracts users independent of purchase intent)

  • Social proof (creator endorsement signals quality/desirability)

  • Sustained engagement (users follow creators, return regularly)

  • Organic virality (good content gets shared beyond creator’s immediate audience)

The creator doesn’t just drive a transaction, they drive sustained relationship with the platform.

This is the difference between rented attention (expires when you stop paying) and owned attention (compounds through creator relationships).

The Margin Structure and Terminal Economics

Now let’s talk about the thing that keeps me up at night when I think about Meesho: the margin structure.

Q3 FY26 numbers:

  • Contribution margin: 2.3% of NMV (down from 4.3% a year earlier)

  • Adjusted EBITDA: -4.2% of NMV

  • FCF: ₹56 crores (down from ₹581 crores in Q2)

This is not a high-margin business. And the margin compression from 4.3% to 2.3% happened because of a logistics shock… some 3PL consolidation forced rapid Valmo expansion, and the transition costs hit margins hard.

The bull case says this is temporary. The bear case says it’s structural.

Here’s my read: it’s both.

The logistics costs are probably temporary… once Valmo reaches higher density in newly expanded regions, costs should normalize. But the low absolute margin level is permanent. This is a structural consequence of the business model.

Think about what happens at ₹265 AOV:

  • Logistics costs eat ~₹80-90 (30-35% of order value)

  • Payment processing, fraud, returns eat another ~₹15-20

  • Customer acquisition and retention eat ~₹20-30

  • Platform costs (tech, ops, customer service) eat ~₹10-15

You’re left with maybe ₹150-175 of contribution margin on ₹265 of GMV—call it 55-65%. And out of that, Meesho takes zero commission from sellers. Their revenue comes from:

  • Advertising (sellers paying for visibility)

  • Logistics fees (charging sellers more than what delivery costs Meesho)

  • Eventual financial services revenue

If you model steady-state margins of 6-7% EBITDA on NMV… which is what management guides to, you get a business that generates ~₹2,000-2,500 crores of annual EBITDA at current run rates (₹40,000 crores annual NMV).

At a 25x EBITDA multiple (generous for a low-margin marketplace), that’s ₹50,000-60,000 crores in enterprise value, call it $5-6 billion. Which is... roughly where it’s trading (₹70,000 crores).

So the market has priced in the current state but not much growth. The bull case requires believing one of three things:

  1. NMV grows much faster than consensus (the TAM is bigger than modeled)

  2. Margins expand significantly (adjacencies like financial services scale)

  3. Both

I think (1) is highly probable and (3) is possible. Here’s why.

The Formalization Premium

The thing the margin analysis misses is that Meesho isn’t taking share from existing e-commerce. They’re formalizing informal transactions that were previously uncounted.

When a woman in Surat sells a saree to her neighbor through WhatsApp, that transaction exists. It creates economic value. But it doesn’t appear in any e-commerce statistics. It’s invisible to market sizing models.

Meesho’s shareholder letter makes this point explicitly: “Not to capture a larger share of existing e-commerce, but to expand what e-commerce can be in India.”

This is the formalization premium: the return to making invisible economic activity visible and measurable.

The pattern repeats across emerging markets:

  • M-Pesa in Kenya didn’t invent mobile money, it formalized airtime transfers as currency

  • Grab in Southeast Asia didn’t invent motorcycle taxis, it formalized the ojek system

  • Mercado Libre in Latin America didn’t invent informal commerce, it formalized it

In each case, the TAM wasn’t visible in any market research because the transactions weren’t being measured. And in each case, the company that formalized the informal economy became extraordinarily valuable.

The question with Meesho is: how much of India’s ₹65 lakh crore retail market is actually informal transactions that could be formalized? Nobody knows. The transactions are, by definition, uncounted.

But here are some data points:

  • 800 million Indians have smartphones

  • E-commerce penetration in India is ~7-8% of retail

  • China’s e-commerce penetration is ~35%

  • Even adjusting for India’s lower per-capita income, the gap is enormous

The directional arrow is very clear. The timing and slope are uncertain. But the direction is not.

The Competitive Moat Synthesis

Let me synthesize what Meesho’s actual moat is, because I think the narrative—”network effects in a two-sided marketplace”—undersells it.

Meesho’s moat is the intersection of six compounding advantages:

1. Demand-side data moat: 1.95 billion orders in 9 months creates a recommendation engine that gets better with every transaction. At ₹265 AOV, these are frequent, habitual purchases—9.78 annual purchase frequency. The data compounds faster than competitors who serve less frequent, higher-value purchases.

2. Supply-side catalog moat: 846,000 sellers listing unbranded products that have no home on other platforms. These sellers can’t list on Amazon (their products don’t meet brand requirements) and can’t afford Flipkart’s fees. Meesho is the only platform built for them.

3. Logistics density moat: Valmo’s 15,000 pincode coverage creates local density that reduces costs. The more orders in a region, the cheaper each delivery becomes. Competitors can’t match this without either building owned infrastructure (capital intensive) or replicating Valmo’s network (requires existing volume to attract logistics partners—chicken-and-egg).

4. Discovery algorithm moat: The browse-first UX creates engagement patterns that generate data for better curation, which improves discovery, which increases engagement—a classical flywheel. But this only works at scale. Below critical mass, curation feels random.

5. Trust translation moat: This is subtle but important. Meesho successfully translated social trust (the WhatsApp boutique model) into platform trust (direct B2C marketplace) by preserving the attributes that made social commerce work: discovery-led browsing, ultra-low prices, content creator recommendations. Competitors trying to enter this market have to solve the trust problem from scratch.

6. Cost structure moat: Operating profitably at ₹265 AOV requires a completely different cost structure than serving ₹1,500 AOV. This isn’t just about efficiency… it’s about what costs you can and cannot have. You cannot have owned warehouses. You cannot have fast delivery. You cannot have extensive returns processing. The constraints force architectural choices that become defensive.

These moats don’t exist independently… they reinforce each other. More demand creates better data, which improves discovery, which attracts more demand. More demand increases logistics density, which reduces costs, which enables lower prices, which attracts more demand.

This is what physicists call a positive feedback loop and what investors call a flywheel. The technical property of such systems is increasing returns to scale: the gap between the incumbent and any challenger widens with each cycle.

The Risk Surface

Pre-mortems matter. Let me articulate the failure modes I’d watch:

Risk 1: The Valmo brittleness. The Q3 margin compression revealed that when logistics shocks happen, Meesho absorbs them while vertically integrated competitors don’t. If logistics disruptions become frequent… driver strikes, fuel price spikes, regulatory changes—the “asset-light” narrative becomes “asset-fragile.” This is a real risk in India where regulatory unpredictability is high.

Risk 2: The COD trap. 72% cash-on-delivery is a massive friction tax. Every percentage point shift to prepaid improves economics, but behavior change is slow. If COD stays structurally high, margins stay structurally low. The counterfactual is that digital payments infrastructure continues improving (UPI penetration, credit access) and gradually shifts the mix. But the pace matters.

Risk 3: Adjacency execution. The real money in value-commerce platforms is financial services—lending, insurance, wealth products. PDD proved this in China. But executing financial services requires completely different capabilities than running a marketplace, and regulatory complexity in India is high. Meesho’s H2 experiments are early and small. This is a “show me” story.

Risk 4: Platform competition. Instagram, YouTube, and WhatsApp are all experimenting with native commerce. If short-video platforms integrate shopping seamlessly, Meesho’s discovery moat could erode. The battle for browsing sessions is the battle for discovery-led commerce. Meesho currently wins this by having better product density and better curation algorithms. But the platforms have distribution and engagement advantages.

Risk 5: Margin ceiling. If steady-state EBITDA margins are really 6-7%, and if adjacencies don’t scale, this is a low-margin infrastructure business… valuable but not explosively so. The multiple expands if margins surprise to the upside. It compresses if they don’t.

These are knowable risks. The scarier risks are the unknown ones—tail events, regulatory discontinuities, technological disruptions we haven’t imagined. The defense against unknown risks is having founders who are paranoid, adaptive, and intellectually honest. Meesho’s four-pivot history suggests they’re all three.

The Pattern Recognition Game

Let me zoom out to the meta-level: what does Meesho teach us about finding asymmetric opportunities in emerging markets where market structure looks similar?

The pattern is this:

  1. Look for illegibility gaps. Markets where the consensus analytical frameworks can’t measure what’s actually happening. These gaps create mispricing because if you can’t measure it, you can’t value it.

  2. Follow the anomalous users. When your product is being used in unexpected ways by unexpected people, that’s signal. The temptation is to ignore anomalies or force them into your model. The correct move is to rebuild the model around the anomaly.

  3. Embrace orthogonal dimensions. When you’re optimizing on completely different axes than incumbents, their moats don’t protect them, they constrain them. Find the dimensional space where the incumbent’s strengths become liabilities.

  4. Make fragmentation work. In low-trust, intermediary-dependent economies, the winning move often isn’t consolidation, it’s orchestration. Make the fragmented network more efficient rather than trying to replace it with centralized infrastructure.

  5. Formalize informal value. The biggest TAMs are often invisible because they’re informal. The formalization premium accrues to whoever builds the infrastructure that makes informal transactions legible and scalable.

This is a repeatable pattern. You saw it with M-Pesa, Grab, Mercado Libre. You’re seeing it with Meesho. You’ll see it again in other markets where informal economies are large and institutional infrastructure is weak.

The returns come from seeing the present rather than predicting the future. The WhatsApp boutiques were already there. The 800 million smartphone users buying offline were already there. The unbranded retail economy was already there.

Meesho just noticed.

Coda: The Geometry of Value

I grew up believing that markets were efficient and that asymmetric opportunities were rare. Both beliefs were wrong.

Markets are efficient at pricing legible information. They’re catastrophically inefficient at pricing illegible information. And illegibility isn’t random, it’s structural. It arises when the analytical tools the consensus uses can’t measure what’s actually happening on the ground.

Meesho existed in that gap—between what was happening (millions of women running commerce operations through WhatsApp) and what the consensus could see (branded e-commerce for affluent metros).

The geometry of value isn’t about predicting the future. It’s about measuring the present more accurately than consensus. And the returns to better measurement compound viciously because capital flows to what can be measured.

Meesho is now a $7 billion public company. The market has begun pricing in what was always there. The illegibility premium has partially closed.

But only partially.

The real question… the one that determines whether this is a $5 billion company or a $50 billion company, is how much of India’s informal economy can be formalized, how fast it happens, and how much value Meesho captures along the way.

Nobody knows the answer. The transactions are still unmeasured. The TAM is still illegible.

Which means the gap is still open.

And gaps are where returns live.

Thank you for reading, see you soon!

There’s a peculiar blindness that afflicts analysts looking at businesses. They see revenue models, competitive advantages, and market opportunities. They build DCF models with hockey-stick projections. They map out TAMs and SAMs. But they miss something fundamental: a business isn’t a line pointing upward, it’s a circle that keeps going around.
“A business is a collection of repeatable processes; but it’s worth going the next step to specify: if a process is truly repeatable, then it must be a loop, because you have to end at the same place you started if you want to do it again.”
This isn’t just semantic precision. It’s the difference between understanding a business and fooling yourself.
Most business analysis is unconsciously linear. We talk about “growth strategies” and “scaling operations” as if companies are arrows launched into the future. The entire vocabulary of business—”pipeline,” “funnel,” “trajectory”—betrays this mental model.
But linearity is the enemy of repeatability. A line, by definition, never returns to its starting point. If your business model is actually linear, you’re not building a business… you’re executing a series of one-off transactions that happen to share a brand name.
There’s a conceptual error that costs investors more money than any spreadsheet mistake ever could: confusing growth with compounding.
Growth is what happens when you sell more stuff. Compounding is what happens when selling stuff makes it easier to sell more stuff. The difference sounds trivial. It isn’t. The businesses that compound value over decades have figured out something more fundamental: they’ve identified a loop that ends where it begins, ready to run again.
Consider Amazon’s flywheel, which Jeff Bezos famously sketched on a napkin. Lower prices lead to more customers, which attracts more third-party sellers, which expands selection, which improves customer experience, which leads to more customers, which enables lower prices. Notice what’s happening here: the loop doesn’t point somewhere. It circles back on itself, each rotation making the next one easier.
This is why Bezos could be so patient with profitability. He wasn’t building a linear path to earnings. He was building a machine that would spin faster with each rotation, where “faster” meant the same sequence happening with greater intensity and at lower marginal cost.
Here’s the pattern: Growth is additive. Compounding is multiplicative. Growth means you did it again. Compounding means doing it again made doing it the next time different—specifically, easier, cheaper, or more valuable.
Eric Beinhocker’s definition of economic value creation—irreversibility (All value-creating economic transformations and transactions are thermodynamically irreversible), local entropy reduction (All value-creating economic transformations and transactions reduce entropy locally within the economic system, while increasing entropy globally) and fitness for human purposes (All value-creating economic transformations and transactions produce artifacts and/or actions that are fit for human purposes)—becomes more interesting when you think about loops. Value is created through an irreversible process that gives a resource’s ‘order’ greater usefulness to other humans. Value isn’t created by doing something once; it’s created by doing something in a way that makes doing it again easier.
Let’s get precise about what we mean by compounding, because the term gets thrown around so loosely it’s become almost meaningless.
Compounding, in a business context, means: A system where outputs become inputs in a way that systematically improves the efficiency or effectiveness of future iterations.
This is where most “competitive advantages” fail the loop test. A patent is linear, it runs out. A brand is potentially circular, each satisfied customer marginally strengthens the signal for the next one. A talented founder is linea, they have finite attention. A strong culture is potentially circular, it attracts people who reinforce it.
The businesses with durable competitive advantages aren’t the ones with the strongest single barrier. They’re the ones where the barrier strengthens itself through the loop. Brian Arthur’s increasing returns don’t happen in straight lines; they happen in reinforcing cycles.
Network effects are the most obvious example. Each user makes the product more valuable to the next user, which attracts more users, which makes the product more valuable. But here’s what’s subtle: the loop only works if you end up back at the customer acquisition decision point. If your network effect makes the product better but doesn’t reduce the cost or friction of acquiring the next customer, you have an asset, not a loop.
The absence of clear loops explains more business failures than most people realize.
Take most media companies. They create content, distribute it, monetize attention, and then... create more content. Where’s the loop? Creating great content doesn’t make creating the next piece of content easier, often it makes it harder, because you’ve raised expectations. The process is repeatable in the sense that you can do it again, but it doesn’t return to the same starting point with accumulated advantages.
Compare that to Netflix’s original content strategy. Each show generates viewing data. That data improves their recommendation algorithm. Better recommendations increase engagement. Higher engagement justifies paying more for content. Better content creates more viewing data. Now you have a loop—each rotation makes the next one more efficient.
Or consider most enterprise software companies. They sell to customers, implement the product, provide support, and then... hope for renewal. The loop only closes if implementation and support systematically reduce future implementation costs or increase future renewal rates. If every customer is still roughly as expensive to serve in year five as they were in year one, you’re not in a loop, you’re climbing a linear treadmill.
The SaaS companies that actually work have figured out the loop: usage generates data, data improves the product, better products expand usage within the account, expansion justifies higher pricing power, pricing power funds better features. Each customer doesn’t just pay, they make the next customer easier to serve profitably.
Understanding why compounding loops break is often more valuable than understanding why they work, because the breaking points are where most investors lose money.
Regulatory Choke Points
The most common way compounding breaks is through regulatory intervention. A business compounds through network effects or data accumulation. That compounding creates market dominance. Market dominance creates pricing power or data monopolies. Those monopolies create obvious public harms. Politicians regulate. Regulation limits the compounding loop.
Facebook’s network loop compounded beautifully for over a decade. Then it compounded right into antitrust investigations, privacy regulations, and public backlash. The loop didn’t break… it’s still running, but the returns from each incremental user decreased as compliance costs increased.
Human Bottlenecks
Most compounding loops eventually hit a human constraint… something that can’t be automated or delegated without degrading the loop’s effectiveness.
Steve Jobs was a human bottleneck at Apple. The design compounding loop—taste → products → reputation → talent → better taste → better products—depended on Jobs’ specific aesthetic sensibility. When Jobs died, the loop didn’t break immediately, but it did degrade.
Incentive Decay
Compounding loops often break because the people running them lose the incentive to keep the loop running optimally. Founders are optimizing for long-term value creation because they have illiquid equity stakes. Professional managers are often optimizing for quarterly performance.
Intel’s degradation is instructive. Their compounding loop was: process leadership → better chips → more revenue → more R&D budget → better process. That loop required continual reinvestment in fabs at levels that seemed economically irrational in any given quarter. When they started optimizing for near-term margins instead of process leadership, the loop degraded. TSMC took the lead because they kept running the reinvestment loop while Intel extracted cash.
Scale Without Learning
The most insidious way compounding breaks is when a business achieves scale without building learning systems, and then assumes the scale itself is the moat.
GE under Jack Welch scaled through acquisitions and financial engineering. They got very large. But they didn’t build learning loops. When the financial engineering became untenable, there was no underlying compounding mechanism to rely on. Scale without learning is just size, and size without compounding is fragile.
Here’s where it gets interesting for investors: the speed at which a loop completes determines both the business’s learning rate and its fragility.
High-frequency trading firms complete millions of loops per day. Each trade generates data that refines the next trade’s strategy. This creates an absurd learning advantage, they can iterate in real-time while competitors are still analyzing last week’s results. But it also means instability: a competitor who figures out a slightly better loop can displace you before you notice what happened.
Contrast that with Berkshire Hathaway’s loop: deploy capital into businesses generating predictable cash flows, reinvest cash flows into more businesses, repeat. This loop completes over years or decades. The learning rate is glacial. But that slowness is a feature… competitors can’t outmaneuver you in six months because the loop doesn’t complete in six months.
The ideal loops have a curious property: they complete fast enough to learn and improve, but slow enough that competitors can’t trivially replicate them. Amazon’s flywheel is beautiful partly because different parts of the loop operate at different speeds. Daily transactions inform pricing decisions, which affect quarterly merchant attraction, which influences multi-year infrastructure investments.
But here’s where most investors completely miss the pattern: The best businesses aren’t operating in single loops, they’re stacking multiple compounding loops on the same infrastructure, and each additional loop changes the economics of all the others.
Let me show you what this looks like in practice, because the theory is interesting but the application is where money gets made.
CarTrade Tech looks like a straightforward automotive classifieds business. Most investors see “car listings” and build valuation models based on comparable marketplaces. What they miss is that CarTrade isn’t operating one loop… it’s a multi-loop compounding machine disguised as a car listing website.
Loop 1: The Basic Marketplace More consumers searching for cars → More dealers listing inventory → Better selection → More consumers → More dealers
This is the base layer. It works. But it’s also not that defensible. If CarTrade were just this loop, it would be a decent business trading at marketplace multiples.
Loop 2: Transaction Services (Financing) CarTrade connects buyers with financial institutions. More transactions → more data on creditworthiness → better credit offerings → higher conversion rates → more transactions. Financial institutions pay for access to qualified leads.
Here’s the crucial insight: Every time you can add a revenue stream to the same pipeline, the profit margins change drastically. You’re putting more through the pipe.
The marginal cost of adding this revenue stream is near-zero, you already have the buyer, you already have the transaction, you’re just capturing more of the value chain. The traffic acquisition cost doesn’t double when you add a second revenue stream. But the revenue per transaction can increase by 50-100% or more.
Loop 3: Inspection and Valuation Services More inspections → more data on vehicle condition → better valuation algorithms → more trust → more volume → more inspections
This loop increases the quality of listings in Loop 1 and reduces friction in Loop 2.
Loop 4: Auction Platform (Shriram Automall) More auctions → better price discovery → more participants → more wholesale pricing data → feeds back into Loop 3’s valuations
Loop 5: Dealer Management Systems CarTrade provides software to manage dealer operations. This creates switching costs while generating data on inventory management and dealer performance that improves all other loops.
Now here’s what makes this truly powerful: These loops don’t just coexist. They reinforce each other, and each additional loop improves the unit economics of all the previous loops.
CarTrade’s financing loop makes their inspection loop more valuable (better data on loan defaults by vehicle condition). Their auction loop makes their listing loop more valuable (better wholesale pricing data). Their dealer management system makes all other loops stickier (switching costs).
The same behavioral data—on-site user behavior, auction patterns, consumer buying patterns, demographic information, feeds into multiple proprietary solutions: search technology, sales conversion tools, personalized ads, recommendation engines, vehicle pricing, real-time financing, inspection/valuation.
Each solution generates revenue. Each solution improves the others. The cost to capture the data happens once. The value extraction happens across multiple products.
India’s automotive market has specific characteristics that make this multi-loop model especially powerful:
Fragmented dealer market: 150,000+ dealers, most with <10 person operations. No single player can own the full stack, so the infrastructure layer that connects everyone captures disproportionate value.
Low credit penetration: Only ~15% of used car purchases are financed vs. 50%+ in developed markets. Loop 2 (financing) has massive runway, and each incremental improvement in credit decisioning directly increases the addressable market for Loop 1.
Trust deficit: Loop 3 (inspection/valuation) solves a real problem, and solving it expands the market for Loops 1 and 2.
This is the constraint-driven innovation pattern we see repeatedly in Indian digital businesses: Reliance Jio solving for high data costs, Zerodha solving for high brokerage fees. The constraint isn’t a bug, it’s the feature that makes the infrastructure layer defensible.
Now let’s look at the broader pattern in Indian financial infrastructure, because what CarTrade is doing in automotive transactions, multiple companies are doing across wealth management and capital markets.
A pure discount broker (Zerodha, AngelOne) captures transaction fees. That’s it. One revenue stream per client. High competition, compressing fees, constant customer acquisition battle.
A wealth manager (Motilal Oswal, 360 One, Nuvama) captures:
Advisory fees (recurring, based on AUM)
Transaction fees (when clients trade)
Product manufacturing fees (when clients invest in their mutual funds)
Lending fees (loans against securities)
Investment banking fees (when wealth clients need capital for their businesses)
Same client. Same platform. Five revenue streams instead of one. This is the reason players like Groww, AngelOne are trying to build out wealth and asset mangement adjacency.
But it gets better: Each revenue stream creates data that improves the others.
Wealth advisory conversations reveal client needs → Insights for product manufacturing (mutual fund strategies)
Transaction patterns reveal risk tolerance → Better loan underwriting
Investment banking relationships reveal company fundamentals → Better investment advice
Mutual fund performance data → Trust building for wealth management
Lending relationships → Insights into promoter quality for investment banking
There’s a phrase that perfectly captures why investors love these businesses: “higher share of recurring revenues.”
But, it’s not just that recurring revenues are more predictable. It’s that in a multi-loop system, recurring revenues are the compounding mechanism.
In a pure transaction business, your revenue next quarter depends on how much your clients trade next quarter. That’s driven by market conditions, which you don’t control.
In a wealth management business with advisory fees based on AUM, your revenue next quarter is your revenue this quarter plus:
Net new client acquisition
Market performance (lifts AUM automatically)
Additional products sold to existing clients (more loops)
Compounding of client wealth (your fees grow as they grow)
If you’ve built proper loops, you’re not just acquiring new clients, you’re expanding revenue per client through additional products, and you’re benefiting from your clients’ compounding (since you’re paid as a percentage of AUM).
This is why markets pay premium multiples for wealth businesses vs. broking businesses. Wealth businesses have built multiple reinforcing loops where broking businesses have built linear transaction processing.
Disruption happens when someone discovers a tighter loop that completes faster and accumulates advantages more efficiently.
Blockbuster’s loop: open stores → rent movies → invest in inventory → open more stores. This loop completed over months or years.
Netflix’s DVD loop: acquire subscribers → understand preferences → optimize DVD inventory and shipping → improve recommendations → acquire subscribers. This loop completed over weeks.
Netflix’s streaming loop: acquire subscribers → gather viewing data → improve recommendations and content → acquire subscribers. This loop completes in real-time.
Each iteration tightened the circle, increased the rotation speed, and compounded learning advantages faster. Blockbuster never had a chance—not because Netflix had better technology, but because they had a tighter loop.
Most investing advice focuses on prediction: predict earnings, predict growth rates, predict multiples. This is backwards. Prediction is overrated, explanation is underrated, and the real edge is understanding why something compounds.
If you’re trying to predict Amazon’s revenue in 2027, you’re competing against thousands of analysts with sophisticated models. Your chance of having a meaningful edge in that prediction is approximately zero.
But here’s what most investors miss: you don’t need to predict revenue to make money. You need to understand whether the business model compounds, and if so, through what mechanism. If you understand the mechanism, you can be roughly right about the direction even if you’re precisely wrong about the magnitude.
Consider Netflix in 2012. You could have built an elaborate DCF model predicting subscriber growth. Or you could have just asked: “Does the content recommendation loop compound?” If yes, then the business should improve over time regardless of the specific numbers.
The analysts who built precise models got the numbers wrong. The investors who understood the compounding mechanism made money anyway.
The investor’s real job is to build a causal model of how the business works, then stress-test that model against reality.
If you can’t explain why a business compounds… not just that it does, but why, you don’t understand it well enough to invest in it. The explanation needs to be mechanical and specific. “Network effects” isn’t an explanation. “Each new user creates data that improves the product, and the improved product has higher retention, which increases lifetime value, which justifies higher customer acquisition costs” is an explanation.
The difference matters because labels don’t help you identify when things break. Causal chains let you monitor the links for weakness.
Markets are reasonably efficient at pricing visible growth. They’re terrible at pricing invisible compounding.
When Shopify IPO’d in 2015, most investors saw “ecommerce software for small businesses.” What wasn’t priced in was the app ecosystem compounding loop: more merchants attracted app developers, more apps made the platform more valuable, which attracted more merchants. By the time the loop was visible in the numbers, the stock had already 10x’d.
The investors who made money weren’t the ones who predicted the revenue numbers. They were the ones who understood the compounding mechanism before it became obvious.
This is the only sustainable edge available to most investors: understanding compounding mechanisms before they’re obvious. Because once they’re obvious, they’re priced.
Here’s how to use this framework in practice:
Green flag: Gross margins are expanding even as the company grows
This means each incremental customer or transaction is more profitable
It’s evidence of loops compounding
CarTrade’s trajectory: As they’ve added inspection services, financing tie-ups, and auction platforms, revenue per transaction increases while CAC stays relatively flat
Red flag: Gross margins are flat or declining despite growth
This means you’re just doing more of the same thing
Scale economies might exist, but compounding loops don’t
Green flag: Product announcements that stack on existing infrastructure
When CarTrade launches a dealer management system, that uses existing relationships and data to create switching costs
When Motilal Oswal launches a new mutual fund, that uses existing wealth relationships to distribute
Green flag: Management talking about “share of wallet” or “revenue per customer”
This indicates they’re thinking about multiple loops per customer
The strategy is to extract more value from the same relationships
Red flag: Management talking only about “user growth” or “market share”
This indicates they’re thinking about one loop (customer acquisition)
Growing the top of the funnel doesn’t matter if you’re not expanding what you capture per customer
There’s a reason this pattern is showing up so prominently in Indian financial and marketplace businesses right now. It’s structural.
Digital Infrastructure Just Got Built: India Stack (Aadhaar, UPI, DigiLocker) dramatically reduced the cost of KYC, payments, and verification. This means the incremental cost of adding loops dropped to near-zero. The platform layer can now focus on stacking services instead of building basic infrastructure.
Market Penetration is Still Low: Auto financing penetration is ~15% vs. 50%+ in developed markets. Wealth management penetration is ultra-high in UHNI but nearly absent in mass affluent. This means companies can grow the market while simultaneously capturing more per customer.
Regulatory Clarity is Emerging: Account aggregators are becoming standardized. This favors companies with multiple loops because data portability means they can build on a common identity layer.
Trust is Still Being Established: Indian consumers are still determining who to trust. This benefits companies that can offer comprehensive solutions from a single trusted brand.
The companies that will win the next decade aren’t the ones with the fastest growth, they’re the ones building multi-loop compounding machines while competitors are still figuring out their core loop.
The full insight is this: Every revenue stream you add doesn’t just increase revenue, it creates data and relationships that make all other revenue streams more valuable.
This is why the compounding analogy is perfect. With simple interest, you get paid on your principal. With compound interest, you get paid on your principal and your previous returns. With loop stacking, each loop generates returns, and those returns (in the form of data, relationships, and network effects) improve all other loops.
The businesses that understand this are building infrastructures that get exponentially more valuable while competitors are building linear growth machines. By the time the market recognizes the difference, the multi-loop companies have accumulated compounding advantages that are nearly impossible to replicate.
Because you can copy a feature. You can copy a price point. You can even copy a business model. But you can’t copy five years of compounding loops running in parallel, each making the others stronger.
So here’s the test for any business you’re analyzing: Can you draw the loop? Not the business model, not the revenue streams, not the competitive positioning, the actual loop. Better yet: Can you draw the loops, plural, and map how they reinforce each other?
Start with the core transaction. What happens? What does that enable? What does that lead to? And most importantly: does it lead back to the starting point, but with accumulated advantages?
If you can’t close the circle, you don’t understand the business. And if the business can’t close the circle, or better yet, close multiple reinforcing circles, there’s no business worth owning at any price, no matter how fast it’s growing. (Unless the price on offer is too enticing to refuse)
The companies that compound value over decades aren’t the ones moving fastest in a straight line. They’re the ones spinning in ever-tighter, ever-faster circles… each rotation making the next one easier, cheaper, and more valuable. And increasingly, they’re the ones that have figured out how to run multiple circles simultaneously, each strengthening the others.
Because in the end, everything that lasts is a loop. Everything that creates enduring value is multiple loops reinforcing each other. And if you can’t find the loops, or worse, if you mistake growth for compounding, you’re probably about to learn an expensive lesson about the difference between motion and progress.

There is a category of financial analysis that almost nobody does, which is to ask not “what is the best business in this sector” but rather “what structural property of this sector makes certain business models inevitable.” The difference sounds semantic but is actually load-bearing. The first question produces a rankings exercise. The second produces a theory.
Before I walk you through the business, let me make the directionality claim explicit, because it is the foundation of everything that follows.
Indian e-commerce will not simplify. It will complexify.
This is not a prediction. It is a structural statement. The forces acting on the ecosystem… marketplace proliferation, logistics fragmentation, regulatory expansion, payment method proliferation, D2C brand formation, quick commerce emergence… are all additive complexity. None of them reduce the number of systems a brand must integrate. All of them increase it.
The entropy of the system is increasing. This is thermodynamically guaranteed.
And entropy has a price.
The price shows up as operational cost, returns fraud, inventory leakage, settlement disputes, reconciliation gaps, and the particular organizational exhaustion that comes from running systems that were never designed to talk to each other. These are not edge cases. They are the central cost structure of Indian e-commerce for mid-market brands.
Unicommerce’s entire business model is a bet that entropy increases. That the coordination cost of the ecosystem rises faster than brands can absorb it internally. That at some point, and the point comes faster as you scale, the rational choice is to outsource the coordination problem to someone who has already solved it.
Unicommerce’s business is to profit from the gradient between that disorder and the structured operational coherence that brands desperately need. This is not a clever insight that Unicommerce stumbled into. It is the fundamental premise of every middleware business that has ever worked
Carl von Clausewitz, who understood warfare the way certain investors understand markets… as a system of systems, not a collection of battles, had a word for the thing you attack when you want to win decisively. Schwerpunkt. Center of gravity. The point from which all other strength flows, the node whose collapse makes everything else collapse. “The first task,” he wrote, “is to identify the enemy’s center of gravity, and if possible trace it back to a single one.” The second task is concentration. Hit it with everything. Hold the ground you take.
Clausewitz was writing about armies. He was also, without knowing it, writing a blueprint for platform businesses. Every durable technology company in history can be described in Schwerpunkt terms: find the coordination problem that everyone needs solved and nobody wants to own; break it; occupy the position; integrate outward. This is what Visa did to payment clearing. What AWS did to compute infrastructure. What Stripe did to payment APIs. What Shopify did to the checkout stack of the small merchant. Each of these companies looked, at the moment of their founding, like an unglamorous tool. Each of them became, a decade later, an unavoidable tollbooth on an essential road.
You position yourself at the point where coordination costs become unbearable, and you charge rent to reduce them. The insight isn’t original. The execution, in the specific context of Indian e-commerce operations, is.
The question for any infrastructure investment isn’t “is this a good product?” It’s “does the structural complexity of this sector make this product increasingly impossible to live without?” Those are very different questions, and only one of them has a durable answer.
What makes Unicommerce interesting as an investment case, and genuinely underappreciated by most people who cover Indian technology, is that the company has been executing this thesis quietly since 2012. It has never raised a large VC round. It has never gone viral. It has never had a consumer-facing product that anyone could admire. Its listing in August 2024 was notable primarily because it was the first profitable listed e-commerce SaaS company in India, not because it represented any kind of inflection in the underlying business. The inflection happened years earlier, when Indian e-commerce crossed the complexity threshold at which mid-market brands simply could not manage multi-channel operations without dedicated infrastructure.
That crossing happened quietly. It doesn’t make for good press releases. But it is the event that matters.
To understand what Unicommerce actually does, it helps to trace what happens in the physical world when someone places an order. Not in the sanitized version that appears in investor presentations, but the operational version that shows up as cost centers on a merchant’s P&L.
Consider a mid-market brand selling on twelve channels — Flipkart, Amazon, JioMart, Myntra, Meesho, their own D2C website, two or three vertical platforms, and a handful of regional marketplaces. Each channel has different APIs, different data formats, different commission structures, different SLA requirements, different return policies, different settlement cycles. Inventory sits across three warehouses. Fifty-plus courier options have different rate cards, different coverage zones, different performance characteristics by corridor and product type. Payment arrives as COD (still 30-40% of many categories), prepaid, wallets, and BNPL, each with different reconciliation logic.
A single transaction requires state coherence across fourteen or more independent systems. Multiply this by 100,000 orders per month, add 5,000 SKUs, and you get a combinatorial explosion of failure modes. Oversell because Amazon shows stock that the warehouse already allocated to Flipkart. Route an order to the wrong warehouse. Select the courier that underperforms for furniture deliveries to Tier 2 cities in monsoon season. Fail to catch a marketplace misclassifying your product in a higher-commission category. Miss a systematic short-payment pattern in settlement.
Each failure has a price. The aggregate price is what economists would call the coordination cost of running a multi-channel e-commerce operation. And this cost scales non-linearly. A small merchant doing ₹2 crore online revenue can manage with spreadsheets… the coordination cost is annoying but bearable, maybe ₹2-3 lakh in staff time annually. A mid-sized merchant at ₹30 crore revenue faces a coordination cost of ₹2-5 crore to build in-house systems, versus ₹15-30 lakh for Unicommerce. At ₹200+ crore, the arithmetic is even more extreme: in-house costs ₹10-20 crore, Unicommerce costs ₹1-2 crore.
This is the critical insight: the ROI on Unicommerce’s product is not constant. It improves with scale. Which means that as Indian brands grow, and they are growing, fast… the economic case for Unicommerce’s platform gets stronger. The business has embedded itself in a natural law of business operations: complexity compounds, coordination costs compound faster, and the point at which outsourcing becomes rational gets hit earlier and earlier.
This is the Schwerpunkt of Indian e-commerce, and Unicommerce identified it thirteen years ago. The coordination problem is the center of gravity. At a certain scale, and the threshold arrives earlier as the ecosystem grows more complex , no brand can solve it internally at reasonable cost. Unicommerce occupies the exact position where the cost of not having a solution exceeds the cost of the solution by a factor that makes the purchase decision feel less like a business decision and more like a law of physics.
The most durable pricing power in enterprise software isn’t the ability to raise prices. It’s the ability to sell to customers who are getting more valuable over time. Unicommerce charges per transaction. As its clients grow, they process more transactions. The pricing model is essentially an equity stake in their customers’ businesses, structured as SaaS.
The company chose patience over pace as a strategic posture, building the business to handle 100 million shipments with the architecture to reach a billion, rather than racing to acquire customers it couldn’t serve well. This is not the modal approach in Indian startup land, where growth-at-all-costs was the dominant ideology through 2021. It is, however, the approach that builds what compound interest builds… something that looks unimpressive for a long time and then, suddenly, looks inevitable.
What patience bought is a particular kind of defensibility that growth-at-all-costs rarely produces: deep integration. Unicommerce isn’t a point solution that plugs into a merchant’s operation. It is, functionally, the central nervous system of that operation — OMS, WMS, reconciliation, courier allocation, returns management, GST compliance. Ripping it out is not like changing your email marketing provider. It requires rebuilding integrations to 200+ channels, migrating years of historical data, retraining an entire operations team, and accepting a non-trivial window of operational risk during the transition. This is why Net Revenue Retention stays above 100% even when the external environment is challenging. Customers do not leave. The only meaningful churn Unicommerce experiences is structural… brands shutting down entirely, exiting e-commerce, or migrating to fulfilled-by-marketplace models.
Notably, that last churn category — FBM migration — is not competitively induced. It’s the structural risk that deserves the most serious attention in any bear case, and we’ll get to it. But the absence of competitive-driven churn says something important: in the specific niche of e-commerce operations middleware, Unicommerce has essentially won India.
What Unicommerce has built is essentially a pseudo-ERP for commerce operations. They’re not calling it that—”ERP” carries connotations of bloated enterprise software and multi-year implementations. But functionally, that’s what it is: a system of record that sits at the center of a client’s operations and orchestrates everything flowing through it.
The value proposition is simple: we turn chaos into data, and data into decisions.
When an order comes in from any channel, Uniware (their core platform) captures it, determines which warehouse should fulfill it, checks inventory in real-time, triggers the pick-pack-ship workflow, selects the optimal courier based on cost/speed/reliability, generates shipping labels, updates tracking across all systems, handles exceptions (address issues, delivery failures, returns), and reconciles the entire financial transaction at the end.
This happens millions of times per day. Currently, they’re processing over 1 billion order-items annually. That number was basically zero a decade ago.
The brilliance is that this isn’t just operational outsourcing, it’s creating an information moat. Once Unicommerce is processing your orders, they see everything:
Which products move fastest
Which warehouses are most efficient
Which couriers perform best for which pin codes
Where returns cluster (product issues vs. delivery issues vs. fraud)
Payment settlement patterns
Seasonal demand curves
Customer behavior across channels
This data doesn’t just make operations smoother. It makes operations smarter. And as the data compounds, the system gets better at predicting and preventing problems.
This is the classic platform dynamic: the more volume runs through the system, the more value the system creates, which attracts more volume. Positive feedback loops are the only durable moat in technology.
Let’s talk about what happens when a company becomes truly embedded in your operations.
Unicommerce isn’t a point solution you can swap out on a whim. They’re integrated into:
Your inventory management system
Your warehouse management workflows
Your marketplace seller panels
Your courier partner accounts
Your financial reconciliation processes
Your customer service operations
Your returns and refunds workflows
Ripping this out isn’t like changing your email marketing tool. It’s like replacing your central nervous system while remaining conscious.
The switching costs are enormous:
Operational risk: Any migration creates a window where orders might be lost, inventory miscounted, shipments delayed
Integration complexity: You’d need to rebuild 200+ integrations to channels, couriers, and internal systems
Data migration: Years of historical transaction data, SKU mappings, supplier relationships
Learning curve: Your entire operations team is trained on Unicommerce’s system
Workflow dependencies: Downstream processes are built assuming Unicommerce’s data structure
This is why their Net Revenue Retention, even in a muted demand environment, stays above 100%. Customers don’t leave. They might reduce volumes temporarily if their business softens, but they don’t switch to competitors.
In fact, the only meaningful churn Unicommerce experiences isn’t competitive, it’s structural. Brands either shut down entirely (e-commerce is brutal), stop selling online, or shift from a dropship model (where they control fulfillment) to a fulfilled-by-marketplace model (where Amazon/Flipkart handles it).
None of these are things Unicommerce can control. But notably, none of them are competition-driven.
There is a version of the Unicommerce bull case that focuses on the transaction processing business — the ₹1.08 per transaction, the 7,596 merchants, the 285+ integrations, the NRR above 100. This is the version that most analysts present. It is accurate but incomplete, because it describes the revenue model without describing the compounding mechanism.
The more interesting version starts with an observation: Unicommerce processes over one billion transactions annually across its merchant base. This gives it something no individual merchant possesses… statistically significant data on the operational performance of Indian e-commerce at scale. Which courier performs best for high-value jewelry deliveries to Tier 2 cities in South India? What is the real RTO rate for COD orders above ₹3,000 to Northeast pin codes? How does delivery time variance affect conversion in home furnishings? What NDR patterns indicate courier gaming versus legitimate delivery failure? No individual merchant, with their thousands of monthly shipments, can answer these questions with any statistical confidence. Unicommerce, with millions of shipments across every geography and category, can.
This data advantage gets productized as ShipSense — AI-driven courier selection trained on historical delivery outcomes across the entire merchant network. It shows up in UniReco — payment reconciliation that identifies systematic short-payment patterns by seeing hundreds of brands’ settlement data and knowing what the distribution of marketplace behavior actually looks like. It surfaces in demand forecasting that incorporates regional preferences and category seasonality learned from the aggregate, not from any individual merchant’s experience. The beautiful mechanism here is that every transaction processed makes these models marginally better. Every new merchant adds data that benefits all merchants. Scale creates knowledge, knowledge creates better outcomes, better outcomes attract scale. The flywheel is real.
Information asymmetries compound in a specific way: the entity that sees the most data across the most participants in a system becomes progressively better at predicting and optimizing within that system, which attracts more participants, which generates more data. This is why AWS never lost its data-center cost advantage despite competition — it always had the largest sample size to optimize against.
The caveat, and it is a real one, is that Unicommerce’s information advantage operates within a structurally constrained perimeter. Unicommerce has knowledge power (accumulated information creates advantage), but limited structural power (control over market structure, ability to set rules).
What they control:
The operational workflow within their platform
Integration points to external systems
Data aggregation and model training
Feature development roadmap
What they don’t control:
Marketplace policies (Amazon/Flipkart determine fulfillment model requirements)
Courier economics (logistics partners set rate cards)
Consumer demand (brands determine products, marketing, pricing)
E-commerce architecture (shift from dropship to FBM undermines relevance)
This matters because structural changes can bypass the knowledge layer entirely.
The CEO stated it explicitly: “Uniware is most aligned to the dropship model, and if brands shift toward fulfilled-by-marketplace models where Amazon or Flipkart takes over warehousing and last-mile, Unicommerce’s event stream shrinks.”
This is the bear case in one sentence.
Fewer transactions processed means slower model improvement means weaker competitive advantage. This is different from the structural power of card networks, where the infrastructure is genuinely unavoidable. Unicommerce’s position is valuable but contingent on a particular fulfillment architecture remaining dominant. The bear case is not that a competitor builds better software… it is that the market structure shifts to bypass middleware entirely.
This risk is real but probably overweighted by skeptics. Brands actively resist ceding fulfillment control to marketplaces for three reasons: margin preservation (marketplace fulfillment is expensive), customer relationship ownership (D2C brands want visibility into their own customer data), and multi-channel requirements (a brand selling across twelve platforms cannot be “fulfilled by” any one of them). The structural incentive to remain in control of fulfillment is strong, which means the structural incentive to maintain an OMS like Unicommerce is strong. The risk is non-zero. It is probably not 50%.
The acquisition of Shipway in late 2024, completed across two tranches for approximately ₹68.4 crore total — is the strategic move that most financial analysis misunderstands. The instinct is to frame it as revenue diversification into courier aggregation, a ₹4,000 crore addressable market. This framing is correct but misses the deeper logic.
Before Shipway, Unicommerce had a significant gap in its data architecture. It managed the order lifecycle from placement through warehouse and dispatch, but once a shipment left the warehouse, it was essentially blind. Tracking updates came through courier APIs, which are unreliable. Delivery performance data was mediated. The granular picture of what actually happens between warehouse and doorstep, which couriers underperform on which corridors, what NDR patterns look like, how COD collection rates vary by geography and product type, how return rates cluster, was largely opaque.
Shipway closes this loop. Now Unicommerce sees real-time shipment tracking across all carriers, granular delivery performance by courier and region, returns initiation and processing, and COD collection patterns. Combined with what it already saw — order placement, inventory allocation, warehouse operations — this creates a unified data layer across the complete order lifecycle. The information moat gets substantially deeper. ShipSense’s courier allocation models, which already outperform manual selection, get smarter because the feedback loop is now closed: the model can observe outcomes, not just inputs.
The financials on Shipway are instructive in their own right. Annualized revenue run-rate grew from roughly ₹65-70 crore at acquisition announcement to ₹100 crore run-rate in Q3 FY26, approximately 50% growth in under a year, while remaining PAT-positive throughout. Unicommerce is running Shipway at breakeven adjusted EBITDA, reinvesting operational profit back into growth investment in a ₹4,000 crore market where it’s still a relatively small player. This is disciplined capital allocation: expand aggressively where the opportunity is large and the platform advantage is real, but don’t subsidize growth with unsustainable unit economics. The ability to grow this aggressively while staying PAT-positive is evidence that the unit economics are sound even in investment mode.
There is a pattern in the best platform acquisitions: the acquired asset looks like an adjacency but functions as a sensor. The strategic value isn’t the revenue. It’s the data that flows through it, which makes the entire platform more intelligent. Shipway is, in this sense, more like an instrument than an asset.
One number that has concerned analysts is the decline in realization per transaction… from roughly ₹1.30 to ₹1.08, a 15% drop that reads, on the surface, like pricing pressure or commoditization. The bear interpretation: margins are thin, competitors are pricing aggressively, and per-unit economics are deteriorating.
The more interesting interpretation: Unicommerce is deliberately optimizing for long-term customer lifetime value over short-term per-transaction yield, and the mix of transactions is shifting toward higher-volume, lower-realization use cases like quick commerce and B2B that have better lifetime economics at scale. On the pricing side, they’ve lowered minimum guarantee thresholds for early-stage brands — acquiring customers while they’re small and growing with them as they scale, which is how SaaS businesses build durable revenue. On the mix side, a quick commerce client processing 10,000 orders per day at ₹0.80 per transaction generates more lifetime value than a D2C brand processing 100 orders per day at ₹1.50.
This matters for the long-term thesis in a specific way. Unicommerce’s pricing model is essentially a bet on the growth of their clients. Every brand that starts small and becomes large represents a compounding revenue opportunity… more transactions, plus higher-value module upsells as the operational complexity of a larger business creates demand for additional products. The cross-sell rate from Uniware to Shipway and Convertway is currently only around 8-10%, which represents enormous upsell headroom. New modules like UniReco and UniCapture create revenue streams that don’t require proportional delivery cost. Price escalation clauses are being built into new contracts as leverage for future increases.
Realization per transaction is a lagging metric. It measures what Unicommerce earned on past transactions, not the earnings power of the platform it is building. The leading indicators… module expansion, cross-sell penetration, enterprise client additions, quick commerce growth rate, point in the opposite direction from the lagging one.
There is a class of competitive analysis that describes a market as “fragmented” and treats fragmentation as inherently a risk, the assumption being that fragmentation implies someone will consolidate it, and consolidation implies margin compression for whoever doesn’t end up as the consolidator. This analysis is wrong when applied to the competitive landscape of e-commerce operations software in India, for a specific reason: what looks like fragmentation is actually irreducible complexity.
India’s e-commerce ecosystem has structural features that prevent the consolidation most people expect. On the marketplace side: Amazon (~25% share), Flipkart (~30%), Meesho (~10-12%), quick commerce (~8-10%), vertical specialists, and a long tail. Government policy actively discourages monopolistic consolidation. Multiple well-capitalized players — Walmart/Flipkart, Amazon, Reliance/Jio, Tata — have strategic incentives to compete rather than consolidate. On the logistics side: national players, marketplace-captive fleets, regional specialists, and quick commerce providers, each with different performance characteristics by corridor and product type. On the payments side: UPI, cards, wallets, COD, BNPL, each with different settlement mechanics.
This is not temporary fragmentation on the way to consolidation. It is structural fragmentation sustained by competitive and regulatory dynamics that are unlikely to change. And structural fragmentation means durable coordination costs, which means durable need for middleware. The complexity is, if anything, increasing — more quick commerce platforms, more D2C channels, more compliance requirements, more edge cases per transaction… not decreasing.
The implication for competitive dynamics is that Unicommerce’s moat isn’t primarily technological. Any competent engineering team can build an OMS. The moat is the combination of: 285+ pre-built integrations that took years to build and maintain; institutional knowledge of how Indian e-commerce actually works (the API quirks, the policy exceptions, the settlement games that specific marketplaces play); data network effects from processing a billion transactions annually; and the switching costs of a mission-critical, deeply embedded system. A new entrant doesn’t just have to build better software. It has to replicate years of integration work, operational domain expertise, and training data. In a market growing this fast, that window of disadvantage is often permanently disqualifying.
Moats built from integration breadth and institutional knowledge are less legible than technology moats, which is probably why they tend to be more durable. Everyone can see a technology advantage and compete against it. Fewer people can see — or reproduce — 285 integrations and a decade of exception handling.
There is an obvious framing for Unicommerce as an investment: it’s a bet on India’s e-commerce growth. This is true but undersells the structural leverage in the position. A direct bet on India’s e-commerce growth would be something like an equal-weight basket of D2C consumer brands, exposure to the growth, but also to the fierce competition at the brand level, the marketing cost inflation, the category concentration risk, the fashion cycle exposure.
Unicommerce is structurally different because of what you might call the tollbooth property: as more commerce flows through India’s digital channels, more of it flows through Unicommerce’s systems, not because Unicommerce wins every brand, but because every brand that needs to manage multi-channel operations is a potential Unicommerce customer. Growth in India’s e-commerce penetration (currently 5-7% of total retail versus 50%+ in China) is not just good for the brands on Unicommerce’s platform. It is good for Unicommerce even through new customer acquisition of brands that didn’t exist five years ago.
The China comparison is useful precisely because it is calibrating, not predictive. India’s e-commerce at 3-4% of China’s current volume, with a demographic curve that suggests significant convergence over the next decade, implies potential 10-20x expansion in transaction volumes at a minimum. Infrastructure businesses with Unicommerce’s positioning — embedded in the operational stack, benefiting from volume growth as well as wallet share expansion within existing clients, are likely to grow considerably faster than the underlying market because of the double lever: more brands plus more spending per brand.
Current financials are consistent with this trajectory. At approximately ₹1,100 crore market cap against an annualized consolidated revenue run-rate now exceeding ₹225 crore and adjusted EBITDA run-rate above ₹53 crore, the multiple — roughly 5x NTM revenue, 21x NTM adjusted EBITDA — isn’t obviously cheap. But multiples are just shorthand. The market cap relative to opportunity size is disproportionately small. What matters is what you’re buying.
You’re buying:
An index fund on India’s e-commerce growth with embedded leverage (they grow faster than the market because penetration increases + they capture more wallet share per customer over time)
A platform with genuine learning loops and data moats that strengthen with scale
Expanding margins as operating leverage kicks in (infrastructure businesses have beautiful margin profiles once they hit scale)
Multiple expansion opportunities (cross-sell, new modules, price escalation, geographic expansion)
The bull case isn’t that Unicommerce is cheap today. It’s that if India’s e-commerce grows anything like China’s did, and Unicommerce remains embedded in the operational stack, the next 10 years of volume growth + margin expansion + product expansion could deliver 10x returns even from today’s valuation.
The real question is not whether the multiple is cheap but whether the compounding mechanisms are real and whether the structural risks are properly bounded.
The honest version of the bear case has three components, each with a different probability weight.
The first is the FBM migration risk: if Amazon and Flipkart successfully push brands toward fulfilled-by-marketplace models, Unicommerce’s event stream shrinks and its data advantage degrades. This is real. It requires believing that the structural incentives for brands to maintain fulfillment control — margin preservation, customer data ownership, multi-channel requirements, are weaker than the marketplace push. I’d assign maybe 15-20% probability to this becoming a material disruption over five years, weighted toward the tail.
The second is AI-native disruption: what if large language models or agentic AI systems make it trivially cheap to build custom integrations, eliminating the integration-complexity moat? This is the version of disruption that is hardest to price because it is genuinely uncertain. The strong version — AI writes and maintains all integrations at near-zero cost, would be devastating. The weak version — AI helps engineers build integrations faster, accelerating new entrants, probably just means the market grows faster, which is good for the incumbent. I’d assign the strong version low but non-trivial probability, maybe 10-15%.
The third is execution risk on the cross-sell and module expansion thesis: the bull case depends significantly on Unicommerce raising revenue per customer by 30-50% through Shipway, Convertway, UniReco, UniCapture, and modules yet to be built. The risk is not that the products don’t work; it’s that enterprise sales cycles are long, customer behavior changes slowly, and the revenue expansion that looks obvious from outside looks harder from inside. I’d assign maybe 20-25% probability that this significantly underdelivers over five years.
These risks are real. They don’t add up to a decisive bear case, but they explain why the multiple is what it is rather than significantly higher. The market isn’t wrong to price in uncertainty. The question is whether it’s pricing in the right kind and the right amount of uncertainty… which is, as always, the investment question, not the analysis question.
The best businesses aren’t just growing, they’re compounding. Growth is linear: add more customers, make more revenue. Compounding is exponential: each customer makes the product better for all customers, which attracts more customers, which makes the product better still.
Unicommerce has several compounding loops:
Loop 1: Integration Network Effects
More clients → more integration requests → broader integration catalog → easier onboarding for new clients → more clients
Each new marketplace integration they build doesn’t just serve one client—it serves every client. The 1,000th client benefits from integration work done for clients 1-999. This is pure leverage.
Loop 2: Data Network Effects
More volume → more operational data → smarter courier selection, inventory optimization, exception handling → better client outcomes → volume expands within existing clients + attracts new clients
This is where AI creates real value, not as a buzzword, but as a mechanism for turning accumulated transaction history into operationally superior decision-making.
Loop 3: Module Expansion
Larger customer base → more diverse pain points visible → higher ROI for building new modules → more reasons to use Unicommerce → larger customer base
UniReco, UniCapture, B2B capabilities, quick commerce workflows… these didn’t exist three years ago. They emerged from seeing recurring problems at scale and building solutions. Each new module increases platform stickiness and wallet share.
Loop 4: Vertical Integration / Horizontal Expansion
Owning more of the value chain (Convertway for pre-purchase, Uniware for operations, Shipway for logistics) → complete data visibility across customer journey → better optimization across entire workflow → higher value creation → justifies capturing more value
This is the long game: become the operating system for commerce, where integrating everything through you creates so much incremental value that the platform fee becomes negligible relative to the efficiency gains.
Clausewitz's strategic insight was that you identify the center of gravity, concentrate your force on it, and then use the ground you've taken to expand outward. Unicommerce identified the center of gravity of Indian e-commerce — the coordination cost that compounds with scale, in 2012. It has been concentrating its force on that point for thirteen years. The ground it has taken is: 285 integrations, a billion annual transactions, a decade of exception handling and institutional knowledge, an NRR above 100%, and a data moat that is now closed-loop across the complete order lifecycle. The outward expansion… Shipway, Convertway, UniReco, whatever comes next… follows naturally from the high ground held.
There is a broader lesson embedded in the Unicommerce case that matters for how you think about value creation in platform economies. Everyone wants to own the consumer relationship — the brand, the interface, the data about what people buy. Consumer brands are aspirational, visible, coveted. They make news. They attract venture capital. They generate the kind of excitement that fills investor decks.
The invisible plumbing that powers them all attracts almost none of this attention. Unicommerce will never be famous. No consumer will ever know it exists. But every brand that uses it is locked in by switching costs that make competitive displacement essentially a non-event. Every transaction it processes makes its models marginally better. Every new merchant enriches the data that makes the platform more valuable to every existing merchant. The flywheel turns silently, without press releases, without viral moments, without the kind of growth that gets written up in the papers.
This is what infrastructure investing actually looks like in practice. Not the big dramatic rollout or the product launch with a keynote. The relentless, compounding accumulation of integrations, edge case handling, institutional knowledge, and proprietary data, none of which is individually impressive, all of which is collectively irreplaceable. You can’t disrupt something you can’t see. And the empire Unicommerce is building is almost entirely invisible.
Patience, the choice Mr. Makhija describes as the company’s defining strategic posture, turns out to be the right approach for exactly this kind of business. Consumer businesses need velocity because they compete for attention in a market that refreshes constantly. Infrastructure businesses need depth, because their moats are built from things that take years to accumulate. The companies that try to build infrastructure with consumer-company urgency usually end up with neither the scale of a consumer business nor the depth of an infrastructure one. Unicommerce has been building depth for thirteen years. The depth is, at this point, quite deep.
The ten-year view isn’t complicated. If India’s e-commerce grows toward Chinese penetration rates — and all demographic, payment infrastructure, and logistics data suggest it will, and Unicommerce maintains its position as the default operational middleware for the mid-market brands who power that growth, then the compounding math over a decade is very favorable even from today’s valuation. Not guaranteed, not without the risks outlined above, but compelling in the way that infrastructure businesses in the path of a demographic inevitability tend to be compelling: boring, predictable, and relentlessly durable.
The best investments are the ones that look boring while they’re compounding and obvious in retrospect. Unicommerce is, at this moment, in the boring phase. Whether the obvious phase follows depends on execution it has already demonstrated and structural tailwinds it doesn’t control. Those are reasonable odds.
This is a bet on infrastructure. Infrastructure is boring. Infrastructure takes time to compound. Infrastructure doesn’t make headlines.
But if you believe India’s digital commerce is going to explode. and all the evidence says it will, then betting on the invisible plumbing that makes it all work is probably smarter than betting on whichever brand has the prettiest Instagram feed this quarter.
The empire nobody sees is often the empire that lasts. This one has been building in plain sight for thirteen years, which is the most Clausewitzian thing about it: the best strategic positions are the ones that look, to the enemy, like they're not even there.
This is the essence of infrastructure investing: find the essential, embedded, compounding businesses that nobody’s paying attention to because they’re too busy watching the flashy consumer brands. Then wait for the decade-long compounding to work its magic. Boring wins. Boring always wins.

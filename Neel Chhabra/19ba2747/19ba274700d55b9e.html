Negentropy at Scale: India’s Protocol Upgrade and the Physics of Friction Removal

On extracting order from chaos, and why the most valuable companies in the next decade will be those that transform informal economies into predictable systems

Neel Chhabra
Jan 9
 
READ IN APP
 

In thermodynamics, entropy measures disorder, the tendency of systems to decay toward randomness. Negentropy is the opposite: the extraction of order from chaos. It requires energy, intelligence, and structure.

For centuries, India’s economy has operated in a state of high entropy. Commerce happened through informal networks, trust was personal not systemic, and coordination costs were astronomical. The corner store knew your family. The taxi driver negotiated every fare. The wholesaler extended credit based on decades of relationship. Beautiful in its human complexity. Economically inefficient beyond measure.

We are now witnessing the most dramatic negentropy extraction event in commercial history.

What is actually happening is the systematic compression of friction across every axis of commercial life in a civilization that has, for millennia, operated on high-context relationships, informal networks, and fragmented atomic units. India is upgrading from a relationship-based economy to a protocol-based economy. And in that upgrade lies billions of dollars of value creation, because every unit of friction removed is energy recaptured for productive use.

The Upgrade: From Relationships to Protocols

The India Stack—Aadhaar for identity, UPI for payments, DigiLocker for documents, and GST for taxation—is not just “digital infrastructure.” It’s a phase transition in how commerce operates.

Consider what these protocols accomplish:

Trust becomes verifiable. You don’t need to know someone’s family to lend them money; Aadhaar authentication gives you cryptographic proof of identity. 430 million e-KYC transactions happen monthly. Trust, once earned over years, is now transmitted in milliseconds.

Transactions become frictionless. UPI processes 20+ billion transactions monthly. The cognitive overhead of “how do I pay this person” has collapsed to zero. Every shopkeeper, every street vendor, and every delivery driver has a QR code. The friction of physical cash… making change, storing it, depositing it, reconciling it… evaporates.

Formality becomes the default. 15 million GST-registered taxpayers. Every transaction leaves a digital trail. The informal economy, that labyrinth of handshakes and ledgers, is slowly being pulled into the light of protocols and data.

This is negentropy at a civilizational scale. The chaos of informal networks is being replaced by the order of open protocols. And on top of that order, a cambrian explosion of commerce models that were previously impossible.

The Physics of Disruption: Three Mechanisms

There are three distinct mechanisms by which new entrants extract value from high-entropy markets:

1. Great Value: Offer customers a meaningful price/quality differential versus fragmented incumbents (Meesho’s ultra-low prices)

2. Unlocking Supply: Bring new sellers/providers into the formal economy (Meesho’s zero-commission model attracting 575K sellers, Rapido’s subscription model attracting discontented drivers)

3. Removing Friction: Eliminate a major obstacle in a key customer need (Quick commerce removing time friction, Urban Company removing trust friction in home services)

What’s remarkable is that these aren’t just business strategies; they’re entropy-reduction mechanisms. Each transforms a chaotic, high-friction system into an ordered, predictable one.

Rapido’s Revelation: The Hidden Variable in Network Effects

Network effects are the religion of technology investing. More riders create more drivers, create more riders in a beautiful virtuous cycle. Except when they don’t.

Ola and Uber built traditional two-sided marketplaces in India. Variable commissions per trip. Surge pricing. Incentive schemes that required drivers to hit arbitrary thresholds. The model “worked”—they achieved duopoly, printed money, the end.

Then Rapido entered with bike taxis (the wedge—classic asymmetric competition) and, more importantly, with a subscription model for drivers.

Instead of paying 25-30% per ride, drivers pay a flat ₹300-500/week. Fixed cost. Known cost. No games.

The Rapido story is perhaps the purest example of negentropy through protocol design.

The incumbent duopoly, Ola and Uber, operated on a commission model. But commissions create entropy: drivers don’t know their take-home, platforms constantly adjust incentives, and the whole system thrashes with uncertainty. Revenue eats revenue through incentive spending to maintain supply.

Rapido’s insight: Replace variable commissions with fixed subscriptions.

Suddenly:

  • Drivers have earnings certainty (less entropy in their income)

  • Platform has cost certainty (less entropy in unit economics)

  • More drivers join because predictability is valuable

  • Higher driver density reduces customer wait times

  • Lower wait times attract more customers

  • More customers justify more drivers

The flywheel spins because the system has less internal friction. You removed entropy from the driver’s decision-making (should I take this ride? Will I hit my incentive target?) and replaced it with clarity (I pay X, I keep everything else).

This is the subscription model as an entropy-reduction tool.

And the results? Rapido went from irrelevant to processing 4.3 million daily rides, overtaking Ola, and becoming Uber’s “tougher competition” in India, in less than two years.

In network effects, the hidden variable is often not demand-side value or supply-side scale. It’s supply-side certainty. Reduce variance for the supplier, and you unlock non-obvious network density.

Why Western Quick Commerce Failed: The Wrong Physics

Gorillas in Germany. Getir in the UK. Buyk in New York. All dead or dying.

The graveyard of Western quick commerce is often blamed on “unit economics.” But that’s lazy analysis. The real issue is physics.

In Western markets, quick commerce faced impossible tradeoffs:

Low population density → Each dark store serves fewer customers → Bad store economics High labor costs → Delivery costs $8-12 per order → Must charge customers high fees → Becomes unattractive vs. existing retail Scaled incumbent retail → Customers can get similar selection at Whole Foods/Tesco → Speed is the only differentiator → Not enough to overcome price premium

The model required customers to pay a massive premium for convenience. Most said no.

In India, the physics are inverted:

High population density → A 2km radius in Mumbai serves 500,000+ people vs. 50,000 in suburban US → Store economics work Low labor costs → Delivery costs ₹30-40 ($0.40) → Can charge customers almost nothing → Becomes attractive even vs. kiranas Fragmented retail → Local shops lack selection, often have higher prices than platforms → Speed plus value plus selection → Overwhelming value proposition

This isn’t about “better execution” or “Indian resilience.” It’s about physics. The same model that fails in low-density/high-cost markets succeeds in high-density/low-cost markets.

Business models are not portable across density gradients and labor cost curves. What fails in dispersion can thrive in concentration.

Geography is destiny.

The Blinkit Pause: The Value of Negative Action

Everyone celebrates growth. Few celebrate stopping.

Blinkit had 603 dark stores and was hemorrhaging money (-28% EBITDA margin). They stopped expanding for 18 months. Fixed unit economics. Got to near-breakeven (-1.4% EBITDA margin). Then they hit the accelerator. They went from 603 stores to 1,272 stores in less than two years while maintaining profitability.

This is negentropy through restraint. The discipline to say, “We will not scale chaos.”

Swiggy Instamart? They scaled first. They went from 410 stores to 1,000+ while still bleeding 16% EBITDA. Now they’re trying to fix economics at scale, far harder, far more capital-intensive, and far more existentially risky.

The timing of when you pour fuel on the fire determines whether you get a furnace or a conflagration. Blinkit built the engine before hitting the gas. Instamart is trying to rebuild the engine while the car is moving at 100mph.

Most startups in India’s internet economy scaled broken unit economics, believing “scale will fix it.” Blinkit did the opposite. They recognized that each additional store built on broken economics just multiplied the problem.

The pause was an entropy-reduction mechanism. They tuned the variables: increased orders per store (utilization), improved average order value (revenue per transaction), added platform fees (high-margin revenue), and scaled advertising (pure profit).

By the time they resumed expansion, each marginal store was additive, not destructive.

In high-fixed-cost businesses, premature scale is the enemy. You’re not building a network effect; you’re replicating a loss-making unit across geography. Better to perfect one store than clone it 2,000 times.

The Meesho Inversion: When Zero Becomes Infinity

Let me tell you about the most elegant business model I’ve seen in years.

Meesho charges sellers zero commission. Zero. In a world where Amazon extracts 15-30%, Flipkart takes 20%, and everyone is in a race to the top on take-rate, Meesho said, We will take nothing from the seller.

The investors must have been apoplectic. “How will you make money?!”

But here’s the magic: By removing commission, Meesho created seller density. Thousands of sellers from industrial clusters in Surat, Tiruppur, and Ludhiana flocked to the platform. Seller density created selection density. Selection density created price competition. Price competition created customer value. Customer value created traffic. And traffic?

Traffic is what you monetize through advertising.

The sellers who once paid nothing in commission now compete to be seen—bidding on search ads, category placements, and featured listings. Meesho’s advertising revenue reached ₹970 crores. Almost pure margin. Directly to the bottom line.

The inversion: Give away the thing everyone else charges for. Monetize the consequence of giving it away.

This is not tactics. This is thermodynamics. You create a low-pressure zone (zero commission) that causes a flow (seller influx) that creates a new source of energy (advertising competition). You move from extraction to orchestration.

Meesho’s Valmo: Squeezing Costs to the Rupee

Meesho operates in a realm where Amazon and Flipkart fear to tread: average order values of ₹323 ($4). At this AOV, even pennies matter.

So they built Valmo—an orchestration layer that sits atop multiple third-party logistics providers and routes each shipment to the lowest-cost option based on destination, weight, and service requirements.

This is logistics as a negentropy engine. Where others saw fragmentation (dozens of logistics providers, inconsistent service), Meesho saw exploitable variance. They built a protocol layer that extracts the best price from competition among providers.

The result? They operate with a 3.9% contribution margin on a ₹323 AOV. That’s ₹12.50 per order. The unit economics are so thin that without advertising revenue (their actual profit source), they’d be unprofitable.

But this is the point: When you serve the most price-sensitive customers in the most price-sensitive categories, cost structure is everything. You can’t outspend your way to growth. You must engineer costs down to the atomic level.

Zero commissions. Batched delivery. Third-party logistics competition. Valmo optimization. Each decision removes friction, reduces cost, and enables lower prices.

Meesho is the anti-Amazon. Amazon adds services and layers and AI and complexity. Meesho strips everything away until only the essentials remain. Different physics for different customers.

The Advertising Revelation: Duty-Free Carts on Every Flight

Amazon’s global e-commerce business is unprofitable without advertising revenue.

Let that sink in. The largest, most sophisticated e-commerce platform in history, with unmatched logistics, unmatched scale, and unmatched data, would lose money on its core business without ads.

The same pattern repeats across India:

  • Nykaa’s profits come substantially from advertising (₹100+ per order)

  • Blinkit’s contribution margin is 60% platform fees (essentially ads + convenience fees)

  • Meesho is unprofitable without advertising revenue from seller ads

  • Myntra’s profitability pivot came from scaling advertising

Why? Because retail, even digital retail, has stubbornly thin margins. Logistics is expensive. Returns are costly. Price competition is brutal. But attention is valuable. The home screen is real estate. Search results are digital shelf space.

Advertising is the duty-free cart rolling down the aisle. The plane was flying anyway. The infrastructure was already paid for. Those pixels would otherwise sit empty. So every rupee from ads is nearly pure profit.

This reveals the true profit model of modern commerce platforms:

Low-margin commerce + High-margin advertising = Viable business

It’s why Google and Meta, pure advertising businesses, have better economics than most e-commerce. And it’s why every commerce platform is becoming an advertising platform.

The Protocol Economy requires dual revenue streams. Transaction fees cover the cost of transactions. Advertising covers the cost of growth and capital.

The Investment Thesis: Bet on Friction Removal

The directional arrow of technological progress points toward friction removal. Always.

Electricity removed the friction of manual labor. Automobiles removed the friction of distance. The internet removed the friction of information access. Smartphones removed the friction of being tethered to desks.

In India, friction is everywhere. It’s in:

  • The fragmented retail that can’t compete on price or selection

  • The informal labor markets where workers have no earnings certainty

  • The logistics networks with no real-time tracking or optimization

  • The trust deficits that make online adoption slow

  • The credit systems that excluded 80% of the population

  • The traffic that makes mobility unreliable

Every point of friction is a potential energy gradient. Businesses that can remove it don’t just create convenience; they capture economic value that was previously dissipated as waste heat.

This is why:

Blinkit works → Removed the friction of going to the store, solved the problem of limited selection at kiranas, and competitive pricing despite 10-min delivery…

Meesho works → Removed the friction of high prices (zero commission), brought sellers online who couldn’t justify 20-30% marketplace fees, and gave tier-3 customers selection they couldn’t access locally…

Rapido works → Removed the friction of uncertain driver earnings (subscription model), reduced customer wait times (more drivers), and offered lower prices than incumbents…

Urban Company works → Removed the friction of trusting unknown service providers (verification, standardization) and bringing professional services to customers who couldn’t find them reliably…

Each is a negentropy engine. Each takes a high-friction, chaotic system and imposes order through protocols, platforms, and aligned incentives.

The Failures Were Necessary

Western quick commerce failed because it had to. The combustion had to happen to find the right oxidizer-to-fuel ratio.

You needed entrepreneurs to try it in Berlin, New York, and London. You needed them to burn capital figuring out that density matters, that labor costs matter, and that incumbent retail matters. You needed them to demonstrate that “move fast and break things” doesn’t work when the unit economics are fundamentally broken.

Those failures created the knowledge that made India’s success possible. The Indian entrepreneurs learned: fix unit economics before scaling, optimize for cost structure not growth rate, and understand your customer’s actual constraints.

Blinkit’s 18-month pause was possible because Gorillas’ failure taught the lesson. Zepto’s reported focus on profitability in 2025 is possible because investors now know that subsidy-fueled growth doesn’t become profitable at scale.

The failures were the tuition paid for understanding the actual physics of these models.

The virtuous cycle only spins if the physics cooperate. Great customer value creates demand density, creates unit economics, creates reinvestment, creates more value. But reverse entropy is real... each link in the chain can break the wheel. Most businesses discover their flywheel is actually a treadmill.

Quick commerce promises speed and convenience. But speed requires proximity (density of dark stores). Density requires volume. Volume requires low prices. Low prices destroy margins on naturally low-AOV baskets. You're trapped between physics, psychology, and arithmetic. The only escape is scale so massive that "high orders per store" overcomes everything else. Until then, you're subsidizing customer delight with investor capital.

There is this fundamental tension... scale economies require density, but density requires subsidy, and subsidy requires belief that tomorrow's profits justify today's losses.

It's a race to prove the model works before the capital markets decide it doesn't.

What This Means—The Thermodynamic Mandate

Let me close with the physics.

Entropy is disorder. Negentropy is order. Businesses that extract order from chaos create value. But in India, chaos is not a bug—it’s a feature.

The fragmentation of retail is what allows Meesho to exist. The traffic congestion is what makes quick commerce viable (because leaving your house is painful). The lack of trust is what makes platforms with verification essential. The dispersion of supply is what makes aggregation valuable.

India’s disorder is the feedstock for negentropy extraction.

The West builds for efficiency from order. India builds for efficiency from chaos. These are fundamentally different problems requiring fundamentally different solutions.

You can view the Western market as an electric circuit designed for low resistance and high efficiency; adding a complex service is like adding a component that draws too much power, tripping the breaker; failed unit economics. India’s market, historically, was a combustion engine running with a dirty fuel mix… cash, informal trust, chaos… generating massive amounts of heat (friction) and smoke but little movement. The “Protocol Economy” acts as a fuel injection system that refines this dirty fuel into a clean mist (digital verification, UPI), allowing the engine to run efficiently. The “Physics of Markets” dictates that you cannot put the American electric circuit into the Indian combustion engine; you must build components (business models) specifically designed to harness the explosive density and pressure of the Indian environment.

The businesses that will win in India are not those that try to impose Western models. They are those who embrace the chaos and find the hidden gradients—in density, in labor costs, in fragmentation, in trust deficits, in time poverty, and in aspiration-income gaps.

We’re watching the largest informal economy in the world get formalized through protocols.

The India Stack—Aadhaar, UPI, the logistics networks, the smartphone penetration—is not “infrastructure.” It’s a platform for negentropy.

900 million internet users. 20 billion monthly UPI transactions. 6 billion annual parcel deliveries. 4+ million daily quick commerce orders. All built on top of digital identity, frictionless payments, and computational logistics.

The bet for the next decade is this:

Commerce models native to India’s physics—high density, low labor costs, fragmented incumbents, and protocol-based infrastructure—will scale within India faster than anyone expects.

Quick commerce will reach profitability in 2026 when Western equivalents are dead. Food delivery is achieving best-in-world EBITDA margins. Ride-hailing is being disrupted by subscription models. E-commerce platforms reaching 500M+ customers.

These aren’t “emerging market” stories. They’re models that only work in emerging markets, specifically markets with India’s unique combination of density, demographics, and digital infrastructure.

The West cannot copy them. The physics don’t work in reverse.

This is India’s structural advantage, and it reveals a deeper truth about technological progress:

The most valuable innovations are often those that solve problems endemic to the majority of humanity, not the wealthy minority.

Most venture capital deploys into problems faced by the 100M affluent Americans and Europeans. But 1.4B Indians have different constraints. Different physics. Different problems.

And solving those problems creates businesses that will ultimately serve the 4B+ people in developing markets globally… markets where density is high, labor is cheap, retail is fragmented, and trust is scarce.

The India Stack is the protocol. The entrepreneurs are the energy. The friction removal is the mechanism.

And the negentropy being extracted… the order being created from chaos, will power the next Multi-Billion-Dollar companies.

The best investors don’t just follow growth, they follow physics. Where do the fundamental forces of density, cost structure, and infrastructure align to make the previously impossible, possible? That’s where the future reveals itself first.

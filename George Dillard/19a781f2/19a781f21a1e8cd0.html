From Railway Madness to Digital Anxiety

How do we describe the stress caused by new technology?

George Dillard
Nov 12
 
READ IN APP
 

People simply weren’t built to go that fast. To put a bunch of fragile bodies made of flesh and blood in a metal canister and hurtle them toward their destination was unnatural. On top of that, the constant noise wasn’t good for the ears or the mind, was it? And what would happen to the vulnerable people inside if there were ever a crash?

Railroad travel in the 19th century must have seemed completely bonkers. For people used to traveling no faster than the speed of a horse — which was under the rider’s control at all times — being transported around the country in a loud mechanical contraption that moved as fast as 60 miles per hour was not just frightening; people feared that the experience pushed the human body and mind beyond their limits.

The effects of railroad collisions were new and somewhat mysterious to doctors. The prominent British doctor John Erichsen wrote a book on Railway and Other Injuries of the Nervous System detailing the symptoms of “railway spine.” He concluded that people who had endured a railway accident — even a mild one — could suffer long-term physical and mental symptoms, even when they had no outward sign of injury. The novel nature of railroad travel compounded the suffering of the victims:

The rapidity of the movement, the momentum of the persons injured, the suddenness of its arrest, the helplessness of the sufferers, and the natural perturbation of the mind that must disturb the bravest are all circumstances that, of necessity, greatly increase the severity resulting to the nervous system.

Survivors of railroad accidents often sued the railways for compensation for their physical suffering. Over time, they started to sue for their mental symptoms, too, whether or not they’d suffered an accident. People with no physical injuries won settlements because they had horrible dreams associated with the train or were too anxious to go back to work.

Some came to believe that the repeated experience of train travel — the noise, the crowding, the stress, the danger — could break someone mentally. Stories began to spread about “railway madmen” who, in the unique conditions of the train, could strip off all their clothes or commit shocking acts of violence. It was hard to say what was more frightening: the possibility that you might encounter a lunatic on the train or the worry that the vibrations of a rail journey might unsettle your own nervous system and drive you to do something unthinkable.

Railway spine and its attendant mental maladies were just some of the ways in which urbanized, industrial society threatened the human mind. For the last two centuries, humans have been fearful that our rapidly changing technological environments — so different from humanity’s “natural” surroundings — are going to break our brains. Today, we worry about diminishing attention spans, depression, and anxiety, but our modern concerns are echoes of previous fears over railway madness and the other mental disorders of industrial life.

What did 19th-and early-20th-century luminaries like Theodore Roosevelt, Jane Addams, William James, Walt Whitman, and Frederic Remington have in common? They all suffered from something called neurasthenia.

The man who coined the term, George Beard, described it in 1869 as “exhaustion of the nervous system.” And what exhausted the nervous systems of 19th-century Americans?

The chief and primary cause of this development and very rapid increase of nervousness is modern civilization, which is distinguished from the ancient by these five characteristics: steam-power, the periodical press, the telegraph, the sciences, and the mental activity of women.

A doctor named S. Weir Mitchell identified the problem as “wear and tear,” which stemmed not just from the new technology but from an increasingly rapacious form of capitalism:

That in one and another way the cruel competition for the dollar, the new and exacting habits of business, the racing speed which the telegraph and railway have introduced into commercial life, the new value which great fortunes have come to possess as means towards social advancement, and the overeducation and overstraining of our young people, have brought about some great and growing evils.

And how did these evils manifest in American society? Beard saw them everywhere:

Among the signs of American nervous­ness specially worthy of attention are the following: The nervous diathesis; susceptibility to stimulants and narcotics and various drugs, and consequent necessity of temperance; increase of the nervous diseases inebriety and nemasthenia (nervous exhaus­tion), hay-fever, neuralgia, nervous dyspepsia… early and rapid decay of teeth; premature baldness; sen­sitiveness to cold and heat; increase of diseases not exclusively nervous, as diabetes and certain forms of Bright’s disease of the kidneys and chronic catarrhs; unprecedented beauty of American women; frequency of trance and muscle-reading; the strain of dentition, puberty, and change of life; American oratory, humor, speech, and language; change in type of disease during the past half century, and the greater intensity of animal life on this continent.

Neurasthenia seemed to concentrate in two groups of people who were starting to behave in ways that might have been frowned upon in previous eras: “ultracompetitive businessmen and socially active women.” Beard especially thought the growing class of “brain-workers” was susceptible to the disease.

By living their new modern lifestyles, people were in danger of using up their nervous system’s supply of energy. Their brains, the experts warned, would simply give out.

So let’s say you found yourself afflicted with the symptoms of neurasthenia — your hair was falling out, you were too hot to too cold, or you were simply too anxious. What could you do to heal yourself?

The cure depended on the patient’s gender. Men were sent west, while women were forced to rest.

When men like Theodore Roosevelt and Walt Whitman went to their doctors complaining of nervousness and exhaustion, they were often told to go outdoors — preferably to the rugged American West. Doctors like Mitchell believed that urbanized, intellectual lives had weakened these men:

The man who lives an out-door life — who sleeps with the stars visible above him — who wins his bodily subsistence at first hand from the earth and waters — is a being who defies rain and sun, has a strange sense of elastic strength, may drink if he likes, and may smoke all day long, and feel none the worse for it. Some such return to the earth for the means of life is what gives vigor and developing power to the colonist of an older race cast on a land like ours. A few generations of men living in such fashion store up a capital of vitality which accounts largely for the prodigal activity displayed by their descendants, and made possible only by the sturdy contest with Nature which their ancestors have waged. That such a life is still led by multitudes of our countrymen is what alone serves to keep up our pristine force and energy.

Roosevelt’s time in the Dakotas became the stuff of legend; he went there an asthmatic, anxious, and, some said, effeminate young man and returned a paragon of vitality.

But if you were a woman, you were expected to take to your bed. Mitchell saw the increasing social and intellectual activity of young women as dangerous to their mental health and a threat to their reproductive abilities:

It were better not to educate girls at all between the ages of fourteen and eighteen, unless it can be done with careful reference to their bodily health. To-day, the American woman is, to speak plainly, too often physically unfit for her duties as woman, and is perhaps of all civilized females the least qualified to undertake those weightier tasks which tax so heavily the nervous system of man. She is not fairly up to what nature asks from her as wife and mother. How will she sustain herself under the pressure of those yet more exacting duties which nowadays she is eager to share with the man?

Women’s mental problems were, Mitchell argued, due to a lack of fat and blood. They needed to rest and eat a prescribed diet to recover from neurasthenia. Women might be isolated in a bedroom and forced to alternate between a “milk diet” that did not provide enough calories and a diet heavy in fat and iron (complete with delicious treats like cod-liver oil and raw beef soup). Charlotte Perkins Gilman based the short story “The Yellow Wallpaper” on her experience under the “care” of Mitchell and other doctors.

But most people couldn’t afford to spend months on a dude ranch or locked in a room. They needed a quicker fix. That’s where the “nerve tonic” came in. These drugs supposedly improved the “tone” of nerves that were made flabby and exhausted by modern life. Nobody really knew what was in them. Some contained a relatively ineffective but harmless brew of herbs, but others contained powerful narcotics like morphine or cocaine, or harmful poisons like strychnine. The less-well-off neurasthenic might just rely on a swig of nerve tonic to take the edge off after a long day at work.

It’s easy to chuckle at the clumsy attempts of people in the past to grapple with the effects of modern life, but are we all that different today? We no longer diagnose people with neurasthenia or railway madness, but we still worry about the ways in which new technology and the accelerating pace of life affect our brains. Nowadays, we give these afflictions different labels: ADHD, anxiety, internet addiction disorder, etc.

Just as in the 19th century, we know that we’re living in a world that our bodies and minds aren’t really built for. We feel the effects of our struggle to adapt to our new circumstances, and we worry that our new technologies are warping our brains. It feels better to place a definitive-seeming medical diagnosis on these problems than to simply know that our world makes us feel bad.

I’m not arguing that modern diagnoses aren’t real, or that people shouldn’t seek treatment for them. But it’s useful to understand that every era has invented its own language for the stresses associated with rapid technological and social change — and searched for remedies, some of which are helpful and some of which are not.

As in the 19th century, the diseases of modernity that we suffer from represent our fears and anxieties about the world that we’ve built and our ability to live within it.

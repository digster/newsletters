On Boxing Day in 1920, 53,000 spectators [ https://substack.com/redirect/7b14ee53-f1ec-4365-9478-65d5208525f3?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] crowded into Goodison Park in Liverpool for a football (soccer) match. It was a sellout; 14,000 more people wandered around outside the stadium, having been turned away. It was a stunning crowd, drawing more spectators than the FA Cup final had earlier that year.
A big crowd at a game on a holiday; nothing unusual about that. But one thing stood out about this contest: the spectators weren’t there to watch Everton Football Club, the stadium’s usual occupants; they were there to see a contest between two women’s teams.
The game, an unofficial women’s national championship, featured the powerhouse teams of Dick, Kerr Ladies and St. Helens Ladies. On the day, Dick, Kerr dominated, winning 4–0. The team’s star was Lily Parr [ https://substack.com/redirect/73dbfed9-782d-46f9-9d9e-5a9dbf84a0e9?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ], who had recently transferred from St. Helens to Dick, Kerr.
A newspaper enthused [ https://substack.com/redirect/41b243e0-a00d-407c-8cf2-148564fe457a?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] over her potential:
There is probably no greater football prodigy in the whole country. Not only has she speed and excellent ball control, but her admirable physique enables her to brush off challenges from defenders who tackle her. She amazes the crowd where ever she goes by the way she swings the ball clean across the goalmouth to the opposite wing.
Parr was a chain-smoking, openly gay defender-turned-winger who stood nearly six feet tall. A story [ https://substack.com/redirect/f35b30fe-6955-459e-8652-19c6094fea0c?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] was circulating that, after a male goalie had challenged her to try her luck against him in goal, her shot had broken his arm. She was only 15 years old on that Boxing Day in Goodison Park.
How did women’s football become so popular in an age of strict gender roles and sexism? And why did it begin to die out soon after that match in Liverpool? The answer has to do with war, money, and chauvinism.
In 1918, as the First World War came to a close, British Prime Minister David Lloyd-George gave tribute [ https://substack.com/redirect/ed7a11bb-2154-4334-aad0-a9e63c72cecb?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] to the service of women in wartime:
If it had not been for the splendid manner in which the women came forward to work in hospitals, in munition factories, on the land, in administrative offices of all kinds, and in war work behind the lines, often in daily danger of their lives, Great Britain and, as I believe, all the Allies would have been unable to withstand the enemy attacks during the past few months. For this service to our common cause humanity owes them unbounded gratitude.
This was, he noted, a surprise:
This war was begun in order that force and brutality might crush out freedom among men. Its authors cannot have foreseen that one of its main effects would be to give to women a commanding position and influence in the public affairs of the world.
Indeed, the war opened the door to rapid and unanticipated social change. A country that had, not long before, elevated the “cult of domesticity” — which dictated that respectable women should avoid public life and devote themselves to their homes and their children — suddenly needed women to step into new roles. Because an entire generation of men was shipped off to fight and die in the trenches, women, especially those in their 20s, postponed marriage and children and instead were asked to step into the country’s manufacturing jobs.
Though working-class women had worked in some industries, especially textiles, before the war, they could now perform all sorts of jobs previously reserved for men. Women worked [ https://substack.com/redirect/2f8c2aff-1098-4f8c-8f37-c82ab9b070c6?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] as police officers, train conductors, and, most crucially, in munitions factories. The nearly one million “munitionettes” — perhaps 5% of all women in the UK — worked in dangerous, dirty, physically taxing positions to produce the shells and bullets that kept the war effort going.
The munitionettes and other female workers quickly demonstrated that, contrary to Victorian ideas about frail, hysterical women [ https://substack.com/redirect/649d7a27-4409-404f-b069-3200fcced89e?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ], they were strong enough for difficult industrial work. As male factory workers had, munitionettes formed social groups. Some of these groups acted out plays, some performed musical numbers, and some played football.
Meet some of the members of the Palmers Munitionettes, the team associated with Palmers Shipbuilding Company Limited, in Hebburn-on-Tyne, near Newcastle.
“Works teams” made up of coworkers were quite common for men, as well. Many well-known football behemoths started out this way in the 19th or early 20th century — Manchester United, for example, began life as the works team for the Carriage and Wagon department of a particular depot of the Lancashire and Yorkshire Railway, playing against other LYR departments or rival railway companies. This was the way it worked for women’s teams, as well. Dick, Kerr Ladies, the team that won the sold-out match at Goodison, was the works team for Dick, Kerr Ltd., a company that made locomotives.
Not everyone was comfortable with women playing football. There was some fear that women weren’t up to the physical demands of the game. Baron Pierre de Coubertin, who founded the Olympics, argued [ https://substack.com/redirect/587176c7-f8d5-4cd1-9b30-2d76ca96e1fb?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] that, “no matter how toughened a sportswoman may be, her organism is not cut out to sustain certain shocks.” You’ll notice that in the picture above, the Palmers Munitionettes are accompanied by a nurse who stood ready to help them lest their frail frames succumb to the strain of sport.
Despite the misgivings of some men, the women’s sport flourished. Teams popped up all over the country. Spectators were desperate for a diversion from the war news and flocked to women’s games; it helped that the proceeds from many of these games were donated to wartime charities, so that everyone could feel that their afternoon out was for the greater good.
By 1917–1918, there were enough women’s teams to hold a “Munitionettes’ Cup,” which was won in front of 20,000 fans by the Blyth Spartans, which went undefeated for the entire season. Their star striker, Bella Reay, [ https://substack.com/redirect/efb9fb83-66e8-4156-bdb2-fbe44172f3ac?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] averaged four goals a game.
Then the war ended. Would the changes to women’s lives last when the soldiers came back home? Some things did change. Lloyd-George, in his speech about women’s contribution to the war effort, went on to say that
In the past I have heard it said that women were not fit for the vote because they would be weak when it came to understanding the issues and bearing the strains of a great war. My recent experience in South Wales confirmed me in the conviction that the women there understand perfectly what is at stake in this war….
Indeed, it was hard to deny (at least some) women the vote after all they’d done for their country. But men were eager to reclaim their jobs, and that meant that women had to go. Parliament passed the Restoration of Pre-War Practices Act, which, in the name of appeasing trade unions, removed women from jobs that they had been able to perform during the war.
And what about football? For a couple of years, even after men’s professional football started back up, women kept playing, often in front of sizeable crowds. The new teams had built a real following, and their spectators continued to show up.
But, as women moved (or were forced) back into more traditional roles, their participation in football began to seem more anomalous. Some men’s teams became frustrated that they were outdrawn by women’s matches on some occasions. It may not have helped that, after the war, several women’s teams got political and raised [ https://substack.com/redirect/587176c7-f8d5-4cd1-9b30-2d76ca96e1fb?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] money for striking mine workers.
Finally, in 1921, the Football Association — the governing body of the sport in England — issued the following statement [ https://substack.com/redirect/b94541f8-470d-451b-9501-024c7abcc72a?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] accusing the women’s teams of, among other things, failing to donate enough to charity:
Complaints have been made as to football being played by women, the Council feel impelled to express their strong opinion that the game of football is quite unsuitable for females and ought not to be encouraged.
Complaints have also been made as to the conditions under which some of these matches have been arranged and played, and the appropriation of the receipts to other than Charitable objects.
The Council are further of opinion that an excessive proportion of the receipts are absorbed in expenses and an inadequate percentage devoted to Charitable objects.
For those reasons the Council request the Clubs belonging to the Association to refuse the use of their grounds for such matches.
Women weren’t banned from playing football, exactly, but they couldn’t hold their matches in any of the stadiums in England capable of hosting a significant crowd.
Some women’s teams kept playing, but most, disconnected from the factories where their players had once worked and deprived of a way to raise money or get exposure, withered.
The famous Dick, Kerr Ladies team kept going for a while. It undertook a well-attended tour of the United States and played on unofficial pitches in the UK, sometimes attracting a few thousand spectators. The team eventually changed its name to Preston Ladies F.C., playing teams from around the continent and sometimes claiming to be women’s world champions. Lily Parr, the fearsome teenage winger, became a mental health nurse who continued to play for the team until she retired from football at the age of 45, in 1951.
In 1962, a women’s team formed in Accrington. They submitted an application to the F.A., which ruled against them. The F.A. spokesman told [ https://substack.com/redirect/f1d01c40-c0e8-4e09-b946-5855000a6872?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] the press that
It may well be that after 41 years women have a better idea how to control the finances of a match. But they have not changed their shape. Soccer is not a game for girls.
It wasn’t 1969 that the F.A. allowed women’s teams to form a Women’s Football Association. In 1971, the ban officially ended.
By that time, Lily Parr, once a fearsome teenage phenom, was 66 years old. Two generations of women had been unable to follow in her footsteps. The story of women’s football in her lifetime is a reminder of how quickly progress can come — and how easily it can be lost.

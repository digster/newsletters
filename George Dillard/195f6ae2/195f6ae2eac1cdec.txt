On July 27, 1859, the New York Times reported [ https://substack.com/redirect/6a8c5809-e366-4e99-99dc-a76037a142de?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] breathlessly on a shocking act of violence. Eighty-seven armed men had descended on a New York City neighborhood:
All were armed with pistols, clubs, or daggers, and a few carried pickaxes and crowbars… A long train of carts and wagons, in which it was intended that articles contraband taken from the enemy should be carried away, followed them. The City Inspector’s force, formed in two divisions, marched first, and then after them came the Police. At Sixth Avenue the two divisions separated; one marched up Sixth Avenue and the other marched up Seventh Avenue, and it was arranged that the two columns should meet at the top of the rocky eminence bounded by the Sixth and Seventh avenues, Fifty-seventh and Fifty-eighth streets, whose summit is crowned by the castle — if it be true that every man’s shanty is his castle — of James McCormick, the king of the offal-boilers.
Those of you who know New York City may understand that the location these men stormed is in the heart of Manhattan. Carnegie Hall sits on 7th Avenue between 56th and 57th Streets, right where the assault occurred. Broadway is less than a block to the west, the Museum of Modern Art is a couple of blocks to the south, and Central Park is a few blocks north. This is an area that’s usually full of tourists or people out for a fancy meal.
But in 1859, this part of midtown Manhattan was different. Central Park had just opened the year before. Carnegie Hall would not be built for another three decades. This area had more pigs than people. Residents often called it “Stinktown.”
These conditions were not unusual for cities in the nineteenth century, which were full of animals. The transportation systems, of course, relied on horses. By the late 1800s, New York [ https://substack.com/redirect/5b92d8f1-ffec-4f2c-943f-69815a7b27aa?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] housed between 100,000 and 200,000 horses and donkeys, tens of thousands of stray dogs [ https://substack.com/redirect/1f322c26-3f69-448a-91f5-6a8ad0a30e07?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] and cats, chickens, and, yes, many thousands of pigs.
To lots of New York residents, the presence of these animals was necessary and normal. The horses and other beasts of burden kept the city moving. Its economy would have simply shut down without the presence of animals to pull carts and carry loads. Many families relied on pigs as a source of income and food — a working-class family might raise a pig or two, feeding it food scraps, and then sell or eat it. Pigs and dogs also carried out a useful waste-disposal service. In the days before New York had any garbage collection or street-cleaning services, roving animals gulped down the organic waste that many households just threw out into the street.
But the animals posed problems, as well. The horses of New York City may have created something like 1 million kilograms of manure [ https://substack.com/redirect/5b92d8f1-ffec-4f2c-943f-69815a7b27aa?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] every day, much of which was left to fester on the streets. Added to this were the bodies of dead animals — you try moving a 1,000-pound horse corpse. And then there were the industries associated with the animals — the butcher shops, the tanneries [ https://substack.com/redirect/932f630e-d97c-4bff-a227-4f19f01a7ef3?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ], the renderers, and the offal-boilers, who were apparently ruled by James McCormick, the man targeted by the police that day in 1859.
On top of that, “Stinktown” smelled horrible, and it wasn’t just a cosmetic issue. Because nineteenth-century people believed in the miasma theory [ https://substack.com/redirect/f5080f3a-3662-45e5-94d2-39c37016dce1?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] of disease (in which smelly air, not germs, caused illness), many New Yorkers saw the porcine residents of midtown as a threat to their lives.
The war over pigs in New York was, in many ways, a class struggle. Less-wealthy residents needed pigs to supplement their incomes and feed their families. Poor neighborhoods kept themselves going with the smelly, messy industries that turned parts of those pigs into food and consumer goods. People had long lived in close proximity to animals and their waste. But wealthier residents found the sights and smells of the Piggery District [ https://substack.com/redirect/99f27163-c800-4a4a-91c0-c681c42f4e7d?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] offensive, especially so close to the grand new park.
They didn’t want their city to smell, literally, like shit.

Must we feed the poor at the expense of human flesh?
When the police stormed James McCormick’s sty, the struggle over urban pigs was decades old. An 1817 law [ https://substack.com/redirect/43fe8f0e-e6ff-4c07-8f02-cfe31475542c?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] made it illegal to let your pigs roam the streets unsupervised; loose pigs would be taken to a pound. There was an immediate outcry from aggrieved pig owners, and the law was repealed. Then the mayor — a man with the delicious name of Cadwallader Colden — decided to bring the whole matter before the courts.
He searched for — and found — an egregious example of poor urban hog-keeping: a butcher named Christian Harriet. According to legal scholar Hendrik Hartog [ https://substack.com/redirect/43fe8f0e-e6ff-4c07-8f02-cfe31475542c?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ], Harriet’s pigs were accused of the following atrocities:
(1) hogs attacked children, (2) boys got into trouble by riding hogs, (3) ladies had been compelled to view swine copulating in open view, and (4) hogs defecated on people.
The case rested on an intriguing legal question: even if there was no longer a law against letting pigs roam the streets, could the government find Harriet guilty of creating a porcine nuisance? Both sets of lawyers engaged in delightfully flowery arguments.
Harriet’s lawyers argued that this was really about class:
[A] great convenience, and almost an essential source on the score of provisions would be taken from the poor or less opulent of the citizens by a conviction… the dandies, who are too delicate to endure the sight, or even the idea of so odious a creature, might exult; but many poor families might experience far different sensations, and be driven to beggary or the Alms House for a portion of that subsistence of which a conviction in this case would deprive them.
The mayor’s lawyers argued that the pigs were a nuisance. They said that the threats to human health outweighed the rights of the poor to keep pigs: “It is said, that if we restrain swine from running in the street, we shall injure the poor. Why, gentlemen! must we feed the poor at the expense of human flesh?”
They also argued that cities should be free of the indignities of the natural world. Ladies should be able to “walk abroad through the streets of the city without encountering the most disgusting spectacles of those animals indulging the propensities of nature.”
In the end, City Hall won. Christian Harriet was ordered to pay a token fine of $1. But, though Mayor Colden had proved that he could prosecute people for letting their pigs roam the streets, the city didn’t take serious action on the issue because of the possibility of pushback from the city’s poor.
Decades later, the pigs were still there. Charles Dickens remarked on New York’s pigs in his 1848 book Martin Chuzzlewit [ https://substack.com/redirect/3fceba56-b64a-48b3-9916-844ced2fa0fa?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] — a character encounters “four accidental pigs” near “a handsome street” called Broadway. They’re soon “joined by two or three friends from the next street, in company with whom they lay down sociably in the gutter.” A man sued [ https://substack.com/redirect/43fe8f0e-e6ff-4c07-8f02-cfe31475542c?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] the city the same year for failing to contain the pig problem — his son had been killed by a rampaging hog.
New York periodically tried to deal with its pig problem over the first three-fifths of the century. There were attempted purges in 1830 and 1847 that had no real effect. Each time, pig owners fought the police or hid their swine indoors. Another effort, after a cholera outbreak in 1849, was more serious. The police seized [ https://substack.com/redirect/012bc347-e4e0-4904-a5c7-97c799e836e1?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] five or six thousand pigs and banished the remaining 20,000 further north, to the less urban areas outside of the city proper.
Many of them ended up in what became “Stinktown.”
Very shabby for gentlemen
Ten years later, New York City appointed a new Commissioner of Health, Daniel DeLavan [ https://substack.com/redirect/b248f254-ec64-4575-b89f-6d10baf59b0f?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ], who decided enough was enough. He told pig owners to get rid of all pig pens, city-wide. Nobody did — why would they, when the city had never been all that serious about enforcing pig-related laws in the past?
But this time, the complaints of the city’s sensitive-nosed elites had led to real action. Colonel Richard Downing, Superintendent of Sanitary Inspection, went house-to-house with a force of 87 men. Though some of the pig owners had talked tough, threatening to shoot the lawmen, most backed down against the overwhelming force.
The Times describes [ https://substack.com/redirect/6a8c5809-e366-4e99-99dc-a76037a142de?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] the scene at one of the houses, laying plain the class and ethnic divisions that lay underneath the Piggery War:
At Patrick Bohen’s shanties… there were about fifty hogs. Here, as at the other places, the officers of the law entered, and amid the barking of dogs, the jabbering of Hibernian females, the noise of falling rubbish, and the grunting and squeaking of swine, plied their pickaxes and crowbars, scattered the disinfecting agent by the pailful, and drove out the pigs.
The wife of Thomas Glennan, another victim of the raid, said: “Very poor revenge to tear down people’s buildings afther the pigs is all sent away intirely. [sic] Very shabby for gentlemen; gentlemen wouldn’t do it.”
In the end, Stinktown was cleared of its hogs and their owners. Some moved far away, to rural areas in Pennsylvania or New Jersey. Some of the pigs relocated further uptown, north of 86th Street, which constituted the new urban frontier. Pigs were still tolerated that far north. An area in Harlem near 125th Street was known as “Pig’s Alley [ https://substack.com/redirect/012bc347-e4e0-4904-a5c7-97c799e836e1?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ]” until the end of the century.
In the twentieth century, animals finally left the streets of New York City. The city hired its first corps of cleaners in 1895 to clear the piles of horse manure and other trash from the streets. The pigs disappeared, then a lot of the stray dogs, then, finally, the horses.
Eventually, even people surrendered the streets to machines: cars, buses, and trucks dominated the spaces in New York and every other American city. The residents of New York had solved one set of environmental problems — the smells, disease, and danger that accompanied urban animals — only to replace them with another.

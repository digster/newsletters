After Charlotte Corday was sentenced to death for the murder of Jean-Paul Marat [ https://substack.com/redirect/fa763efe-eb62-4df1-9f23-b8ed6c4e01af?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ], she asked the court to give her time to have a portrait made. Corday seemed to know that she would soon lose control of her own life story — if she had not lost control of it already — and was determined to have her real self sketched before her life ended. 
The court granted her request, so she asked a soldier, Jean-Jacques Hauer, who had been making sketches of her during the trial, to draw her portrait. Hauer later turned the drawing into a painting:
Soon after, Corday was taken in a cart to the guillotine. Lest the gathered crowd have any questions about why this pretty young woman was being sent to her death, she was dressed in a garment that made her crime clear. She wore to her execution a bright red shirt that designated her as a convicted murderer, as you can see in this 19th-century painting of the guards preparing her for execution:
Even in the final moments of the victims of the Terror, appearance mattered. Murderers like Corday were forced to wear a garment that made their crime clear to the spectators who gathered to watch them die. Their clothing was also functional: most of the 17,000 people [ https://substack.com/redirect/d8bc8f55-ebf5-4028-ad9d-997496e0bfa7?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] who were guillotined during the Terror had their hair cut short and they wore a shirt with a low or torn collar, so as not to obstruct the path of the blade. 
Some prominent victims got to choose their outfit: Louis XVI wore a brown coat with breeches. Marie Antoinette, executed after her husband, wanted to wear a widow’s black dress. That request was denied by revolutionary authorities who didn’t want her to seem sympathetic. She was forced instead to wear a simple white chemise — the garment that the poorest criminals wore, and one not that different from the dress she wore in the controversial 1783 portrait we discussed last week [ https://substack.com/redirect/98b7178b-d431-4e19-9985-198078295b93?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ]: 
And then a strange thing happened: when the fever broke and the Terror ended, the haircuts and garments associated with the guillotine became strangely fashionable. Men and women started to get rid of their elaborate powdered hairstyles and embrace the “Titus cut.” Named after the character of Titus in Voltaire’s play about the ancient Roman Brutus, it bore an uncanny resemblance to the short haircuts that guillotine victims had been given. Some people started calling it the coiffure à la guillotine. 
It also became fashionable for wealthy women to dress in simple white dresses that resembled the clothing that victims might have worn to their executions. Some even wore red choker necklaces, as if to indicate where the guillotine might have severed their necks:
This may have been the result of the shifting tides of fashion — after all, the trend toward simpler hairstyles and clothing was in line with revolutionary culture — but some saw this as a way to make an ironic reference to the recently concluded Terror. There were even reports of “victim’s balls” where aristocrats gathered, wearing red ribbons around their necks, to drink and dance and mourn their dead relatives (though the prevalence of these events has probably been exaggerated [ https://substack.com/redirect/694098b0-fa65-45c2-ae2d-43568aa0a1bd?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] in popular history, and historians argue [ https://substack.com/redirect/bbbadb48-ee73-4fba-9cbb-5ccffafe4e4f?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] about whether there is sufficient evidence that they really happened).
The exhilaration of the elites who survived the Terror — and their optimism that, after the Thermidorian Reaction had removed the radical Jacobins from power, they would be able to reclaim their place at the top of the social and economic hierarchy — led to a backlash against revolutionary fashions. 
Young aristocrats started to strut through the streets of Paris again. These privileged young people (who, depending on your viewpoint, you might think of either as victims of trauma celebrating their survival or arrogant elites thumbing their noses at the people’s revolution) decided to flaunt their opposition to revolutionary values through their attitude (haughty), their behavior (they spoke with over-the-top posh accents [ https://substack.com/redirect/f2379810-3c06-4ac0-91d7-73aa1ba38240?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ]), and, most of all, their clothing. 
These elites (the men were known as Incroyables, or Incredibles, and the women were Merveilleuses, or Lady Marvels) were playing a game — how could they signal their disdain for the revolution without getting in trouble? Men affected elaborate jackets, skin-tight breeches, and lots of accessories, all of which would have marked them as counterrevolutionaries in 1793:
Their female counterparts wore thin, often see-through dresses that scandalized more conventional tastes:
This cartoon mocks the fashion as a “blind” man “accidentally” steps on a rich woman’s dress, tearing it and exposing her naked buttocks:
The excesses of the Incroyables soon got old, even for them, and fashion during the Directory period (1795-1799) settled into a sort of compromise. 
Theresa Tallien, a noblewoman who had been imprisoned during the Terror, had a portrait made of herself in jail. Here she makes the simple prisoner’s garments, and even the fact that she’s had her hair chopped off (she holds it in her hands), seem fashionable. 
During the Directory period, she and some of her associates, including Josephine Bonaparte, Napoleon’s wife, established a new “Directory style.” It was inspired by the revolutionaries’ embrace of neoclassical influences, as you can see in this portrait of Madame de Verninac:
And this one of Tallien:
Dressing like this was still seen as decadent by many people — Talleyrand said [ https://substack.com/redirect/34b64221-9d4a-4ffe-a715-d48be84466f8?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] of Tallien that “One could not be more sumptuously unclothed!" But the new fashions were liberating for women. Historian Anne Higgonet [ https://substack.com/redirect/03d15304-46d5-4f92-9f22-8ad6efc4408a?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] writes that
If you had been laced into stays, burdened with petticoats, and festooned in silk gowns all your life, the revolutionary dress felt like miraculous relief. You were suddenly freed from constriction, load, and drag. You felt weightless and mobile. Compared to what you had been obliged to wear before, the new dress radically changed the spatial relationship between your clothes and your body. Revolutionary dresses did not merely tweak style. The combination of their fabric and construction allowed women to move. Women could feel their dynamic potential from inside their clothing. Their movement could be seen from the outside by others. Revolutionary fashion brought a very basic individual and social freedom.
Theoretically, these outfits were accessible to ordinary women, too, but wealthy people like Tallien made sure to accessorize in ways that made it clear they had money. She once visited the Bonapartes wearing 8 sapphire rings and a ruby tiara. 
For her part, Josephine, the future empress, took a more luxurious approach to fashion. Though she could be seen in simple revolutionary dresses at times:
She often favored more exotic clothes, adorned with colorful toucan feathers [ https://substack.com/redirect/03d15304-46d5-4f92-9f22-8ad6efc4408a?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] and jewels. An 1801 print shows Tallien, Bonaparte, and a compatriot representing the “three graces” of fashion — from the simpler neoclassical style on the left to Josephine’s more exotic style in the middle and more modern attire on the right. 
Before the French Revolution, the silk industry (or “fabrique”) in Lyon had been quite profitable, mostly because it sold its luxury fabrics to the aristocracy. You’ll recall that Marie Antoinette’s infamous cotton chemise was scandalous partly because she was expected to support the French silk industry with her clothing purchases.
After the revolution came, the silks produced by the fabrique became unfashionable and even dangerous — to swan around Paris in fancy silks was to risk being seen as a counterrevolutionary. The silk industry fell apart, which is one reason why Lyon became a center of resistance to the revolution, leading to a bloody siege by the revolutionary government in 1793:
Silk garments became nearly worthless for a while. At one point, wool was more valuable [ https://substack.com/redirect/2b762d91-9a95-44ab-adc7-328ed581f7d3?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] than silk, and the government sold off royal garments for 1% of their former value. 
But, once he had overthrown the Directory and consolidated his power over France, Napoleon tried to combine revolutionary values with an old monarchical approach to power. 
One sign that the revolution had ended and something else had started was, once again, fashion. The new emperor revived the silk industry that Marie Antoinette had supposedly endangered. He instructed [ https://substack.com/redirect/9899ea30-db86-4472-81f7-4eb0480904d5?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] his officials to wear silk outfits like the one below and placed huge orders for silk decorations for his palaces. 
Wealthy women wore clothes with simpler, revolution-inspired lines, but made out of fancier silks:
The new era would not go all the way back to pre-revolutionary splendor, but as Napoleon’s officials swanned around in Lyon silk, everyone could see, as they’d been able to throughout the revolutionary period, which way the winds were blowing by simply looking at the clothing people wore.

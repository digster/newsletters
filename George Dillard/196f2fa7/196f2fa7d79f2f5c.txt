Quick, what do you know about Gouverneur Morris?
If you’ve heard of Morris at all, you likely know just one thing: that he was a founding father, and therefore worthy of your reverence.
I get the sense that most Americans picture the founding fathers as a bunch of staid, gentlemanly visionaries who changed history with their radical ideas about freedom and independence. And, yes, they certainly did change the course of human events.
But these were men, not demigods, and Morris [ https://substack.com/redirect/eb0d8f94-1a6e-4009-875b-2cc3271f3373?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] is a good example of how the color has been washed out of our understanding of the founding fathers:
He had a disfigured arm from a childhood accident with boiling water and had one leg amputated in 1780 after a violent carriage accident. The doctor told him after the surgery that the amputation had been unnecessary(!) Morris spent the rest of his life hobbling around on a peg leg.
Despite his physical ailments, he was a notorious womanizer. Some of Morris’ contemporaries spread the rumor that he had actually broken his leg jumping out of a woman’s window to escape her husband. That may not have been true, but he carried on many torrid affairs with married women. John Jay, in a great understated quip, once expressed the wish that Morris “had lost something else [ https://substack.com/redirect/eb0d8f94-1a6e-4009-875b-2cc3271f3373?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ]” in his accident.
When he finally married, it was to his housekeeper, more than two decades his junior, who had been accused of killing her baby (which may have been Morris’ child, conceived out of wedlock).
He wouldn’t shut up, making more speeches than anyone else at the Constitutional Convention. But he did provide a valuable service as one of the final editors of the Constitution, cleaning up the language and making the document flow better.
He was a big advocate of unity among the states, changing the Preamble’s beginning from “We the people of the States of…” (and then listing all the states) to “We the People of the United States.”
He passionately opposed slavery, saying [ https://substack.com/redirect/3e2220df-0528-4de1-ab0d-a1bc214a66ed?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] at the Convention that “It was a nefarious institution. It was the curse of heaven on the States where it prevailed.”
Despite his advocacy for unity, he encouraged his fellow New Yorkers to secede from the union during the War of 1812, believing that the war was a lost cause and had been engineered by the southern states.
And he died the worst death of any of the founders, when he tried to do some DIY surgery to clear up a urinary blockage. His whalebone catheter didn’t work (he seems to have grievously injured his genitals), and he ended up dying of a painful infection in 1816.
I tell you all of this not only because it’s a captivating life story but to illustrate the fact that Gouverneur Morris, like all of the founders, was very, very human. He had some wonderful ideas and some terrible ones. He combined the bravery necessary for revolution with political ambition and a messy personal life.
So when did Americans decide that guys like Gouverneur Morris were secular saints?
Technically, Warren G. Harding — not exactly one of America’s great political figures — coined the term “founding fathers.” He was the first prominent person to use the phrase in a convention speech in 1916 and again in his 1921 inauguration speech [ https://substack.com/redirect/afd2946e-0a76-4de7-b194-f9f858615001?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ]:
Standing in this presence, mindful of the solemnity of this occasion, feeling the emotions which no one may know until he senses the great weight of responsibility for himself, I must utter my belief in the divine inspiration of the founding fathers. Surely there must have been God’s intent in the making of this new-world Republic.
But others had referred to the politicians of the Revolutionary and Constitutional eras as fathers of the country long before that.
John Adams understood early on that the country would make myths about his colleagues. He wrote [ https://substack.com/redirect/c1097e2a-7292-467b-9e41-dd7b7a438a1c?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] in 1790 that
The history of our Revolution will be one continued lie from one end to the other. The essence of the whole will be that Dr. Franklin’s electrical rod smote the earth and out sprung George Washington.
Within a few decades, his prediction was coming true. Adams wrote [ https://substack.com/redirect/5a81e6cb-47bc-40e3-ac30-0db4d3103988?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] in an 1811 letter that his generation was receiving too much praise:
I ought not to object to your Reverence for your Fathers as you call them, meaning I presume the Government and those concerned in the Direction of public affairs, much less can I be displeased at your numbring me among them. But to tell you a very great secret, as far as I am capable of comparing the Merit of the different Periods, I have no reason to believe that We were better than you are. We had as many poor Creatures and selfish Beings, in proportion among us as you have among you: nor were there then more enlightened Men, or in greater Number in proportion than there are now.
Adams wouldn’t get his wish. The next generation of American leaders found it useful to engage in hagiography. His own son talked [ https://substack.com/redirect/7606a80c-b067-4d81-a120-df96cf6eb3ca?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] about the Constitution as the work of America’s “forefathers… [who were] the most eminent men” in his 1825 inauguration speech.
By the 1820s, artists were creating a visual hagiography of the founders. John Trumbull’s paintings of the signing of the Declaration of Independence (at the top of this post) and George Washington resigning his commission (below) were both placed in the rotunda of the Capitol during that decade. Trumbull used the techniques he had learned as a painter of scenes from the Bible to make two scenes with very little visual interest (a bunch of guys signing a document and a man quitting his job) more compelling. Trumbull tried to emphasize the “silence [ https://substack.com/redirect/afd2946e-0a76-4de7-b194-f9f858615001?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] and solemnity of the scene” around the signing of the Declaration, casting central figures like Jefferson, Adams, and Washington in a heroic light.
But Americans in the early 19th century [ https://substack.com/redirect/fd3593ac-bbd9-4fa8-8f97-ed18a4e7ab05?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] didn’t all revere the founders. Jacksonian politics of the “common man” dismissed aristocrats like Washington and Jefferson, and the messy compromises they’d made were in danger of falling apart as the country expanded and the struggle over slavery became fiercer.
As the nation tumbled toward civil war, leaders who wanted to keep the union intact tried once again to turn toward the founders as a unifying force. Abraham Lincoln in his 1860 Cooper Union speech [ https://substack.com/redirect/468ab2f9-00ee-4b49-aec4-b766fcbb85c8?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] said that
It is surely safe to assume that the thirty-nine framers of the original Constitution, and the seventy-six members of the Congress which framed the amendments thereto, taken together, do certainly include those who may be fairly called “our fathers who framed the Government under which we live.”
And in his most famous speech, the Gettysburg Address [ https://substack.com/redirect/42135bf2-3de2-4549-b9ab-20a2cbd44ae2?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ], he again venerated the founding generation:
Four score and seven years ago our fathers brought forth on this continent, a new nation, conceived in Liberty, and dedicated to the proposition that all men are created equal.
The memory of the founders wasn’t enough to prevent civil war, but American leaders turned back toward them after the conflict as part of a civil religion that could heal the nation’s divisions.
The 100th anniversary of the Declaration of Independence in 1876 provided a perfect opportunity for a divided nation to unify itself with some mythmaking. George Washington, especially, became a figure of pop-culture adulation, as people bought up paintings, badges, medallions, and statues [ https://substack.com/redirect/85acdd72-f211-4389-b564-451cd9fc2460?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] in his likeness. The 1876 Centennial Exposition [ https://substack.com/redirect/09ed9711-db2e-4d94-bbc8-871adc760324?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] in Philadelphia combined the wonders of the Second Industrial Revolution with a commemoration of the founders in the city where they had signed the Declaration of Independence.
Authors of the late 19th century wrote admiring biographies [ https://substack.com/redirect/fd3593ac-bbd9-4fa8-8f97-ed18a4e7ab05?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] of men like Washington and Franklin. Henry Cabot Lodge’s biography of Alexander Hamilton gives us a sense of the tone of these books:
He was a great orator and lawyer, and he was also the ablest political and constitutional writer of his day, a good soldier, and possessed of a wonderful capacity for organization and practical administration. He was a master in every field that he entered.
By the time Warren Harding first used the phrase “founding fathers” in the early 20th century, the reputation of the men he referred to was clear.
Each generation since has used the founding fathers for its own purposes.
During the Progressive era, Charles Beard’s An Economic Interpretation of the Constitution of the United States looked at the founders as if they were robber barons, arguing that many of their actions were more in the name of economic self-interest than freedom and democracy. The generations who lived through world wars and the depression looked to the founders for inspiration, taking solace in their courage. By the 1970s, revisionist histories were emphasizing the founders’ hypocrisies and shortcomings. And then Lin-Manuel Miranda remixed the founding generation for the Obama era with his musical Hamilton.
Now we find ourselves in a new period, one in which the values of our democracy are in danger and in which Americans are bitterly divided. Next year, we will celebrate the 250th anniversary of the signing of the Declaration of Independence. I have no doubt that the founders will get a great deal of attention over the next year — but how will we reshape their legacy for our own purposes this time around?

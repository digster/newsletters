On July 21, 1969, astronauts Neil Armstrong and Buzz Aldrin prepared to lift off from Tranquility Base on the surface of the Moon. Their journey was the culmination of years of work and billions of dollars in spending. The astronauts’ journey had captivated the world and would be trumpeted as a triumph of American ingenuity, capitalism, and democracy. It was the climax of the Space Race, a blow from which the Soviet space program would never recover and a feat that NASA would never be able to match. 
I’m sure you knew all that. But did you know that, at the very moment Aldrin and Armstrong were preparing for liftoff, another craft [ https://substack.com/redirect/e9bbdcc9-5ccb-4a45-b9af-1f3d58e6264d?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] plowed into the surface of the Moon, only 350 miles away from Tranquility Base? This was Luna 15, an unmanned Soviet probe designed to land on the Moon, collect some samples, and fly back to Earth. 
Luna 15 had been launched ahead of Apollo 11 in an attempt to bring material back from the Moon before the Americans could, although even the leaders of the Soviet space program would have to admit that collecting some dirt would pale in comparison to humans walking on the Moon. Sadly, the probe didn’t even achieve its goals. Technical problems [ https://substack.com/redirect/16836fda-6b2f-4500-bdab-3f4d7d69b085?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] kept it from reaching the Moon on schedule, and the landing went awry — the probe crashed into the side of a mountain at 300 miles an hour. 
Meanwhile, Buzz Aldrin and Neil Armstrong lifted off, their names secure in the history books. 
The Soviet attempt to reach the Moon seemed cursed at times. Its rocket designers got into a bitter feud [ https://substack.com/redirect/6d9c4abc-0a60-4d17-b456-b1a465888033?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ], and, as a result, the giant 30-engine N1 rocket ended up deeply flawed. Four N1s exploded in succession in launch tests, with one crash so catastrophic that it destroyed the entire launch pad, which would take more than a year to rebuild. After this disaster, the Soviets knew that they had no chance of beating the Americans to the Moon. All they could do was send a probe, and even that ended in embarrassment. 
The loss of Luna 15 seems almost too on-the-nose to be true, a damp squib of a finale of the Soviet bid for the moon. It, combined with the success of Apollo 11, was a terrible disappointment for the Soviet leadership, especially since they had reached almost every other space milestone before the Americans. Soviets had launched the first satellites, put the first living beings into orbit, sent the first man (and woman) into space, landed the first probe on the Moon, and conducted the first spacewalk. 
The Soviet space program was the pride of the nation until it wasn’t. And when the Soviets were proud of something, they put it on a stamp. Here, for example, is the stamp set commemorating Luna 16 — a probe similar to Luna 15 that, unlike its predecessor, actually succeeded in bringing back samples from the Moon:
Though the Soviet space program eventually fizzled, the country’s stamp game remained strong. Each achievement was commemorated with beautiful postage stamps that reflected the spirit of the times.

The humble postage stamp used to be a simple thing, a little square that showed that you’d paid for the right to send a letter. The first postage stamp with a picture — Queen Victoria’s — came out in 1840, more as a way to prevent counterfeiting than to glorify the monarch. As time went on, postal systems realized that, as long as they were putting pictures on the stamps, they might as well have a little fun. By the late 1800s, the commemorative stamp was commonplace, usually honoring a leader or a significant event. 
The political struggles of the twentieth century created a new demand for commemorative stamps as governments realized that they could be used to shape the opinions of people at home and abroad. Nobody was as aggressive in using stamps for propaganda as the Soviets. Their postage trumpeted their industrial progress, their bountiful harvests, their sporting achievements, their leadership, and, especially, their accomplishments in space.

After the firsts and the victories over the Americans started to slow down, Soviet stamp designers began to emphasize other virtues of the space program. One common theme was how the space program tied the Communist bloc together. The Soviets conducted launches involving cosmonauts from other countries, and the stampmakers used these as an opportunity to portray the Communist world as happily united.

These stamps tell the story of the Soviet space program’s trajectory — and perhaps the trajectory of manned space flight overall. The examples from the 1960s are grand and exciting, exulting in the thrill of historic firsts and new frontiers. But, over time, the horizons contracted a bit. By the 1980s, they were commemorating medical checkups:
And the ability to exercise and shower in space:
Once space travel became more commonplace — and once it was clear that the Soviet Union had come out second-best in the Space Race — the claims got much less grand. But they still represent a hopeful vision of what humanity can achieve in space, as well as a sometimes misleading image of the success of Soviet society and science. 
Take the case of Vladimir Komarov, who burned up on re-entry in 1967 while "cursing [ https://substack.com/redirect/ae490795-17eb-4ae4-9d22-3938a2f43fdd?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] the people who had put him inside a botched spaceship." Kormarov’s death, which many people had seen coming — a pre-flight inspection had revealed over 200 technical issues with his rocket — infuriated Yuri Gagarin, Kormarov’s friend. Far from exhibiting the placid smile we see in his official portraits, Gagarin vowed to confront Leonid Brezhnev, who, in his view, had needlessly sacrificed the life of a fine man.
You won’t find a stamp of that event, nor will you find one commemorating the Luna 15 probe slamming into a mountain while the Americans set foot on the Moon and walked away with victory in the Space Race.

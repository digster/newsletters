The Birth of the Modern Moral Panic

How the gin craze set a template for fear of intoxicants

George Dillard
Aug 13
 
READ IN APP
 

There was a new intoxicant loose on the streets of the big city. It was stronger than anything people had seen before — and cheap, too, which meant it was suddenly everywhere. It was so new and so readily available that leaders had no idea what to do about the wave of addiction that swept the country.

The drug seemed to prey on a certain type of person — down and out, low on opportunities, looking for an escape from the difficulties and disappointments of life. In a society with weakening social ties, where people had less access to family and fewer friends than before, too many people seemed to find solace in intoxication.

Though the stuff caused plenty of heartbreak and even death, improbable stories swirled about its effects. It inspired people to stop eating! It rendered almost half the population insensate! It caused women to combust spontaneously!

All this (well, not the spontaneous combustion stuff) was said about marijuana in midcentury, crack cocaine in the 1980s, and opiates and fentanyl today. But one of the earliest social panics about a drug took place over something that you may have in your liquor cabinet right now. The “gin craze” of the early 1700s captivated Britain and set a template for narratives about the impact of new drugs that is still influential today.

Before 1700, it was hard to get dangerously drunk in England, even though the country was awash in alcohol. Rich people drank some wine, and folks in apple country consumed cider, but most people drank beer or ale. The beer of the early modern period was less a recreational beverage than a fortifying one. The alcohol in the brew kept dangerous bacteria at bay, and beer provided calories for people who had to work hard, physical jobs. It’s hard to tell for sure, but premodern beer was likely less intoxicating than what you can buy today.

Since beer was a part of most people’s everyday lives, it was subject to social rules. Britons drank throughout the day — in agricultural societies, there was less of a sense of “being at work,” a time when you’d have to behave differently than when you were “off work.” Especially after the Protestant Reformation, people — mostly men — would also gather at pubs and other types of public houses to drink. Drinking, especially in public, was mostly a male activity; women were expected to drink less and to do so in private.

This culture of drinking was widely accepted in England, but new technology threw everything into disarray. By the 1700s, people had figured out how to distill spirits (as opposed to fermenting drinks like beer and wine), which allowed them to make much more potent beverages. Though distillation was known to ancient cultures and was practiced in very limited ways in medieval Europe, the widespread use of distilling emerged in tandem with the advances in chemistry that came with the Scientific Revolution.

A German author named Hieronymus Brunschwig published Liber de arte distillandi de simplicibus (Book on the art of distillation out of simple ingredients) around 1500, and the technology made its way into Britain by the 1600s with the help of King William, who brought gin from his homeland of the Netherlands when he assumed the throne in 1688.

The British government passed an “Act for encouraging the distilling of brandy and spirits from corn” in 1690, designed to reduce the importation of French brandy and replace it with domestic production of spirits. After this, the British gin industry, inspired by King William’s enjoyment of the stuff, took off. Gin was cheap to make, relatively unregulated (distillers did not need a license, and taxes on gin were lower than those on beer), and ten times more potent than beer or wine. It would soon come to dominate the drinking habits — and the imaginations — of British people.

Gin found its way into England at a time of great social change. London was evolving into the first industrial metropolis in the history of the world; its population would double over the course of the 1700s. The capital was an entirely different beast from any of the other towns in the country; it was 30 times the size of the next largest urban area.

As all of these people moved to the big city, they left behind rural social networks that had governed their behavior. Migrants found themselves in a giant, anonymous metropolis where anything went, and they often ended up living difficult, disappointing lives as the city turned out to offer less opportunity than they had expected.

Women, especially, found themselves in new social circumstances. Gender norms were different in London. Women were more likely than their counterparts in other parts of the country to leave the house for work and pleasure and to have spending money of their own. This changed their drinking behavior.

Into this unsettled society came gin, a beverage for which there were no established social rules. It was cheap, potent, and widely available. British people consumed vast quantities of it — 2.2 gallons per person by 1743. According to historian Rod Phillips, in some poor parts of London, one in every four houses doubled as a shop where you could buy gin.

And, yes, gin, like almost everything else in England, had class associations. Upper-class people like Henry Fielding declared that gin created a “new kind of drunkenness… almost wholly confined to the lowest order of people.” Gin shops were portrayed as chaotic, unruly places where violence and crime lurked. While wealthy people’s drunkenness took place mostly in private and they were trusted to manage their own vices, the upper and middle classes fretted that the “gin craze” was too much for the poor.

It’s unclear whether gin took special hold among women, or whether observers just paid more attention to women’s drinking, since it had previously been socially constrained. Women weren’t welcome in alehouses, but they could buy as much gin as they wanted. They could sell it, too — women opened dram shops or just sold gin on the street. Commentators worried about gin’s threat to the feminine virtues, especially their ability to raise children. One 1736 report said that:

With Regard to the Female Sex we find the Contagion has Spread even among them, And that to a Degree hardly possible to be Conceived. Unhappy Mothers habituate themselves to these distill’d Liquors, whose Children are born weakly & Sickly and often look Shriveld & old, as tho they had Numbered many Years; Others again daily give it to their Children whilst young and learn them even before they can go to taste & approve of this Certain great Destroyer

A poem called “Strip Me Naked” tried to capture the desperation of a gin-besotted mother abandoning her household duties and selling everything to get another drink:

I MUST, I will have gin! — that skillet take,

Pawn it. — No more I’ll roast, or boil or bake.

This juice immortal will each want supply;

Starve on, ye brats! so I but bung my eye.

Starve? No! This gin ev’n mother’s milk excels,

Paints the pale cheeks and hunger’s darts repels.

No social panic is complete without exaggerated stories of women’s moral depravity. During the gin craze, Britons obsessed over the story of a woman named Judith Defour, who lived in a workhouse for the poor. She was accused of strangling her own child and then selling the child’s clothes to buy more gin. The story combined tropes we still see today — intoxication leading to sensational cruelty, inflicting suffering on children, and causing ungrateful recipients of welfare benefits to abuse the system.

The most famous artifact of the gin craze is “Gin Lane,” a 1751 illustration by William Hogarth that combines many of the themes we’ve seen here — crime, sloth, financial ruin, wayward women, and abandoned children:

It was originally published with this poem:

Gin, cursed Fiend, with Fury fraught,

Makes human Race a Prey.

It enters by a deadly Draught

And steals our Life away.

Virtue and Truth, driv’n to Despair

Its Rage compells to fly,

But cherishes with hellish Care

Theft, Murder, Perjury.

Damned Cup! that on the Vitals preys

That liquid Fire contains,

Which Madness to the heart conveys,

And rolls it thro’ the Veins.

Parliament reacted relatively quickly to the concerns around gin. And, despite the fact that it sparked something with all of the characteristics of a moral panic, gin does seem to have been a problem. Though some contemporary reports may have exaggerated the impact of gin, fueled by class bias, sexism, and anxiety about a changing society, it does seem that crime was on the rise, as were poverty and public disorder.

In 1729, Parliament hiked taxes on gin and other spirits, charged a licensing fee on those who wished to sell it, and imposed stiff fines on people who resisted these laws. Gin drinkers protested, but their most effective resistance was simply to ignore the law. Enforcement was sloppy, probably because the government didn’t want to crush the gin industry, which created a lot of tax revenue.

The government tried again with even stricter laws in 1736, but people again found ways around them. One particularly clever method was the “puss and mew,” which functioned like a vending machine. This allowed people to sell gin while concealing their identity. A 1738 article described its workings:

The Way is this, the Buyer comes into the Entry and Cries Puss, and is immediately answer’d by a Voice from within, Mew. A Drawer is then thrust out, in which the Buyer puts his Money, which when drawn back, is soon after thrust out again, with the Quantity of Gin requir’d; the Master of this new Improvement in Mechanicks, remaining all the while unseen; whereby all Informations are defeated, and the Penalty of the Gin Act evaded.

In 1743 and 1751, the government passed more gin laws, but, crucially, they had given up on trying to eliminate gin consumption among London’s poor. Now they just wanted to regulate it. London’s ruling classes no longer tried to control the behavior of the growing working classes. The best they could do was try to nudge people in a certain direction.

In the end, the gin craze died down. Government policy had something to do with it, but changing cultural ideas, new products (people started to drink more porter and less gin), and changing socioeconomic conditions probably had a larger effect.

The gin craze became a sort of template for how societies often see new drugs, associating them with scary big cities, lower-class people, loose women, and improbable tales of depravity and destitution. Over time, gin became just another drink, but the panic over it remains a model for our concerns about intoxicants today.

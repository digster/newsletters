People in the past didn’t leave a lot of evidence about how they conducted the most mundane parts of their lives. For example, it took an impressive amount of detective work for historian Roger Ekirch [ https://substack.com/redirect/bd5d41da-0c1e-4607-8688-3f4bdb90acf6?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] to figure out that medieval people slept on different schedules than we do because people rarely wrote down anything about their sleep habits. Why would they?
Similarly, if you lived in a society where writing was rare and writing materials were costly, would you really waste resources on recording what you gulped down before heading out the door to work? You might want to make note of the exciting birthday dinner you made for your spouse or the delicious restaurant you visited, but would you really write down “I ate oatmeal for breakfast for the 47th day in a row?”
So historians are grasping in the dark a bit when it comes to the origins of the morning meal. They tell us that the ancient Romans and Greeks [ https://substack.com/redirect/411c4b0e-5118-42ce-a510-286e96a2f0cc?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] routinely ate breakfast, but the ancients also seem to have believed that eating only one meal a day was better for health [ https://substack.com/redirect/68344f25-b72c-41ef-9559-d69f8de5c23e?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] (and who would waste that meal on breakfast?). Medieval Europeans thought [ https://substack.com/redirect/056bfa53-db21-4ab3-b244-5e2b33560a01?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] that eating too early was gluttonous and therefore sinful. Or maybe breakfast was reserved [ https://substack.com/redirect/1dfa57b0-690f-438d-ae45-3663251e01f3?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] for the wealthy, while most people ate a big lunch in late morning and then a dinner in the evening.
The history of our most mundane meal has reflected our work habits, economic conditions, and morality. Whether you think that breakfast is the most important meal of the day or you skip it entirely, your morning habits are the result of historical forces.
If breakfast was an unremarked-upon and inconsistently consumed meal for much of Western history, it started to become routine in the 1600s and 1700s. In fact, Dutch artists like Pieter and Willem Claesz created a number of breakfast-related still life paintings in the middle of the 1600s:
Walters Art Museum [ https://substack.com/redirect/ff1138eb-43bf-453f-a4d6-b8cd832d0357?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ]
Art Institute of Chicago [ https://substack.com/redirect/b8b19445-ab8e-4bba-96df-633416fc0335?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ]
You’ll notice that the second painting, with an abundance of oysters, doesn’t look like the breakfasts you may know and love today. Elaborate breakfasts for the wealthy didn’t resemble today’s combinations of eggs, breads, and perhaps a bit of meat. An 1847 cookbook [ https://substack.com/redirect/a9828e19-c9a4-464f-8efe-a4ae48e52b0f?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] lists several possible breakfast menus, some of which would be welcome today, and others that seem quite strange:
Coffee and tea, or chocolate, either or both; broiled beef-steak, or veal or lamb, fried or broiled; with boiled hominy or mashed potatoes, or potatoes fried, baked, or roasted; and sliced wheat bread and corn breakfast-cakes or griddle-cakes.
Broiled or fried chicken; or clams stewed, or fritter, or fried; or broiled or fried ham, with eggs fried, boiled, poached or omlet [sic]; with ashed or fried potatoes, and such warm and cold bread as may be desired.
Salt or fresh fish, fried or broiled, and hot rolls or cold bread, with tea or coffee, constitute a very popular family breakfast in summer; …
[In winter,] Fried oysters; or soft-shell clams, fried; or fried or broiled chicken; or tripe, broiled or fried; with boiled hominy, or hot corn bread and rolls; and tea or coffee.
Buckwheat griddle cakes, with fried steaks, ham, or sausages, or cold meat or hash, are a popular breakfast for winter. With many families, buckwheat cakes are the constant breakfast, with some little varieties of meat, and tea or coffee, during the winter…
Cold meat, sliced, and fried potatoes, and parsnips, fried; with boiled hominy, or corn bread or griddle-cakes, may be liked.
It may be that the enthusiasm for breakfast among the wealthy and powerful started with beverages. As coffee, tea, and chocolate drinks entered Europe, people clamored for the caffeine they delivered in the morning. The Catholic Church, which had previously discouraged a morning meal and had other rules about fasting, declared that “ [ https://substack.com/redirect/056bfa53-db21-4ab3-b244-5e2b33560a01?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ]Liquidum non frangit jejunum [ https://substack.com/redirect/056bfa53-db21-4ab3-b244-5e2b33560a01?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ]”: “Liquid doesn’t break the fast.” [ https://substack.com/redirect/056bfa53-db21-4ab3-b244-5e2b33560a01?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] These liquid breakfasts soon came to be accompanied by all sorts of other foods.
By the Victorian period [ https://substack.com/redirect/b182329e-f7de-4942-8526-999b0b36695b?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ], breakfast had become another way for the prosperous to show off, as the middle class copied rich people’s lavish breakfast spreads, complete with sausages, eggs, fish, and other meats.
Ordinary people’s breakfasts emerged from a different set of needs. While people worked for themselves on farms, they tended not to eat much for breakfast. They’d work for a while in the morning, take a break for lunch, then work until dinner. But by the early modern period, urban workers were no longer in control of their own time. They didn’t get to decide when they ate, nor were they at home to fix a tasty lunch. It became necessary to fill one’s stomach before leaving for work.
The need for breakfast only intensified as cities became industrialized. Factory workers had to rise early and work crushingly long hours. They had to count on breakfast [ https://substack.com/redirect/98c10432-8f4d-4a19-909c-f5cf9f8f9551?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ], eaten at home, to get them from the start of work at 6 am to the meager lunch — often covered with soot from the machines — at midday.
Most of these people ate utilitarian breakfasts, be they a hunk of bread with cheese or some of the previous night’s leftovers. There might have been a liquid breakfast [ https://substack.com/redirect/469cd605-4b39-4ebb-b2fe-555eaa21e2f8?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] for these folks, too, but it could have been cider or beer rather than tea.
So breakfast became, for some, a luxurious morning ritual and for others a grim necessity before a brutal day of work. But most breakfasters didn’t think that their choice of morning food was important to their health until the late 1800s.
Breakfast became once again tangled up with ideas about morality when Dr. John Kellogg watched the gorillas [ https://substack.com/redirect/de058162-8359-42cd-b09b-179e340320c3?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] in the zoo. He saw that they were defecating up to five times every day, and Dr. Kellogg was jealous because he believed that frequent evacuation was essential to health.
Kellogg was a Seventh-Day Adventist whose religious ideas about health (plant-based foods, no alcohol) interlocked with his scientific ideas about “biologic living.” The result was that he pushed a breakfast free of the fatty sausages and eggs that characterized many people’s morning meals. Instead, he pushed bland whole grains.
Kellogg started baking [ https://substack.com/redirect/de058162-8359-42cd-b09b-179e340320c3?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] grains at high temperatures (he thought this made them easier to digest) and eventually happened on a formula for flaky cereal (the key was allowing the grain to ferment a bit, which he discovered by accident when he left it out for a few days). The age of cereal was born.
Cereal wasn’t just a cheap and convenient breakfast; it was supposedly healthier and futuristic. Scientists had deployed their expertise to create technologically advanced foods that would make people feel better and live longer. Quaker Oats called [ https://substack.com/redirect/df535f9e-cf52-416b-b0a1-1af61f99b4af?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] its puffed rice cereal “the eighth wonder of the world” because of its supposed health effects and the industrial processes used to produce it (the puffs were the first “food shot from guns”). Grape-Nuts supposedly cured [ https://substack.com/redirect/de058162-8359-42cd-b09b-179e340320c3?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] diseases like malaria. Eating cereal was good for you, and feeding it to your family was simply the virtuous thing to do.
As more and more companies began manufacturing cereals, they needed to distinguish their little flakes and O’s from one another. And that’s where marketing started to shape breakfast, especially in the United States.
Breakfast became the “most important meal of the day [ https://substack.com/redirect/cf5e03a4-cf98-48c0-8a31-83f9596a4d71?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ]” thanks to a Grape-Nuts ad campaign [ https://substack.com/redirect/6cab4f15-01f9-4784-940f-2a47556b2f4d?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] in the 1940s, which told consumers to “Eat a Good Breakfast — Do a Better Job” and cited “nutrition experts” who said that eating breakfast was crucial. This campaign, and others like it, capitalized on cereal’s healthy reputation while the industry began to load up breakfast cereals with sugar [ https://substack.com/redirect/754f2c2f-5adc-4a51-a5e3-78e8fa832949?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] to attract kids. Frosted Flakes, Froot Loops, and their sweet, colorful brethren told parents, improbably, that they were “part of a balanced breakfast” while enticing kids with gobs of sugar and cartoon characters.
Other parts of the “balanced breakfast” also emerged as a result of clever marketing campaigns. In the early 20th century, orange growers had a problem [ https://substack.com/redirect/c773e970-31f4-4bf6-906a-e2fe66708ad2?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ]: they were growing too much fruit. They could either reduce production or find a new outlet for their oranges. They chose to market orange juice as a staple of the breakfast table.
In the 1920s, the discovery of vitamins further revolutionized how people understood health. “Vitamania” allowed orange growers to market their juice as a vital source of vitamin C and the cure for “acidosis [ https://substack.com/redirect/ab1d122b-54c7-4ab6-9cc9-8c95271fbc3b?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ],” caused by too much acid in the blood. Through the middle of the 20th century, encouraged by the U.S. Department of Agriculture, orange growers created the idea that you should drink your fruit, and that orange juice belonged on the breakfast table.
The idea that a good breakfast full of nutritious food was the key to a productive day became ingrained in American culture, but we continue to revise our idea of what a good breakfast looks like. For a while, breakfast was supposed to be a hearty meal of eggs and meats. Then we decided that lighter, blander, sweeter foods like cereal were the way to go. After that, we turned against the sugary cereals and juices — a “good” breakfast centered on less-processed foods. Now, we seem to be obsessed with cramming as much protein [ https://substack.com/redirect/7863a4cc-4cbd-4292-b73d-f99b4d9f7df4?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] into our bodies as we can before we leave for work.
Unless, of course, breakfast is bad for us again. The promoters of the intermittent fasting [ https://substack.com/redirect/ff861b21-921e-4e4d-a8cc-957a4692f6ba?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] trend told people a few years ago to eat all of their calories in an 8-hour window and fast for the rest of the day. In practice, this usually means skipping breakfast. Intermittent fasters supposedly lose weight, sleep better, and have less inflammation. For many, the practice helps them feel more disciplined and virtuous than breakfast-eaters.
There was, of course, a backlash to intermittent fasting, and now breakfast seems to be back. But one thing has remained the same throughout breakfast’s various incarnations: it’s a reflection of social, economic, and cultural forces as much as it is an actual necessity.

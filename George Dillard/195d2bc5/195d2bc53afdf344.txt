Thomas Edison invented a lot of really nifty stuff. He’s a big part of the reason that the people of the twentieth century could turn the lights on at night, listen to recorded music, and make and watch movies. His inventions were integral to the development of modern life.
But even Edison didn’t bat 1.000.
Scattered amongst his big hits were many duds, including an electric pen that didn’t really work well, talking dolls that broke when actual children handled them, and concrete houses that nobody wanted.
But Edison’s strangest failed project was — well, I’ll let you read what A.D. Rothman of the  [ https://substack.com/redirect/c170e8cc-4ae8-43af-a1ff-015f366494fe?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ]New York Times [ https://substack.com/redirect/c170e8cc-4ae8-43af-a1ff-015f366494fe?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] had to say about it in 1921:
WITHOUT a peer in the field of scientific invention, Thomas Edison has announced his entrance into a new sphere, that of psychic research… When so-called “death” appears to drive life from our bodies Mr. Edison thinks that our personality “units” do not die, but continue to exist as substantial material things…. [Edison said that] “There are held a dozen ways of making a machine or of approaching the problem… A man with very puny strength sets into motion a machine with power infinitely greater than his own.”
Edison wasn’t sure he would succeed: “He is not prepared to say if he succeeds in constructing an apparatus that can register the stimuli of personality units… will mean the opening of free communication with the dead.” He rejected occult ideas like Ouija boards, but he believed that technology could crack the barrier between life and death. With a typically optimistic tinkerer’s attitude, he said, “The ball has got to start a-rolling. I am taking a try at it.”
The ball didn’t roll very far. It’s unclear [ https://substack.com/redirect/3b5278e0-c222-4aa0-b5a6-364eae37ac52?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] if Edison ever actually tried to build this thing; his preliminary designs included a phonograph-like horn that would somehow catch the “vibrations” of the “life units” that were floating around. Edison’s “valve” to the world of the dead never opened.
Though Edison’s desire to create a device that could speak to the dead might seem laughable, when you look at it in context, it seems a little more reasonable. The truth is that Edison had created a link between the living and the dead.
When we think of the phonograph and the movie camera, two of Edison’s most important inventions, we tend to focus on their entertainment value. People could listen to music or watch a filmed performance. But, as delightful as those uses were, Edison’s inventions created a more profound opportunity — to preserve and replay the movements and voices of people who were no longer living.
Many of the inventions of the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries made the impossible seem possible. Railroads allowed people to traverse seemingly impossible distances, and then airplanes allowed them to soar through the sky. Invisible energies pulsed through wires to light up the night and power machines. The telegraph allowed people to communicate instantaneously across oceans, and then the radio somehow transmitted voices through the air.
These new technologies were all magical. No wonder people began to think that a machine might make it possible to communicate with the spirit world.
Photography, invented in the middle of the nineteenth century, was the first step in this process. For all of human history up until this point, only the wealthy and powerful could make themselves last beyond the grave through art, and the paintings or sculptures that they commissioned weren’t always very accurate.
The camera allowed everyone to capture a moment in time — by the 1850s [ https://substack.com/redirect/d541a142-d3b8-4bc7-9200-29c9225a5de5?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ], even working-class people could afford a family portrait that would preserve the faces of their loved ones even after they were gone. Many people even made sure to photograph [ https://substack.com/redirect/e5275190-27ae-4724-a253-879cad833827?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] their family members after they were gone — during the Victorian era it was common practice to photograph the corpses of deceased family members, often propping them up into lifelike poses.
Since early photography was so connected to bridging the gap between the living and the dead, it’s not surprising that many people in the nineteenth century believed that this miraculous technology might be able to pierce through the barrier between life and death.
“Spirit photography [ https://substack.com/redirect/daa11cad-04e8-4a41-9873-420295e8cb3a?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ]” — which purported to capture ghostly presences on film — started as a simple mistake (a photographer named William Mumler mistakenly reused an already-exposed plate), but photographers soon recognized a sterling business opportunity. They offered customers the opportunity to see the spirits that those customers so desperately wanted to believe were there. Mary Todd Lincoln asked Mumler to capture her assassinated husband on film, and Arthur Conan Doyle (the author of the Sherlock Holmes books) was a prominent proponent of spirit photography through the 1920s.
If you’re curious about spirit photography, I wrote a whole post about the subject — you should read it!
The new technologies of this period appeared alongside — and clearly influenced — the growth of spiritualism, a loosely organized movement that believed it was possible to communicate with the spirit world. A lot of spiritualist rituals were technology-free (Ouija boards, seances, and the like), but it was not a coincidence that spiritualists called mediums “spiritual telegraphs.” As religion scholar Christopher White writes [ https://substack.com/redirect/960f2bf8-8962-47e5-a1b8-bed77b94de93?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ], occult beliefs were bound up in the language and culture of technological progress:
Spiritualist mediums spoke of spirit communication as spiritual telegraphy, a technology that formed an electro-spiritual circuit between heaven and earth. At séances, men and women alternated at séance tables to balance positive (male) and negative (female) polarities, just as zinc and copper plates were alternated in batteries. (Conducting these invisible forces could be improved by having séance participants hold magnetized ropes with their ends dipped in buckets of water containing copper and zinc. As one scholar has written, as “Spiritualist belief crossed the country along with the unreeling cables of the telegraph companies, Spiritualist books such as The Celestial Telegraph and periodicals such as The Spiritual Messenger frequently invoked popular knowledge of Morse’s electromagnetic telegraph to explain their model of spiritual contact.” Telegraph cables and entranced mediums in upper-class drawing rooms were conduits for the crackling electrical energy that connected people across continents and joined spirits across the firmament.
During seances, spirits were thought to communicate through “rapping [ https://substack.com/redirect/c4935d54-e1ff-46c2-95ac-added5cf4b6f?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ];” their coded communications supposedly worked just like Morse-code telegraphic communications. Some mediums used the new technology to fool their customers. Others employed “spirit trumpets [ https://substack.com/redirect/bca608c4-23aa-472e-80be-e6a0dd7ed400?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ]” that supposedly amplified noises from the spirit realm. In reality, the trumpet contained a telephone receiver that would broadcast the voice of an accomplice in the next room.
It’s easy to chuckle at the way Victorian people mixed technology with spiritual beliefs. Those of us who have grown up with the technologies that seemed miraculous a century ago now take them for granted. We don’t think much about how amazing it is that there’s energy coursing through the wires in our walls at all times, or that the air around us is full of information. We now know what these technologies can do and what they can’t, so we can afford to snicker at the belief that you can catch a ghost on film.
I wonder if we’re entering a new age of convergence between technology and the supernatural with the development of artificial intelligence. It’s too early to say where AI is headed and what it’s capable of. Some people see AI as a very costly and energy-intensive word-prediction machine, capable only of producing lowest-common-denominator “slop.” Others speak as if we’re headed for an age in which a godlike superintelligence will either solve all the world’s problems or enslave humanity.
I suppose I lean toward the former assessment, but I do have to say that, on occasion, AI seems like it can perform magic. When I put an essay prompt from one of my classes into a chatbot and watch it churn out in a few seconds a passable essay that would have taken a human writer an hour to produce, or when I dump a long PDF into an LLM and instantly get a decent summary of the text, I’m pretty astonished. AI isn’t always accurate, and I still don’t trust the results I get from it, but it’s already doing impressive things.
One thing that I find fascinating about AI is that nobody really knows [ https://substack.com/redirect/d5dcde0f-a9fe-4cf8-a790-56124d204a8f?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] how it works, and that includes the people who have designed and programmed it. The models routinely do things that confound their creators, who can’t always explain what the models are “thinking” or how they generate their output.
Our astonishment at our own technology is not that different from the incredulity that nineteenth-century people must have felt about radio waves flying through the air. This means that the age of AI may upend our spiritual assumptions just like the age of the photograph and the telegraph did.
While we thought we understood the line between human and machine, we now find that line getting blurrier. Are the machines thinking? Is AI sentient [ https://substack.com/redirect/eea57ee8-b8f1-46d9-bc66-fd68f124b9d1?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ]? How would we even know? Can you have an AI friend? Should Claude be your “computational guardian angel? [ https://substack.com/redirect/86592830-c537-445f-b347-66bf3761021f?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ]” Can you fall in love [ https://substack.com/redirect/55a882db-33c9-4274-ba39-0fa49a4f297a?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] with an AI? When you tell the chatbot your deepest secrets, what’s talking back to you?
AI is even being used to blur the line between life and death — a decade ago, resurrecting a dead loved one through technology was the subject of a dystopian episode [ https://substack.com/redirect/3dc14c39-60df-44c1-90ca-8f79e4cda0dc?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] of Black Mirror; now, AI companies are offering [ https://substack.com/redirect/74c3afad-26af-4c71-9a57-545d7caadc10?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] that service in the real world.
A lot of people are engaging in what used to be called prophecy around the capabilities of artificial intelligence. Some “doomers [ https://substack.com/redirect/433d42ab-1648-45e4-935d-c8222e868ab1?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ]” are predicting an apocalypse of sorts, in which a superpowered AI takes the place of a vengeful god. Others dream of a utopia in which our new technological creations deliver us from suffering and perhaps even death. A few speculate, starry-eyed, about “transhumanism [ https://substack.com/redirect/e32806d6-b4e3-439c-8dc0-a9d44dd7927a?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ],” the idea that AI and other technologies will allow us to rise beyond our human limitations.
These predictions probably won’t come to pass, and many of them may look pretty silly to people 100 years from now. But this is what happens when revolutionary new technology changes our perception of what’s possible. We lose our sense of where science ends and the supernatural begins.

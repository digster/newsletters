Thomas Edison invented a lot of really nifty stuff. He’s a big part of the reason that the people of the twentieth century could turn the lights on at night, listen to recorded music, and make and watch movies. His inventions were integral to the development of modern life. But even Edison didn’t bat 1.000. Scattered amongst his big hits were many duds, including an electric pen that didn’t really work well, talking dolls that broke when actual children handled them, and concrete houses that nobody wanted. Edison wasn’t sure he would succeed: “He is not prepared to say if he succeeds in constructing an apparatus that can register the stimuli of personality units… will mean the opening of free communication with the dead.” He rejected occult ideas like Ouija boards, but he believed that technology could crack the barrier between life and death. With a typically optimistic tinkerer’s attitude, he said, “The ball has got to start a-rolling. I am taking a try at it.” The ball didn’t roll very far. The truth is that Edison had created a link between the living and the dead. When we think of the phonograph and the movie camera, two of Edison’s most important inventions, we tend to focus on their entertainment value. People could listen to music or watch a filmed performance. But, as delightful as those uses were, Edison’s inventions created a more profound opportunity — to preserve and replay the movements and voices of people who were no longer living. Many of the inventions of the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries made the impossible seem possible. Railroads allowed people to traverse seemingly impossible distances, and then airplanes allowed them to soar through the sky. Invisible energies pulsed through wires to light up the night and power machines. The telegraph allowed people to communicate instantaneously across oceans, and then the radio somehow transmitted voices through the air. These new technologies were all magical. No wonder people began to think that a machine might make it possible to communicate with the spirit world. Photography, invented in the middle of the nineteenth century, was the first step in this process. For all of human history up until this point, only the wealthy and powerful could make themselves last beyond the grave through art, and the paintings or sculptures that they commissioned weren’t always very accurate. Those of us who have grown up with the technologies that seemed miraculous a century ago now take them for granted. We don’t think much about how amazing it is that there’s energy coursing through the wires in our walls at all times, or that the air around us is full of information. We now know what these technologies can do and what they can’t, so we can afford to snicker at the belief that you can catch a ghost on film. I wonder if we’re entering a new age of convergence between technology and the supernatural with the development of artificial intelligence. It’s too early to say where AI is headed and what it’s capable of. Some people see AI as a very costly and energy-intensive word-prediction machine, capable only of producing lowest-common-denominator “slop.” Others speak as if we’re headed for an age in which a godlike superintelligence will either solve all the world’s problems or enslave humanity. I suppose I lean toward the former assessment, but I do have to say that, on occasion, AI seems like it can perform magic. When I put an essay prompt from one of my classes into a chatbot and watch it churn out in a few seconds a passable essay that would have taken a human writer an hour to produce, or when I dump a long PDF into an LLM and instantly get a decent summary of the text, I’m pretty astonished. AI isn’t always accurate, and I still don’t trust the results I get from it, but it’s already doing impressive things. This means that the age of AI may upend our spiritual assumptions just like the age of the photograph and the telegraph did. While we thought we understood the line between human and machine, we now find that line getting blurrier. Are the machines thinking?

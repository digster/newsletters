Thomas Moran’s family suffered what so many did during the Industrial Revolution. His parents were hand weavers [ https://substack.com/redirect/790d6256-655b-4e98-956b-83456ccff8a6?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] in Bolton, England, but by the time Thomas was born in 1837, the machines were putting people like his mom and dad out of business. Meanwhile, rapidly industrializing Bolton was becoming an inhospitable place to raise a family. Friedrich Engels described [ https://substack.com/redirect/50a31426-05de-4c65-977f-8e6c134b38b3?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] the town in very unflattering terms:
Among the worst of these towns after Preston and Oldham is Bolton, eleven miles north-west of Manchester. It has, so far as I have been able to observe in my repeated visits, but one main street, a very dirty one, Deansgate, which serves as a market, and is even in the finest weather a dark, unattractive hole in spite of the fact that, except for the factories, its sides are formed by low one and two-storied houses. Here, as everywhere, the older part of the town is especially ruinous and miserable. A dark-coloured body of water, which leaves the beholder in doubt whether it is a brook or a long string of stagnant puddles, flows through the town and contributes its share to the total pollution of the air, by no means pure without it.
The Morans fled this “dark, unattractive hole” to America. They settled in Philadelphia, where teenage Thomas apprenticed for an engraver and studied painting. As soon as he was old enough, he headed west. Perhaps his bleak, urban childhood created his desire to visit and capture the majestic natural spaces in America. 
After painting the Great Lakes and achieving moderate success, he looked for an opportunity to see the spectacular areas of the West. The chance came when Ferdinand Hayden invited Moran and the photographer William Henry Jackson to come along on a geological survey of Yellowstone in 1871. On this trip, Moran first saw the Grand Canyon of the Yellowstone, which became a lifelong obsession. He painted the area again and again:
Moran’s greatest painting of the canyon, which he completed in 1872, sparked real excitement back in Washington. Congress bought the 7x12-foot canvas for $10,000 [ https://substack.com/redirect/790d6256-655b-4e98-956b-83456ccff8a6?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ], which was a lot of money back then, and hung it in the Senate chamber:
The painting caused a stir. According to Moran’s contemporaries, it played a pivotal role in Congress’s decision to make Yellowstone the first national park. After all, the members of Congress who voted on the legislation had no real knowledge of what the Yellowstone area was like. You had to see it to understand it. A member of the Army Corps of Engineers said [ https://substack.com/redirect/224156b0-8a65-4790-8d63-e43f7e846716?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] that Moran’s paintings “doubtless convinced everyone who saw them that the regions where such wonders existed should be preserved to the people forever.” Later in life, Moran would sometimes write his name “Thomas Y. Moran” — you guessed it, the Y stood for Yellowstone. 
There are a few other things you should probably know about Moran’s painting. 
First, Moran wanted to capture the spirit of Yellowstone more than he wanted to precisely record what he saw. He didn’t mind adding details, like the Native Americans dwarfed by the landscape in the foreground of this painting, to make the image even grander than its real-life inspiration. Moran admitted [ https://substack.com/redirect/224156b0-8a65-4790-8d63-e43f7e846716?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] that “My general scope is not realistic; all my tendencies are toward idealization.”
Second, Moran got his big chance to accompany the Hayden expedition at the behest of railroad baron Jay Cooke [ https://substack.com/redirect/d482e31d-5188-449a-9279-25ad5caf5ad1?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ], who was interested in things other than sponsoring the geological sciences. 
As the man in charge of the Northern Pacific Railroad, Cooke wanted to boost traffic on his trains. He employed Nathaniel Langford, an ambitious man whose Wikipedia [ https://substack.com/redirect/30ccc6bf-4dab-4f82-a3ef-af586304147c?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] entry describes him as an “explorer, businessman, bureaucrat, vigilante and historian,” to promote the Yellowstone area in 1870, two years before Moran painted it. 
Langford recruited General Henry Washburn to lead an “expedition” to Yellowstone. The expedition didn’t discover anything [ https://substack.com/redirect/d54fa89e-3c7b-4762-9ab1-3bcd01465049?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] so much as it toured the area and promoted it. Upon his return, Langford boosted his own career and Cooke’s interests by touring the East and lecturing on the wonders of Yellowstone. These lectures inspired Ferdinand Hayden, the government’s top geologist, to go to Yellowstone himself. 
Cooke then helpfully suggested that Hayden take Thomas Moran along to paint what he saw. The painting ended up in Congress. Two years after hatching the idea, Cooke had his national park, which, not coincidentally, made the Northern Pacific’s rail routes much more lucrative. 
So we have a choice: we can see the creation of Yellowstone and the other national parks as a pure expression of the American love of nature, or we can see the same events as the result of crass capitalistic machinations. Or maybe they’re the ultimate expression of the American national character, where noble pursuits are never entirely free of the influence of commerce. 
Although, as Franklin Roosevelt said [ https://substack.com/redirect/b4ef6ea0-016e-4b68-8972-585a65c53e22?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ], “the fundamental idea behind the parks … is that the country belongs to the people,” the national parks have always been intertwined with businesses like railroads. And the railroads worked hard to turn the parks into travel destinations.

The railroads weren’t the only ones who advertised these places. As John Clayton writes [ https://substack.com/redirect/aadbd318-346f-4962-b60d-9eec2a05dd08?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ], the first director of the National Park Service, Stephen Mather, also believed in marketing the parks. Mather was a marketing expert, having made a fortune selling Borax to the public (he came up with the idea of advertising it as “20 Mule Team Borax”) before becoming an outdoorsman and government official. Mather paid a marketer named Robert Sterling Yard with his own money (Congress wouldn’t let him spend taxpayers’ money to promote the parks to taxpayers) to tell the country about its national parks. 
Yard constructed National Park Portfolios to entice Americans to visit their parks. They’re beautiful, as you can see from these pages from the 1921 edition [ https://substack.com/redirect/79ad7e7b-2db5-471b-af34-82d34b853b71?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ], again marketing the parks as stunning natural settings and fun places for people to enjoy themselves:
We should keep in mind that, during the early 20th century, the parks were hard to get to and people rarely visited them. In 1904, the first year records were kept, only 120,690 people [ https://substack.com/redirect/07f0d051-8126-429d-9518-6d2f1e11cf9b?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] visited the parks for recreation. In 1906, that number was only 30,569. This means that there was only one visitor to a national park for every 2,500 Americans. 
In 2024, 331 million people visited the parks, constituting about one visit for every American citizen. Today, some people argue that the national parks are being “loved to death [ https://substack.com/redirect/47cc5946-dc7f-4a57-b1b3-45e7412ab0bf?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ].” It’s always been difficult for the parks to balance their twin missions of nature preservation and tourism. If you’ve ever experienced the crowds that descend on the most popular parks (Smoky Mountains, Zion, Yellowstone, Yosemite, Grand Canyon, etc.), it’s easy to understand why people might feel that the balance is out of whack.
But early on, the parks did need some marketing. If no one visited or even thought about them, they might have been put toward a more “productive” use, their rivers dammed and their minerals extracted. We can thank the marketers — and the images they used — for making such things unthinkable today. 
It’s tempting to try to set up two opposing values: pure nature on one side and filthy commerce on the other. But the truth is more complicated than that. Commercial interests helped create the national parks in the first place, and they continued to shape the idea of the national parks in the American mind long before most Americans could see them for themselves. If we’re going to consider the national parks as an expression of what America’s all about, we’ll have to acknowledge the fact that commerce has never been far from the core of the American experience.

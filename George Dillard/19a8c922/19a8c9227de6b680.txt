It’s hard to get a bad view of Yosemite. It’s one of the most beautiful places in the world, visited by over 4 million [ https://substack.com/redirect/864dc6fc-47c5-4563-a941-088a0f18924f?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] people every year. In the busy summer months, it can be a zoo, with immense crowds of people thronging around its natural wonders (it helped us to visit from the eastern part of the country — we used jet lag to our advantage, leaving our rental very early and getting into the park for a couple of peaceful hours before the madness began).
Yosemite is also one of the most-photographed locations on planet Earth. There are probably billions of photos of the park’s most iconic locations. In places like Yosemite, I always feel contrasting urges. On the one hand, I want to photograph everything to commemorate my experience. On the other, maybe I should spend my time looking at the real thing rather than peering through a camera lens. After all, I know that millions of photos of, say, Half Dome exist on the internet that are better than anything I’d take.
Why bother when someone already took a photograph of Yosemite from the “best general view” way back in 1866?
Carleton Watkins took this photo in very different circumstances from those in which you take your vacation pictures. He transported 2,000 pounds [ https://substack.com/redirect/219b6f95-8835-4f8a-9ddc-b810773354cb?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] of camera equipment — including glass plates, hazardous chemicals, and a portable darkroom — into the remote, roadless mountains on a mule train. Because no one knew how to enlarge images from negatives, his camera was gigantic, more than two feet on each side, to accommodate the “mammoth plates [ https://substack.com/redirect/e17e6afe-a0fe-4ef2-8276-79a5ff7f4550?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ]” that allowed him to capture huge vistas. 
Inside his darkroom tent in the wilderness, Watkins had to treat the 18”x22” plates with the chemicals while preventing them from getting dirty. Then he’d insert the plate into his camera, expose the image, and whisk the negative back to the darkroom and coat it with another set of chemicals to set the image. And then he would somehow have to transport his glass negatives back out of the mountains.
The stakes were high. He could only bring about a hundred negatives with him on each expedition. Getting a shot wrong or messing up a glass negative would mean that days of work might be for naught. To improve his chances of success, Watkins sometimes combined the best features of his photos into one shot. The image [ https://substack.com/redirect/7d63e1ac-802a-4547-807a-bc3063f8a2c3?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] above is actually the composite of two negatives; he liked the clouds in a second shot better, and included those in his final print. 
Watkins wasn’t the first person to photograph Yosemite, but his photos made the place internationally famous. His images captured the size and beauty of Yosemite and much of the West. In the words [ https://substack.com/redirect/219b6f95-8835-4f8a-9ddc-b810773354cb?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] of curator Elizabeth Kathleen Mitchell, “He created an image of the American west for the world.”
Watkins, born in New York, first traveled to Yosemite in 1861. He couldn’t have gotten much farther away from the chaos that was enveloping the nation while remaining within its borders. As his country devolved into civil war, he trundled up the Mariposa Trail in search of the best views of the natural wonders there. 
Why did Watkins trek into the wilderness? He had previously worked on more commercial photography projects, taking pictures of mines and ranches to help their owners document claims and attract investment. His 1861 Yosemite expedition was funded by Thomas Starr King [ https://substack.com/redirect/f46fc067-0ebd-4417-a564-7a59f34d79f4?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ], a Boston transcendentalist who had moved west to serve as a Unitarian minister in San Francisco. King wanted photographs that would stir the souls of his friends back east.
When Watkins returned from Yosemite, King sent his photos to friends like Ralph Waldo Emerson (some are still displayed in Emerson’s house). These luminaries built hype for the images. When exhibited, the Yosemite photos — many times larger than any photograph that had previously been taken of the valley — captivated audiences. Oliver Wendell Holmes called [ https://substack.com/redirect/106f9de1-b3d6-4859-ab02-20c15de38e75?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] them “a perfection of art which compares with the finest European work.”
Watkins became a celebrity and published his pictures in books. Some of these books made their way to Washington, DC, where members of Congress and President Abraham Lincoln [ https://substack.com/redirect/dae28d01-a237-4e90-a1a9-ec2a1ece5b99?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] (likely) saw them.
I can imagine Lincoln taking a break from the bloody business of the war to page through a book of photos that California Senator John Conness had thrust into his hands. Perhaps the stunning imagery — so unlike any place Lincoln had ever seen with his own eyes — provided the president with a brief respite from the nonstop stress of holding the nation together.

Watkins’ photos helped inspire Congress to create the Yosemite Grant, a law that protected the area and presaged the development of the national park system.

Despite his fame and influence, things didn’t go well for Watkins. He built a beautiful gallery [ https://substack.com/redirect/51347a8f-aa10-4216-b1bd-fed0d56f42f4?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] on Montgomery St. which did well for a while, until the boom-and-bust economy of San Francisco ate into his business. Backers went bankrupt, he became unable to pay his debts, and he lost everything. A rival named Isaiah Taber bought up his negatives and started publishing them without crediting Watkins.

It didn’t end well for Carleton Watkins. He started to go blind in the 1890s and eventually became homeless, living [ https://substack.com/redirect/51347a8f-aa10-4216-b1bd-fed0d56f42f4?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] with his wife and children in an empty boxcar. He arranged to sell his archives to Stanford University, but before he could deliver them, the San Francisco earthquake and fires of 1906 destroyed his life’s work. His negatives and records were lost to the fires. 
Blind, broke, and broken, Watkins spiraled into insanity. His family committed him to the Napa State Hospital for the Insane, and his wife started calling herself a widow. His last six years were spent in the institution; he finally died at age 86, interred in a pauper’s grave. 
As much as anyone else, Carleton Watkins helped to define the American West for the world. It was, through his lens, a place of sublime natural beauty, a remote and rugged wilderness. 
But as much as Watkins’ photos captured the American West, they also shaped it. Watkins’ photos mostly excluded the people who had lived in Yosemite [ https://substack.com/redirect/660b5151-15b6-4fa0-8ce0-3fcae20e8d5a?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] for hundreds of years. These people were eventually forced out of Yosemite to make Watkins’ ideal of pristine nature available for the white visitors who came pouring into the park. They’ll be our subject next week.

The Lost Valley

Was the damming of Hetch Hetchy a crime against nature or a necessity?

George Dillard
Dec 14

I think it matters whether I start the story with this image:

Or this one:

The first picture is an 1875 painting by Albert Bierstadt of the Hetch Hetchy valley in Yosemite National Park. The second shows the devastation of San Francisco in the moments after the 1906 earthquake, as much of the city burned to the ground.

If your focus is on the natural beauty of the valley, you’ll probably see this week’s topic — the building of the O’Shaughnessy Dam on the Tuolumne River in California — as an awful mistake, perhaps even a crime against nature. If your focus is on the needs of the growing city of San Francisco, you’ll be more likely to see the dam as a necessity, justified by sober cost-benefit analysis.

The controversy over damming Hetch Hetchy was, in some ways, a culmination of many of the themes we’ve been looking at for weeks now in this series — the idea of wilderness, the winners and losers of the “conquest” of the American West, and the ways in which money and nature were in constant collision.

We’ll start with the pretty pictures.

You may be familiar with the “star attractions” of Yosemite National Park. Five million people visit Half Dome, El Capitan, and Bridal Veil Falls every year; these areas are fairly close to one another in the Yosemite Valley and are fairly easy to access by road (it’s a national park truism that 95% of the visitors to a national park see the same 5% of the park because they don’t venture very far from their cars). These places are worth the trip and the crowds; they’re truly stunning.

But just north of the Yosemite Valley was a place of similar beauty, another river valley filled with flower-specked meadows and ringed by granite cliffs. You could hear the rush of the waterfalls that cascaded down the mountainsides. This was the Hetch Hetchy valley, along the Tuolumne River. The name came from the Miwok people who lived there.

A Sierra Club booklet described the area with rapturous language:

The main part of the Tuolumne Valley is a beautiful spacious flowery lawn four or five miles long, surrounded by magnificent snowy mountains. It is about 8,500 feet above the sea, and forms the grand central High Sierra campground from which excursions are made to the noble mountains, domes, glaciers, etc.; across the range to the Mono Lake and volcanoes; and down the Tuolumne Canon to Hetch-Hetchy…. My last visit to the valley was made in the autumn of last year, with William Keith, the artist. The leaf-colors were then ripe, and the great god-like rocks in repose seemed to glow with life. The artist, under their spell, wandered day after day along the beautiful river and through the groves and gardens, studying the wonderful scenery; and, after making about forty sketches, declared with enthusiasm that in picturesque beauty and charm Hetch-Hetchy surpassed even Yosemite.

It’s impossible to tell the story of Hetch Hetchy without talking about Muir, who you can see here in the valley:

Muir returned to the Yosemite area countless times over several decades and became the area’s greatest champion. It was his writing and activism that made this place a blueprint for the national park system. He tried to capture the exhilaration of spending time in a place as beautiful as Yosemite:

I was out every day, and often all night, sleeping but little, studying the so-called wonders and common things ever on show, wading, climbing, sauntering among the blessed storms and calms, rejoicing in almost everything alike that I could see or hear: the glorious brightness of frosty mornings; the sunbeams pouring over the white domes and crags into the groves end waterfalls, kindling marvelous iris fires in the hoarfrost and spray; the great forests and mountains in their deep noon sleep; the good-night alpenglow; the stars; the solemn gazing moon, drawing the huge domes and headlands one by one glowing white out of the shadows hushed and breathless like an audience in awful enthusiasm, while the meadows at their feet sparkle with frost-stars like the sky; the sublime darkness of storm-nights, when all the lights are out; the clouds in whose depths the frail snow-flowers grow; the behavior and many voices of the different kinds of storms, trees, birds, waterfalls, and snow-avalanches in the ever-changing weather.

To people like Muir, a place like Hetch Hetchy was holy, and no amount of economic reward would justify sullying it.

In San Francisco, the view was different. The city had grown from having 50,000 residents in 1860 to 340,000 in 1900, becoming the country’s 9th biggest urban area. The main constraint on the city’s growth was, as was so often the case in the West, water.

In the 19th century, the city relied on the Spring Valley Water Company, which had a monopoly on the water business.

Spring Valley was widely regarded as corrupt and extractive. Its prices were high and the public resented it. This 1908 cartoon from the San Francisco Call sums up how a lot of San Franciscans felt about their water barons:

The water issue came to a head when an earthquake measuring 7.9 on the Richter scale hit in April 1906. The quake damaged the already inadequate water system, and citizens had to watch their city burn as bucket brigades fruitlessly tried to put out the flames.

The earthquake turned the city’s attention to a plan that had already been brewing for some time: damming Hetch Hetchy. The very features that stunned visitors with their beauty — sheer cliff walls, a winding river, and a flat meadow — made the area a good candidate for a dam and reservoir. The only problem was that it was in a national park.

What ensued over the next seven years was a battle between the engineers and the economists on one side and the nature-lovers on the other.

The engineer John Freeman, for example, pushed for a dam on the grounds that Hetch Hetchy was an ideal site for a dam, it might be nice to have a lake there, and, after all, it wasn’t quite as pretty as the Yosemite Valley anyway:

From my own investigations, which have now been in progress, off and on, for more than two years, I am convinced that the Hetch Hetchy is much the best mountain source for the San Francisco District, and I am also convinced that the city in course of its development of works for water supply will make the Hetch Hetchy valley more beautiful, and a far more useful instrument of pleasure than it is today...

What one finds there today is beautiful, but it is relatively tame in comparison with the far more grand and varied Yosemite.

The flooding of the valley floor, giving in its place a deeply sheltered lake with an outlet so planned that the bottom could never again become uncovered, would present features different from anything found in the Yosemite or elsewhere in California. The flooding of the margins and the stocking of the lake with trout would cure the mosquito pest and would thus double and perhaps treble the length of the season in which one can visit the valley in pleasure.

...it can be proved that by taking any source for its future water supply other than those found above the Hetch Hetchy, Eleanor, Cherry and Poopenaut reservoir sites, the San Francisco District would waste certainly more than ten million dollars; ultimately perhaps more than thirty million dollars,....and it is a matter of plain common sense that an extra burden of ten or twenty or thirty million dollars should not be placed on the tax payers and other citizens of the cities around San Francisco Bay merely to satisfy the peculiar views of a few solitude lovers or in order to meet the unreasonable demands of some sentimentalists who have been led astray by misstatements of the case and who have had no direct knowledge of the facts.

The local press framed the Hetch Hetchy project as a way to liberate San Francisco from the clutches of the evil Spring Valley Company:

Meanwhile, Muir and his environmentalist allies sputtered with disbelief that anyone would alter such a beautiful place for something as base as a municipal water supply:

That anyone would try to destroy [Hetch Hetchy Valley] seems; incredible; but sad experience shows that there are people good enough and bad enough for anything. The proponents of the dam scheme bring forward a lot of bad arguments to prove that the only righteous thing to do with the people’s parks is to destroy them bit by bit as they are able. Their arguments are curiously like those of the devil, devised for the destruction of the first garden. . . .

These temple destroyers, devotees of ravaging commercialism, seem to have a perfect contempt for Nature, and, instead of lifting their eyes to the God of the mountains, lift them to the Almighty Dollar.

Dam Hetch Hetchy! As well dam for water-tanks the people’s cathedrals and churches, for no holier temple has ever been consecrated by the heart of man.

The engineers won. Congress passed the Raker Act in 1913, authorizing the building of the O’Shaughnessy Dam inside the national park. Construction took more than 20 years.

Eventually, the valley was logged and submerged beneath 300 feet of water. It’s the source of 80% of San Francisco’s water supply today.

Although the engineers were right about some things — Hetch Hetchy was a practical location for a reservoir — they were wrong about others. The lake did not provide another way for people to “visit the valley in pleasure.” Nobody really goes there; only about 50,000 of the park’s 5 million yearly visitors bother to drive up to the reservoir. Hetch Hetchy no longer inspires anybody.

But the failure of preservationists to protect Hetch Hetchy did have an effect elsewhere. “Remember Hetch Hetchy” served as a useful slogan in future battles over development in national parks. Environmentalists defeated attempts in the 1950s to build dams in Dinosaur National Monument and Grand Canyon National Park by invoking the memory of the flooding of Hetch Hetchy.

In the end, San Francisco got the water it needed, but the West lost something irreplacable. Was the damming of Hetch Hetchy a triumph or a tragedy? That depends on how you think about the history of the West. Was the story of the West a narrative of the conquest of a land of opportunity, the harnessing of resources to build a thriving nation? Or was it a tale of the exploitation of people and nature that couldn’t protect themselves?

These questions remain unanswered, and the battle over Hetch Hetchy continues — there’s now a growing movement to demolish the dam and attempt to restore the valley.

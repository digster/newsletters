Having your picture taken in the 1860s was not very fun [ https://substack.com/redirect/871e5da5-d81e-4d79-b67d-6670c95b71f6?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ]. You couldn’t just snap a quick selfie; instead, you’d have to go to a portrait studio and plunk down a fair amount of money. Because early cameras needed more time and exposure to capture an image than modern ones do, people would have to remain still for quite a long time while staring into blinding light. Photographers often utilized constricting braces to limit people’s ability to move their heads. If you sneezed or fidgeted, you’d ruin the whole thing.
This is one reason why early photos look so stiff and artificial to us today. The people in these images were trying to convey as much as they could about themselves — their social class, their inner qualities, their dignity — while enduring an awkward and uncomfortable experience. The photos we see of people from this era often represent their one and only shot to capture their images for posterity. Ralph Waldo Emerson [ https://substack.com/redirect/871e5da5-d81e-4d79-b67d-6670c95b71f6?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] was not alone in thinking that his photo was “the portrait of a mask instead of a man.”
But, as often happens with a new medium, some people [ https://substack.com/redirect/1961c42a-23fd-43c8-a74c-8c0c416d46d9?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] had an intuitive grasp of the potential power of the camera. Perhaps the most consequential person to embrace photography was the most photographed American of his era [ https://substack.com/redirect/f01db2a4-4b3f-4879-afc3-89975f24a4e6?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ], Frederick Douglass.
That’s right — the abolitionist and orator appears in more photos than Abraham Lincoln, Ulysses S. Grant, or any other person of his era. And that was on purpose. Douglass was an enthusiastic adopter of this new technology and used it to achieve his political goals. He understood earlier than most that controlling one’s own image was a form of political power.
In 1861, as the nation was falling apart into a Civil War, Douglass got up in front of a crowd in Boston and spoke about the link between “Pictures and Progress [ https://substack.com/redirect/d293e70b-7a01-41d1-87aa-682983293f94?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ].” The speech reveals him to be an optimist about the democratizing possibilities of new technology.
Douglass saw Louis Daguerre, the inventor of photography, as an underappreciated figure who had brought new possibilities to the masses:
Men of all conditions may see themselves as others see them. What was once the exclusive luxury of the rich and great is now within reach of all. The humbled servant girl whose income is but a few shillings per week may now possess a more perfect likeness of herself than noble ladies and court royalty, with all its precious treasures could purchase fifty years ago…. That so little is said of the Author of this pictorial abundance is explained only on the principle that men are proven to value things more for their rarity than for their excellence
He went on to appreciate the emotional power of an image:
As to the moral and social influence of pictures, it would hardly be extravagant to say of it, what Moore has said of that of ballads, give me the making of a nations ballads, and I care not who has the making of its Laws. The picture and the ballad are alike, if not equally social forces — the one reaching and swaying the heart by the eye, and the other by the ear.
As an instrument of wit, of biting satire, the picture is admitted to be unrivalled. It strikes human nature on the weakest of all its many weak sides, and upon the instant, makes the hit probable to all beholders. The dullest vision can see and comprehend at a glance the full effect of a point which may have taxed the wit and skill of the artist many hours, and days.
Douglass argued that humans are unique among animals in their ability to make and appreciate images. Then he made a rather abrupt transition toward his usual topics — abolition, national disunion, and racial justice. Audience members might have wondered what Douglass’s interest in photography had to do with his larger political project until he ties political and technological progress together at the end of the speech:
The Increased facilities of locommotion, the growing inter communication of distant nations, the rapid transmission of intelligence over the globe — the world wide ramifications of commerce — bringing together the knowledge, the skill, and the mental power of the world — cannot but dispel prejudice[,] dissolve the granite barriers of arbitrary power, bring the world into, peace and unity, and at last crown the world with just[ice,] Liberty, and brotherly kindness. …
In every locommotive a herald of progress — the startling scream of the Engine — and the small ticking sound of the telegraph are a like phrophecies of hope to the philanthropist, and warnings to the systems of slavery, superstition and oppression to get themselves away to the mirky shades of barbarism.
It’s a real techno-optimist manifesto (so to speak [ https://substack.com/redirect/bc19dab7-9345-43ba-ac2c-c5b8979a82a3?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ]). Douglass saw new technologies as connecting and liberating people rather than oppressing them. And he especially believed that photographic technology, used well, could propel the cause of equality forward.
Frederick Douglass got his picture taken as often as he could because he saw photography as a less prejudiced medium than a painting or drawing could ever be. He had long believed, as he wrote [ https://substack.com/redirect/58209078-b2a4-4d7b-9638-6e1dde697f9b?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] in 1849, that
negroes can never have impartial portraits at the hands of white artists. It seems to us next to impossible for white men to take likenesses of black men, without most grossly exaggerating their distinctive features.
But a photograph, Douglass believed, told the truth. He could be seen as he really was, not as a white person imagined him to be. He could use photographs of himself to combat the flood of racist caricatures that misled white Americans about black people’s abilities and intelligence. These pictures helped Douglass to become [ https://substack.com/redirect/e066d2cb-38c8-490f-aa26-4d83c1700d0f?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] a “Representative American man,” as his contemporary and friend James McCune Smith put it.
Douglass’ portraits, whether taken early in his life or as he aged, show an individual of fierce intelligence and dignity. He never smiled, because he didn’t want to lean into the stereotype of the “happy slave [ https://substack.com/redirect/f01db2a4-4b3f-4879-afc3-89975f24a4e6?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ].”
Not only did he sit for well over 100 portraits, Douglass also worked hard to circulate his image. He carried pocket-sized photos (called cartes de visite) with him. Every time he left his image with someone, he was spreading the idea that he was a man like any other.
I doubt you needed to see these pictures of Frederick Douglass to know what he looked like, because his campaign was so successful. Every American history textbook, every documentary about the Civil War, and every museum exhibit about the period serves as evidence that Douglass was correct about the power of the photograph to influence the way people saw him and his fellow black Americans.
We’ve become a much more image-oriented society in the years since Frederick Douglass went around handing out little photos of himself. Does Douglass’s optimism about the power of the image still hold true?
So much of our information about the world comes from quick glances at images. The choice of a lead photo on the front page of a newspaper or website can shape the opinions of the people who will never read the article. People spend hours every day flicking between visual stimuli on Instagram or TikTok, allowing the images they encounter to shape their understanding of reality.
Sometimes, when I run through the park near my house, I will see a young couple with expensive-looking camera equipment trying to get an Instagrammable shot of their kid in a scenic spot. If you’ve ever seen something like this — where the husband instructs the wife again and again to lift their child up into the air at just the right angle to catch the light through the leaves — you know that the photo they eventually post online will be, if not a lie, not quite truthful either. It would be more representative to post one of the 37 failed attempts, maybe the one where the kid is on the verge of tears or the one where a random jogger like me runs through the background.
There are more consequential ways in which an image can mislead, as well. On the one hand, the fact that everyone has a high-powered camera in their pocket allows more of life to be recorded than ever before. Ordinary citizens are able to capture important events like the behavior of ICE agents and circulate them to a global audience. On the other hand, a well-cropped photo or cleverly-edited video snippet can give us the wrong impression about an event.
Now, as AI tools get better at simulating reality, we’re likely to struggle even more with photographic images. I frequently find myself wondering if a too-good-to-be-true photo is real or if it’s AI. The current administration has already manipulated [ https://substack.com/redirect/633a2c47-4e77-4914-807c-19675d23870d?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] photographs (of a person of color, no less) and passed them off as real. These days, the photograph — especially the AI-manipulated image — seems more likely to be a weapon that weakens democracy than something that liberates us.
But maybe Frederick Douglass would be more at home in our era than it seems. After all, was his constant portrait-taking much different from the efforts of the influencer to project a particular image to their followers? Douglass didn’t necessarily show his true self in these images; he showed the side of himself that he thought America needed to see.
Martha Cutter writes [ https://substack.com/redirect/446d9207-907c-4de5-b5a7-98d4b8e2450d?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] that “Douglass understood that if he did not direct the creation of his image, others would control it.” He used photography to spread the version of the truth that he wanted Americans to see, carefully curating his image in the American mind.
Frederick Douglass knew that the image, properly orchestrated, could be a liberating force — and that, in the hands of bad actors, images could be used to mislead and even oppress. In this, as in many other areas, he was a man ahead of his time.

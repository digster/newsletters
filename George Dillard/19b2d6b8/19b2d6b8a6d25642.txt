Disgust is an interesting reaction. On the one hand, it’s evolutionarily necessary. Most scientists agree that we evolved our sense of disgust to keep ourselves safe. We avoid the health problems that would come from consuming rotting meat or poisonous plants because we think those things are gross.
On the other hand, our sense of disgust is cultural and personal as much as it is biological. In some cultures, people eat insects as a normal part of their diets; in others, nothing could be more icky than crunching down on the thorax of a cricket. These can change over time, too. I remember when most people I knew thought raw fish was revolting; now, after decades of exposure, Americans generally love sushi. And we each have our own personal disgust triggers — something about my childhood convinced me that drinking a glass of milk is incredibly gross, while others learned to guzzle the stuff with relish.
But there’s something else about disgust that I find fascinating — a lot of the time, we kind of like being disgusted. In fact, we seek out all sorts of unpleasant experiences. That’s why horror movies and roller coasters are big business.
So we simultaneously find disgusting things like rotting flesh or crawling insects repellent and fascinating. We can see our complex reaction to disgust in reality TV, which spawned a whole genre of shows that forced people to eat “disgusting” foods for our amusement: The Amazing Race made its contestants eat cow lips and insects, while Fear Factor forced people to eat horse rectum and leeches.
Why do we flee disgust in some circumstances, while we’re drawn to it in others?
Psychologists [ https://substack.com/redirect/8a42e1f8-0b0b-4ba3-bd28-414a9827dd11?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] speculate that our brains are attuned to pay attention to disgusting things, so we’re drawn to the gross for evolutionary reasons. Coltan Scrivner argues that we have a streak of “morbid curiosity [ https://substack.com/redirect/51b6f0d0-45c9-4eab-9341-c36f6fd34bec?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ]” because it’s valuable to learn about things that might someday pose a danger to us.
In circumstances where we know we are perfectly safe — like when the horse rectum is safely on the other side of a TV screen — psychologist Nina Strohminger [ https://substack.com/redirect/8a42e1f8-0b0b-4ba3-bd28-414a9827dd11?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] says that we can experience “benign masochism,” a sensation where our adverse reaction disappears and we enjoy the “arousal” of disgust. As our societies have become safer and the natural world has become less of a threat to us, we’ve become especially attuned to the disgusting because we’re still looking for the thrills that we’ve mostly edited out of our lives.
We’ve been enjoying the thrill of disgust since the very beginning of filmed entertainment.
You can see it in this famous movie from 1903; it’s called The Cheese Mites:
It’s a pretty good example of silent filmmaking. The story is clear (dapper gentleman sits down to enjoy a cheese sandwich, but when he looks closely, he sees bugs crawling all over his cheese), and the footage is compelling (he’s disgusted and a bit fascinated by what’s growing in his lunch).
Cheese mites [ https://substack.com/redirect/a6e981f3-d20e-4741-a636-a3de26711e62?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] are a real thing. If you’ve eaten aged cheese with a rind, like some cheddars or Stilton, you’ve probably enjoyed mites’ handiwork. These tiny arachnids — just visible with the naked eye — feed on the fungi that grow on cheese and deposit [ https://substack.com/redirect/464a5617-4e7a-4594-8322-42a2bd45c8d3?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] certain funky flavors.
The mites on this man’s cheese are harmless (as are the mites that are all over us and our houses [ https://substack.com/redirect/2768ecb3-2fb5-4744-a5d1-4714bffaa734?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ]). People usually don’t end up eating the mites — cheesemakers keep them from penetrating beyond the rind — but it’s still a bit disconcerting to learn that your lunch could be swimming with little bugs.
The Cheese Mites film was part of a series created by Charles Urban, a well-known British producer, and F. Martin Duncan, a naturalist, to interest the audience in science and the natural world. These films allowed people to watch previously unseeable phenomena on the big screen, at an effective magnification [ https://substack.com/redirect/227bea4b-1367-47c4-b977-12383f6249df?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] of up to 76,000,000 times.
The Cheese Mites was part of a set of films on the “Unseen World.” It was shown alongside other films which seem to have been lost, but the titles (Circulation of Blood in a Frog’s Foot, Typhoid Bacteria, The Water Hydra, and Red Sludge Worms) give us a sense of their content.
The films captivated audiences and received a flurry of press attention. Their technique — essentially filming through a microscope — had never been tried before, and was hailed as a major breakthrough. One article enthused [ https://substack.com/redirect/227bea4b-1367-47c4-b977-12383f6249df?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] that “it is now possible to watch upon the screen the actual processes of protoplasmic life, and show a large audience what has hitherto only been observable by the scientist.”
While some of the films were simply fascinating, others, like The Cheese Mites and, presumably, Red Sludge Worms, were meant to provoke the audience. In reviews, the press happily played up the disgusting nature of the movies. A typical article said [ https://substack.com/redirect/227bea4b-1367-47c4-b977-12383f6249df?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] that “A visit to the Alhambra [Theater] last Monday evening resulted in a firm resolve never to eat cheese or drink water again. A microscopic examination of the former…. revealed one of the most blood-curdling pictures imaginable with mites of various shapes and sizes, but all equally hideous, prowling around in the most businesslike manner.”
The reviews themselves reflect the combination of revulsion and excitement that the filmmakers surely meant to provoke. The Topical Times advised [ https://substack.com/redirect/227bea4b-1367-47c4-b977-12383f6249df?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] that “anyone in want of a fine attack of the jim-jams cannot do better than hurry up to the Alhambra.”
The Cheese Mites was a sensation in its time, a predecessor to movies [ https://substack.com/redirect/a3300d67-d24e-4d97-b1ce-6ca71c477b5b?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ], from Misery to Saw to Braveheart, that attracted attention for scenes that made the audience squirm.
But The Cheese Mites wasn’t just an unsettling little scene. Like those other movies — and unlike Duncan and Urban’s other grossout films — it had a bit of context. Rather than just showing the mites, the movie put the viewer in the position of the protagonist, leaning in to realize that their cheese was crawling with tiny bugs.
Filmmakers learned that you needed more than just some footage of red sludge worms or cheese mites to captivate your audience. You needed some stakes, a protagonist on whose behalf you could enjoy a bit of benign masochism.
Duncan and Urban had happened upon another useful thing, as well. Filmmakers could use the power and allure of disgust to interest people, especially children, in the natural world. The logic of The Cheese Mites has since animated thousands of science curricula [ https://substack.com/redirect/a3c47203-d257-49c4-9c24-1acc5f11e9c2?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] and nature TV shows [ https://substack.com/redirect/84a5110a-741a-4699-93ec-cc03f0e1c103?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ]. It’s now a standard tactic of science educators to try to hook kids with stories [ https://substack.com/redirect/c7bb42de-6d32-464e-be25-be659d7669e4?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] about “poop cubes” and “zombie crickets.”
Educators and filmmakers have learned, as Urban and Duncan did, that sometimes you have to catch the viewer’s attention to make them interested in learning something. And there are few better ways to make somebody pay attention than to gross them out.

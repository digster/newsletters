At some point around the turn of the last century, a New York food merchant sent a telegram to a colleague in New Orleans. He a [ https://substack.com/redirect/a0a90925-340d-43de-870f-4af187e5fc03?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ]sked the people in New Orleans to contact a Mr. Simmons, from whom the cranberries would be bought. The message [ https://substack.com/redirect/a0a90925-340d-43de-870f-4af187e5fc03?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] read: “Cranberries rising. Send at once 50 barrels, per Simmons.”
Not long after, the merchant received far more persimmons than he could handle, with apologies that his correspondents couldn’t quite find 50 barrels of them in New Orleans.
This rather expensive mistake was a result of the technology that these people were using to communicate. Telegraph operators used Morse code, which spells out letters through a series of dots and dashes. They paused briefly to indicate that they were moving on to a new letter, and a bit more to indicate a new word. It seems that the New York and New Orleans operators weren’t quite on the same wavelength, leading the man in New Orleans to write down “persimmons” instead of “per Simmons.”
The telegraph opened up amazing new possibilities for communication after its invention in the middle of the 19th century. It must have felt like magic to send a message across the country in a matter of minutes. But this new technology also changed the way people wrote and spoke.
Telegraphic communication required brevity and specificity — brevity because people generally paid by the word, and specificity because shortened messages like the one above were easily misinterpreted, and it was expensive to go back and forth clarifying matters. So, journalist Robert Lincoln O’Brien tells us in his 1904 article [ https://substack.com/redirect/a0a90925-340d-43de-870f-4af187e5fc03?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] “Machinery and English Style,” English prose started to conform to the shape of telegraphic communication.
The telegraph pushed people into formulaic speech. One way to save money on a telegram was to use a code word [ https://substack.com/redirect/e2fb56af-d579-4eea-b68b-d8db01a7f2d3?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] to convey an entire generic sentence. If sent by a merchant, “GULLIBLE,” for example, meant [ https://substack.com/redirect/3563d844-8070-4578-b875-e645b2ceef73?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] “Baggage seized by the Customs.” People became more used to stock phrases because they were easier and cheaper to send; some of the variety and invention seem to have disappeared from the way people wrote and spoke.
The technology also encouraged longer, more specific words. As O’Brien notes, since you were paying by the word, not the letter, and didn’t want misunderstandings, you might write “superintendent” instead of “chief” or “head” because a word that long would be harder for the telegraph operators to confuse with something else. Operators also started to cut down on adverbial phrases at the beginning of sentences, which were expensive and too easy to attach to another sentence, as well as complex sentences requiring punctuation beyond periods (which were clearly indicated with the word “STOP”).
So the new technology of the telegraph enabled much more written communication, but it also shaped it. These changes didn’t stay isolated in telegrams; the more people became used to sending and reading these sorts of messages, the more these quirks of telegraphic English leaked into the culture at large.
A little while back, Sam Kriss wrote an interesting piece about AI-assisted writing [ https://substack.com/redirect/a93340f1-a9e0-44dd-bccc-33329d4b0920?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] (AI-ese, if you will), essentially asking why it is so weird. AI-ese has a number of tells that I’m sure you’ve started to notice.
Kriss summarizes a few of them: LLMs love the words “delve” and “tapestry,” they mess up sensory metaphors, and, “As everyone knows, A.I. writing always uses em dashes, and it always says, ‘It’s not X, it’s Y.’”
He also notes the rapidity with which AI-ese is making its way into our society at large:
In the British Parliament, for instance, transcripts show that M.P.s have suddenly started opening their speeches with the phrase “I rise to speak.” On a single day this June, it happened 26 times. “I rise to speak in support of the amendment.” “I rise to speak against Clause 10.” Which would be fine, if not for the fact that this is not something British parliamentarians said very much previously. Among American lawmakers, however, beginning a speech this way is standard practice. A.I.s are not always so sensitive to these cultural differences…
The more we’re exposed to A.I., the more we unconsciously pick up its tics, and it spreads from there. Some of the British parliamentarians who started their speeches with the phrase “I rise to speak” probably hadn’t used A.I. at all. They had just noticed that everyone around them was saying it and decided that maybe they ought to do the same. Perhaps that day will come for us, too. Soon, without really knowing why, you will find yourself talking about the smell of fury and the texture of embarrassment. You, too, will be saying “tapestry.” You, too, will be saying “delve.”
This is probably true — we’re likely headed for a future in which more and more of our language starts to sound like AI-ese, either because it’s generated by an LLM, it’s smoothed out by an AI tool like Grammarly, or because we absorb its preferences by osmosis. Some human writers will mimic AI (which, in turn, is trying to mimic human writers). Others will work to distinguish themselves and their writing from the AI-generated stuff (as an example, I’ve been debating whether to leave the em-dash in the first paragraph of this sentence lest it look too AI-adjacent).
But we shouldn’t be too alarmed by these changes, I suppose, because our technologies have been changing how we speak and write for a long time.

Before the printing press, people didn’t write all that much. Most communication was oral. It seems to have taken place in a bewildering variety of dialects, and there were a lot of different ways to spell the same word. In England [ https://substack.com/redirect/d4f47c09-a6d4-47d8-9555-8670fa0d44ed?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ], for example, there were five main regional dialects. Historians have found that there were 30 different ways to spell “church,” 45 ways to spell “receive,” and nearly 500 spellings of “though.” There were significant structural differences, too: Northerners, for example, said “-and” at the end of a gerund instead of “-ing.”
These variations were troublesome for printers, who made more money when they could quickly and efficiently typeset books. As T.H. Howard Hill writes [ https://substack.com/redirect/eaeb4fa0-92b3-4363-ab7a-ad6d49ffa747?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ], “The standardization of spelling occurred in the printing house, at first unselfconsciously, when (unavoidably) motives of efficiency, through which any technology advances, came into play.”
So printers started to prune the English language of its variety, making the spelling of words easier and more consistent. They removed runic characters [ https://substack.com/redirect/d4f47c09-a6d4-47d8-9555-8670fa0d44ed?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] from English (replacing “þe” with “the”) because they couldn’t reliably get hold of type sets with those characters (”y” was the closest they could get to “þ,” giving rise to “ye,” as in “Ye Olde Shoppe”).
The fact that people were producing books in English at all was a fresh development during this period. Before the printing press, most European books were in Latin. As printing spread, more books were printed in vernacular languages, but printers didn’t give all languages equal exposure. Some languages and dialects won out, and others withered away. Elizabeth Eisenstein writes [ https://substack.com/redirect/1213911a-cd11-4628-9a34-67247be52bd3?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] that
Printing “preserved and codified, sometimes even created” certain vernaculars. Its absence during the sixteenth century among small linguistic groups “demonstrably led” to the disappearance or exclusion of their vernaculars from the realm of literature. Its presence among similar groups in the same century ensured the possibility of intermittent revivals or continued expansion.
The printing press had a standardizing and somewhat diversifying effect on language, shaped by the efficiencies that the new technology provided. It became possible in the early modern period to get exposure to many more types of writing than had been previously available, but only those styles that dovetailed with printing technology.
If you read political speeches from the 1800s — well, let’s be honest, you probably won’t read a political speech from the 1800s, because you’ll have a hard time making your way through something so wordy and dense.
Check out the beginning [ https://substack.com/redirect/1500fba5-9459-46f6-b092-5dd949ceb5ba?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] of one of Abraham Lincoln’s speeches from a debate with Stephen Douglas in 1858, as he starts to unpick his complex opinions on racial equality (the entire speech is 32 pages long):
While I was at the hotel to-day, an elderly gentleman called upon me to know whether I was really in favor of producing a perfect equality between the negroes and white people. [Great Laughter.] While I had not proposed to myself on this occasion to say much on that subject, yet as the question was asked me I thought I would occupy perhaps five minutes in saying something in regard to it. I will say then that I am not, nor ever have been, in favor of bringing about in any way the social and political equality of the white and black races, [applause]-that I am not nor ever have been in favor of making voters or jurors of negroes, nor of qualifying them to hold office, nor to intermarry with white people; and I will say in addition to this that there is a physical difference between the white and black races which I believe will forever forbid the two races living together on terms of social and political equality. And inasmuch as they cannot so live, while they do remain together there must be the position of superior and inferior, and I as much as any other man am in favor of having the superior position assigned to the white race. I say upon this occasion I do not perceive that because the white man is to have the superior position the negro should be denied every thing. I do not understand that because I do not want a negro woman for a slave I must necessarily want her for a wife. [Cheers and laughter.] My understanding is that I can just let her alone.
In comparison, a linguistic analysis [ https://substack.com/redirect/3b2faaeb-1ae9-4466-882f-c87c97e36b24?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] of the Trump-Biden debates in 2020 found that both candidates spoke at a fourth-grade (Trump) or fifth-grade (Biden) level, with sentences that averaged 9 or 10 words in length. What happened? In part, it’s television’s fault. Daniel Hallin writes [ https://substack.com/redirect/afa8701f-e3ae-45ae-a4c5-1472a11113ee?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] about the reduction of American political rhetoric to little sound bites suitable for the TV news. In the early days of TV, news programs would show clips of politicians speaking for a minute or more. But as attention spans shrunk, the news showed shorter clips of political speech, and the politicians adapted to the changing conditions. By 1988, the average sound bite on TV was 8.9 seconds long, and politicians were expressing themselves more broadly, more pithily, and much more briefly.
Television also standardized the way people talked. A study [ https://substack.com/redirect/c0fd3b80-1ec5-4236-b2fe-61cb2ce67a24?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] on people from Glasgow found that the more they watched EastEnders, a popular British TV series that depicted Londoners, the more likely they were to pronounce words like the characters on TV did.
In the United States, television has promoted what linguists call “Standard American English [ https://substack.com/redirect/cea38048-cdcd-4648-b91f-d03b4fe5bf52?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ]” (essentially, how newscasters speak) as the most prestigious and “normal” version of the language, along with some regional accents that show up more often in our pop culture (think “valley girl” Californian). In many cases, people code-switch in their speech, ditching their distinctive accent in formal settings while doubling down on it when with friends and family.
Finally, in recent decades, the internet has changed how we speak and write.
My children sometimes say “lol” (pronounced “lawl”) to indicate that they think something is slightly funny but not actually laughter-inducing. It’s a testament to the ever-evolving nature of language that “lol” means precisely the opposite of what it was originally supposed to mean (“laughing out loud”). In many cases, as John McWhorter [ https://substack.com/redirect/39ab72d8-a188-4c87-8451-e4318cdd6a91?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] points out, “lol” is not about humor at all but rather “basic empathy between texters. What began as signifying laughter morphed into easing tension and creating a sense of equality.”
This kind of text-speak is an artifact of the technologies of my kids’ childhoods: the inefficiencies of early texting, the character limits of early social platforms, and the shortening attention span that their online lives have encouraged. Our language has been littered with acronymic slang (TLDR, IDK, BTW) from the world of texting. Some artifacts of social media and texting have made their way into speech (someone might, for example, say aloud, “hashtag winning”). Meanwhile, the incentive for people on social media to convey their thoughts as briefly and quickly as possible has created new conventions, like “because-noun [ https://substack.com/redirect/30e41a94-b349-4ab0-a143-2418646d7d40?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ]” (”We can’t afford that because math”).
As more of our communication has moved away from speech into text, we’ve started to use more “tone management” techniques to make sure the person on the other side of the conversation understands our intent. Exclamation points [ https://substack.com/redirect/a6df7b1e-72ac-4a18-9f83-58fa69d67300?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] now litter our emails so that our colleagues know we come in peace.
Written language has also expanded beyond the alphabet. Emojis are making their way into our written text — I’ve even had students insert emojis into essays in recent years. Emojis are deployed as punctuation or intensifiers, which change the meaning of sentences. My children now think a period at the end of a text means I’m mad at them; for them, there’s a huge difference between “I’m looking forward to picking you up 🥳” and “I’m looking forward to picking you up.” These illocutionary markers [ https://substack.com/redirect/e1b3ab92-978b-4a39-a928-823730bdd95d?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] tell our readers how to interpret what we’ve written.
And now along comes the large language model, designed to simulate human writing. We’re already seeing that it’s not just going to copy us, it’s going to guide us — and as we delve deeper into our relationship with this new technology, we’re going to create a rich tapestry of changes in the way we speak and write.

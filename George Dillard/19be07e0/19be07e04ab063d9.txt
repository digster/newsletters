There’s going to be an arch, apparently. The best arch. The president tells us [ https://substack.com/redirect/8ea80ae1-d580-4d14-9016-0f3aaeb54698?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] that
It’s something that is so special, it will be like the one in Paris. But to be honest with you, it blows it away, blows it away in every way. … It’s the only city in the world that’s of great importance that doesn’t have a triumphal arc. You know what that is. And this one is going to blow them all away.
There’s going to be a parade, too, and a big fireworks display. People can attend a “Great American State Fair” with exhibits from every state. The White House will host a UFC fight on the president’s 80th birthday. And each state will send two young athletes to compete in the “Patriot Games,” may the odds be ever in their favor.
As the plans take shape for the celebration of the country’s 250th birthday, several themes are emerging. The events will reflect Donald Trump’s personal tastes and the conservative ideology of his supporters (one of the four main sections of the official website [ https://substack.com/redirect/b535acfe-8f8a-48ed-b669-1331bc9cc107?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] is “America Prays”). It will be big and bombastic, and the president will be at the center of it.
It remains to be seen whether this will be a unifying event for the country (the president’s stated goal [ https://substack.com/redirect/834441d9-6358-4694-9b65-d9d3634bb3f1?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] is to engage every American in the festivities) or whether Trump, as he so often does, will use it to exacerbate the nation’s divisions. But it’s clear that the anniversary celebrations will happen at a particular fractious time in American history, a period when most Americans [ https://substack.com/redirect/91f95e6a-cdff-4ddc-937a-617c043b8bdb?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] think the country is on the wrong track and there are serious questions about the health of American democracy.
So it’s like some of the nation’s other big anniversaries, then. The centennial and bicentennial celebrations, in 1876 and 1976, respectively, also took place at difficult times in American history. Leaders used the occasions to try to transcend the country’s troubles, but each only provided a temporary distraction. Will the same be true at the 250th?
The centennial celebration in 1876 took place in the shadow of the Civil War. President Ulysses Grant — best known as the methodical and ruthless general who had pounded the South into submission — presided over the festivities. The celebrations were both an expression of relief that the terrible war was over and a tentative effort to craft a new, unifying narrative for the nation.
The shadow of the Civil War wasn’t the only problem facing the country on its 100th anniversary. Reconstruction was failing, as white supremacists in the South regained control of the levers of power there. More widely, the country was enduring the “Long Depression [ https://substack.com/redirect/90f608c0-74c0-445a-a9f8-d62aa5b9b90a?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ],” which had been triggered by the Panic of 1873. Things were incredibly bad — the economic contraction of the 1870s lasted longer than the one in the Great Depression (65 months vs. 43 months [ https://substack.com/redirect/71aea25f-46cd-4fde-a0b7-2f5bf1c45c43?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ]), unemployment spiked, and wages couldn’t keep up with prices. This shock was the beginning of the Gilded Age, which was characterized by political corruption (there was plenty of it in the Grant administration [ https://substack.com/redirect/adbce49e-af53-4af5-ba6d-01acf427b39b?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ]), economic inequality, and popular discontent with the nation’s trajectory.
The planners of the celebration had a lot to overcome, so there was quite a bit riding on the Philadelphia Centennial Exposition, the centerpiece of the commemoration efforts. This was designed as a world’s fair — America’s first — with pavilions from different countries, oddities like “the largest knife and fork in the world,” and grand structures with exhibits for the 10 million people who attended.
The fair’s motto [ https://substack.com/redirect/3c524f9f-e997-4f16-a11d-48e822c8a73a?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] was an attempt to bridge the country’s sectional divides: “No North, No South, No East, No West — The Union One and Indivisible.” There was a focus on history, but not messy recent history. Instead, the organizers (and merchandise sellers) focused on the country’s founding. This was when George Washington [ https://substack.com/redirect/992bdc3b-77ae-492a-ae68-a5d91a16155e?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] was elevated to the status of a secular saint; his face appeared on countless ribbons and medallions.
But the centennial exposition was less about the past than the future as its organizers chose to highlight the country’s industrial prowess. After his opening speech, Grant, accompanied by Dom Pedro, the Emperor of Brazil, made his way to Machinery Hall and pulled the lever that started a 45-foot-tall Corliss Duplex steam engine [ https://substack.com/redirect/ba98b7ee-cb49-4c08-931d-65d17bd7cdc8?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ]. It roared to life, powering the hundreds of other machines — printing presses, sawmills, looms, and more — in the building. The exhibition featured cutting-edge technology like the first telephone, which apparently wowed [ https://substack.com/redirect/ac8521d7-f4a4-44ce-a28c-a623d60e1547?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] Dom Pedro, and exotic foods like bananas [ https://substack.com/redirect/c7d93a70-0b81-45a8-80e8-723da2f61774?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ], which caused a sensation when they were sold in bite-size chunks to fairgoers.
Historian John Maass writes [ https://substack.com/redirect/09bef6f1-fb29-4b53-b49f-eab62f05e82a?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] that “A kind of Centennial euphoria seems to have come over the travelers that made them see no evil, hear no evil, and speak no evil about America in 1876.” But the feeling was ephemeral.
The Statue of Liberty, donated by France in honor of the centennial, was supposed to be one of the centerpieces of the exhibition. But the Franco-Prussian War delayed its manufacture, so only the hand and torch were ready by 1876:
Like the statue, America was very much a work in progress in 1876, and the country’s divisions soon reemerged. The depression continued for a few more years, and labor unrest mounted — the Great Railroad Strike of 1877 would claim more than 100 lives and lead to declarations of martial law in some parts of the country. The election of 1876 ended in a deadlock, which was resolved by a tawdry backroom arrangement in which Rutherford B. Hayes ended Reconstruction in the South (condemning black Southerners to decades of discrimination) in exchange for the presidency.
The story told at the Centennial Exposition — of a united democracy poised to conquer the world with its technological might — was partially accurate at best.
A century later, the United States held another big celebration in a period of national turmoil.
As it had been in 1876, the country in 1976 was deeply divided by political, economic, and military problems. Social movements like the civil rights and women’s movements had challenged the status quo. The Vietnam War had cost the country tens of thousands of lives as well as its reputation; most Americans agreed that it had been a profound failure. The Watergate scandal, which had led to Richard Nixon’s resignation two years earlier, further contributed to Americans’ sense of national decline. It didn’t help that the country was mired in a period of “stagflation [ https://substack.com/redirect/84b821ad-d996-4c6f-a08d-7c3ebb9b41fe?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ]” marked by staggeringly high unemployment and rising costs. [ https://substack.com/redirect/e7993db3-c4ec-4119-973d-3ac71f082b0d?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ]
So when Gerald Ford — a caretaker whose main distinction was that he was the only president never elected to either the presidency or vice presidency — presided over the bicentennial, he had to overcome some seriously bad vibes in the country.
Even the bicentennial celebrations themselves were shaping up to be disappointing. The planning had been led by the American Revolution Bicentennial Commission, founded in 1966 by Lyndon Johnson. It was made up of businesspeople and politicians, and at first planned a big world’s fair in Boston or Philadelphia, similar to what had taken place in 1876. But, when he entered the White House, Richard Nixon [ https://substack.com/redirect/ac6349c8-1857-4617-997c-420a77b738bb?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] packed the commission with his own supporters, and the ARBC became seen as corrupt and wasteful.
The commission fell into disarray and eventually dissolved, replaced by an American Revolution Bicentennial Administration (ARBA). ARBA recommended a smaller set of regional events rather than one big national celebration. There were other disappointments, too — Los Angeles and Denver had bid to host the 1976 summer and winter Olympics as part of the bicentennial, only to be beaten out by Montreal and Innsbruck.
When the anniversary finally rolled around, the tone was not always exuberant. Gerald Ford asked [ https://substack.com/redirect/bc4557ff-7592-4597-880d-f4bab2a39894?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] the crowd at a preliminary event in Concord, Massachusetts, “to place the hand of healing over the heart of America.” The celebrations were smaller and more local than in 1876. It seems to have been kind of underwhelming.
The overall theme of the bicentennial seems to have been nostalgia [ https://substack.com/redirect/948ffb21-a56a-47c7-9605-26cd3a96f28b?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ], symbolized by a cross-country wagon train [ https://substack.com/redirect/9535c712-fbd9-44f0-8169-74b4c73e66b5?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ], “Operation Sail, [ https://substack.com/redirect/d05a1b39-3ea8-4991-b4fc-7be5dec2350d?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ]” a “Grand Parade of Sailing Ships,” and a “Freedom Train [ https://substack.com/redirect/d6fdde2d-4889-4ef7-9a77-36c7f8a3ff03?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ]” that toured the country with historic artifacts. Some re-enactors even crossed the Delaware River just as George Washington had in the famous painting by Emanuel Leutze.
The plan, such that there was one, seemed to be to bring the country back together by reminding Americans of unifying moments in their past. Americans might be divided in 1976, but they could at least agree on the core principles and heroes of the founding. As the scholar Svetlana Boym has written [ https://substack.com/redirect/0bd447fe-f519-408e-b5b1-302f1cd545d5?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ], this was fitting: “The twentieth century began with utopia and ended with nostalgia.” She notes that periods of nostalgia often happen “after revolutions,” as people try to soothe their unsettled nerves by imagining an idealized past; perhaps this is what happened in the United States after the upheavals of the 1960s.
The good feelings of the bicentennial didn’t last long, either. The economy tanked in the late 1970s while Jimmy Carter floundered in the presidency and the Iran hostage crisis served as a symbol of American impotence. It seems like more than a coincidence that Ronald Reagan’s candidacy for the presidency in 1980 was drenched in nostalgia for an earlier time, just as the bicentennial had been.
So America’s 250th anniversary will be held in familiar circumstances. Once again, a nation divided against itself, enduring economic precarity, and on shaky democratic ground will celebrate a major birthday. 
We will, no doubt, be regaled with fireworks, bombastic speeches, and hazy paeans to an idealized past. Maybe that arch will really get built. As in 1876 and 1976, the country’s divisions won’t be healed by a big patriotic celebration. 
But I wonder whether we’ll even get the short-lived sugar high of whatever combination of nostalgia and promise for the future allowed people to come together for past celebrations. It seems very possible that, with Donald Trump at the center of it, the 250th anniversary of America’s founding will push the country further apart.

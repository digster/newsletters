Charles Morgan, Jr. was born in Cincinnati, spent his youngest years in Kentucky, and moved to Birmingham, Alabama when he was 15 years old. There, he belonged to a “good” white family. They made a point [ https://substack.com/redirect/f2e9cc6a-2b2b-4e68-a76d-9976156f9df8?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] of being nice to their black servants.
Morgan went off to the University of Alabama, studied law, and returned to Birmingham. He got married and had a son. He started his own law firm, served [ https://substack.com/redirect/faf1e1bb-16cb-436f-bbf8-86e5e9cec6bc?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] on award committees, ran the Junior Bar Association, and generally started to establish himself as a prominent citizen of the city. Since Alabama was essentially a one-party state, this meant involving himself in the politics of the Democratic Party, which at the time was the party of white supremacy in the South.
The young lawyer was all set up to have a comfortable career. He could have spent his days taking mundane cases for paying clients, spending Saturdays at Alabama football games or down at the club with the other wealthy and powerful men in Birmingham. Maybe he could have run for political office or served on the philanthropic boards. He could have been a prosperous, respected pillar of the community. All he had to do was avoid rocking the boat too much.
But it quickly became clear that this was not Morgan’s style. Unlike so many of his fellow white Alabamians, he could not ignore or explain away the racial oppression of black people.
Birmingham was the site of some of the most egregious racist violence of the period. Some called it “Bombingham [ https://substack.com/redirect/8b7b6ea0-32dd-4ada-ac95-27f013f319e5?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ]” because of a string of explosions dating back to 1948, which targeted outspoken black ministers or black families who were perceived to be encroaching on white neighborhoods.
In April, 1960, the New York Times published [ https://substack.com/redirect/4a5ca7f7-83ab-45b5-bca3-c670ac290407?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] an article in the bottom left corner of page A1: “Fear and Hatred Grip Birmingham.” The man who wrote it, Harrison Salisbury, was much more scathing than you might expect from the Times, which works so hard these days to maintain the appearance of neutrality*.* His article described a deeply polarized city under the firm control of a harsh government.
To be honest, if you change the focus and the details a bit, much of it could describe America today.
The article captioned a picture of Police Commissioner “Bull” Connor saying he was elected on a “race hate platform.” It presciently quoted an unnamed black woman who said, “I just don’t understand the white people around here. They seem to act so crazy. It doesn’t make any sense. Don’t they know there is a limit to what people will stand?” Salisbury wrote:
Ball parks and taxicabs are segregated. So are libraries. A book featuring black rabbits and white rabbits was banned. A drive is on to forbid “Negro music” on “white” radio stations.
Every channel of communication, every medium of mutual interest, every reasoned approach, every inch of middle ground has been fragmented by the emotional dynamite of racism, enforced by the whip, the razor, the gun, the bomb, the torch, the club, the knife, the mob, the police and many branches of the state’s apparatus.
In Birmingham neither blacks nor whites talk freely. A pastor carefully closes the door before he speaks. A Negro keeps an eye on the sidewalk outside his house. A lawyer talks in the language of conspiracy….
Volunteer watchmen stand guard twenty-four hours a day over some Negro churches. Jewish synagogues have floodlights for the night and caretakers…
Birmingham’s whites and blacks share a community of fear.
City officials sued [ https://substack.com/redirect/63f89f80-489b-4a93-8021-216865e30b3f?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] Salisbury and the Times for more than $1 million, calling the articles “malicious” libel. The journalist needed a local lawyer. Charles Morgan took the case and won. Watching his fellow civic leaders — politicians, news editors, and leaders of fraternal organizations — testify under oath that there was “no trouble between whites and Negroes in Birmingham at the time of Mr. Salisbury’s article” made Morgan realize that Birmingham needed lawyers like him to stand up for truth and equality.
So he started taking other clients who questioned the racial status quo. After he represented a white minister who had dared to try to bridge the gap between white and black citizens, he got death threats [ https://substack.com/redirect/f2e9cc6a-2b2b-4e68-a76d-9976156f9df8?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ]: “You better watch out, tough guy. Some night we’ll get you alone.” He defended black people accused of crimes and represented black applicants in a lawsuit against the University of Alabama.
And then, the day after the church bombing that killed four girls in 1963, he stood up at the Young Men’s Business Club of Birmingham. He looked out at the audience: respectable, white local burghers gathered for a nice little lunch. His speech [ https://substack.com/redirect/7b568e2f-a2a2-464b-a278-b9345c4df858?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] shattered the genteel expectations of the gathering. I’m going to quote it at length because it’s so good and its themes are still so relevant:
All across Alabama, an angry, guilty people cry out their mocking shouts of indignity and say they wonder “Why?” “Who?” Everyone then “deplores” the “dastardly” act.
But you know the “who” of “Who did it?” is really rather simple. The “who” is every little individual who spreads the seeds of his hate to his neighbor and his son. The jokester, the crude oaf whose racial jokes rock the party with laughter.
The “who” is every governor who ever shouted for lawlessness and became a law violator.
It is every senator and every representative who in the halls of Congress stands and with mock humility tells the world that things back home aren’t really like they are.
It is courts that move ever so slowly, and newspapers that timorously defend the law.
It is all the Christians and all their ministers who spoke too late in anguished cries against violence. It is the coward in each of us who clucks admonitions.
We are ten years of lawless preachments, ten years of criticism of law, of courts, of our fellow man, a decade of telling school children the opposite of what the civics books say.
We are a mass of intolerance and bigotry and stand indicted before our young. We are cursed by the failure of each of us to accept responsibility, by our defense of an already dead institution.
Yesterday while Birmingham, which prides itself on the number of churches, was attending worship services, a bomb went off and an all-white police force moved into action, a police force which has been praised by city officials and others at least once a day for a month or so. A police force which has solved no bombings. A police force which many Negroes feel is perpetrating the very evils we decry….
And who is really guilty? Each of us. Each citizen who has not consciously attempted to bring about peaceful compliance with the decisions of the Supreme Court of the United States. Every citizen who votes for the candidate with the bloody flag. Every citizen and every school board member and schoolteacher and principal and businessman and judge and lawyer who has corrupted the minds of our youth; every person in this community who has in any way contributed during the past several years to the popularity of hatred, is at least as guilty, or more so, than the demented fool who threw that bomb.
If this were a movie, Morgan would have gotten a standing ovation and Alabama would have changed right then and there. But life is not a movie. The audience clapped [ https://substack.com/redirect/f2e9cc6a-2b2b-4e68-a76d-9976156f9df8?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] politely for Morgan, and then one of the men in the room asked whether the group should admit its first black member. The civic leaders looked at each other nervously; nobody said a word. Somebody called an end to the meeting, and everybody just went home.
But Charles Morgan’s life was changed forever. Anonymous callers threatened [ https://substack.com/redirect/f2e9cc6a-2b2b-4e68-a76d-9976156f9df8?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] him — “we’ll shoot you down,” they promised, “like a dog.” The callers threatened his wife and son, as well, intimating that they were following the family around town. After a few weeks, Morgan was forced [ https://substack.com/redirect/60b6ee1d-22d2-4e17-ad99-3df0b187ffaa?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] to move his family out of Birmingham.
Having taken his stand, Charles Morgan focused his life on getting his country to live up to its promise. He led lawsuits for racially fair elections in Alabama and for desegregation in courthouses and prisons. He worked for the ACLU, where he represented [ https://substack.com/redirect/fe4eb803-d2cc-4c09-8675-f05e8e278a20?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] an Army doctor who refused to work with the Green Berets out of opposition to the Vietnam War. He represented Muhammad Ali in his draft-evasion case. He also, it must be said, had a late phase in his career where he seems to have cashed in, representing the tobacco industry and other large corporations against lawsuits. He wasn’t a saint, but he did a lot more good than bad.
Charles Morgan’s choice in the early 1960s — to risk his career, his social position, and his safety to do the right thing — helped to make America better.
Why resurrect the story of Charles Morgan right now? Because our national situation feels, in some ways, like Birmingham and other parts of the country must have felt in the 1960s, and I went looking for stories that could help me understand how ordinary people met that moment. I found some inspiration in the story of Morgan.
Perhaps you’ve had the experience of realizing that a relative or friend lived through some turning point in history — World War II, the Civil Rights Movement, Vietnam. If you’re like me, you’ve asked them two questions: What was it like? And what did you do?
Not everybody who lived during the Civil Rights Movement turned their life upside down like Charles Morgan, of course. A lot of people helped in the ways that they could, attending protests, establishing local organizations [ https://substack.com/redirect/d7e56791-85f4-401d-93fa-52f1e34d44df?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] to address inequality, or just helping their neighbors however they could. An even larger group of people — including many of the people I’ve asked — didn’t really do anything; they were too busy going to college or raising a family or getting a job. They weren’t “into politics.” And there are probably a lot of people who are ashamed to admit that they were on the wrong side of history, that they supported the forces of “law and order” against the “troublemakers.”
Someday, I suspect that your children or grandchildren will ask you what it was like to live during the siege of Minneapolis or whatever else might descend on us before all this is over. What will you be able to say that you did?

2 Good 2 Be 4 Gotten, Early Modern-Style

If you’re of a certain vintage, you probably have an old high school yearbook somewhere in the basement, and that yearbook is full of signatures.

I remember yearbook day clearly. It came toward the end of the school year. We gathered in an un-air-conditioned gym, where the heat was rising along with our anticipation for summer. Teachers handed out the heavy hardcover books, and, once they were in our hands, we did one of two things: flip through the glossy pages to look for our own faces, hoping we wouldn’t find anything too mortifying, or seek our friends out for a signature.

At the school where I now teach, students no longer sign each other’s yearbooks. They don’t hand out signed wallet-sized pictures of themselves on picture day, either. Why do any of this, when you’ve got Instagram?

So, kids, if you’ve never signed a yearbook, here’s how it went down. The ritual of asking for a signature was a little like asking somebody to dance (another extinct practice; there are no slow songs at school dances anymore). Especially if the potential signer was someone you weren’t tight with, you’d approach them with a slight, unfounded fear of rejection: Will you sign my yearbook? When asked to sign someone else’s yearbook, you felt a bit of stress — you had to write something cool and fun that made it clear you saw your relationship with someone the same way they did.

When historians someday study old yearbooks, they’ll find that the signature pages held all kinds of coded information. The sheer number of inscriptions in your yearbook served as a proxy of your general level of popularity. Individual signatures held clues to the nature of your relationship with various classmates. The guy you sat behind in math and spoke to four times might write something generic like “Have a good summer!” People would exaggerate the importance and intensity of shared memories (“English class was CRAZY!”). Lots of offers would be made to keep in touch and get together over the summer; many people making them would have no real intention of doing so. Closer friends might go on for paragraphs, dropping inside jokes or gushing about all you’d been through together.

And, of course, someone would be sure to inscribe your book with the coolest math equation ever: 2 good + 2 be = 4 gotten!

Yearbooks are an old idea. The first printed, bound yearbook seems to have been produced for Yale’s class of 1806. Photographs didn’t exist yet, so the printers used silhouettes of the students instead. By the late 1800s, many schools gave their students yearbooks — some fancy ones had photographs of everyone in them, while others would simply be lists of names or scrapbooks in which students would insert their own drawings and memorabilia. Over time, the yearbook became a slickly produced artifact, with pictures of every team and club, abundant candid photos, grids of student ID pictures, and pages at the back for signatures.

But the Yale class of 1806 wasn’t the first to collect signatures in a book. People have long wanted a keepsake of their memorable experiences and time with friends. Before the yearbook, people used something called an album amicorum — literally, an album of friends — to remember the places and people that were meaningful to them. This week, we’re going to look at one of the most interesting examples of this genre.

Michael van Meer was a German army officer who lived in the Hague and Hamburg at various points in his life. He died in 1653. His father was from Antwerp, and he had a brother named John.

That’s most of what we know about this guy! Though he seems to have lived a relatively interesting life, it was interesting in the way lots of lives are interesting. Most of the Michael van Meers in world history have disappeared over the centuries, fading from memory as the items and documents that identified them are destroyed or repurposed.

Van Meer would have suffered this fate, too, if not for his album amicorum. The only other historical record of his existence is a notation of his death in the Hamburg archives. But, luckily for us and his legacy, his remarkable album survived.

Historian June Schlueter tells us that albums like Van Meer’s were usually small, pre-printed books. People sometimes took a published book and added empty pages; others bought purpose-made albums with little printed pictures and borders to make the signature pages more attractive. People would often adorn their albums with little inscriptions in Latin, making reference to common interests. Some of the inscriptions were quite elaborate and intended to impress all who read the book. It was an honor to be asked to sign a prominent person’s book, as Nicolaus Vilvordiensis wrote to Abraham Ortellius:

This little [Album], which contains the names of your friends, shows how much you favour the arts and those who cultivate them. Would that by these verses I might earn imperishable glory and your friendship; there is no one in your Album who has a greater regard for you than I, though I am unworthy of being in the company of such learned men, whom all posterity will praise. But though I am unworthy of you, still my verses will I hope evince my grateful disposition towards you who are so kind and free from pride. With these verses, which will be to you a pledge of my affection, I pray you to count me among your friends.

It’s certainly a step up from “Math class was WILD! Let’s hang out this summer!”

Michael van Meer’s book is a cut above the rest. He seems to have had a flair for the original, illustrating his book with original watercolors (it’s unclear if he painted the pictures or hired somebody else to do it for him). He saw the album as an expression of his identity — it served as a travelogue, documenting his impressive travels, a who’s who of prominent people he came across, and an expression of his personality and interests.

It seems that van Meer started his album in anticipation of a trip to London in 1614. Though he added to the book over the years, most of the pages come from his visit to England. They make up a charming and informative tour of the city.

All of these pictures are nifty, but, as any yearbook will tell us, the most important thing is the friends he made along the way. I find the signature pages of van Meer’s book even more affecting than the images, as each of them captures a relationship that meant something to the people involved that has been lost to history. I wonder how often, in his later years, Michael van Meer flipped back through his album, thinking about the time he met the king or the afternoon he witnessed people riding around on a weird windsurfing contraption. I wonder if he hummed that song to himself as he remembered enjoying it with his friend.

It’s the nature of documents like this that we only get little hints about the events they reference. We can’t really know what Michael van Meer’s life was like, but his album can at least remind us that the sands of history have swallowed up billions of fascinating lives, full of meaningful moments and beautiful friendships.

Luckily, we have a few items like van Meer’s little book of memories and signatures. They remind us of all the moments in the past that were 2 good 2 be 4 gotten.

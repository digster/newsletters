The Bid/Ask Spread for Top Talent


A fun but necessary practical joke the white collar career path plays on people is that there's a set of technical skills that you hone early in your career, which inform the rate at which you advance in your job, until suddenly, with very little notice, they aren't the main things the job requires, and the focus moves to something with a more interpersonal flavor. There's a correlation between being good at designing distributed systems and being good at implementing a design that's been handed to you, and a lawyer who knows contracts in and out has a distinct edge when pitching legal services to a new client. This doesn't happen everywhere, and there are non-manager tracks that still have a lot of responsibility, but even those involve a fair amount of self-managing; it's hard to manage when your direct report, you, has all the same biases and shortcuts and knows exactly which excuses work.


There's some logic to this: the most profitable partner for a given firm might be someone who's 85th-percentile good at what the firm does and 99th-percentile good at strategy, schmoozing, taking risks, not losing one's mind when some of those risks inevitably turn out badly, etc.


One thing I've heard from people in both finance and tech is that at some of the best-performing organizations, there are a lot of people who, while objectively good, aren't adding a lot of value to the firm. They aren't subtracting, but the standard is high—the high cost of both errors of omission and employee time means that there's a premium on moving fast, and teams tend to operate at the pace of their slowest member.[1] [https://www.thediff.co/r/c2410a38?m=f90780fc-498d-4ffe-a546-8a3e61574e99] Meanwhile, these same organizations have some superstar employees who reliably create a huge amount of value, but, outside of sales roles or risk-taking positions with a P&L cut, they aren't compensated quite in line with their performance.


But put all of these clues together and a story emerges: if they really do have a high bar for mere mediocrity, but they also have the operating leverage to make a successful employee have a bigger impact on the world (and a bigger impact on their own net worth) than anywhere else, then their optimal strategy is to be a bit bloated specifically so they have a farm system for identifying those stars.


This shows up in multi-manager hedge funds, which are a unique case because the career's endpoints all involve being directly compensated for generating profits. Early career hiring tends to focus a lot on skills—can you build a model? Can you do it really fast? If your model produces nonsense values, are you going to pointlessly argue about it for an hour or just find and fix the case where you moved a decimal? (And are you going to have an annoying-to-anyone-watching-you habit of injecting all sorts of little audits and checks?[2] [https://www.thediff.co/r/21f6f981?m=f90780fc-498d-4ffe-a546-8a3e61574e99]) On the quantitative side, the story is also similar: they're generally testing some combination of how quickly someone can produce an answer, and making sure they have good statistical fundamentals, but generally not testing the ability to spot exploitable anomalies or, perhaps more importantly, to have a nose for strategies that are worse than they look.[3] [https://www.thediff.co/r/8488aa59?m=f90780fc-498d-4ffe-a546-8a3e61574e99]


For funds, if the excess returns come from a small percentage of the managers, and it's hard to predict which managers and which strategies will work, a strategy presents itself:


 * Hire a large number of managers, each of whom has some analysts.
 * Carefully track where the best ideas come from.
 * Design a compensation scheme that's slightly redistributive in order to keep everyone around, and that gets away with this by deferring a growing share of pay for whoever's making the most money.
 * Fire people when they underperform, but don't just fire for adequate performance; that's demoralizing.


The goal is to have a large cohort of people who 1) have cleared a very high skill hurdle just to get there, 2) can be more easily and accurately measured by their current employer than by someone else, and who 3) face economic friction in the form of deferred compensation and noncompetes in the event that they want to leave. In this model, the entire end goal is to widen the bid/ask spread for the best talent, in order to retain it.


It's worth noting that this model predicts the same behavior as another one: that different strategies go through unpredictable seasons where there are not just more opportunities for alpha generation but also opportunities to get a lot more exposure. SPAC arbitrage, for example, was a very different business in 2018 ($11bn in SPAC IPOs) compared to 2021 ($166bn, and a lot more volatility both pre-deal and after deals were completed). The optimal amount of capital to allocate to merger arbitrage went down in recent years, not just because fewer deals were completed, but because discretionary antitrust intervention creates a correlated risk that makes the whole portfolio a bet on the FTC and DOJ instead of a diversified set of trades that moved on separate catalysts. (This kind of mix-of-skills hedging also applies in tech, where it’s often unclear exactly what the technical limiting factor of the next big thing will be. In that case, it’s a good idea to be slightly overstaffed for any skill that might someday be a bottleneck, and to always bring in young people who can quickly acquire new skills to contribute to the task.)


It's likely that the second mandate for diversification was chronologically first, and there are stories of some of the big pod shops timing the market in particular kinds of talent, by interviewing everyone who worked on a particular recently-blown-up strategy to see who just got unlucky and who had a lucky streak that finally mean-reverted.[4] [https://www.thediff.co/r/2b91cdef?m=f90780fc-498d-4ffe-a546-8a3e61574e99] But they've evolved towards the second model: the more skewed the distribution of investing talent, the more it pays to just warehouse a lot of uncertainty and hold on to the upside when it arrives.


This model means that the biggest funds have the same advantage in talent-spotting that they have in portfolio construction: at their scale, they get enough benefits from diversification that they can afford to take risks that smaller competitors can't.


But that's also a market inefficiency: not every great investor ought to be in the business of putting together intra-quarter reads in order to predict earnings, or parsing the nuances of how management rounds various numbers up or down. (As good a business as that is, it's not the only way to skin a cat.)


This market inefficiency begs for a solution, so The Diff is happy to be launching our inaugural stock pitch contest; we're soliciting ideas across a few categories, from obscure microcaps to pair trades in liquid large-cap stocks (like I said—the pod shop model is great if you can run with enough leverage and diversification). We're open to macro, too, since it's one of the purest kinds of alpha—if you're betting on something that's literally never happened before, it's not exactly easy to backtest. We're happy to get submissions from college students who are looking for jobs or internships, but we'd also be excited to talk to people who aren't on the standard track.


And there's an important reason for that last point: institutional investors who wouldn't be caught dead with a factor bias in their portfolios that they didn't have a very good reason for tend to be systematically long plenty of factors in their talent portfolio. There's the Ivy factor, the STEM degree factor, right-internships factor, etc. And, like regular investing factors, these got identified because they're a source of profits! But they're also pretty crowded bets. Any strategy that's sufficiently optimized for every quantifiable metric is taking lots of tradeoffs against the qualitative ones, and the simplest solution to that is to inject a little variance into the process and see what comes next.

De Minimis


Regulatory arbitrage doesn't get enough respect. There are some companies that come into existence entirely to take advantage of poorly-drafted rules, but those companies are a symptom: if the regulations are good, there isn't an arbitrage. Costco is the most impressive example: what started as a workaround for rules allowing manufacturers to fix the prices at which retailers sold their products turned into a company that produces plenty of upside for customers and employees and has not exactly left shareholders out. So it's not strictly wrong that some companies take advantage of rules exempting shipments worth less than $800 from tariffs; the law told them which activities are tax-advantaged, and they listened. But, as Costco illustrates, a business that starts out exploiting legal quirks needs more substance than that; the "de minimis" exemption is going away, so some cheap goods from China will be a bit less cheap in the near future [https://www.thediff.co/r/4f22a391?m=f90780fc-498d-4ffe-a546-8a3e61574e99]. This offers a relative advantage to companies that pitch something other than price, which in the case of e-commerce means some combination of discovery, selection and speed. There will still be cheap substitutes, but they'll have to rethink what their niche really is.



ARR


OpenAI's ChatGPT subscriber count rose to 15.5m at the end of the year, up from 5.8m a year before [https://www.thediff.co/r/b069e4a9?m=f90780fc-498d-4ffe-a546-8a3e61574e99] ($, The Information). This actually represents a drop in their conversion rate, but that's exactly what happens in companies with rapid unit cost deflation: as costs decline, OpenAI can make older generations of their product free, to broaden the top of the funnel. Deflation means that there's always room for new products, like their recently-released o3-mini reasoning model [https://www.thediff.co/r/10900e46?m=f90780fc-498d-4ffe-a546-8a3e61574e99] and Deep Research [https://www.thediff.co/r/952a1eeb?m=f90780fc-498d-4ffe-a546-8a3e61574e99], which allows these new reasoning models to access the internet and conduct multi-step research projects autonomously. And in this case, the huge volume of text that's already GPT-generated means that free users who are serious have an ongoing incentive to upgrade, while OpenAI has continuous chances to upsell them.



Jurisdictions


Meta is considering relocating from Delaware to another state, possibly Texas [https://www.thediff.co/r/6892181f?m=f90780fc-498d-4ffe-a546-8a3e61574e99] ($, WSJ). Delaware has spent a long time trying to outline the exact responsibilities of managers and investors, and has gotten pretty experienced at it. But there's always room for jurisdictional competition, and Texas is pitching a more streamlined process that tends to take the side of controlling shareholders when they negotiate with the rest of the shareholder base. This kind of thing can obviously be abused, but there are definitely cases where dual-class stock prevents shareholders from shooting themselves in the foot. Meanwhile, Dropbox says it wants to leave Delaware for Nevada [https://www.thediff.co/r/888def66?m=f90780fc-498d-4ffe-a546-8a3e61574e99]. If nothing else, this will change the signaling value of a Nevada incorporation (for whatever reason, a large number of scam mining stocks are headquartered in Nevada, and this extends to other fringe companies).

Forward-Deployed Engineers


The Diff recently wrote about forward-deployed engineers [https://www.thediff.co/r/2cf673c0?m=f90780fc-498d-4ffe-a546-8a3e61574e99] ($), a job category that makes the most sense when a company is trying to figure out the exact applications for whatever it's built. Meta is using a similar model right now, embedding some of its engineers with ad agencies in order to start applying generative AI [https://www.thediff.co/r/f1f6f17c?m=f90780fc-498d-4ffe-a546-8a3e61574e99] ($, The Information). The primary motivation is probably exactly what it sounds like: they have a new offering that it will take their clients a long time to understand, and working through agencies means outsourcing some of the least scalable parts of that while identifying and building out the more scalable ones. But one of its side effects is that it makes Meta even more of an advertising black box: if they automate everything scalable, and outsource everything that isn't, they're both improving the economics of the platform and commoditizing the business of directing dollars to that platform.



Software and Hardware


One of the classic arguments for software businesses is that the cost of mistakes is low: you can easily test changes, and the only thing you need to ship for most product recalls is a stream of bits. Hardware is harder. But Sonos' recent problems, including an app update that was so poorly received it led to the CEO stepping down, illustrates that software isn't easy [https://www.thediff.co/r/4cb9b2c8?m=f90780fc-498d-4ffe-a546-8a3e61574e99] ($, FT). When a company does a major update, in a literal sense it's just selling a new stream of bits, but in a practical sense what it's shipping is a new set of customer behaviors. Those aren't cheap to test—unless the previous product was notably bad, any major overhaul is going to briefly inconvenience users while they adapt to the new setup, and lead to a temporary surge of customer support queries. If the update works, those are just an investment that eventually pays off in less churn and more pricing power. But it's an investment that's still putting brand equity at risk, so even if there's a high-margin part of it, this kind of product change ends up with a risk profile that's a lot closer to classic hardware product risk.

Drone Delivery: Accelerating to Inevitable
One of the great illustrations of the sunk cost fallacy is the extreme frequent occurrence in which a one-pound burrito is delivered point-to-point by means of:
A 2,800-lb car, driven by
A person devoting 100% of their attention to delivering said burrito, while
Following a prescribed route consisting of a series of turns on a mostly-2D grid, while
Moving at a variable speed because of stop signs, stoplights, and other obstacles.
Of the ~2lbs of CO2 emitted in a three mile version of this little transaction, 99.6% are spent on moving a person and a vehicle, while the other 0.4% are devoted to moving the food. The cost breakdown is harder to estimate, but a big chunk of it is paying a person to maneuver a fairly large vehicle around other rather large vehicles, all to deliver something comparatively tiny.
There is, in other words, an inflated cost involved in using cars to deliver small packages. While that's most salient for food delivery, small errands are a plausibly larger category. It's hard to ballpark how big they are, because many errands are part of a trip—stopping by a drive-through on the way home from work adds a few hundred feet to a trip that was already going to happen. But plenty of small convenience purchases do involve a quick road trip, with its attendant costs.
This situation is temporary.
Soon enough, autonomous drones will be the dominant way to deliver small payloads—lunch, a snack, coffee, medication, household products (yes, your emergency toilet paper roll will be flying directly to your door), batteries, band-aids, that one dowel for your new Ikea dresser that you could swear you saw just a minute ago, etc. Basically any small payload that needs timely delivery can be sent more efficiently by a drone than by a car, and now that companies doing delivery have material market share in the overall errand business, and are sensitive to their unit economics, adoption can happen fast.
Alphabet's Wing is a fascinating case study in how autonomous delivery drones can work as a product: Wing had done 100,000 deliveries since launch as of late August [https://email.mg2.substack.com/c/eJwlkMluxCAQRL9muNlijeHAIZf8hsXSY6Ng7LDMyH8fPJZQl1CputTPmQrLnk997KWia8z1PEAneJcItUJGrUCeg9eEiy_GiEJec0-kkCiU-ZkBNhOirrkBOpqNwZka9vRJKM4wWjXFlinmHRfE8wkkU4Jj6qTwcpq4cnevaT5AcqDhBfncE6Co11qP8mDfD_rTn437Mr5DWka3b_1PMSVdsOyjWyYNppWaTQxm8LlvGDzEcG0bnDlCNXFc6xZR0FeSUIIJw4LLkY6TspOhDjsnPbXOjhxn3F7734PjbaFjabZU436vZpR1KKtJ43V495dLP0a_f-66tRTqOUMyNoK_0dSb8AfWvECC3Mn72VRNvpginAjB1cRvFJ0dF1QSRSTqzX7vqaR9eD7_AWMzkFc], and the latest number from Wing is over 130,000 deliveries, in their current markets of Australia, the US, and Finland.
There are several categories that work especially well for drones:
Anything that's needed infrequently and on short notice. Consider everything that's in your first-aid kit, or anything you suddenly realize you need just before thinking "I really ought to have a first-aid kit."
Anything that's often needed by people who are less mobile: this can include frequent purchases by elderly or disabled people, who may have more difficulty than average going to the grocery store or drugstore. It can also mean anything you'd consume while you're sick. For someone who is in desperate need of Pepto-Bismol, a trip to the store can be a high-risk decision. A related use case here is for parents; if one parent is watching several kids, a trip to the store can mean strapping three people into carseats in order to make one quick trip. Like some short-haul flights in the US, the process of boarding takes longer and is more stressful than the travel itself.
Anything that you would be willing to visit a store, but can't, because of Covid restrictions. (This one is—hopefully!—temporary. But it can establish other consumption patterns, as we've seen with Zoom and buy-online-pickup-in-store. And Covid has probably shifted the social norm around going to work or a store during the flu season if you have sniffles.)
Any food product that doesn't travel well. This is an important one: one of the constraints on food delivery is that burgers get soggy, fried chicken gets less crispy, perfect sushi becomes imperfect, ice cream turns into a disappointing milkshake, and hot coffee risks either getting consumed lukewarm or dangerously hot [https://email.mg2.substack.com/c/eJwlkE1uxCAMhU8zLBG_CVmwqFR11W56AUTAyaBJIAUyo9y-ZCJZfrKtpyd_zlaYUz70lkpFZzP12EBHeJUFaoWM9gLZBK-pkB3ndEBeC0-VVCgUM2WA1YZF17wD2vZxCc7WkOLbMQhO0F3LbuC2G0FNjngFnHeqm8AzCWICPsGVa3cfIDrQ8IR8pAho0fdat3LjHzf21QoifoVH2MAHi1Oe2-qcm3wHGME9zBObH_eZol38jUnWF_MLpdo921gLCpoRRimjhHIihcIM98PYW-aIc8qz0Y1YkEz2Z_q7CbLODJd9bH73wC6tKOtQ7jbi8-F2n099H9rfpum6x1APA9GOC_gLSb3IviGZGSLkRtwbWzXt-EAFlVIMvbgQNGZCMkUHqlBL9qm5ovZhmv4BEiOPgQ].
Autonomous delivery drones end up unbundling food and ambiance, which can have some interesting effects on dining. It's helpful for the ghost kitchen business, of course, especially since drones require a smaller physical footprint for pickup than humans driving cars. But it also means that it's easier to order dinner from one place, dessert from another, and drinks from a third restaurant. Helsinki has picnic spots [https://email.mg2.substack.com/c/eJwlkMuOhCAQRb-m2UkAwceCxWzmNwyPUkkjOIBt_PvBNqnUDdTjpo5RBZaYLrnHXNCdpnLtIAOc2UMpkNCRIU3OSspF17Z0RFZySwcxIJenOQFsynlZ0gFoP7R3RhUXw3di5C1Bq5zpLFhnCBOD6jvKDNPCWujAaADWk8dXHdZBMCDhA-mKAZCXayl7frU_L_ZbQ_u44NOFBZu41TcjjFYhoqYTEjRamXfjQrOCzy68XTPH1NxdjQq2CfHEa9k8cvL-o4wS2hLBB8xwP-peMUOMGSzTRmNOEjk-8e_FybYwnA-dS91-O6MkXV5VwPfhtb7c-i3U-6eq2xFcuSYISnuwD5ryEP7CmhYIkCp5O6kiadeOlFMh-NjzB0VlxwUb6EgHVJ1trFNBWjfP_0trj7c] in parks where people can get food delivered.
This ends up having further effects on cities and real estate. To the extent that Starbucks is not a bank [https://email.mg2.substack.com/c/eJwlkMuOwyAMRb-m7CbCBFpYsJjN_EbEw0mZJpDh0Sp_P6SVLF_Zln3l40zFJeVD76lUcqapHjvqiK-yYq2YSSuYp-A1cHEdR1DEa-5BCklCmeaMuJmw6pobkr3ZNThTQ4rvDcVHSu5azNx5I_2NA_LZzgpASGnAzVz4maqPr2k-YHSo8Yn5SBHJqu-17pfx-8J-evzujxRDXAa7pqXsqQ4ubb3PKKguVPZUqsm2uUf52vqFXhxfpe2Y9_TCPNzrtpKgGWUADCiMVHA5sOGm7M0wR52TnllnB04zbc_0d-F0W9hQmu2H3eM0JFmHcjdxON_u8-XU96B_P3XdWgz1mDAau6L_gKkfvm9U04IRc-fuJ1M1XEcFHITg6sY_IDo5LpgEBZJ0Z5_6VtQ-zPM_W_aRig], it's a real estate company that specializes in buying access to frequent foot traffic from coffee consumers. If drone delivery gets as fast as going a block or two out of the way, or spending a few minutes in line, it radically reduces the return from better real estate. (Starbucks will still do fine here: part of the magic of their app is that they replace a series of $2-5 credit card transactions with $25 card top-up transactions; adding a few points to the margin on a series of small purchases adds up, and a company with a loyalty app can also use push notifications to guide customers towards making orders at favorable times and prices.)
Drones affect another kind of space, too. I've written before [https://email.mg2.substack.com/c/eJwlUUmOxCAMfE24JWLLduAw0qi_EYFxOqgTyLDMKL8f0i1ZNrhklV0FOuMzxEudIWVypyVfJyqPf2nHnDGSkjAuziom-0EINhOrpGVTPxGXljUiHtrtKseC5Cxmd6CzC_49MUtByaYE0GGkCHwAbrQxHCinA5eMjRJgXj-8uliHHlDhL8YreCS72nI-UyO-Gv6oYa7ocQtGx9wdaF05OghHBSDohG3esDUhWoytwfyH6O9WPa7V3rZn1JAdYMusocAkB8uwEY8USoT6-F5jpbdp2Z1_NXxIr9oT48h7GOa1LmqmQffMTqMQFGfNBDVAnOKUM8YZrf9eTh3vxtmMup4IMFluwHSSRlp-w08j6fHkXSomZQ2ve3cSlUub9t2tYcWfd30DVcql1qN4l68FvTY72o_K-WPWW_fliR5jNdEuOis2iJlJ1vdyHuVH1WqD7PnEZjaRymxDnfLKunX9B9LtqTk] about how drone delivery creates new property rights, and it's becoming an actual problem. One drone delivery company can manage its routes to avoid collisions, but if there are dozens of companies it becomes a problem. Enter the InterUSS Platform [https://email.mg2.substack.com/c/eJwlkE1uxCAMhU8z7BoBgSQsWHTTa0RgnAxtAik_U-X2JRPJsi1b1vP7wBRcYzr1EXMhV5rLeaAO-Jc3LAUTqRnT7J1mQg59zxRxWjg2yYn4PC8JcTd-0yVVJEe1mwdTfAzvCyV6Sp56MnxBI4xU0C_OUjbacTAAkoEybmC3rqnOYwDU-MJ0xoBk089SjvzoPx_8q0U8MORYE2C3xrhu2GZHit8IJbfWh_Zuzfnj2ExZYtqJ15xyxjijrKdSTB3vRmVHw4ECTI5bsJ2gidZX_H0Iuq-8y9XmYuCng7iTpH1-mtBdDtt-vep70YzOre41-HLOGIzd0N0Myo3yTWVeMWBqiN1simZDr5hgUgo1ittzgyQkn5hiE2nKLraroJ1fln_JmIuf], a protocol for communication between different networks of autonomous drones. Since there's a lot more three-dimensional space than two-dimensional space, and since drones take up less space than cars, it will be a long time before there's anything resembling a drone traffic jam, but it won't be long before drones from one company need to ensure that they're not colliding with drones from another. As part of building a protocol for drone delivery, Wing offers OpenSky [https://email.mg2.substack.com/c/eJwlkEuOxCAMRE_TLCO-aViwmM1cA_Fx0qgTyADpVm4_JJEs25JVLr3ytsGcy6G3XBs6m2nHBjrBty7QGhS0VygmBk24GBkjCgXNA5FColjNVABWGxfdyg5o290SvW0xp0uhOMPopcM4jlZOgCcLk3KOKDJKLIRTbGLO-tvX7iFC8qDhA-XICdCiX61t9cF-HvS31zemefB57WveINX30TcUNcWUEEowYVhwOdDhqdzTUo-9l4E67waOC94_-e_B8TrToe6uNuvf5zNUdKwvm4YTo9_nc16HTmP6XPcU22EgWbdAuEHbndeFbmZIUHqOwdimycgU4UQIrp78ButJcEFlp5aoO4fcVUmHOE3_tdmARA], which lets drone owners request access to airspace without manually filling out paper forms.
And collisions bring up the topic of safety: do we need more objects falling out of the sky [https://email.mg2.substack.com/c/eJwlUUuO7CAMPE2zIwICnbBgMZt3jYiPk6AhkAdkRn37cbol5LIo7MJlbztspb7MWVond1j66wST4bcl6B0quRrUJQbDpXqOI9ckGBn4rGYS27JWgMPGZHq9gJyXS9HbHkt-V2g5MrIbqUBZPapJ66Dck69cOD_NXGGfMML80bVXiJA9GPiB-ioZSDJ772d7jF8P8Q9PgIzU4F1Lxds0-HLgrWCCIzBx5_z9zNXY6JlsBgp5iwihlrPRvtdybTutpazUYTzWCCnQveCQ9IxQPTT6HbvfIVMPMcW8YUcSza3CBWd8ZErOgxgm7SYrPPN-DjiOGySr7Pop_x-SHZsY2uVat_77_iWpJrbd5uG2CvntxjeBji2Ix5Vjfy2QrUsQPmb2z07e9i4bZKi4q7DYbvhz1FxypaSe5Mc8dFsqMXPNZ4LKoWBVNiGu6x_JwqKG]? There are two components to this:
Minimizing the odds that a drone will make an uncontrolled descent, and
Minimizing the harm if it happens.
Aviation has a long tradition of prizing redundant systems (and people who violate that norm learn why the hard way [https://email.mg2.substack.com/c/eJwtkcGupCAQRb-m2WEAscUFi7eZ3zAllC3zFBwKn-PfD3ZPQihyK0XdnOug4Cvly-6JCruvsVw72ognrVgKZnYQ5jF4K3X3bFs5MG-1l6YzLNA4Z8QNwmpLPpDtx7QGByWk-J4YdCvYYs0wgBdKOKWfUoFx3ezd3BvTQw8d6M9eOHzA6NDiD-YrRWSrXUrZ6dF-PdSves7zbAihlOosbEiNS1uVp4NCRKL7mTDEFwfMiXZwWCXgK7hvnmae0R_RQ3QBiafI-7bnG_zldFHBjS9AfIJ5XtHzaiHysiRCfi6Jnyl_VzXdGvLfWOq_LFgllJRKCtmKTptGNf0w9aCccM54Nbmp0SKL4yf9eWixvVRDx0SlurmNs2wDLRCbG1_tv-76blSKY63bEUO5RowwVUsfwOWT0xv5-MKIuebnRyhWPttBatl1euj_A60J6E4ZOUjD6maf6lS0PszzP-iBq6E]). But that's a probabilistic approach, and any time odds that round down to zero collide with a rapid increase in sample size, there are unpleasant surprises. But the other safety dial to turn is to reduce the weight of drones so that even an uncontrolled descent is not especially high-risk. The Wing drones are 11.4 lbs, or 14 lbs with packages [https://email.mg2.substack.com/c/eJwlkMmOhCAQhp9Gbm3YXDhwmMu8BmEplbSCA9jGtx9sk0otqVT9-X6rC8wxXXKPuaA7qXLtIAOceYVSIKEjQ1LeScK7njEikJPckbEbkc9qSgCb9qss6QC0H2b1Vhcfw_dCcIbRIrEdqBEjpgyGTvSG98JYN00cW2YciEdXH85DsCDhA-mKAdAql1L23LCfhv7WOH2YWxu32i7xfPnyOmN65zoiLymmhFCCCcMdH1vaDsIMmlps7eiosablOOHjE_8ajreZtvkwuWj7vj-iJH1edGhvlrqf7_pdVCRV63YEXy4FQZsV3ENbHtO-_GqGAKma6ZQukvRMEE66jouBP3TVDt7RkQgyoqrsYr0K0vlp-gfZkYGZ]; this doesn't eliminate risk, but significantly mitigates it.
Delivery drones share a go-to-market strategy with voice search, the Apple Watch, the Roomba, and Tesla: the product is cool as a gimmick but turns out to be useful, too. There's just something intrinsically appealing about dealing with a mild inconvenience by summoning a flying robot to fix it for you. But once that's happened once or twice, it becomes weird to consider summoning a human being, at a higher cost in terms of money and environmental impact, to do basically the same thing. So if you think getting a drone delivery is something you’d brag about, best to do it early during the brief period where drone delivery is available but not a proasic modern convenience.
There are a few possibilities for long-term drone economics. The most obvious right now is that drone-specific companies will build fleets and work with multiple counterparties. Retailers could do it themselves—Amazon started the hype early by talking about a delivery drone in 2013 [https://email.mg2.substack.com/c/eJwlkMmOhSAQRb_msdMwKbpg0Zv-DcNQ-kgr2Awa--sbnwnhJlXUvdQxKsMS4iX3kDK6rylfO0gPZ1ohZ4ioJIiTs5LwrmeMjMhKbsnQDcilaY4Am3KrzLEA2otenVHZBf-ZGDnD6C0FtULMvWKgjDCsI0bRue-tUSMdB6GfXFWsA29AwgHxCh7QKt857-nFvl70u57zPFuj0_231oStVg5nIVRVm_oLvjEQmuIPcGtqbKwWjYXV3XaNCdV6z_UtcpJiSgglmDDc8aGlrRi1UNRgYwZLtdEtxxGXI_y-ON4W2qaiU1bm545FUbr0Vr6996795dZPo64_Vd2Kd_mawCu9gn3I5Afwh9W0gIdYwdtJZUl6NhJOuo6Pgj8kKjre0YGMZEA12YY65aV18_wPs-WSoQ]. At the time that Amazon announced it, their proposed five-pound payload covered 86% of the items the company delivered, and they planned half-hour delivery times. Eliminating substantial delivery costs is appealing to any retailer. On the other hand, the fixed cost and operational uncertainty makes this a tricky bet: if the cost of drone delivery starts high and falls rapidly with scale, it's an open question whether retailers will capture the scale or whether it will be pure-play delivery networks. Walmart has been investing in drone delivery companies [https://email.mg2.substack.com/c/eJwtUcuOhCAQ_JrhpuHl68BhL_sbBqF1yCq4gE78-210E9LV0K_qwugMS4iX2kPKpJgxXzsoD5-0Qs4QyZEgjs4qJptWCDYQq6RlfdMTl8Y5AmzarSrHA8h-TKszOrvg74pBCkreituhnzXImdq2kQjdLCbDoJknLqwZnrn6sA68AQUnxCt4IKt657ynl_h68W88JsQ9RGRcf_S66ZhrEzZ8L2RjuF1OOUOgLRrWofnPrJw_IeWEWNmIzY-9ym-o_E324yxUASO4i7dPAl5WV5hUewwnJkTiVGnPOKNM0Eb2Na-7Yeo0N9SY3vLJTLWkkR5n-H1Jui28TseUsjY_hSmJyqW39nURDONLwTuAuo2I2-FdvkbwelrBPpLm52dukccFPJT97aizYq0YmGRNI4dOPhKi5rLhPRtYT3CyDVjllXXz_AcaWKaX], with deliveries to some customers in Arkansas [https://email.mg2.substack.com/c/eJwlkcuOhSAMhp_msNNw88KCxWzmNQxC9ZBBcACPcZ5-QBPyN2lpf_iqVYY1xEvuIWVUZcrXDtLDmRzkDBEdCeJkjSS86xkjAhnJDRm7Edk0LRFgU9bJHA9A-zE7q1W2wd8dgjOM3pIsdGFcAIVFzGAG4Fxg0rOOwUBwLx5fdRgLXoOED8QreEBOvnPe04t9veh3Oed5tvldyyu0OmwlRTElJZBbxpqgw9gLIup15TYVc_Nnd2c9NCaGquBsNWjKgA2itso15Ycfq6FR8Uf5pBKysg4mlGDCcMfHlraDmAdFNdZ6NHTWc8txxMcn_L443lbapmNOWemf-jAUpU1v5duKptTXGu9CITRV48PbfE3g1ezAPPDys4Mb57SCh1h2YyaVZUElCCddx8XAH1iFLu_oSAQZUXE2oXR5aeyy_AMXwpt7].
This is another instance of brick-and-mortar retailers being indifferent to whether their real estate consists of retail stores, showrooms, or fulfillment centers; whatever the most cost-effective way is to generate demand and then fulfill it will determine how the location gets used.
What drone delivery ultimately does is weaken the car bundle. Cars are useful for commuting, errands, and long-distance travel, and for some set of consumers, errands are the last marginal reason to choose owning a car over not. Interestingly, this is a case where an external change in the logistics business can actually benefit some of the companies that don't participate in it; fewer car owners can mean more demand for Uber and Lyft. (If you would spend $5k/year on owning a car, and you replace that with $4k/year in Uber and Lyft spending, you come out ahead—but so do the ride-sharing companies.)
A lot of this is dependent on the unit economics of the drone delivery business, which remain somewhat opaque. Manna, which uses human-operated drones, says its delivery costs are 90% lower than drivers [https://email.mg2.substack.com/c/eJwlUUuO6yAQPI3ZYfGNYcFipKe5hsWnHTPjgB_gRL794ERCVdCt7hJV3ja453KaPdeGLpjbuYNJ8KobtAYFHRXKHIOhQt44pxoFIwJVUqFY56UAPGzcTCsHoP1wW_S2xZzeE1pwglbDlZ0mEoSlkoZelFoDWyavb5Jw7-Cja48QIXkw8IRy5gRoM2trex3418C--3m9XuOSi4M6-vzohRob1M4_eU2_GVqNUPqTEUY7EdWBXhBKX4cDbPFajWPF1w23HOyJbQo4too1wX4Fu0PBbbUJe1uwsxUC7gY8o38rDfy7rgP_J7kKdnFUBMJQNJciZZRQTqRQIxsn7SbLPPFeBea8GwUp5Hjm_4Mgjzsb6-Fqs_73-ggqJtauOF5G9v794nej-zl3fhwptnOGZN0G4WN1-yT2Nn--Q4LSkwyzbYbeuKaCSin0JD7W9iyEZIpqqlBXDrlPJRPisvwBt26qSg]. Even if that's not a fully-loaded cost, it does imply that drone delivery can achieve lower marginal costs than the traditional kind. And while the drone companies have to build fleets, a disadvantage relative to gig economy businesses that can rely on the existing stock of cars, but many of the components of drones have favorable experience curves. Once drones have reasonable incremental margins, a decent safety record, and consumer adoption, the main problem to solve for is capital. And capital for growth businesses has never been more abundant.

The Working Capital Accelerant
Stripe has published some stats around its Stripe Capital service [https://email.mg2.substack.com/c/eJw9kM1uhSAQhZ_msqtxEBQWLLrpaxh-Ri-pguXnNr598Zo0IRwmk-HM-awuuMZ0qiPmQq5rLueBKuBv3rAUTKRmTLN3ChgfhwEkcYo5EFwQn-clIe7ab6qkiuSoZvNWFx_De0KyoSdPBeAQF7cwMY7SAJ8GqcXIhQGzAEN3--rqPAaLCl-YzhiQbOpZypEfw-eDfrWTS_IHdjburbg2TPH_2cTqwxe9faT2Qaj44fdD20K8oj0FoNDD0HMmOtpN0kya2t5a4aixpmN96usr_jxYv6-0y9Xkou33ZUWS8vmpQ3fFbP310nejpZ2b7jX4cs4YtNnQ3SDKzfONZl4xYGqc3ayLgnGQwIBzJid2B2-kGKcCJAjSnF1sU0E5vyx_g6SMSQ]:
In an analysis of Stripe users, those with Stripe Capital financing saw revenue growth 114 percentage points higher than peer companies not on Stripe Capital. The impact of financing was even greater for smaller firms—businesses processing less than $100,000 per year saw revenue growth of 140 percentage points over their peers. These are among the first findings to illustrate a clear causal link between financing and growing payments revenue.
It's easy to get caught up in worrying about how rapidly alternative lenders are growing, but at least in the SMB space there's a lot of ground to make up: the cost and regulatory structure of the US financial system makes it fairly easy for individuals to borrow for consumption, and for large companies to lean on debt in order to return more capital to shareholders. Small businesses have been comparatively under-served, and now that payments offer both a lower-cost way to reach them and data to underwrite the loans, a large gap is being rapidly filled in.

Younger SPAC Targets
Crunchbase crunches the data and finds that among venture-funded companies the median traditional IPO is for an eleven-year-old company, while the median SPAC deal is for a company that's just eight years old [https://email.mg2.substack.com/c/eJwtkM2OrCAQhZ-m2WkAsRsWLGZzX8NAUSoZG7z8OPHtB9tJKpyEk6pT9YEpuMR06j3mQq5nKueOOuBP3rAUTKRmTJN3monxOQxMEaeFY3KUxOdpTohv4zddUkWyV7t5MMXH8OlQYqBk1eNMQfFRWME4PI2UoOaZDlIZMRsr4M411XkMgBoPTGcMSDa9lrLnx_D14P9aXUv1kGqA1ZqMPcT332-TAzpr4Btdl3cDXTFpwZK7I3d-j80nXnPKGeOMsoGOQva8fyn7MhwogHTcgu0FTbQe8f9D0PfC-1xtLm3olUSS9nk1ob_Obf5y6cdoV09N3zX4ck4YjN3Q3UDKzfWDaFowYGq83WSKZs9BMcHGUaiXuAE0YmLkkikmSUt2sXUF7fw8_wKFio5s]. Some of this is because of disclosure differences; SPACs can include forward projections, while regular IPOs generally shy away from this, and the younger a company is the more interesting its future is relative to its past. In one sense, SPAC investors suffer adverse selection, since the companies they're investing in aren't going public the usual way. But in another sense, SPACs compete with late-stage growth rounds as a way to provide capital to companies that a) are at a point where they need outside funding to grow, but b) aren't at the point where current financials speak for themselves. And given how many sophisticated investors have moved into the late stage VC space, it's a category with a vote of confidence.
Consumer-Facing DeFi
Coinbase is offering a DeFi yield product to non-US investors [https://email.mg2.substack.com/c/eJwlkUmOxCAMRU9T7IiYMi1Y9KavETE4KdQE0gwd5fZNKpLFB1sfW89GFdhiuuQRc0H3sZTrABngzB5KgYRqhrQ4K6noB87pjKwUlk79hFxe1gSwK-dlSRXQUbV3RhUXw8cxC07QW3IitJ3EQBSh_cQtmZmxegQFAxH9qJ--qloHwYCEP0hXDIC8fJdy5Bf_erHvFud5dtrHuGtIW2fi3nL3oE1UKs54uK-MMIopw2RuDxNd0CoD9qoG84aMLawOXw68xUeKtpqC15hwiAFXnLGpucQdUkZO3j9RRgnlpBdTx7px1qNihhgzWaaN7gRJpP7F35cg-8a6XHUuyvzcs6EkXX6r0N14Wn279VNolJamew2uXAsEpT3YB2B59vBBumwQILX92EUVSQc-U0H7XsyjeIA1wqJnE53phFpnG5srSOvW9R9cjp5w]. This is not one of the Ponzi-esque DeFi products offering 100%+ annual yields that will blow up at some point; Coinbase's yields were "between 2.83% and 5.39% in October," per Bloomberg. That's a low enough rate that the yields can be subsidized by actual returns from lending to speculators, which is not zero-risk but is at least something that can survive in actuarial terms. Two of the big implicit taxes on the developing world's middle class are 1) inflation, which tends to be an ongoing material cost outside of the developing world, and 2) a banking system that provides poor returns and charges high fees for small savers. Giving people a dollar-denominated savings vehicle with a yield that doesn't round to zero is a significant benefit for small savers in poor countries around the world.

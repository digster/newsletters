ID.me's incentives are not ideal: the worse they are at verifying people's identities, the more they can report that fraud is a frequent occurrence. There's a tricky balance with privatizing functions like identity verification: a private company may have a better shot at delivering a working product than fifty states independently building their own version. But it can also have an economic incentive to build the wrong product.

The piece doesn't come to a definitive answer, but does bracket a few reasonable estimates. Trust is a resource institutions have, which they sometimes need to wager or spend in order to achieve some other goal. But the relevant odds are hard to estimate even after the fact.

Kishi and Japan: The Search for the Sun [https://email.mg2.substack.com/c/eJw9kU1v5CAMhn9NuE3ERxLIgcPuVnvY9tbtGTngZNAmJOKj0fTXl5mRVrJssDG2ntdCxmWPN33sKZO7M_l2oA54phVzxkhKwmi806zrByHYSJzuHFO9Ij6ZOSJu4FedY0FylGn1FrLfw6Nj7AQlV02nfhIDk1ZZnJhUFJwakaMcHMhesedcKM5jsKjxE-NtD0hWfc35SI340fDf1c7zbGGDrz20dt9q4tWnq7_8gQPCJSFEe7281ONriV8bhPrAHdX9pFT-Fb_ePlQj6jfDf1shLAUWbMQLBvPxXlMRZ1OvkMzqTUomr8RrTjmnjHMmKRWy5e1MQc2j7RwIOlrF2mLPWcRjbDq6LbxNZUoZ7L_7miTquiWE9o6p1pd7fBQqLVPjVoLPN4MBphXdE2R-6vFAaxYMGKtOzkDWbOi4klRJOfTjE1wl3cme865KUie7vXYF7fw8fwOrZZ3C]: Nobusuke Kishi is a very odd historical figure. He was a bureaucrat in the Japanese puppet state of Manchukuo, where his rule led to bloodcurdling abuses. He signed the declaration of war against the US, tried to manage the economy during the war, was imprisoned afterwards for war crimes—and then ran for office again after the war and served as Prime Minister. (The book opens with a chapter on Kishi's home life, with a brief cameo in which his grandson Shinzo [https://email.mg2.substack.com/c/eJwlkEmOwyAQRU8TlojJAwsW2eQCfQCEoWyj2GAxdOQ-feNYQvyC0q_Sf9YUWGI61RFzQdely3mACvDJG5QCCdUMSXunqOh6zqlETglHx25EPus5AezGb6qkCuio0-atKT6Gr0MKTtCqJOfWOuMIFa2EDuRgej7TgdiJEcfvvaY6D8GCgl9IZwyANrWWcuQHfz7Yqx0I-OPf_gDnDY5paV_Xu8nP6sNf1M8JkFeMMEYoY20-4QNmeCZmnKUVznAi7UhxtZ-Zp0M-BNkXhnOdcjH2jW3cUVI-rybgK1XrL5d-Gy2cbrrX4MupIZhpA3fnLje-Lwm9QIDUsDptiqK9YONAxmHoO3nnbGDE0DEmGsG22cXmCsr5ef4HI7SFHg] shows up after school to watch cowboy shows on TV.) This book is basically a work of cold war propaganda; it was published in 1960 and somehow omits the brutal policies of the prewar years. Still, it's worth thinking about Kishi's career: it's useful to look at the last common ancestor [https://email.mg2.substack.com/c/eJwlkM2OhSAMhZ_msiT8KbpgMZvZzTMQhOolo2Cgzo1vP6hJ0waanpPzeYew5HKaPVckV7N47mASfOoKiFDIUaHYGAxXXS8lH0kwKvChG0isdi4Am4urwXIA2Y9pjd5hzOm-GJVk5G2043oKQci5U0E2jYFLN0HQsneyg_D4uiNESB4M_EE5cwKymjfiXl_y6yW-W0Gin_gbdwjR0VyW9nW92_i5BAp4SGh93rbm75pUxVxINIIJwbgQXDMmNRV0Zm6YR6-Ck2z0A6eH_8yy7ONLsW0RtB5TRed_adMixcT6doleMdt-uea9aGlvsyNFPC0kN60QHhD48LzR2AUSlMY5WIeG90oMmg1a9934BG-klO6EUA1pcw65XSUT4jz_A7DAjJQ] of wildly different ideologies. Kishi's management of Manchukuo influenced the postwar Japanese economic model, which was deeply integrated with US capitalism—but that ideology itself was influenced by the same kind of thinking that led to Stalin. It's fortunate for the world that modern states inherited a much less brutal model of rapid industrialization.

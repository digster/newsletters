I've been writing my thoughts on life, psychology, and mindset for some time. I've considered transferring some of these writings from Notion into this post. In physics, three laws can explain 99% of observations. However, in life, psychology, and mindset, 99 laws can barely explain 1% of observations. What I've written here stems from my unique life experiences. It's natural to find inconsistencies since our life experiences vary.

Action relieves anxiety

Working on a problem reduces the fear associated with it. When you are afraid of something, face it directly by confronting it. Address it first thing every day. Talk to people and learn how they approached similar problems. As James Clear ( https://jamesclear.com/3-2-1 )  says, 'It is hard to fear a problem when you are making incremental progress — even if the progress is imperfect and slow.'

Dogged incremental and constant progress over a long time

Everyone knows that hard work, healthy eating, and working out regularly yield rich rewards over the long term. However, there's a difference between knowing this and consistently acting on it. As Peter Kaufman says, we are all functional equivalents of Sisyphus, pushing the boulder up the mountain for eternity without ever reaching the top.

https://lh3.googleusercontent.com/RfTg8gIL5SPxxbNFb4viT9fnveqeMjXNCrN9ZrOeZ3C61qRvC8veImTXdQhWaWWO5Dd8GkU1TKyT6zDSBsYtfQqovSuWWyAVNRCscSkbD-76oH_u3KcUer8plzQERkoyz6EZeDDZTNJRrY7gRe-5v7k

The task we want to accomplish is like a boulder. We push it halfway up and feel the pain. A voice in our mind says, "Enough for today; get to it another day." By the time we come back, the boulder has returned to its original place. Our half-hearted attempts result in the boulder remaining in the same spot. Compounding stalls, leading to mediocrity.

As Peter Kaufman states, the key to success in life is to make dogged, incremental, consistent progress over a very long time frame.

Focus on adding value with what I know

Our mind has a tendency to compare ourselves with others. The default nature of our mind is to feel terrible when our skills aren't as good as those of the people around us. This default thought of "I am not good" or "I am a fraud" is known as imposter syndrome.

https://lh5.googleusercontent.com/LzaY0fY7uOWvwO2YdCnaw2Ns7zzg5sKUcznyb9Wl1XTx6cwz8GT3QoclxnLaR5V4gmoVEAqAcSWVqzxXfGbJE0rGjiEgTUFJg5tipGneBfQHI1ZaLqOYj44PYDq5VBAQCfdaIbrFDlLZB_ciRgGq35Q

Why does our mind do that? Perhaps such thoughts gave us survival advantages in the past. Imposter syndrome creates a vicious cycle and causes us to lose self-confidence. Is it even worth it? Why venture into something that has only downsides without any upsides?

There's no need for hero worship. Everyone has their strengths. Play to your strengths. Don't succumb to imposter syndrome by envying others' abilities. It's okay. Focus on becoming a better version of yourself.

The world is ultra-specialized. To deliver anything meaningful, we need a team of specialists. You can't be an expert at everything. Instead of feeling powerless when observing others, our focus should be on adding value to society based on what we know. For things we don't know, we should see them as opportunities to learn. That way, we can become lifelong learners.

Do I deserve it?

Every investor wishes they had invested $100k in Tesla stock in 2015. Every employee wishes to be a CEO. Every family in the Bay Area wishes to own a property in Palo Alto. Dreaming big is okay, but as Charlie Munger wisely puts it, "To get what you want, you have to deserve what you want. The world is not yet a crazy enough place to reward a whole bunch of undeserving people."

Enough

I wanted a debt-free house and a car. This had been a long-standing wish of mine. That wish was fulfilled. Did I stop wishing? No. I aspired to build an equity portfolio of a certain size. In a few years, my equity portfolio surpassed the target I had in mind.

Was I satisfied after that? No. I kept adding more items to my wishlist. It was akin to the game of collecting water bottles. Initially, you collect bottles filled with water to quench your thirst. But over time, the act of collecting bottles becomes more about the game itself, and you lose sight of the original purpose (quenching thirst) and begin to collect even empty bottles. It becomes an endless pursuit.

Here's a poignant story about a king learning the perils of human desires from a beggar.

https://lh6.googleusercontent.com/XprZgzr8af6ZyNlflQY6BpZsDyiwfzxwH9o2uJLBvppCPUDxUI2rJuBA0r0_p0bqcwszi-wZf-Xxenw-1DhP5Kiw8OpL11pD-ttArX58Y2reMbb7vBKU7da9I03aDG-OoZMNAx_Ek07inEKKKEXvT4w

Our biggest challenge is ensuring our life's purpose doesn't devolve into a beggar's bowl, a bottomless pit of desire, always seeking the next best thing that we believe will bring us happiness or that we think we need to be happy.

What's the solution? "Enough." போதும் என்ற மனமே பொன் செய்யும் மருந்து. A contented mind is the  golden medicinal elixir. Easier said than done.

What matters in life changes with age

During a recent chat with a wise 70-year-old, I gained invaluable insights into life.

What we regard as an asset at a young age might become a liability as we age. The wise-and-old person mentioned the challenges of managing real estate properties. Though he owns them, they aren't very useful to him now. I'm sure he spent most of his life passionately accumulating those assets.

When he was young, he didn't have much wealth, but he had the energy and vigor to pursue it. Now, he has wealth, but his body and mind can only handle so much. It reminds me of the phrase, "water, water everywhere, but not a drop to drink." It's like a thirsty person surrounded by an ocean.

https://lh3.googleusercontent.com/ZNEAlYjfBNuqKtYLeqghjjHMxn5iofyo5zwDtHdn_Gj3OR7AzWKRf-s_LmZB5YS5IBI-muhY76o6Gt6-JWF9Dv_fZy6btZMZBVJN4klPzoZctuHLFYAezlw7S2ndc_AKi9l1PGRpAzvlvwYshcrSYk4

What's the point of accumulating assets beyond a certain limit? We are all like trees that will inevitably fall one day.

Impermanence is permanent

Human beings often believe they are the center of the universe, with everything revolving around them. However, the truth is that no one is indispensable. Even if a cataclysm eradicates life on earth, life will emerge in some ocean corner. But we won't be there to see it.

Many believed that Apple would decline after Steve Jobs' death, predicting the end of its innovation. Yet, Apple introduced the Apple Watch and AirPods. Its high margin Services business alone generated $80B in revenue. Apple's market capitalization increased almost 9x after Steve Jobs' passing.

The phrase 'Main pal do pal ka shayar hoon ( https://www.filmyquotes.com/songs/1256 ) ', Dhoni announced his retirement with this song ( https://www.instagram.com/p/CD6ZQn1lGBi/?utm_source=ig_embed&ig_rid=15464112-8b56-49ab-9771-2faf8747d1bf ) , eloquently captures the enduring truth that impermanence is the only constant.

https://lh4.googleusercontent.com/i1YlRLJOC1k0tTV5-zegcCsDNuO-2-5vZrNIy4ZIPDqaHKCtbfWO4Dbzezibg06lYD-EahELAgQxSwWAjs88jqko14myafd7xhcykTzfgOzDHsjs35HC5V5iQnr1Uj_t_w0-5Pi3xaggTrlUK1Pei9o

99% of all species that ever lived on Earth are now extinct. I once read that our universe has a lifespan of 70 billion years. On average, we live for 70 years. What do you get when you divide our lifespan by the universe's? Practically zero. Do good, stay humble, enjoy life, and move on.

All is well

Who doesn't face challenges? Sometimes, our brain makes us believe we're the only ones with problems. Social media doesn't help either. Social media feeds, full of smiling faces, can make our issues seem bigger. Everyone on the planet has problems.

http://img.youtube.com/vi//0.jpg  ( https://www.youtube.com/shorts/qXZgH6hEUU4 )

The best way to address a problem is to tell yourself "all is well" and confront it directly. Saying "all is well" doesn't solve the issue, but it gives you the courage to tackle it. As Aamir Khan mentions in the movie 3 Idiots, "I realized that people easily get scared. Trick your mind. Whatever the problem, tell yourself 'all is well.' Does it solve the problem? No, but it gives you the courage to face it."

Inner Scorecard

Would you rather be known as the world's greatest lover, yet have everyone believe you're the world's worst? Or would you prefer to genuinely be the world's worst lover, but have everyone believe you're the best? It's best to be the world's greatest lover, unconcerned with others' opinions.

Buffet's question about being the greatest lover brought an architect's tale to my mind. An architect stood, studying the temple he designed. A passerby, seeing his intense focus, asked why he stared so long at the temple. The architect replied that the finial (kalasam in Tamil) was slightly misaligned. The passerby commented that the flaw was so tiny, no one would notice. However, the architect said, "But I see the defect."

The architect wasn't concerned with public opinion. He focused on his inner scorecard, which recognized the slight misalignment. My many years of life have taught me that what truly counts is our inner scorecard. It's genuine and can't be faked. Give your all in everything you do. Even if outcomes don't meet expectations, your inner self will find contentment.

When Kamal Haasan felt like nothing was going right, he talked to his mom. He was scared and asked if he would amount to nothing. His mom said she'd be proud even if he cleaned toilets. But she wanted him to be the best toilet cleaner in India. Ignoring outside judgment and simply giving your best leads to a true inner scorecard.

If someone had told me three and a half years ago that there would be something called ChatGPT that could answer questions across almost any discipline, I would have called them a lunatic. And yet, here we are.
That’s exactly what ChatGPT does today. I can ask it coding questions, ask it to improve my grammar, get a simple explanation of tariffs, or understand how amortization math works. For most things, I can just ask, and I get a usable answer.
Most of the time, it gets things right. It’s a probabilistic system, so of course it makes mistakes. But the product is so good that sometimes it makes me wonder: should I even bother learning anything deeply if I can just ask it for answers?
Thinking about this more carefully, I realized something important. I get the best answers from ChatGPT on topics where I already have some understanding. If I know nothing about a subject, how do I judge whether the answer I’m getting is correct? I simply don’t have any intuition yet.
For example, if I already understand the basics of mortgages, I can quickly spot when an amortization explanation makes sense or when something feels off. If I’ve read a bit about geopolitics, I can tell whether an explanation of tariffs is shallow or nuanced. Without that base, everything sounds equally convincing.
That led me to two ideas that really stuck.
First, you need a broad understanding across multiple disciplines. Not deep expertise in everything, but enough to have intuition and context.
Second, you need to know how to ask good questions. The world has always rewarded people who can ask better questions. With tools like ChatGPT, that skill becomes even more valuable. The quality of the output depends heavily on the quality of the input.
So it really comes down to this:
- Build a broad base of understanding.
- Learn how to ask the right questions.
If I were starting fresh today, knowing this, what would I read to build that kind of broad, first-principles understanding across disciplines?
Below is a list of books and resources that helped me in this journey. I’m not an expert, and I certainly haven’t mastered all of this. These are simply things I’ve found useful over time.
What I like about these books is that they don’t just dump facts on you. They slowly shape how you think, how you reason, and how you build intuition. They help you develop a calmer, clearer way of looking at the world, whether the topic is money, health, technology, or society.
For me, this kind of learning isn’t just about being smarter. It’s about becoming a bit wiser, making better decisions, and staying mentally healthy in a noisy world. When you have that foundation, you can ask better questions, spot nonsense more easily, and use tools like ChatGPT in a much more thoughtful way, rather than just taking answers at face value.
These books helped me build that base, one small step at a time. This isn’t meant to be read top to bottom. It’s more like browsing a bookstore or a library. Pick what catches your eye, ignore the rest, and come back when your curiosity changes.

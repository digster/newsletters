Good Morning Ishan,

If you thought banks hiking fixed deposit rates was the silver lining in all this rate hike gloom, Anand has a reality check for you. 

And Arundhati has a reality check for Indian fintechs like Slice and Paytm*, who have made some important product decisions these last few weeks. But these aren’t merely features, and the product decisions affect the one thing that fintechs have the least of, as it is. 

And no, we aren’t talking about profits.

Trust—the ultimate casualty of the fintech winter

The kinds of features that a company rolls out or rolls back say a lot about its priorities, and the context under which it is operating. 

Here are two examples. 

First, darling of the fintech lending world—Slice. A company that shot to fame with its easy-to-apply, easy-to-use credit card-like product which allows people to split their payments into three interest-free monthly instalments. A credit card challenger; cleverly named to effectively communicate the nature of its star feature—slicing up due payments into more easily payable instalments. 

And it was quite a hit, too. Slice’s three-month EMIs, along with the fact that it approved and issued its cards easily, proved to be a big draw, especially for time credit card users. Such was the love for Slice that its monthly card issuance numbers were almost on par India’s top credit card-issuing banks. 

As my colleague Jaspreet had written in a prescient story, the startup was issuing as many as 200,000 to 250,000 cards a month, trailing only HDFC Bank and ICICI Bank, both of which issue between 250,000-300,000 credit cards each month. 

So imagine the surprise of its users a few weeks ago when it said it was going to be selective about who could use the ‘Pay-in-3’ feature.

Tiger Global-backed fintech Slice will allow only select customers with a good credit score or repayment history to use its standout ‘Pay-in-3’ feature on its credit-linked Visa card, in what is a key departure from its previous model. The move signals a more conservative approach amidst a tough growth environment with a focus on a profitable business model.
Slice turns conservative amid slowdown, restricts ‘Pay-in-3’ feature to select card customers, Moneycontrol

Some of these users found overnight that if they didn’t make a full repayment of their bill in 30 days, they could pay annual interest rates of over 20% if they carried over the instalments for a couple of months, as seen from the example below.

Source: Abhishek Shukal, LinkedIn

None of this is surprising, considering capital is drying up and the focus for all companies is shifting to profitability. Afterall, even in the good times, Slice was operating on hazy economics.

Even though Slice positions itself as a challenger to credit cards, its economics is admittedly weaker than the credit card business. For instance, in credit cards, the issuing banks retain the majority of the MDR. This typically ranges between 1.5-3.5% of a transaction. Lenders like Slice, on the other hand, usually end up seeing only between 0.8-1.5% of the spend-based charges, with their partners pocketing the rest.

Additionally, monthly spends on Slice average between Rs 10,000-15,000 (US$135-200) as opposed to retail-focussed credit cards—between Rs 30,000-40,000 (US$400-540). This further shrinks Slice’s MDR revenue.
Lending is easy for Slice, making money isn’t, The Ken

But, if this is really what it takes to make Slice a profitable, sustainable business, maybe it should have begun with this. That way, users would have known exactly what they were signing up for. 

Which brings us to my second example: a new feature from fintech major Paytm*. This one is quite likely to drive up the usage of the company’s postpaid offering, which is essentially another buy-now-pay-later (BNPL) product. 

The Paytm wallet is one of the payment methods I have saved on Swiggy. And each time it was time to make a transaction, it would show me what my wallet balance was. If there wasn’t enough, I’d either use another payment method or load up the wallet. 

Until, a few weeks ago, Paytm began clubbing the limit for its postpaid product along with the wallet balance, resulting in the display of an inflated balance. The feature is designed in a way that even if the wallet balance runs out, the remaining amount is covered by the available postpaid balance. 

Clever? Too clever, if you ask me. 

As we’d written before, postpaid and BNPL products are nothing but loans, with all the liabilities and risks associated with them. If users are not vigilant, they could end up taking a small loan without even knowing it, where a delayed payment could ding up their credit scores. 

The new feature doesn’t allow users choice in how they want to pay, because it doesn’t tell them whether they are spending their wallet balance or taking a loan. For Paytm, though, it means more postpaid users and more loans disbursed.

So there we have it. Two examples—a roll back, and a roll out. 

And trust, the casualty in both. 

Because as fintechs try to navigate through suddenly rough waters, those who are feeling the pinch first are the customers. And whatever little trust the companies had managed to earn with all those VC dollars, they are now putting at stake with such tactics.

*Paytm founder Vijay Shekhar Sharma is an investor in The Ken

Getting real about real rates

It’s repo rate hike season. 

In a surprise move in early May, the Reserve Bank of India (RBI) had bumped up the repo rate by 40 basis points (bps) to 4.4%. And now, with the Monetary Policy Committee (MPC) set to meet this week, another increase is imminent. 

The RBI’s Governor Shaktikanta Das has actually already called this a no-brainer (just in case you were having doubts). Both the government and the central bank are scrambling to rein in soaring retail inflation, which shot up to 7.79% in April. So much so that the government finally cut excise duties on petrol and diesel in May, and the RBI is now reloading its rate hike gun. 

This interest rate upcycle may be tough news for borrowers, but for investors in fixed-income instruments like bank deposits, it is actually a much-needed respite. After suffering years of declining/stagnant interest rates, they can finally expect to earn more on these investments. Many banks and financial institutions have already started upping deposit rates and will only continue to do so if this week’s expected rate hike comes through. 

But.. and there’s always a but… this joy needs to be tempered. 

Because inflation is still running far ahead of deposit rates offered by most banks. And this is likely to stay this way for a while, rate hikes notwithstanding. 

This makes real rates negative. 

The real rate is what you get if you subtract inflation from the nominal rate of interest on your investment. Or, in other words, it is the actual rate of return after factoring in the rise in prices. 

So, for instance, if a bank today offers you 5.5% on its deposit and inflation is, say, 6.5%, the real rate you earn is a negative 1%. That’s a bad situation to be in, simply because your expenses are growing faster than your earnings. And those depending on such income to meet expenses will have to dip into their corpus to keep going.

When will real rates turn positive again? No one really knows. 

Here’s what the RBI Governor said in a recent interview:
We will move towards that (positive real rates). How soon will depend on the evolving situation, and it will not be correct on my part (to say when). In fact, it is not possible also to forecast how soon because the situation is so dynamic.

That’s not the only trouble. Positive real rates, if and when they come about, may not help everyone. Sure, positive real rates are better than negative real rates. But they are not the panacea for all ills, as it is often made out to be. 

This is because of something called cumulative inflation.

Inflation, essentially, means that prices rise. Low or falling inflation means that prices will still rise, only at a slower pace. Prices fall only when inflation is negative. Inflation also has a cumulative effect, that is, it builds up on the previous year’s base. Say your total expenses in Year 1 is Rs 100 and annual inflation is 5%, your expenses will be Rs 105 in Year 2, Rs 110 in Year 3, Rs 116 in Year 4, Rs 122 in Year 5, and so on. 

Now, let’s consider two scenarios of real rates—negative and positive. Negative real rate means that the return on your investment, say 4%, is less than the 5% inflation in the example above. Positive real rate means that the return on investment, say 6%, is higher than the 5% inflation.

Many folks, especially retired people, meet their expenses from the return on such fixed-deposit investments. For such folks, a negative real return results in income falling short of expenses from Year 1 itself.  And they will have to start dipping into the corpus right from the start. This has a cascade effect, because in the future years, the return is earned on a lower corpus. 

A positive real return is obviously better than a negative one. The income will be sufficient to meet expenses for more years. But eventually, cumulative inflation could become formidable, annual expenses would start exceeding income, and the need to dip into the corpus would arise here too, if after a few years. 

There are ways to reduce the risk of having to dip into the corpus. 

One, you could increase the size of the positive real returns—instead of, say, 1%, aim for 2-3% of positive real returns. Two, you could increase the size of the corpus itself. Both of these will help keep the return on investment exceeding expenses for a longer period. And yes, while calculating real returns, you have to consider post-tax returns, because that’s what you ultimately get in-hand. 

A suitable asset allocation with equity investments in the mix will help improve those positive real returns. And systematic, step-up investing over the years will help increase the size of the corpus. 

The goal, ultimately, should be to earn returns that comfortably beat cumulative inflation.

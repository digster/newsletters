Good Evening Ishan,
Nestlé’s instant-noodle brand Maggi has seen its ups and downs.
Among Indians, it triggers nostalgia—fond memories of hot steaming comfort food and chilly hill-station vacations. That’s a powerful asset for any brand, but there is also a period that has left a bit of a bad taste in the mouth and tainted its image. About a decade ago, at the height of its market dominance, Maggi found itself embroiled in an adulteration scandal. It was [even banned](https://www.cnbctv18.com/business/companies/nestle-india-cmd-suresh-narayanan-to-retire-in-july-2025-from-maggi-crisis-to-demonetisation-to-teaching-19488345.htm) for half a year in 2015.
A routine check by the Food Safety and Drug Administration of the Uttar Pradesh government and a few more checks after that led to a ban on Maggi due to the presence of MSG and high levels of lead.
The 17.2 ppm (parts per million) found in the sample was said to be over 1,000 times more than what Nestlé India had claimed. At the time, Nestlé’s best-known product had a whopping 63% share of India’s ₹5,000 crore noodle market.
Being banned for lead contamination is not a good look on any food brand, and crafting a comeback is probably as challenging as any crisis communication campaign gets. That’s exactly what its new managing director Suresh Narayanan faced when he took the reins of Nestlé India in August 2015. By June of 2016, though, Maggi was back on shelves and had [clawed back](https://www.livemint.com/Companies/xyFCHn7hGJm1zUkesEVy5L/How-Nestle-is-rebuilding-in-India18-months-after-the-Maggi.html) a fair bit of the 70%+ market share it had lost—57% compared to zero a year earlier. In the decade since Narayanan took over, Nestlé India’s sales grew 150% and profits 578%.
But earlier this month, the company [announced](https://www.moneycontrol.com/news/business/nestle-india-says-cmd-suresh-narayanan-to-retire-on-july-31-2025-12837023.html) that Narayanan would be retiring as chairman and managing director next year, in July 2025. Taking over the reins will be Manish Tiwary, a former country manager for Amazon India.
Maggi is in a [far better position](https://www.business-standard.com/companies/people/brand-maggi-stronger-than-ever-as-nestle-india-gears-up-for-new-leader-124100801142_1.html) now than it was in 2015, but that doesn’t mean its new leadership won’t have some daunting challenges of their own to face.
Maggi’s biggest [challenges](https://www.financialexpress.com/life/lifestyle-noodle-wars-the-instant-noodles-space-is-hotting-up-in-india-3545838/) have to do with how the instant noodle segment itself is now perceived, being associated more with taste and flavour than health. In India, the size of this market is expected to grow from US$1.88 billion to US$3.83 billion by 2028, but certain products find themselves increasingly out of favour among the health conscious, and newer players are exploiting this breach.
According to the Mordor Intelligence report, “Over the medium term, the market is driven by the players coming up with a new segment of healthier noodles as the consumers in India have become more health conscious. Also, this has opened an opportunity for the players to tap into the innovation part of making instant noodles with functional benefits…Moreover, instant noodles made from white flour were considered unhealthy, and players are coming up with noodles made from atta.” While health can be an enabler for companies, it can also be a major restraint.
It is a bit hard for Nestlé to claim to be a “healthy brand” because most of its product portfolio does not meet this definition, including several products under the brand Maggi. Something that the world’s largest food company has arguably admitted in some of its statements.
The world’s largest food company, Nestlé, has acknowledged that more than 60 per cent of its mainstream food and drinks products do not meet a “recognised definition of health” and that “some of our categories and products will never be ‘healthy’ no matter how much we renovate”.
Nestlé document says majority of its food portfolio is unhealthy, [Financial Times](https://www.ft.com/content/4c98d410-38b1-4be8-95b2-d029e054f492)
Secondly, and perhaps more importantly, Maggi is already facing competition from several formidable rivals. One of whom is Wai Wai noodles.
The story of Wai Wai dates back to 1984 Nepal, where the concept of instant noodles was just gaining popularity. It was launched by Binod Chaudhary, a Nepalese businessman who was looking for a product that could be made out of the surplus white flour from his Maida mills. These noodles are, by most accounts, already among the top three most popular instant-noodle brands in India now, and Wai Wai’s success got Chaudhary into Forbes’ billionaire list as early as 2013. The 2015 ban on Maggi also helped Wai Wai and ITC’s Sunfeast Yippee! to increase their market shares.
Currently, Wai Wai has a 28% share in the [Indian instant noodles market](https://www.moneycontrol.com/news/business/markets/instant-noodle-wai-wai-plans-pre-ipo-funding-for-indian-unit-12824708.html) and clocked an annual revenue of Rs 800 crore (US$95.5 million). And Chaudhary is envisaging an IPO in India by the first quarter of 2026.
Maggi has a strong brand legacy here, but Wai Wai made its initial inroads and gained popularity due to [word-of-mouth marketing](https://www.socialsamosa.com/2021/07/interview-varun-chaudhary-wai-wai-india-marketing-strategy/), and it is a nimble brand that has been constantly innovating despite having far more limited resources than Nestle. Now, it is getting more serious about competing in India, and Chaudhary’s biography—Making It Big: The Inspiring Story of Nepal’s First Billionaire—claims that the brand has a [history of outperforming](https://scroll.in/article/1071330/from-the-memoir-how-binod-k-chaudhary-of-wai-wai-noodles-fame-became-nepals-first-billionaire) Maggi in smaller markets.
And finally, Korean-inspired noodle products have also hit the Indian market now, and they position themselves as healthier options because they don’t use MSG.
Four-year-old Masterchow, for instance, [launched](https://www.passionateinmarketing.com/masterchow-heats-up-the-instant-noodles-market-with-k-chow-launch-launches-new-campaign-with-bold-flavors-and-slurp-games-challenge-am-media/) Korean Ramen brand K-Chow earlier this month. While instant noodle Indian flavours pioneered by Maggi Masala and the Thai-inspired Wai Wai have established markets by now, Korean flavours are the new kids on the block, and they’re being seen as disruptors.
The big and small, traditional and new-age firms are tapping deep into the Korean craze. “The Spicy Korean Cup Noodles was among MasterChow’s initial offerings precisely to tap into the Korean craze, and it has been well-received by our customers. As we continue to innovate, we invite our customers to stay tuned for more exciting products that may be Korean-themed,” Kataria says.
CG Foods’ Shukla continues to term the Korean craze as a “disruptor,” and data, too, hints at the massive rise in the demand. According to the consumer intelligence firm NielsenIQ, the size of the Korean noodles market has grown from `2 crore in 2021 to `65 crore in 2023. “The trend has sustained for a decent amount of time, so we can safely say it’s sort of a disruption,” Shukla says.
Noodle wars: The instant noodles’ space is hotting up in India, [Financial Express](https://www.financialexpress.com/life/lifestyle-noodle-wars-the-instant-noodles-space-is-hotting-up-in-india-3545838/)
Maggi may still be the market leader in India, but if it wants to preserve its crown for longer, it needs to reinvent its story for new consumers and a new market.

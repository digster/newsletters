Long and Short
Wed, 18 Jun 25
The story behind the numbers and motivations driving India's stock market..
Good Morning Ishan,
I’m not sure it’s just a coincidence that Sandeep Kataria’s resignation as chief executive of Bata Group has come at a time when the footwear maker’s India unit is struggling to emerge from its deep funk.
India is one of the Lausanne, Switzerland-headquartered company’s largest markets, where its shoes and sandals have been sold for nearly a century.
In fact, “What, Bata is not an Indian company?” is a familiar refrain among its shoppers here. Kataria ran Bata India for three years before being given the top job at its parent in 2020. Under his watch, Bata India shares doubled in value, despite the impact of Covid.
But investors have since soured on the stock. It’s down 10% over the past five years. The Nifty 500 index has jumped 170% in the same period.
Footwear retailers have had a rough time over the past couple of years, as consumers cut back on discretionary spends, but Bata’s woes are conspicuous. For eight consecutive quarters, its sales have barely grown. Its quarterly profits now are among its lowest since the pandemic.
Bata, like every mass-market consumer company, unfailingly talks about selling more “premium” products. But its gross margins don’t quite conform to the narrative. At 58%, they are no higher than they were three years ago.
A company aimed squarely at the middle class, Bata has had a Sisyphean struggle selling to consumers higher in the pyramid. The fact that its rival Metro Brands has been able to do just that is partly why its market value—Rs 32,700 crore ($3.8 billion)—is more than double Bata’s. Metro’s shoes are on average priced at Rs 2,400, or roughly 1.5X Bata’s.
In two years, the revenue contribution of Metro’s products that cost more than Rs 3,000 has risen sharply—from 46% to 55%. Metro’s high-income consumers are less sensitive to price rises than Bata’s and tighten their purse strings later in an economic downturn.
That it’s not aspirational is not Bata’s only problem. Zudio and Meesho, with their cut-price shoes, have redefined value like only Bata could until two decades ago.
In other words, Bata can’t aim up. Or down. That’s a terrible predicament.
Little wonder, then, that Bata is trading at 65X its profits over the past 12 months. While Metro is valued at 93X.
What few appreciate about Metro is its ability to sell over 90% of its products at full price. The figure for Bata is close to 70%, an analyst told me last year. Metro was also lucky to be the India franchisee for Crocs as its clogs became all the rage during Covid.
If you’re not convinced about the impact Crocs—with its unabashedly ugly designs and bright colours—has had on the footwear market, see this slide from Bata’s latest investor presentation.
Bata’s attempts to remake itself extend to decluttering its stores and offering fewer price points to choose from.
But neither shoppers nor investors are playing ball yet.

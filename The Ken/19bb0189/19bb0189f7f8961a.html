Circular deals and clever financial structuring reached a fever pitch this past year when Big Tech bankrolled the AI boom.

It was mostly a spectacle for ordinary mortals. But similar circular deals are routine in the world of private equity, especially in Indian healthcare and education, which PE firms can’t seem to get enough of.

Still, other than hand-wringing, there’s not much one can do to understand how big finance operates. Except when one of its interconnected entities decides to go public.

Elevate Campuses, the PE-backed edu-infra platform that owns St Michael’s School, has filed its IPO papers to list on Indian bourses. Thanks to mandatory disclosures, we finally have clear answers to the puzzling question: How do PE firms make money from schools that are required to run as non-profits in India?

The writer of today’s story, Mutasim Khan, has diligently parsed Elevate’s DRHP and sourced several documents from the Ministry of Corporate Affairs and the Telangana government to stitch together a story that is eye-opening, to say the least.

At The Ken, we’ve written about PE titan KKR’s acquisition of school chain Eurokids International and how such acquisitions shift priorities for schools—from essential services to telegenic ones like pickleball courts.

As a teaser, St Michael’s in Alwal, Hyderabad, pays higher rentals than Google and TCS do for their commercial-grade premises. It’s simply because the landlord and the tenant are the same entity for the school.

“It’s a Fight Club-like arrangement. And the first rule of Fight Club is you don’t talk about Fight Club,” writes Mutasim in today’s story. You cannot miss this one.

One way to read this situation is through the lens of a Stag Hunt coordination problem of Game Theory. The “good” equilibrium is firms investing in indigenous R&D and owning core IP, offers higher long-term payoffs, but only if many players move together. The current outcome looks like the “safe” equilibrium or rather bad equilibrium where firms rationally focus on manufacturing scale using borrowed technology because unilateral R&D investment is risky, slow to pay off, and hard to justify when peers aren’t doing the same.

In that sense, the persistence of borrowed tech may not just reflect a lack of capability or intent, but also a coordination failure, where individually rational choices lock the ecosystem into a collectively inferior outcome. Moving to the better equilibrium likely requires credible coordination through policy, shared R&D platforms, or anchor firms, rather than expecting individual firms to break away on their own.

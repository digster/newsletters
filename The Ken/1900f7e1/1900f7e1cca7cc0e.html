Good Morning Ishan,

Less than two years ago, India’s neobanks (digital-only banking startups) were all the rage. And Jupiter was a popular leader of the pack.

Its founder’s fintech pedigree allowed the startup to raise US$165 million; its valuation ballooned from US$70 million to US$700 million in just about two years, even when it launched no products or registered any revenue.

Targeted at younger demographics, Jupiter, like other neobanks, wanted to ride on the back of a superior digital experience that it offered for accessing banking products like savings accounts and fixed deposits of its partner banks.

But RBI clipped its wings, like it did for other fintechs. Since Jupiter realised the regulator also doesn’t like startups calling themselves a “bank” in any form unless they are licensed to be one, it morphed itself into a non-bank.

It got a license and embarked on an expensive journey of making money via lending. In the year ended March 2023, for every crore of a total of Rs 7 crore (US$838,000) in revenue earned, it lost Rs 47 crore (US$5.6 million).

In her reporting, Anjali figured the startup likely did better in the 2024 financial year. But in the wake of getting its finances in order, the neobank-turned-nonbank may just about become another annoying bank app. Check out the story.

The EU just ‘electrified’ trade wars

Even though a few countries such as Germany and Sweden advised the European Commission against slapping any new import duties on Chinese electric vehicles (EVs), the EU notified the carmakers on Wednesday about additional duties from next month.

With the existing 10% duty on Chinese EVs, the bloc’s new tariff goes up to 50%, half of what the US has imposed on Chinese EVs.

China, which applies a 15% tariff on European EVs, had already warned that it’d retaliate. The additional tariffs will hit Chinese automakers including BYD and SAIC, and even the American giant Tesla which runs factories there.

While China says it’ll seek to persuade other countries to oppose the EU’s decision, it has been aggressively looking eastward. Chinese brands accounted for about three-fourths of the Southeast Asian region’s EV sales in 2023.

EV sales doubled in Southeast Asia in the past year. As The Ken reported on Wednesday, China’s BYD sold more than 3X of what Tesla sold. Just like in India, Tesla’s Southeast Asian plan seems to be in limbo, leaving some white space for Chinese automakers.

China is calling the EU tariff “protectionist”; the EU is sticking to “competition must be fair”. In all this, India’s stance mellowed a bit in March when its new EV policy allowed imports from any country, including China. It’s time to watch the BYD and SAIC showrooms in India.

Trade Tricks
Tue, 14 Jan 25
A weekly newsletter about the biggest changes in commerce—focusing on shifts that matter to you..
Good Morning Ishan,
India’s most successful retail chief executive has called time on his stint at the country’s second-largest retailer. Over the weekend, Navil Noronha announced that he will vacate the corner office at Avenue Supermarts, which runs the supermarket chain Dmart, in a year.
While Dmart is associated more with its reclusive investor-founder Radhakishan Damani, Noronha made Dmart not just the most efficient grocer in India but in the [entire world](https://the-ken.com/tradetricks/dmart-and-investors-rekindle-their-love/).
Damani hired him to head Dmart’s operations in 2004, when Noronha was a twenty-something executive with the Indian unit of global FMCG giant Unilever—the current employer of his successor, Anshul Asawa.
Dmart had a mere five stores then. Now, it runs 387.
When Noronha took Avenue Supermarts public in 2017, it was valued at Rs 40,000 crore (US$4.6 billion). Its market capitalisation has since leapt sixfold. More than two decades after Dmart’s first store in Mumbai, grocery chains struggling to make money are common enough. Dmart, on the other hand, netted over Rs 2,500 crore in FY24.
But if a summary of Noronha’s stewardship of Dmart ended here, it would be grossly incomplete.
I have written a number of pieces arguing why Dmart doesn’t appear quite as invincible as it used to. Dmart [supersized](https://the-ken.com/tradetricks/dmart-and-the-supersizing-imperative/) its stores hoping to persuade its shoppers to add high-margin clothes and footwear to their cart. But a slump in discretionary spends and [Zudio](https://the-ken.com/tradetricks/zudio-wanted-dmarts-apparel-shoppers-dmart-is-now-hurting/)’s cut-price apparel took the wind out of Dmart’s sails.
But the strongest criticism of Noronha’s stint is that he was perhaps too sanguine about Dmart’s resistance to the e-commerce threat.
In the initial years after Avenue’s listing, Noronha managed to parry analysts’ questions about the company’s [lukewarm approach](https://the-ken.com/tradetricks/dmarts-e-commerce-bet-has-gone-from-counterintuitive-to-obsolete/) to e-commerce. Sure, online-grocery shopping was growing, but no one was close to making money. And Dmart was quite good at constantly becoming a better version of its frugal, highly profitable self.
So investors thought Dmart knew something they didn’t. But quick commerce’s emergence against all odds has made them rethink that assumption. Dmart, too, knows that it needs to act.
In the December quarter, the supermarket operator sacrificed some of the profits it holds so dear at the altar of growth. It ramped up discounts to persuade shoppers spoiled rotten by 10-minute delivery to keep visiting its stores. Sales in stores that existed a year ago grew 8%, but margins dipped.
Much of what Dmart shareholders believed was cast in stone is already changing—like stores will only become bigger, not smaller, and that Dmart offers enough value for customers to overlook the convenience of online shopping.
Asawa, the next CEO, will be judged as much on how close to instant delivery he can take Dmart—and how consistently—as on the revenue per square foot from its stores.
Dmart has already squandered too many years underestimating e-commerce. And the direction Asawa chooses for the retailer in his first two years can either burnish Noronha’s legacy, or mar it.

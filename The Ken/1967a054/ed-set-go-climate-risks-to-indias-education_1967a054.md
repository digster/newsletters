Ed Set Go
Mon, 28 Apr 25
The one stop to understand the biggest shifts in education, written by the best edtech journalist in the country..
Good Morning Ishan,
On my daily video calls with my parents in Gurugram, I watch my father toggle the buttons on the AC remote every few minutes. It’s either too hot or too cold in his room. The last time I called, I saw my mom in the background, coated with dust to the last fingernail. She was trying to save her plants from an unexpected dust storm.
Now, all of these things—heat spikes and dusty gusts of wind—are normal harbingers of summer in Delhi. But there’s a heightened alacrity to how quickly the weather shifts now, how unpredictable it’s become, and how unprepared we are to deal with it.
And maybe one of the sectors most affected by climate change—though the connection is seldom made—is education.
Schools run on strict cycles of completing the syllabus and prepping students for exams, peppered with breaks they know are coming.
Take the summer, for instance. Schools know how long students will be out, how to portion their teaching in a way that students retain some memory of what they’re learning in class, and exactly how long teachers have until the next exam.
But this clockwork pattern just doesn’t work anymore. These are headlines from last year in May, when a heat wave struck the north and schools scrambled to comply with official orders to shut down for the summer:
A [report](https://www.orfonline.org/expert-speak/india-s-children-at-risk-climate-change-is-deepening-education-inequities) published by the Observer Research Foundation (ORF) on 25 April notes that an increasing number of states are already planning to bring their summer breaks forward or change school timings to beat the heat. It’s not a complete reprieve for students though—they’re going to come to longer school days and extra classes.
In 2024, an estimated 54 million students in India were impacted by heatwaves alone. Moreover, school days were missed due to air pollution, flooding, and other climatic shocks—all adding up to a loss of cognitive and emotional growth for young kids.
Hot days and days with poor air quality lower children’s attention, memory retention, cognition, and academic performance. Studies from multiple countries, such as India, Brazil, and China, show a direct association between higher particulate matter (PM2.5) and nitrogen dioxide (NO2) with lower test scores. The World Bank estimates that students living in the hottest 10 percent of Brazilian municipalities lose nearly 1 percent of learning annually due to heat exposure alone. Evidence in India also shows air pollution’s negative impact on cognitive abilities and reduced levels of reading and maths outcomes.
India’s Children at Risk: Climate Change Is Deepening Education Inequities, ORF
There are also other problems caused by climate change—school buildings could be damaged in a flood, social services like hot meals provided by schools could be disrupted. Families may have to move away from their homes and find habitable areas to live again, taking kids away from their schools.
Globally, schools and nonprofits have sought alternatives to prevent long disruptions to the school year. After a major earthquake hit Lombok in Indonesia in 2018 and damaged school buildings, a charity organisation created “[Lego-like](https://www.aljazeera.com/features/2024/7/7/how-life-goes-on-after-an-earthquake-the-lego-schools-of-lombok)” blocks that could be built into a classroom. This material was tested to be quake-resistant. More recently, schools lost in California wildfires regrouped within the campus of an old Sears department store that had long been shut.
These arrangements, while useful, can’t replace the sense of security and continuity that comes from going to the same school everyday. There’s only one answer here: to educate future generations about the risks of climate change, the effects of which are abundant in their lives right now.
In the medium term, as the ORF paper suggests, there should be a focus on pivoting quickly to remote or online options to continue the school year. This would take significant skill, empathy, and creativity. It’s not enough to stick a kid in front of a screen if their school’s shut. We’ve seen it work poorly during the pandemic. If climatic shocks are a feature and not a bug, then there has to be some serious thinking behind how students can seamlessly toggle between real and virtual classrooms.
The ORF article ends on an interesting note. It offers a thought experiment: if almost a third of India’s population is under 18 and still on their educational journey, how can we prepare them for the constant risk of interruptions caused by climatic shocks? What kind of resilience do we need to build in them? And will their experience of “schooling” be totally different from the generations before them?
It’s a tricky time to be a young person. But that might just push us towards better solutions.

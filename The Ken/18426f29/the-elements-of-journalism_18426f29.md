I recently picked up a foundational textbook on journalism after an organisation requested a masterclass on journalistic storytelling. The task of preparing the curriculum for the same gave me an opportunity to go back to the basics.
It is funny but I realised how often journalists or other professionals forget why we do what we do. Or what is the definition, in my case, of journalism?
I admit, after a decade of reporting on and writing stories, I needed a refresher. And I found it in a short book called The Elements of Journalism, which breaks down all my mildly complex thoughts and experiences in an insanely vast and complex world into 10 elements.
The first of those elements has been etched in my memory since I read it earlier this month. It is one of those simple but profound statements that often come out of the mouths of mentor figures in Disney movies or Yoda himself and clarify everything.
Journalism’s first obligation is to the truth.
It is the first principle in the book. “All truths—even the laws of science—are subject to revision, but we operate by them in the meantime because they are necessary and they work,” write Kovach and Rosenstiel. Journalism, they continue, thus seeks “a practical and functional form of truth".
The stories we wrote this week are wonderful examples of how, as journalists, we tell stories that appear practical and functional but they are, in their heart, about truth.
For instance, on Tuesday, the day after Diwali, Aayush wrote about Swiggy’s newest offering called Minis, through which the foodtech major wants to leverage its 25 million users to pitch online storefronts to small brands that usually use social-media platforms like Instagram. However, even though Swiggy’s truth to go on a new journey to transform the decade-long hyperlocal food- and grocery-delivery business into something much bigger is bold, he
[asked](https://the-ken.com/story/why-swiggy-is-building-a-shopify-for-local-brands/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter), can it be successful in the real world with all the challenges ahead?
The day after followed with one of
The Ken’s newsroom-favourite forms of truth-telling. Things are not always as they seem. There is a thesis that is popular, widely believed and accepted, but there is something hidden behind it. It lives in the details. In Nadine’s
Counterthesis, Bhumi Varta’s Martyn Terpilowski argued that venture capital, which has been flowing into Indonesia and backing growing cohorts of startups, is also holding them back. The previous era gave rise to consumer-technology giants like GoTo, which is now listed on the Indonesia Stock Exchange (IDX). But Terpilowski says the model has its limits, and in reality, venture capital is hindering transformation in Indonesia because it isn’t fostering new tech. It's an amazing piece. Read it
[here](https://the-ken.com/story/counterthesis-bhumi-vartas-martyn-terpilowski-on-why-venture-capital-has-hindered-indonesian-entrepreneurship/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter).
On Thursday, Sashwata
[wrote](https://the-ken.com/why-payment-apps-and-p2p-lending-platforms-are-scratching-each-others-back/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter) that there is a lack of trust in the P2P-lending model because of a high number of defaults and no means for lenders to recover their money. They are now accepting it and moving on by partnering with payment apps. Fintech apps, on their part, hope to increase assurance by lending to people that their customers trust.
The week ended with Gaurav's story in which he said patience is a virtue when your true vision is too ambitious. By setting in motion the process to acquire AMRI Hospitals, Manipal Hospitals, the second-largest Indian hospital chain, is closer to an IPO and its chairman Ranjan Pai’s mission to build Manipal into one of the largest nationwide healthcare providers, because it is a journey with conversations around valuation, competition, and efficiencies, and that is
[complicated](https://the-ken.com/uncharted-accounts-manipal-hospitals-road-to-an-ipo-is-paved-with-pricey-acquisitions/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter).
It is not always fun, and it is certainly not easy to try to find the truth within and outside of ourselves, stick our necks out and speak it by wrapping it in disclaimers, context, and its shiny but limited merits. There is always a lot to learn and more to unlearn, about ourselves and our audiences. It is what we as journalists and storytellers do.

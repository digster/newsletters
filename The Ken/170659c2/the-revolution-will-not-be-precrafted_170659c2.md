Everyone loves Ikea.
What’s not to like about cheap and easy-to-assemble furniture that actually lasts?
What about applying that concept to your house? Yes, you’re less sure now.
Prefab housing—the official term for this ‘Ikea house’ concept—is designed entirely to make the process of building houses repeatable. But it has a reputation for being poor quality and, well, cheap. It’s one thing to buy a table—if it breaks, you buy another—but that doesn’t translate to your home.
Revolution Precrafted, a startup from the Philippines, set out to make prefab housing sexy. CEO and founder Robbie Antonio hails from a real estate family. He wanted to be the man who made prefab housing an international phenomenon with a focus on design and quality. Antonio won the backing of investors, including K2 Global and 500 Startups, who, in turn, valued Revolution Precrafted at over $1 billion—the first (and only) unicorn in the Philippines.
With funding in the bag, media attention, and a glut of agreements to develop complexes both locally and overseas, the stage was set. But the revolution hasn’t arrived as promised.
I mean that literally. One of Revolution Precrafted’s showcase developments in the Philippines is being constructed the traditional way—with cement rather than using prefabricated parts. The Ken went, saw, and took photos.
To be clear, we aren’t talking WeWork levels of combustion, but the company hasn’t hit financial targets, hasn’t delivered many homes, and is already dabbling in over a dozen new businesses as it seeks new sources of revenue.
The foundations are, indeed, key.

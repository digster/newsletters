Good Morning Ishan,
Yotta is the latest firm to offer services as a GPU cloud provider, and it is doing so with enormity. That power to handle tasks related to machine learning, big data processing, graphics rendering, and other heavy lifting is backed by 4,000 Nvidia H100, each of which has a price tag roughly equivalent to a Tesla Model 3.
There’s real demand. As it turns out, independent cloud providers—such as Yotta and its publicly listed rival E2E Networks—are the preferred service for mid-sized startups with a US$10–50 million annual run rate. Over the next 12 months, you can expect to see a surge in companies hitting that mark.
That’s why the company—backed by the real-estate giant, the Hiranandani Group—is starting its GPU cloud compute business at this moment, even though some factors work against it.
But it might just be taking it a little too far. One source put it bluntly: “The only way you’ll need these many H100s is if India enters a war.”
In one of today’s stories, Abhirami mapped out Yotta’s logic for making such a big splash and why the move might come back to bite the company. [Check out her report](https://the-ken.com/story/nvidias-ai-chips-are-about-to-flood-the-indian-market-but-who-needs-them/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter).
Then, our new contributor, Shanies, explores how Taiwan has managed to attract Indonesian students who are ready to specialise in STEM subjects and become engineers in the future. Taiwan’s semiconductor sector, in particular, needs to hire technical personnel from overseas because its own workforce is rapidly shrinking.
Given Taiwan’s many trade ties with Southeast Asian countries, it’s no surprise that its enterprises are sourcing talent from these locations. Young Indonesians who are eager to explore opportunities that aren’t as common at home now increasingly view Taiwan as a desirable place to study and build the foundations of their career. Many even plan to take their expertise back to Indonesia and start companies of their own.
This is all to say that in five to 10 years, Indonesia’s tech scene may look much more advanced, and it’ll be partly because the country’s future entrepreneurs had the chance to gain insights into Taiwan. Read Shanies’ story to find out more about the [current flow of students from Indonesia to the territory](https://the-ken.com/story/why-taiwan-poaching-tech-talent-isnt-bad-news-for-indonesia/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter).
Whether you like it or not, the nature of your job is changing, and parts of what you do are replaceable. There are a select few investors who will make a killing from it. They wrote cheques, and it was cash on tap for AI companies.
But maybe that’s changing.
In 2023, for the second year in a row, global investments in AI firms went down, according to data cited by TechCrunch. Last year specifically, mergers and acquisitions in this space fell by 31.2%.
At the same time, more AI developers are closing funding rounds. That means the cheque sizes are getting smaller, but more teams are getting the chance to build new products. As I’ve mentioned in a recent edition of The Snapshot, the latest Y Combinator Demo Day was packed with AI startups.
Even though there are plenty of projections, nobody really knows how AI will impact workforces around the world. We all have an understanding that we’ll have to coexist with AI-powered tools. Tomorrow’s versions of companies like Canva and Salesforce are being built now. Even though products like overpriced AI pins have hogged the spotlight, this ride is far from over.

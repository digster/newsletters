Good Morning Ishan,
 
When I was younger, I did all the things kids used to do inside cars. I traced raindrops, stared at the big billboards, and got excited when I thought that the moon was following me.
 
I was also backseat-driving my dad at age eight. I always asked him to steer clear of trucks carrying fuel and cement, mostly because I thought the gas in fuel trucks would explode, or that the big, churning drum of cement would spill onto our car and quickly harden, trapping us inside.  
 
As an adult, cement trucks don’t really worry me anymore, but today’s story reminds us that there’s still reason to be wary of the effects of how cement is produced on the environment and on a person’s well-being.
 
Govi takes us to Vietnam, the world’s largest exporter and third-largest producer of cement. The industry burns 10 million tonnes of coal each year, releasing toxins and greenhouse gases into the air.
 
Hoping to curb the industry's harmful health and environmental effects, the country is trying something new. Instead of using coal to fire cement kilns, they are using waste.
 
Cement plants may not be burning coal anymore, but the substitute doesn’t seem much better. Since plastic also releases toxins, there’s other issues getting in the way of a cleaner cement industry in Vietnam.
 
Spoiler alert: a black-market “mafia” of waste could also be part of the mix.

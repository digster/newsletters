Good Morning Ishan,
A proof-of-concept app from a one-person company becomes the basis of a massive nationwide roll-out of Digiyatra, India’s digital initiative for ease-of-flying. It also becomes a reasonable hit with 3.3 million app downloads in its first 15 months.
An Indian dream, right? A tiny company wins a startup challenge run by Niti Aayog and bags a Rs 10-crore (US$1.2 million) contract for building out the tech infrastructure.
Well, it’s a nightmare; even for a country that gives a fig for data security.
After an outage of a week, old Digiyatris have had to download a new app since 1 April. Whatever story they are being told about the old app—which collected personal identifiable data—becoming dysfunctional, it fails to encompass:
The hollowness of what the Digiyatra Foundation told The Ken in a statement: “The Digiyatra app is preparing for the future”.
The absence of any central tech stack running the app and how negligently the whole process was offloaded to a third party. In this case, the vendor was in the middle of carrying out a financial fraud while it won the central government contract.
Whether you use Digiyatra or not, you must read Rounak’s meticulously reported story on how Digiyatra1.0 was not just “bad hygiene” but “dangerous” for data protection. It’s a [free story](https://the-ken.com/story/you-need-to-download-digiyatra-again-but-its-less-about-a-tech-upgrade-and-more-about-a-scam/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter), so share it away.
When did a mechanical engineer make headlines in India?
For record placement salaries or otherwise?
Not in recent times, as far as I can tell.
Electronics manufacturing is making waves once again. After consolidating its iPhone manufacturing, Tata Electronics has reportedly bagged a “semiconductor” deal from Tesla. There’s a scramble for hiring apprentices in large numbers.
Apprenticeship programmes are about training-on-the job, including on the shopfloor. Such people are easy to train; it just requires resources and initiatives. But what is hard to find, leave alone train, are engineers who get the finer aspects of, say, mobile phone manufacturing. Such as precision engineering, CNC (computer numerical control) machining, PLC (programmable logic controller), laser deburring, and so on.
The latest model of a smartphone today is made of titanium—not steel or polymer of yesteryears. How many engineering grads can deal with this material?
More than two generations of engineering students in India have gravitated toward computer science and/or allied disciplines. It’s time electronics companies made some new branches of engineering sexy on Indian campuses.

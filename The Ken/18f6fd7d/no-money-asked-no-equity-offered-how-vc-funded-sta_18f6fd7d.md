Good Morning Ishan,
Why do you think entrepreneurs go on Shark Tank India? Other than having a shot at fleeting fame, of course.
To publicise their creations so that venture capitalists, large and small, know of their existence and hopefully write them cheques, right? A Bigg Boss-esque way station present on an otherwise typical, bruising startup journey.
But what if I told you there’s a whole bunch of founders coming to the show after a round or two of funding? Including from well-known investors such as Blume Ventures and Kalaari Capital.
Over one in four of the 143 startups in the first season of Shark Tank had been funded prior to the series. In season 3, which ended in March, that number rose to one in three. The average amount raised had shot up from US$96,000 to US$1.5 million.
In a sharp Monday [story](https://the-ken.com/no-money-asked-no-equity-offered-how-vc-funded-startups-found-a-hack-in-shark-tank-india/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter), Gaurav asks, and answers with examples, this simple question: why would these startups want to be on the show, especially when the hard bargain the “sharks” drive on valuation is detrimental to the companies’ existing investors?
Big spenders shore up smartphone brands
Last year, we wrote about how those buying pricey cars and premium homes were powering India’s [credit growth](https://the-ken.com/story/young-indians-are-driving-credit-growth-by-riding-premium-living-swanky-but-theres-a-catch/), and how different businesses were tapping the fortune at “[the top of the pyramid](https://the-ken.com/the-nutgraf/fortune-at-the-top-of-the-pyramid/)”.
In February, Goldman Sachs said India would have 100 million people making over US$10,000 each a year by 2027—up from 60 million in 2023.
There’s now yet more evidence for this big shift. In the March quarter, smartphones priced more than Rs 30,000 (US$360) accounted for one-fifth of the sales volume—the highest ever—and over half of the total value, according to research firm Counterpoint. Samsung’s average selling price in the period hit a new peak of US$425.
That’s almost one-fifth of India’s per capita income.

Good Morning Ishan,
During a meeting hosted by Indian finance minister Nirmala Sitharaman last week, one official posed a question just as the group was about to break for lunch. Vivek Joshi, secretary to the Department of Financial Services, asked a roomful of bankers about gold loans and what their institutions were doing to “curb the malpractices,” referring to irregularities in gold loan books.
Gold loans are hot now because the commodity’s value has risen over 20% in the last 12 months, while interest rates for this type of credit—which start at 9.15% per annum—are 2–4 points lower than that of unsecured personal loans.
The manipulation of gold loans is widespread. An investigation by The Ken indicates that public and private banks and non-bank financial companies engage in fraudulent lending practices through their gold loan verticals. Typically, these entities manipulate the loan value by misreporting the gold’s weight or purity. For some borrowers, collateral is even waived.
Lenders have different ways to bump up the loaned amounts. This, of course, is not meant to benefit borrowers. To find out what’s in it for anyone offering gold loans, [read Rounak’s report](https://the-ken.com/story/need-a-gold-loan-come-through-the-back-door/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter).
Over in Manila, Isa immersed herself in the world of Philippine Offshore Gaming Operators, or POGOs. Originally built to service offshore gamblers, the space is chock-full of crime, with unlicensed operators dabbling in kidnappings, money laundering, and black-market surgeries. In one high-profile case, a mayor was found to be involved with unlicensed POGOs that were involved in human trafficking.
The problem with POGOs is so severe that President Ferdinand Marcos Jr. issued an order to shut down all of them by the end of 2024.
By some estimates, there are more than 170 POGOs in the country, including 40 licensed operations. Up to 80,000 people work for these casinos; many are dealers, drivers, or IT support staff, but some are hired to do unsavoury things like run online scams.
President Marcos said his government would help all of these people find new employment, while the Philippines’ casino regulator is seeking ways to replace the income it expects to lose from evaporated POGO taxes—a nifty US$124 million paid by licensed operators each year.

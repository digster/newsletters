This is a somewhat awkward year-end wrap to write, as Zero Shot is just a little over four months old. So, let me end the year with how Zero Shot began, and with five book recommendations. 

It all started with Zeus, our wonderful (AI-generated) mascot. 

To be honest, not enough subscribers have really noticed Zeus. My wonderful colleagues (and co-producers of Zero Shot) Brady Ng, Rohin Dharmakumar, and I discussed how a zebra-like brontosaurus became the face of Zero Shot in our debut podcast episode. It’s a fun story, and you should listen to it. 

As we got deeper into 2025, it became apparent to us that artificial intelligence was really changing almost every career, industry, and business. The scale of this disruption exceeded normal technological cycles and had clear geopolitical implications. So we figured that the only way we could talk about AI meaningfully was to do it as a multi-perspective, multi-geography column-podcast, involving Bengaluru and Hong Kong.

And thus, Zero Shot was born—and with it, Zeus. 

Since then, we’ve been writing and discussing AI from multiple angles. I’ll readily admit that we aren’t the most optimistic voices on AI, but we are among the few seriously examining its first, second, and third-order effects. 

For instance, just a cursory look at the columns we’ve published this year spans the range of topics and emotions about AI. Here are some of my favourites:

Why are AIs so hard to trust? It’s not just because they hallucinate by Brady Ng 

One of my hobbies is to go and read great, compelling research papers written by academics. The denser it is, the better. The best ones blend a little bit of everything—statistics, anthropology, technology, and consumer behaviour. And well, Brady beat me to writing a column about what I consider one of the best research papers on AI published in 2025. Both the premise and the conclusion of the paper are straightforward and counterintuitive: AI language models’ hallucinations are features, not bugs. And as Brady writes, “If you’re like me and like to get down in the weeds, then you know how exciting it is to see irrefutable proof.”

China and the AI puck by Rohin Dharmakumar

I bet there are large parts of the world that’ve never watched ice hockey or even know what a puck looks like, but they know that quote that’s frequently attributed to Wayne Gretzky (but originates from his father, Walter). In this edition, Rohin channels that and argues, “When it comes to AI, watch China, not the US.” True to form, he also adds a ton of snippets and links about all things interesting about AI that he’s reading. In fact, one subscriber wrote in to say, “It took me 15 minutes to read the column and two hours to read all the links.”

Worth it.

The tech pilgrimage reversal: Bengaluru heads to the Bay by Praveen Gopal Krishnan

One of the things I enjoy writing about is the culture of technology in Bengaluru. And honestly, as far as AI is concerned, the truth was that there was almost no culture of AI at home. In the same weekend, I met two senior executives who were travelling to San Francisco not to hire or to raise funding, but just to be closer to whatever the hell was going on. This is startlingly new, and it reminded me of a pilgrimage. So I wrote about it.

Five book recommendations

At first, I thought about making this edition about AI predictions for 2026, but after giving it some thought, I decided not to. Mostly because I think it’s clear now that AI’s trajectory is going to be much longer (and likely more sinusoidal). On top of that, the geopolitical angle makes it feel much more unusual than the usual story of tech adoption. 

So instead, I decided to end the year with my five book recommendations. I tried to pick books, as much as I could, that cover technology, history, and politics, and that feel timeless. Some of these are some of my favourite books of all time. (None of these are affiliate links.)

The Whale and the Reactor by Langdon Winner 

Occasionally, a book comes along that makes you wonder just how remarkably prescient its author was in anticipating technology’s sweeping—and not entirely positive—effects. 

Langdon Winner published The Whale and the Reactor exactly 40 years ago. Despite this, nearly every chapter reads as though it were written for the age of AI, during a time when techno-optimism was the dominant global mood (just like today). What makes it even more compelling is that it’s not a book about technology, but also about politics, society, power, and liberty. Technical decisions that seem logical are, in fact, political decisions, according to Winner.  This is a perspective that STEM-educated tech bros miss completely, and if you want to fill that gap, this is the only book you should read. Winner writes with an acerbic style that makes it a wonderful, joyous experience. 

Long-time subscribers of my earlier newsletter, The Nutgraf, are well aware that I’ve been a longtime fan and admirer of Langdon Winner. I referenced his most famous paper, “Do artifacts have political properties?” in one of the most popular editions when I wrote about the floods of Bengaluru. 

As AI changes everything over the next few years, I predict more and more people discovering the genius of Langdon Winner. On that day, I hope I’ll be able to say, “I read him before all of you.” And you can say it too.

***

Everyday Technology: Machines and the Making of India’s Modernity by David Arnold

There are books about “big” technology, but this is a book about “small” technology. 

David Arnold argues that if you really need to get a picture of how technology seeped into India’s society, you need to understand the evolution of small machines and consumer goods. These machines, which originated in Europe and North America, became objects of everyday use in India in the late 19th and early 20th centuries.

Think about objects like sewing machines, bicycles, and typewriters. How did they proliferate in India?

This book tells that story through the lens of society, class, and caste. The writing is a bit dry, but the research is excellent. 

***

Midnight’s Machines: A Political History by Arun Mohan Sukumar

I described Midnight’s Machines to a friend recently as, “India After Gandhi but exclusively about technology”.

This is the book that chronicles how technological progress happened in India, told through the perspective of governments, policy, and geopolitics. In fact, I’m going to quote the description of the book on Amazon, and you can decide if any of this sounds familiar to you in 2025: 

“Beneath its soaring rhetoric on the virtues or vices of technology, the state buried a grim reality: India’s inability to develop it at home. The political class sent contradictory signals to the general public. On the one hand, they were asked to develop a scientific temper, on the other, to be wary of becoming enslaved to technology; to be thrilled by the spectacle of a space launch while embracing jugaad, frugal innovation, and the art of ‘thinking small’. To mask its failure at building computers, the Indian state decried them in the seventies as expensive, job-guzzling machines. When it urged citizens to welcome them the next decade, the government was, unsurprisingly, met with fierce resistance. From Jawaharlal Nehru to Narendra Modi, India’s political leadership has tried its best to modernize the nation through technology, but on its own terms and with little success.”

If you’re trying to make sense of India’s AI policy in 2025, I guess this is the book where you must begin.

***

Zen and the Art of Motorcycle Maintenance by Robert Pirsig 

The first time I read Zen and the Art of Motorcycle Maintenance, it was a full 20 years ago, when I was still in engineering college. The second time I read Zen and the Art of Motorcycle Maintenance was last week, when I was on a break, staying at a beachside home off the Konkan coastline. I found it amidst a pile of old books in the library at the Airbnb where I was residing, and picked it up to see if it still held up. 

It did. 

In fact, in many ways, it’s even better. I guess the inquiry about melding Eastern and Western philosophy, the classic and the romantic, and above all, the ways to think about technology and our relationship with it, and the ephemeral nature of “quality” is what makes it much more relevant in an age of AI slop. If you (like me) are now struck and amused by tech people suddenly writing about “taste” on LinkedIn, remember that Pirsig got there first, decades ago.

***

Planning Democracy: Modern India’s Quest for Development by Nikhil Menon

Although this is a book about India’s planning project, there’s a chapter in here that exclusively covers how India managed to procure advanced technology, specifically supercomputers, and walked the Cold War tightrope. 

The book itself is wonderful, but you can read the chapter as a standalone article, which is titled “Fancy Calculating Machines”.

 That’s all from me. Have fun, and we’ll see you in 2026!

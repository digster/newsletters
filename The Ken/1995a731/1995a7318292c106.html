I’ve long been a gold sceptic. 

Growing up in the 2000s, we were told equity was the asset class to bet on. Stocks would make you rich, preserve purchasing power, and leave gold looking like an archaic relic—a shiny rock that produces nothing, pays no dividends, and just sits in vaults racking up storage costs. 

Warren Buffett thought the same. In 2011, he famously wrote:

“Gold… has two significant shortcomings, being neither of much use nor procreative… If you own one ounce of gold for an eternity, you will still own one ounce at its end.”

I agreed with him. Until I didn’t.

If you’ve been dissecting gold’s performance as an investment over these last few months and years, you probably understand why. In rupee terms, for instance, gold has appreciated nearly 40% in value this year alone. In 2024, it rose over 30%; in dollar terms, 27%. And before you think this is some short-term spurt, let me say for the quarter-century leading up to 2025, gold prices in rupee terms have increased at a compound annual growth rate (CAGR) of 13.2%—handily beating inflation. 

If you had invested Rs 1,000 in gold in 2000, that would now be worth over Rs 23,000. The Nifty50’s CAGR during the same period is just around 12%.

Now sure, recent geopolitical volatility may be driving up gold demand because it’s seen as a safe-haven asset. But there’s more to this than just hedging. 

Dig a little deeper and you’ll see that gold’s resurgence is strongly tied to steadily increasing patronage from the very institutions that once abandoned it half a century ago. 

Central banks.

What is money, really?

When Covid arrived in early 2020 and the global economy tanked, the US government fired up its “money printer” and created 26% of all dollars in existence out of thin air. Around the same time, I stumbled upon Nick Szabo’s essay Shelling Out: The Origins of Money. It forced me down a rabbit hole. 

The fiat currency system—the one we all take for granted now—is only 50 years old. 

Simply put, fiat money is just currency backed by trust in the issuing central bank and nothing else. It’s called “fiat”—Latin for let it be done—because its value comes from government decree. A Rs 100 note has value not because it’s redeemable for something tangible like gold or silver, but because the Reserve Bank of India says so—and because everyone else accepts it in exchange. 

This is the case for most of the world right now. 

But things were different before 1971. 

Because it was only on 15 August that year that US President Richard Nixon cut the dollar loose from gold. 

You see, until then, the world’s monetary system had been governed by the famous Bretton Woods Agreement. At the end of World War II, with a global economy in tatters, the world’s nations had gotten together to hammer out a new monetary framework—the US dollar would be pegged to gold at $35 an ounce, and other currencies would be pegged to the dollar. 

Once in effect, this made the US dollar “as good as gold”.

And for 25 years—for better or worse, depending on who you asked—it worked. The US dollar became the world’s reserve currency. Europe and Japan were rebuilt. The US enjoyed roaring prosperity.

But by the late 1960s, cracks started to appear. The American economy had grown too big. Dollars were being printed far faster than gold reserves. Imports surged, exports lagged, inflation crept in. Foreign governments started asking for gold in exchange for their dollars—more gold than the US had!

By 1971, this mismatch had become unsustainable. There were four times as many dollars in circulation as there was gold to back them. 

Which led to that day in August 1971 when President Nixon went on television and announced the decision which soon came to be known as the “Nixon Shock”. The US dollars would no longer be redeemable for gold. 

He called it temporary. It never ended. And the Bretton Woods system collapsed. 

Since then, we’ve lived in a credit-money world—where all money is backed only by confidence.

“Gold is money. Everything else is credit.”

J P Morgan

Fiat failing

Nixon’s decision didn’t dethrone the US dollar as the world’s reserve currency, though. But ever since, global trust in the dollar has found expression in just one instrument: US Treasuries.

Think of a Treasury as an IOU from the US government. You lend Washington money, and it pays you back with interest. And because the US economy was dominant, these instruments were considered the safest asset on earth. Central banks across the world preferred them over gold—they were liquid, “risk-free,” and generated a return.

But cracks began to show during the global financial crisis of 2008. To keep the economy afloat, the US Federal Reserve began printing unprecedented amounts of money and buying Treasuries by itself. The balance sheet swelled again during the Covid pandemic, when 26% of all US dollars in circulation now were created in just a few months. 

It became increasingly more obvious that US debt was being financed not by sustainable demand, but by the Fed’s printing press. 

For central banks, Treasuries started to look less like a safe asset and more like a slow-moving liability. 

And then came 2022, when—in response to the outbreak of the Russia-Ukraine conflict—the G7 nations froze $300 billion of Russia’s reserves. This revealed a second risk: geopolitical. Treasuries might be safe financially, but not if you ended up on Washington’s wrong side. 

Faced with both reckless money printing and geopolitical weaponisation, central banks began to look for a safer anchor. 

The answer they arrived at was the same shiny metal that had backed money for centuries: gold. 

Fast forward to today, and—like I mentioned at the beginning—gold is having its best run since 1978. That’s on top of strong returns in 2023 and 2024. Even in 2022, when equities sank under rising rates, gold held steady.

Almost all of that is being driven by one cohort of buyers: central banks. Since late 2022, they’ve been buying gold in record volumes, and prices have more than doubled to over $3,600 an ounce.

“Central banks have accumulated over 1,000 tonnes of gold in each of the last three years, up significantly from the 400–500 tonne average over the preceding decade.”

Juan Carlos Artigas, World Gold Council

For years, investors were told to keep gold at 5–10% of their portfolios. As a hedge against equity volatility, nothing more. But central banks aren’t treating it as a hedge anymore. They’re treating it as a geopolitical asset. Since 2014, they’ve stopped accumulating dollar reserves and instead purchased more than $600 billion in gold.

The Russia sanctions changed the game. If Russia’s assets could be frozen, so could anyone’s. And since gold carries no credit or counterparty risks, it is steadily regaining its status as the most crucial reserve asset worldwide. To quote macro analyst and Forest For The Trees founder Luke Gromen:

“Gold is not just a hedge anymore. It’s becoming the neutral reserve asset in a world where trust in fiat is breaking down.”

As the US national debt approaches $38 trillion and foreign demand for Treasuries dries up, central banks are insulating themselves from “the inevitable devaluation of fiat currency,” Gromen notes. 

Which brings us to what all this means for investors. Yes, gold can have its share of underperformance during certain periods. But zoom out, and it has quietly done its job: protecting purchasing power better than most. In fact, if you had invested in gold in August 1971 instead of the S&P 500 index, your gains would have actually been better—a return of 84X versus 66X, respectively. 

So yes, gold may just be a shiny rock. It produces nothing. It doesn’t innovate. 

But it has outlasted empires and beaten even the mighty S&P 500 across five decades because it has no counterparty risk, and no one has the monopoly to mine gold at their whims and fancies.

That makes it deserving of more than a token place in your portfolio.

Good Morning Ishan,
Airtel Payments Bank can’t hold a lot of your money. So it’s selling that as a feature.
Recently, the bank noticed that an increasing number of users were parking Rs 1,500–2,000 in their accounts and setting up UPI autopay. For electricity bills, OTT subscriptions, bus passes, and whatnot. But this wasn’t convenience-driven behaviour.
Users are essentially treating their Airtel account as a risk-limited spending wallet—a place where a fraudster could only steal so much because the regulator won’t allow them to hold more than Rs 2 lakh anyway.
Airtel didn’t create this hack. Consumers did. And so the bank had an epiphany: if people already think of it as the “blast-radius account”, why not just market the blast radius?
Thus began the campaign: a bank telling you to keep your real money somewhere else and use its own only for UPI spending. “Open a second account,” Airtel now practically says, “and lose less when the inevitable fraud hits.”
But payments banks were never meant to scale. There are no big depositions or lending. And there’s barely a margin. Earning Rs 24 per user while spending Rs 21, as the bank’s CEO tells Arundhati, the author of today’s story. So, it desperately needs tens of millions of Indians to adopt this safety habit.
But what if the behaviour is so universal that users can replicate it with literally any other bank account they already own? In that version of the story, Airtel ends up running an expensive national awareness campaign. [Today’s story](https://the-ken.com/story/why-airtel-payments-bank-sees-its-no-1-future-tied-to-being-your-no-2/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter) follows that tension between a clever user hack, a trapped business model, and a bank trying to turn its regulatory ceiling into a mass-market virtue.

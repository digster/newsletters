A weekly newsletter about the biggest changes in commerce—focusing on shifts that matter to you..
Good Morning Ishan,
When you decide to paint your house, what matters more: the variety in the color catalogue or what’s printed on the bucket holding that paint?
With the market struggling to grow, one player is now scrambling to use prettier packaging and fresh messaging to make customers pick their paints.
For instance, its buckets now have a regional touch. Label graphics feature a caricature of a Bharatnatyam dancer in Tamil Nadu or a drawing of the Taj Mahal in Uttar Pradesh. The facelift is specifically for the premium and luxury ranges offered by market leader Asian Paints.
That’s not all. It has also taken a page out of skincare companies’ playbook, where consumers are educated about the ingredients of their serums. In a recent earnings call, Asian Paints said they are highlighting additives like “PU serum” (for durability) or “graphene” (for strength) on bucket labels.
Asian Paints is putting in all of this extra work because it is struggling to hold onto its market share, which fell to 52% at the end of FY25, down from 59% a year prior, per a [Reuters report](https://www.reuters.com/world/india/birlas-big-paints-bet-rattles-asian-paints-india-reign-2025-05-13/) citing Elara Securities data. In comparison, since its launch in February 2024, market share of the new entrant Birla Opus, which is owned by Grasim Industries, has reached 6.8%, the report added.
Analysts were already bearish, with more than half who track Asian Paints issuing a [“sell” call](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-05-08/asian-paints-draws-record-sell-calls-ahead-of-quarterly-earnings) for the company’s stock ahead of its latest quarterly earnings call in May. Then, Reliance Industries said on Friday that it had sold a 3.6% stake in Asian Paints for [Rs 7,704 crore](https://www.msn.com/en-in/money/markets/reliance-sells-36-stake-in-asian-paints-in-rs-7700-crore-deal/ar-AA1GBFsi). The conglomerate made the sale to SBI Mutual Fund in a block deal through its affiliate, Siddhant Commercials, after holding onto the shares for 17 years.
So, why are these cracks appearing for Asian Paints?
In the May earnings call, Asian Paints CEO Amit Syngle said his company was facing the “double-whammy” of a slowing market and heightened competition. He also pointed out FY25 was the first year in two decades for the overall paint market to decline in value.
Matters were dire specifically for decorative paints, which account for over two-thirds of the overall pie and is a segment where Asian Paints leads. It [grew](https://www.european-coatings.com/news/markets-companies/asian-paints-reports-weaker-earnings-for-fy-2025-amid-market-headwinds/) just 1.8% in Q4.
While brands like Berger Paints and Akzonobel, which sells Dulux, still managed to grow their business, Asian Paints has been regressing over the past five quarters.
All of this is taking place while the Competition Commission of India, the antitrust regulator, is reviewing a confidential complaint filed by Birla Opus, which alleges Asian Paints is abusing its market position, per a Reuters [report](https://www.reuters.com/sustainability/boards-policy-regulation/birlas-paints-unit-files-antitrust-case-against-indias-asian-paints-sources-say-2025-06-06/).
Conditions for Asian Paints are gloomy, but a new entrant sees much opportunity in the paints industry. During the May earnings call of Grasim Industries, which sells Birla Opus paint, managing director Himanshu Kapania pointed out that India’s paint consumption is 3.5 kg per capita, while the global average is 10 kg per capita. The number goes higher in developed nations, reaching 25 kg per capita.
The Birlas are not shying away from moving in on the incumbents.
A Birla Opus ad that first aired in November 2024 starred actors Vicky Kaushal and Gajraj Rao. Rao’s character says to his friend, “Humesha wala lagana tha” (“Should have used the usual one”), nudging him to use legacy brands. The protagonist, played by Kaushal, retorts and says that we need to evolve with the times and try new things.
New players like Birla Opus have deep pockets, they have been targeting the same distributors used by well-established brands like Asian Paints. They compete by offering more volume at the same price, says Amnish Aggarwal, head of research at Prabhudas Lilladher, a brokerage firm headquartered in Mumbai.
Another new entrant, Pidilite, which entered the paint space in 2023, could also sell its paint at lower prices and offer better trade terms for distributors. This competitive intensity will likely sustain for the next several quarters, said Aggarwal.
Facing this competition, Asian Paints is keeping its prices as is, even if that move is at the expense of the company’s market share.
Asian Paints’ share price also tells the story of its slump. While the Nifty 50 grew by 6% over the past 12 months, Asian Paints’ stock fell by 23% in the same period, making it one of the worst performers among paint stocks.

Still a market leader, the company is opting to emphasise the legacy and trust that its brand has built over time. A recent ad campaign runs the line “India ka har dusra ghar kehta hain Asian Paints”, which translates to “every other house in India chooses Asian Paints”, playing up how it still controls a bit more than 50% market share.
But should it focus on painting every second home in its colors, or should it prepare for a new chapter as the market for paint changes? That’s something the company needs to ponder.

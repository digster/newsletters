Good Morning Ishan,
 
In another month or so, the first wave of talented and ambitious youngsters will be graduating from India’s premier colleges. And what is the sector that most of them dream of joining? Consulting, of course.

But what if I told you that commercial consulting is actually creating a huge welfare loss for society by getting some of our smartest talent to work on absolute junk?
 
Wait, what? 'That’s a harsh statement to make this Wednesday morning, Rohin; what have you been reading,' you’d probably say. 
 
Fair. 
 
But what if I put it like this: “Commercial consulting is actually creating a huge welfare loss for society by getting some of our smartest talent to work on absolute junk. That's a big problem, says Gaurav Gupta."
 
Now, who’s Gaurav Gupta, you’d ask. 
 
He is a managing partner with Dalberg Advisors, a global social impact and strategy consulting firm with nearly 600 employees and close to $100 million in revenue.
 
It gets interesting now, doesn’t it?
 
In an uncharacteristically (for a strategy consultant) candid, reflective, and sharp conversation with Seema Singh, our editor, Gupta punctures many aspects of consulting that we, sort of, take for granted. 
 
For instance, India has proven to the world that building public goods in areas like identity and payments can dramatically multiply innovation and growth. So, shouldn’t consulting companies that work in these sectors, and especially on public-focused problems, make some of their public goods recommendations?
 
That’s not my rhetorical question, but Gupta’s suggestion, one that runs contrary to the “principles” of consulting—secrecy (often in the name of confidentiality).
 
Gupta proudly talks about a bunch of other heretical stuff. Like “destroying equity” as a firm so that none of the partners, current (40) or future, would have any larger say over what the firm does. Or housing the Dalberg brand in a non-profit trust.

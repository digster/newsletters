Online retail in India is booming, but actually, it should not. Because the regulations prevent it. In fact, India’s commerce regulators have tried their utmost to keep online retail in check.
 
By not allowing foreign investment in retail for the longest time. Then allowing it, but only for physical retail. Then forgetting to define what online marketplaces were. Then defining it after a few years, but saying no one seller could sell more than 25%. Then saying the same e-commerce company couldn’t sell food and groceries alongside smartphones and apparel. That the warehouses had to be different, too. 
 
India’s e-commerce regulations are what lawyers dream of. Capricious, constantly changing, vaguely worded and capable of inflicting immense damage if you end up in their crosshairs.
 
And yet, e-commerce in India is booming. Walmart bought Flipkart for $16 billion. Amazon is spending billions to become numero uno. How?
 
Welcome to Loophole Games: Subsidiary Edition. Large e-commerce giants have stayed one step away from the letter of the law (didn’t you get the memo? Nobody really cares about the intent these days) by floating new subsidiary companies to sidestep every new regulation.
 
As a result, Indian e-commerce is this elaborate Kabuki ritual with the government, regulators and companies engaged in a highly choreographed and complex dance routines which make the viewer forget the fact that loopholes have become the new entry doors. 
 
Vivek’s story today looks at the subsidiary structures of the three e-commerce giants—Amazon, Flipkart and Paytm—and tries to map them back to regulations and business strategy.

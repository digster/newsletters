Budget-branded clothes in India aren’t necessarily chic or durable. In 2018, Zudio set about fixing that while also redefining “cheap”: it priced everything in its stores under Rs 1,000.

That its parent, Trent, is part of the much-loved Tata Group immediately instilled trust in customers. In short order, Zudio pulverised the competition and took Trent’s shares to stratospheric heights (up nearly 1,500% in five years).

Zudio is now working on an encore in cosmetics, selling sheet masks at Rs 50 a pop and deodorants at twice as much. The business of beauty has over the past decade drawn a number of venture-capital-backed brands. One of them, Mamaearth, even managed to go public late last year.

Central to their rise is a simple fact: the existence of contract manufacturers who are happy to make (almost) the same creams, oils, and scrubs under different labels. “You can get any product you want. If you take Wow [Skin Science] and Mamaearth, it’d be the same formulation with 2% of one raw material being added or 2% of another being reduced with a change in fragrance or colour. That’s all,” a former executive with one such manufacturer told us.

Factories-for-hire can make as few as 1,000 units of a product. Put differently, they have not merely lowered the entry barriers for a new cosmetics brand; they have blown them to smithereens.

Sudeshna and Seema spoke with a range of manufacturers, brands, and their distributors to answer this question: how easy is it to be a beauty brand in India?

Good Morning Ishan,

Have you ever played The Boat is Sinking? It’s a children’s game played in groups. A leader shouts, “the boat is sinking; group yourselves into (insert random number here)!”

Then, everyone would have to huddle together in groups of that number. Whoever fails to find a group is out of the game.

Something similar is happening in Vietnam. The nation’s biggest internet conglomerate, VNG, is trying to stop its own boat from sinking in a similar way: by looking for people’s support.

Last month, it was forced to enter a local small-scale market called UPCoM, where loss-making companies can go public and sell shares without listing on an exchange. It offered around 36 million shares, which could help it stay afloat by maintaining capital flow.

After retreating into UPCoM, VNG announced that it will still make good on its promise of a US IPO, though on a delayed schedule. VNG had announced plans for a Nasdaq listing in 2017.

But if VNG is Vietnam’s biggest internet conglomerate, why did it have to resort to UPCoM? And why is the loss-making group still trying to save face by reminding the public about its Nasdaq IPO plans?

Van Anh uncovers the hole that’s sinking VNG’s boat: an enduring decline in gaming, its core business.

Evolving preferences are turning gamers away from VNG’s model, and to make matters worse, Vietnam’s gaming industry also has regulatory challenges to face.

Can VNG stop its boat from sinking? Maybe, if it can form the right group of investors, revenue sources, and a new business structure.

Good Morning Ishan,
 
Last Sunday, right about this time, I was on a bridge on the Zambezi river between the borders of Zimbabwe and Zambia. I was trying—a little too hard—to catch a glimpse of hippopotamuses swimming just before the water sharply descends 354 feet. The vapour rising up like smoke from the ground from one of the largest waterfalls in the world was beautiful, but unfortunately, I couldn't see any hippos. 

In fact, I saw the Victoria Falls, a rainforest, sunsets, rainbows and even moonbows made from moonlight, but no animals (except a lone elephant). However, that did not stop me from trying to spot animals everywhere since I returned to India. In shadows, in the shape of the cushions on the sofa and even in the stories we published last week.

On Monday, Sumanth argued that enterprise messaging startup Slack, which goes public next month, could shape reshape the fundamentals of VC economics and startup playbooks. To me, it is the hippopotamus this week. It may appear calm herbivore resting in the water but is one of the more unpredictable species—an extreme outlier among the likes of Google, Facebook and the recently IPO-ed Uber. 

Arundhati on Tuesday wrote on how Razorpay is gearing to be a lion, the king or the market leader of payment gateways. For a five-year-old startup in any sector, annual growth of 450% would be considered extraordinary. But payments is a bit of an anomaly, she writes. The jungle, as it were, is constantly changing, making leadership a constant challenge, and Razorpay is reinventing itself as a neo bank to cope.

In the middle of the week, Harveen's story of over two dozen video streaming services licensing their original content to telecom companies and other aggregators reminded me of zebras. They are flashier than their domesticated cousins—horses and donkeys—but essentially herd animals. Read the story to find out how entertainment companies of all hues rushed into streaming, afraid to miss out, business models be damned.

On Thursday, Pradip dove into the question—why are graduates from Indian business schools becoming less employable? He spoke to multiple professors and experts to answer the question that affects all potential students of MBA, business schools and the corporations that employ them. Think giraffes spending a lot of their time standing and ruminating to solve a complex problem. Read the story here and share, it's free. Something to chew on this weekend. 

The working week ended with our contributor Sampad Swain describing what he calls gig entrepreneurs, hidden within India's broader gig economy. Not cab drivers and delivery executives, but tech-savvy, highly skilled freelancers. A developed, thriving freelancer ecosystem is a world of cats that like to work on their own. 

The weekend began with another contributor piece on a new market shaping for period care. Just like a crocodile lying low and submerged in marshes, a market for managing PMS has been there forever—and companies are now going after it. Prachi Sibal takes the reader into the world of menstrual products focused on health and comfort. This one's free too.

I may not have witnessed much in the way of wildlife during my Zimbabwean vacation, but it didn't stop me from buying two miniature wooden souvenir meerkats for our two desk editors—Ranjan and Durga, a horned rhinoceros for Harveen and a tortoise for myself. But that is a story for another time.  

Which animal do you associate with? Whichever one it is, I hope you get to go on a vacation and build wonderful memories this summer. For now, have a great Sunday.

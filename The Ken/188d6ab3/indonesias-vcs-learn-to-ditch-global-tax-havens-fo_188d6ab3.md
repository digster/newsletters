Good Morning Ishan,
Half-jokingly, one of my finance professors told the class that the whole field came into existence because humans learned to ask one question: what’s in it for me?
Ideally, investors and investees should both like the potential answers to this question before inking any agreements.
Unlike more rigid, traditional VC structures with long funding cycles, Indonesian VC funds function more like a mutual fund scheme, where investors can pledge or redeem their investments quarterly. This softer, more flexible model attracts curious but cautious players that only want to dip a toe in the VC pool.
Assets managed by VCs in Indonesia hit US$1.6 billion at the end of 2022, a 5% increase over the last year’s corresponding quarter, according to figures from the Association of Indonesian Venture Capital and Startups (Amvesindo).
But after interacting with Indonesia’s leading venture capitalists, Nadine discovered that locally incorporated funds are struggling due to several handicaps.
For one, Indonesia-domiciled VC funds tend to wait longer for approvals and are at a disadvantage compared to their counterparts based in tax havens such as the Caymans, the British Virgin Islands, or Singapore.
At a roundtable discussion, Amvesindo Chairman Eddi Danusaputro and other leading venture capitalists spoke about the model’s flaws and what’s being done to overcome them.
Despite these shortcomings, Indonesia badly needs more locally incorporated funds as they can boost the local startup ecosystem, the VC executives believe.

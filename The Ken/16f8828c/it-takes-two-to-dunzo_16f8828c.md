A little less than two years ago, shortly after joining The Ken, my colleagues in Bengaluru introduced me to a new verb—to Dunzo. The on-demand delivery service had become the latest addition to the lexicon of everyone I knew in the city. Even my mother—a lady who embraces tech as readily as a politician does accountability—was an avid user.
Need smokes? Forgot keys? Buying flowers? "Dunzo it". The only limit to its use cases was imagination. In a city plagued with traffic jams and desperate for convenience, it wasn’t hard to see why Dunzo took off.
In many ways, 2018 was a banner year for the company:
1) Google Inc chose Dunzo for its first investment in India.
2) Transactions on the platform grew 15X to 1 million a month by November.
3) It expanded from Bengaluru into other major cities like Delhi, Chennai, and had Mumbai in its sights.
4) And, in an interview with us, Kabeer Biswas, Dunzo’s CEO, made it clear that the company was actively working towards profitability at the delivery level.
But if 2018 was about optimism, 2019 was a reminder of just why, as Biswas told us in the past, close to 90 VCs opted against backing Dunzo. Losses for the year ended March 2019 stood just shy of Rs 169 crore. Revenue, meanwhile, was Rs 3.5 crore. The year also saw the company scale back in multiple cities, while food delivery giant Swiggy finally decided to gatecrash Dunzo’s party as well.
For a business that has run out of cash before, Kabeer Biswas admits to Abinaya in today’s story that he needs to prove that Dunzo can, indeed, be a profitable business. He’s given himself a deadline as well—6 months to prove profitability in one city. How?

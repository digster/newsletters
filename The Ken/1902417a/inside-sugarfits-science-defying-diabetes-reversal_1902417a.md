Good Morning Ishan,
Perhaps you’ve seen firsthand what it’s like to live with diabetes. The blood glucose checks, being cautious about what’s eaten, and always having an emergency kit ready—these become constants. Diabetes doesn’t stand in the way of living a full life, but many activities become coloured by it.
If you’re a diabetic and someone were to come along and say they could reverse your diabetes, that would sound like an enticing proposition, one that might draw you in to learn more about what they offer.
That’s the case of Sugar.fit, a healthtech company backed by investors like B Capital, Massmutual Ventures, and the fitness unicorn Cult.fit (formerly Cure.fit). Here’s what the company presented as outcomes during a webinar held earlier this month, if those in attendance were to buy into its programme:
- “All your medicines and all your insulins will be completely stopped. Zero medicines, zero insulin.”
- “Your blood sugar level will be normal.”
- “You follow the simple things—throughout your life, you will not need even one medicine, even one consult, even one diagnostic… That’ll be like freedom from diabetes forever.”
And all it costs is Rs 17,990, or a little over US$200. (The regular price is Rs 29,990.)
Those stated outcomes are, of course, too good to be true. Sugar.fit’s 25,000+ members—a figure shared during the webinar—are put on a diabetes management regimen by the company. The promised miracle is beyond reach.
For today’s story, Shivani sifted through the claims made by the company and spoke with Sugar.fit employees, customers, and other healthtech founders to show you how deep the deception goes. Don’t miss the [article](https://the-ken.com/story/inside-sugar-fits-science-defying-diabetes-reversal-plan/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter).
It was another day in May, with yet another piece of news about software that could converse with us—or at least everyone in China. A range of news outlets published reports about “Chat Xi-PT”, supposedly a chatbot being trained on the thoughts of Chairman Xi Jinping.
It’s the “latest answer to OpenAI”, according to the [Financial Times](https://www.ft.com/content/43378c6e-664b-4885-a255-31325d632ee9), and while the chatbot is currently used at a research centre, “it may be released for wider use”. [The Wall Street Journal](https://www.wsj.com/tech/ai/chat-xi-pt-chinas-chatbot-makes-sure-its-a-good-comrade-bdcf575c), on the other hand, said: “It is unclear whether it will be made public”. Plenty more media organisations jumped on the news, including those in India.
If those and other reports are to be believed, internet users in China could soon be talking to Cyber Xi. That sent me down a rabbit hole for the next two weeks. I wanted to find out what this could look like, and how “core socialist values” could be implemented in a large language model.
There are a few layers to this saga, but let’s skip ahead: Chat Xi-PT doesn’t exist, at least not in the ways nearly all English-language media ran news about it. (I wasn’t the first to spot this. Manya Koetse, who tracks trends and digital culture in China’s corner of the internet, wrote about it on her website, [What’s on Weibo](https://www.whatsonweibo.com/about-the-ai-chatbot-based-on-xi-jinping-story/).)
There are plenty of chatbots that are indeed Chinese-language alternatives to ChatGPT, but this one was meant for specialised researchers. That isn’t as catchy as imagining Cyber Xi telling you not to jaywalk or to prepare for an invasion of Taiwan. The misreported Chat Xi-PT is a manifestation of the general anxiety surrounding the proliferation of AI, expressed through an observation of a corner of the internet that exists in relative isolation. After all, what bigger nightmare is there than for Big Brother to take over the entirety of your information flow?

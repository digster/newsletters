Good Morning Ishan,
In September, the top deck of the world’s richest shuffled, if only briefly. Larry Ellison took the crown from Elon Musk, with a single-day $240 billion surge in Oracle’s market capitalisation catapulting its founder and largest shareholder to the top spot. Driving the surge was Oracle’s mega deal with OpenAI to build new data centres in the US.
Data centres are now the indispensable infrastructure powering the seemingly unstoppable AI flywheel. Countries are scrambling to get them going. India, too, is on overdrive, even if it is playing a long game of catch-up with the US and China.
In just the past few weeks, there has been big action. The Ken has learnt that India’s biggest data centre operator, NTT Data, has cut a deal with Oracle to build more of these powerhouses. At the policy level, the government has proposed a 20-year tax holiday for those building at massive scale—though it is still under discussion, not yet official.
Earlier this year, Reliance Industries and its chairman, Mukesh Ambani, Asia’s richest man, had already announced plans to build the world’s largest data centre in their Jamnagar stronghold in Gujarat. Many smaller players are also now joining the race. States across the country are vying with each other to grab a piece of the pie. Meanwhile, the country’s long-delayed digital personal data protection rules, expected to drop soon, could provide a much-needed fillip to data centre providers—at least on paper.
Trouble is, what’s on paper can be very different from what actually plays out on the ground. As Inderpal writes in his deep dive today, a tangle of approvals, high costs and risks (will the customers come?), and regulatory curveballs could leave many, including the big guns, stranded. Smaller players are especially vulnerable. For an industry that thinks in decades, the cost of delay could be counted in trillions.

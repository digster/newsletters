How India's telcos solved a 1% problem
“Is it a 1% problem?”
 
If you are a product manager, you probably know this question. It’s one of the first things you get asked when you propose a solution to a problem. Everyone is going to ask you this question, from your manager to your developers.
 
The philosophy of the question has two parts:
 
  • Is the problem something that less than 1% of your users face?
  • Is your solution going to inconvenience the remaining 99%?
 
If you are a product manager, you’ll be surrounded by 1% problems. If you have a ride-hailing app, 1% of your customers enter the wrong cab by mistake. Another 1% of your drivers probably use multiple SIM cards and create fake trips to claim incentives. Maybe 1% of your customers insist you add Diners Club as a payment method.
 
One way to look at it would be to say that if the answer to both questions are yes, then it’s worth ignoring. “Don’t solve a 1% problem” is what you’ll hear in many product-driven organisations. This is excellent advice, but it’s a little more nuanced than that.
 
The tricky part is to figure out which 1% problems to solve and which ones to ignore. You might decide that the wrong cab problem, even though it’s a 1% problem, is worth solving. And so every rider must announce a four-digit pin to the driver to start a trip. To solve a 1% problem, you decide to inconvenience the 99%.
 
Things get more complex, because what makes news are 1% problems. Russia using Facebook to target disinformation to voters in the United States is a 1% problem. An e-commerce shipment that has a brick instead of a mobile phone is a 1% problem.
 
Picking 1% problems is more art than science. And has consequences. You may prevent something bad for 1%, but you add something to the other 99%.
 
Today’s edition is about a 1% problem that the Indian telecom industry faced, and what it did to solve it.
Everybody hates telemarketing messages
 
Our story begins about a decade ago, back in August 2010, when a gentleman, right in the middle of an important meeting, got a call from a telemarketer offering him a loan of Rs 2 lakh or about $2,600. He picked up the call, listened, and said  “No, no. Not now. I am in a meeting.”
 
That gentleman was Pranab Mukherjee, the union finance minister of India at the time. He was in a meeting with the opposition party leaders about a logjam in the Parliament.
 
The story goes that this was where the crackdown against telemarketers began. The telecom minister, A Raja, wrote a note to his secretary, and asked him to convene a meeting and take steps to stop these calls.
 
Everybody hates telemarketing messages and calls. And nobody was spared. Not even the chairman of the Telecom Regulatory Authority of India (Trai), J.S. Sharma.
"I don't pick up calls that originate from telemarketers. But I have no control over spam messages. I generally receive three to four spam messages from telemarketers in a day," said the helpless chairman of Trai, which is meant to check unsolicited commercial calls by telemarketers. The regulator has failed to put in place an effective filtering mechanism for unsolicited calls and messages. Trai has made several attempts to curb unsolicited commercial calls, including creating a 'Do Not Call Registry', but without much success. Pesky voice calls have reduced to some extent, but SMSs have increased manifold.
 
Trai is even said to be under pressure from telecom lobbies to delay the implementation of filters indefinitely. The regulator has already deferred the implementation of the filtering mechanism four times, the last instance being the March 21 deadline. "I cannot give you any specific deadline for an effective filtering mechanism for unsolicited calls and messages. But DoT (the department of telecommunications) has agreed to all our objections and shortly we will see it rolling out," claimed Sarma.
 
Telecom Regulator head J.S Sarma gets a taste of spam, Business Today
That was back in May 2011.
 
Since then, in India, the problem of telemarketing calls and SMSs has remained in the public discourse. Courts have given rulings against telcos for violating guidelines. Trai has upped the fines.
 
Did it work?
 
Hell no. In fact, last year, one study found that a staggering 96% of Indians receive unwanted text messages every day.
 
But why?
 
Well, let’s say you receive a text message from a spam telemarketer. Say you decide to report it. The process is cumbersome. You need to fill out a lot of details. You send it. What happens next is...nothing.
 
There’s an extensive article on Moneylife that explains what happens when you report a telemarketer. Read and weep.
 
Essentially, Trai, like most other entities in India had an enforcement problem, which it attempted to cover up using a penalty system.
 
Then, in November last year, after several attempts, Trai finally came up with a solution.
It was...blockchain.
 
No, I am serious.
The 1% Solution
 
In 2018, Trai released a draft proposal on how blockchain could be used to counter telemarketing. It called it a distributed ledger technology (DLT).
 
Then, by the end of 2019, it started firming things up.
TRAI Secretary SK Gupta had noted earlier that a subscriber may have given consent for a service but that consent is liable to be misused. Under the new system, the mobile subscriber will be able to revoke the consent given to entities whenever he or she desires through TRAI app or some other mechanism that will be provided under the regulation.
 
The enterprise will also need to have the SMS templates approved by the telemarketers. Once the DLT platform is live, all the enterprises will need to register and procure unique sender IDs with telemarketers registered for the new platform.

The process of acquiring sender IDs will be similar to registering a domain. Once an ID is registered, it will be blocked across all operators and can be used only by the registrant.
Trai could launch blockchain-based DLT platform to counter SMS spam by the end of Nov
All of this sounds good, doesn’t it?
 
One person who seemed quite impressed by it was Vijay Shekhar Sharma*, founder and CEO of Paytm, India’s largest fintech company.
 
There’s a very good reason Paytm wanted this to happen. Over the last decade, SMSs started assuming greater importance, particularly for fintech. The mobile revolution was a godsend for fintech because it solved two problems simultaneously—identity and authentication.
 
Your mobile number was the user ID, and the OTP sent to that number via SMS was the authentication of the transaction. More spam SMSs meant customers stopped looking at it, or ignoring it, or worse, getting defrauded by it. Paytm had strategic motivations to want this problem to be solved, and if blockchain meant that it made its problems of fraud go away, then so be it.
 
Of course, it also meant that telcos had to be ones to do it.
 
That’s where the DLT came in.
 
The plan was simple. After creating a DLT for India’s telcos, Trai even issued guidelines on the rollout over ten phases. In the first phase, the telemarketer would have to get themselves registered and submit documentation. One website listed these documents as below:
The telemarketer would also have to pay Rs 5,900 ($78) as a one-time registration fee. In subsequent phases, it would have to register its headers, and its templates. After this, the telemarketer would have to register themselves on the DLT itself, across all the telcos.
 
The deadline for doing this elapsed two weeks ago.
 
That’s when the new regime kicked in.
"Aap broker hai ya party?"
 
I’ll give you a hint on what happened next. Just look at the DLT portals for Airtel, Vodafone Idea and BSNL, three of the four major telco operators in India.

Are you getting it? Are you getting what’s going on?
 
The DLT for these three companies are made by the same company. A small, Hyderabad-based company called Tanla.
 
So what happened next? Well...
The Telecom Regulatory Authority of India’s (Trai) ambitious plan to deploy the world’s largest blockchain use-case in the telecom sector to curb the menace of spam messages faces a legal hurdle, following protests by telemarketers.
 
One such telemarketer, Venets Media, has filed a petition in the Delhi High Court, alleging that Trai’s move is "detrimental to free, fair commerce & competition, regulatory ethics, and also the arm’s length principle." It added that software company Tanla Solutions – an implementing partner of the distributed ledger technology (DLT) for Bharti Airtel, Vodafone Idea and state-run Bharat Sanchar Nigam Ltd. - is itself a telemarketer, thus raising conflict of interest issues.
Trai's blockchain solution to tackle pesky calls, messages runs into legal hurdle, ET Telecom
There's more...
In response to ET’s query, Hyderabad-based Tanla said it was a technology company and not a telemarketer. “Tanla has developed a platform in compliance with TCCCPR 2018 and offered this platform to mobile operators in India, which is managed by the mobile operators. Hence, Tanla is a technology provider and not a telemarketer.”
 
But Arun Adlakha, advocate for Venets Media, told ET that in 2018, Tanla acquired Karix Mobile, a retail telemarketing company which provides A2P marketing solutions including bulk SMS. “Karix is a wholly-owned subsidiary of Tanla and together, they control 40% volume of the entire SMS industry. This is what has put the entire industry against Tanla,”

You might say, “Hey, but this is a blockchain. The whole point of a blockchain is decentralised power.”
 
Nope, not in the DLT.
 
Because DLT is a private blockchain. Not a public ledger.
 
What this means is that the entities who own and operate the ledger have complete visibility (and control) into what goes in and what’s going on. Tanla, by virtue of its position, could theoretically keep an eye on who is using what, and not just at a telemarketer level, but at a client level.
 
Law firm Ikigai Law did a study of the regulations back in 2018. And noted the following problem with the proposed DLT.
The fundamental intent behind adopting DLT networks is to keep data cryptographically secure with no centralized point of control. However, in a private DLT network, the central authority (here, the access provider) acts as a gatekeeper to the ledger itself, controlling who goes in and out of the ledger and thus, who mans the ledger nodes. This undermines that very rationale of adopting DLT in the first place.
Blockchain as a regulatory tool - "TRAI-ing" too hard?, Ikigai Law
Say you are a SaaS (software-as-a-service) company. You have several clients. You send SMSs to users on behalf of those clients. Important messages. What happens on the DLT? Does the end-user have to opt-in for every single service? Every year? Does the end-user even know? Or care? Does the actual business have to register on the blockchain? Because that’s what the regulation seems to say.
 
In fact, over the last week or so, chances are you aren’t receiving SMSs from places you should be receiving. I know a few people who aren’t receiving transactional messages like OTPs. An executive at a prominent e-commerce company and another executive who runs a SaaS company have both independently told me the same thing.
 
I’ll say it.
 
This is a 1% problem.
 
Look, I get why Paytm wants to solve this problem. In fact, last month, it got fed up and dragged the telcos to court seeking Rs 100 crore ($13 million) in damages from fraud that arose due to spam and phishing messages. In its petition, which runs to hundreds of pages, Paytm chronicles some instances of fraud that arose on its platform due to this.
 
This may be a big problem for Paytm, but if you take the financial ecosystem as a whole, the volume of this fraud is minuscule.
 
In the year 2018-19, the Reserve Bank of India, in a report, estimated the total bank frauds that year as Rs 71,543 crore ($9.4 billion). The biggest chunk of this was loans issued by public and private sector banks.
 
What was the share of frauds relating to cards, internet banking and deposits?
 
A little more than Rs 200 crore ($26 million).
 
Or, 0.3%.
 
For perspective, the entire revenue of all the major telcos put together in the same financial year was Rs. 2.1 lakh crore ($27.6 billion).
 
This isn’t a 1% problem.
 
It’s more like a 0.1% problem for telcos.
 
Tell me again why we need to solve for spam SMS using blockchain?
 
Here’s a radical thought. Why don’t we look at SMSs the way we look at emails? We treat spam emails like how they need to be treated. Ignored. Trashed. Filtered. We don’t create crippling regulations using complex technology and gatekeepers to decide who can send emails to whom. Like email, SMS is an open protocol. And right now, you don’t need to register yourself on a blockchain to send an email to a list.
 
Here’s a possible solution. Forget telcos for a moment. Fraud happens through multiple methods. SMS happens to be the dominant one today. It could be something else tomorrow. Maybe it’ll be WhatsApp. Or Instagram. Or OLX ads.
 
Instead, why not solve this through financial regulation? Say through a simple regulation by the RBI called zero customer liability. If you are a consumer and a transaction was made that you didn’t authorise, you don’t lose any money. Period. The liability rests with the banks to solve the problem.
 
This is what the United States has. Broadly. The consumer is protected.
The zero liability policy applies regardless of how the fraudulent transaction was conducted; the customer will not be responsible for unauthorized transactions made in person, by phone, online, or through a mobile application.
In India, however, we have a limited protection against such liability, and both the bank and the customer share the liability depending on the scenario.
 
That is not a 1% problem.

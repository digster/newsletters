Good Morning Ishan,
“For 25 years, my father shipped T-shirts to the same US buyer, Walmart…He built our factory for that one relationship.”
That’s a second-generation exporter in Tiruppur. He is now deviating from the norm to build his career on new relationships in other geographies. “It’s harder—but safer.”
Even before Donald Trump’s 50% tariff in late August came as a big blow, India’s apparel industry was in a bit of a limbo—comfortable supplying to decades-old destinations and set in their way of manufacturing.
Unsurprisingly, US buyers like Walmart and Target were shifting to cheaper hubs like Vietnam and Bangladesh, where garment workers earn about Rs 9,000 a month versus India’s Rs 16,500, mostly paid on a piece-rate basis. Indian garment exports have stalled at $15 billion over the past few years.
But Trump has delivered the drug the world’s seventh-largest knitwear-exporting nation needed. At Tiruppur, which produces more than half of India’s total knitwear exports, the shift in the exporters’ customers, supply chain, product mix, and above all, attitude is visible.
Even on the shop floor, the contrast between Tiruppur’s two main markets, US and Europe, is striking. From the fabric to the embroidery.
Valli, who visited Tiruppur and spoke to over 60 workers and exporters, writes there’s even a redistribution of labour underway in this cluster. Similar recalibrations are unfolding in apparel hubs of Mumbai and Kerala.
Can you imagine Trump wearing a white tee that says in red “Make Tiruppur Great Again”?

WeWork popularised the term “adjusted Ebitda” about four years ago. (The cash-guzzling startup argued it was a useful metric of performance when you ignore the cash burned on expansion.)

Byju’s must be credited with making “revenue recognition” a common term. And not just among business journalists.

The edtech’s auditor of many years, Deloitte, quit in June over revenue recognition disagreements, among other things.

Now, the jewel acquisition in its crown (heh) is losing its sheen under this creative accounting.

Byju’s paid a hefty $950 million to acquire the 33-year-old test-prep chain Aakash in April 2021. So it was natural that the edtech would want to make the most of Aakash—use its existing revenue/ profits to improve its top line and bottom line, as well as sell its respected brand of offline courses.

Arundhati and Alifiya report that Byju’s’ brand of aggressive selling first raised red flags among Aakash’s sales and franchise partners, then it sparked conflicts between the teams and, finally, sounded revenue-recognition alarms.

Byju’s sales force no longer sells Aakash’s offline courses, and the integration of the customer relationship management systems between the two companies has been put on hold.

The sales company that is Byju’s, deployed 3,000 sales executives to drive Aakash+Byju’s products. This is twice the number of salespersons Aakash had across its 300 branches. A relentless sales culture doesn’t imply mis-selling, but in Byju’s case, it does.

Earlier this month, WeWork said it faced “substantial doubt” about its ability to continue as a going concern. Creative accounting can’t rescue it.

For Byju’s, its revenue recognition tactics have soured its relationship with Aakash, and the merger is in limbo.

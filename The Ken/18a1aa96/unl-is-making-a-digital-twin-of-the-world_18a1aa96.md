Slow-inspiring newness
A little over two months ago in Singapore, over cups of steaming hot coffee, I asked the venture capitalist sitting next to me about the city-state’s deep tech segment.
You see, among the founders and technical chiefs I’ve interviewed for [Present/Future](https://the-ken.com/sea/present-future/stories/), few were born and bred in Singapore. Instead, they were from India, the United Kingdom, Italy, or Australia. And their team members were often from beyond Southeast Asia too.
It’s no secret that Singapore’s tech sector is shored up by entrepreneurs and innovators from all over the world. It’s cliché to call it a melting pot, but it’s precisely this convergence of people with the shared goal of building something new that makes the grassroots of its tech sector exciting—with corners where big ideas still have space to be shaped, and where constant experimentation is still a priority.
There are fantastic labs where connections are made. The National University of Singapore, for example, is home to the research facilities where scientists and engineers hone their craft, discover new technical developments, meet collaborators, and study under experienced principal investigators. It’s where some of the entrepreneurs featured in Present/Future realised that they could build a business out of the scientific work they’d been involved in.
So I asked the VC whether there was a drive to cultivate more local technical talent. How was Singapore encouraging kids—young kids—to take STEM subjects more seriously?
As it turns out, they told me they had been tossing such ideas around with someone who was part of the Singapore government—informally, I presume—and that they had landed on a popular science-fiction franchise. The two seemingly agreed that depictions of advanced technology might spark the curiosity needed to embark on the long, grinding journey of creating something new.
I think the point here is that deep tech depends on scientists and engineers who refuse to take the short, convenient path of cranking out hotfixes. It requires people who view the world a little differently, in ways that might seem foreign at first.
Rethinking 3-Space
Imagine seeing the world in little interconnected cubes, packed tightly and uniformly. I’m not referring to the game Minecraft. Instead, it’s how a startup called UNL is spatially dividing the world.
Mihaela Georgieva, the COO and co-founder of UNL, told me that by voxelating the world, her startup is developing what the team calls an “internet of places.” Defining that concept gets tricky, but think of it as a web that indexes the physical world, one cube at a time.
Since these cubes are stacked in 3-space, a path could be mapped through the air (such as for drone deliveries) or within buildings (finding the exact location of someone’s corner office, for instance).
|
|
If this mapping takes place at a global scale—and the rhetoric of UNL does suggest that they intend to attempt that—then there’s a universal system for designating any location reachable by a human being. More than that, this also provides “addresses” to people who previously didn’t have one, such as some folks who were in deep rural areas, opening up the possibility of registering for services like banking, the right to exercise their votes in elections, and to receive social welfare.
More interestingly, this creates a digital twin of our physical space. It isn’t clear how it will be continually updated, such as when new skyscrapers go up, but this does provide a foundation for other businesses to build new or improved services. It’s a technological stack that fits emerging markets particularly well, provided that UNL’s maps are refreshed rapidly.
If nothing else, UNL shows us what can be done when we view our surroundings through a new lens. I’ll leave that with you until our next newsletter launches.

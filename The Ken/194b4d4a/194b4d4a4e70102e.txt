Fintechs are trying to save India’s Digital Rupee. Trump may have just killed it. Two years on, India’s CBDC is struggling to find relevance

India’s central bank digital currency, or CBDC, looks dead in the water.

The Digital Rupee (e₹) launched in December 2022. It is legal tender: e₹1 is functionally the same as Rs 1 in physical cash. The only difference is that it exists entirely in electronic form.

But disagreements within the Reserve Bank of India (RBI) and shifting goals for use cases of the Digital Rupee have given the project the flavour of a soap opera, even while there’s dizzying activity in the private sector to develop ways to use it.

On Tuesday, Cred rolled out the beta version of its CBDC wallet. Its functions are as you’d expect: peer-to-peer transfers and merchant payments, as well as UPI QR code scans, with all transactions performed using the Digital Rupee. It’s being talked up https://x.com/kunalb11/status/1884128138524803467 as the next big thing.

Mobikwik joined the party and launched its own CBDC wallet in collaboration with Yes Bank.

More will come. The Ken’s sources say there are at least six other CBDC projects under development. Phonepe and Flipkart Labs, for instance, are reportedly preparing to offer their own transaction features involving the Digital Rupee.

Flipkart, in particular, is working on a blockchain-based CBDC project with a major private bank and Polygon, a blockchain scaling solution provider. Such a development could eliminate fees for users in some situations. A person familiar with the development at Flipkart explained it like this: let’s say you’re a shopper on the platform and have earned Supercoins. “Spending” those Supercoins involves using a third party like Razorpay to convert them into cash, a process that requires a fee. 

But if the Supercoins are backed by the Digital Rupee, then “spending” those reward points means transferring real currency directly to the merchant, bypassing intermediaries. A bonus is that all transactions are recorded on-chain, making those records easy to query.

There’s plenty more development involving major banks. Xaults Technologies, which won the RBI’s “Harbinger – Innovation for Transformation” hackathon in 2024, is collaborating with the Bank of Baroda and SBI Payments, a subsidiary of State Bank of India, on two projects to introduce CBDC-based loyalty programmes for small merchants.

In the public sector, India’s CBDC has been used in transfers https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/news/india/odisha-launches-subhadra-yojana-first-direct-transfer-scheme-using-digital-rupee/articleshow/113450351.cms?from=mdr for Subhadra Yojana, a welfare scheme for women launched by the Odisha state government. Beneficiaries receive Rs 10,000 annually; last year’s payments were made using the Digital Rupee. Meanwhile, the Project on Climate Resilient Agriculture (PoCRA), a collaborative effort involving the Government of Maharashtra and the World Bank, is mulling the possibility of running pilot projects with CBDC use cases.

Yet none of these come close to the mass adoption that the Digital Rupee was designed for. In fact, usage figures tell a sobering story.

The Digital Rupee hit a milestone in December 2023 with 1 million daily transactions. Six months later, however, that number had tapered to just 100,000.

This stark decline highlights a key issue: Indians seem to prefer UPI, which is already well-established, free to use, and extremely convenient.

Even with UPI’s integration of the Digital Rupee, few are opting to transact using the CBDC.

It doesn’t help that Donald Trump banned CBDCs in the United States, calling them a threat to privacy and financial stability. Given that India has followed the US’s lead regarding CBDCs and cryptocurrency regulations in the past, in turn formulating the goal to replace crypto transactions with the Digital Rupee, the latest developments in the US run counter to the RBI’s aims to make e₹ a leading cross-border payment instrument.

This is a significant blow to India’s ambitions for the Digital Rupee. Trump said he would impose 100% tariffs on BRICS nations if they develop a rival to the US dollar—a function of CBDCs like the Digital Rupee when they are used to settle international payments. This threat from the White House makes it difficult to convince any of India’s trade partners to come onboard and use the Digital Rupee. Right now, the CBDC appears to be adding needless complexity.

“It’s not easy. Do you know how many years it took to enable cross-border UPI transactions with Singapore? Over four years.”

A former RBI executive director

Resistance at home is strong too, according to the same former executive director of the RBI. When the Digital Rupee launched, the central bank’s personnel were divided over its purpose and potential. Some questioned whether it was even necessary, given that existing systems like UPI already functioned well. 

A member of the CBDC expert committee revealed to The Ken that the group hasn’t held a meeting in nine months, indicating that the Digital Rupee is no longer a priority within India’s central bank.

One of the main challenges with the digital rupee is the trade-off between privacy and traceability. The RBI promotes it as a digital equivalent of cash, but the fact remains that online transactions are traceable, unlike physical cash. This lack of privacy https://www.thehindu.com/incoming/former-rbi-deputy-governor-flags-potential-privacy-issues-for-cbdcs/article67954063.ece undermines one of the key advantages of cash and raises questions about the appeal of CBDCs. And even if startups like Xaults develop solutions that give CBDC users a higher degree of privacy, there still isn’t a major driver for demand.

The Digital Rupee was wrapped in the promise to be the future of payments in India—and for cross-border transactions involving the country. For now, it appears to be a solution in search of a problem, chock-full of contradictions https://the-ken.com/story/can-indias-digital-currency-still-be-another-upi/ and unfulfilled purpose https://the-ken.com/kaching/the-curious-case-of-cbdc-r-as-a-pastime/.

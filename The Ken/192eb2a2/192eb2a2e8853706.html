Swiggy braces itself to be the middle child. The next 12 months are going to be testing ones for Swiggy, with several unknowns
By Praveen Gopal Krishnan
10 min read
View in browser

Good Morning Ishan,

You are reading our subscriber-only newsletter The Nutgraf—a weekly commentary on fundamental shifts in business, technology, and finance

By next week, the world will be changed in two ways—the United States will have a new President, and Swiggy will be a public company. 

Good for Swiggy. I suppose going public is the corporate equivalent of a life-event like getting married, or perhaps having a kid. You’re never ready, but you just do it anyway, and hope you’ll figure it out as you go along. I imagine Swiggy’s leaders and executives are going through all sorts of emotions. In less than a week, Swiggy will be a different company, and with much more to live up to—under a more public eye. 

In some ways, this is going to be good for Swiggy? As long-time readers of this newsletter know, I’ve been somewhat critical about Swiggy this year. I’ve argued that it lost ground because it made errors in the way its products interacted and meshed with each other, and because it chose conservatism over aggressiveness. You may disagree with my explanations, but it’s hard to dismiss the underlying facts that led up to it last year, i.e., Swiggy had squandered an early, decisive advantage in not one but two businesses. It conceded a significant lead to Zomato in food delivery, and to Zepto and Blinkit in the 10-minute delivery business. In both, Swiggy was the first-mover, and had entered these verticals before either of its competitors. Others simply came later and out-executed Swiggy. 

So how will Swiggy the private company adjust to going public? I suppose investors will hope that all that scrutiny will make them more disciplined and focused, which is going to make them a better performing company. Historically, this is broadly what has happened when private tech companies go public. For a few quarters, they flounder and flail around, and then slowly, they start figuring things out. 

Perhaps the same will also happen with Swiggy in the long-term, but I believe that the transition is going to be more turbulent than normal. Specifically, the next 12 months are going to be quite testing, because Swiggy is going to go through a version of the middle-child syndrome. 

Everyone knows middle-child syndrome. It’s when you have an elder brother or sister who is an overachiever and a younger one who is carefree and cheerful. The middle child is caught in the middle—they can neither live up to the elder sibling’s accomplishments, nor can they match the younger sibling’s casual successes. Middle children have it hard because they are forever compared to both siblings, and fall short of both. 

After Swiggy goes public, it’s going to be benchmarked against Zomato for a long, long time. And not just Zomato. It’s Zomato plus Blinkit and now District, the company’s new going-out vertical. No matter how you slice and dice this, Swiggy is going to fall short of Zomato for the foreseeable future. Some analysts believe that Swiggy “seems to be four to six quarters behind Zomato in food delivery and quick commerce”. In food delivery, Zomato has 31% more monthly transacting users than Swiggy—a difference it has maintained for nearly two years now. In quick commerce, the difference between Swiggy and Zomato becomes more stark. Blinkit is ahead of Instamart on all key metrics—monthly transacting users, average order value, efficiency, and take-rate. Some projections suggest that by the end of the current financial year, Blinkit will be double the size of Swiggy Instamart in terms of number of orders. 

In the eyes of the public market, Swiggy is going to be Zomato’s younger sibling—for a long, long time. Analysts are going to be comparing the two companies obsessively to make sense of what’s going on. Swiggy’s financial performance will never be its own. It’ll always be relative to Zomato. It’ll be frustrating for Swiggy. They will believe it to be unfair and they will chafe at the comparison. But it’s going to happen. I just hope Swiggy’s leaders are practising their responses in earning calls. 

This is already annoying, but there’s another company that Swiggy is going to be compared to, and that’s the flashier younger sibling—Zepto. 

Zepto is a remarkable company that’s growing rapidly right now in India. It’s a company that can seemingly do no wrong, and is on the wishlist of every major VC in the world. It raised over a billion dollars just this year, but the way it got this capital makes it look like it can raise another billion just as easily. Some analysts have suggested that Zepto has overtaken Instamart in market share, and that Zepto has the momentum and execution behind it. 

Zepto has a distinct advantage over Swiggy. In a recent interview with The Arc, Sriharsha Majety, CEO of Swiggy, was asked about whether Zepto was bigger than Instamart, and this is what he said:

“We have been hearing that we are number three for five to six months now. I have nothing to comment on that. I think you can look at our numbers and maybe Blinkit’s numbers, which are available publicly. It’s a more rational conversation to comment on stuff that is available and audited fully.”

These comments are revealing. Essentially, Majety is saying don’t get distracted by stuff that you are hearing about Zepto because Zomato and (soon) Swiggy are public companies, and have a higher standard of disclosures and audits. They can’t just say anything they want about their business metrics and get away with it. This stuff moves stock prices, and is much more closely regulated. 

Zepto, on the other hand, can afford to be more… loose with how it describes its business performance, both publicly and in private. 

I’ll share an example to illustrate what I mean, and it’s to do with Relish, Zepto’s in-house meat brand. Zepto launched Relish around a year ago, and even by Zepto’s standards, the business appeared to be doing really, really well. In March, some reports claimed that Relish was already doing an annualised revenue run rate (ARR) of Rs 150 crore (US$18 million). By June, Zepto’s co-founder and CEO Aadit Palicha said that Relish was growing rapidly and was close to a Rs 500 crore ARR business. This is a pretty striking growth, even by Zepto’s standards, considering that Licious makes somewhere around Rs 800 crore a year, and has seen growth flatline. Zepto kept talking up these numbers—and projected that Relish would hit Rs 1,000 crore by 2026. The business media lapped it up. Linkedin bros got to work. The narrative for Relish got stronger and stronger. 

And well, here’s the news from earlier this month.

Relish’s monthly sales have been flat in the past three months, per Zepto’s internal sales documents reviewed by Moneycontrol.

The numbers showed that Relish’s monthly sales were in the Rs 12-15 crore range from April to September, or the first six months of the current fiscal year, which translates to an ARR of Rs 144-180 crore, in contrast to recent reports, where Relish was projected as a Rs 500 crore business.

A current ARR of Rs 144-180 crore is close to the revenue run rate it had achieved in March 2024 which means Relish’s sales have more or less been steady in the last two quarters, the documents showed.

Is Zepto’s meat brand Relish on track to become a Rs 500 crore business? What the numbers say, Moneycontrol

Zepto’s explanation is that the ARR number seen by Moneycontrol does not include the sale of eggs, which pushes the numbers much higher. With this, Zepto claims that it’s doing an ARR of Rs 300 crore and expects to cross Rs 500 crore by the end of the year. 

Who can argue with this?

I mean, sure, it’s unconventional, but this is what Zepto can do. It’s a private company, raising private capital, dropping interviews to an entranced business media. If Zepto wants to add eggs to the ARR of Relish, who are we to say they can’t do that? Again, Zepto is hardly the first private startup to define its numbers however it wants to. Others have been playing this game for years before them, and some are doing this even today. 

You can see why Zepto can be really annoying to Swiggy, just like most younger siblings can be.

Swiggy is caught between having to live up to the performance of Zomato and the narrative of Zepto. Both companies have an advantage over Swiggy in two different departments. If I were Swiggy, I wouldn’t want to be here, but I have no choice now. Swiggy has to set itself apart from both these companies, in business and in narrative. That’s not going to be easy, and in many ways, I sympathise with Swiggy’s position.

This, in and of itself, is going to make Swiggy’s position difficult, but I also believe that the next 12 months are going to bring in three distinct developments in quick commerce in India, and their impact is as yet unknown. 

First, we’ll find out the true limit of the quick-commerce market in India. It took us nearly a decade to discover the limits of e-commerce in India. Next year, it’ll be five years since we’ve had quick commerce, and I suspect it’ll become apparent that this is a smaller market than we expected it to be. I’m sceptical of stories that quick commerce will expand to cover the length and breadth of India, and go deep into our cities. I think we’ll find out that quick commerce is viable, but only in specific urban clusters of some of our biggest cities.

Second, the first regulations are going to come for quick commerce. Already, the narrative and concern around this sector is getting louder, and I don’t think the powers that be will ignore it for much longer. I’ve already articulated how this may pan out, and I expect someone to argue that quick commerce is just another way to funnel FDI in retail.

Third… well, Reliance. 

I mean, I don’t really have much to add. Reliance entering something is usually enough to cause a unique form of disruption that’s completely unpredictable. And right now, Reliance is also scaling its quick-commerce offering, in its own way. 

All of these are going to put Swiggy in an interesting, and somewhat unconventional position for the next 12 months, in full view of the public markets. 

As always, it’s unfair on the middle child, but they have to find a way.

Regards,

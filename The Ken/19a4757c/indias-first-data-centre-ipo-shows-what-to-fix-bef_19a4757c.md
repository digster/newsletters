Make India Competitive Again
Mon, 03 Nov 25
What India is doing, will do, and should do—to not just survive but thrive in the chaos unleashed by Trump.
Good Morning Ishan,
There’s a funny thing about India’s AI revolution. It has a lot to do with… err… rotation. Of cooling fans. Of power meters. And of the hype cycle itself.
You see, the recent revolution began with one headline across the board. “India reaches $20B in new cumulative AI investment commitments”. Peel that back and you will find that most of those billions aren’t going to young startups training foundation models in co-working spaces. They are going to those who pour concrete and install chillers.
Google wants to build a $15 billion AI data hub (its largest outside the US). AWS will spend $13 billion soon. Microsoft promises $3 billion. TCS has plans for another $5–7 billion. The government, too, has thrown in $1.2 billion for GPU farms and LLMs.
In other words, most of India’s AI “investment” is going into racks, not reasoning. And that makes Sify Infinit Spaces, the company behind India’s first data-centre IPO, a rather honest mascot for this moment.
Sify doesn’t build chips or train LLMs; it rents out space to those who do. Colocation, as the industry calls it, is essentially high-security, industrial-grade real estate for servers. Clients bring their own machines; Sify supplies the power, cooling, and connectivity.
The company is the data-centre arm of Sify Technologies—the same Sify that gave India dial-up internet in the 1990s. Now, it operates 14 data centres across six cities with 188 megawatts of built IT-power capacity—roughly 15% of India’s total, according to its IPO documents.
But India’s “total” is modest. The US and China measure data-centre capacity in gigawatts, not megawatts. Though India generates nearly a fifth of global data traffic, it holds only 3% of capacity, according to Cushman & Wakefield. The gap between bytes and buildings is vast.
| Source: Kotak Mutual Fund |
Elsewhere, the market has raced ahead. The global colocation industry grew about 10% a year for the past five years and could speed up to 15% as AI workloads soar, says JLL. Capacity worldwide has more than doubled—from 20 GW in 2019 to 42 GW in 2024—and could reach 65 GW by 2027.
The problem is that AI is an energy glutton. Training large models is 3–5X more power-intensive than regular computing. AI already eats up 15% of global data-centre capacity; by 2030, it may demand over a third.
India’s hunger could grow even faster. JLL expects AI-related capacity here to rise at a blazing 80% annually through 2027 and another 40% until 2030. That growth will come mostly from “hyperscalers”—Amazon, Microsoft, Google—who are both some of Sify’s biggest clients and its biggest risk.
Because for all the talk of “cloud” computing, it’s still powered by very physical things: transformers, chillers, diesel generators, and people who fix them. Power, not AI, is the true limiting factor.
This isn’t even unique to India. As The New Yorker recently [reported](https://www.newyorker.com/magazine/2025/11/03/inside-the-data-centers-that-train-ai-and-drain-the-electrical-grid), a single Nvidia GB300 rack can draw as much electricity as a hundred homes. Multiply that across the thousands of racks required to train foundation models, and even America’s power grid is starting to sweat.
Now imagine trying to do that in India, where a stable power supply is still a policy aspiration, not a baseline assumption. Sify knows this better than anyone.
| Source: Kotak Mutual Fund |
It boasts a glittering client list—“three of the top four global hyperscalers, seven of India’s ten biggest banks”, and over 500 names in total. But quantity isn’t the same as diversity. The company’s revenue still leans heavily on a handful of hyperscale and enterprise contracts.
That concentration flips the industry’s supposed advantage—broad demand from streaming, gaming, and enterprise—on its head. In India, hyperscalers already account for more than half of all data-centre demand, and they know it. They can build, rent, or renegotiate at will. The five biggest operators control three-quarters of the market; NTT and STT alone own about half.
So while everyone talks about “AI capacity”, what these operators are really juggling is client concentration and grid reliability. The DRHP’s risk section reads like a municipal power audit: power supply risk, grid reliability risk, cost escalation, tariff risk. The word “power” appears more often than “AI”.
Which brings us back to Sify’s balance sheet that shows just how capital-hungry this business really is.
Sify has been on a building spree. Between FY23 and FY25 it added nearly 95MW of capacity—the fastest expansion among Indian peers—and it is now constructing two more facilities in Chennai and Navi Mumbai. That is where most of the country’s data-centre muscle already sits: Mumbai hosts 52% of capacity, Chennai 11%. Everyone wants to build where the undersea cables land and the electricity mostly stays on.
| Source: Kotak Mutual Fund |
The problem is that everyone builds there. The next 1,000–1,200 MW of planned capacity (2025–27) is already 50–60% pre-leased by hyperscalers. Which is great if demand keeps rising linearly. If not, you get oversupply with very expensive cooling bills.
But there’s another number missing from the bullish slides: how full these sites actually are. Sify emphasises its 188 MW build-out, but its public disclosures don’t show how much of that capacity is occupied or contracted long term. Building is one thing; filling and locking in tenants is another.
But oversupply, paradoxically, is part of the business model. Data-centres cannot be built “just in time”. You buy land, sign long-term power-purchase agreements, pour the concrete, and wait for workloads to arrive. Sify’s IPO proceeds are a map of that cycle.
Of the Rs 3,700 crore issue, about Rs 2,500 crore is fresh capital; Rs 465 crore goes to finishing its second Chennai tower, Rs 860 crore to the Navi Mumbai project, and Rs 600 crore to repay debt. That last bit tells you this isn’t a hot tech play so much as a REIT with routers. You borrow, build, lease, and hope the interest-rate cycle is kind.
The numbers bear this out, too. Sify made about Rs 1,400 crore in FY25, with 44% margins. The profit, however, was only Rs 126 crore. That’s the nature of the beast: every megawatt costs Rs 50–60 crore and pays back over 10 years. A 10–15% ROE business if all goes well, and a very expensive way to generate heat if it doesn’t.
American hyperscalers have found clever ways to manage that heat. Meta, for instance, financed its $27 billion Hyperion data-centre through what Bloomberg columnist Matt Levine [calls a “box”](https://www.bloomberg.com/opinion/newsletters/2025-10-29/put-the-data-center-in-the-box)—a special-purpose vehicle that lets it borrow without appearing to. India’s market is not quite there yet. For now, the borrowing is very much on the books.
Policymakers, meanwhile, are trying to help the private sector keep pace. The RBI’s 2018 mandate requiring all payments data to stay in India, Sebi’s 2023 local-storage rule for financial entities, and the Digital Personal Data Protection Act 2023, which gives the government power to restrict cross-border transfers—all implicitly reward anyone building domestic capacity. The India AI Mission, with Rs 219 billion budgeted under MeitY for FY25, is the same story in policy form: subsidise compute, pray for uptime.
So India has the laws, the demand, and the investment announcements. What it doesn’t yet have is redundancy. Data-centre reliability still depends on diesel backup and city-specific electricity boards. A momentary grid flicker can knock out half your racks; an extended one can cook your margins.
So, what should India do? For starters, treat power infrastructure for data as strategic, not incidental. The same way it treats semiconductors or defence. Incentivise private grid redundancy, streamline data-centre permitting, and subsidise renewable energy tie-ins.
This is precisely the boring work that will determine whether India’s AI decade actually happens. In 2024, global data-centre capacity stood at 42GW. India’s was 1GW. Even if it quadruples by 2030 (as JLL expects), it will still be a fraction of what the US adds every year. The physics are hard to skip.
That’s what makes Sify Infinit’s IPO oddly refreshing. It’s not selling a fantasy of sovereign AI or a proprietary foundation model. It’s selling megawatts. It’s an admission that intelligence may be artificial, but the infrastructure is real.
The prospectus might read like a power-engineering manual, but that’s the point. AI hype has to be translated into HVAC load, cooling tonnage, and interest coverage ratios before it becomes cash flow. In that sense, this is the most truthful “AI play” in India so far.
Ultimately, you can’t train models on marketing copy. You need electricity, steel, and a place for the heat to go.
So, if India’s $20 billion AI boom is the headline, Sify Infinit is probably the footnote. Whose success on the bourses won’t tell us whether India can build better algorithms. It will tell us whether it can keep the lights on long enough to run them.
And that might be the more interesting question.

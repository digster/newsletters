Good Morning Ishan,
Good morning, dear Ishan, I’m back after a brief vacation last weekend. I met a bunch of former classmates and good friends on the eve of the 25th year of our graduation.
I returned to Bengaluru and promptly fell ill, catching one of the various end-of-the-year flus circulating through cities, airports, taxis, and restaurants. Guess my flu vaccine defenses did not account for this variant, or its strength.
The skies are thankfully visible and bright, though still painted in dull shades of white and blue. The sun has been warm and kind. The mornings are chilly—we even had one day this week where it was cold enough to see our breath turn to vapour.
Attendance at The Ken’s office has been uneven. The traffic, tolerable (though this is just me making peace with our city’s terrible traffic). Our post-lunch coffee walks have found two new Indiranagar destinations: the wonderful [Café Plume](https://www.instagram.com/plume.delicacies/?hl=en), a French pâtisserie that serves the most delectable pastries too, and the newly relocated [Infinitea](https://www.instagram.com/infiniteablr/?hl=en), a restaurant I’ve been eating and drinking teas at for over two decades!
Meanwhile, closer to home, Gabru has taken to just about tolerating Bribo accompanying him on walks. The trick, I’ve realised, is to not allow more than five seconds of close contact. That’s when Gabru’s un-neutered defensive-aggression mode gets triggered (most instances of what look like “alpha” aggression in dogs are really fear-based). Bribo seems to understand this much better, so he gives Gabru a nice and submissively friendly berth while accompanying us.
2025 has been a great doggo year for me because of Bribo. He didn’t just make me fall in love with him; but also helped me really start “seeing” all the other street dogs in our neighbourhood. Over the year, I’ve befriended at least seven or eight of them. It started as a way for me to bribe them to avoid getting into a barking match with Gabru (usually the one to initiate most matches), but over time, I have started looking forward to seeing them each day.
Some of you might remember how Bribo’s very name came from the First Principles community. Reader Jude D’Souza was the one who called him “Brindle Boy” (because of the leopard-like patterns in his fur) in an email to me. I turned that into Bri-Bo!
Enough about Bribo and me, what about you? How are you feeling as this chaotic year draws to a close?
Decembers are tough months. There are so many more things drawing down our energies than lifting them up. Sure, there are the holidays, the festivities, the travel, the friends and families, the string lights, the smell of freshly made coffees and cakes.
But then there’s the grey skies, the cold weather, the flus, the smog, the state of the world, the weight of a year gone by, and the sheer magnitude of the future and its vicissitudes.
It feels like a null state.

Are you scrambling to finish your reading goal for this year? Do you even have one?
And, what’s your favourite read of the year? We would love to share your recommendations so it can become someone else’s favourite read for the coming year. [Here’s your cue.](https://theken.typeform.com/FPBooks?typeform-source=the-ken.com)
Personally, I am not a big fan of reading goals that are quantity-focused.
They remind me of something we used to do in school: the student who read the most books (the proof of reading was a written review) was awarded “most-read person of the class” and given a new book as an award. I started off reading what I liked, but quickly realised I was falling behind in numbers—so I started picking thinner books instead.
That bored me.
Maybe once or twice, it’s a fun workaround. But choosing a book just because it’s short (and limited to what’s in your school library) gets old fast. I never did win the most-read award—and I am actually proud of my younger self for not chasing pages when others did. It felt unfair then, but I like how it turned out.
At least back then there was no ChatGPT to write your reviews for you. I wonder if schools still run these kinds of awards, and if they do, how are they judged now? If you are a teacher, parent, or anyone in that world, I would genuinely love to hear your take. You can write to [fp@the-ken.com](mailto:fp@the-ken.com) with your thoughts.
That said, I do like reading goals, the kind that are quality-focused. Lists built around themes, places, voices, or ideas. They give you an anchor, and still leave you room to wander.

Are we past peak Amazon in India? As the American giant tries to recover lost ground in e-commerce, it’s on the lookout for other areas where it can press an advantage

5 min read

Good Morning Ishan,

You are reading the Two by Two newsletter—a weekly commentary on the most important business stories around you

Until I moved to Bengaluru last year from my small hometown in Kerala (or village, if your bar for town is high), Amazon was the place where I shopped online. Delivery was reliable, there was an incredible amount of choice, finding what you wanted was easy, and the odd recommendation that popped up was useful. 

But since my move, I’ve noticed that the frequency of my orders on Amazon has slowed. Not because I buy less frequently online—in reality, the breadth and scale of my online purchases have increased by a scary amount. Bengaluru, however, is the bastion of quick-commerce, and I admit I’m among the many who have fallen for the convenience. 

So when we started recording our Two by Two episode for this week, and hosts Praveen Gopal Krishnan and Rohin Dharmakumar sat down with our guests, I already had a thought or two about the subject at hand. 

Is Amazon really past its peak in India? 

Well, its position certainly isn’t as strong as it once was—it’s lost e-commerce market share to Flipkart, and Meesho is threatening its tier-2 and tier-3 markets quite effectively. 

And quick-commerce… well, quick-commerce is coming for everyone. 

But the conversation we ended up recording ranged far wider, and deeper, than just Amazon’s fortunes in e-commerce. And a large part of that is due to the insight and experience our two guests brought to the table. 

First, there was Professor Vishal Karungulam, who teaches a broad range of subjects at the Indian School of Business (ISB), including software product management, digital innovation, and disruptive technologies. He is also one of those rare academics who’s worked at companies such as Google and Microsoft, where he built cloud and analytics products.

Second was Srikanth Rajagopalan, CEO of Perfios Account Aggregation Services. Srikanth, who was also a guest on the very first episode of Two by Two, has served in a number of roles at major companies, including Nokia, Amex, and most pertinently for us, Amazon. 

Both of them brought to bear unique perspectives on not just Amazon’s position in e-commerce, but the things it can take advantage of elsewhere. 

Can it win with its other verticals? What about AWS? Prime? Payments? What and where are the opportunities it can tap? 

Our first Two by Two episode of 2025 touched on all of this, and more.

AWS is first choice?

Amazon Web Services (AWS) has been Amazon’s fastest growing vertical for the last seven quarters. But while the company hasn’t really pressed this advantage in India fully yet, it does look like it is finally ready to do so. 

Both Srikanth and Prof. Karungulam batted strongly for AWS as Amazon’s next big opportunity.

Prof. Karungulam: Look, one, they can exit the e-commerce business completely in India. That’s one choice they definitely have.

The other choice is, they can go the quick-commerce route, which they seem to be doing evidently.

Or they can even say, ‘I’ll become very selective as far as e-commerce is concerned.’

But I’d double down on my investments in AWS.

[…]

So if you look at the company, the margins in their retail business have always been very low. And the AWS business is just phenomenal from a margins perspective.

So the question to really ask as a shareholder is, would I want them to continue to invest in the e-commerce business? Or do I actually want them to move the money over to AWS?

Now, obviously, that has a lot of downsides. We should talk about what will happen if they get out of it. Will AWS continue to do what it’s doing if they don’t have the retail chain?

But my opinion on this matter is… I don’t think the peak of e-commerce is what we’re going to see.

I think AWS is going to be the big thing for them.

Is payments an option?

The latest numbers say Amazon has lost some of its UPI market share to newer players like Navi and super.money, but that hardly means payments is an insignificant opportunity. 

Especially if you zoom out and employ a slightly wider perspective.

Srikanth: The reality is that about 85–90% is UPI. So of your TAM (Total Addressable Market) of payments, 80% is already zero revenue gain.

So where is the value capture?

The other option is… you’ve got this massive marketplace of millions and millions of buyers and thousands of sellers, where each of them has a very unique financial need.

[…]

Can you remove friction from a purchase decision by making affordability guaranteed? Can you get a seller to commit more inventory to your marketplace than to Flipkart’s or anybody else’s? Can I make a customer’s purchase decision even more trustworthy, because I offer an insurance product on top?

I think if you look at the more popular metric… a vanity metric like UPI market share… there are players there. They have their own business reasons for that.

But if you look at payments or slightly wider financial services as a capability… 

As a business unit, I think Amazon has taken a different route. They don’t necessarily want to win that race to zero. And Amazon being Amazon, it’s damn good at switching out noise and focusing on the signal.

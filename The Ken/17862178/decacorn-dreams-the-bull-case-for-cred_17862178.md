Good Morning Ishan,
Here's a scenario for you:
Imagine you're a VC investor who has been approached for investment by a startup founder. They expect an investment of US$200 million at a nosebleed valuation of US$2 billion. You have the money, but want to know the cold, hard numbers that support this ambitious ask.
The founder looks you dead in the eye and tells you that in the last financial year, the company lost nearly US$50 million. Its revenue for the period was... US$72,000.
Would you invest?
Most people, I'd imagine, would not.
But when Kunal Shah, the founder of credit card payments startup CRED, lay this exact proposition at the feet of investors, they saw opportunity. According to reports, CRED will soon be a unicorn twice over despite being massively loss-making and generating precious little in terms of revenue.
Understandably, people are questioning CRED's business model. What on earth does it do that justifies its valuation? We've even asked these questions in the past.
Today, however, we're doing things a little different. Instead of trying to identify why it isn't worth the valuation VCs have bestowed on it, Sumanth teamed up with a VC to understand how CRED could potentially justify this valuation and even exceed it five times over.

This month witnessed perhaps the most consequential move in India’s insurance industry in recent years.

The Bajaj Group announced a week ago that it’s buying out its partner Allianz SE’s 26% stake in its life-insurance and general-insurance ventures for close to Rs 24,200 crore ($2.8 billion). This marked the end of a 24-year relationship between the Indian conglomerate and the German financial-services company.

While three Bajaj entities are involved in the deal, the one central to it is Bajaj Holdings & Investments, which is acquiring a fifth of the insurers.

Bajaj Holdings is not your garden-variety corporate parent. It’s the listed holding company of Bajaj Finserv, itself a holding company. Bajaj Finserv is the majority owner of Bajaj Finance, a publicly traded non-bank lender which is, in turn, the promoter of the recently-listed Bajaj Housing Finance.

If you’re feeling dizzy reading this, all you need to know is this: Bajaj Holdings is the big Matryoshka doll that contains several smaller dolls within. Such structures are not uncommon at all. Reliance Industries, for instance, owns Reliance Retail Ventures, which controls the confusingly-named Reliance Retail Ltd, which then owns a number of entities. Tata Investment Corporation, a listed subsidiary of Tata Sons, owns stakes in several group companies too.

But Bajaj is the only instance of a major listed company at so many layers below the mothership. What makes the group even more fascinating is that unlike Bajaj Finance and Bajaj Housing Finance, which have lending operations of their own, the companies at the top of the tier—Bajaj Holdings and Bajaj Finserv—are holding companies.

In other words, the reason for their existence is their ownership in other entities.

Such companies often suffer from what is termed the holding-company or conglomerate discount. Put simply, their investors value them lower than the combined market value of their holdings. This is for a variety of reasons:

  • The complexity of managing diverse interests
  • The extent of ownership in its investees (the higher the stake, the lower the discount)
  • The liquidity of the investments (Bajaj Holdings is far likelier to sell its non-Bajaj investments than its ownership in Bajaj Finserv and motorcycle-maker Bajaj Auto)

But Bajaj Holdings has to deal with the conglomerate discount twice over. First, investors peg Bajaj Finserv’s worth below the value of its stakes in Bajaj Finance and the insurance companies. Then, they do the same with Bajaj Holdings.

Bajaj Holdings’ stakes in other listed companies are together worth Rs 2.03 lakh crore, according to data portal Trendlyne. But its own market cap is only Rs 1.4 lakh crore.
But something intriguing has played out in recent years.

Bajaj Holdings has outpaced three major companies under it, directly or otherwise, in the past year and five years.

So, in effect, investors have lowered Bajaj Holdings’ conglomerate discount. That could be a result of expectations that Bajaj Housing Finance would be taken public, which it was in September. Its promoter, Bajaj Finance, pocketed less than half the Rs 6,350 crore from the sought-after share sale, which possibly means higher dividends up the totem pole, all the way to Bajaj Holdings.

There is another big payday in the offing for the company. Sanjiv Bajaj, managing director of Bajaj Holdings, recently confirmed that the two insurers would list, even though he didn’t share a timeline for the same.

The fact that Bajaj Auto, of which Bajaj Holdings owns a third, has made up for lost time in electric scooters and has pulled ahead of rivals will only add to the latter’s allure.

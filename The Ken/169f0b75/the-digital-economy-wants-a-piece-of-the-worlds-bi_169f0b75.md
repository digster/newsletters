Allow me to request you to zoom in this beautiful Saturday morning.
There are close to 900 million voters who will cast their vote in the Indian General Elections in 2019.
There are 543 Lok Sabha constituencies.
If you go by back of the envelope calculations and no one particular source, about 450 million people of these 900 million voters have access to the Internet. These 450 million people have access to a whole host of Indian, US and Chinese owned Internet applications. Some have downloaded these applications for entertainment; others in search of information, hoping that they will get ahead of the curve, and then tell their friends they are ahead of the curve. A lot of these people are on WhatsApp, owned by Facebook. A lot of them are on Facebook itself. A significant number are on Bytedance-owned Tik Tok, the Chinese app that’s the latest social media wildfire; in a country where outside of internet applications, numerous wildfires exist in agrarian crisis, unemployment, bigotry and vigilante justice.
There are people on India’s homegrown app, Sharechat. On YouTube. On DailyHunt. On email and myriad other media outlets.
These are the people that the politicians contesting for Lok Sabha seats need to reach.
To lure them with their propaganda. Their manifesto. Their hate speech, bile and misinformation; all mixed and mashed together like a tasteless, leftovers feast at the end of an excruciating day.
Enter middlemen. Of all sorts and sizes and emphatic, persuasive claims; one which simply goes like this: You want them, I will get them for you.
Today’s story is about them. The middlemen, who call themselves advertising technology agents; those who promise political parties that they will get the message through. “There is no second prize for losing an election. We help you win.” Goes the pitch from Gurugram-based reputation management firm Astrum.
Today’s story is about cogs in the wheel.

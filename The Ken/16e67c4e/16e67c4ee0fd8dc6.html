Good Morning Ishan,
 
In the heyday of mobile service recharge platform Freecharge—shortly before it was acquired by Snapdeal—I figured I was part of its target demographic. A broke journalist in Delhi subsisting solely on a diet of steamed veg momos and cheap cigarettes, how could I not be? So I downloaded the Kunal Shah-founded app in the hopes of saving a few bucks.
 
I didn't stick with Freecharge very long. At a time when I calculated my remaining bank balance in terms of smokes, my monthly savings from Freecharge wouldn't even get me through a good morning, never mind a heavy news day. The offers, too, were from brands that were either nowhere near me or were simply too minor to bother redeeming.
 
Today, almost half a decade on, I'm considerably less broke. Not because I'm more fiscally prudent but because I also have a credit card to manage expenses. Which leads me to my second tryst with Kunal Shah (and the subject of today's piece)—CRED, "a platform to aggregate and reward people who have good credit scores."
 
Before I joined CRED, I didn't even know I had a good credit score. But once I knew I did, I was expecting the red carpet. The experience, however, has been middling. I like the UX, and I still use it to pay my bills, but the rewards—much like with Freecharge—just don't feel commensurate.
 
As Sumanth points out, though, pandering to ingrates like me is hardly CRED's biggest challenge. After raising $150 million in less than a year (with a valuation of $500 million!), there's enough gas in the tank to do that. The bigger question is, how will CRED use the massive funding it has raised to give its investors the sort of exit Shah provided Freecharge's backers?

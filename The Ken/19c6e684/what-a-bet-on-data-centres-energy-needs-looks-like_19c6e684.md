For much of the clean-energy boom in India, which began over a decade ago, little changed in the business model of power producers. They bid for government projects by quoting the lowest possible tariff, and supplied state-run utilities with electricity.
It didn’t matter that the government could choose to cancel bids if the tariff wasn’t low enough or that the power-distribution companies couldn’t pay up in time.
But something began to shift a few years ago. Factories and offices began demanding renewable energy in their quest to slash emissions. They, along with small businesses, consume half of India’s electricity, so obliging them was also good economics.
So clean-power producers began building solar and wind farms to exclusively meet the needs of such users. Sure, it doesn’t offer the scale of government projects, but the odds of getting stiffed by the buyer are long.

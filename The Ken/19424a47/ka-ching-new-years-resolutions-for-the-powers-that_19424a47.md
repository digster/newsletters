Ka-Ching!
Thu, 02 Jan 25
A weekly newsletter about how finance is getting supercharged by tech in India, and how you can make money work for you..
Good Morning Ishan,
Here’s to a wonderful 2025 from Team Ka-Ching!
Instead of the usual, mostly-meant-to-be-broken resolutions that people take for themselves every new year, I thought it would be fun if this edition—the first of 2025—was about things that would perk up our collective personal-finance experience if others were to accomplish them.
And that’s a big if, given that these ‘others’ who have to budge are the powers-that-be.
But no harm in a nudge, yes?
So, first, here’s hoping that Sanjay Malhotra, the new governor of the Reserve Bank of India (RBI), will usher in big change by undoing something really odd that his predecessor Shaktikanta Das had done.
Malhotra will earn the hurrah from a multitude of mutual-fund investors if the RBI increases overseas investment limits for Indian mutual funds.
Nearly three years ago, in February 2022, mutual-fund houses were told to [stop](https://the-ken.com/story/rbis-7-billion-damper-on-indias-overseas-investments/) investing in overseas stocks because the RBI-imposed cap of US$7 billion on these investments was about to be breached. And in April last year, the RBI squeezed even harder, telling mutual funds to stop accepting fresh inflows in overseas exchange-traded funds (ETFs). Again, because the RBI-imposed cap of US$1 billion was about to be breached.
As [I wrote then](https://the-ken.com/kaching/the-mutual-fund-door-to-foreign-investments-just-got-shut-tighter/), the reasons were unconvincing, and the consequences undesirable.
The country’s forex reserves were at record highs, Indians were transferring record sums abroad through the liberalised remittance scheme (LRS) mechanism, and a good part of this was for investments in foreign stocks and bonds. Why then single out mutual funds—a preferred and desirable investment vehicle for retail investors?
These overseas investment limits for mutual funds were set more than a decade-and-a-half ago, and the industry and the country’s forex reserves have grown leaps and bounds since.
Despite repeated requests to raise them, however, the RBI under Das did not relent. Das is [reported](https://www.business-standard.com/markets/mutual-fund/mutual-funds-knock-rbi-door-for-hike-in-foreign-investment-limits-124020701069_1.html) to have said that the call would “be taken once the central bank is confident the currency is stable on a durable basis.”
But as I asked earlier, why should the burden of stabilising the rupee fall on mutual fund investors, when others merrily use a lot more dollars for travel, education, and direct investments in foreign stocks?
Some market experts think that the restrictions on mutual funds are a way to boost investor participation from GIFT City, Gujarat.
If that’s the case, a most unkind gift it would be.
Because the squeeze on Indian mutual funds’ overseas investments has hurt their investors badly. They have been deprived of the big bull run we’ve seen in tech stocks like Nvidia over the past few years.
Even otherwise, many experts have, for a long while, recommended having some foreign exposure in investors’ portfolios.
It’s not just about the benefit of buying stocks unavailable in India; overseas investments help reduce India-risk and help investors gain from the depreciation of the rupee vis-a-vis currencies like the US dollar. Indeed, had mutual funds been allowed to buy overseas stocks, the recent weakening of the rupee to its all-time low of Rs 85 to the US dollar would have added to investors’ returns.
The impact of this squeeze is, in fact, getting worse.
Motilal Oswal Mutual Fund has paused investments in its two international funds from 5 January even for existing systematic investment plans (SIPs). The reason? Exhaustion of the allowed overseas investment limit.
Investors are exhausted too. Will the RBI oblige, and fast?
There’s also a fair bit that the finance ministry can do.
Last August, I had [written](https://the-ken.com/kaching/gimme-my-grandpa-too/) about how investors in debt funds and gold—like their real-estate counterparts—deserved their grandpa too.
For context, the tax rules on indexation on capital gains were radically altered in July last year. A firestorm of protest saw the government finally offer the concession of ‘grandfathering’, but only to real-estate investors. Grandfathering means that a rule change is not applicable or mandatory on transactions done before the date of implementation of a new rule.
Grandfathering is the fair and right thing to do, but the government did right only by real-estate investors—who lobbied loud and hard. Those in other asset classes, such as debt mutual funds and gold, were left out in the cold.
This was patently unfair and penalised investors for no fault of theirs. Taxation changes should be prospective (going into effect after the day of the announcement) and not retrospective. This first principle of law was violated in the July 2024 Budget.
Will the finance ministry make amends in this year’s Budget, which is just a month away?
The finance minister and the GST Council she leads could also put the recent [brouhaha](https://www.tribuneindia.com/news/india/gst-councils-popcorn-tax-decision-triggers-meme-fest-middle-class-backlash/) over the popcorn tax behind them. This they can and should do by quickly reducing the tax on something far more important and consequential—insurance.
Yet again, in its meeting last month, the GST Council deferred a decision on reducing the taxes on health and life insurance. This delay was [attributed](https://www.businesstoday.in/personal-finance/insurance/story/gst-council-meeting-decision-on-reducing-health-life-insurance-premiums-deffered-more-clarity-sought-458147-2024-12-21) to the need for further analysis and detailed examination.
Except, insurance customers and patients are becoming more impatient and edgy by the day.
An 18% tax on the premiums of health policies and many life policies is considered as way too excessive by many, especially for products that should ideally not be taxed at all, or taxed at the lowest rates. As it is, insurance premiums are spiking, and the high tax might be deterring many people from buying policies that are essential.
While on the subject of insurance, the upcoming Budget should also seriously consider offering deductions on insurance premiums in the new tax regime too. The lower tax rates are all well and good, but the trouble with the new tax regime is the risk of many folks not buying policies, simply because they do not get tax breaks for insurance.
Classic penny-wise, pound-foolish. A tax nudge seems necessary to set this right.
Also, will this Budget really offer [relief](https://timesofindia.indiatimes.com/business/india-business/govt-considers-income-tax-relief-for-middle-class-earning-up-to-rs-15-lakhs-report/articleshow/116686948.cms) to those earning up to Rs 15 lakh a year? Or is this just a trial balloon floated by unnamed sources?
The fact is, substantial tax relief is long overdue. Both consumer sentiment and savings are hurting from high inflation and low income growth, and we are at a point where we need much more than just lip service.

Downrounds are somewhat of a taboo in the startup universe, for reasons I’ve never fully understood. While share prices of listed companies can move up or down freely, privately funded startups are expected to go in only one direction—up. I’ve previously argued about the downstream and second-order effects of this inequitable expectation, and usually the price (pun intended) is paid by the retail investor when the company goes public.
I’ve [described this](https://the-ken.com/the-nutgraf/how-much-longer-can-the-foie-gras-isation-of-startups-continue/) in somewhat morbid terms as “the great fattening followed by the great slaughter”, or the foie-gras-isation of Indian startups. Consequently, we end up with curves that look like this.
Anyway, I’m all for downrounds. It’s fine. It’s also bland, and frankly, boring.
The far more interesting part in all this is that over 25% of Cred’s new funding came from its founder, Kunal Shah.
This isn’t how things usually go. Founders typically start by building something, selling the vision, hiring a team, and getting things moving. Then, at some point, they approach people with money—usually VCs—and try to convince them to bet on the dream. The VCs look at the pitch, the people, the product. If it all checks out, they shrug, nod, and write a cheque in exchange for a slice of the upside.
But sometimes, this is not what happens. Instead, the founder decides to do it all themselves. These are the founders who have the capital and the will to do what I’m calling “faith-funding”.
Faith-funding is when founders change roles and become VCs to invest in their own companies. Like fantasy heroes who ride back to defend the castle when the walls are crumbling, these founders protect their stakes with not just swords, but also gold and money.
They even double down when others retreat. They don’t go out and sell their vision to others. They don’t ask others to trust them. They fund their own faith.
In other words, they don’t just lead in the battlefield—they bankroll the war.
It’s not like founders did not do this in the past, but previously, those situations have been wildly different, and dare I say… not ideal. There are several examples of founders who’ve done this in India for different reasons, under strange circumstances.
In the past, there have been three scenarios when founders have had to deploy faith-funding.
The first kind is when the founder more or less funds (and controls) their company right from the beginning. If you exclude companies where founders have actively avoided VC money because they want to stay in control, such as Navi and Sachin Bansal, then what’s left are startups that, well, nobody really understands. Perhaps it’s way too risky to invest in them, even for VC investors, and it just so happens that the founder has access to enormous amounts of capital. Expect to see no clear revenue streams. Think of it as a massive hobby project.
Just last week, I wrote [about](https://the-ken.com/the-nutgraf/bhavish-aggarwal-is-finally-indias-elon-musk/) one of these: Krutrim and Bhavish Aggarwal.
The other kind of faith-funding happens when the company is distressed, and the founder sees an opportunity to assume greater ownership when there are no easy ways for other investors to object to this seizure. Famously, this happened with Byju’s almost exactly a year ago, when its founder Byju Raveendran wanted to raise money at a 99% cut in valuation, essentially putting a gun to the heads of all existing investors to either participate or face a dramatic dilution of their equity. At the time, I was even [somewhat sympathetic](https://the-ken.com/the-nutgraf/the-starvation-of-byjus/) towards Byju’s, even though the situation was quite amusing (and bizarre).
Yet another kind of faith-funding is where the founder does something so strange and unprecedented that people just… roll with it.
Oh, hello, Oyo* and Ritesh Agarwal.
I could spend an entire edition trying to explain all the strange things that Ritesh Agarwal has done to inject funds into Oyo, and I doubt we’ll see the whole picture. Suffice to say, the bottom line is that through a series of complicated loans, share pledges, and other things that I’ve already [written](https://the-ken.com/the-nutgraf/ritesh-agarwal-and-the-missing-850-million-dollars/) about, Agarwal owns nearly 33% of Oyo, and Softbank has another 46% of it.
You can see why founders doing faith-funding was generally seen as an undesirable thing. Faith-funding was viewed with suspicion, and for good reason—a founder putting money into their company is a sign that they couldn’t convince someone else to do the same. It’s fair to say that faith-funding was a taboo of sorts.
However, strange things happen during a downturn. VCs get more skittish, and some founders see this not as a crisis, but as an opportunity.
Kunal Shah isn’t the first startup founder to do this in recent times. A few months back, Gaurav Kumar, founder and CEO of the fintech company Yubi, invested Rs 250 crore into his company. Founders sometimes don’t even have to pay the entire amount upfront. In October, consumer lending and payments startup Slice raised Rs 72 crore ($8.6 million) from its founder and CEO, Rajan Bajaj, through partly paid-up shares.
Over the last few months, faith-funding hasn’t just become palatable, it’s become more powerful. The taboo has been wiped away, and the founders and companies are much more attractive.
Like with most things in life, this is all a form of game theory. When everyone has capital, founders doubling down in their companies is viewed with suspicion. But when capital is scarce, and when some founders are actively [trying to exit](http://the-ken.com/the-nutgraf/founders-are-choosing-respawning-over-resistance/) the companies they founded, the founder who is doing faith-funding holds more appeal. It’s a sign that this founder isn’t going anywhere, and that they believe in the startup so much that they are willing to buy shares at this price, confident that there will be an upside later.
And VCs, who are creatures of FOMO, usually ask themselves, “What does this founder know about their business that we don’t?”
That’s usually enough to get past the line.

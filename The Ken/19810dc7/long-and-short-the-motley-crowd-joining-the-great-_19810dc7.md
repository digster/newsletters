Long and Short
Wed, 16 Jul 25
The story behind the numbers and motivations driving India's stock market..
Good Morning Ishan,
Earlier in July, Jio Blackrock Asset Management, the newest kid on India’s mutual-fund block, announced its eye-popping Rs 17,800 crore ($2.1 billion) fundraise from just its first three launches—all short-term debt funds.
Its overnight, liquid, and money market funds reportedly got investments from over 90 institutional investors and 67,400 retail investors. The sizeable asset mop-up immediately catapulted the just-about-born money manager to the 29th position among India’s 48 mutual-fund houses. In the debt-fund category, Jio Blackrock is now the 16th largest in terms of AUM.
Great start, but along expected lines.
The 50:50 joint venture between the financial-services firm backed by India’s richest man, Mukesh Ambani, and international investment giant Blackrock would have settled for nothing less than a big splash.
On 4 June, The Ken, in this newsletter, [wrote](https://the-ken.com/long_and_short/jio-blackrock-is-not-your-usual-reliance-offensive/) why Jio-Blacrock’s idea of disruption in India’s mutual-fund industry seemed impractical. “Jio and Reliance Industries’ group companies will likely put their surplus in the short-term liquid and money-market schemes of Jio Blackrock Mutual Fund. It could be a group mandate,” a senior industry executive had then said.
“This will see the fund house garner sizable AUMs in a year or so, even if it earns low revenue,” he added.
Whether or not that’s the case, we’ll only know in August when industry body AMFI publishes the fund flows for July, which would have the contribution of sponsors and their associates, too. It should make for an interesting read.
Jio-Blackrock is not the only financial-services firm chasing the pot of gold in India’s Rs 75 lakh crore-and-counting mutual-fund industry—even if it is the most prominent among the new players. Over the past couple of years, more than half a dozen players—from broking houses Zerodha*, Groww, and Angel One, to portfolio-management-services firms Helios, Unifi, and Capitalmind, to financial-services giant Bajaj Finserv—have thrown their hat into the ring.
And many more are waiting in the wings. It’s a motley crowd.
In March, Pantomath Capital Advisors, a merchant banker and provider of alternative investment funds (AIFs), got in-principle approval from the market regulator Sebi to launch its mutual-fund house. This is the first instance of a merchant banker, one that deals mostly in IPOs of small and medium enterprises, looking to launch its own mutual-fund schemes in India. Pantomath is now waiting for Sebi’s final nod, a process that could take many months.
Sebi is also said to be considering giving in-principle approval to five more entities. Alphagrep Securities, a quantitative trading and investment firm that develops algorithmic-trading strategies, is the country’s first such business trying to get into the mutual-funds space. Then, there are the likes of Nuvama Wealth Management and Wealth First Portfolio Managers—the first players from the wealth space seeking to run their own mutual funds. There’s also the stockbroker and financial-products distributor Monarch Networth Capital and PMS provider Marcellus Investment Managers, the latter founded by veteran market commentator Saurabh Mukherjea.
Soon, there may be 50 mutual-fund houses in India—maybe in a year or so. Prima facie, given the under-penetration of mutual funds in the country, there’s scope for many more players. In fact, India’s mutual fund AUM-to-GDP ratio, despite the pick-up over the past many years, is still under 20%, shows the draft IPO document filed recently by [ICICI Prudential AMC](https://the-ken.com/story/icici-prudential-amcs-ipo-is-as-strange-as-it-is-timely/), the second-largest mutual fund in India in terms of assets managed. That’s much lower than many other markets and the world average of 64%.
Yet, the Indian mutual-fund space remains top-heavy and dominated by a few big players. The top 10 players command nearly 77% in the industry’s AUMs—down from about 83% in 2021.
Bank-sponsored AMCs—the likes of SBI, ICICI, HDFC, and Kotak—dominate the most. Of the total AUMs, the top 10 bank-led AMCs account for nearly two-thirds, almost unchanged from March 2020. This is primarily due to their massive branch networks across the country that double up as formidable distribution arms.
The top players are raking it in, too. ICICI Prudential AMC, for instance, is the country’s most profitable asset manager. In FY25, it made over Rs 2,600 crore in profits, while HDFC AMC made about Rs 2,500 crore. This is an outcome largely of the huge AUMs they have amassed over the past few decades. ICICI Prudential AMC had nearly Rs 9 lakh crore in assets as of March, and HDFC AMC had assets worth nearly Rs 7.75 lakh crore.
The pot of gold is alluring to everyone who’s watching and wanting in.
But for several new players, it will be a multi-year uphill climb to cover even half the distance. The Ken [hinted](https://the-ken.com/story/zerodha-navi-and-the-new-wave-hoping-to-be-indias-vanguard/) at it about four years ago.
Going by the experience of players such as Parag Parikh Mutual Fund, which was also once a PMS before morphing into a mutual fund in 2013, it could take at least five to seven years to build scale and turn a profit. That is, of course, if all goes to plan
This is especially true for fund houses that seek to run only passive funds and low fund-management fees. As my colleague Archishma [reported](https://the-ken.com/story/zerodha-navi-wanted-a-passive-investing-revolution-nope-said-older-fund-houses/) in March, breaking into the increasingly competitive and complicated passive market is hard for new players; legacy players have hijacked the space. Fund houses would need at least Rs 50,000 crore in passive fund AUM to turn profitable, as estimated by a person close to the industry.
That could take a long, long time.
Active funds have better fees, but they also come with associated high costs like fund manager salaries that could run into crores of rupees each year. Besides, without a track record of good outperformance that could take years to build, it’s hard for active fund managers to build AUM.
Barring [Bajaj Finserv](https://the-ken.com/story/how-bajaj-finserv-carpet-bombed-its-way-to-2-2b-in-mutual-funds/#:~:text=Anand%20is%20Finance%20Editor%20at%20The%20Ken.,when%20he%20left%20a%20career%20of%20seven) Mutual Fund, which has been able to scale fast to about Rs 25,000 crore, primarily by courting distributors and leveraging its brand, the other new players, launched over the past five years, still manage assets under Rs 10,000 crore. Many of them manage under Rs 5,000 crore. Breaking even is nowhere near the horizon.
Those joining in now, and even those waiting in the wings, will have to play the long game of patience, perseverance, and deep pockets to sustain. Not everyone comes with inbuilt advantages like Jio Blackrock.
*Zerodha’s perennial fund, Rainmatter Capital, is an investor in The Ken.

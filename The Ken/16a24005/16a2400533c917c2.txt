Without invoking the demo(n), let’s just say the government, in order to drive cashless payments, has tried everything. To list a few:

·      Slashed the fee merchants had to pay banks for accepting payments

·      Gave banks targets to hook more merchants with PoS terminals to accept digital payments

·      Has been looking at pushing low-cost solutions like QR codes

Incidentally, the fundamental flaw of misaligned incentives has not been addressed. The basic card payments network has barely expanded beyond the existing mid-to-large retail shops. Banks across the board have little incentive to push for greater use of offline card payments. Customers keep using these cards at ATMs, increasing the cost all around.

One way to fix that, argued Srikanth Rajagopalan, a veteran of the Indian payments industry, is to flip the fee structure on its head.

Six weeks ago he wrote "How to kill cash" https://the-ken.com/story/how-to-kill-cash/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter, making a case for inverting the fee structure. Today, he returns with a “graded solution”. Why this volte-face? Because his article triggered a spirited debate. While the banks and card networks are stuck in a chicken-and-egg problem, in a parallel universe, UPI—the real-time bank-to-bank transfer system launched in 2016—is scaling beyond what anybody may have imagined.

In the game of kill-cash, the cards are stacked against the debit cards, writes Rajagopalan, but there is a finite window for the incumbents, the clubby card networks and banks, to get up to speed. What is that window? For that, you’ll have to read his piece, which, interestingly, is also well-reported.

Even as countries race towards a vaccine for the Covid pandemic, another race is playing out alongside it. One to find, test, and deploy existing drugs to treat patients suffering from the virus.
It's been a frantic, scattershot effort. Pharma companies have pored over their drug catalogues, looking for anything fit for purpose. The popular Remdesivir, for example, was developed to treat Ebola. Itolizumab, Biocon's controversial "breakthrough" drug, was actually meant to treat psoriasis. Hydroxychloroquine, which found a mascot in Donald Trump, is an anti-malarial. Anything that works could be a windfall for drug makers, and a shot in the arm, sometimes literally, for those fighting off Covid.
Pharma companies are racing against the clock. This has seen small trials, which at any other time would be considered statistically insignificant, become enough for obscure drugs to be hailed as the next big thing. And as drug makers attempt to rush these to market, drug regulators are placed in an unenviable position. Approve potentially ineffective, even harmful, drugs; or reject them and, should they later prove effective, risk looking incompetent.
An increasingly common compromise is Emergency Use Authorisation, or EUA—the temporary approval of promising but as-yet-unproven drugs for use in Covid cases. EUA, though, is hardly a marker of efficacy. It is the last bastion of weary regulators and governments desperate for a glimmer of good fortune. Often, these drugs eventually prove to be more hype than hope.
Take Tocilizumab from pharma major Roche, for example. In the early days of the pandemic, based on limited data, it was thrust into use as a treatment for severely affected Covid patients. The past two weeks, however, have brought results from more extensive trials that show it to be ineffective. Yet it remains part of India's national clinical management guidelines.
In today's piece, Olina dives into the murky waters of EUA. She painstakingly sifts through the various drugs and therapies that have taken this route, trying to discern what the bar is for a drug to win this coveted status, the failsafes that exist to protect patients, and whether this is the best way to go about things, pandemic or not. It's fascinating stuff.

India’s stock-market investors have not had to wrestle with questions about the sustainability of private credit until now.
For the handful of listed financial-services firms that are in this business, private credit, which is non-bank lending by investment firms, is much smaller than their mainstay, as is the case with 360 One WAM. Or it is too recent a diversification to warrant serious scrutiny, as is true of Motilal Oswal Financial Services.
But investors will soon have a firm that is more about private credit than about anything else.
EAAA India Alternatives recently filed for its initial public offering. The entire proceeds of the Rs 1,500 crore share sale will go to EAAA’s holding company, Edelweiss Securities and Investments.
With $6.8 billion in assets under management (AUM), EAAA is the largest homegrown firm in alternative assets, which include private equity and real-estate funding. Almost two-thirds of the fund’s AUM is in private credit.
EAAA is going public at an odd time for private credit in India. The country is a [blip](https://the-ken.com/story/private-credit-has-tasted-blood-in-india-what-comes-next/) on the private-credit scene. It has under $30 billion in private-credit assets, compared with over $2.1 trillion globally. But no public-market investor looking to bet on a private-credit firm can overlook all the doomsday talk around the asset class.
“When you see one cockroach, there are probably more,” JP Morgan Chase chief Jamie Dimon said in October, after frauds at auto-parts supplier First Brands and subprime auto lender Tricolor forced them into bankruptcy. Both companies had borrowed from private-credit funds. JP Morgan had lent to Tricolor.
Jeffrey Gundlach, a prominent bond investor, alluded to “garbage lending” in private credit and said it could cause the next financial crisis. Raghuram Rajan, a former governor of India’s central bank, chimed in to say that private credit hasn’t been “stress-tested” like bank loans.

Long-form features 🗞️

TCS offers quirky titles and little else in recruiting revamp to attract 40K hires

The Mumbai-based behemoth wants to make IT sexy again for young professionals. Its facelift, though, fails to hide long-standing woes. 

For the first time in nearly two decades, TCS’ total headcount dipped by almost 13,000 in the last financial year, but the Mumbai-based IT Services giant is planning to hire 40,000 freshers this year to make up for it. The firm is banking on a three-tier framework to not only achieve this goal, but also make the IT space attractive for freshers again. 

However, while these tiers are quirky, they fail to address fundamental issues that have plagued the industry for years. [Read the full story here.]

600 new mutual funds in three years is a win for everyone. Almost everyone

Fund houses and distributors are raking it in with new fund offers (NFO) galore. But investors could be left with the short end of the stick. 

Mutual fund houses raised Rs 66,000 crore (US$8 billion) from 185 NFOs in the last financial year, and a lot of new offers are launching to catch a ride on this upswing. Experts, however, caution against diving into most of these offers due to hefty commissions and a lack of performance history. Also, with the regulator having proposed and retracted reforms over “switch” transactions, investors could be left to fend for themselves. [Read the full story here.]

Amazon got rid of its largest seller only to replace it with other ‘preferred’ sellers

The e-commerce giant had to part ways with its ‘preferred sellers’ in India after regulatory interventions. But newcomers are still getting advantages.

Amazon had to sever ties with its top sellers Appario and Cloudtail after regulators turned up the heat. But now, preferred sellers Cocoblu and Clicktech are getting exclusive resources and count ex-Amazon executives among their top leaders. Amazon may be compliant with regulations, but the ‘revolving door’ blurs the lines between marketplace and seller. And its influence is most evident when conflicts of interest arise, allowing Amazon to benefit far more than anyone else. [Read the full story here.]

**********

KKR turned Eurokids into a ‘billion-dollar’ education giant. But not everyone’s applauding

In just five years, the PE titan is setting its sights on a 5X jump in the school chain’s value, riding on corporate direction and revenue boosts from recent fee hikes.

KKR, the global investment firm, seeks to sell its 92% stake in school chain Eurokids—acquired for US$200 million in 2019—for US$1 billion. The partnership turned Eurokids into a diversified education juggernaut. Private equity not only provided capital for Eurokids’ expansion but also introduced business managers who professionalised the education brand. 

But, as the brand’s value grows, not everyone is pleased. Parents are resisting fee hikes and franchise partners are feeling the heat as they struggle to meet annual targets. [Read the full story here.]

**********

Waycool’s pivot and the false promise of agritech

The startup, worth US$700 million, wanted to reshape the food supply chain. But it increasingly looks like an FMCG company.

Waycool Foods wanted to solve India’s broken food supply chain by linking farmers directly with end consumers. But once it started struggling to scale margins in its mainstay business, the company resorted to layoffs and restructuring. 

And now, Waycool has pivoted from its core business of agri supply chain services into offering SaaS solutions and consumer goods. Why? Will it really help? [Read the full story here.]

**********

A Philippine regulator on the digital-banking blueprint that once got India’s attention

The country has set a benchmark for digital banking governance by enabling new technologies while also keeping the unfettered enthusiasm for such innovations in check.

Southeast Asia is setting the pace in digital banking regulation, with five states already implementing licensing frameworks. The Philippines became the second country from the region, after Singapore, to introduce a regulatory structure for digital bank permits in 2020. But now, four years later, regulators are assessing whether they will need to issue a new set of licences on top of the six pilot approvals given earlier. [Read the full story here.]

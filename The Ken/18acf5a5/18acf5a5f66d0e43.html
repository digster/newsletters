Good Morning Ishan,

When a colleague’s son made it to IIT Bombay, Makemytrip founder Deep Kalra told him he didn’t realise how big a deal it was.

He wasn’t just referring to the fact the teenager, an urban resident, had cracked one of the world’s most competitive entrance tests.

“I said, you know, hats off to this kid who can do it today because he’s competing with someone who, for the last four years of his or her life, has only had one goal in mind: to go to Kota factory and crack IIT,” he said on The Ken‘s podcast “First Principles” in January.

Kota’s fame, or infamy, is well earned. Roughly 200,000 students arrive in the western Indian city every year, making it the undisuputed capital of entrance-test coaching. But it’s notorious for its hyper-competitive, make-or-break environment and the gruelling schedules the aspirants are put through, driving some to take their own lives.

This happens year in, year out, but it’s always business as usual. That’s the unfortunate appeal of Kota.

Not anymore, though, as my colleague Alifiya discovered on her recent visit to the city.

In 2023, there have been 24 student suicides in Kota, the highest on record. Naturally, parents are anxious. Admissions are down 20% since June, under-construction hostels are being turned into flats, and coaching institutes are dreading what comes next.

But there’s more than suicides fuelling Kota’s worst crisis to date, and you’ll find no better story than Alifiya’s to make sense of that: https://the-ken.com/story/kotas-rs-6k-cr-coaching-business-may-never-be-the-same-again/ (10-min read)

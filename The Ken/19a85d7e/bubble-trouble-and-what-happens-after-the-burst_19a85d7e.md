Good Morning Ishan,
A nicely designed graphic by Bloomberg made its rounds in October. You know the one: it [charted](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2025-10-07/openai-s-nvidia-amd-deals-boost-1-trillion-ai-boom-with-circular-deals?sref=R8NfLgwS) how the deals between OpenAI, Nvidia, AMD, Oracle, and other companies involved in artificial intelligence are circular. Move $100 billion here, $300 billion there, and it all becomes flush, with everyone connected to everyone else. The notion was that a rising tide—a torrent of profits brought on by AGI—would lift all boats. The implicit bluff was that it wouldn’t be possible for (nearly) every company to fail at the same time.
This seeded the idea that OpenAI was becoming too big to fail, or at least it was trying really hard to give off that impression. Talks of bubbles in the artificial intelligence sector saturated headlines and airwaves. The impending pop, if or when it happens, will be the most predicted correction in the history of speculative markets.
It’s worth rethinking this situation by flipping it around. Consider slipstreams: there are fish that [hitch rides](https://www.abc.net.au/news/2025-11-03/hitchhiking-suckerfish-reveal-humpback-whales-web-of-life/105950862) by swimming close to humpback whales, cruising where there is the least current. Dolphins, horse jockeys, speed skaters, truck drivers, and birds that fly in V-shaped formations all take advantage of the same effect to give themselves a boost in speed without expending extra fuel or energy.
OpenAI is doing precisely that, barreling ahead at full speed in the wake of companies such as Microsoft and Nvidia. We tend to place immense attention on breakthroughs in language-model training—understandably so because there are benchmark scores that make it easy to compare, and user experiences that make us think about what it will mean to co-exist with artificial intelligence. But what really matters in the long run is the hyperscalers’ infrastructure, capital, and integration with enterprise customers. In a way, these are all part of the “boring” side of the business of AI, but they matter a lot more to regular users than you might think.

After the recent lukewarm public offerings of hot tech companies, the perceived wisdom is that public investors are not as generous as private investors. B2B e-commerce marketplace IndiaMART’s listing on the Indian bourses yesterday reversed the sentiments. At least to an extent, and for the time being.
 
A large number of analysts had given it an “avoid” rating, saying the offering was “aggressively priced”. As it often happens when the IPO is for an investor exit. Yup, no proceeds will go to IndiaMART. The 23-year-old company, which ended 10.4% higher than its opening share price, was valued at Rs 3,748 crore, again higher than what analysts had estimated.
 
Of course, this valuation pales in comparison to what some of the new B2B companies such as Udaan, BlackBuck and others are valued at. Interestingly, these are not the companies CEO Dinesh Agarwal considers as competitors. At the IPO celebration at the Hyatt in Noida last week, he told the gathering it was the Chinese conglomerate Alibaba, whose B2B platform in India serves new, growing and established businesses, that he wanted to beat.
 
The battle between subscription and transaction models, aka between IndiaMART and Udaan-BlackBuck that is playing out in India, is a trailer of what has already reached a climax in China. Between Alibaba and JD.com, the China's second largest e-commerce firm.
 
With the cash-rich newcomers on one side, Alibaba on another, and RAW (Reliance, Amazon, Walmart) on the third, IndiaMART is bracing for an interesting post-IPO life. At the Hyatt last week, Agarwal said that in 15-20 years, everything will move online and there won’t be any offline transactions and vendors. Abinaya’s story examines why Agarwal said what he said.

Are fixed and recurring deposits regaining some of their lost lustre? FDs and RDs have long been relegated to the middle of the investment popularity rankings. Equity’s recent record may be changing that
By Rounak Kumar Gunjan
5 min read
View in browser

Good Morning Ishan,

You are reading our subscriber-only newsletter Ka-Ching!—a weekly commentary on fintech and personal finance

I hope you’re having a great week, because mine isn’t really going all that well. 

When I was growing up, the grey-haired around me consistently asserted that fixed deposits (FD) were the bomb. Guaranteed returns, insurance for contingencies, safe… it wasn’t long before young me started dreaming about accumulating enough money to park in an FD or two. 

Then, someone else introduced me to the idea of starting a recurring deposit to accumulate the corpus I needed to put into an FD. And so the FD daydream made room for RDs too; this FD+RD tango would be my route to wealth, I decided. 

But a little before I started working, a loud, confident crowd of elder cousins, chartered accountants, financial advisors, and finally finfluencers descended on me, collectively counselling I shun FDs and RDs. The returns were often below the effective inflation rate, they claimed, so I wouldn’t be accumulating money so much as losing it. 

I bought into the argument. So FDs and RDs made way for equity, mutual funds, and Systematic Investment Plans (SIP). And thus it has been for many years, with me comfortable in the received knowledge that I was finally on a sureshot path to wealth. 

At least I was comfortable, until this Monday, when I had the opportunity to speak to two wealth managers. And then rang up a retired department of financial services official to confirm what they told me. 

“Put the largest percentage of your corpus in fixed deposits, or if you’re trying to build a corpus, put it in recurring deposits, at least for the next two years,” one of the Mumbai-based wealth managers said. “Banks are desperate for your money, use it to your advantage. SIPs and equity can be small disposable parts of the corpus that you don’t mind losing, because market-linked results have been disappointing and will continue to be muted for a while. So why risk it?”

Wait, what? Invest largely in FDs and RDs? But hadn’t I been told they were the worst form of investment?

Muted equity

Behind this sudden upturn in interest for deposit instruments is a slowdown in equity-linked returns over the last three years.

“Last year, several mutual fund investors in India experienced a decline in returns. There has to be a reason behind it, right? These are early signs reflecting broader market trends.”

Retired DFS official

In calendar year 2023, the BSE Sensex index rose over 18%. In calendar 2024, this growth slowed to 8.2%. And so far this year, the index has actually slumped—by over 3%. The Nifty 50 index has traced a similar trajectory. Indian indices have lagged global counterparts too—the Nasdaq, for instance, registered a growth of 25% in 2024, the Dow Jones grew by 12%, and the Nikkei 225 by 10%.

“Weak corporate earnings, rising inflation, slower-than-expected GDP growth, have all eaten into investor confidence. Then, there’s the falling rupee. The markets will take some time to bounce back. So will market-linked investments,” said the second wealth manager.

Foreign Institutional Investors (FIIs) significantly reduced their investments in Indian equities in 2024. Data from the National Securities Depository (NSDL) shows that FIIs withdrew over Rs 50,000 crore from Indian equities in 2024, a sharp increase compared to outflows of Rs 28,000 crore in 2023. “While FIIs outflows are not compulsorily linked with falling confidence, a continuous and increasing withdrawal from them does need close study. It usually points towards choppy times,” said the first wealth manager. 

The FII selling trend has continued in January and February 2025 as well. January witnessed FIIs selling on 22 out of 23 trading days in the month.  

FIIs represent a significant portion of equity investments, and exits puts additional downward pressure on the market and affect the performance of mutual funds with high allocations to FII-heavy sectors.

Old allies falling short

Mutual funds have also been let down by sectors that have usually been good bets to make in the last decade. 

Funds with high exposures to energy and consumer goods have dipped into the red. According to data from the NSE and the Association of Mutual Funds in India (AMFI), energy stock returns stood at negative 4% in 2024 due to rising raw material costs and slower demand, while consumer goods recorded negative 5% returns due to sluggish domestic consumption. Both sectors had delivered consistently positive returns in the ten years prior to 2024—even during the pandemic. 

A jump in redemptions may also point to investors losing some confidence in equity. “In 2024, equity fund redemptions grew by 40% year-on-year,” said the Mumbai-based wealth manager. According to a Business Standard article that quotes brokerage Motilal Oswal, at Rs 4.6 lakh crore, this is the highest-ever amount redeemed in a single year. “This can mean two things. Either investors hit their target price and withdrew, or they’re worried about a sluggish market ahead,” the wealth manager added.

While there can be no one-size-fits-all investment strategy, all these factors seem to have convinced the people quoted above that fixed-return financial instruments like FDs and RDs must be brought back into portfolio mixes in a significant way. Especially in the short-term. 

Given FD rates offered by public sector banks, the most conservative on offer, range between 6–7.5% per annum for 1- to 5-year tenures, and RD rates between 6–7% for 1- to 3-year tenures, maybe that’s not such a bad idea.

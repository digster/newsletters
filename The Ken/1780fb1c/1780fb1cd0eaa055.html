Welcome to both a brand new week and part 2 of Seema Singh’s incredibly deep dive into the rabbit hole that is government use of strategy consulting services in India. Strap in!

Okay, let’s start with a simple quiz: do you think India’s government is too “big”? Do you think it has too many permanent employees? Do you think it ought to “rightsize”—let go off employees and get smaller?

Most of you would, of course, say the Indian government is too big and has too many employees. Heck, even the Indian government believes it’s too big.

Actually, the size of the Indian government’s staff budget is a fraction of the US staff budget (in proportion of the two economies, not absolute numbers). India has a smaller size government given its size and stage of development when actually it needs a bigger, and of course, more effective government.

I don’t say this. Arun Maira did to Seema Singh, our Editor, in today’s story.

Who’s Maira, you ask? He’s the former chairman of the Boston Consulting Group India and one of the sharpest and most respected minds operating at the confluence of business, government, and policy in India.

If the disease Maira talks about is a government that is much too small and under-resourced for what India needs, the symptoms we can observe as a result is the rise of consultants working for the Indian government.

Wait, what, you ask.

Here’s how it works. Government departments are hesitant to add more full-time employees, especially if they cannot ensure they’ll get smart hires. So, they hire temporary consultants instead.

Sometimes the consultants are paid by the government. Sometimes they’re paid by private foundations and billionaire philanthropists. Sometimes they’re not paid at all.

A redesigned corporate subscription page

This one was a long time coming. Over 200 teams across India’s leading companies subscribe to The Ken, but our corporate page didn’t really do a great job of convincing anyone about it. 

One of the reasons why this was happening was because a large chunk of corporate subscribers were originally individual subscribers and struggled to describe the value of The Ken to their managers or leaders. Many of them wrote to us expressing this frustration, so we decided to solve it for them.

We’re quite proud of the redesign, because it finally speaks to the target persona across the board—HR leaders, CXOs, and L&D department heads in a language that’s familiar to them. Combine that with the fact that we have a clear, transparent pricing structure, along with a powerful administrative dashboard, and the corporate subscription is a no-brainer for any organisation that wants its team members to experience The Ken.

Gifting that works like magic

One of the biggest benefits that subscribers get is the ability to share gift links to paywalled stories with non-subscribers. This is one of the first major innovative products we launched nearly seven years ago at The Ken (even before The New York Times!). It’s also one of the most powerful ways non-subscribers can experience The Ken, even before they decide to take up a subscription. 

Story gifting is fun, but it’s still a bit clunky. There are gift credits that get deducted, and you constantly have to keep track of how many you have left. 

In February, we’ll dramatically change this experience and make it feel like magic. 

If you’re gifting a story to someone, it should feel like sharing any other link. 

If that sounds like magic, well, you’ll soon see what I mean. 

Search that actually understands what you want

There’s a question I often ask in Product Manager interviews—why is the search experience at all news websites so bad? 

I won’t tell you the answer yet, but the sad reality is that searching on The Ken’s website and app is just as bad as most of the others. This despite the fact that a large percentage of high-intent searches eventually lead to users becoming subscribers. Search is one of the most efficient ways to get a pulse of whether The Ken is for you or not—users just land and enter the name of a company (usually the one they work at) to check how often we’ve covered it. This is one of the primary paths through which they see value in The Ken. 

So we asked, what if search could be…intelligent? 

Forget stuff like names of companies, could it be used to answer questions like “stories about fintech companies that went bankrupt before Covid”, or “why are fresher salaries so low”, and our search would just throw up the results. 

This is semantic search—one of our first AI-powered features, built in-house and hosted on private servers. Building it wasn’t hard, but doing it in a way where we kept control of the data completely took a little time.

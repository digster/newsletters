Good Morning Ishan,
 
Last week, we officially switched off ACs in our Delhi office. The city roared with Durga Pujo-Dussehra celebrations. The temperatures dropped ever so slightly and long days became shorter.

These changes have also marked the coming of what we Delhi-ites now indulgently call ‘smog season’. They tell me things are 25% better this year, but given how the air pricks my eyes and chokes my throat on my daily auto ride to office, I’m scared to think how much longer it’s going to take to make another 25% leap of improvement.

At PM 2.5 levels already nearing an ‘unhealthy’ 189, I realise that I can run, but I can’t hide.
 
Facts are facts. Green crackers or not.
 
This week at The Ken, we sort of took this idiom to heart. And out poured four strong stories that held up a mirror. Let’s take a look.
 
When ride-hailing company Grab, released a “social impact” report claiming it had added $5.8 billion to Southeast Asia’s economy between April 2018 and March 2019, our SEA colleague Jon’s ears perked up. In his piece on Monday, Jon digs deep into what this social impact report symbolises. Grab’s no longer positioning itself as just a hard-nosed start-up but a company that cares about the economic development of the regions it operates in (now where have we heard that before?). But as Jon points out, social impact is a convenient garb to hide the deeper fissures running through its business model. An Uber-like competitor in Gojek, heavy losses and regulatory issues – issues that will collide to fuel growth sans sustainability for Grab.
 
Speaking of garbs, the Amazon-Flipkart e-retail duopoly has played many cloak-and-dagger games with the sellers listed on their sites. There have been several tactics, but none dents a seller’s profits (and trust) like mega discounts. But as Abinaya explains in her piece on Tuesday, aggregators have boxed sellers into one of two options: give discount or die. For instance, Flipkart offered better visibility on its platform for lower prices. And if you thought incentivising incentives is a bad business model, Flipkart made a cool Rs 8400 crore in GMV sales this Big Billion season.
 
From big numbers to tiny ones. If 15 seconds is all you have to teach through video, what would it be? For most content creators on TikTok’s latest campaign #Edutok, it’s English slang words. TikTok claims that Edutok has already crossed 42.5 billion views, attracting even serious edtechs to a platform most known for lip-syncing to songs. This elaborate PR stunt opens a gateway for Bytedance to enter the edtech space in India. But as I wrote in our Wednesday story, edtech is a complex operation and belies the very DIY nature of TikTok. Like its Chinese counterparts, will Indian TikTok manage to cross the bridge between fun and learning?

As we got closer to the weekend, we had to close out the week with food. On Friday, Sharath’s story looked into how Rebel Foods (of Fassos, Behrouz and OvenStory Pizza fame) has come to dominate the cloud kitchen industry. But scaling is no piece of cake. With margins, delivery and set-up cost eating into revenues, Rebel Foods has made an international leap of faith by expanding to Southeast Asia, backed by Travis Kalanick and Gojek. This fifth pivot might just work for Rebel Foods, but will it ever escape the inherent plot hole in its scale story?
 
That’s it for this week, folks. Enjoy your weekend. Treat yourself to some Behrouz. And always keep that mask handy.

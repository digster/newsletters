Good Morning Ishan,
It’s nice to be back in your inbox after a two-week break. I had a lovely vacation and am suitably recharged and re-energised!
I came back after being away from work for 12 days. And yet, I did not feel guilty, backlogged, or anxious because of what may have happened while I was away or all the pending emails and to-dos that would have piled up.
One reason is I’ve got a great bunch of colleagues who held the fort.
A younger me might’ve been a tad insecure. If no one really missed or needed me, then was I really even needed?
But the older me is thrilled. It’s great that I was (hopefully) not missed or needed. As a founder though, is there a higher rung in Maslow’s hierarchy of entrepreneurship than having your colleagues run the entire show? I don’t think so.
Thanks to my wonderful colleagues, I’ve become emboldened in my out-of-office responses too. I no longer provide the exact period of my vacation, but only say when I’ll be back in the office. I tell people that they should email me again once I’m back at work, instead of expecting me to clear and answer all backlogged emails received when I was on vacation. I also tell them to contact a colleague only if they need help with something urgent.
Over the decades, we’ve taken on too much unneeded stress by treating every email addressed to us as our burden to bear.
Vacations and breaks deserve our full and undivided attention. They’re not merely “time when work is not getting done,” but time we invest to have fun, be with family, and explore the world.
It’s not just me saying it, here’s HBR (ha, this is First Principles after all!):
Shift your mindset. First, ditch the myopic view of breaks as time when work is not getting done in favor of one where breaks are time invested into future work and the people who enable it. Instead of counting lost minutes, appreciate the broad returns: employees who perform better after short breaks and, longer term, are less likely to burn out or quit.
[…]
Breaks are not obstacles to greatness. They are part of its foundation. When individuals get the downtime they need, they return sharper, more creative, and more engaged. When teams normalize breaks, they collaborate more effectively and sustain their performance. And when organizations embed recovery into their culture, they unlock long-term resilience, innovation, and retention.
[A Guide to Taking Better Breaks at Work](https://hbr.org/2025/02/a-guide-to-taking-better-breaks-at-work) by Kira Schabram and Christopher M. Barnes
Anyhoo, while settling back into work after the vacation was breezy, back at home we had to face the tragedy of our fully grown mango tree dying. All its leaves—and I mean every single one—had turned brown and curled up. The mangoes that were ripening on its branches were still there, but they were shriveled and browned.
It was a shock. How does a fully grown mango tree (resilient and native to India) just die in a week and a half?
After much research and connecting some earlier signals I’d missed, I am fairly confident it was brought down by the dreaded [Mango Dieback](https://pestsdiseases.com/mango-dieback-disease-management-symptoms-treatment-chemical-biological-natural-and-organic-control/) disease. I think I had missed the earliest signs of it infecting our tree (leaves at the very topmost branches or edges starting to curl up or dry), likely caused by the “bark ringing” that our gardener had done to its trunk earlier this year in an attempt to get it to fruit better. But the heavy rains and humidity when we were away on vacation hastened the disease’s spread. We were left with just this 🙁

1. Questions that force you to think (about health)
2. One not too many
I’m a huge fan of questions. I love asking them. I also love answering great ones, because they make you introspect and think harder. (The best part is no nuclear or coal-fired power plants are required to answer questions—just your own brain!)
This newsletter and the podcast that preceded (and well, succeeded) it, both called First Principles, are based on asking questions and pondering answers. In earlier editions we’ve talked about why [questions](https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/showing-up-asking-questions-and-getting-lucky/) are often more valuable than answers, or why [problems](https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/value-problems-more-than-solutions/) matter more than solutions.
The best questions help uncover people’s feelings and emotions, not merely their clinical answers.
For instance, if I were to ask you: Forget everything you’ve read, heard, or watched about health, fitness, and wellness. If you had to offer up to three pieces of advice to others your age based on what you’ve learnt through personal experience, what would they be?
You can’t answer that immediately. You have to think. Do you offer one piece of advice, two, or three? Are they really based on personal experience? Oh wait, they’re for others your age, so you’ll need to do a double take.
Here’s what my answer was:
1. Build muscle when you’re younger, because it gets progressively harder as you grow older. Like with speed, it’s easier to maintain muscle than it is to develop it.
2. Commit yourself to calorie tracking a few months in a year, just so that your brain is able to understand the true calories in specific foods you eat, and in your daily diet.
3. Once you’re in your 40s, set aside 15–20% of your workout time for activation, mobility, and functional training. Either that, or pay through injuries.
Here’s another follow-up question: Are there any contrarian views you hold strongly around health, fitness, or wellness?
Again, the use of the adjective “contrarian” stops us from rushing to answers. We take it as a challenge. We pick, discard, and sort potential answers in our head. Are they contrarian enough, though? What does it say about us if we cannot come up with even three responses? (That’s me, all the time!)
Here’s what I came up with:
1. Ignore ALL specific types of diets and just focus on total calories and macros.
2. Develop your grip strength. It’s hugely underrated.
3. Post middle age, either focus on building strength/muscle, or endurance/stamina. You cannot excel at both.
Do you feel they are contrarian enough? Do you agree? No?
Okay, answer this then: What are the three biggest issues you struggle with in your quest for health, fitness, or wellness?
Thinking about what you struggle with is hard. Do you admit it? Do you give a soft answer? (As you can gather, I have a fondness—weakness!—for things that come in threes.)
Here’s what I struggle with:
1. Putting on muscle post middle age
2. Avoiding joint injuries from repetitive/high-load stress of strength workouts
3. Losing fat without losing muscle

It’s Hari here, and I’m writing from a cloudy Mysore with… a small library. Who packs five books for an impromptu one-day trip? Apparently, I do. The idea of a trip always triggers me to reach for a book. One isn’t quite enough. Five is… well, indulgent. But having choices is bliss.
I didn’t want to feel like I was carrying dead weight, so I committed to giving each book a fair chance. “I’ll flip through the first 10 pages, at least,” I made a promise to myself as I placed the books in my bag.
Then the journey began. With over an hour to kill on the metro before boarding my non-stop bus to Mysore from Deepanjali Nagar, I split my attention: Misbehaving in my ear (the audiobook) and [Misbehaving](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/26530355-misbehaving?ref=nav_sb_ss_1_11) in my hands. Six chapters later, a budding relationship with behavioural economics bloomed—thanks to Richard Thaler—and I resolved to finally listen to Daniel Kahneman’s [Thinking, Fast and Slow](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/11468377-thinking-fast-and-slow), which was lying deep in my audiobook library.
Metro done, bus bound. It’s still early, I was planning to run in Cubbon today, but instead set new sights on Mysore, so let’s give Murakami another shot. My copy of [What I Talk About When I Talk About Running](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/2195464.What_I_Talk_About_When_I_Talk_About_Running) still bore a deep crease from where I’d last folded a page. I flipped back a few pages and started reading to jog my memory. Soon enough, I was lost in the runner’s high woven into the author’s words.
Sightseeing interlude…
The clouds had turned dark as I made my way back to the bus stop. I decided to rekindle my love for Alain de Botton’s words through [How Proust Can Change Your Life](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/23420.How_Proust_Can_Change_Your_Life?ref=nav_sb_ss_1_10). Discovering another literary figure through the lens of an author you already revere? That’s the perfect way to pick up a book recommendation.
Final tally: Three out of five read—only partially, but it’s progress!

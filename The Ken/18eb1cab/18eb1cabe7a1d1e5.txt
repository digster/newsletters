Good Morning Ishan,

Here’s my one-line summary of 2024 so far: 

Something has fundamentally changed in e-commerce in India. 

Just look at what’s been happening. 

First, 10-minute grocery deliveries have escalated the stakes and dramatically changed e-commerce in the country. Over the past few weeks, I’ve written about the surprising resilience of quick-commerce https://the-ken.com/the-nutgraf/the-surprising-resilience-of-10-min-grocery-deliveries/, and about the audacious decisions made by Swiggy Instamart and Blinkit https://the-ken.com/the-nutgraf/why-is-swiggy-losing-to-zomato-and-blinkit/ to fundamentally change how India buys online. At The Ken, we’ve been tracking this closely—my colleagues have written some incredible stories about how Zomato rewrote the rules using Blinkit https://the-ken.com/story/how-zomatos-blinkit-buyout-rewrote-the-quick-commerce-story/?searchTerm=zomato, how dark stores fare quite favourably https://the-ken.com/tradetricks/what-if-the-quick-commerce-warehouse-was-a-supermarket/?searchTerm=zomato compared to retail outlets, and the implications of q-commerce’s ambitions https://the-ken.com/tradetricks/is-there-anything-e-grocers-wont-sell/ to deliver all categories of products such as beauty, electronics accessories, etc.

Fashion e-commerce growth has slowed down. I’ve already written about the Great Wall of Fashion https://the-ken.com/the-nutgraf/the-great-wall-of-fashion/, and how Myntra, Flipkart Fashion, Meesho, and others are in a place where they’re finding it harder and harder to achieve scale.

This has led to all kinds of consequences. 

Flipkart, India’s largest e-commerce player, has transformed overnight into a wartime company https://the-ken.com/story/inside-flipkart-a-high-pressure-workplace-thanks-to-its-ipo-dreams-and-walmart/. Sometime ago, I wondered what it would take for Flipkart to copy Blinkit https://the-ken.com/the-nutgraf/cant-flipkart-just-copy-blinkit/. A short while after, reports emerged that Flipkart was working on a plan to enter into quick commerce. Flipkart is done taking blows, and has decided to punch back. 

Kirana stores in major cities are also seeing a noticeable impact due to quick commerce. Just last week, The Ken reported that https://the-ken.com/story/blinkit-and-instamart-threaten-kiranas-in-ways-that-bigbazaar-and-amazon-never-could/, “nearly two dozen kirana store owners across cities such as Delhi, Jaipur, Bangalore, Kolkata, Mumbai, Chennai, and Thiruvananthapuram that The Ken spoke with unanimously agreed that the rampant rise of last-minute delivery apps has impacted their sales.” Offline retail being hit means quick commerce isn’t just taking share from online but also expanding the market. So basically, not just winning, but SO MUCH WINNING. 

GMV as a metric has made a comeback. Like I wrote back in February https://the-ken.com/the-nutgraf/the-rise-fall-and-return-of-gmv/, this is because we’re now living in an era where everyone is eating into everyone else’s pie. So, GMV becomes the most reliable way to track actual changes in market share, and is no longer considered the vanity metric that it used to be during an era when the pie was growing fast and so everyone was growing together. 

It’s like a major earthquake has hit e-commerce in India and the shockwaves are spreading through the system. Everyone has been forced to make hard decisions. Roll-up e-commerce platforms such as Mensa Brands have felt the heat and changed their business models https://the-ken.com/story/what-is-roll-up-unicorn-mensa-without-its-acquisition-spree/ at a fundamental level. There are reports that Meesho is ready to raise funding at a lower valuation. Even Tata’s Bigbasket, which isn’t generally known for being particularly aggressive, has read the room and started spinning up its quick-commerce plans. 

This is tectonic stuff.

Amidst all of this, one company has been remarkably silent. 

Where is Amazon India? 

At a time when e-commerce companies are running around with their heads on fire, Amazon India has been busy with other things. 

Here’s the latest news from last week.

Amazon India has notified sellers that it will change its fee structures from April 7, with several categories set to attract higher outgo, depending on the price bracket of the products.

On an average, the overall commission increase is in the range of 10-30% across major segments.

With the cost of servicing each order increasing for sellers, margins per sale will be affected. Merchants may decide to pass on at least a part of the fee hike to consumers, according to industry executives.

Amazon’s communication to sellers, which ET has seen, said the ecommerce company is revising a variety of fees around shipping, referrals and technology costs. In a statement, Amazon India said seller fees and incentives were “strong long-term levers” for individual businesses as well as the marketplace.

Amazon India to revise seller fees from April 7, The Economic Times https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/tech/technology/amazon-india-to-revise-seller-fees-from-april-7/articleshow/108725251.cms?from=mdr

Sure. Why not? Loosely speaking, Amazon India works on a marketplace model, and it has decided that it wants a bigger cut out of every sale that happens on its platform. Companies like Flipkart and Amazon revise seller fees all the time. 

But in this specific case, I’m not sure if Amazon India has got the timing right. 

Look, at one level, this makes sense. Amazon India’s marketplace business barely saw any growth last year https://inc42.com/buzz/amazon-india-marketplaces-loss-widens-33-to-inr-4854-cr-in-fy23/, and its loss increased by 33%. Maybe this seller commission structure is Amazon’s way of saying, “We don’t expect to grow the market, so we might as well cut our losses”. Every internet company in India is focused on profitability, so why should Amazon India be any different?

This strategy would make sense… in 2023. But we’re now in 2024, and the situation has changed radically in e-commerce. I’m not sure Amazon India has grasped the fact that it’s making decisions with a time delay. 

Now, quick-commerce companies like Blinkit, Instamart, and Zepto don’t exactly follow a commission model because they control all the inventory and buy it outright. According to my colleague Aakriti, who tracks this sector closely, margins are much higher on quick commerce. This essentially means that if you’re a seller, you get to keep a bigger percentage of what you sell on Amazon, as opposed to say, Blinkit. 

Here’s a senior quick-commerce executive https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/tech/startups/zepto-blinkit-adding-fashion-electronics-beauty-and-more/articleshow/108185426.cms?from=mdr explaining what brands are thinking.

We’ve seen brands sell the same products cheaper on Amazon because the cost of doing business with quick commerce may be higher. But as we scale up, we are seeing them bring quick commerce to parity… or in a few cases, go more aggressive on quick commerce. But that’s their journey.

But now that there’s a lot of buying on these platforms… and quick commerce has become an important channel, they’re putting a lot more focus.

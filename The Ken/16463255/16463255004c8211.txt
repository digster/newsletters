No technology can go 40 years without hiccups. You know that. Yet, the payments messaging system Swift managed to do so until the cracks began to become public. The most recent instance is the infamous Punjab National Bank fraud which many think used a vulnerability in the Swift system.

It’s not surprising then that some in the distributed ledger technology (DLT) space are trying to push out incumbents like Swift. And where most of them have a hammer and are still in search of a nail, it appears one 14-year-old company may just have hit it on its head with cross-border payments. It is Ripple.

Since Swift isn’t fit to process low-value payments in abundance in many global corridors, it is this underserved market that Ripple is helping banks serve. And Indian Banks are not missing any chance.

Ripple claims it can handle large as well as low-value transactions a lot faster and cheaper than Swift. For a messaging system used by 11K banks worldwide, that’s an existential threat, right? Ripple (and others) may replace Swift as a medium of communication, but can it replace the correspondent banking network?

Swift has been testing its own tech for a while. It even released results earlier this year. But it isn’t ready for large-scale deployment.

How does that position Ripple which is out to disrupt Swift with a two-pronged strategy? Alibaba and Ripple’s former co-founder are close on its heels with their respective products.

Sidhartha has done all the spadework to bring a detailed analysis of what chance Ripple has. Its token, XRP (which rallied so high this year that it made its founders rival Mark Zuckerberg’s wealth), isn’t doing too well though.

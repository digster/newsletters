Trumpism exposes HAL’s Achilles’ heel: speed. Hindustan Aeronautics risks losing both local and global defense deals as military spending surges worldwide
By Seema Singh
6 min read
View in browser

The sonic boom of fighter jets at Hindustan Aeronautics Ltd’s (HAL) manufacturing facilities in Bengaluru and Nashik may have quieted down, but New Delhi’s decision last week was no less sky-ripping.

For the first time, India has cleared the way for private companies to manufacture fighter planes. Until now, if the Indian Air Force (IAF) wanted a fighter jet—or a helicopter, or a transport plane—it had just one option: state-owned HAL.

Defense reform has been in the works for years, but the IAF chief’s rebuke of HAL at the airshow in February over delays in the delivery of Light Combat Aircraft (LCA) was the straw that broke the ministry’s back. That outburst, combined with the government’s freshly announced FY26 budget—the largest allocation this year across all departments at Rs 6.8 lakh crore, with 25% earmarked for the private sector—was enough to tip the scales finally.

And it’s not just India. The world is spending more on defense, a lot more. Ever since Donald Trump won the US presidency back in December and took office in January, global geopolitics and trade have been in turmoil. Countries that once relied on others for protection are being forced to open their wallets—whether voluntarily, like European nations, or under duress, like Taiwan.

After somewhat outsourcing its defense to the US for a good period—Europe’s share of global arms production declined from 2008–2016—the defense spending by European Union member states is expected to rise significantly over the next two years, per a 27 February Goldman Sachs research report. The estimated increase of around $84 billion is equivalent to ~0.5% of the EU’s GDP.

“European manufacturers have joined the global surge in arms production and are now poised to expand at a faster rate than their US counterparts, according to market pricing,” wrote Goldman Sachs.

India’s urgency to attract the private sector for defense manufacturing in general, and fighter planes in particular, must also be seen in this light. Indian private companies’ contribution to defense production has been going up—it was 20.8% in FY24, up from 16.7% in FY23. If they utilise this year’s budget, they will end up with a 25% contribution in FY26, a nearly 10 percentage-point increase in just three years.

HAL, meanwhile, is on borrowed time. The IAF chief’s frustration with its lack of “mission mode” execution isn’t just a domestic problem. For years, European defense manufacturers have relied on India’s maintenance, repair, and overhaul (MRO) services. If private companies deliver faster, HAL could lose that business, too.

Investors seem to know this. As India’s stock market cools off, HAL’s valuation is correcting faster than any major index. And it shouldn’t surprise anyone because at 7.1 market cap-to-sales ratio—down from 12 a few weeks ago—it’s still an extremely overvalued stock. (For context, a ratio between 1 and 1.5 is considered ideal and signals mildly bullish.)

Galloping order book, limping deliveries

If the IAF chief hadn’t chided HAL in public, its delays would have been swept under the tarmac, and domestic brokerages would have continued to tom-tom its order books. It’s estimated to cross Rs 1.3 lakh crore in FY25 and will exceed Rs 2 lakh crore in 2026.

Big numbers, sure. But if you can’t deliver on time, an impressive order book size doesn’t mean much. Not even to the IAF.

And delivery isn’t exactly HAL’s strong suit. According to its FY23 investor presentation, HAL’s pending orders as of April 1, 2022, were Rs 82,154 crore. By the end of that financial year, outstanding orders had barely budged—Rs 81,784 crore. Basically, HAL is running hard just to stay on the same delivery plane.

Then there are the “unbilled revenues”—some pending for over three years—which the company blames on “customer-driven changes to projects” and “delays related to testing and approvals.”

But it’s time HAL and its promoters did some system-level introspection, something even the IAF chief alluded to. For instance, the investor presentation says HAL wants to add one new platform every year “to expand its product profile”. One could argue that it should consolidate its existing footprint and increase the value chain rather than add newer platforms.

For one, HAL sells products to 25 countries, but its export contribution to its revenue is a measly ~Rs 300 crore or 1.1% of its total revenue. Low-brow export game for a company of its vintage, right?

HAL pins the delays on the supply of critical components and spares from foreign companies—including the engine delay from GE for the LCAs—but it hasn’t done enough to get a better grip on LCA manufacturing even after decades of research and development.

Being in Bengaluru, it also hasn’t learnt from ISRO how to work with nimble private companies to address the components pain in its supply chain. (Two of HAL’s important joint ventures, including one with the Tatas, didn’t work out in FY2023.) Now, the increase in FDI limit from 49% to 74% through the automated route will intensify competition with private capital, fueling domestic and foreign participation.

More significantly, India will float a tender—delayed since 2018—for 114 fighter planes any day now. Both Russia (for its fifth-generation Sukhoi-57) and the US (for its F-35) are vying for that deal. Whoever gets the order, it is understood that most of the fighters’ components will be produced in India under India’s defense offsets policy.

How much of that HAL can garner is anybody’s guess. In the past six months, shares of HAL are down more than 30%, compared to Bharat Electronics Ltd’s, another state-owned aerospace and electronics manufacturer, stock’s 17% tumble.

As the world’s biggest arms importer, India’s decision to finally diversify beyond HAL has landed on time.

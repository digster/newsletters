Good Morning Ishan,

In space, no one can hear you scream.

When it comes to India's space communication policy, however, it's a whole other matter. India's Department of Space put out a new draft of the policy in October, and satellite operators, broadcasters, and even officials in other government departments have been vocal in their criticism.

The irony is that this policy was meant not to upset but to appease. According to Indian Space Research Organisation (Isro) chief K Sivan, the push to reform the two-decade-old policy came from none other than Indian PM Narendra Modi himself. He wanted to liberalise the space.

DoS got the memo and swung into action. Sort of. While the proposed policy lowers the entry barriers for low-earth orbit satellite operators like OneWeb or SpaceX's Starlink, geosynchronous satellite operators haven't been as lucky. The policy imposes stringent regulations on them.

Perhaps the most audacious is DoS' demand that these operatorsвЂ”all of whom are foreign, due to India's prevailing space-com policyвЂ”transfer spectrum rights, given to them by their respective governments, to Indian authorities.

All of this could raise satellite service prices by 20-30%, and potentially deter satellite companies from doing business in India. This, even as India's demand for satellite bandwidth outstrips supply 2:1.

So why is DoS being difficult? Perhaps because it has skin in the game. IsroвЂ”which falls under DoSвЂ”accounts for half of India's geosynchronous satellite bandwidth. It is even a mandatory go-between for buyers looking to do business with foreign satellite operators. DoS, therefore, is both regulating and competing with private operators.

Even as DoS hopes to forge ahead, a parallel space communications policy is brewing. This one is from the Department of Telecommunications, which has frequently clashed with DoS' handling of satellite policy. Caught between DoSвЂ™ self-interest and DoTвЂ™s potential intervention is the future of IndiaвЂ™s satellite space.

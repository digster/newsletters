The pyramid structure has underpinned modern knowledge-driven organisations, ranging from strategy consulting to IT services to investment banks. Leadership is the narrow and pointed top, which then expands into numerous managerial layers, and finally there’s a large base of relatively young and inexperienced employees at the bottom.
For the pyramids to work, you need to keep hiring entry-level employees every year, who then learn and grow on the job over time. Many end up quitting, but those who survive rise through the ranks. Some become managers, others eventually become leaders.
N. Chandrasekaran, chairman of Tata Group, joined TCS fresh out of college in 1987. Sandeep Bakshi, ICICI’s CEO, started his career with the bank in 1986. Priya Nair, CEO of Hindustan Unilever Limited, started out as a management trainee with the company in 1995. Amit Syngle, CEO of Asian Paints, joined as a management graduate in 1990.
I could go on, but you get the point.
Some of the best managers of 2035, the best leaders of 2045, and the best CEOs of 2055 are today’s ambitious and eager 25-year-olds looking for their first jobs.
Alas, many of today’s CEOs and managers are figuring out how to not hire ambitious and eager 25-year-olds.

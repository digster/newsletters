A Paytm lesson for Phonepe. RBI’s punishment should have been a body blow for Paytm, but the company has managed to pull through because of one underrated strength

I have a hard time believing companies when they say—“oh, we never look at others”—when we ask them about competition. 

Journalists, especially the competitive ones, have an annoying quirk. We tend to get insanely jealous when a rival publication’s reporter out-scoops us. A day of sulking ensues, then we try to figure out which source the rival reporter could have gotten the exclusive from. And God forbid if it’s a common one. The flipside, of course, is the even more annoying “we broke it first”. 

Anyway, what might seem like a petulant trait is what keeps us on our toes. 

To be competitive is to be human. And if I were among the leaders at Phonepe, I would be paying very close attention to Paytm*. 

After all, Paytm has the one thing that Phonepe doesn’t have enough of.

The secret sauce to payments

Early this week, Paytm (One97 Communications) posted its 2025 financial results. It was the first full year of operations after the blow dealt to its payments bank by the Reserve Bank of India (RBI). And even though the fintech posted Rs 6,900 crore in revenue from operations, a nearly 30% drop from FY24. But its stock price was up 8% after the results because of its consistent recovery over the past year. 

Phonepe has not filed its FY25 numbers yet, but in FY24, it grew over 70% and got to about half the size of Paytm’s Rs 10,000 crore revenue for the period. 

FY25 could see the revenue gulf between Phonepe and Paytm narrow even more. 

Phonepe is also looking to IPO this year, targeting a $15 billion valuation. Sure, that’s a lot less than the $20 billion at which Paytm listed in 2021, but still much much more than what its Noida-based rival is currently trading at. 

Now, Phonepe and Paytm might be in the same business, but they cannot be more different. 

Phonepe has many more users—200 million vs Paytm’s 70 million. Its UPI market share is ~48% vs Paytm’s ~7%. And the “culture” of the two companies are very different. For the longest while, Paytm has been all about the hustle, breaking things and growing fast, while Phonepe has usually been more measured. Moreover, Phonepe has never gotten into any regulatory trouble publicly, while Paytm has had its fair share of RBI visits. 

But if there is one thing that has held Paytm in good stead—something that Phonepe badly needs—it’s Paytm’s quality of merchants. 

After RBI punished Paytm by killing Paytm Payments Bank, it should have been a body blow to the company. But it has managed to survive thanks to having the largest base of merchant customers, some 42 million. But more important is the 35% GMV market share it has by virtue of being an acquirer of merchants—by issuing them QR codes, point of sale machines, smart speakers, etc. This means it’s got better ability than Phonepe to earn recurring rental income from the devices, merchant fees from non-UPI transactions, and income from loans it distributes to its merchants.

Until now, the government has been giving banks and payment apps incentives to power UPI transactions as a way to keep them free for merchants. And the way the incentives flow right now gives us a hint about how UPI MDR (merchant discount rates) will flow if it is eventually introduced. (MDR is the fee banks and payments companies charge merchants for processing their digital payments. Right now, there is no MDR for UPI.)

Phonepe earned Rs 500 crore from the government’s MDR incentives in FY24, while Paytm earned about Rs 290 crore. Phonepe earns these incentives by being more of an issuer powering consumer payments, while Paytm earns its incentives by being more of an acquirer of merchants. But JM Financial says that “despite processing 15% the GMV of Phonepe, Paytm earned roughly 60% the UPI incentive”, showing that it’s far more lucrative to serve merchants than consumers. 

But things aren’t as simple for Phonepe as just going after GMV market share. 

Right now, even though Phonepe has a 48% UPI market share as a consumer payment app, UPI operator National Payments Corporation of India (NPCI) has stayed itself from imposing a 33% market-share cap it announced way back in 2019. 

But as a fintech executive told me recently, if Phonepe grows big in the merchant segment too and gains GMV market share over Paytm, it could become very difficult for NPCI to keep stalling the issue.  

So how far can Phonepe really go with merchants?

Inciting Incident is our weekly newsletter about the most powerful tool of our age—stories. Stories told by businesses, leaders, governments..
Good Morning Ishan,
“And the moral of the story is?”
Children often ask (and are asked) this question at the end of most stories. In the case of my nephew, the assumption is that each story I tell him makes the point that he should be good. Maybe my storytelling tools are too basic, or maybe he has gotten too used to my stories. We will never know.
Whatever the moral in question, all such narratives fall into two broad categories—the subtle version and the in-your-face version. The former tries to induce a shift in the way the audience thinks and feels which eventually nudges them to change their behaviour. The latter straight up asks you to make the choice the storyteller wants. The objective is the same, though: get the audience to do something.
When businesses do this—either to convince customers to make a purchase or investors to send some capital their way—they often weave elaborate narratives that have a lot of complex thought going into them. Does the story accurately define their identity? Does it properly articulate their purpose and vision?
There’s one thing that a lot of them forget to consider, though. And that’s the morality of the actions they want to induce.
Marshall Ganz, executive chair of Harvard Kennedy School’s narrative science programme—Public Narrative: Leadership, Storytelling, and Action—believes this is a critical missing piece.
Because a stance on morality is an imperative for any leader who wants to effect public change.
In his book People Power Change, Ganz says there are three fundamental elements in any story.
Characters and plot, yes, but also morals.
“A story is like a poem. It moves not by how long it is, nor how eloquent or complicated. It moves by offering the experience of specific moments through which we grasp the feeling or insight the poet communicates. The more specific the details we choose to recount, the more we can move our listeners, the more powerfully we can articulate our values, what moral philosopher Charles Taylor calls our “moral sources.” Like a poem, the particularity of a story moment can open a portal to the experience of the transcendent. Stories teach a moral—an experiential moral.”
[…]
“Stories teach what it feels like to deal with adversity, temptation, and arrogance, what it feels like to choose well—or poorly—in response, and where to go to get the courage to choose well—the values. The experience of these stories then becomes a part of the child’s own experience—a moral resource. Bruner describes this as agency training: a way we learn to make mindful choices in the face of uncertainty.”
Excerpt from Marshall Ganz’s “People, Power, Change”, Apple Books
In the [past](https://the-ken.com/incitingincident/coffee-gets-high-on-emotional-appeal-in-india/), I have spoken about how emotions are at the heart of storytelling, because the most compelling stories target the heart.
But Ganz says audiences look for more from public narratives than just an emotional experience. They seek guidance and inspiration, and a leader who shares their social or cultural values. And if a leader is able to weave a story with all three elements, they can launch a challenge, struggle through it, and win.
More specifically, his formula for public narratives is three-fold:
In the first seven minutes of his keynote address to the 2004 Democratic Convention, Barack Obama begins by sharing his story: the choices that shaped his life and the values driving those choices. He then reminds us of our own shared stories: the values that unite us, the choices we have made, our sources of hope. He then confronts us with challenges to those values, enlivens our sources of hope, and inspires us with hope: he describes for us the America that could be, but only if we step up, accept responsibility, and make it happen: a story of now. He wasn’t calling us to choose what he wanted. He was calling us to find the courage to choose what we wanted. He shared a “story of self,” a “story of us,” and a “story of now.” Although he didn’t call it that, this was his public narrative, a talk with which he introduced himself to America. Public narrative wasn’t invented at Harvard. In fact, it’s more of a discovery than an invention. ”
Excerpt from Marshall Ganz’s “People, Power, Change”, Apple Books
When leaders tell stories that inspire people to take up a struggle, and connect them with a purpose and community, they end up doing much more in the process—they give hope; they provide a moral compass; they offer a path to “goodness”.
I admit that much of this is in the political, social, and cultural leadership, but it can be applied to business leadership too. I’ll sign off with an excerpt from a [lecture](https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/news/india/success-beyond-profits-why-business-leaders-should-adopt-a-value-driven-approach-and-prioritize-humanity/articleshow/113396606.cms?from=mdr) delivered at IIM Ahmedabad by Dr Faruk G Patel, chairman and managing director of KP Group.
“At its core, value-driven leadership is about aligning business objectives with ethical practices that benefit employees, customers, and the community at large. This approach moves away from the conventional profit-first mindset, focusing instead on principles like integrity, transparency, and inclusivity. Companies that adopt this strategy cultivate trust and loyalty, crucial factors for long-term success.”

1. Writing to convince yourself first

I’ve known Archit Gupta, the co-founder and CEO of Clear, for close to a decade now. In an earlier life (for me), we worked out of the same office in Bengaluru’s HSR Layout, overlooking Agara Lake. Archit was a few years into Clear (then called Cleartax) and I was starting my first business. His enthusiasm and drive were abundantly apparent and infectious.

Since then, Clear has repositioned itself primarily as a cloud-based software provider for businesses to manage their financial operations. I spoke with Archit in August last year for the First Principles podcast. I also spoke with him a few days ago to ask him about his favourite decision-making mental model.

“Writing,” he replied. “It’s extremely valuable for us. I spend a lot of time doing it. And we try to do it at various levels at Clear—writing one-page memos, three-page memos, six-page memos, PRs, FAQs, etc.” 

This is, of course, the Amazon “narrative thinking” playbook, as Archit himself pointed out to me before I could. “If people like Charlie Munger and Jeff Bezos have figured this out, I don’t think we want to reinvent the wheel,” he added.

“If you were to ask recently hired Amazon employees about what has surprised them most in their time at the company so far, one response would certainly top the list:

“The eerie silence in the first 20 minutes of many meetings.”

At Amazon, after a brief exchange of greetings and chitchat, everyone sits at the table, and the room goes completely silent. Silent, as in not a word. The reason for the silence? A six-page document that everyone must read before discussion begins.

Amazon relies far more on the written word to develop and communicate ideas than most companies, and this difference makes for a huge competitive advantage. In this chapter we’ll talk about how and why Amazon made the transition from the use of PowerPoint (or any other presentation software) to written narratives, and how it has benefited the company—and can benefit yours too.

Amazon uses two main forms of narrative. The first is known as the “six-pager.” It is used to describe, review, or propose just about any type of idea, process, or business. The second narrative form is the PR/FAQ.”

Excerpt from Working Backwards by Colin Bryar

Why is writing so important, I ask Archit. 

“Writing forces clarity, especially around problems. And not just on a whiteboard, which is hand-wavy and not rigourous enough. Often, I’ll ask people to go back and spend more time thinking about problems and writing them down. Have they framed it well? Can they spend more time doing that well before jumping into solutions?” he explains.

We’ve talked about the importance of problems earlier. 

I also ask Archit how he’d broadly allocate the time available to build a new product between problem definition and solution development.

He tells me he’d allocate around 25% to discovering and understanding the problem, and the rest towards building a solution. “And that is 25% of calendar time, not shower time, which all smart people do anyway.”

As someone who gets his best ideas in the shower (my explanation is that it’s the sensory deprivation aspect), I smile, but ask him what he means by calendar time. 

“It means we literally tell teams to calendar a few hours in a week to think, discuss, and frame customer problems without having to worry about solutions. Being allowed to calendar this time means they don’t need to spend extra hours after work or on weekends for this. Often, in startups, people get busy with the day to day execution. I get that it’s how they generate momentum, but it also becomes a reason to not think enough,” he says.

2. The Album Hypothesis

After putting together our last FP Playlist “Spring” (our earlier ones were on “Reflections”, “Journeys”, and “Focus”), I was reflecting about the process of making one. It took me back to the ‘80s and ‘90s, when platforms and playlists didn’t exist and mix tapes required manual curation. 

The mix tape meant something. It represented and said something. Of the effort, of the curation, of the initial songs, of the final song, of the transition songs, of the order of progressions. 

For those of us from that era, our preference for TDK, Maxell, or Sony cassette tapes said something about us. 

Did our mixtape sleeves have handwritten notes or printed ones? What did it say if we broke the record tabs (even though they could be unbroken with some scotch tape)?

All of that has been replaced with the omnipresent sameness of today’s platform algorithms and their machine-generated playlists and self-imprisoning bubbles. Unless we actively resist, we’re constantly being pushed into bubbles labelled “you might like this” or “here’s more like this”.

We (or at least I) so rarely listen to albums these days, preferring “Essentials” playlists. Even within hyper-optimised playlists, we’re always just seconds away from hitting the skip button. Forget giving a whole album a chance, we rarely give even a whole song a chance! 

It’s like stitching together a Stephen King “Essentials” playlist by taking the “best” chapters across 20 of his best books. I know, I am exaggerating, but only a bit. 

In their defence, Apple Music and Spotify have made music so much more accessible and affordable. 

Sure, albums may not exactly be comparable to books. But they did have artistic intent and thought. The songs and their order were how the artists intended us to hear them. 

So, my new resolution since last week is to only listen to albums on Apple Music for an entire month. No “Essentials”, no “Best of”. In an age of algorithms, AIs, and bubbles, it’s my attempt to take back a little bit of agency. An attempt to momentarily zag when we must all zig. 

And in just the first few days of conscious listening, I’ve started “listening” to so much more. For instance, Apple Music’s accompanying notes for artists and albums is wonderful. Here’s what I learned about the fantastic Neil Young from his earliest album, “Harvest”.

“Graham Nash tells a story about visiting Neil Young at his ranch in Northern California in 1971. Young asked Nash if he wanted to hear something, Nash said sure. Young led Nash down to a lake, where the two men got into a rowboat and started rowing. Nash said Young had been a mystery to him his entire life, so having to row out to the middle of a lake to have a conversation made sense. It turns out Young had outfitted his house and adjoining barn with enormous speakers, and the two men sat on the water and listened to Harvest.”

3. The first filters

Welcome to another issue of #SilentSunday photographs, Ishan, it’s Jonathan here. 

Since we published our first #SilentSunday contributions from our click-happy readers all those many months ago, we’ve received nearly 200 entries vying for space in FP editions. For anyone keeping even a cursory track of things, it would be instantly clear that we haven’t published even half of that number in our #SilentSunday section so far. 

Photography is art, and so intensely subjective—what moves you may not move me, after all. But setting aside those many many little things that people love to debate over when it comes to what makes for a good photograph (balance, framing, composition, and whatnot), I think most of us can agree on a few ground qualifiers—a couple of filters you need get through before you can start playing. 

Nearly three-quarters of the pictures we’ve chosen not to carry in First Principles, for instance, have been stymied by two of these checkpoints: 

  1. Too much or too little exposure: The picture was either so dark or saturated with so much light that we couldn’t see much, even the subject
  2. Low resolution: The picture was so highly pixelated that it was difficult to make out the subject of the picture

I understand that much of this depends on the capabilities of the device you are working with, and these days, that’s often a mobile phone that may or may not have a good camera. However, you can avoid at least some of these problems by clicking pictures when there is good but not overpowering light (such as mid-morning and evening) and avoiding very low-light situations unless you know your camera can deliver.

4. Looking at the world a little differently

Dear Ishan, are you stuck in a loop? Because I might be. 

This is Anushka, with your Sunday morning serving of book recommendations from the First Principles community. Allow me to follow up my dramatic opening: this is not about being stuck in the sisyphean ordeal of life, though that might be something a lot of us feel is true. I mean to ask you, as a reader, do you sometimes feel like you’re stuck reading the same sort of books? The same genre or the same style?

Perhaps you only pick up books written by folks in the industry you work in, or something you’ve been deeply interested in for years. Me? I often seek books written by Indian and women writers, fiction or nonfiction. I know I can expect a certain kind of literary output from these books. I know I will end up relating to some aspects of the story, and how I’ll feel by the end. 

This is a powerful loop. Your friends, family, and devices are privy to your interests, and they will inevitably end up influencing your recommendations or your book-store visits. And then, there is the nice, warm, happy anticipation of comfort you get when you just know you’ll enjoy a certain kind of book. 

But this can get boring, sometimes, no? It’s often thrilling—in the most mundane way—when you somehow read something far outside your comfort zone and find yourself loving it.

You may end up finding a new favourite genre. Or you may never read a book like that again. Who cares! It was fun! 

When I first looked through our set of recommendations for this edition, I had to fight the loop. This is a very interesting collection: almost all our books this week ask you to consider looking at the world a little differently. They present, as offerings, evidence from around the world, from the past and present and even from the workings of your own body, to convince you to perhaps change a belief you’ve had for years.

A little different from what I pick up instinctively. But I need a reset, so I’m going to take up one of these recommendations and give it a go (I’ve already made my choice). And I think this could be the perfect set for you to do the same thing.

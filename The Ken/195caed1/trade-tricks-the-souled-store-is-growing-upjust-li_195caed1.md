Trade Tricks
Tue, 25 Mar 25
A weekly newsletter about the biggest changes in commerce—focusing on shifts that matter to you..
Good Morning Ishan,
Back in 2019, when I was still in college and fangirling over Marvel’s Avengers series, The Souled Store was our go-to place to pick up Marvel t-shirts. Our group of friends had rather strict rules about wearing the correct t-shirts to the correct movies, and my designated Marvel outfit was straight out of The Souled Store’s catalogue.
And it was hardly just me; The Souled Store’s t-shirts and t-shirt dresses were all the rage then. Harry Potter, Avengers, Bugs Bunny… if you were a fan (of nearly anything) and wanted to flaunt it, chances were you’d find what you were looking for in The Souled Store.
In fact, the company was among the first in India to sign agreements with entities such as Disney, Warner Bros, IPL teams, Viacom18, etc. to bring licensed merchandise to the Indian market.
Not surprisingly, for a large part of its early journey, from when it launched in 2013 up until 2019 or 2020, more than 80% of its sales came through such licensed merchandise. It’s the segment The Souled Store cut its teeth in.
But the last five years or so have seen a marked shift in these numbers.
I spoke to Vedang Patel, co-founder and executive director, The Souled Store, a few days ago, and he told me the split between licensed and unlicensed products has actually flipped. Now, over 55% of its sales come from non-licensed merchandise.
There are a few reasons why, but the most common thread is this: the customers it built its brand and business on are growing up. And Marvel and Harry Potter t-shirts are no longer enough. They want good, trendy office-wear; comfortable dresses they can wear for an evening out with friends and colleagues.
And unless The Souled Store wants to risk losing them to a Zara or H&M or Zudio, it needs to widen its offerings.
Let me quote Patel here:
“We started realising that we are becoming a lifestyle destination, and the main reason for that is that customers really love the brand. They love the fit, they love the quality… But once they’ve bought all your Harry Potter t-shirts, then what?
So we decided to introduce the non-licensed stuff and saw a lot of traction happening, and organically. People come first for Batman or Harry Potter, but they stay for fit and quality. And building on our non-licensed vertical gives customers more choices once they move past the fan merchandise.”
In fact, it was around the same time as I was shopping for Marvel tees in college that The Souled Store started selling plain, solid colour t-shirts. I loved them. And a little while later, when I began my flannel phase, guess what? I found that The Souled Store had just introduced a flannel line too. The brand was, quite simply, growing up with me.
There are advantages beyond just broader customer appeal for non-licensed merchandise. For one thing, licensed merchandise comes with the added cost of the licence, even if that is largely offset by the lower marketing costs for big fan favourites. “I don’t have to convince anyone to buy Harry Potter. JK Rowling did that,” Patel pointed out. But for smaller, niche segments, things become difficult. “Japanese licenses are still very hard to attain and achieve,” Patel noted, because the segment is still very unorganised and scattered in India, resulting in international brands not taking most such opportunities seriously.
One result of its product expansion is that The Souled Store’s average buyer is now around 27 years old, a shift from the early-twenties of just a couple of years ago. And the brand’s expansion into the offline store channel has had quite a bit to do with that too.
The brand opened its first offline store in 2019, and has 43 outlets across 20 cities right now. And when I walked into one of these stores in Indiranagar, Bengaluru, last week, I saw a far broader age group of shoppers than I might have imagined. 26-year-old Akash said he keeps coming back because he trusts the quality and can pick up a whole outfit here now, something you wouldn’t have been able to even a handful of years ago. 21- and 22-year-old Sneha and Drumil were on a different floor picking up themed t-shirts. They told me they were looking for ones they didn’t have yet, and that the offline store was now a weekend stop for them to add to their t-shirt collections. And 32-year-old Vishith was leaving the store with what I thought was a plain tee, but on closer inspection, had a tiny Popeye logo on it. Vishith is a fan, but it wasn’t his style to sport a full-blown graphic tee, he said.
So cool tees, plain shirts, and something in between—The Souled Store is heading in a direction where it has something to offer every type of fan out there.
“We truly cater to the fan. There are subtle ways to do it too. For instance, a sneaker with a small logo on it. We see that designs which are intricate are more appreciated at stores because on the screen, you really can’t see it.”
If you look at the numbers, there’s no doubt the strategy seems to be working for the brand. The Souled Store turned profitable in FY24 with Rs 360 crore in revenue and a net profit of Rs 18 crore, against a loss of Rs 16.5 crore in FY23. And its sales in the four years up to FY24 have grown at a compound annual growth rate of 65%.
The company now wants to replicate its offline-store success in other markets like the US and the United Arab Emirates, where the market for licensed apparel remains quite niche even now.
Of course, The Souled Store isn’t the only company to have realised the value of catering to what 20-somethings want. Bewakoof, another quirky and relatable D2c casualwear brand, was recently acquired by Aditya Birla Fashion and Retail, while others like Bonkers and Redwolf are following The Souled Store’s lead too. The market for fan-focused prints beyond apparel is also growing. Chumbak, which plays in this space, was recently acquired by Goat Brand Labs.
What The Souled Store has also recognised, though, is that while there’s value in standing out and wooing buyers with quirky offerings, there’s even more value in giving them a few good reasons to stay.

Good Morning Ishan,
 
2019 was a year to forget for grocery delivery company Honestbee, which became Southeast Asia’s startup soap drama.
 
The Singapore-based company was feted widely in recent years for attracting tens of millions of dollars in investment from US venture capital firm Formation 8—which includes a member of the LG dynasty among its founders. The company quickly grew to cover eight countries in Asia, even foraying into food and laundry delivery in parts of Southeast Asia.
 
But this year, the party ground to a halt… literally.
 
Honestbee suspended operations in all countries outside of Singapore as the reality of its financial mess began to emerge. Broke and unable to raise new funding, it laid off most of its employees and ended up in court in Singapore as creditors sought nearly $200 million in unpaid IOUs. 
 
It got worse. That bee, it turns out, may not have been honest.
 
CEO Joel Sng, who stepped down in May to take the fall, is being investigated for past transactions under his management. This includes the alleged purchase of a five-bedroom chalet in the Japanese ski town of Niseko and the acquisition of a company he is said to have founded.
 
But, dear reader, I write to you today with news that things may bee about to improve.
 
Jum’s story today breaks the news of a deal that Honestbee is hatching in the Philippines with JG Summit, a conglomerate that runs an $11 billion business and is controlled by a billionaire-family that ranks third on the country’s rich list.
 
JG Summit was Honestbee’s partner, which helped its supermarkets sell to customers from the comfort of their homes. It plans to throw the troubled company a lifeline in the form of an investment. 
 
The deal could offer a much-needed boost for Honestbee, which has been told by a Singapore court that it has until the end of January to come up with a plan to repay its creditors. If it is concluded successfully, the union with JG could prompt Honestbee to woo strategic investors in other countries in a bid to salvage its wreckage.
 
As we move into 2020, Honestbee’s new executive team—and much shrunken employee base—will hope that the new year brings new hope.

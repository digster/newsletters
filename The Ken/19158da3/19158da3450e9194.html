This week, that story is Swiggy.

The Swiggy of 2024 is a shadow of its former self.

Boxed in by younger, nimbler, and hungrier competitors from all sides, it has been defending itself for so long that it seems to have forgotten how to play offence.

It wasn’t always like this. Swiggy used to define innovation, product chops, and “Bengaluru cool”.

In many ways, it pioneered food delivery in 2014 after pivoting from being a courier service. In fact, Swiggy’s origins and ambitions in logistics ended up becoming a powerful competitive advantage even in food delivery, where professionals and experts praised its operational excellence. The old Swiggy delivered meals faster and better than anyone else.

Zomato, originally a restaurant-discovery company, got into food delivery a year after Swiggy. But initially it worked—and struggled—with partners Grab and Delhivery. Because what Swiggy was pulling off was incredibly challenging. So much so that within just a year, Zomato was shutting down delivery operations in four of the 14 cities it operated in—Lucknow, Kochi, Coimbatore, and Indore.

It may have started as a late follower, but today, Zomato’s market share in the food-delivery space is estimated at 56–57% by Goldman Sachs, with Swiggy in second place.

Then there’s quick commerce.

In 2020, Swiggy was the first to launch a quick-commerce grocery business, which we now know as Instamart. Its promise—groceries in 15–30 minutes—was quite radical then. It led the “dark store”-enabled retail revolution in India, even betting US$700 million from its 2021 US$1.25 billion fund raise on Instamart.

Grofers (which pivoted to Blinkit), Dunzo, Zepto, and Bigbasket Now were all—in a sense—late adopters of a business model Swiggy pioneered.

But then, Zomato bought Blinkit and rapidly integrated and scaled it across India. Once again, it would go on to beat Swiggy in market share. Blinkit is estimated to have a 45% market share in quick commerce, followed by Swiggy at number two.

Even that second place isn’t as stable, given how fast Zepto is raising funds and growing. Zepto’s founders clearly have Swiggy and its market share in their sights.

There’s also Tata-owned Bigbasket, which after years of dithering on quick commerce, has made a hard-right turn and is now fully aligned to the space.

Underpinning all of Swiggy’s business were its apps and products, long considered the gold standard of user experience and design. They were slick, intuitive, fast, and fun. Zomato was always great at its branding and advertising, but Swiggy was the undisputed king of product.

But Swiggy’s apps today are a haphazard and constantly changing collection of sub-products, menu items, offers, and distinct sections.

There is an oft-repeated cliché about how you can surmise the organisational structure and power equations of a company based on the way their product is designed. It’s called “shipping the org structure”, and the Swiggy of 2024 hews pretty close to that description.

That’s it. Let’s switch back to the discussion, and Swiggy.

Praveen Gopal Krishnan: I think the first thought that comes to my mind is (Swiggy) have shipped their org chart.

So when they ship their org chart, what this means is that sometimes, when you open certain apps, you can see that if the products there are not products that are, you know, that make sense to the consumer, but they are actually like various divisions inside the company.

Each of them have their dedicated real estate.

Arnav Gupta: The funniest part is like if you change tabs, the tab bar itself also changes. It feels like the app restarted.

“It’s very intriguing to see no innovation coming from Swiggy,” said a Bengaluru founder to me.

Instead, Zomato’s products, including Blinkit, is now where the innovation is taking place.

Arnav Gupta: The one edge that Zomato, I think, will always have is that Deepi (Deepinder Goyal) is a pain in the ass. I’m saying it in a good way.

Rohin Dharmakumar: What does that mean when you say that?

Arnav Gupta: So I think he’s totally in love with the app himself, first of all, and he really gives a lot of attention to a lot of minute details. And he thinks of how a user who is trying to discover food, thinks in terms of food. A lot of things, like logistics, sales, are not his strong points. And he has hired very good leaders at Zomato who actually look after those things. But he has always been the Chief Product Officer of Zomato from day one, and he still is that. And I still remember, one of the good things about that is that, even when you run a company that big, anybody can come up and think of improvements in the product. The easiest way to ship those at Zomato was just to ping Deepi on chat and say, hey, I think the menu search can be better. And we can search using keywords or something. And he’d be like, yeah, ship it, and next week it is out. Similarly, you go through a bunch of strategic meetings with 10 product managers and engineers, and you build a lot of things, and, finally, the day before launch, Deepi will be like, this looks like crap. He is super opinionated about the product.

How did it come to this?

The price of not running out of money?

In April 2021, Swiggy raised US$1.25 billion at a valuation of US$5.2 billion.

Just a few months later, in July, Zomato went public. Deepinder Goyal, Zomato’s CEO, said the company was running out of cash and had only six months left, so it was either IPO or bust for him.

Swiggy, meanwhile, was on a roll. Six months after Zomato’s listing, in January 2022, it raised another US$700 million at a much higher valuation of US$10.7 billion. In a few months, its valuation would overtake that of Zomato, whose shares had been beaten down to just Rs 40 (US$0.50) apiece on the markets.

Cut to today. Zomato’s valuation is over US$25 billion and Swiggy is reportedly selling pre-IPO shares to high-net worth individuals and family offices at US$9.6 billion.

It’s IPO or bust again, but this time for Sriharsha Majety, Swiggy’s CEO. The tables are now turned.

Deepak Shenoy: I think that narrative is being built right now, or has been built, around quick commerce. And that quick commerce is the next big thing. And therefore, there is a valuation that has been placed. You might think 200,000+ crore for [Zomato] is a lot of money. That would be (close to) Dmart, one of the largest chains, with extremely efficient operations and very high EBITDA margins compared to the rest of the industry. Zomato is nowhere close, not even in the vicinity of having an EBITDA margin. And I say this with all honesty. While they say Adjusted EBITDA, once you remove the word “adjusted”, it is not EBITDA. You know, the A in the EBITDA is adjusted? The problem with this is that, in the end, when you come to public markets, you’re going to have to drive, eventually, on the back of these narratives. If the narrative is strong, you’re going to get valuations. Swiggy’s IPO, I think, comes on the back of these valuations.

Rohin Dharmakumar: What’s the Zomato narrative, which is driving this narrative?

Deepak Shenoy: Quick commerce.

Rohin Dharmakumar: Blinkit.

Deepak Shenoy: Yeah, it’s Blinkit gonna, you know, and that’s, by the way, some of the reasons why I didn’t like the stock is because I thought Blinkit is a terrible operation after the… I just have to say, they paid Rs 3,500 crore for it. They’re already paid 1,000…

Rohin Dharmakumar: But arguably, then, with the benefit of hindsight, one of the best acquisitions…

Deepak Shenoy:  As of today, because we’re getting the narrative, because you’re getting the narrative, it’s okay. I’m a market guy. I accept any narrative. 

Selling the family crown jewel

The Economic Times reported in July that both Amazon and Flipkart had been talking to Swiggy as potential strategic investors. Both were said to have been interested in Swiggy’s quick-commerce business, Instamart. Amazon reportedly even considered buying Instamart. The reason is Zepto.

A lot of investors are looking at Zepto—and valuing it—as a challenger to Amazon and Flipkart. As an eventual pureplay e-commerce company. They have expanded into numerous segments like cafés, electronics, and even clothes, mobile phones.

Quick-commerce rivals like Zepto can keep slicing product after product from Amazon’s and Flipkart’s most profitable categories, offering them to their own customers in 10–20 minutes. Amazon and Flipkart fear getting relegated to “longer tail” items over time, which are more expensive to service and earn lower profits.

That is why Amazon and Flipkart both covet Instamart. They need it to go after Zepto.

But why would Swiggy want to sell?

One person I spoke to said selling off Instamart when buyers are really interested in paying top-dollar for it might allow Swiggy to raise a significant amount of cash, and then become a more focused food-delivery business in an effectively duopolistic market.

The synergy between quick commerce deliveries and food deliveries is overrated, they said.

Deepak Shenoy: If I got US$6 billion for it (Instamart), I’m going to have to pay income tax on maybe capital gains. I’ll call it a capital gains investment. And I pay 20% of that as something. So, maybe I’ve lost enough money so I can offset some of it.

But then, I still have US$6 billion of cash in the company. How do I pay back my investors?

I can do only two things, pay them dividends or do buybacks, both of which are taxed at 40% effectively.

With the benefit of hindsight, did Swiggy get too comfortable because of the ZIRP era and close to US$2 billion from investors in a short span? As a consequence, did they miss the brutal, but ideal, IPO window?

Swiggy, today, is being valued at approximately 40% of Zomato’s valuation by private investors. When Swiggy goes public, will public investors correct that upwards, or push it further down? Zomato “paid the price” for going public when its shares were battered badly. Does Swiggy have the stomach for that?

The signs aren’t good.

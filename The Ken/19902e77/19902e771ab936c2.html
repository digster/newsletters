An undercurrent of mild shock is palpable in most reactions to India’s nationwide shift to E20 petrol, by now almost a month old. A month that’s been filled with reports and rumors about falling mileage, increased chances of damage to vehicle parts, increased maintenance costs, voided warranties, and no insurance coverage. 

It’s clear from the steadily growing outrage, as one possible cost of the transition after another becomes more common knowledge, that India’s vehicle owners are feeling woefully unprepared to deal with the change. 

The number of those impacted isn’t small either. 

India’s automakers began rolling out vehicles ready to run on E20 petrol—or 20% ethanol-blended petrol—only in 2023. Almost every petrol vehicle sold before that year is E20 non-compliant. 

To put it in numbers, that’s about 240 million vehicles in total; 40 million passenger cars. Almost every owner of which, in the last month, has had to start brushing up on what putting E20 petrol in a non-E20-compliant car would do to their vehicles. 

What they’ve been finding is a lot of costs. And little to make up for it. 

“If petrol is Rs 100, E20 should be Rs 95. After all, the government is saving on imports… the customers should be offered E20 Petrol at 5% less to motivate them to shift to greener fuel,” Vinkesh Gulati, a former president of FADA (India’s largest dealers’ association) said last week. But, nothing like that has happened. 

The result is an almost entirely (and unpleasantly) surprised cohort of vehicle owners, numbering in the millions and expected to swallow significant extra costs if they want to keep using their vehicles.

Their surprise isn’t… surprising. 

India’s transition to E20 petrol has taken place at express speed, to say the least. 

The government’s original National Biofuels Policy 2018 aimed for a far more sedate transition, marking full E20 adoption only for 2030. It wasn’t until June 2021 that this date was unceremoniously yanked forward to 2025. At that point, ethanol blending in petrol nationwide was around 8.5%. In a little over three years, the government decided, it would take that level to 20%.

To say this was ambitious was an understatement. This would mean India would blow past intermediate stages like E12 and E15. An entire ecosystem—from oil refiners and oil retailers to automobile manufacturers across categories—would hold hands and make a collective jump, speedily skipping an entire stage in fuel evolution. 

You may wonder at the source of such confidence, but there was actually a reasonably decent one. 

You see, India had accomplished something very similar just a year earlier.

One jump to another

In April 2020, India made the transition from selling older Bharat Stage 4 (BS4) automobiles to BS6, skipping the BS5 checkpoint altogether. 

Talk of this began in 2015, when rapidly growing pollution in Indian cities was regular headline news and global initiatives to roll out more stringent emissions norms were starting to gain serious traction. Moving from BS4 emissions standards directly to BS6 emissions standards would be tough, experts noted, especially on automobile manufacturers, but the pay off would be in significantly lower emissions. 

By 2016, the government had announced this was exactly what it would do. By 2020, Indian automakers would convert their range of models from BS4-compliant to BS6-compliant. 

There was some opposition, with automakers pointing out that this would require significant investment to upgrade production lines and that the cost of new vehicles would increase for customers. Oil companies would also have to invest in making their fuels BS6-compliant. Estimates put the total cost of the transition from Rs 60,000 crore to as much as Rs 1 lakh crore.

But, with a little help from the Supreme Court, the transition was achieved on time. Automakers passed on the cost—at least partially—to consumers through gradual price hikes. But there was little popular outrage. 

Perhaps this is what convinced the government that it could pull off another such jump, when less than a year later, it announced that it would pull the E20 transition forward by a whole five years.

But that was probably the wrong lesson. 

Unlike the BS6 jump, where the owners of existing non-compliant vehicles saw little negative impact and most of the cost was borne by OEMs, the E20 transition is opening older-vehicle owners to a host of unpleasant possibilities. 

First, there may be mileage losses ranging from 2% to 20% depending on the car’s emission standard and age, according to experts I spoke to. Older vehicles need upgrades if they are to run efficiently on E20—tanks, hoses, injectors, ECUs all have to be replaced or adjusted. Maruti Suzuki, for instance, is offering an E20 upgrade kit priced between Rs 4,000 and Rs 6,000; labour costs are extra. And some automakers are charging far more. 

Automotive experts we spoke to say this is not a one-time spend. Every five to seven years, some level of maintenance will be necessary, and depending on the vehicle, the total bill could range from Rs 5,000 to Rs 25,000. 

Then, there’s the increased danger from water exposure:

“Ethanol is hygroscopic. It can absorb water, which in turn can corrode the fuel pump, fuel tank, and fuel pipes. In coastal cities like Chennai and Mumbai, or in India’s long monsoon months, the chances of such things happening go up.”

Puneet Gupta, director of S&P Global Mobility

Experts also say that vehicle owners risk voiding their warranties for vehicle damage from E20 use, and that insurance won’t cover such instances. 

A former NITI Aayog executive estimates that even at conservative assumptions, E20 upgrades could collectively cost Indian motorists more than Rs 1 lakh crore.

Course correction

Back in March 2023, our colleague Seema wrote about how—despite pulling forward its deadline—the government seemed to be making little effort to educate motorists about the coming shift and how they could prepare for it. To quote her:

I carried out a minor recce this weekend, visiting four car showrooms and two service centres in north Bengaluru. (I’d not like to name the brands because it was not random sampling, it clearly had a proximity bias.)

None of those places could answer what a vehicle owner needs to do now to deal with the fuel transition. A Japanese car dealer tells me they have received circulars about the new OBD (On Board Diagnostics) Stage II norms, which also comes into effect from 1 April—which applies to only manufacturing—but nothing about E20.

This gap in awareness is certainly one reason why the government is facing such public criticism over its move. And while it may be a case of locking the barn door after the horse has bolted, there is still value in taking a serious shot at increasing awareness about the impact on non-compliant vehicles, and how to best manage the negative fallout. 

Another favourable intervention would be to do what Gulati says: make E20 petrol cheaper, and transfer some of the import savings that the government has been touting to consumers. The maths would support that view. In Bengaluru, ethanol costs about Rs 67 a litre compared to petrol at Rs 103. Yet, none of these savings have been passed on to consumers.

There are also other costs the government should be looking at subsidising to reduce the strain of such a rapid transition. 

Fuel stations need upgraded tanks, pipes, and pumps to handle E20, all of which would require significant capital expenditure. 

So do automakers who are working on flex-fuel models capable of running better on E20. Tata Motor, Mahindra and Mahindra, and Maruti Suzuki have all just unveiled flex fuel-based engines for certain models such as Punch, 3Xo, and Wagon R. But wholesale upgrades to product portfolios require significant investment, and unlike EVs, which have been a policy priority for a while, ethanol-friendly engines receive no subsidies. In the absence of government support, all of the costs may end up being transferred to customers. 

Also, having taken the E20 plunge, India’s government should pay close attention to how it will ensure a steady and adequate supply of ethanol. 

Ethanol in India is primarily made from sugarcane and paddy, which together consume nearly 70% of the country’s irrigation water. A NITI Aayog task force has already flagged the unsustainability of this approach. And while the government has begun promoting maize and other cereals for ethanol feedstock, and second-generation biofuels made from crop residue and waste, such infrastructure is still at a nascent stage. 

For all the opposition it’s seen this last month, and the challenges mentioned above, ethanol is not such a bad bet. It is domestically produced, increases payouts to farmers, and is strategically safer than imported oil. And India has shown it can move fast on energy transitions.

But speed doesn’t always make up for other problems. 

And it’s past time to address them.

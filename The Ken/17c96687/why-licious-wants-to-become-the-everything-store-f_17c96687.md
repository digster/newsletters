Till a few years ago, if you wanted to buy something meaty that you could quickly throw on a pan or into the microwave, your choices were mostly limited to chicken nuggets and lollipops.
But every time I saw nuggets in the freezer at a grocery store, I was reminded of what Anthony Bourdain said about the McNugget being the worst thing he had ever eaten. And I was never crazy about lollipops.
It's a different, tastier world now, though.
From beef—sorry, buff—patties and pork chops to mutton seekh kebabs and crumb-coated prawns, it's a smorgasbord out there.
This, and not raw meat, is where all the action is for online meat delivery startups. Just ask Licious, a recently anointed unicorn fresh off a $52 million fundraise.
Within two years of its launch, the company's high-margin ready-to-cook (RTC) and ready-to-eat (RTE) products are already a Rs 200 crore ($27 million) category—that's 20% of Licious' total expected business for the year ending March 2022.
If Venky's and Godrej Tyson's Real Good are the brands you associate with packaged raw meat, Licious wants to be exactly that for RTC/RTE offerings. But the problem is that its rivals such as Meatigo want the same, too.
And Licious is not alone in its omnichannel quest, either, as Bhumika writes in today's feature. Its Chennai-based peer Fipola already has 40 stores and another 15 in the works. Licious may have been the first of its kind to get to a billion-dollar valuation, but the contest in the online meat delivery world is still wide open. Let's savour the story:

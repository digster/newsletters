Peter Thiel is a legend in Silicon Valley. He’s a tech billionaire who co-founded companies like PayPal and Palantir, and has funded companies like Facebook, Airbnb, and SpaceX.
In his bestselling book, Zero to One: Notes on Startups, or How to Build the Future, he writes about the single best interview question he usually asks candidates to decide on whether to hire or fund their company:
What important truth do very few people agree with you on?
According to Thiel, this is why he asks this question:
“This question sounds easy because it’s straightforward. Actually, it’s very hard to answer. It’s intellectually difficult because the knowledge that everyone is taught in school is by definition agreed upon. And it’s psychologically difficult because anyone trying to answer must say something she knows to be unpopular. Brilliant thinking is rare, but courage is in even shorter supply than genius.”
I don’t know if you’ve noticed, but for the last few months, we’ve been running a series of stories at The Ken. We call it: Counterthesis. Essentially, the premise is this—there’s the conventional wisdom, and then there’s the unconventional wisdom. And we’ve gone and asked founders, CEOs, and leaders a version of Thiel’s famous interview question—what is your counterthesis? The answers have been incredible.
Today we have another counterthesis. The conventional wisdom is that the biggest growth in India’s electric-vehicle (EV) market will come from two-wheelers. And, until now, that has been the case. The thesis is that the way to win is by getting to the market as quickly as possible and grabbing customers.
Well, Amitabh Saran, co-founder of Altigreen—an EV startup—has a different point of view.
Seema speaks to Amitabh, who believes that the EV market is going to be disrupted by three-wheelers, and not two-wheelers. He says that, unlike two-wheelers, electric three-wheeler companies have had to engineer products for the Indian market. And that electric three-wheelers have a much longer tail.
Here’s the kicker: he believes that we need to have showrooms for three-wheelers because it matters. This is Amitabh Saran’s truth, which very few people agree with him on.
Do you?

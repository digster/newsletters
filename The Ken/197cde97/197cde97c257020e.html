Phonepe is fervently preparing to go public this year, and in the run up to the listing, India’s largest payments app has launched its first co-branded credit card in partnership with HDFC Bank. The card will be powered by Rupay, the card network operated by retail payments body National Payments Corporation of India (NPCI). 

The new card allows users to make UPI payments through a credit card instead of tapping into their bank balances, a feature allowed by the NPCI as early as 2022. 

For Phonepe and its bank partner, this opens up many attractive possibilities.

Cobranded cards like these drive stickiness among customers, which in turn cranks up the number of transactions. And it’s a way for the bank to get more customers by riding on the popularity of Phonepe.

But there is more. The credit card can be an important tool in growing Phonepe’s lending business, which it appears to be getting more aggressive about. If you are a Phonepe user, you may have noticed the app nudging you towards loans even if you aren’t really looking for one. Some of the company’s other products, such as insurance, have not contributed much to its topline—most of its Rs 5,000 crore revenue still comes from its payments business.

So a lot rides on this. 

When you become a cobrand partner, you earn from much more than just distributing credit cards to users, which Phonepe has been doing thus far. A credit-card industry executive who did not wish to be named says that it could create a business that rakes in hundreds of crores from ongoing spends on the card. 

What’s really curious, in fact, is just how long it took Phonepe to launch this.

With 150 million active users, one would have thought Phonepe would be among the first companies in India to enter into a co-branding partnership, especially after NPCI allowed credit card-backed UPI payments. After all, many other companies of similar size have co-branded cards now. Amazon, with about 200 million monthly active users, has one of India’s most popular co-branded credit cards with ICICI Bank. Swiggy, with just around 18 million monthly active users, also has a successful one in partnership with HDFC Bank. 

Turns out, the payments app was waiting for approvals from the Reserve Bank of India (RBI) for nearly 10 months to launch its card, according to a well-placed payment executive.

The RBI has been quite particular about the kind of products that payment apps launch and distribute. And given Phonepe’s size, there is always the concern of concentration risk—the payments firm processes 50% of all UPI transactions by value, and 47% by volume. When Google Pay wanted to let its users open fixed deposits issued by Equitas Bank on its app, for instance, the RBI prevented it. 

But Phonepe has approval now, and the card ends up giving it all the leverage it needs to stave away any conversations about its growing market share or size—which are concerns that the NPCI has expressed earlier. If anything, this move could cement Phonepe’s high UPI market share, because if you have a credit card that incentivises using the app, it ensures that more customers transact on Phonepe.

Can NPCI bottle lightning twice?

Phonepe’s new credit card isn’t just important for Phonepe but also NPCI.

NPCI’s longstanding ambition has been to beat Visa and Mastercard in the card infrastructure game. That’s why when NPCI allowed credit cards on UPI, they could only be on the Rupay card network. Visa and Mastercard were kept out.

But so far, Rupay remains well behind Visa and Mastercard in India. In 2024, it had only a 15% market share in credit cards. 

Phonepe played a large role in UPI becoming the behemoth it is currently, so NPCI could use some of the same magic for Rupay credit cards too. 

But for that to happen, the card needs to be an attractive one.

What does the new card bring to the fight?

The Phonepe credit card is positioned as a premium offering, since it has an annual fee of Rs 999.

They had me at 10% reward points on Phonepe spends, but when I looked closer, I realised this is 10% reward points on the money I spend within the Phonepe app, not on the UPI payments that I’ve initiated on other platforms via Phonepe.

For example, if you paid your mobile bill within Phonepe’s app, that will fetch you 10% in reward points. But if you order food on Swiggy and choose Phonepe to make a payment, it will not be eligible for the reward.

When you compare this to popular cobranded cards like the Swiggy-HDFC card or the ICICI-Amazon card, their rewards seem more attractive than what Phonepe is offering. For one, there is no “reclaiming the reward point” hassle; you get flat cashback in your Amazon or Swiggy wallet. Plus when Swiggy offers a 10% cashback on Swiggy, it’s a stronger proposition than the 10% rewards on Phonepe spends, because most spends within Phonepe may be for more monthly use cases such as mobile or internet bills. Users are likely to spend more frequently within the Amazon or Swiggy platforms.

That said, the Swiggy and Amazon cards cannot be used to make UPI payments, since they are not powered by Rupay. So Phonepe may get away with not having to match the most premium cards out there. 

But for the sake of both Phonepe and NPCI, how valuable Phonepe’s users find the new card will be crucial to the future success of their pet projects.

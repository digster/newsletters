Good Morning Ishan,
Rishi Gupta has had a rollercoaster of a month.
Just last month, the banking regulator approved Gupta to continue as CEO and MD of Fino Payments Bank for the next three years. A month later, he was arrested.
Last week, the country’s tax police alleged that Gupta enabled online real-money gaming firms to evade taxes, and took him into custody at 3:55 a.m. on 27 February.
Until now, the tax authorities had pursued promoters of biryani chains and local businessmen. This is the first time they have arrested the CEO of a listed company over tax avoidance. (Incidentally, Fino is the first payments bank in the country to pay taxes.)
The tax inspectors believe Gupta was one of the “masterminds” behind moving nearly Rs 3,000 crore through a network of entities that helped onboard fake merchants, resulting in an estimated loss of about Rs 840 crore in tax revenue to the exchequer.
The Hyderabad unit of the tax intelligence department has implied multiple violations in this case, including money laundering, onboarding gaps, improper KYC, all culminating in tax evasion.
Here’s how events unfolded for Gupta.
On 9 February, GST officials sought transaction data from the payments bank. On 26 February, officials visited Fino’s office to gather more information related to the current financial year. That same evening, the Hyderabad unit issued a summons to Fino’s MD and CEO. By early next morning, the bank learned that officials had placed him under arrest.
In its statement, Fino denied all charges made by the tax intelligence officials. The bank’s share price has fallen 11% since the arrest. The bank, which serves 16 million customers and holds nearly Rs 2,500 crore in deposits as of the December quarter, was among the first payments banks to receive in-principle approval to convert into a small finance bank.
An arrest of a CEO means that the tax department possesses evidence that Gupta was personally complicit in the tax crime. Gupta has moved the Telangana High Court [challenging](https://www.storyboard18.com/gaming-news/fino-payments-bank-md-rishi-gupta-moves-telangana-hc-challenging-gst-arrest-in-tax-evasion-probe-91207.htm) his arrest, and the bank has denied that either he or the institution played any role in the alleged tax evasion. It is rare for bank CEOs to be held directly liable. And when they are, authorities typically have strong reasons.
For instance, in 2019, when the managing director of the beleaguered Punjab and Maharashtra Cooperative Bank (PMC), Joy Thomas, was arrested, it was because he was [directly](https://www.moneylife.in/article/pmc-bank-fraud-exmd-joy-thomas-became-junaid-khan-for-second-marriage-say-reports/58404.html) involved in allocating 76% of the bank’s loan book to Housing Development and Infrastructure Limited (HDIL) and then hid 44 bad loan accounts of HDIL—which had turned into non-performing assets (NPAs)—by creating nearly 21,000 fake accounts.
The Ken saw a copy of the arrest memo for Rishi Gupta. The memo lays out the various entities involved in the alleged tax evasion and refers to Gupta as a “mastermind”, but it lacks specific evidence showing how Gupta was behind the crime or his connection to the so-called program managers or the 36 shell entities they onboarded.
In the memo, tax officers stated that Fino worked with three program managers:
PS Rao Digital Solutions, Billexpress Solutions, and Powerfin Technology Private Limited. These program managers are payment service providers who identify merchants and refer them to the bank for onboarding, while also providing end-to-end payment services for UPI transactions.
These three intermediaries contributed approximately 8–10% of its overall UPI transaction volumes for the year, the bank said in an investor call. The bank also said that multiple banks worked with these program managers, not just Fino.
The bank added that its role is not to monitor the GST payment of its program managers, and it cannot be held responsible for its program managers’ tax evasion. Managers, which the GST department found to be dummy entities.
If these entities were indeed fake, then the problem is not Fino’s alone. Fino said it checks if the merchants referred by the program managers have an existing banking relationship with other banks for facilitating UPI transactions.
This means the GST department is also raising questions about RBI’s audit of banks’ onboarding frameworks. After all, the banking regulator audits the onboarding process of all the financial institutions it regulates.
The arrest memo extends the bank’s liability even beyond due diligence during onboarding. It states “the bank was responsible for periodic inspections and audits of its merchant for associated risk monitoring. However, they have failed in their duty.”
RBI is now looking [into](https://www.moneycontrol.com/news/business/mc-exclusive-rbi-may-put-on-hold-fino-s-sfb-conversion-plans-13847446.html) the bank’s conversion into a small finance bank, according to Moneycontrol.
Gupta’s arrest has got the industry [calling](https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/industry/banking/finance/banking/fino-payments-bank-ceo-arrested-fm-sitharaman-to-check-gst-evasion-case/articleshow/128881660.cms?from=mdr) for deeper scrutiny of the tax enforcement department’s actions. But some are worried about the possible repercussions.
“All this social media attention is only going to make it worse, right now it is about proving who is right rather than facts of the case,” said a fintech founder. He added that his instructions to his team, should something like this ever happen, is to simply sort it out in the courts. “You don’t want to be caught in a public ego war,” he says.
So far, the RBI has penalised entities for KYC violations with fines, onboarding bans, or account freezes, resulting in business losses. But this arrest as a result of onboarding gaps (deliberate or not) is now sending shivers down executives’ spines. “If onboarding gaps are going to result in tax evasion further leading to arrests, then all bank CEOs will end up being arrested,” said the fintech founder.
And arrests like this will only make things worse for customers.
“If banks are asked to police their customers, then people and firms can be refused banking services based on non-justiceable no-appellate grounds. Bank executives will protect their own seats instead of serving customers. And for those customers, the consequences will be devastating,” said a consultant to banks.

Good Morning Ishan,
Private-equity math usually sounds simple enough: buy a fast-growing consumer company, install professional managers, pump the numbers, and flip it for 3X the price. In 2021, KKR thought it had found its Indian poster child in Vini Cosmetics, the maker of Fogg deodorant, which had humiliated Axe and grabbed over 20% of the market. The Patels, its Gujarati founder-brothers, pocketed hundreds of millions, KKR paid $625 million, and everyone assumed the story would smell like success.
Instead, it reeked.
KKR treated perfume like enterprise software—by stuffing distributors with more stock than the market could possibly absorb, calling those shipments “sales”, and declaring growth. Unsurprisingly, prices collapsed, margins shrank, and the bina gas wala spray started spraying trouble. To protect profitability, KKR also cut Fogg’s ad budget.
Meanwhile, Denver, the former also-ran, hired Shah Rukh Khan and tripled its market share.
If this wasn’t chaotic enough, KKR wanted the Patels to cede control to a new professional CEO, while the Patels wanted to keep running their business as they always had. No one was fully in charge, everyone interfered, and eventually the entire professional team quit, leaving the founders back at the wheel with KKR nodding along.
Today, Vini still has one hit product from 2011, no new breakout launches, weaker margins, and a PE owner that can’t quite admit its biggest Indian consumer bet spells regret more than reward.

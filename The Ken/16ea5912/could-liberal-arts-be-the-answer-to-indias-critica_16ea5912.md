You’ll need to forgive me for using yet another Matrix dialogue in an email. But do you remember the scene when Neo (Keanu Reeves) wakes up - I mean, truly wakes up - inside the real world, all pale and withered away and plugged full of acupuncture needles?
Neo: What are you doing?
Morpheus: Your muscles have atrophied, we’re rebuilding them.
Neo: Why do my eyes hurt?
Morpheus: You’ve never used them before.
If you replace “muscles” with “critical thinking", the bulk of students entering college in India face a similar situation. They’ve never been taught, much less encouraged to use it. Most schools, with the exception of “alternate” schools (a euphemism to describe schools that don’t focus on by-rote learning) or international schools following the IB curriculum, push children through an 10-12 year assembly line of textbooks, syllabi, exams and tests.
So when those children land at the gates of Ashoka University in a remote, dusty corner of Haryana, their critical thinking muscles need to be rebuilt.
Through a combination of foundation courses and critical thinking seminars, Ashoka’s curriculum targets development in two distinct, yet complementary, directions. Foundation courses give a wide flavour of disciplines that range from literature to mathematical thinking. Great Books, among the most popular of these courses, involves reading at least eight different texts in one semester, that connects authors and ideas across centuries, and languages. Alongside Great Books, students also have to take courses like Political and Social Formations, History of Indian Civilisation and Introduction to Mathematical Thinking. They are meant to expose students to key intellectual discourses within different disciplines. Classes at Ashoka are usually 1.5 hours, of which a good chunk—almost 40 minutes—is reserved for open discussions and class presentations. Students are encouraged to debate concepts and texts in smaller groups, and even take these discussions outside the classroom.
Part Ivy league, part Oxbridge, its curriculum and pedagogy are unique against the Indian clamour for “professional” degrees. Ashoka University also turns around the liberal arts stigma on its head by charging roughly 10 times what a publicly funded college might.
In the concluding part of our series on India’s most ambitious private attempt to build college from scratch, Olina goes inside Ashoka’s attempts to reengineer an education for tomorrow. In a world filled with disruption, uncertainty and fluid careers, could critical thinking be the skill students need? And can Ashoka be the one to provide that, in the face of regulatory antagonism?

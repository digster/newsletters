I’d be lying if I said we always know what our subscribers want. But every long-form story at The Ken begins with the objective of delivering value in the ten minutes they give us every morning. So it’s a great feeling when our subscribers not only read some of our stories in large numbers but also share them widely.

These are among our most-read and most-gifted stories of 2024:

“Where is Kunal Shah?” ask most Cred employees

Cred burst onto the fintech scene thanks in no small measure to its founder Kunal Shah’s insistence that his employees be self-reliant. But Rounak argued in the story that the same hands-off managerial style turned out to be counterproductive when Cred battled problems on multiple fronts.

Tiger Global’s downturn hits Bain India. One in every five consultants loses job

Deeply reported stories of the Big Three of consulting are hard to come by. But Abhirami managed to unpack, with some telling details, the impact of the dwindling fortunes of Tiger Global and its portfolio companies on Bain & Company’s India office.

Why RBl killed Paytm Payments Bank

In a veritable death sentence, India’s central bank ordered Paytm Payments Bank to halt its business. Arundhati and Rounak joined forces to understand the motivations behind one of the biggest jolts to India’s fintech space in 2024.

​

India’s ‘other Warren Buffett’ won’t stop selling while everyone’s still buying

Pulak Prasad’s Nalanda Capital has earned a reputation for being a “permanent investor” in companies. This was reason enough for Anand to ask if Nalanda’s exit from more than a dozen companies was the canary in the coal mine for an overheated market.

Why more 40-somethings are becoming ‘unwilling retirees’

The foundation of Vanita’s alarming story was a survey we ran to identify the extent of involuntary retirement among those in their 40s and 50s. As she wrote, “The financial prospect of retirement at 40 is already a bust. Employers are caught between a rock and a hard place. And for those hit by the silver tsunami, their chances for a fulfilling career are slipping away even though they still have much to offer.”

Great Place to Work… or not? A CEO’s ouster sparks trust crisis

Great Place to Work is in the global vanguard of workplace culture and diversity, equity, and inclusion. So it was somewhat ironic, as Abhirami detailed in the story, that the chief executive of the consultancy’s India unit found that it wasn’t a great place to work at when she was summarily dismissed.

One in 10 stocks is valued at 100X of profits in this crazy bull run. You caused this!

Anand peeled back the curtain on the irrational exuberance in India’s stock market by putting the individual investor at the centre of the bull run.

You need to download Digiyatra again. But it’s less about a tech upgrade and more about a ‘scam’

When the foundation behind Digiyatra—a contactless, biometric system for checking in at airports—said travellers had to download a new app, it cited a potential surge in usage as the reason. But Rounak showed otherwise in this exclusive.

Masters’ Union touted placements to rival IIMs. Now, it struggles to place its students

As a four-year-old alternative MBA school, Masters’ Union knew that the only way it could make its presence felt was to talk up its placements. And it sure did. But this year turned out to be particularly hard on that front, as Alifiya chronicled in great detail.

Inside Flipkart: a high-pressure workplace thanks to its IPO dreams. And Walmart

Flipkart CEO Kalyan Krishnamurthy dialled up internal targets in order for the e-commerce major to grow amid intense competition and reach for elusive profits on the road to an IPO. Not surprisingly, his employees felt the heat, which Nuha shone a light on with her reporting chops.

The death of the independent doctor

In her evocative story, Alifiya talks to young doctors about the diminishing prospects of running a private practice. Competition from hospital chains, prohibitive costs, and the pressure of online ratings have combined to make life hell for them.

Visa slots for sale: how Indian travel agents game VFS, consulates

Anyone who has applied for a US or Schengen visa after the pandemic knows what an ordeal it can be. You can travel around the world in the time it takes to get the visa. But there are travel agents who can ease your pain and take you to the front of the queue quickly—all for a fee, of course. Nuha broke down the modus operandi in this story.

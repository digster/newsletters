Indigo’s crown of thorns

DGCA data shows that in October 2024, the airline’s cancellation rate of 0.78% was much lower than that of Spicejet and Vistara and only somewhat higher than Air India. Its number of complaints per 10,000 passengers—at 0.2—was also far lower than Air India’s 0.6 and Akasa Air’s 0.5. Besides, Indigo’s on-time performance at four metro airports (about 72%) was better than that of the competition.

But here’s the thing. In absolute terms, a far greater number of fliers were hurt by Indigo’s cancellations and delays. Nearly 24,000 passengers were affected by the airline’s cancellations in October, and close to 100,000 fliers were stranded by flight delays longer than two hours. This is far worse than the travails inflicted on passengers by rivals, including its closest competitors, Air India and Vistara.

5G is slowing in India. Airtel is speeding up spending

Airtel didn’t exactly dive into 5G—it dipped a toe in and called it a cannonball. Rather than splurging equally on infrastructure and spectrum, it hedged its bets.

Now, it’s catching up.

And yet, Airtel still delivers the “best 5G experience,” according to Opensignal. How? Airtel’s subscriber base, while smaller than Jio’s, is more curated—premium users and fewer freeloaders. Jio, with its standalone (SA) 5G architecture that’s technically superior, suffers from its size: Jio has 75 million more subscribers than Airtel, many of them low-revenue, dragging down average speeds. It’s a classic tortoise-versus-hare dynamic, except the tortoise has a higher Arpu.

Tata-owned Indian Hotels’ art of being all things to all people

Famed for its Taj chain of top-tier hotels, the Tata Group enterprise is now as much about its cheaper variants, with none as prominent as Ginger, its budget-hotel brand.

With 70 hotels, Ginger is second only to Taj in footprint. Sooner than later, it’ll eclipse the latter.

A room at Ginger costs under Rs 4,000 a night on average. Staying at one of Taj’s palace-turned-hotels can set you back by over Rs 50,000. Two wildly divergent experiences. Not to mention a whole bunch of options in between. All offered by a single company. Whoever said you can’t be all things to all people?

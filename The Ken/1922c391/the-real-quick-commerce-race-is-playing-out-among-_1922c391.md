Good Morning Ishan,
Let’s start with a few numbers:
Zomato’s Blinkit: 640. Swiggy Instamart: 500. Zepto: 400.
What are these digits? That’s how many “dark stores” India’s quick-commerce giants are currently running, give or take.
Their target? A 1,000 each by the end of the year.
Now, if you were one of these platforms, trying to set up your next dark store—a small, hidden warehouse packed with groceries, gadgets, and avocados—you wouldn’t just toss a dart at a map. You’d obsess. You’d drill down to pin-code-level data, study delivery patterns, and argue with your real-estate team over how even 200 metres off could wreck the whole “15-minute delivery” promise.
Because quick commerce, besides being about speed—is also about real estate. It’s a land grab disguised as a convenience-arms race, argues Gaurav today. The formula is simple: more stores, faster deliveries, happier customers, and, hopefully, bigger profits.
But finding the perfect location, everywhere, is tough. Last year alone, demand for dark-store space was 24 million square feet, and it’s only getting bigger. Besides, with Flipkart and Amazon jumping into the game, even logistics companies like Delhivery and Ecom Express want a piece of the pie.
So what do you do? You throw down 2X and even 3X the usual rent to lock down prime spots in big cities and even tier-2 ones. And it’s not just about stocking avocados anymore; these stores need to keep high-end items like, say, the new iPhone 16.
With the numbers getting trickier and trickier, the real quick-commerce showdown is, in fact, playing out among real-estate brokers.

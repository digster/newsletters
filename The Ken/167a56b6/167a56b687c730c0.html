Soaking in a barrel of beer and inhaling salty fog. Recreational tree climbing. Golf ball massages. Sleep-away camps with competitive water volleyball…Globally, spas are getting creative, even if a tad gimmicky. It’s also no more a women’s thing.
 
Back home, rejuvenation is a sliver of the rapidly growing physical wellness market. But there was someone who sniffed the opportunity and started India’s first spa chain, back in 2009. It had everything going for it—from premium services for premium hotels. It had plans to expand to high streets, malls, railway stations, trains, tier-2 cities, corporate wellness programmes.
 
O2 Spa Salon’s vision was to eventually become the largest wellness provider in the world, one that would have it all—hair and skin clinics, products, Ayurveda and yoga. Even a destination spa resort. When it raised $11 million from a Mauritius-based fund in 2016, it became the first spa chain in India to get private equity investment. A milestone for the nascent wellness sector, an envy of all other spa chains. Until the bubble burst in autumn this year. 
 
Wellness is a hard nut to crack for organised players. It is hard to organise and scale, and everyone has struggled to get close to 50 spa centres. Skilling. Compliance. Standardisation, with just the right mix of local variation. It’s a high-touch business. O2 had 120 centres when it started winding down.

Back to the future. Yeah, that’s what describes the comeback of measles, ‘slowbalisation’, and whooping cough.
 
While the rise of measles cases is linked to anti-vaxxers who exercise their right of choice and shun vaccination, the resurgence of whooping cough is linked to the kind of vaccines used. A change from a painful to a less painful vaccine, albeit one whose effectiveness wanes in a few years, is most likely causing the resurgence of this serious respiratory illness.
 
In the last 5-6 years, many countries have seen whooping cough become a leading cause of hospitalisation and death in infants. India has begun noticing a resurgence, too. And I have to say, it’s a riveting account of big brand wars, doctor influence, and a private market where the higher the commission, the more popular the vaccine. Mostly.
 
India’s public health programme uses the first generation vaccine, which is cheaper and more painful, but efficacious. India’s private market is ruled by brands from GSK and Sanofi, which are expensive, less painful, but less efficacious. Doctors know this, the Indian Academy of Pediatrics knows this, (perhaps parents don’t know this) and yet these vaccines are popular. Why?
 
In today’s story, Ruhi deconstructs the vaccine choices doctors and parents are making in India. Vaccines are inherently a community issue. It’s everyone’s issue because only when a large-enough section of the community is immunised, can people be protected from a contagious disease. In this case, whooping cough, which can cause serious illness in people of all ages.
 
While we’ll know the precise resurgence numbers only after June, which is when the ongoing study results are out, you must read the story of a war which, actually, has no clear bad guy: https://the-ken.com/story/gsk-sanofi-whooping-cough-vaccination/

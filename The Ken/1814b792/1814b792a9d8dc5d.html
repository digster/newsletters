Good Morning Ishan,
 
When we talk about organic foods, in most cases our lens is that of the consumer. It’s a fad. It’s too expensive. It’s for the elites. It’s unverifiable. It’s just a label.
 
Rarely do we think about it through the lens of a farmer. But if enough farmers weren’t invested in organic farming, we simply wouldn’t have enough organic goods on our supermarket shelves.
 
And if there’s one company that’s seen both sides of this equation over the years, it’s Sresta Natural Bioproducts, the parent company of what is arguably India’s largest organic (packaged) food brand—24 Mantra. They’ve been quietly and diligently slogging away at convincing both consumers and farmers to go organic since 2004.
 
Organic products back then used to cost around 170-180% the price of their conventional counterparts. Today, this has come down to 140-150%.
 
"With scale, we think we can bring it down by another 10%,” says Rajashekar Reddy Seelam, the founder of this fascinating business, in a wide-ranging and reflective interview with Seema Singh, our Editor. His first two companies, both of which were related to CRM, didn’t survive after getting caught up in the economic aftermaths of the Dotcom bust and 9/11 terrorist attacks respectively. Reddy blames his timing.
 
After a slow, arduous journey, that has led to a 35,000-strong farmers network across 12 states cultivating over 190,000 acres. Last year, they generated over Rs 313 crore in sales, which they feel they can triple using largely their existing network of farmers and their acreage.
 
It was during the due diligence for his company’s IPO that Reddy realised his products were sold in over 550 Indian cities. "That was surprising to us because we thought we were present only in 200+ cities. We learnt that smaller cities have less sales, but there is a core segment that wants these products,” says Reddy.
 
Please read this truly candid and insightful interview with a long-haul founder who now feels that timing is everything. And that he is now on its right side.
 
"Today, three million households consume organic food, but their consumption is very low. The potential to grow is huge; the immediate addressable market is 25 million households."

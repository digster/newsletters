Founders getting forced out of their companies is not unusual. Investors lose confidence, and founders get pushed out. Or the company grows, and the founders make way for professionals. It can happen to any founder. (As a co-founder, I say this with all seriousness.)

But when it becomes a slinging match between the two parties, then everyone loses. It appears that’s what is happening at FundsIndia, one of the top mutual fund distributors in the country with 1.1 million investors and total assets of Rs 5,444 crore ($762.3 million) under management.

A few weeks ago, the Board and the founders shot contradicting missives to the employees. While the Board claimed they had to oust the founders after “observing certain irregular actions”, the founders warned people how the Board was “trying to take control of the company to the detriment of founders, employees and customers”.

When VCs Inventus Capital and Foundation Capital bet on FundsIndia in 2010, it was the only game in town for buying mutual funds online. Faering Capital followed suit, and the company saw 357% growth in its assets under management between 2013-2016. But by 2017, it was being ambushed by newer, savvier digital platforms.

Time for some tech hires? Sure, it did happen. Krish Seshadri, former head of Facebook’s Internet.org as president, came in to build a team and the ‘tech’. But was it merely window dressing for better valuations? Or was there a widening disconnect between what the Board wanted and what the founders had planned? An exit by late 2018 was certainly on the minds of the investors.

Not only is that in doldrums, FundsIndia today is staring at three petitions filed against it for wrongful termination, a dwindling customer base and a depleting cash reserve. Arundhati brings you the sobering story of how FundsIndia lost its pole position when the investors and founders refused to see eye to eye.

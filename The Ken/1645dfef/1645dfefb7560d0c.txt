Good Morning Ishan,

History is a cruel reminder of change. If not that, then surely the lack of it. 

I’m saying this because I was reading up, researching on today’s story. On the nursing profession in India. The absolute, horrible poor pay. The long working hours in cruel shifts. The shortage that this country will face, of nearly 6 million nurses by 2030. A double-digit attrition rate. Nursing seats in colleges going empty. And much of the demands of this profession falling on deaf ears. Year. After year. After year. 

Because guess what, in the year 2000, the All India Government Nurses Federation, in their representation to the prime minister of India, said: “We the nurses of India, known as ‘sisters’ by the society, are not treated equally with other professionals. Being women—a weaker section of the society—we have never been given justice by the Ministry of Health and Family Welfare. We face a lot of difficulties during our service hours, but hardly any concern is shown for this community.” 

[Quote in the book, Indian Sisters - a History of Nursing and the State #, 1907-2007 https://books.google.co.in/books?id=AHo9BAAAQBAJ&pg=PR9&lpg=PR9&dq=Nursing+union+India&source=bl&ots=IslJhiMWz0&sig=renRiDF3TWr2OUd2Uylf_CQDtYI&hl=en&sa=X&ved=0ahUKEwjRhL6lw4DcAhUdT48KHdKXDgU4ChDoAQhHMAY&utm_campaign=The%20Ken&utm_source=Email&utm_medium=Newsletter#v=onepage&q=Nursing%20union%20India&f=false]

18 long years later, not much has changed. 

Except maybe in Kerala, one of the Southern states in India. In April this year, Kerala became the first Indian state to fix a minimum wage for nurses. With a base rate of Rs 20,000 ($292) per month. By no means is this a lot of money. But still, this is an improvement, and there is hope that more states will follow Kerala’s lead. There is hope that this profession will soon get the attention it deserves. I know, I hope so. The hero of today’s story Ila hopes so, too. In fact, she believes in it.

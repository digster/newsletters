Good Evening Ishan,
A lot has happened in the world of sport this past week. Rafael Nadal won the French… err… Nadal Open for the 14th time. Saudi Arabia officially launched its latest sportswashing project—a golf tournament. Mithali Raj, one of the greatest Indian cricketers of all time, called it a day. And England batter Joe Root entered the 10,000-run club in Test cricket.
There was also some A-grade buffoonery on social media from Sourav Ganguly, former India cricket captain and the current president of the Board of Control for Cricket in India (BCCI). And so, ahead of a huge weekend for the BCCI, with the auction for the next five-year cycle of the Indian Premier League’s (IPL) media rights scheduled for Sunday, let’s talk about what the board president did.
It’s something he has been doing, rather unabashedly, for a long time.
Sourav Ganguly: the prince of conflict
The first tweet landed at 5.20 pm on 1 June.
|
|
Within minutes, #CricketTwitter was abuzz with speculation that Ganguly was going to resign from his position as BCCI president, four months before his three-year tenure ends. The Indian media soon got in on the action, as dozens of publications fuelled the speculation, with some saying that the 49-year-old was about to enter politics. Eventually, news agency ANI managed to contact BCCI secretary Jay Shah, who finally [put the rumours to bed](https://www.hindustantimes.com/cricket/sourav-ganguly-bcci-president-resignation-jay-shah-101654086821986.html).
A few hours later, Ganguly himself [clarified](https://www.business-standard.com/article/current-affairs/not-politics-but-educational-app-my-new-venture-clarifies-sourav-ganguly-122060101503_1.html) his statement, telling the media that it was “a simple advertisement stunt” and there was “no question of me resigning” from the BCCI. “I am going to launch a commercial educational app, which will be my own venture,” he added.
The following morning, though, Ganguly threw another googly on Twitter. At 11.32 am, he posted this:

Cue more speculation and rumours.
The announcement didn’t come at 12 pm, but almost two hours later. And surprise, surprise: it was indeed an education app.
|
|
|
|
I will not get into this “genius” social media marketing strategy (which probably deserves another piece) 🙄.
But there are two other things to note here:
- Ganguly has been the brand ambassador of Classplus for at least
[18 months](https://brandequity.economictimes.indiatimes.com/news/marketing/classplus-signs-sourav-ganguly-as-its-brand-ambassador/79733157). Here’s a[video](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Gfj4A8TuBHk)of an ad he did for the edtech in December 2020. So, there’s nothing new about this association to warrant all the drama—or at least Ganguly hasn’t made it clear in his tweets. - But more importantly, the BCCI, whose president Ganguly has been since October 2019, already has an edtech sponsor in BYJU’s. This association dates back to July 2019, with BYJU’s currently
[paying](https://www.hindustantimes.com/cricket/byjus-stay-on-as-india-shirt-sponsors-to-pay-bcci-10-more-101646671668864.html)~Rs 240 crore (~US$30 million) per year to be the jersey sponsor of the Indian cricket team.
Which means Ganguly wilfully got involved with a brand that’s a rival of the sponsor of the organisation he heads. It’s a classic case of [ambush marketing](https://scroll.in/field/851543/why-renaming-the-wankhede-stadium-could-be-a-turning-point-for-indian-sports), of which there have been a [number of examples](https://scroll.in/field/898156/prithvi-shaw-vs-swiggy-and-freecharge-what-this-means-for-social-media-marketing-in-sports) in the world of sport previously. I had written about telco [Reliance Jio](https://the-ken.com/moneyball_edition/inside-reliances-jio-dhan-dhana-dhan-ipl-sponsorship-strategy/) doing it during the IPL a few years ago.
But when it comes to individuals from the world of sport getting involved with rival brands of their organisation’s sponsors, it’s usually the athletes. Ganguly may be a former cricketer, and a very popular one at that, but he is currently an administrator. And he’s no ordinary administrator. He’s the president of the most powerful cricket board in the world.
|
|
If you’ve been reading this newsletter regularly, you’d know that this is not a one-off. Ganguly also endorses My11Circle, a fantasy sports platform that’s a competitor of another official BCCI partner, Dream11. And while Ganguly signed with My11Circle in [May 2019](https://www.exchange4media.com/marketing-news/fantasy-cricket-platform-my11circle-ropes-in-sourav-ganguly-as-brand-ambassador-96863.html), which was five months before he became BCCI president, he was an advisor for the Delhi Capitals IPL team that year. Dream11 was one of the team’s [official sponsors](https://www.dreamsports.group/newsroom/dream11-signs-up-with-7-ipl-teams-and-7-cricketers-for-its-marketing-campaigns/).
Okay, perhaps that’s minor. But once Ganguly became BCCI president in October 2019, shouldn’t he have stepped down from his brand ambassadorial role at My11Circle? That’s what former British track-and-field athlete Sebastian Coe was [forced to do](https://www.theguardian.com/sport/2015/nov/26/sebastian-coe-iaaf-nike) after he became the president of the International Association of Athletics Federations (IAAF) in 2015.
Coe, a two-time Olympic gold medallist, had been a Nike ambassador for 38 years. He was initially reluctant to give up the US$150,000-a-year contract, but eventually did it because “the current noise level” around it was becoming a distraction. “It is clear that perception and reality have become horribly mangled,” he had said at the time.
This was after a BBC investigation [revealed](https://www.theguardian.com/sport/2015/nov/24/sebastian-coe-role-eugene-2021-bidding-process) that Coe had discussed his support for Eugene’s bid to host the 2021 World Athletics Championships with a senior Nike official. Eugene in Oregon, United States, is the birthplace of Nike.
Ganguly has been similarly dismissive of the perceived conflicts in his endorsements. “It is not [a conflict],” he [had said](https://sports.ndtv.com/cricket/my-involvement-with-my11-circle-not-a-conflict-of-interest-sourav-ganguly-2152182) about his My11Circle association after becoming BCCI president. “If I was endorsing another fantasy cricket platform, that would have been a conflict of interest.” He is, of course, referring to Dream11.
|
|

|
So, if Ganguly is endorsing My11Circle, and then My11Circle becomes the BCCI’s sponsor, that would be a conflict of interest, according to the constitution. With My11Circle and Classplus, it’s more of an ethical conflict. And if the BCCI’s history with ethics is anything to go by, it should come as no surprise. Ganguly is [hardly the first](https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/news/politics-and-nation/supreme-court-cites-conflict-of-interest-bars-n-srinivasan-from-fighting-bcci-polls/articleshow/45985594.cms?from=mdr) BCCI honcho to get embroiled in such controversies.
In Ganguly’s case, though, it’s not just a matter of ethics. He has also been involved in clear conflicts of interest. Multiple times.
In October 2021, Ganguly decided to [step down](https://indianexpress.com/article/sports/cricket/sourav-ganguly-to-relinquish-atkmb-role-to-avoid-conflict-of-interest-7594145/) from his position of director at Indian Super League (ISL) football club ATK Mohun Bagan to avoid any potential conflict of interest. This came days after the RP Sanjiv Goenka Group (RPSG) won the bid for the Lucknow IPL franchise. Goenka owns 80% of ATK Mohun Bagan.
Shouldn’t Ganguly have stepped down from his director post as soon as RPSG entered the bidding process for the IPL franchise? What was the point of shutting the stable door after the horse had bolted?
Then, there’s Delhi Capitals. After becoming BCCI president, Ganguly announced that he had given up his [advisory role](https://www.delhicapitals.in/sourav-ganguly-joins-delhi-capitals-as-advisor) at the IPL franchise. However, he continued to be an ambassador for JSW Cement, a brand under the JSW Group conglomerate, which co-owns the Delhi Capitals.
|
|
When asked about this conflict, Ganguly [reportedly said](https://www.indiatoday.in/sports/cricket/story/sourav-ganguly-conflict-of-interest-delhi-capitals-jsw-cements-brand-ambassador-jsw-sports-1697282-2020-07-05), “How do I influence? I am not brand ambassador of JSW Sports (a JSW Group subsidiary that operates Delhi Capitals). I don’t think the cement company is a sponsor of the team. I don’t see any conflict in it. I am not associated with their cricket; had I been, it would have been a conflict.”
The Delhi Capitals role had also attracted a separate conflict-of-interest [controversy](https://indianexpress.com/article/sports/cricket/sourav-gangulys-defence-cac-is-defunct-so-confict-of-interest-clause-is-irrelevant-5681161/) since Ganguly, before becoming BCCI president, was heading the Cricket Association of Bengal (CAB). His brother Snehashish Ganguly is currently the secretary of the state association.
Then, there were also reports earlier this year that Ganguly regularly attends selection committee meetings—which isn’t allowed as per the BCCI’s constitution. Only the BCCI secretary is allowed to attend these meetings and that too only as a convenor, with no say in selection matters.
|
When Ganguly was asked about his proximity to the selection committee, here’s what he told reporters: “I don’t think I need to answer anybody, anything [on this] and dignify any of these baseless accusations. I am the president of the BCCI and I do the job of what the president of the BCCI should be doing. [I have played] 424 international matches for India. Not a bad idea at times to remind people about it, isn’t it?”
Sure, Mr Ganguly, no one is denying that you were a great player and captain for India. But if you feel your inputs are so vital for the selection committee, perhaps you should apply to be a part of it rather than being BCCI president? Oh, but then even that would be a conflict given your brother’s post in CAB. My bad.
Senior sports journalist Sharda Ugra, who has covered Ganguly’s cricketing and administrative careers, can only laugh about his various infractions. “He is incorrigible. There is a certain brazenness about him—this is what I do and this is how I earn money. And he has no qualms about it. See, all cricketers love money, there’s no denying that. But Ganguly is not apologetic and he doesn’t hide it,” she says, laughing.
Ganguly’s brazenness is helped by the fact that the BCCI governance has been so fluid ever since the Supreme Court-appointed Committee of Administrators’ (CoA) stint [ended](https://www.indiatoday.in/sports/cricket/story/coa-33-month-tenure-vinod-rai-diana-edulji-vikram-limaye-ramchandra-guha-ravi-thodge-bcci-lodha-reforms-1612254-2019-10-23) in 2019. The CoA was appointed by the apex court in 2017 to govern BCCI and oversee the implementation of the [Justice Lodha committee reforms](https://www.espncricinfo.com/story/a-timeline-of-the-bcci-and-lodha-committee-reforms-case-in-the-supreme-court-of-india-1030889). The Lodha committee was [appointed](https://www.espncricinfo.com/story/all-you-need-to-know-ahead-of-the-lodha-panel-verdict-898285) by the Supreme Court in 2015 in the aftermath of the 2013 IPL spot-fixing scandal to streamline the BCCI’s working, among other things.
Among the Lodha committee’s [recommendations](https://www.espncricinfo.com/story/bcci-given-six-months-to-implement-lodha-committee-reforms-1036059) that were accepted by the Supreme Court were these: No person can hold more than three three-year terms as a BCCI official, no official can serve consecutive terms, and no person can hold office at the BCCI and state cricket associations simultaneously.
This is why Ganguly stepped down as CAB president when he was elected unopposed as BCCI chief. However, Ganguly was supposed to be BCCI president only for 10 months and then enter a mandatory [cooling-off period](https://www.espncricinfo.com/story/sourav-ganguly-likely-to-take-over-as-new-bcci-president-1203682) of three years because of his tenure as CAB chief, according to the Lodha committee recommendations. The cooling-off period applies to any Indian cricket official who has served six consecutive years in any position at the national or state levels.
However, Ganguly’s BCCI [approached](https://www.business-standard.com/article/sports/bcci-moves-to-sc-for-full-3-year-term-for-president-ganguly-secretary-shah-120052300430_1.html) the Supreme Court seeking an amendment in the board’s constitution that would allow him and secretary Shah to complete their full three-year terms without serving the cooling-off period. The matter was never resolved and, as things stand, Ganguly and Shah’s three-year tenures will end in October 2022.
|
|
It remains to be seen what Ganguly and Shah will do once their terms end. But Ugra says the biggest disappointment of the Ganguly era in the BCCI has been that he has also become a BOGO. Short for BCCI’s old-guard officials.
It’s a term she coined in a [piece](https://www.espncricinfo.com/story/sharda-ugra-what-sourav-ganguly-needs-to-do-in-his-time-as-head-of-the-bcci-1208446) for ESPNcricinfo after Ganguly’s BCCI started focusing its attention on overturning the Lodha recommendations. As a player-turned-administrator, his appointment had generated feel-good headlines after the turmoil in BCCI over the preceding few years. He was supposed to be different and bring about change. “Go on, Sourav, you’re not a BOGO. You can do it. Be a star,” she had written.
Unfortunately, he has become exactly that. “He’s a blue-chip BOGO,” says Ugra, with a resigned titter.

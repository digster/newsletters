And guilt is the common thread
 
Good Evening Ishan,
 
Last week, we sent out a survey with a mission: to build a collective of working mothers’ wisdom. We’re on a quest to learn what enables working mothers to thrive, what doesn’t, and what the future must look like. 
 
Over 600 incredible women responded to the survey. If you’re one of them, thank you so much for your time, courage, and candidness. 
 
In the survey, one of the things we set out to understand was whether conditions were improving for working mothers in the workplace at all, and if so, to what extent. The results indicate they are. Not by much, and by no means enough, but the needle is in forward motion, especially since the amendment to the Maternity Benefit Act in 2017. 
 
Jincy Varghese, a Mumbai-based software engineer, wrote in and said she was responsible for the Maternity (Amendment) Bill 2017 becoming a law. She lobbied for the bill to get passed by the Parliament by writing cold emails, circulating petitions, and pushing for internet viraldom. And then, one day, it worked. One of her emails caught the attention of the labour ministry. 
 
This is that email, sent on 10 February 2017.
 
“I am 39 weeks pregnant. My pregnancy is a high-risk pregnancy since I am diabetic and over 30 years of age as well. And this is my first baby. I work for an IT company in Bombay. I am a software engineer with seven years of experience. I am still working, even though I can go into labour any moment from now. Why? Is it the love for my job? Not really."
 
She goes on to reveal the real reason.
It is because it is my majboori. My company grants us only 12 weeks of paid maternity leave, which includes weekends. … I have to continue working. Even though it is risky!
Jincy Varghese, in an email to the labour ministry

And then Jincy really took it up a notch. She addressed the email specifically to Maneka Gandhi, then Union Minister for Women and Child Development.

“I have one wish, though – I wish I was a pregnant dog or any other animal. It would most definitely catch the attention of Maneka ma'am and would fetch a reply in my inbox too! Sadly, I am not a dog or any other animal. My fate led me to be born as a woman, a working woman in the private sector in India.”


Maneka Gandhi ​responded. And a month later, on the first day of the Budget session in March 2017, the Maternity Benefit (Amendment) Act came into effect.

That amendment brought in some changes for women in the workplace. But implementation remains weak. This, in turn, affects how workplaces continue to disadvantage working mothers. 

 

In this week’s episode of Cost to Company, we tell you all the things we learned from hundreds of our survey respondents. The strong signals, insightful ideas, gaping holes, and many profoundly humbling stories.

HR folks, people leaders, policymakers, politicians, friends or family of working mothers, and working mothers themselves, please listen to this. In this episode, we will tell you what 600 working moms shared with us.

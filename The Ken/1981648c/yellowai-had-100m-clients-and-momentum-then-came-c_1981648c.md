Good Morning Ishan,
If you’ve ordered pizza from Domino’s, asked Sony’s chatbot about its products, or used Hyundai’s automated customer assistance, there’s a good chance you’ve interacted with a product made by Yellow.ai. It’s a company that offers conversational AI software, where users can talk to a chatbot—by voice or text—and receive responses that hopefully include the answers or solutions they need.
Not too long ago, companies like Yellow.ai felt like the future. But times have changed. Yellow.ai’s offerings aren’t so uncommon anymore, and much bigger names have tossed their hats into the ring.
Meta acquired voice-replication startup, PlayAI, this month. Microsoft partnered with Twilio for its conversation-AI offering. Accenture bought Cresta in late 2024. Google and Amazon have retooled their AI-enabled customer service. When enterprises can use products sold by tech’s biggest names, why would they bother with Yellow.ai, even if it was once a market leader?
Former Yellow.ai employees who spoke to The Ken said the company has been experiencing troubles. Its products weren’t built to scale, code bases would break, and clients would need to contend with downtime. Also, there’s been rapid churn within the ranks of company leadership.
With that said, Yellow.ai may have a way out of its slump. Abhirami has the [story](https://the-ken.com/story/yellow-ai-had-100m-clients-and-momentum-then-came-chatgpt-big-tech/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter).
While I have your attention… AI providers need to constantly adapt or become obsolete. This is part of a bigger shakeup in the way products are built, as well as how product managers, designers, engineers, and others work together.
It was once en vogue to move fast and break things, but it’s now necessary to ship, ship, ship—and ensure everything that goes live is ready for real-world users.

Dear Ishan, good morning! It’s wonderful to be back in your inbox and your Sunday.

Bengaluru has been grey, cold, and wet for much of the week, even after we got handed the relatively less-intense effects of the Northeast Monsoon that’s been lashing our neighbouring state of Tamil Nadu.

The near-daily rain has left all the street dogs wet, sad, and sullen, including Bribo and Svelty (yup, those are the names I’ve given the son-mama indie duo who often feature in these newsletters).

I came back from vacation to a backlog of pending work and emails. As if dealing with that wasn’t enough, we also took up some repainting work at home. So, I’m writing this from the middle of a sofa piled on the left side with things moved from a different room and a curled up Gabru on the right.

There are few things as frustrating as your home being taken over by strangers coming in or going out at will, your possessions randomly scattered, your personal spaces destroyed. You become a willing-unwilling prisoner who can’t leave and can’t complain.

A younger me would be severely stressed out by now. But as I’ve grown older, I’ve learnt to “pre-accept” disruptions by telling myself that there is nothing I can do about them except get through to the other side.

Email inboxes will get down to zero (FWIW, I had achieved “inbox zero” the day before leaving for vacation). The walls will get done. Rooms will go back to being warm and personal.

I can either get there stressed, or calm.

But right now, I’m frustrated. Which is what I want to talk about today.

1. Treasure your frustration

“Do you ever find yourself in that moment of exhaustion, tiredness, or sleepiness where you suddenly feel like you have extraordinary clarity about things? It could be about relationships or work, but in that liminal state of being completely worn out, there’s this unusual degree of certainty and clarity,” said a good friend to me recently.

I instantly recognized the feeling. Most of my personal breakthroughs have often come after such intense periods of frustration. So much so that I now value frustration as the prerequisite for genuine breakthroughs.

I especially loved my friend’s use of the word “liminal” to describe what he felt.

liminal

adjective

1. Psychology. of, relating to, or situated at the limen, the threshold at which a stimulus begins to produce an effect.

2. of or relating to a transitional or intermediate state, stage, or period.

If frustration is the state between status quo and breakthrough, then liminal https://www.dictionary.com/browse/liminal—things that exist at the threshold (or border) between one thing and another—is a great way to place it.

“Yes!!” I told my friend. Not only did I recognize his state, I’d learnt to value it. And not just in myself, but in my colleagues too.

Where earlier I would proactively offer feedback or help when I saw a colleague frustrated, now I consciously hold myself back. “I let them wallow in it,” I told my friend.

I wasn’t joking.

Because your frustration is unique to you. If you let someone else “solve” it for you, you’re giving away your own potential learnings and breakthroughs.

We need to treasure our frustration.

Of course, I am not suggesting that all frustration is worth treasuring. Observe what is making you uncomfortable, angry, or frustrated. Is it a problem worth solving? If not, abandon it. Else, embrace it.

There is also a fine line between being frustrated and becoming despondent.

At some point, it is useful to ask for help. But just like you decide how long you want to use your frustration as an asset, it is you who must also learn when it becomes debilitating. That’s when others can potentially help you break out.

When I said I like to let my colleagues wallow in frustration, it isn’t because I am sadistic (I hope). But because I want them to emerge out of it on their own terms.

My friend put it more eloquently. “Jumping in to give people answers immediately is often more harmful than helpful in the long run. It’s something I’ve grappled with in my own leadership—realising that it’s not about having the right answers all the time, but about asking the better/right questions.”

I am also a parent. It’s even more tempting to swoop in and help your kids at the very first instance of them getting frustrated.

Don’t. Every time you swoop in, you’re robbing them of an opportunity to develop resilience.

“There is something to letting people sit with discomfort, and I don’t see it as ‘tough love’ or suffering. To me, it’s about resilience—letting people figure things out themselves so they’re stronger for it later,” as my friend explained.

If you’re not convinced yet, let me approach this from another direction: happiness. Since we’re talking about happiness, it’s only appropriate we start with a TED Talk.

Flow, the secret to happiness https://www.ted.com/talks/mihaly_csikszentmihalyi_flow_the_secret_to_happiness?subtitle=en—by Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi

“What makes a life worth living?” Noting that money cannot make us happy, he looks to those who find pleasure and lasting satisfaction in activities that bring about a state of “flow.”

Born in 1934, the late Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi was a psychologist known popularly as the “father of flow.”

Perhaps you’ve heard of “flow state”?

In Csikszentmihalyi’s exploration into the concepts and causes of optimal and positive experiences, he became intrigued by artists who would get lost in their work—so immersed that they would disregard basic animal cues for food, water, and sleep. Several of his interview subjects described their experiences through the metaphor of a water current carrying them along. Thus, the term and positive psychological concept of a “flow state” was born.

Csikszentmihalyi’s studies on flow included interviews with scientists, athletes, musicians, artists, business executives and others—particularly creative professionals—because he wanted to know when they experienced optimal performance levels and how they felt. His research led him to conclude that happiness is an internal state of being, and as a matter of external factors.

Individuals, he found, were their most creative, productive, and happy when in a state of flow. As Csikszentmihalyi explained it, flow is “a state in which people are so involved in an activity that nothing else seems to matter; the experience is so enjoyable that people will continue to do it even at great cost, for the sheer sake of doing it.”

University of Chicago https://news.uchicago.edu/story/mihaly-csikszentmihalyi-pioneering-psychologist-and-father-flow-1934-2021

But what does flow state have to do with frustration?

“In our studies, we found that every flow activity, whether it involved competition, chance, or any other dimension of experience, had this in common: It provided a sense of discovery, a creative feeling of transporting the person into a new reality. It pushed the person to higher levels of performance, and led to previously undreamed-of states of consciousness. In short, it transformed the self by making it more complex. In this growth of the self lies the key to flow activities.”

So, don’t ever make the mistake of conflating frustration as inability.

If breakthroughs are the outcome, frustration is the process.

If resilience is the ability, then frustration is the necessary ingredient.

Instead of aiming for outcomes like wealth, career milestones, or health directly, aim for the frustration that you must encounter and overcome before them.

And then you’ll realise why it will be, well, frustrating, when your colleague or boss jumps in to help you when you’re frustrated.

3. Solitude 📚

Hello, dear readers, it’s Hari here with our weekly book recommendations. I’ve wanted to move out of my childhood home for, let’s say, forever. Maybe because that’s where I’ve lived for most of my life. It is a quiet village where everyone knows everyone, and there wasn’t ever very much to do. And it was lonely, even though I had all the people I cared for around me. That tends to happen when you’re quiet by nature.

I said I wanted to move out for college, but didn’t fight too much for it. Now, I tell myself it wouldn’t have mattered because I would have been home anyway because of the pandemic. I finished college, got my first job, and I thought: ‘Yes! I’m finally leaving home!’. Well, turns out, the company wants me to work from home. Foiled again. 

A year later, though, I did finally manage to break free. I was off to The Ken; to Bengaluru, a proper city at last. Meeting new people, seeing different faces everyday, and most importantly, not feeling lonely. Or at least, for a while. Because I knew that even with the opportunity I probably wouldn’t change much of my ways. I preferred my quiet because it worked for me. So loneliness still tags along—not as close as it used to, but never very far away. 

Enter Olivia Laing and her stirring book, The Lonely City https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/25667449-the-lonely-city?ref=nav_sb_ss_1_11, and the way I looked at the whole thing changed. Here’s an excerpt from the book that sums it up pretty well:

“You can be lonely anywhere, but there is a particular flavour to the loneliness that comes from living in a city, surrounded by millions of people… mere physical proximity is not enough to dispel a sense of internal isolation… Cities can be lonely places, and in admitting this we see that loneliness doesn’t necessarily require physical solitude, but rather an absence or paucity of connection, closeness, kinship: an inability, for one reason or another, to find as much intimacy as is desired.”

https://the-ken.com/?post_type=fp_newsletter&p=1483533?utm_source=ro_hdr&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=View in Browser https://the-ken.com/?post_type=fp_newsletter&p=1483533?utm_source=ro_hdr&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=

Laing’s The Lonely City is biography, art criticism, and memoir combined. Perhaps as an escape, or an attempt to understand what she herself was going through, she turns the lens on artists from New York such as Andy Warhol, Edward Hopper, Henry Darger, and David Wojnarowicz to understand how they contemplated loneliness and how it reflected in their work.

I must clarify that this isn’t a book that celebrates loneliness, that would be too grim. But throughout, Laing shows us how solitude allowed these artists to capture empathy in others and how it reflected in their creativity. As for Laing, she doesn’t fully come to terms with her own loneliness, but by the end of the book, she does tell us that she isn’t alone in feeling lonely. It is a shared human condition. 

Okay, that was a long winded way of saying if you feel alone, try getting out. Do something by yourself if you find it hard to connect with other people. Look around, and know that you’re not alone in this feeling.

4. Interesting reads 🔖

The joy of clutter https://aeon.co/essays/the-life-changing-magic-of-japanese-clutter—by Matt Alt

‘Cluttered spaces reflect the lifestyles of their occupants,’ Tsuzuki told me. ‘Minimalism, on the other hand, is a way of establishing privacy. You can’t tell what that person wears or eats.’

‘Clutter isn’t really an East versus West thing,’ continues Tsuzuki. ‘It’s a rich versus poor thing. Wherever you go, anywhere in the world, the wealthy have the luxury of living in clean, minimal spaces, while the poor have to make do with small, cramped ones, without any way to hide their belongings.’

He continued: ‘Consider the teahouse, which so many foreign people associate with Japanese minimalism. It’s a small space with nothing inside, right? But you need to have the space to build a teahouse. You need money to build one. A minimalist space is something only those with space and money can create.’

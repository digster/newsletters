The economic slowdown has begun knocking on our doors. For those among us not-laid-off, it’s changing the way business is done. It’s changing how we work. What work we do. It’s changing the relationship between employer and employee. It’s changing our plans for our own futures.

Cost to Company, The Ken’s weekly podcast about work and workplaces, is foremost a community based podcast. And we want to hear all the ways in which your work itself, how business is done where you work, and how you’re planning your future, has changed in this economic moment. We want to know what new trends are being seen in your professional lives because of the macroeconomy. We want to know your stories.

If you’d like to participate in the podcast, please fill out this survey. It’ll take no more than five minutes, and it will make Cost to Company a podcast truly for and of The Ken subscriber.
Back to today’s episode of CTC, which is about one way in which the economy is shaping business.
In the last few months alone, Amazon, Meta, and Salesforce announced that they were going to begin the process of flattening their organisations. That they were going to have fewer and fewer hierarchies in their organisations.  Middle and senior managers in engineering at Meta were asked to start "doing things". Coding, designing, whatever. For most, managing people itself was no longer going to be a role.

The pattern here appears to be that in tough economic times, when efficiency becomes paramount, businesses are rethinking their need for managers. And concluding that they don’t need that many.

This week, Cost to Company, spoke to three experts about this. And learnt, rather counter-intuitively, that a  more complex business will always need more managers, no matter the economy. And a business looking for efficiency by cutting its middle, will suffer for it.

Yesterday on Daybreak

When was the last time you stood in a queue to buy a movie ticket?
 

Who does that anymore? It's too big a risk to take, especially if it's a movie everyone has been looking forward to. And why would we even when we have an option to skip the queue all together.
 

BookMyShow: the foolproof way to get a movie ticket minus all the hassles. It wouldn't be a stretch to say that the platform now has become synonymous with watching movies. But what if I told you that over time, it might not be the same BookMyShow you have known. If it were a person, imagine them going through a makeover after which you’re never able to look at them the same way.
 

You see, the movie-ticketing giant has gone through some pretty rough patches since its inception in 1998. But the pandemic was quite the knockout punch. Somehow though, to everyone’s surprise, the platform survived and is on a a path to swift recovery.

However, the leadership wants to use this as an opportunity to transform the company into more than just a movie ticketing platform. They think live events is where the money is at.
 

But live events are historically known to be money guzzlers. Can BookMyShow prove otherwise?

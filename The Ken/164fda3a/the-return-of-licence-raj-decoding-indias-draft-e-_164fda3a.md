Phew! What a week it has been. A fusillade of draft policies fired from New Delhi. While we are still wrapping our head around some, there’s one that absolutely merits a dissection, like now—the draft e-commerce policy.
Mystery, stupidity, destabilisation…are written all over it. Starting with the way it was leaked on 30 July, with no author name, date or terms of reference. By now it’s been PDF-ed and circulated widely. It’s sending shockwaves across the industry because, in its current form, it’d force a good part of the internet economy to change its engine mid-flight. On their way out are its existing US-style engines featuring open innovation, rapid venture-backed scaling and laissez-faire regulators. And in their place are coming in Chinese-style ones where a powerful state and well-connected businesses will use concepts like patriotism, nationalism and security to decide who can succeed in India.
If the last 10 years were the age of hungry entrepreneurs backed by aggressive venture investors, the e-commerce draft policy makes it clear the next 10 will rely a lot on lawyers.
The old order changeth; the “Bombay Club” gives way to the “Delhi Club”. If a group of industrialists went up to the then finance minister Manmohan Singh seeking a level playing field in 1993, a group of re-invigorated older business houses, trade bodies and “Indian” e-commerce companies have sought a different level playing in 2018.
“It seems like someone’s wishlist that has been articulated without much thinking,” says a policy executive.
Handing over source code to regulators, preventing bulk sourcing of electronic goods or deep discounts, a weird focus on promoting RuPay…the list is long and baffling. It’s like the policymakers have suddenly woken up to the Chinese model of shielding the internet economy from foreign players, with zero ground preparation.

If you can’t fight them, join them. And if you don’t want to join them, hire them.
 
Coopting a threat is an art Facebook has mastered to perfection. For a half-trillion company with over 2 billion monthly users, it displayed that skill to textbook perfection when faced with the threat from Snapchat. It just copied most Snapchat features into its own photo-sharing app, Instagram, and supercharged the experience with its second-to-none network effects.
 
Something similar is happening in India too, albeit on the policy front. In February 2016 it suffered a very public defeat at the hands of civil society activists who loosely banded together to support Net Neutrality and to oppose Free Basics, Facebook’s zero rated program to offer “free” (you know by now nothing’s really “free” on the Internet, right?) Internet access to Indians. Less than a year after starting Free Basics, Facebook had to shut it down after India’s telecom regulator banned all forms of traffic discrimination.
 
A year and a half later, Facebook is beefing up its policy team with a lot of hires, including “civil society” legal and policy professionals. What did I say? If you can’t fight them, hire them.
 
Why is Facebook doing this? Because it has three major policy battles shaping up - privacy, connectivity (access) and data localisation. Each of these could individually upend its plans for India, its second largest market by number of users after the US. Collectively, they could be catastrophic to a company that’s already considered too big, too powerful and too unchecked by users, competitors and regulators around the world.

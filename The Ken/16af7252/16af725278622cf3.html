If there’s one brand “trying” to upend education in India (and soon overseas) it’s got to be Byju’s. The edtech giant, currently valued at $5 billion, is trying unusually hard. So hard that its sales of multi-year subscriptions, paid fully up front for the entire period, ranging from 1 to 8 years, are no different from people buying a vehicle and getting it financed through a bank loan.

Nothing wrong with that. Except Byju’s sales pitches are triggered by an app, and sealed by aggressive sales executives, on behalf of gullible parents who often either don’t know or don’t register that they are not just buying a tablet for their child, but signing up for a third-party loan.

Since the launch of its learning app in 2015, Byju’s has really known no barrier to growth. Its next logical step was deciding to offer consumer loans to prospective customers. It’d still get paid in full upon a successful sale (which, as you’ll read, is an act in itself), parents would pay monthly instalments to lenders, with interest on the loans borne by Byju’s.

While Byju’s astute sales practices may not have caught up with it yet, they have begun to create a massive hole in their partner lenders’ pockets, with many parents directing their banks to stop EMI payments, or simply emptying out their bank accounts. Parents The Ken spoke to were unable to return the product as the sales agents were never available to facilitate the return.

Non-banking financial institutions like Avanse Financial Services, India Infoline, Capital Float, ZestMoney and Bajaj Finserv have together issued loans worth more than Rs 1,000 crore ($143.9 million) to Byju’s users since 2017. Of the Rs 500 crore ($71.9 million) consumer finance book that Capital Float alone built, nearly Rs 400 crore ($57.5 million) came from Byju’s.

If one analyses the meteoric rise of Byju’s test-prep business, like in any normal distribution curve, the answer lies in the short tail of the curve: The poor financial literacy of parents, who don’t know what they are signing up for and don’t care how their credit history is getting impacted. (Rating agency Standard & Poor’s found in 2015 that three out of every four adult Indians are not financially literate.)

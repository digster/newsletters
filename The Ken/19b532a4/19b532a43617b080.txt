In 2025, we witnessed one of the worst air crashes in Indian aviation history. The resulting anger and blame, fired at everything that went wrong, took in an unsuspecting victim—the SBI Card. India’s second-largest credit card issuer quietly rolled back an air-accident insurance cover that offered compensation of up to Rs 1 crore on certain cards. At first glance, it seemed then like a decision made in the wake of the tragic incident.  

Immediately, social-media warriors took to their keyboards, calling it a cold, heartless, calculative move from SBI Card. The Ken has learnt that even the State Bank of India’s top management—the bank has a stake in the card company—enquired about the timing of the move. 

But most reports on the development missed a small yet important detail.

SBI Card had rolled out these changes a month ago, well before the tragedy on 12 June. The notice to cardholders intimating them of this rollback can be traced back to 31 May with the help of the Wayback Machine, a digital archive of the World Wide Web. SBI Card, unfortunately, did not carry this date on its notice, a step that could have easily avoided this bad PR. 

Arundhati Ramanathan brought us the numbers that made sense of their reasoning–such premiums were costing the credit card company at least Rs 18 crore annually. “And every penny saved counts for the card company, which is already having a tough time, with profits dipping 20% year-on-year in FY25,” she wrote. In other banking news, Anand Kalyanaraman, examined a promising alternative at a time of falling bank deposit rates: the Floating Rate Savings Bond (FRSB). 

Introduced in July 2020, the FRSB has much going for it. It is issued by the RBI on behalf of the government, so it is as safe as it gets. It’s open to all. Any investor can invest any amount in the FRSB without restrictions. Its deposit tenure of seven years is longish, but not as long as the PPF. And the interest is paid out every six months, which is good for those who seek regular income.

Perhaps most importantly, the interest rate it currently offers—8.05%—is much better than what most banks give.

A possible downside, though, is evident in its name. The FRSB’s interest rate is a “floating” rate, pegged to the prevailing rate on the National Savings Certificate (NSC), another small savings scheme offered by the government.

Read more about what Anand calls an oasis in a desert of drying interest rates in his piece.

March was particularly brutal for Indusind Bank and its former managing director and CEO, Sumanth Kathpalia. Rounak Kumar Gunjan covered a weekend of bombshells for the bank in question. 

On Friday, the Reserve Bank of India approved https://www.business-standard.com/industry/banking/indusind-bank-shares-drop-4-as-rbi-okays-short-term-ceo-extension-125031000899_1.html only a year’s extension to the bank’s managing director and CEO Sumant Kathpalia. This, despite the bank’s board wanting him to remain at the corner office for three more years.

Then, on Monday, the lender disclosed a Rs 1,580 crore discrepancy in its derivatives portfolio, potentially impacting 2.35% of its net worth. The issue had been first identified internally in September 2024, and an external agency has been hired for a review, Indusind said. 

The bank’s stock has tanked. Despite a mild recovery on Wednesday, Indusind’s share prices are down 24% since the disclosure—a market cap erosion of close to Rs 17,000 crore. Several mutual funds https://www.financialexpress.com/money/mutual-funds-lose-over-rs-7300-crore-as-indusind-bank-stock-plunges-over-27-check-worst-hit-funds-3774406/ with high exposure to the bank have taken a hit. 

Since then, Kathpalia has resigned, and the company is facing a fraud probe. However, the RBI has recently allowed HDFC subsidiaries to buy a 9.5% stake in the bank, and its stock seems to be recovering. We wait, watching what the new year might bring for Indusind.

Switching gears, seeing how many workers had low provident fund balances in their retirement corpus, the Central Board of Trustees (CBT) of the Employee Provident Fund Organisation (EPFO) took hold of the matter. They released new PF withdrawal rules, locking in 25% of the fund by increasing the waiting period for full withdrawal to 36 months. While it sparked some outrage, Archishma Iyer wrote that this was a case of misplaced fear, bringing out all the detail-devils.

As the government hastened to clarify https://www.pib.gov.in/PressReleasePage.aspx?PRID=2179689 after public opposition started building to the new rules, the new lock-in periods for withdrawals only apply to 25% of a subscriber’s PF balance. The rest of the 75% can be withdrawn immediately after the loss of employment. So while a full withdrawal will take longer (two months earlier versus one year under the new rules), 75% can be withdrawn with no wait time, unlike the one-month lock-in period in effect before. 

I spoke to a few investment experts to understand whether the government’s move to extend the waiting periods for full withdrawals make sense, and they say the angst amongst subscribers arises from not seeing their PF and pension corpuses the way they should ideally be seen: namely, as a long-term investment that takes advantage of compounding effects. Instead, for many, PF ends up becoming an emergency fund.

And finally, Arundhati traces payment companies’ attempts to break into e-commerce and vice-versa, through Phonepe’s two-year-old quick commerce bet, Pincode, that closed shop earlier this December, as a case in point. 

Payments and e-commerce companies have for some time been engaged in an elaborate digital roleplay. 

Over the last few years, Amazon, Flipkart, and Swiggy have all launched their own versions of a payments play. And payments companies like Phonepe and Paytm* ventured into hyperlocal commerce. These are two very different sets of companies with very different business realities, yet united by one sentiment—how to make their millions of users count in more ways than one. 

On paper, cracking quick-commerce could have potentially unlocked double the revenue for Phonepe, estimates the executive quoted above, as it would allow the company to earn 5–10% of a transaction as commission. “There are few categories that can offer such a prospect,” he added.

But if it believed it could solve quick-commerce by relying on its merchants, it’s also those same merchants who stand in the way of a payments company solving it.

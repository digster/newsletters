Seniors pay 5X the premium for just a fifth of the coverage in critical medical cases. Ayushman Bharat’s new Rs 5 lakh health cover for those over 70 aims to fix that
Health insurance for the elderly is both inadequate in coverage and exorbitant in premiums. After the age of 45, premiums spike if you have a policy; if you don’t, getting one at a reasonable rate is difficult. The government seems to have doubled down on health insurance for seniors with rule changes for insurers and a new policy for everyone over 70. Private insurers seem to have sniffed their shortcomings, with some new efforts to better their products for seniors. Family offices are booming across India, going from 45 to 300 in just six years—and they’re not just a big-city affair. Just ask the millionaires in smaller commercial hubs

Startups are scrambling for venture debt as equity funding dries up, but with VC money scarce and past mistakes weighing heavy, the lenders are getting pickier
Startups, feeling the pinch from the drought in big-ticket equity funding, are knocking on venture-debt firms’ doors for some working-capital relief. In 2023 alone, venture debt came with around US$1.2 billion—up at least 4X from five years ago. But venture debt is dependent on equity deals, and so far in 2024, only US$8 billion in VC money has reached startups. Moreover, some poor bets in the past have forced venture-debt firms to now devise new criteria in picking startups they want to fund. [RBI had better explain why Navi and DMI Finance are locked out of the loan market](https://the-ken.com/story/rbi-had-better-explain-why-navi-and-dmi-finance-are-locked-out-of-the-loan-market/?utm_source=weekly_email&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=weekly_recap_card)
The regulator has singled out four non-banks for charging “usurious interest rates, frivolous penalties” even as others are playing by a similar book
In the latest in RBI’s squeeze on non-banks, it has barred four of them lending activities on the grounds of charging “usurious interest rates and frivolous penalties”. But the regulator hasn’t defined any usury ceiling for lenders. Also, if the profile of customers were similar, big banks and non-banks would charge a similar rate. Non-banks charge high interest rates because they lend to riskier customers with poor credit history, have to borrow from banks at high rates, and have higher related expenses. Introducing a usury cap, limiting cost of funds for non-banks, and rationalising processing fees will help drive financial inclusion more cheaply and responsibly. Will the RBI bite these bullets?

The Big Story

The govt gives the elderly free health cover, and insurers a kick in the pants

Seniors pay 5X the premium for just a fifth of the coverage in critical medical cases. Ayushman Bharat’s new Rs 5 lakh health cover for those over 70 aims to fix that

Health insurance for the elderly is both inadequate in coverage and exorbitant in premiums. After the age of 45, premiums spike if you have a policy; if you don’t, getting one at a reasonable rate is difficult. The government seems to have doubled down on health insurance for seniors with rule changes for insurers and a new policy for everyone over 70. Private insurers seem to have sniffed their shortcomings, with some new efforts to better their products for seniors. [Read the full story]

It’s capital

Why these scions are ditching their families’ decades-old investing style

Family offices are booming across India, going from 45 to 300 in just six years—and they’re not just a big-city affair. Just ask the millionaires in smaller commercial hubs

The number of family offices in India has increased to over 300 from 45 in just six years. But unlike in the past, wealthy Indian families are not limited to metro cities. Even in tier-2 and -3 cities, family offices are on the rise. Take Surat, for example, an industrial hub for diamonds and textiles, where the city’s rich are allocating a portion for non-core investments. Families are venturing away from real estate and gold to unconventional bets like startups, hedge funds, and more. [Read the full story]

Startups can’t get enough of venture debt. But debt can’t get enough venture capital

Startups are scrambling for venture debt as equity funding dries up, but with VC money scarce and past mistakes weighing heavy, the lenders are getting pickier

Startups, feeling the pinch from the drought in big-ticket equity funding, are knocking on venture-debt firms’ doors for some working-capital relief. In 2023 alone, venture debt came with around US$1.2 billion—up at least 4X from five years ago. But venture debt is dependent on equity deals, and so far in 2024, only US$8 billion in VC money has reached startups. Moreover, some poor bets in the past have forced venture-debt firms to now devise new criteria in picking startups they want to fund. [Read the full story]

CMOs are a dying breed. Nykaa, Nestlé India, and others are living proof

Companies are trading in the chief marketing officer for channel-specific specialists, leaving marketers to juggle tactics without a unifying strategy

As modern marketing fragments and specialisations crop up, CMOs are losing control over the product and their influence. Fixated on performance marketing and data, they are no longer tuning into the authentic voice of consumers. The focus has shifted to short-term profits, sacrificing long-term brand building. While companies want full-stack CMOs, low pay and age biases are pushing industry veterans to go solo. [Read the full story]

RBI had better explain why Navi and DMI Finance are locked out of the loan market

The regulator has singled out four non-banks for charging “usurious interest rates, frivolous penalties” even as others are playing by a similar book

In the latest in RBI’s squeeze on non-banks, it has barred four of them lending activities on the grounds of charging “usurious interest rates and frivolous penalties”. But the regulator hasn’t defined any usury ceiling for lenders. Also, if the profile of customers were similar, big banks and non-banks would charge a similar rate. Non-banks charge high interest rates because they lend to riskier customers with poor credit history, have to borrow from banks at high rates, and have higher related expenses. Introducing a usury cap, limiting cost of funds for non-banks, and rationalising processing fees will help drive financial inclusion more cheaply and responsibly. Will the RBI bite these bullets?

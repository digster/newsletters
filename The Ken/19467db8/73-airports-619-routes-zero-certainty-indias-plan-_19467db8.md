Good Morning Ishan,
Here’s a riddle: what do you call an airport without flights? In India, some call it “development” and keep spending generously to build more of them.
If you frequent India’s tier-2 and -3 cities, you’ve probably encountered the paradox of regional air travel. It promises to be affordable, accessible, and abundant—but mostly delivers cancellations, delays, and existential despair.
Take Nagpur’s airport: 300 flights cancelled, 100 delayed, and enough stranded passengers to form a small support group. Nationwide, nearly 12,000 flights were cancelled between January and September 2024—almost as many as 2022 and 2023 combined.
But here’s the thing: India is spending big on regional aviation. The government plans to invest Rs 92,000 crore to expand airports—part of a grand vision to go from 159 operational airports in 2024 to 200 this year. Sounds great, except that half the routes under the UDAN, or Ude Desh Ka Aam Nagrik—a scheme meant to make flying common in smaller cities—never even launched. And of those that did, most needed subsidies just to survive.
The cancellations aren’t just about bad luck or weather, too. Operational issues now account for 23% of cancellations on regional routes, a sharp jump from just 4% pre-Covid. Then there’s the infrastructure—or lack of it. And the airlines? A few saw the scheme as an opportunity to collect subsidies rather than run flights. Meanwhile, smaller carriers are posting cancellation rates as high as 20%.
So, what’s the result? Rs 4,500 crore spent on the first phase of UDAN, another Rs 1,000 crore earmarked for the second, and passengers wondering if they should’ve just taken the train.

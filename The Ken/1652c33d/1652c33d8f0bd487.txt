Good Morning Ishan,

Filter bubbles are the bane of digital platforms. Your views, your friends, your colleges, your family, your tastes. You can’t escape them.

Now digital platforms, in turn, are the bane of the internet. Everything is now served via a platform—groceries, books, movies, music, education and even friends. You can’t escape them. 

Take music, for instance. I have this insane six-monthly Apple Music algorithmic purge ritual where I reset it back to knowing *nothing* about my music tastes, what songs I’ve listened to, or what artists I follow. It’s the only way I can jolt myself out of a long-term bubble filled with easy nostalgia—our most formative years when it comes to music tastes are the early teens—interspersed with somewhat more effort-driven acquired tastes, subsequently. 

The evolution of our tastes peaks around 24, after which it starts retreating into familiarity. By 30, we experience the equivalent of a “musical paralysis”.

In today’s story, Karthik Srinivasan, a prolific listener, cataloguer and reviewer of music points us towards a potential cure for this paralysis that has been there all along. 

Algorithms. And playlists.

Over the course of 13 years, Karthik has reviewed over 3,000 film music albums in Hindi, Tamil, Telugu, Malayalam, Kannada, primarily, and a smattering of Punjabi, Marathi, Gujarati and Bengali. And he has a theory about the musical tastes of those living in South India. And what that’s doing to the artists who produce music in southern languages.

"Linguistic differences seem to be reducing and merging at a common point of 'South India' and technology is playing an interesting role in that merger.”

South India has always been multilingual. But now, thanks to the rise of music streaming services backed by mobile platforms, affordable 4G and powerful discovery algorithms, South India’s multilingualism is changing the way its citizens listen to music.

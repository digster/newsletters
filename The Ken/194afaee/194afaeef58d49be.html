Indiamart is bleeding sellers. And the $1.4B-mcap B2B company has no idea why
By Seetharaman G
3 min read

Less than three years ago, Indiamart Intermesh was adding roughly 10,000 suppliers every quarter who would use the business-to-business marketplace for a fee.

That number then slowed to a trickle—a rude shock to its investors and analysts.

Last week, Indiamart threw them yet another curveball when it said it lost 3,700 subscribers in the December quarter. It’s not that the classifieds portal didn’t bring in new suppliers, but more fled the platform than came on board.

View in Browser

The stock promptly shed 10% of its value after the latest quarterly results. It has tumbled by a third in six months, with its market cap currently at Rs 12,500 crore ($1.4 billion). Indiamart’s subscriber base has only fallen twice in the past five years—the last one in 2021.

The three-decade-old company depends on sellers of everything—from construction materials to toys, who pay Indiamart monthly or for a few years at once to get leads from potential buyers. The problem now for the platform is not just any supplier but those who pay the lowest—Rs 500 a month. They just don’t find the subscription as valuable as they used to. And they form a chunk of Indiamart’s 214,000 paying subscribers.

The platform’s power users have, however, softened the blow at the bottom of the pyramid. Half of Indiamart’s revenue comes from the top 10% of its subscribers.

The company is now in a bind. It doesn’t want to spend a lot on getting new subscribers before finding a way to stop its existing ones from leaving. But one way to make up for the churn, at least in the short term, is adding enough suppliers.

Indiamart cannot afford for its supplier base to shrink. It risks alienating buyers, who use the portal for free, which in turn makes new suppliers feel they are not getting enough leads.

“It’s easy for the company to give in to analysts’ demand,” said Ankit Kanodia, co-founder of Smart Sync Services, an investment-advisory firm. “It can drop prices or add new categories, but these are short-term solutions.” Kanodia added that he has recommended the stock to his clients at its current valuation. But brokerage Nuvama slashed its target price for the stock by a fifth and asked its investors to “reduce” their holdings.

Reducing the subscription fee doesn’t address Indiamart’s most intractable problem: persuading small businesses to pay. Kanodia pointed out that his family’s timber sawmill in Gujarat was one among 400 in the region. But only a couple are subscribers to Indiamart. “I couldn’t even get my dad to subscribe,” said Kanodia.

Should Indiamart fail to arrest the exodus in the next few quarters, it will have to turn to increasing the average revenue from its existing subscribers, especially the premium ones, to drive its growth. The average annual revenue from the top 10% of its subscribers is over Rs 3 lakh, or five times the number for the overall platform.

Dinesh Agarwal, chief executive of Indiamart, struck a conciliatory tone on the earnings call last week.

I think Indiamart is a slightly more refined version of [Google] with a slightly lesser TAM. So, I’m still confident that structurally there is a whole lot of opportunity available in front of us. We are trying to find that blind spot which we can fix by doing many different experiments.

Yes, you are right that it has taken five–six quarters, and nothing has resulted so far, but I think we have done it time and again in the past many years over the last two–three decades. I think we will be able to fix this.

— December quarter earnings call, Indiamart

Agarwal perhaps hopes his investors’ patience hasn’t already frayed too much.

Ed Set Go
Mon, 20 Jan 25
The one stop to understand the biggest shifts in education, written by the best edtech journalist in the country..
Good Morning Ishan,
Happy January? It’s a bit hard to muster a chirpy tone in the midst of biting cold winds and snow. When I call home to Delhi, my parents, too, pan to a foggy, miserable view from their windows. Please, someone in sunnier climes, send us a few pictures.
I’m going through a fair bit of déjà vu—the federal government in the US, where I am now, has banned the use of Tiktok across the country. Remember when we woke up to a ban on all Chinese apps in India in 2020? Here, Tiktok thinks it has a recourse in Donald Trump when he takes power on Monday.
But today’s edition isn’t about Tiktok, or Byju’s, or Unacademy. It isn’t even about Sparkl, the new AI-based edtech platform [launched](https://yourstory.com/2024/12/aakash-chaudhary-edtech--sparkl-edventure-4-million-seed-rainmatter) by Aakash Chaudhry, the former heir to the Aakash coaching empire. It isn’t—gasp—about current fave Physics Wallah either.
I want to talk about Intercell, a virtual mentoring network that connects students to professionals across the globe. Intercell was founded about seven years ago, and according to its founder and CEO Anurabh Varma, has the [potential to target](https://www.27zero.agency/en/edtech-mentor-interviews/arunabh-varma) 10 million students, with 5,000 mentors already on board. Intercell also says it’s AI-fueled: it uses the technology to match mentors to mentees, generate advice, and find the right internships for students.
But why am I talking about Intercell all of a sudden, even if its potential reach does sound impressive? Because it is one of the 22 edtech companies that have signed MoUs with the All India Centre for Technical Education (AICTE) to join the fourth cohort of NEAT, or the National Education Alliance for Technology. In the latest edition, AICTE has trimmed the number of edtechs it has signed on from 58 to 22.
It seems even the government is realising that not every edtech is worth the marketing dollars spent on its brand.
NEAT was launched in 2019 to take edtech solutions to the vast number of students who don’t have access to quality, affordable learning options online. Edtechs signing onto NEAT know that they have to provide their solutions for free to at least a portion of the student population that uses the network.
NEAT aims to be a sort of conduit for a large number of students to access good edtech products—students who have the need but don’t have the means. But it’s a difficult task to understand who the government thinks it’s worthy to sign on, and why.
The newest version of NEAT [has prioritised](https://digitallearning.eletsonline.com/2025/01/aictes-neat-4-0-focuses-on-emerging-tech-drops-overpriced-edtech-products/) “quality over quantity” and cut out edtechs that were “overpriced”, according to a press release the government sent out. As a result, the number of products being offered has come down to 22 from 58 in NEAT 3.0.
There’s also a keen focus on buzzy new-age tech terms (“AI, ML, robotics, and drones, AR/VR/MR and gamification, semiconductors and 5G technology, biomedical engineering and quantum computing”) which while undoubtedly important, are still only a vague indicator of what’s on offer.
There are a few clues, however, in who’s part of the new cohort. Intercell is one. But there are two other names that don’t look like the typical edtechs we’re used to.
Ansys Software, one of the companies mentioned in the press release, is a 50-year-old business that’s created “simulation software” for “innovators across industries to push boundaries using the predictive power of simulation.”
Ansys has a section on its website that appears to provide resources to students and teachers to work with and learn from its simulation software. But is selling edtech products its bread and butter? Unclear.
Then there’s Dheya Career Mentors, which offers mentorship products to a large audience—right from eighth grade students to mid-career professionals. Dheya was founded in 2008 as India’s “first career assessment and guidance portal”, according to the Linkedin bio of its founder and current CEO Anand Desai.
Dheya looks and feels more like an education product, though it’s hard to say if its focus is solely on AI or robotics careers. The website seems to offer more run-of-the-mill mentorship and career assessment services.
Impossible, again, to tell what criteria the government followed when they decided to put Dheya into the mix.
Here’s my question: did all the well-marketed, sought-after edtechs fail the affordability test? Did they try to match the criteria the government was after, inscrutable as it is? Didn’t they offer to provide at least a portion of their services at reduced or no cost, if for nothing else but the exposure?
The twain may never meet. But here’s a [quote](https://www.27zero.agency/en/edtech-mentor-interviews/arunabh-varma) from Anurabh Varma, the founder of Intercell, that left me even more confused about the edtechs that now comprise NEAT 4.0:
“One of our biggest challenges is balancing profit and helping students in weaker economic regions. Some institutions want our service but can only afford to pay very little. Since we’re bootstrapped and a for-profit organization, we can’t offer it for free. But it’s hard because those students really need industry mentoring.”
Is the government going to make up Intercell’s shortfall? Maybe Varma’s had a change of heart about profitability. Hard to tell, when the government puts out no information about how all of NEAT’s previous versions went, or how many students the programme serviced. I guess we’ll have to wait for NEAT 5.0.

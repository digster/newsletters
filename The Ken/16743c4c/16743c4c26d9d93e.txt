Good Morning Ishan,

Here in Bengaluru, we are moving to a new office in a week’s time. While it will be a big relief to no longer be stepping on each other’s toes and calling dibs on chairs, leaving this office won't be easy. It has seen its share of screaming matches, sleepless nights, whoops of joy, and high fives. But more than dealing with the waves of nostalgia, I think I’m wary about what the new office will feel like. Everyone promises that it will be bigger, better and brighter. But I will reserve my judgement till I find my writing nook.

We're not the only ones going through a time of change though. Many of the companies we wrote about this week are also dealing with the eternal truth—nothing in life is constant except change.

Take the television industry, for example. A sector notoriously resistant to change. On Monday, Harveen observed https://the-ken.com/story/tv-industry-subscriptions-era/?utm_source=recap&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter how a regulatory overhaul in the TV industry has got companies kicking and screaming. This was brought on by the telecom and broadcasting regulator Trai instituting a new tariff framework that will change the age-old rules of TV channel pricing, packaging, and distribution across the country.

Then there's Vodafone and Idea. Their merger was seen as a match made in heaven. But the merged entity—Vodafone Idea—is in a bad way. Its maiden quarterly results were a massive disappointment. Shashi, in our Thursday story, https://the-ken.com/story/vodafone-idea-merger-results/?utm_source=recap&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter says the company's first ever analyst meeting was only meant to make the newlyweds look good. But really, there is no sidestepping reeling debt and fleeing customers. This was our free read of the week. Do read and share.

Vodafone and Idea sought out change, and it may not have worked. But for some, change works exactly as they hoped. Gayathri's story highlights https://the-ken.com/story/tierra-agrotech/?utm_source=recap&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter just this. She wrote about a little-known company called Tierra Agrotech, which had ambitions of being India’s Monsanto. So, it went ahead and bought the cotton businesses of giants Monsanto and DuPont Pioneer to become Asia’s largest cotton seed company.

In other cases, change is the only way to survive. Which is what Ruhi’s Wednesday read https://the-ken.com/story/indian-hospitals-greener-pastures/?utm_source=recap&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter was all about. She wrote of how hospitals like Narayana Hrudayalaya have decided to expand abroad rather than in India as measures like price control have left Indian hospitals looking overseas for succour.

Sometimes change come flying thick and fast. And reacting, dodging and staying on top of it determines survival. That is Dunzo for you. For our Friday read, Rohin had a https://the-ken.com/story/a-dunzo-button-for-mr-biswas/?utm_source=recap&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter freewheeling interview with the on-demand delivery service app’s CEO Kabeer Biswas. And he gets down to answering what Dunzo needs to do to keep ticking. Keep an eye out for part two of the interview which will be part of next week's offerings.

And as we gear up to bring you this Sunday's story, let's not forget our last TKOW piece. Ashish's story was https://the-ken.com/story/the-media-buying-business-has-hit-rock-bottom/?utm_source=recap&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter a cautionary tale of how companies that don’t change at all are beyond redemption. It brings to light the rot in the media buying industry. A rot brought about by practices that were perpetrated and perpetuated over the last decade

Just how creditworthy are Cred’s users? If Cred is doing what it says it's doing, bankers think it should be a whole lot bigger than it is today
By Arundhati Ramanathan
7 min read
View in browser

Good Morning Ishan,

Kunal Shah was in a relaxed mood at Cred’s press conference on Saturday morning, when the company released its financials for the year ended March 2024. It’s been 16 years for Shah in fintech, and in a light moment, he called himself the “uncle of this category”.

But he’s better known these days as the founder of the six-year-old credit card payments platform, and it’s only over the last three years that Cred (valued at $6 billion currently) has begun monetising. 

According to the company, it’s been able to do this by acquiring high-quality users. Shah reiterated that Cred’s focus remains steady on India’s most creditworthy, since it’s the top 10% of households who drive 60% of consumption. Even with UPI, he said, it was the top 30–40 million that drove billions of UPI transactions. 

And out of that target audience, Cred claims to have about 13 million monthly active users. For the financial year ended March 2024, it saw revenue rise 66% to nearly Rs 2,500 crore (US$298 million), and losses shrink 41% to about Rs 600 crore (US$71 million). And its monthly transacting users grew at 34%.

What it has going for it is average revenue per user (ARPU). Cred’s ARPU looks quite a bit better than even Phonepe’s, which has nearly 20 times the users, double the revenue, but only one-tenth of Cred’s Rs 1,900 (US$23) ARPU. Cred says it has monetised roughly about a third of its monthly active users. And this monetisable base expanded 58% from a year ago. 

All this is definitely good news for a company that has been constantly hit with the question, “But how does Cred make money?” 

Cred’s revenue comes from three top sources: fees earned from banks when credit card holders make their payments on Cred, and from distributing insurance and loans. In fact, distributing loans is an important part of how it makes money—it has distributed credit worth Rs 15,000 crore (US$1.8 billion) currently. (For comparison: Paytm* is the largest distributor of loans among fintechs, accounting for over Rs 50,000 crore, or US$6 billion, in the year ended March 2024.)

Cred says it does not present the option to take a loan for many of its users. And while a little more than a third of them are qualified to borrow, only about 10% have taken on a loan. According to Shah, Cred has taken a deliberately conservative approach here. 

“We are an unusual fintech that way,” he said, adding that while banks do all the underwriting and come up with the rules, it is Cred that tells the banks to be conservative, because it doesn’t not want to contribute to an overheated credit market. 

Well, one cannot pick holes with a conservative approach, especially with credit. 

But if Cred’s platform has the most creditworthy and risk-free users, then it should be a lot bigger—and a lot more popular with big banks—than it is right now.

The gap

At the press conference, Shah said Cred’s focus on prime and super-prime customers made him “popular with the chief risk officers of banks in India versus the CEOs.”

“We’ve ultimately been a sourcing partner, and would like to make sure that banks do not have any surprises.”

Right now, Cred works with lending partners like IDFC First Bank, DBS Bank, and non-banks like Credit Saison, Vivriti Capital. More recently, it also started working with Yes Bank and L&T Finance. All of them disburse loans on Cred’s platform, and Cred makes a commission on distribution. And according to Shah, the platform will strike more relationships with other banks in the future, though he did not disclose which ones. “We see more and more top institutions wanting to work with us, and some of them exclusively wanting to work with us.”

One reason he cited for this is the company’s affluent base. In fact, a source associated with Cred claimed that 60% of all new credit cards issued in India are to existing Cred members, that 65–70% of Cred members invest in equities, and that 30% of outstanding home loans are with Cred members. 

But, a banker I spoke to recently wasn’t as enamoured with Cred as you might think. Banks like his are not so keen to partner with Cred just yet, he said, and their primary grouse is: “Why should I give out my loan on Cred and then pay a commission when I can give it directly to my customer?”. This is also why big banks are absent from even platforms such as Paytm*.

There’s also the question of just how creditworthy Cred users actually are. A financial executive aware of the matter who works with Cred said that the average salary of a Cred user is Rs 6–7 lakh (US$7,150–8,340) per year. “So how can they be the crème de la creme?” Cred’s spokesperson says the company doesn’t track the income data of its users. 

To be sure, low-income customers can be creditworthy and high-income ones may be bad credit risks. Intent to repay is not dependent on income. 

But according to the banker, Cred should also be seeing many more of its users qualify for loans if it has a highly creditworthy set of users. After all, even for large private banks, more than 30% of credit-card users would be eligible for a personal loan. And these are all kinds of credit card users, not just the “top” ones that Cred says it has. Cred also gatekeeps its platform and onboards only those with a credit score over 750 credit score.

“So if we are talking of a base of all high-quality users in the platform, then at least half of those users should be able to qualify for a loan.”

Anyway, that someone with a credit score of 750 is a good user is also a myth that needs to be busted, because in India, the credit score assigned by credit bureaus is largely broken. 

In order to calculate a proper credit score, bureaus would need to give equal weightage to five criteria: repayment history, utilisation of credit limit, number of loan enquiries, the mix of secured and unsecured loans one has, and the age/vintage of the credit history. “But the bureaus give an inordinate amount of weightage to just repayment behaviour,” explained the financial services executive. 

Which means that credit scores aren’t always a measure of good creditworthiness. And Cred will only get some real cred once most of its “high-quality” users can get a loan on the platform. 

*Paytm founder Vijay Shekhar Sharma is an investor in The Ken

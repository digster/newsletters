The one stop to understand the biggest shifts in education, written by the best edtech journalist in the country..
Good Morning Ishan,
We don’t live in an era of static job responsibilities. Every job now evolves, demands new skills, and raises the bar on performance when you see others in the same role doing something better or different. Even if your job is monotonous, I bet at some point, you did something as simple as learn a new hack on Excel or any other software that you use, draw up a list of sympathetic clients, or time your request for an extension just so that your boss agrees.
It’s all an attempt to adapt to our changing work circumstances, which taken to their extreme, mean learning constantly on the job. In biz-school parlance, we call it upskilling.
As a concept, upskilling has been part of the Indian edtech landscape from the very beginning. Traditional upskilling courses on platforms like Upgrad, Eruditus, Great Learning, and Udemy have rubbed shoulders with newer-age formats like the now-defunct Stoa School, Masai, and Scaler. Even Physicswallah launched an upskilling arm a few years ago, because of the potential to grow in a still-young country with a giant working population.
Now, the challenge with running an upskilling startup is the hype cycle. It’s easier to convince people that they need to learn the latest tech or marketing skills, but much harder to actually show how those skills help someone’s career. Upskilling companies can’t promise placements or promotions with any degree of certainty, and India’s working population, while vast, doesn’t have huge amounts of disposable income to spare on extra learning.
That’s why staffing firm Teamlease’s [edtech report](https://www.teamleaseedtech.com/pdf/the-impact-of-upskilling-on-performance-appraisals.pdf) makes for an interesting read. The company surveyed over 14,000 working professionals about what kind of upskilling courses they spend on, how they fund these, and what they got out of it.
Here are the highlights:
1. Long-duration degrees and diplomas reign supreme. Almost 70% of those surveyed said these courses give them a better shot at career growth.
2. Professionals want to learn the technical skills required in their field; they are less focused on soft skills.
3. Amongst the professionals who’ve taken an upskilling course, over 40% said they paid for it themselves; only 23% took company-sponsored courses.
This is an interesting matrix of choices for someone wanting to start an upskilling company. Do you go niche and offer short-format technical courses, or do you go after university partnerships that can add heft to your portfolio of long-term courses and diplomas? Do you offer soft skills at all?
The other consideration around upskilling is pricing. According to Teamlease’s data, people in tech funded their own education at a higher rate than those in other sectors.

It’s possible that people in tech are seeing quicker returns on their investment.
Fifty-three percent of respondents said their courses helped them improve “real-time job performance”, while 42% said they were promoted or got a salary hike within 18 months of doing a course.
People also do more upskilling courses closer to their appraisals, but the data shows that about 30% of professionals upskill themselves without any specific expectations of enhancement or promotions. These all-weather learners could also be an interesting demographic to pick up on.
For upskilling companies, the pool of potential customers is much smaller than for test-prep edtechs. But the spend per customer could be higher, which makes the statistic around using one’s own funds to upskill very interesting. Could edtechs push companies to match their employee’s investment? Or help them institute an incentive program, especially for those who invest in their own growth? This could open doors for other customers who’ve been on the fence about an upskilling course.

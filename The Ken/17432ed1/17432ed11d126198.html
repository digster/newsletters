The race for the supermart top spot in India's $460 billion grocery industry is in its last lap.
 
Storied business houses like the Aditya Birla Group and the Godrej Group have admitted defeat in the grocery segment and sold their supermarket chains. The Tatas haven't buckled yet, but the joint venture between their retail company Trent and UK's Tesco doesn't have much to show either.
 
Sixteen years in, Star has just 57 outlets, compared to Reliance Retail's 797 and DMart's 214. Suddenly, what retail professionals say today—scale is the only survival strategy—begins to make sense.
 
When Tesco invested $140 million in the joint venture in 2014, it became the first foreign retailer to enter India's multi-brand retail sector. It raised hopes for other foreign brands and should have been a turning point for the then 10-year-old Star. But it wasn't.
 
For the year ended March 2020, Trent Hypermarket, which operates Star, grossed Rs 1,235 crore ($165 million) in revenue. A mere 3.6% of Reliance Retail's grocery revenue and just 5% of DMart's sales.
 
A lack of scale, missing frugality, and a near-constant identity crisis has brought Trent to a point in the race where, as the last lap legends go, every incremental step is critical. Reliance and DMart are elbowing it out.
 
In his story today, Seetharaman argues that Star needs to establish its identity and stick to it. Now, what is that elusive identity?
 
And can Trent give up its experimentation which, paradoxically, has rested on a belief that "If you know you are going to lose money, what's the point in spending money"?

Good Morning Ishan,
 
Indian tech startups that went public to much fanfare last year are way off their highs. And venture capitalists have hit pause on writing eye-popping cheques simply because that was the norm for a hot minute.
 
But there's no turning back the clock on some founders' enviable status in India's startup ecosystem. Budding entrepreneurs want them on their cap table so that they can pick their brains on how to build a leadership team or when to raise funds. Or just to have bragging rights.
 
The number of angel investments in India surged by two-thirds to nearly 670 in 2021, driven in no small measure by founder-turned-angels. Kunal Shah, the founder of credit card payments startup CRED, has invested in over 150 companies.
 
But what happens when these founders' own companies want to invest in or acquire ventures they have backed?
 
CRED, for instance, is close to buying investment platform smallcase, which counts Shah among its investors.
 
Zomato got a lot of flak for putting money into instant grocery delivery startup Blinkit when the foodtech major's founder and CEO Deepinder Goyal had invested in the company in his personal capacity.
 
One can rely on the capital markets regulator Sebi and proxy advisory firms to keep an eye on such transgressions in listed companies.
 
But what about privately-held startups, which are free of such oversight? What kind of guardrails should they have?

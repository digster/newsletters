Today’s story is a real inside view of the power play between India’s payments platforms.

Juspay was ready to seal the deal and raise over $100 million last year from Kedaara Capital, Warburg Capital, and Westbridge. These investors believed that Juspay’s status as a payments orchestrator—a centralised platform that places a range of fintech service providers in one place—made it an enviable investment target. The funding round would have taken Juspay’s valuation well above $1 billion.

But when Phonepe, Razorpay, Cashfree, and Paytm* all revoked support for Juspay in early 2025, the “aggregator of aggregators” lost its cachet. The funding it managed to raise was many millions lower than what it wanted. Juspay now needs to pit itself against the other platforms and hold on for dear life.

A Razorpay executive, meanwhile, told The Ken, “This cannot be a surprise. We had highlighted to Juspay over six months ago that continued promotion of its own PA over others could have implications on our partnership.”

Juspay believes merchants will stick with it. Razorpay and its peers say 80% of the merchants that use their services will remain with them. Both can’t be true.

This showdown has been unfolding over months. It now comes down to which platform can appeal to merchants and keep hold of their business. It’s the nastiest fight that India’s payments sector has ever witnessed. Arundhati’s coverage today will tell you more.

Last month, Optiemus Infracom partnered with a Chinese mobile company to produce AIoT products. It also joined hands with a Taiwanese drone maker to produce drones in India. The stock market seems elated—Optiemus’ scrip is up about 20% in the last one month. That said, this is a company with no history of making drones.

In this week’s Long and Short, Seema looks at how this company is avoiding Chinese and embracing Taiwanese even as India continues to fiddle with the definition of “local”.

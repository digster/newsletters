I’ve been privy to discussions where Indian healthcare entrepreneurs and international business consultants have argued ad nauseam that after IT services, India could be an export powerhouse for healthcare services. That, like Cuba, the country should position itself accordingly.
 
In the past, a few hospitals have ventured overseas, but there hasn’t been a notable success like in IT services. On the contrary, the international expansions of hospital group Fortis and health tech company Practo in recent years have ended up as dumpster fires.
 
But as today’s story shows, working at the world’s lowest price points, Indian healthcare companies indeed have a natural advantage. Provided they innovate and stay the course. Last month, India’s largest dialysis chain, NephroPlus, acquired a 51% stake in Philippines’ dialysis care chain RCDC, and considers it this the first step in its quest for Asian dominance.
 
Since we first wrote about NephroPlus in 2018, it has also acquired the Indian operations of the world’s second largest kidney care provider DaVita and raised $45 million. How it has built its 229 centre network in India is how it intends to grow in Asia. Except, it won’t be squeezed like in its domestic market, where most of its business comes from onerous out-of-pocket spending.
 
In the Philippines and the rest of Asia, Nephroplus-RCDC will target the sub-US$100 market where 80% of patients will have nearly full coverage. Yet, to achieve its ambitions, the NephroPlus-RCDC combine must wade through a crowded market, with at least five big local chains and dozens of small ones jostling for space.
 
In what is a truly cross-border story, Olina in Delhi and Jum in Manila, get deep into the business model and economics of kidney care at a time when rising diabetes numbers have made dialysis a life-saving service.

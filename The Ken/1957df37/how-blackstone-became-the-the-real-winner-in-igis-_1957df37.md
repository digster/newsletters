Good Morning Ishan,
If you hear the sound of jingling jewellery, it’s India’s diamond exporters shaking.
Tariffs from the White House have the sector in anguish because the United States is its biggest buyer. The fear is that India could lose 50% of its gems and jewellery exports.
One of the businesses that would be impacted by that stall is the International Gemological Institute’s India arm, which went public in December 2024. It’s an important part of the global diamond trade, currently controlling 33% global market share in all gems and jewellery certification, making it the second-largest provider of this service.
With nine out of 10 natural diamonds mined worldwide being cut and polished in Surat, those stones’ clarity, carat weight, colour, and other qualities need to be certified so buyers and sellers can determine a fair price for them.
Where IGI truly shines, however, is in lab-grown diamond certification. Rolling with the times, it now signs off on the quality level of 65% of lab-grown diamonds that are sold around the world. This has turned out as a great hedge: while natural diamonds lose lustre, IGI India remains relevant by grading the synthetic stones that are ending up in consumers’ jewellery boxes.
But that leads to a more fundamental question: Why do lab-grown gemstones need certification to begin with? Debanjali’s [story](https://the-ken.com/story/how-blackstone-became-the-real-winner-in-igis-global-dominance-in-lab-grown-gems/) gets you the bigger picture.

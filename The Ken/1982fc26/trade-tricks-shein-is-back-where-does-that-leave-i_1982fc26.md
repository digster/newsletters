Trade Tricks
Tue, 22 Jul 25
A weekly newsletter about the biggest changes in commerce—focusing on shifts that matter to you..
Good Morning Ishan,
When I made my first Newme purchase, it was a last-minute resort, practically forced down my throat. I needed a very specific outfit for a friend’s goth-themed birthday party (we don’t talk about that party, so I won’t say anymore), and the only place I could find one was at Newme.
Of course, I’d known about the fast-fashion brand for a while. You can’t cover this beat and not at least hear about the “Shein of India”, which is what some touted Newme as when it first launched in 2022. Shein—the Chinese ultra-fast-fashion giant—had been booted out of the country just a year ago, and there was a space in the rapidly growing market that Newme, and others like it, rushed to fill.
And they did it by essentially replicating Shein’s low-inventory, fast-turnover playbook. Backed by Accel, Fireside Ventures, and AUM Partners, Newme raised $18 million last year and clocked Rs 180 crore in revenue in FY25, with several lakh orders processed every month. Others have found similar success.
But now Shein is back. This time, supported by one of India’s largest conglomerates—Reliance.
So, how do brands that have built their businesses on the Shein model compete against Shein itself?
Sumit Jasoria, co-founder and CEO, Newme, acknowledges the challenge of having a Reliance-backed Shein in the market, but says it doesn’t really change much for Newme. “We always knew that someday (Shein) will come back,” he told me during a recent chat.
Size certainly helps, but he believes the real key to success in fast fashion is cracking execution. “That means getting good designs, amazing price points, and getting customers.”
But it also means acing your supply chain and putting in place a strong data-driven tech framework. After all, the Chinese apparel retailer revolutionised fast fashion by nimbly catering to constantly changing trends while maintaining minimal inventory.
According to Jasoria, Newme drops a new collection (500 fresh designs) every Friday at 3 p.m. And within 24 hours, its proprietary demand-prediction model forecasts the required quantity for each style. The prediction efficiency of this system has improved from 50% two years ago to 70% now, he added, which allows the business to maintain very low inventories.
It’s also why Newme has doubled down on its investment in data science, demand prediction, and inventory management, Jasoria said.
The company operates 15 Mumbai factories, but imports around 60% of its products from China.
Newme, however, also has something Shein never did—a brick-and-mortar presence. The Indian brand operates 14 stores currently and Jasoria thinks the omnichannel approach will be a strong differentiator, with the company drawing lessons from fashion retailers such as Zudio and Vishal Mega Mart who have a strong physical footprint.
To be sure, the market Shein has entered is hardly the one it exited in 2021. Five years ago, the Chinese giant was the undisputed pioneer of ultra-fast fashion, processing 20,000–50,000 orders everyday before being banned. Now, it plays in a [crowded field](https://restofworld.org/2025/shein-india-reliance-amazon-flipkart/), in which Newme is only one of the many competitors.
There’s also the nature of Shein’s partnership with Reliance, where the Indian conglomerate has secured the rights to sell Shein-branded products in the market, but is nevertheless having to figure out pretty much everything else on its own—including manufacturing, sales, and supply chain. Shuchi Pandya, principal at Fireside Ventures, which backs Newme, says this means “execution is super critical (for Shein)”, and that it will have to sort out “inventory and make sure the design language is superior to others”.
Reliance’s sheer size and knowledge of the Indian market will certainly help. But it might also knock a couple of points off Shein’s nimbleness.
As Jasoria puts it, if you are a Titanic, you’ll need a bit of time to change course.

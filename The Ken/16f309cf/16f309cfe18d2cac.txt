Good Morning Ishan,

India is burning!  

And pollution levels are off the charts. The terrible air quality has already shaved off 1.7 years from the average Indian lifespan, one study says. 

The Supreme Court minced no words last month: "We have become a laughing stock. The government cannot provide clean air and water to the citizens in its capital city. What is the point of all this development? What is the point of being a world power?"

The Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB) says India needs 4,000 official monitors to get an accurate representation of air quality. It currently has 793!

But, where there is adversity, there is opportunity. Air quality data is the newest goldmine for environmental start-ups, reports Olina. 

The likes of AMBEE, Kaiterra, and AirVeda are making low-cost, personalised monitors for homes, schools and offices—a network that tracks indoor air quality. These monitors can tell you whether it is safe to open the window (No, mostly!) or buy another set of pollution masks (Yes, obviously!). They can even identify pollution hotspots such as parking lots.

This is precisely the sort of hyperlocal data that could augment the government's patchy monitoring network.

Except the CPCB refuses, citing the absence of official standards to certify these monitors or their data. This, when we are at least 5-10 years away from setting certification standards for commercial air monitors, says one government official.

That, however, has not slowed down these start-ups. They have had the odd tie-up with municipal corporations and some government-related projects.

A drop in the ocean, but already better than the Supreme Court's reading that pollution levels are so dangerous that citizens would rather be "finished off with explosives" than suffer a slow, painful death.

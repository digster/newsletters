Accelerator programmes are designed to turn the most promising startups of today into the tech giants of tomorrow through a combination of investment and mentoring.
And no accelerator has delivered on that premise better than Mountain View-based Y Combinator (YC).
The famed US programme has graduated giants like cloud storage firm Dropbox, which listed on the Nasdaq in 2018; travel giant Airbnb, which is tipped to go public next year; and major startups like Stripe, the online payment company that's valued at $36 billion.
They are just three of the more than 20 billion-dollar 'unicorn' companies in the YC alumni network. That level of success is unparalleled.
India has seen some slice of this action. The country was the second-largest supplier of startups in YC's most recent cohort, accounting for 10% of the 240 participating companies. To date, YC has backed over 50 startups from India.
But despite its hallowed reputation, YC hasn't actually cracked India.
- It has no Indian unicorns—although Razorpay is said to be close to raising a monster round
- It doesn't have a physical presence in India and local rivals have upped their game—Sequoia's Surge programme offers more lucrative terms
- India's startup ecosystem has matured—founders can build rich networks locally and mentors have practical insight into India that can't be found in the US
Can YC find its mojo and anoint India's future startup kings? Or will it have to contend with slim pickings? Sumanth asks and answers the question with his typical insight and depth.

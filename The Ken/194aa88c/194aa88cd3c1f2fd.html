Pidilite, Birla, and the fight for glory in paints. The Johnny-come-latelies are already hurting the incumbents, including Asian Paints

“‘Buy commodities, sell brands’ has long been a formula for business success.” 

So said Warren Buffett in his annual letter to Berkshire Hathaway shareholders in 2012. The canny investor may have been referring to the likes of consumer-product giants Coca-Cola and Wrigley.

But his remark applies just as much to decorative paints in India. 

Unlike fast-moving consumer goods, which have always been inextricably linked to brands, paints were once largely bought and sold as commodities. Until Asian Paints came along.

Imagine making Indians seek out a brand of something they don’t consume, wear, or display in their living room, and buy just as frequently as a television. 

This is what Asian Paints did. 

Combining an iconic mascot created by the famed cartoonist RK Laxman in 1954 with some memorable marketing campaigns certainly contributed to Asian Paints’ rise.

Asian Paints’ glorious success is no longer predictable, Trade Tricks by The Ken

In the decades after its launch, Asian Paints was the Indian paints industry. Even now, its market share is over 50%—the kind of dominance deep-pocketed challengers love to chip away at. The JSW Group, which runs one of India’s largest steelmakers, launched its paints business in 2019. Three years later, building-materials platform Infra.Market bought into Shalimar Paints. 

In early 2024, the Aditya Birla Group, one of India’s biggest conglomerates, unveiled its own paints venture. The chief executive of the business had no qualms taking a dig at his older rivals on an earnings call in November:

“It was a question of a market leader and everybody else following it like the Pied Piper of Hamelin. We have kind of broken that comfortable ecosystem and I think it’s for the benefit of the consumer because there is a better product, there is more choice.”

Around the same time as Birla’s entry, Pidilite Industries threw its hat in the ring too. 

Pidilite is Asian Paints on steroids. We think even less about adhesives than we do about paints. But ask any Indian to name a brand of adhesives, and Fevicol will quickly roll off their tongues. Pidilite made that happen. 

The new entrants have expectedly set the ball rolling on consolidation, with a number of companies reportedly circling the Indian unit of Dulux Paints owner Akzo Nobel India, worth about US$2 billion. Among the potential acquirers are JSW Paints, Pidilite, Berger Paints, and Indigo Paints. 

Why exactly are companies so eager to be in this business? 

Kuldip Raina, director of sales and marketing at Shalimar Paints, gave me three statistics that immediately, even if partly, answered the question: 

  • More than half the surfaces in rural areas that can be painted are not yet painted. 
  • Two thirds of Indians live there. 
  • Just four paint brands (Asian Paints, Berger, Kansai Nerolac, Dulux) control 85–90% of the market. 

“Birla Opus has brought in the kind of price disruption we have never seen in paints,” said Raina. The price difference between the top four players is 5–6%, with Asian Paints commanding the highest, he added. But Birla has launched its products at a 12% discount to Asian Paints. 

Birla has another inherent advantage over the other newcomers. Grasim Industries, which owns Birla Opus, sells white cement, and 70% of white-cement dealers sell paints too. The overlap in steel is a mere 5% and in adhesives, 20%. It’s for this reason that JK Cement, the other large white-cement maker, bought a regional paint brand in late 2022. 

Paints are a tricky business. Unlike cement, steel, and adhesives, which have distributors (middlemen) like FMCG, paints are a direct-to-dealer commodity. There are 5,000 stock-keeping units in paints, said Raina, and that’s too many to handle for any distributor.

Scale in paints is also tied to the ability to persuade both consumers and “influencers” such as architects, interior designers, and painters. “How many times have you bought paint yourself?” asked Raina. “About 60% of paint sales happens through influencers. Asian Paints has been targeting both for years.” 

But the Johnny-come-latelies have already begun to hurt the old guard. After growing at roughly 15% a year in the four financial years to FY23, the incumbents’ top line expanded by just 4% in FY24 and even fell in the next six months. 

The pain extends to profitability too. Asian Paints’ operating margins in the September quarter slumped to 16% from 24% in the corresponding period of the previous year. 

A quarter of Asian Paints’ market value has evaporated over the past year. In contrast, the benchmark Nifty50 index is up 5%. 

After the launch of Birla Opus, I wrote:

One analyst could barely conceal his excitement at Grasim’s attention-grabbing launch: “Grand. Audacious. Scaled-up startup.” Another analyst said Asian Paints continuing to be numero uno, while likely, is not a “shoo-in”. 

Despite a number of incumbents and new entrants, the business of paints has largely been the story of just one company. 

Maybe it’s time for a new protagonist.

I just didn’t expect it to play out so soon.

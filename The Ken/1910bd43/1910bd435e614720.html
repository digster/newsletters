Good Morning Ishan,

We’re interested in a range of subjects here at The Ken, and today’s as good a day as any to give you a little taste.

First up, we have the third episode of our latest, premium podcast—Two by Two. Seasoned retail consultant Arvind Singhal and I joined the hosts, Rohin and Praveen, to talk about Zepto, the rapid-delivery startup.

It’s not just another company that has raised hundreds of millions of dollars. It unleashed something that changed the way urban Indians shop online. But it also makes claims that are both audacious and absurd, like this one: “We will surpass Dmart in 18–24 months”.

So what is Zepto’s angle here? How did Amazon and Flipkart not see it coming? Will quick commerce leapfrog supermarkets and e-commerce as we know it? Will Zepto surpass both Blinkit and Swiggy in the race they started? And the question rings in your mind, Is Zepto a gold medalist or a bronze medalist in this quick commerce race?

Next, we have a fascinating, expansive interview with a former Texas Instruments (TI) executive who went on to start his own semiconductor company. No, we’re not referring to the legendary Morris Chang, who founded chip-making giant Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company (TSMC) at 55.

P Raja Manickam, who worked with TI in the early 1980s, turned entrepreneur at 48. But he sold his first venture, Tessolve, a semiconductor-services company, to Hero Electronics a few years ago. Then came a pit stop at a unit of Tata Electronics.

Now, he’s on to his second startup, iVP Semiconductor, which could be a game changer for a country that’s woefully late to anything to do with semiconductors, never mind all those headlines about the government’s US$10 billion subsidy to kick-start the industry.

Manickam knows India is on the back foot here. “If I start designing a chip, then getting to the fab is a three-year process. I don’t have three years of my life to do that; I’m too old for that,” he told Seema.

So he wants to source chip designs from elsewhere and take them to fabs in Taiwan and Japan he has tie-ups with and bring the chips to his customers in India. “I can find the best possible IP, and my product will be better than TI’s.” And in the process, he is eyeing a top line of US$70–100 million in three years.

The interview is as much an expert’s take on India’s chances in this space as it is a sexagenarian entrepreneur’s reflections on what it takes to start afresh.

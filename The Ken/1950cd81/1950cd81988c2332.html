This Titan jewellery brand found its perfect match in the crazy rich 40+ Indian woman

Zoya, the ultra-luxury jewellery brand, with 15 years of costly patience behind it, is finally profitable and aiming for Rs 400 crore in FY25—proving exclusivity, not scale, drives its luxury appeal

Zoya has a unique approach to turning its ultra-luxury jewellery business profitable—sell the notion of exclusivity and craftsmanship to affluent women above the age of 40. The company is targeting steady growth over scale—with only 12 outlets in strategic neighbourhoods, IP-protected designs and stone-cuts, and exclusive manufacturers. While over half its customers are repeat buyers, the company wants to turn the rest of its uber-rich clients into brand loyalists. And so it’s wooing them by hosting cocktail parties, and organising cruises and safaris. While global brands like Forevermark and Bvlgari are entering the domestic market, Zoya’s boutique-chain model remains unique, giving it a first-mover advantage. [Read the full story]

Rebirths and renewals

Wework has to kill its former self to be reborn a listed company

Its ability to enthuse public-market investors depends entirely on how far it’s willing to go from “co-working”

Despite Wework’s collapse, the workspace provider’s Indian business is thriving, still leading the market, and now prepping for an IPO. But the industry as a whole is moving towards the managed office model, which means Wework will have to shift gears and move away from what it knows best: co-working. This pivot could help Wework lock in long-term, high-volume deals and reduce customer churn. But Wework isn’t the only player in the game—rivals like Awfis, already ahead of the curve and now listed, are ready to give it a run for its money. [Read the full story]

Wysa’s Jo Aggarwal is betting AI can fix what social media and job uncertainty broke

Mental health care is broken, and so is its business model. Wysa thinks it has a fix—starting with the US

Between genetic inheritance, early life experiences, what happens to our body and in the world temporarily and what is happening right now, no two people have a mental-health experience that conforms with anyone else’s. AI-led chatbots have been around, but with generative AI, the prospects of a personalised, effective therapist look more promising than ever. Wysa believes offering AI-based self-care management is the way to equip millions of young people graduating every year and entering an uncertain job market. Because people remain unemployable not so much for a lack of hard skills as a lack of resilience. [Read the full story]

In Hexaware’s return to the bourses, Carlyle will make a killing. Guess who won’t?

Two private equity giants have much to smile about from the mid-tier IT firm’s delisting in 2020 and its relisting in 2025. For most others, it’s a whole lot of nothing 

Hexaware’s ongoing Rs 8,750 crore IPO is turning heads, becoming India’s largest IT services public offering yet. Its promoter, Carlyle, is bagging top dollar in quick time now, just like its former promoter, Baring did in 2021. Unlike these PE giants, though, public investors may have little to gain from this IPO. Pricey valuation, weak growth, and market uncertainties combine to create a perfect storm. Gold-leasing apps ‘assure’ up to 21% returns. It’s a P2P play escaping RBI’s crackdown

Gullak, Safegold, Plusgold, and others promise easy money, but in an unregulated market with no safety net for investors, even industry insiders are sounding the alarm

By promising 15–21% returns, platforms like Gullak, Fiydaa, Plusgold, and Safegold are making digital-gold leasing hard to resist. Aggressive advertisements offering these “attractive” returns could be seen as misleading, given there are no fixed market returns on gold. The companies, who act as intermediaries to lease out gold to “trusted” jewellers, may not be qualified enough to assess their creditworthiness. The digital-gold leasing model, besides being unregulated, is akin to peer-to-peer lending—an area that the RBI has increased scrutiny on. [Read the full story]

Zoya, the ultra-luxury jewellery brand, with 15 years of costly patience behind it, is finally profitable and aiming for Rs 400 crore in FY25—proving exclusivity, not scale, drives its luxury appeal
Zoya has a unique approach to turning its ultra-luxury jewellery business profitable—sell the notion of exclusivity and craftsmanship to affluent women above the age of 40. The company is targeting steady growth over scale—with only 12 outlets in strategic neighbourhoods, IP-protected designs and stone-cuts, and exclusive manufacturers. While over half its customers are repeat buyers, the company wants to turn the rest of its uber-rich clients into brand loyalists. And so it’s wooing them by hosting cocktail parties, and organising cruises and safaris. While global brands like Forevermark and Bvlgari are entering the domestic market, Zoya’s boutique-chain model remains unique, giving it a first-mover advantage. [Gold-leasing apps ‘assure’ up to 21% returns. It’s a P2P play escaping RBI’s crackdown](https://the-ken.com/story/gold-leasing-apps-assure-up-to-21-returns-its-a-p2p-play-escaping-rbis-crackdown/?utm_source=weekly_email&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=weekly_recap_card)
Gullak, Safegold, Plusgold, and others promise easy money, but in an unregulated market with no safety net for investors, even industry insiders are sounding the alarm
By promising 15–21% returns, platforms like Gullak, Fiydaa, Plusgold, and Safegold are making digital-gold leasing hard to resist. Aggressive advertisements offering these “attractive” returns could be seen as misleading, given there are no fixed market returns on gold. The companies, who act as intermediaries to lease out gold to “trusted” jewellers, may not be qualified enough to assess their creditworthiness. The digital-gold leasing model, besides being unregulated, is akin to peer-to-peer lending—an area that the RBI has increased scrutiny on.

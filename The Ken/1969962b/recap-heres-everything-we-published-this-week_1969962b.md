Grand claims and the pitfalls of excess plague protein products in a country that can’t get enough of such foods
Urban consumers are increasingly turning to protein-fortified products like chips, yoghurt, and lassi over natural foods to meet their protein needs. The protein market is booming, worth $1.5 billion and growing at a compound rate of 6.5% with Delhi NCR leading the pack. A 2024 study found 70% of protein supplements in India were mislabeled, with 14% containing toxins. Protein marketing has evolved from bodybuilding imagery to a more lifestyle-oriented angle, targeting all kinds of urban consumers. [Zomato, Firstcry, and others love this ‘magical’ number. You should hate it](https://the-ken.com/story/zomato-firstcry-and-others-love-this-magical-number-you-should-hate-it/?utm_source=weekly_email&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=weekly_recap_card)
With adjusted Ebitda, accounting again manifests as the art of the possible
India’s internet companies love reporting adjusted Ebitda, an unusual metric of profitability, than their actual net profit or loss. By their logic, adjusted Ebitda is an “additional tool” to help investors compare business operations over multiple periods. But experts believe the not-so-real bottom line actually helps no one other than these companies themselves. What’s good for the management may not be good for investors, and so, it may be time for the regulators to get into the act. [Inspired by Nokia, a $3B manufacturer charts a path through Trump’s tariff turmoil](https://the-ken.com/story/how-zetwerk-built-a-nokia-shaped-escape-hatch-from-the-tariff-chaos/?searchTerm=zetwerk&utm_source=weekly_email&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=weekly_recap_card)
Faced with a fresh wave of tariffs and trade turbulence, Zetwerk is scattering factories across borders, blending supply chains, and betting that predictability will trump price in the new manufacturing order
Zetwerk has been planting factories around the world—a strategy that suddenly looks very smart now that tariffs are back in fashion. Spreading your supply chain across borders means you’re less likely to wake up one morning and find your production costs have exploded—a trick Nokia figured out when it was still relevant. Conveniently, Zetwerk has some ex-Nokia people, like Josh Foulger, who apparently kept the old playbook and are now dusting it off. As markets start shedding real demand, manufacturers are rediscovering the old wisdom: it’s nice to build stuff near the people who want to buy it.

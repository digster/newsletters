A little over a year ago, we wrote about how the CCI finally got its new antitrust chief, Ravneet Kaur, though her path ahead was a minefield. Today, we’re back with a follow-up on how India’s 15-year-old competition watchdog—often derided as a toothless tiger—is finally learning to bare its teeth under Kaur’s leadership.

Kaur—a 1988-batch IAS officer from Punjab and the first woman to chair the CCI—was handed the task of cleaning the house of judicial challenges, regulatory delays, and leadership gaps. And she’s wasted no time to introduce a slew of changes.

One of them came on 10 September, when the CCI rolled out new M&A rules as part of Kaur’s broader plan to tighten the screws. Now, companies have to notify the CCI based on the value of their deals, not just their market share or revenue.

But the solutions themselves have created new problems.

Suddenly, a lot more deals are under the CCI’s microscope, and the Commission, even at its peak, operates at sub-70% capacity. This, from a regulator that is already known for missing deadlines and dragging out high-profile probes of tech giants like Google and Apple.

Even the CCI’s budget of Rs 51 crore hasn’t budged from last year. What’s worse, since 2011, it has levied Rs 18,300 crore (US$2.2 billion) in penalties but managed to collect only 2.3% of that.

As Dhanendra Kumar, the CCI’s first chairperson, told The Ken: “We have only one competition agency for a big country like India.” For context, the US has a competition body in every state. It’s a vicious spiral, really.

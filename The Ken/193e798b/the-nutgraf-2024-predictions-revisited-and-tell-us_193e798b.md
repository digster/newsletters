Good Morning Ishan,
For the last few years, around this time, I’ve been putting out a post with predictions for the year ahead. Some of them turn out to be right, others turn out to be fabulously wrong.
2024 is no different. Like I wrote [last year](https://the-ken.com/the-nutgraf/2024-predictions/):
Overall, I expect 2024 to be a year of divergence. While most of us can agree what 2023 was like, I expect 2024 to be very different, with a far more complex narrative. This is going to be a confusing year filled with contradictions. For every new trend that emerges, there will be enough evidence to suggest that the opposite is also true. Some people and companies will take one path, and others will take a completely different one, and this will lead to mixed results. Things will look simultaneously good and bad for a while. All in all—be wary of blanket narratives. If someone tells you that things are improving and getting better, take it with a grain of salt. If someone tells you everything is hopeless, add some pepper to it. Both of them are right, and wrong.
2024 Predictions, The Nutgraf
Anyway, let’s see how I did.
Prediction 1: Products and services will degrade for most consumers
I wrote:
I believe that 2024 is the year when almost every product and service we’ve gotten used to for years will decisively move into the final phase. We’re going to witness a broader trend of degradation and deterioration, and most consumers will be forced to accept this new reality and be dragged into a new era kicking and screaming.
This has happened before, but in specific pockets. Prepaid and postpaid plans offered an exceptional amount of value at bottom prices, until the telecom market consolidated and led to a broad degradation of service quality. Ubers and Olas used to be top-notch, reliable, and cheap a few years ago, but after both these companies decided that it wasn’t sustainable to chase growth, the service became more expensive, worse, and less dependable. Even in 2023, several other products reached the third phase. Credit card rewards got demonetised. Lounges got more crowded. 10-minute grocery deliveries started taking longer. Domestic flights got more expensive… and also worse than before. House rents. Insurance. The list goes on and on. Everything seems to be getting more expensive, and yet, quality is decreasing.
This is going to accelerate in 2024. Better start getting used to worse experiences, longer delivery times, poorer quality. You’re unlikely to experience something and say, “wow, that was amazing; I would’ve gladly paid more for it.”
On one hand, 10-minute deliveries (which essentially came out of nowhere in 2024) gives us the belief that experience and quality across the board have become much better this year. And if you define experience through the narrow prism of delivery time and convenience, then yes, 2024 has been a great year for the consumer. You can now get anything from a packet of tissues to a Playstation delivered to your door in less than 10 minutes.
But if you take a much broader view, I think 2024 has been a much worse year where products and services have degraded in ways that we never imagined.
This cuts across everything. For example, I can’t imagine a time when the airline travel experience in India has been worse, or so expensive. Vistara shut down in 2024. And the market leader hasn’t fared much better—Indigo was [ranked](https://www.business-standard.com/finance/personal-finance/indigo-ranks-103-out-of-109-air-india-at-61-best-worst-airlines-of-2024-124120500282_1.html) as one of the worst airlines in the world recently. Airline experience is just one part of the story. Tourism in India has started to see a noticeable decline—especially in destinations like Goa, which has seen a [drop](https://skift.com/2024/11/15/goas-tourism-drop-fewer-foreign-visitors-rising-complaints-and-tougher-competition/) in international tourists, who now prefer destinations such as Sri Lanka and Southeast Asia. Delhi’s air is at its worst in [nearly](https://www.business-standard.com/india-news/no-good-air-days-for-delhi-this-year-when-will-the-capital-breathe-easy-124112600329_1.html) six years, and Bengaluru witnessed its [first-ever](https://indianexpress.com/article/cities/bangalore/bengaluru-water-crisis-cape-town-mismanagement-t-v-ramachandra-9250290/) major water scarcity crisis. And I’m not even getting into stadium experiences. “Why are we paying so much tax and what are we getting in return?” seems to be a much more popular theme this year.
Even for internet products and services, it’s quite apparent that the convenience they offer isn’t as inexpensive as it used to be. As an example, Zomato’s platform fee used to be Rs 2 back in 2023. In 2024, it increased it to Rs 4 per order, which it later raised to Rs 10 during the festive season. We’re paying more to get the same thing.
If you want a much broader view of the degradation of products and services, I recommend reading Ed Zitron’s fantastic long-read about how digital experiences have gotten worse because tech companies don’t care about users anymore.
In the last year, I’ve spent about 200,000 words on a kind of personal journey where I’ve tried again and again to work out why everything digital feels so broken, and why it seems to keep getting worse, despite what tech’s “brightest” minds might promise. More regularly than not, I’ve found that the answer is fairly simple: the tech industry’s incentives no longer align with the user.
The people running the majority of internet services have used a combination of monopolies and a cartel-like commitment to growth-at-all-costs thinking to make war with the user, turning the customer into something between a lab rat and an unpaid intern, with the goal to juice as much value from the interaction as possible. To be clear, tech has always had an avaricious streak, and it would be naive to suggest otherwise, but this moment feels different. I’m stunned by the extremes tech companies are going to extract value from customers, but also by the insidious way they’ve gradually degraded their products.
Never Forgive Them, [Ed Zitron](https://www.wheresyoured.at/never-forgive-them/), December 2024
Set aside half an hour and read the whole thing.
You’ll be furious too.
Prediction 2: Byju’s becomes a zombie company
Last year, I [wrote](https://the-ken.com/the-nutgraf/2024-predictions/):
If you’ve reached a certain amount of fatigue about news around Byju’s, like I have, then I have good news for you. I believe that 2024 will be a remarkably quiet year as far as Byju’s is concerned, as it retreats to the background. It’ll go into a rebuilding phase, start stabilising itself, and spend a lot of time getting its house in order. I’m not saying it’ll emerge from this victorious, just as I’m not saying that its end is imminent.
2024 Predictions, The Nutgraf
Narrator: Its end is imminent.
Like I said:
My reasoning for this comes from a fundamental truth—despite what you want to believe, big companies do not disappear overnight, or implode dramatically in a short period of time. Usually, even after the writing is on the wall, companies go through a long “zombie” period, during which they aren’t as relevant as before, but are still operational as a business.
What can I say? Even Byju Raveendran [agrees](https://www.fortuneindia.com/enterprise/byjus-net-worth-is-zero-confesses-founder-byju-raveendran/118847) that the company is practically worthless now. And Byju’s is fighting multiple court cases across nations. It’s all so complicated that I’ve stopped trying to figure out what’s actually going on. This whole thing may drag on longer, but for all practical purposes, 2024 was the year when Byju’s became a zombie company.
So I guess I got this right, but it really wasn’t a hard prediction.
Prediction 3: Fintechs will discover the nightmare of running a bank
This is one prediction that didn’t exactly happen.
Last year, I wrote:
Most couples will tell you that the hardest part of a marriage happens immediately after the wedding once both people start living together. That’s when you discover things you didn’t know earlier. And in this case, I think fintechs are going to be extremely stressed and hassled all through 2024. There’s a reason why fintechs chose to become fintechs instead of trying to create a bank—it’s easier and more familiar to do the former. Running a bank is a hard, painful ordeal, and I suspect the cracks will start to show by the end of the year.
Expect to read several business stories in 2024 about the frustration within fintechs trying to make this marriage work.
There were some strange things that happened with fintechs this year. But no company went through major problems with bank mergers. In fact, Slice completed its merger with North East Small Finance Bank just last month, so perhaps the teething problems will show up in 2025.
However, the RBI had an especially active year—it placed restrictions on the lending operations of some NBFCs, which we covered in [detail](https://the-ken.com/newsletters/two-by-two/the-mystery-of-usury/) in our episode of Two by Two a few weeks ago.
Prediction 4: TVS crosses Ola Electric in sales to become India’s largest two-wheeler EV company
This is one prediction I called quite early, and got right just in time.
In December 2023, Ola Electric had a commanding lead over TVS and Bajaj. By mid-2024, this lead had become even bigger and I was starting to wonder if I’d completely misjudged Ola Electric.
Well, as I’m writing this, based on recorded sales so far in December, Bajaj and TVS have overtaken Ola Electric in sales.
This is also the first instance of this happening in India’s electric two-wheeler history.
I did get one thing wrong though—right now, the new market leader in electric two-wheelers appears to be Bajaj and not TVS. Although technically, I still got it right.
Prediction 5: Phonepe’s fairytale journey finally hits roadblocks
Hah. No, this didn’t happen at all.
For one simple reason—Paytm*.
I expected 2024 to be a difficult year for Phonepe, but as it turns out, the company that got really affected was Paytm.
After the Reserve Bank of India effectively killed Paytm Payments Bank, Phonepe jumped to take advantage of the situation. As my colleague Gaurav wrote back in February:
Paytm’s rival and India’s largest UPI app, Phonepe, has already managed to grow its 500 million-strong user base by about 15% within the first few days of February. “We are seeing a surge in inbound requests from merchants for QR codes and smart-speakers, and we are making sure we are meeting that demand,” said a spokesperson for Phonepe.
Meanwhile, merchant-focused UPI payment fintech, Bharatpe, saw its new merchant acquisitions jump by nearly half over the same period, claimed the executive.
Phonepe jumped at the opportunity and started running full-page print ads in the Times of India while lending fintech Slice launched its new UPI product.
Paytm’s foot soldiers are in a race against time, [The Ken](https://the-ken.com/story/paytms-foot-soldiers-are-in-a-race-against-time/)
All in all, 2024 was probably one of Phonepe’s best years.
The fairytale continues for one of India’s biggest fintech companies.
Prediction 6: VCs will get bored and start funding companies again
I asked a fintech founder about his best counterintuitive take about what would happen in 2024, and he said, “we’ll see a bull market again. All the VCs will get bored and cut checks and party like it’s 2021”.
At first, I thought he was being facetious (and perhaps he was). However, his reasoning seemed intriguing. For the first half of 2024, we’ll continue to see a “famine”, i.e., a continuation of what we are witnessing right now. But things will change by the second half of the year, driven by interest rate cuts and a post-election euphoria, which will lead to more investors deciding to invest in companies again.
The other aspect is that by the end of 2024, it’ll be close to two years of little investments by VCs, and at some point, some of them will throw caution to the wind because they need to deploy money.
After all, there are only so many fireside chats and podcasts that VCs can start—or join—before they realise they need to give money to companies.
Well, this did happen.
Indian startups secured $9.2 billion in venture capital (VC) funding in 984 deals between January and October 2024, marking a 44.4 per cent increase in funding value from the year before, according to data analytics firm GlobalData.
Deal volumes rose 5.8 per cent during the same period, indicating a renewed investor confidence in the market. The same period in 2023 saw 930 VC deals worth $6.4 billion.
VC funding rises 44% to $9.2 billion in the first 10 months of 2024, [Business Standard](https://www.business-standard.com/industry/news/vc-funding-in-india-hits-9-2-bn-in-first-10-months-of-2024-up-44-124112900434_1.html)
Maybe now we’ll finally see fewer VCs on podcasts?

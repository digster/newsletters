Though synthesis and analysis are two sides of a coin, my preference and love has always been for the side that helps us see how things are interconnected and part of complex systems we cannot even fathom. That is, synthesis.
Sometimes, though, it is invaluable to be able to do the opposite and break down things into smaller parts. Seeing my own life (and career) as a wave, in turn composed of dozens of other smaller waves, is one of those obvious and yet hard realisations. I would even go out on a limb and say it is the most important perspective—aka, “mental model” – that has allowed me to “calm down” and take a more strategic and long-term view of my life and career.

I am sure you will immediately start to think about how your own curve fits with this. Your mind starts wondering what the relevant time periods must be. Is it this year? The last few years? The last decade? All of your life? What are the “ups” and “downs”?
You see my point, right? Our lives can absolutely be visualised as waves that rise and fall (sometimes below sea level!) over time.

You may still be with me when I say that each of our waves is composed of numerous other smaller waves.
What might the green one be? The red one? The yellow one? Health? Finances? Romantic relationships? Kids? Careers? Ambition?

The real “unlock” for me happened when I realised that the timescale I’m looking at could be a week, a month, or a quarter. I could then “zoom out” and see the various waves that were playing out through me and learn to focus on them.
Here, a related analogy might be useful. If you’re of a, umm, certain age, you must have owned music systems that had built-in graphic equalisers and displays? You know, the ones where you could change the signature of the music you were listening to by fiddling with low, mid, and high frequencies on a 5 or 10 (usually) slider?
Equalisers died away as first the focus shifted to amplifiers, speakers, and fidelity; and then to bitrate; and finally, to streaming convenience.
The image of a graphic equaliser pops up in my mind many times a month, and often multiple times in some particularly busy weeks. It often happens when I’m slipping many times on deadlines for something important. Instead of berating myself and feeling guilty and defensive, I often try to “see” what the multiple waves are during that week. Are there other ones I’m doing well on? Are there too many playing out at the same time? Is there a deeply negative one that’s sapping my energy?
And then, I allow myself to “slide” some of the waves up or down on my personal equaliser. In some ways, it is a combination of seeing (the waves), reflecting (about which ones are playing out, and which ones are important) and finally, controlling (by damping or amplifying some, or even completely shutting down some).
Knowing that you’re on the “highs” of some waves as you’re struggling to deal with the “lows” of some other ones is immensely reassuring.
It’s our ongoing search for harmony and coherence.

I just got back from attending the Bangalore Literature Festival. The heat and the crowd certainly put me off but I enjoyed sneaking into the children’s section and enjoying fun emotive storytelling.
My first ever literature festival was the Tata Literature Live! in Mumbai, back in 2019, just before the pandemic. It was a vibrantly alive affair, pulsing with workshops and book discussions. I was deeply saddened when the festival’s founder and director, Anil Dharker, passed away in March 2021; I had greatly admired his vision. True, the events were hosted in elite spaces, but that year, every single workshop and session was free—a gift to all.
That festival was also the day I met an unforgettable constellation of writers. William Dalrymple, ever the gentleman, insisted on finding me a cab as we exited late into the night. Perumal Murugan, with quiet defiance, refused to speak in English despite knowing the language, speaking only in Tamil as his translator wove his words for the audience. Madhavi Menon, hosting a session on Midnight’s Children, confessed with a laugh that she hadn’t finished reading the book—an admission that made me feel infinitely better about my own last-minute projects.
Then there was Anthony Horowitz, who shared a rather harrowing story. He explained how he often used his teenage children as case studies to understand how boys might react in chaotic situations. He recounted, with a note of pride, the time his son was stung by a jellyfish, and his first instinct was to ask, “How does it feel?” before offering help. To each writer, their own methods, I suppose.

Now, onto the recommendations:
Nalin Mathur shares why [Taj: A Story of Mughal India](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/631232.Taj) by Timeri N. Murari is a must-read.
“The book paints a very graphic picture of the times around which the Taj was built. Chapters are interleaved such that two parallel stories are ongoing—the building of the Taj and the subsequent death of Mumtaz and how Shah Jahan dealt with it. It’s a historical fiction grounded in fact, and paints a picture of the times like I have not read elsewhere. It’s also a beautiful and raw love story. A must read!”

Nandhini Anand shares a Harry Potter re-imagining called [Harry Potter and the Methods of Rationality](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/10016013-harry-potter-and-the-methods-of-rationality) by Elieze Yudkowsky:
“It is a re-imagining of a Harry Potter that’s brought by a physics professor in a scientific society with a lot of understanding of the experimental method and science knowledge (physics and cognitive science).”
Aditya Agarwal (who assures us that he is not a relative of the author!) recommends [The Renunciation](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/243598607-the-renunciation) by Pragya Agrawal:
“Contemporary, alternate take on Ramayana from Sita’s perspective—raises some important questions, but remains entertaining throughout.”

Ajay Bhat and Pawan Gadia both share books that they have reread.
For Pawan Gadia, it’s [Shogun](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/52382796-sh-gun) by James Clavell
“Everytime I reread the book ( done it thrice ) I discover nuances of thoughts of the character, specially Toranaga, I am awed at the way he plans, says, does things, and the end result is very different from the plan which was the original unrevealed plan.”
And [Haroun and the Sea of Stories](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/4835.Haroun_and_the_Sea_of_Stories) by Salman Rushdie is Ajay Bhat’s choice, and soon maybe his daughter’s too.
“I read this when I was in college for the first time and re-read it every few years. The Hinglish puns, the humor, the theme (on the power of stories) all resonate more strongly than ever. I started reading it to my 7 year old daughter recently. She doesn’t understand all of it, but she’s loving the core story and the clever wordplay.”
Diksha Bihani recommends [Lessons in Chemistry](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/58065033-lessons-in-chemistry) by Bonnie Garmus:
“Lessons in Chemistry, the name might make you wonder, it’s about science, but no, it’s about resilience, about women who refuse to stay quiet. About finding your own way when the world keeps shutting doors.”

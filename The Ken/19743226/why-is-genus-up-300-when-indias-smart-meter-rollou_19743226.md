Good Morning Ishan,
India loves “smart”. We want our boys and girls to be smart. We take pride in our smart weapons. We aspire for smart classrooms. We dig smartwatches.
Why, a decade back, our government rolled out the Smart Cities Mission with great fanfare. The plan was to make 100 cities across the country—from New Delhi to Chennai, from Prayagraj to Bengaluru—smart. This grand mission closed quietly in March. Over 8,000 projects and Rs 1.65 lakh crore later, have the outcomes been smart enough? That’s a debate for another day. What’s certain, though, is that crowd management in our cities is not smart at all. The consequences are tragic.
India wants to be smart in another crucial area too—its power consumption. Shristi’s story today is about these plans, their execution, and consequences. A few years back, the Indian government set a highly ambitious target—of switching 250 million regular meters to smart ones by 2025. The deadline is nigh. How many smart meters have been installed so far? 20 million. Ouch.
But, surprise surprise. Genus Power—a key cog in this transition wheel—commanding half the market for smart meters in the country, is on a roll. Its revenue and profit soared over the past two years, and its stock price is up 300%.
What explains this dichotomy between a near-impossible-to-meet target, and a leading player that seems oblivious, even impervious, to the chasm? Is this even sustainable? Is the stock market not being so smart, after all? Shristi lays out the many chinks in India’s smart-metering armour and the ironies that let Genus seemingly defy them.

India loves “smart”. We want our boys and girls to be smart. We take pride in our smart weapons. We aspire for smart classrooms. We dig smartwatches.

Why, a decade back, our government rolled out the Smart Cities Mission with great fanfare. The plan was to make 100 cities across the country—from New Delhi to Chennai, from Prayagraj to Bengaluru—smart. This grand mission closed quietly in March. Over 8,000 projects and Rs 1.65 lakh crore later, have the outcomes been smart enough? That’s a debate for another day. What’s certain, though, is that crowd management in our cities is not smart at all. The consequences are tragic.

India wants to be smart in another crucial area too—its power consumption. Shristi’s story today is about these plans, their execution, and consequences. A few years back, the Indian government set a highly ambitious target—of switching 250 million regular meters to smart ones by 2025. The deadline is nigh. How many smart meters have been installed so far? 20 million. Ouch.

But, surprise surprise. Genus Power—a key cog in this transition wheel—commanding half the market for smart meters in the country, is on a roll. Its revenue and profit soared over the past two years, and its stock price is up 300%.

What explains this dichotomy between a near-impossible-to-meet target, and a leading player that seems oblivious, even impervious, to the chasm? Is this even sustainable? Is the stock market not being so smart, after all? Shristi lays out the many chinks in India’s smart-metering armour and the ironies that let Genus seemingly defy them.

Let’s dive in.

Interesting article. However, I wouldn’t be too alarmed by the big players elbowing in this segment. This thing happens across categories – small players create niches while the big players wait and watch. Once the niche is deemed big enough and offer potential, the big players swoop in those niches, often buying out the smaller players rather than trying to establish a new brand or extensions of their existing brands. Usually, the valuations are very attractive since such niches have high potential – we have seen this play out with Organic India as well. So I foresee more acquisitions in this area, which will be a vindication for the entrepreneurs.

Saurabh Malkar Commented

This is one of my favorite subjects, one that I am personally involved in. Right off the bat, seed oils, palm oil, hydrogenated products, highly processed foods are bad when eaten in excess. There is sufficient evidence to cut out trans fat from the food chain. However, eliminating these ingredients won’t tame obesity, HT, or DM. And that’s where this story needs to be complemented with stories describing the “carb” problem.

Carbs, including sugar, is the go-to source of energy for the body. Insulin regulates this largely. However, carbs also displace fat as energy source and turns it into fat which settles right under the skin or around the organs in the abdomen (visceral fat). So, if you couple carbs (wheat, rice, pulse, lentils, and their associated products) with the best oils on the planet – olive, avocado and coconut, most participants will continue gaining fat.

To stop this and to start burning fat, one needs to eliminate carbs which are a fairly new addition to human diet. I am talking in terms of human existence. Eating salads with fruit-oil based dressing + meat, eggs, sea food, nuts will shift the body’s go-to fuel source from carbs to fat.

In closing, fat or cholesterol isn’t bad in and of itself. However, when it’s displaced by carbs and sits idle, it becomes a risk factor. The Indian diet is one of the worst diets on this planet, with its carb-heavy (sugar and non-sugar), fat-accompanied, and low/no-protein composition. Not only does it upset the fat metabolism in the body, it fails to supply essential micros. Add to that the Indian dietary practice of serving all courses at once, eating large morsels/bites (due to eating with hand), and insisting on seconds and thirds, and you have an unhealthy population.

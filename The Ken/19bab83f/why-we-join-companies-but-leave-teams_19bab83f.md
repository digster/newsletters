People join (good) companies but leave (bad) managers, goes the adage.
But in a world where even the world’s most successful and wealthy organisations are [getting rid of managers](https://www.cnbc.com/2025/08/27/google-executive-says-company-has-cut-a-third-of-its-managers.html), we may have to find new scapegoats.
The adage also isn’t very accurate. Because people leave bad teams, not bad managers.
In most modern organisations, we work in teams, not alone. Research shows that great teams have the strongest correlation with work satisfaction, happiness, and ambition.
At Cisco, for example, we’ve seen that when someone’s experience of their team moves from the top half, companywide, to the bottom half, their likelihood of leaving the company increases by 45 percent. When people choose not to work somewhere, the somewhere isn’t a company, it’s a team. If we put you in a good team at a bad company, you’ll tend to hang around, but if we put you in a bad team at a good company, you won’t be there for long. The team is the sun, the moon, and the stars of your experience at work. As Edmund Burke, the Anglo-Irish writer and philosopher put it as far back as 1790, “To love the little platoon we belong to in society is the first principle (the germ, as it were) of public affections.”
When we push on the data, and examine closely its patterns and variations, we arrive at this conclusion: while people might care which company they join, they don’t care which company they work for. The truth is that, once there, people care which team they’re on.

Unfortunately, most of us have no idea how critical the role of teams is in our careers until we’ve paid the price for our ignorance through some wasted years and dead-end jobs.
One key reason for this is the way we are educated.
“I think it’s a cultural thing in India. Individual performance is valued a lot more than team performance in our education system, which conditions us really poorly for life,” my colleague (and teammate) Praveen Krishnan said to me last year.
(If you’re wondering how I remembered his exact words after so long, it’s because I’d written them down in a document for later. Today is that “later”.)
Most of our school education is based on individual learning and performance. Collaboration is not only not de-emphasised but often actively discouraged. How can you collaborate with the same peers you are meant to beat to get a better rank or grade in a scarcity-driven, zero-sum game? Competitive exams only reinforce winning over learning.
The scene isn’t much different at most colleges, including premier engineering and management ones. I remember the concept of “RG-ing” (beating someone via relative grading) your classmates.
The first time we enter workplaces as young professionals, it’s a shock for us to truly understand the importance of teamwork. We’ve been taught to view our peers as rivals, not collaborators. Not enough has been written about this.
While schools and colleges may not understand the importance of teamwork and collaboration, modern organisations absolutely do.
What results is a clash of beliefs and expectations, often leading even smart professionals to lose their way and end up destroying teams, too.
“Few factors are more corrosive to teamwork than the employee who skates through life taking advantage of the much harder work of others.

What teams are you planning to stay with or join in 2026?
What’s in plain sight, when we study excellence at work, is the groups of people doing actual work together—what’s in plain sight is teams.
This is why teams matter, and it’s why they matter much more than cultural plumage matters.
Teams simplify: they help us see where to focus and what to do. Culture doesn’t do this, funnily enough, because it’s too abstract.
Teams make work real: they ground us in the day-to-day, both in terms of the content of our work and the colleagues with whom we do it. Culture doesn’t.
And teams, paradoxically, make homes for individuals. Whereas culture’s focus leans toward conformity to a common core of behaviors, teams focus on the opposite. Teams aren’t about sameness—they aren’t, at their best, about marching in lockstep. Instead they’re about unlocking what is unique about each of us, in the service of something shared. A team, at its finest, insists on the unique contribution of each of its members, and is the best way we humans have ever come up with of harnessing those distinctive contributions together in the service of something that none of us could do alone.

Hello, fellow lovers of beautifully compiled words. Do you have a favourite word? Mine changes every month or so. Right now, it’s a simple-sounding word with a lot of meaning: sonder.
I love the way it rolls off the tongue. Do you know what it means? It rhymes with ponder, so I assumed it involved soul-searching, and I wasn’t entirely wrong, because it is a byproduct of introspection.
For those (like me, until this year) who didn’t know: sonder is that lightning-strike realisation that everyone we encounter has a life, as rich as ours, as conflicting as ours, as special as ours. I didn’t have the word for it at 14, but I felt it when I read Anne Frank’s diary. Here was a girl my age, whispering to her diary just like I did. Except, she was in war-time Europe, from a time where even my parents weren’t born and a place I had never seen in real life, and yet I understood her. And suddenly, I wondered what life must be like for every other 14-year-old girl around me.
I felt decentred. It certainly dialled down my teenage ego. And sparked this weird, warm sense of connection. Sure, I’d noticed people before, but this was different. With friends, it was all inside jokes and playground drama, and diaries were where you shared your most private thoughts. So, it was Anne Frank who made me realise that other children around me also have “deep” thoughts and a complex life.
Books are beautiful like that. When we can’t share everything with the people beside us, we find kinship in characters. Yes, they are passive, but if they make us feel less lonely, then aren’t they a part of our community?

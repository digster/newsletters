Good Morning Ishan,
What’s in a name? What’s in a date?
Ask hapless lenders running from pillar to post to retrieve crores of rupees from borrowers. These lenders are being forced to give chase, without much avail, thanks to one name, Section 10A, and one date, 25 March 2020.
You see, Section 10A of the Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code (IBC) grants borrowers a leeway. It protects them from insolvency applications for any debt default up to a year after 25 March 2020.
Notice the date? That was when the government of India first imposed a countrywide lockdown to stop the spread of Covid-19. Innumerable businesses were left shackled by the black-swan event. There was a clear danger of many companies defaulting on the loans they had taken.
And so, the government, in good faith, introduced that benevolent provision in the IBC – Section 10A.
But kindness can be cruel. What was meant as an act of consideration for borrowers has now become a major pain point for many lenders – long after the pandemic has ended.
Several borrowers – including big names such as Cafe Coffee Day, Zee Entertainment and SpiceJet – are resorting to this special ordinance, slugging it out across courts, and refusing to pay lenders on the premise that their defaults happened in the time window.
The number of such cases has surged this year. So much so that it is also making law firms set up special teams.
But can borrowers use a limited-period offer to continue to default? What are the implications of this? How are lenders and the insolvency regulator responding?

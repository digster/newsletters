Good Morning Ishan,
If you want to scare telecom companies, your best bet is to invoke the scariest bogeyman of all—the "dumb pipe". There is nothing telecom companies dread more than to be relegated to providing dumb pipes that carry data like how utilities pipe water.
What happens if you happen to be a manufacturer of those pipes? If being a provider of dumb pipes is a bad thing, is being a maker of dumb pipes also bad?
Sterlite, India's largest maker of fibre optic cables and also a top 10 global player, seems to think so. Fibre, the nervous system of a telecom network, has become increasingly commoditised with cheap, Chinese-made fibre flooding the market. Fibre prices have dropped, and consequently, Sterlite's profitability has declined by 16% for the year ended March 2020.
It's no wonder, then, that it is desperate in its efforts to diversify. It wants to step away from making what are, essentially, light pipes. And how.
It wants to develop wireless and 5G solutions; offer edge computing and network management; make telecom software, and become a systems integrator.
Anand Agarwal, Sterlite's CEO, calls this the transformation of his company into a "3.0" version which would take its addressable market from $30 billion to $75 billion by 2023.
Except, as Pratap reports in his story today, this isn't the first time Sterlite has tried to transform itself. Its earlier efforts to modernise itself ("2.0" perhaps?) starting 2017 did not really deliver what investors expected. Will 3.0 be any different?

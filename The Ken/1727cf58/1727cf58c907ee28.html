Japan is taking interoperability to the next level 

Arundhati

How many payments products (credit card, debit card, wallet etc) do you have? Do you have an answer? Okay, now read on. 
 
Japan’s tech chops are legendary. But funnily enough when it comes to digital payments, the country—much like India—still largely runs on cash. 
Over the past couple of years, both the Japanese government and its corporations have laid out plans for this [cashless] revolution — one that could fundamentally reshape and revitalise the world’s third-largest economy and even, say some, help Japan’s decades-long battle with deflation. Foremost among these plans is the government’s “cashless vision” of doubling the ratio of card and e-money transactions from their current low levels within the next eight years, to create a $1tn-plus cashless market.  
 
The painful path to curing Japan of its cash addiction, Financial Times
And like India, it has numerous digital payments systems. 
Source: Financial Times
India hogged the world's limelight when the country's retail payments body, the National Payments Corporation of India (NPCI), brought all its banks together to create a cutting-edge digital payments infrastructure. Unified Payments Interface (UPI), the country's swift digital payments system, is used by nearly 100 million people, recording over a billion transactions a month. Even though the user interacts with a payments app controlled by big tech corporations like Google, the back-end is all banks. 

The NPCI handles the infrastructure for various retail payments systems. UPI is one of them. The other digital payments systems are IMPS (Immediate Payment Service), another peer-to-peer payments system, and, of course, cards. Among all of these, UPI was special because it was interoperable. You could easily send money from one UPI app to any other one. Like an email. 

And getting all the banks together for this was a mammoth effort. Interoperability meant all of them had to be on the same page. 

But what Japan has in mind takes interoperability to a whole new level. It wants to create a common infrastructure for all types of digital payments systems. 

Japan’s three biggest banks have set up a study group that will look at possibly building a common settlement infrastructure for digital payments, reported Reuters. It’s an initiative backed by the country’s central bank and financial regulator.
"Japan has numerous platforms for cashless settlement, but they have yet to overwhelm the usage of cash," Hiromi Yamaoka, a former central bank executive who will chair the group, said in an online briefing. "One solution could be to enhance the interoperability of digital currencies and infrastructure."
Japan to look at building a common infrastructure for digital payments, Reuters
This could mean the ability to send money from, say, a debit card to a wallet. It could do away with the need to have multiple payments systems. Coming back to the question I first asked: I have four different payments products. How many do you have? Now, imagine if all you needed was your debit card. To achieve this kind of simplicity, I would imagine it would be a complex operation that needs an incredible level of cooperation. But isn’t that what Kaizen is all about?
Mask + bank = ??

Savio

You’ve probably seen variations of this joke. 
Well, it’s not a joke for Brian Brooks, the new acting head of the Office of the Comptroller of the Currency, the US federal agency that oversees its top banks. His letter to various mayors and governors, earlier this week, about the effect of public activity on banks included this:
 
Finally, lengthy and potentially permanent requirements that individuals wear face masks in many or even all public spaces create the very real risk of increases in bank robberies. Historically, banks had policies prohibiting customers from wearing masks and certain head coverings in branches for this reason. Many banks relaxed these prohibitions temporarily during the height of the COVID-19 crisis out of concern for the health and welfare of their customers. While that may have been a prudent decision when the extent of the health risk was still unknown, recent reports of face-covering-related robberies at bank branches and other establishments make clear that broadly applicable face mask requirements are not safe or sustainable on a permanent basis.
The problem, though, is very real, and not just in the US. Though, as you know, not everyone wearing a mask is a thief.
Taking the stimulus public

Seetharaman

The Indian government is becoming quite creative in its economic relief measures.  It has promised micro, small and medium enterprises (MSMEs) collateral-free loans from banks and non-bank lenders. It has expanded the investment and revenue criteria to benefit more MSMEs. Now, it wants to do one more thing: help these companies go public. 

As part of a proposal that predates the pandemic, the government will use an initial outlay of Rs 10,000 crore (US$1.32 billion) to invest in MSMEs’ initial public offerings (IPOs). It plans to buy up to 15% of a company’s shares. 
 
Whether or not a company is able to raise money in public markets is a function of what investors make of its growth potential. But when the government steps in to back an IPO, it may not be an accurate reflection of the company’s prospects. We have seen the state-owned Life Insurance Corporation (LIC) rescuing public issues of other government-run companies in the face of lukewarm demand from investors.  

Furthermore, this whole plan is predicated on the government being able to sell these shares at a profit later, so that the money can be invested in other MSMEs. So if the stock doesn’t do well, the government could end up holding the shares longer than it wants to. 

Governments elsewhere in the world, too, have picked up stakes in listed companies in times of crisis. The US government’s bailout of automaker General Motors in 2009 gave it a 61% stake in the company. It exited the company in 2013 at a $10.5 billion loss. US President Donald Trump has said he supports the idea of the government owning a piece of companies taking federal aid during the pandemic. Germany has created a €100 billion (US$112 billion) fund to invest in struggling companies. It has helped airline Lufthansa in exchange for a 20% stake. 

There is a difference between this and what India wants to do. These companies in the US and Europe are either strategically important to those countries or are large employers. But the Indian government may be trying to fix a problem where there is none. 
An ambitious plan to decongest overcrowded Manila

Jum

Covid-19 has given Philippines President Rodrigo Duterte the perfect reason to renew his administration’s push to decongest the Manila capital region, one of the world’s most densely populated areas.

Up to 5 million jobs are forecast to be lost due to the pandemic. And most of those are in the capital region, where many Filipinos from provinces have migrated to over the years.

Duterte is luring those people into moving back to the countryside in exchange for as much as US$2,200 worth of cash and goods (per household) under a programme called “Balik Probinsya” (“Back to the Province”).

That’s the minimum yearly income required for a family of five to stay above the poverty line.

For poor households, that may sound like a generous offer. But the reality is: it’s unsustainable.

The main reason for internal migration in the Philippines is employment. Others are related to life events such as education and marriage.

The capital region has the lowest poverty incidence in the Philippines at 3.9%, based on the 2015 Poverty Consensus. Outside the capital region, the poverty incidence goes up to as high as 53.7%.

So it will take more than providing incentives for people to finally relocate. Many of those who had migrated probably do not have a place to go back to.
 
The “Balik Probinsya” program should recognize the economic triggers of population movements and should put in place regional development programs that will address the prevailing regional poverty differentials. Unless and until people perceive a more balanced regional development that addresses these socio-economic inequalities, it may be difficult to draw them back to the provinces. And when migrants indeed move back, we need to give them enough reason to stay there for good.
 
“Balik Probinsya” in time of Covid-19, University of the Philippines Population Institute
It’s easier said than done.
Unsafe at work, unsafe working from home

Durga

Workplace harassment doesn’t stop at the office. It never did.

In fact, in India, as per POSH—Prohibition of Sexual Harassment Act 2013—misbehaving with a colleague or a client outside the workspace falls squarely under sexual harassment. 

The definition of sexual harassment as per POSH is, “any unwelcome, sexually determined physical, verbal, or non-verbal conduct”.

Now, there are basic guidelines on which each organisation builds its POSH policy. But virtual harassment during a pandemic is a complexity that most wouldn’t have seen coming.

As people relax and work from their ‘home offices’ with the formality of ‘work hours’ out of the way, it becomes easier for harassers to fly under the radar and make unsolicited calls at odd hours.

Virtual sexual harassment isn’t just a pandemic problem, but it’s one that needs active prevention and redressal. Nirmala Menon, founder and CEO of Interweave Consulting, explains how in The Times of India.
When one is in an office, there are certain norms one instinctively follows. Employees pick up cues that tell them that they need to behave appropriately. On the other hand, at home there is likely to be a certain feeling of informality that lends itself to more personal conversations and disclosures. With no one monitoring, the informality of home working (from a couch or from your bedroom for lack of space) combined with the odd hours that many have accepted as the norm in home working, there is a higher tendency to engage in conversations that one may not otherwise indulge in, in an office environment.
Nirmala Menon, founder and CEO, Interweave Consulting
A large percentage of reported sexual harassment cases come from women. The reported cases at Indian companies saw a 14% spike as of the year ended March 2019. The reported virtual harassment numbers wouldn’t be too different. 
 
This is a double whammy for female employees, trans employees, and people of other sexual minorities. Because, as we noted in an earlier edition, there’s already a ‘shecession’ underway, with sectors that hire more women—like tourism and hospitality—hit hard this pandemic.

Harassment’s evil twin, bullying, is just as much a problem. Especially when the bully can hide behind a screen.
“Cyberbullying” can range from posting offensive comments on social media sites to micro-managing an employee or extensive monitoring of an employee when working from home or even excluding employees from emails or meetings. Issues may also arise due to the fact that correspondence over email at times can be tricky because it can be more difficult to gauge a person’s tone or messages can be misconstrued and employees can feel more isolated.
 
Employers may need to deal with existing bullying or harassment issues which have now simply moved from the physical workplace to online, but they may also need to deal with new issues which have arisen during lockdown, potentially caused by the lack of structure, perhaps less access to HR than before, changes in reporting lines with some employees on furlough and the blurring of work and home life.
Coronavirus: Bullying and harassment in the virtual workplace, Farrer&Co
More women are likely to drop out, or worse, be discriminated against in terms of hiring as harassment cases rise. 
 
But for workplaces to weather Covid, they need female leadership...
Because women can manage risk

Durga

Check out this data published in the Financial Times.
Smaller hedge funds led by women have seen narrower losses and have performed better than those run by men through Covid.
Hedge funds run by women lost 3.5 per cent in the first four months of this year, as measured by Chicago-based data group HFR’s Women Access index. That beat a 5.5 per cent fall in its HFRI 500 Fund Weighted index, a broader measure of performance that incorporates both men and women-run funds.
 
“In the round [female managers] appear to have a better approach to managing risk,” said Russell Barlow, global head of alternative investment strategies at investment firm Aberdeen Standard.
Women-led hedge funds beat male rivals in coronavirus crisis, Financial Times
Apparently, women just do better in risk-management roles. So if you’re an employer hedging your bets during Covid, have a chat with your HR?
Hunting for hotspots

Savio

Are fewer hotspots a good thing? Sure, as far as controlling the first wave of the Covid-19 pandemic is concerned. But not necessarily for vaccine development.
Scientists in Europe and the United States say the relative success of draconian lockdown and social distancing policies in some areas and countries means virus transmission rates may be at such low levels that there is not enough disease circulating to truly test potential vaccines.
 
[...]

“Ironically, if we’re really successful using public health measures to stamp out the hot spots of viral infection, it will be harder to test the vaccine,” said Francis Collins, director of the National Institutes of Health in the United States.
Scientists hunt pandemic hotspots in race to test vaccines, Reuters

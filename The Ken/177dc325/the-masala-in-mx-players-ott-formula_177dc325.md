Good Morning Ishan,
‘Masala’ films aren’t easy to make. You’re probably rolling your eyes, but hear me out.
Though the basic elements of a masala film have remained pretty much the same across film industries and through the years, it's not often that you see these elements come together in a manner that leaves you satisfied when the end credits roll. There’s a strategy to this; the director needs to know what ingredient to add when, and in what quantity.
On the rare occasion that the director knows what she’s doing, you get something like the wildly-popular Baahubali.
This is something the Times Internet and Tencent-backed OTT platform MX Player seems to have figured out well. In just around three months, its controversial show Aashram racked up over 1 billion unique views.
The company knows its audience—young Indian males, often from tier-2 and tier-3 cities and towns, in search of edgy, local fare. It understands they probably can't shell out money for content like their urban counterparts, which is why its business model is advertising-led, rather than subscription-based.
It has built a well-stocked streaming library, with both original and syndicated shows, to entice these viewers. The winning combination of sex, violence, and crime in its home productions doesn’t hurt either. When that isn't enough, MX Player’s 200 million monthly active users can distract themselves with its other offerings like music streaming (it has a tie-up with Times Internet-backed music streaming app Gaana), a nascent gaming scene, and live TV. And most importantly, they flock to Times Internet’s wildly successful TikTok clone, MX TakaTak.
MX Player has seemingly perfected its masala formula. Users now spend more time on its platform than on big-name rivals like Netflix or even Disney+Hotstar.

"You can't teach an old dog new tricks."
Agree? Or, do you think it's clichéd?
Let's start with a company that has its roots in pre-independent India, in the mid-1940s. That's when it began a bicycle parts business, from which it went on to make bicycles and became India's largest bicycle manufacturer. It moved to motorised two-wheelers and again dominated the market. The empire now spans finance, education, and renewable energy.
I am, of course, talking about the Brijmohan Lall Munjal-founded Hero Group.
Its latest trick, begun five years ago, is Hero Electronix. The company is involved in emerging technology such as AI-powered products and the Internet of Things (IoT). Led by third-generation scion Ujjwal Munjal, the deep tech company's strategy is part-invest in and part-build next-gen products.
It acquired a controlling stake in companies such as Zenatix, which connects ACs and refrigerators to the internet for enterprises, and MyBox, which customises set top boxes. But its pièce de résistance is chip-maker Tessolve, whose tech feeds well into Hero Electronix's other businesses. None more so than its in-house brand Qubo, which makes smart home cameras, smart plugs, etc.
It's also one of the few early investors in the sector, which is why the head of one investee company likens Hero Electronix to PE firm Tiger Global's legendary former head, Lee Fixel. "When people were still not sure about e-commerce, Fixel invested in Flipkart and the rest is history."
With the might of the Hero Group behind it and a $200 million chequebook in hand, Hero Electronix intends to have a solution for everything from automobiles to sports.
Easier said than done, though, and not only due to competition. One of its flagship companies has lost its way, while pricey gadgets and adoption are a hurdle. Vandana's story is a fascinating one on how one of the oldest business groups in India is learning the newest of new tricks.

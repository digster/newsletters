Good Morning Ishan,
It’s been nearly two decades since I was a paying gym member. I joined because graduate school involved many hours sitting in my cubbyhole, so late-night workout sessions were a nice way to reset. But I really kept going because I enjoyed the company of people who would be there at the same time—a Marine Corps veteran who helped me with my form, an accountant who became concerned about his heart health after a physical exam, and a couple others who would chat with us around the water cooler.
We were probably the ideal customers of the gym—there for some sweat, but the rapport kept us going back.
You may have a similar experience if you’re a member of one of India’s gym chains. Today’s story looks at how Anytime Fitness India is doing against Cult.fit, the leading chain in the country. Sure, Cult has close to 700 gyms, fitness clubs, and swimming pools, but it’s losing money. Anytime, on the other hand, is focused and premium, running only gyms and staying largely in the northern part of the country for now.
The gym business demands patience: it generally takes Rs 1–3 crore to set up a gym, depending on how basic or fancy the facility is meant to be. It then takes up to four years to recoup the initial investment, and that’s assuming there are no major disruptions that prevent people from pumping iron or logging miles.
Anytime was profitable for the year ended March 2025. But it’s a relative newcomer outside its home base of north India and the novelty could wear off for its members, or another fresh entrant could lure them away. That means Anytime needs to figure out how to keep gym-goers on its benches and treadmills while it expands from 200 facilities to 500 over the next four years.

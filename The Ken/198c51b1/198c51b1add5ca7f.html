India’s federal government can, in one fell swoop, change the fortunes of businesses like telecom, pharmaceuticals, and energy. A new policy to allow the entry of a disruptive rival, price controls on drugs and medical devices, the cancellation of bids for a power-generation project—you get the drift.

But think about having to navigate the caprices of multiple states. Year after year. It’s a survival skill for distillers and brewers.

United Spirits, the Diageo-owned maker of McDowell’s, Johnnie Walker, and Smirnoff, and also India’s largest liquor company, knows this better than anyone else. In just the last decade, it’s seen the introduction of prohibition in Bihar in 2016, followed by restrictions on the sale of alcohol in Andhra Pradesh three years later, then the flip-flops in booze retailing in Delhi in 2021 and 2022. 

States can inflict other kinds of pain too. 

In June, Maharashtra raised the excise duty on Indian-made foreign liquor, or IMFL, from 300% of the manufacturing cost to 450%. The state was kinder to imported and local spirits on account of their relative insignificance in India’s alcohol market. Even so, the move was wonderful news for the state’s coffers. 

The government expects the whopping hike to increase its revenue to Rs 57,000 crore, up Rs 14,000 crore from the Rs 43,620 crore collected in FY 2024–25. It expects 10% of the estimated revenue receipts of Rs 5.60 lakh crore for the financial year 2025-26 to come from this.

Maharashtra: Excise duty on liquor goes up by over 50%, retail prices to rise from 14% to 60%, Hindustan Times

The duty hike wasn’t the only shocker, though. The government also introduced a new category of tipple that will be grain-based and made only in Maharashtra. Most importantly, it’s exempt from the excise-duty hike. The plan to provide drinkers with a cheaper alternative to IMFL could reportedly add another Rs 3,000 crore to the state’s revenue. 

But investors in United Spirits and rival Radico Khaitan panicked, and drove their shares south. It’s not hard to see why. Maharashtra is India’s second-most-populous state, and its per capita income is among the country’s highest. It accounted for roughly 17% of United Spirits’ FY25 sales of Rs 11,600 crore, according to one estimate, and possibly contributes a little more to its Ebitda, a measure of operating profitability. 

United Spirits had to increase prices by 30–40% in the state, its chief executive Praveen Someshwar said on an earnings call last week. The impact on its sales could be considerable. Dolat Capital, a brokerage, expects the current fiscal’s sales from the state to fall by nearly one-third and Ebitda by half. 

But  a state cares little for liquor producers and their shareholders when its own finances are strained. In August 2024, Maharashtra rolled out a monthly handout of Rs 1,500 for women from low-income families. The largesse is believed to have helped the Bharatiya Janata Party-Shiv Sena alliance win the state election three months later. 

Roughly 22 million women are eligible under this scheme, which means the government has to rustle up Rs 3,300 crore every month. What throws this into sharp relief is the fact that Maharashtra’s debt, at almost a fifth of the state’s economic output, is set to reach a new high. 

Last year, Andhra Pradesh chief minister N Chandrababu Naidu reversed his predecessor’s policies on alcohol and once again allowed privately run liquor stores. While this may have been music to alcoholic-beverage companies’ ears, Andhra is in no great fiscal shape. It has set aside Rs 11,000 crore this fiscal alone for three key welfare schemes and needs to find ways to fund them. (Andhra, incidentally, has the second-highest average annual expenditure on alcohol, behind its neighbour Telangana.)

And Maharashtra may have set a precedent here. If a state which already taxes liquor heavily can squeeze more out of manufacturers, why can’t other states just follow suit? 

With welfare schemes, Indian politicians’ approach is simple: announce first, scramble for funds later. 

This scramble eventually leads them to booze. And they tend to hit the bottle, or the bottle-makers, hard. In the decade to FY22, states’ excise revenue from alcohol rose from 165% of production cost to 480%, adjusted for inflation. 

Given the kind of curveballs states keep throwing at liquor companies, is it any surprise that United Spirits shares have underperformed the Nifty Next 50 index—which the company is a part of—over the past five years?

On the earnings call last week, the company tried to calm analysts by saying that for “every headwind, there is a tailwind”, pointing to beneficial policy changes in other states. But no one is getting a bottle of bubbly yet.

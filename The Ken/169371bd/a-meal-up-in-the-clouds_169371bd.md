When I first moved to Bangalore, my now-colleague in Delhi, Ranjan, was the only "Bangalore person" I knew. So he'd recommend things. Right from where to look for a house to household items to food. Except I didn't care much for his food recommendations for I'd found a friend in a cloud kitchen called 48 East. It was cheap, fast and surprisingly tasty.
Alas, that got shuttered and I had to reacquaint myself with the city. But this isn't about my food habits; it's about how fast cloud kitchens have caught on. And one can certainly see the appeal for they're as convenient for officegoers looking for a quick lunch as they are for students avoiding hostel dinners.
There's Freshmenu, Swiggy, Foodpanda, Rebel Foods, Innerchef, even Zomato. And they all have a bunch of brands under them that you'll be super familiar with, like Faasos or Behrouz Biryani.
But what exactly is a cloud kitchen? Seems like a simple enough question. But it's defined differently by different brands. Different companies also strategise their selection of spaces, cuisine mixes and staff distinctively. Roshni spent a lot of time walking through these kitchens, talking to all the right people to understand how a cloud kitchen works. Is it sustainable? Is it a growing trend? What about costs?
Not so long ago, food brands wouldn’t give a second thought to planning their kitchen spaces. This is now taking prime spot in strategy. The essence is a delivery-only kitchen with no storefront or dine-in option, an apparent solution in these paradoxical times of shrinking spaces and burgeoning real estate prices.
Given that kitchen functions and structures are metamorphosing because of various cloud models, can menus be far behind? Nope. Founder of Birinz, Ravi Morampudi, has something interesting to say about this.

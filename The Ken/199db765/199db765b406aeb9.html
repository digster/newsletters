When it comes to jewellery, I’ve never been much of a gold person. But seeing the unstoppable gold-price rally, I wish I were.

At prices of about Rs 1.2 lakh per 10 g, it is growing out of reach for most people. Yet, that is the least of the country’s top jeweller Malabar Gold and Diamonds’ problems.

The Kerala-based jewellery chain with 379 stores across the world pipped competitor Tanishq to become the number one player back in 2021. And since then, its sales have jumped five times in six years to Rs 66,000 crore in FY25, even as Titan’s Tanishq and Kalyan Jewellers have seen sales grow at a slower 3X and 2.8X respectively.

It’s the result of a strategy designed for fast growth. Right from the way it structures its franchisees to the way it positions its making charges, Malabar’s strategy has been all about context.

While it helped the retailer see industry-beating growth, it has ended up with a labyrinthine ownership-and-capital structure that it needs to change if it wants to work itself up to an eventual IPO.

In today’s story, Aakriti peels back the layers of Malabar Gold and Diamonds, which has historically been a tight-lipped company.

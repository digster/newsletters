Why are FMCG distributors always angry? Also featuring Swiggy and Dmart 
 By Seetharaman G https://the-ken.com/writers/seetharamang/

You are reading our subscriber-only newsletter Trade Tricks—a weekly commentary on Indian retail and commerce 

Today’s edition of Trade Tricks is one of those quick round-ups we do every once in a while to catch up on interesting developments we haven’t really had time to touch upon. 

To kick things off, there’s FMCG distributors’ forever war against “deep discounts” and “predatory pricing” on e-commerce and quick-commerce channels. After having penned an open letter https://x.com/aicpdf_official/status/1909896560454078637?ref_src=twsrc%5Etfw%7Ctwcamp%5Etweetembed%7Ctwterm%5E1909896560454078637%7Ctwgr%5E7f948b97f9dc1b3a39efa1a8a57e2f58442e4da2%7Ctwcon%5Es1_&ref_url=https%3A%2F%2Fwww.medianama.com%2F2025%2F04%2F223-aicpdf-urges-fmcg-giants-halt-quick-commerce-supplies%2F to FMCG companies on 25 March, and prodding them for meetings between 20–30 April that some manufacturers didn’t take seriously, the All India Consumer Products Distributors Federation (AICPDF) has now called for a “‘non-cooperation https://www.thehindubusinessline.com/companies/distributors-body-plans-action-against-fmcg-firms-over-deep-discounts/article69537382.ece” movement.

The organisation will take action against FMCG makers that have not responded to the body’s open letter seeking a level playing field for general trade.

“We have discussed with a few FMCG companies how quick commerce and e-commerce are impacting the business. The companies have assured us that they would implement a process to ensure fair play for general trade. However, there are FMCG makers who have not responded to our multiple calls. The body will be taking action, and a non-cooperation movement will begin soon,” said Dhairyashil Patil, National President of AICPDF, to businessline.

Distributors’ body plans action against FMCG firms over deep discounts, The Hindu Businessline

Something similar is already playing out with Colgate distributors in Maharashtra under the aegis of the AIPCDF, with many having announced that they will call off purchases from 12 May citing unfair practices https://www.thehindubusinessline.com/companies/colgate-distributors-to-stop-product-purchases-in-maharashtra/article69544361.ece in the company’s sales to quick-commerce platforms. 

None of this is some sudden outpouring of discontent, though, just the latest in a long line of protests, boycotts, and negotiations spanning years. The Ken has written multiple times about the strained relationship between FMCG majors (such as HUL and Colgate) and their offline distributors—in 2021 https://the-ken.com/story/jiomarts-way-out-of-udaans-fmcg-squeeze/, in early 2022 https://the-ken.com/tradetricks/fmcg-companies-uneasy-truce-with-distributors/, and again later that year https://the-ken.com/tradetricks/fmcg-distributors-are-angry-with-hul-again/. Things have been on a constant simmer since. 

Here’s what I wrote in my last piece on HUL’s earlier difficulties:

In addition to being a vital intermediary in the relationship between FMCG companies and kiranas, distributors are also an informal source of credit for these stores. And, as a distributor told me last year, FMCG launches often mean losses for distributors, who are asked to put enough time and resources into making sure the new products are visible on store racks.

But they still do FMCG companies’ bidding till the products take off or are binned for good. So, it’s not at all surprising that HUL, despite being India’s largest FMCG company, has reciprocated by repeatedly rushing to placate its distributors.

Do offline distributors still command enough clout to get FMCG manufacturers to do the same again? How these latest murmurs of discontent play out should give us a clue.

Shaky business

Quick-commerce may concern offline distributors enough to get their hackles up, but that doesn’t mean quick-commerce companies are having a field day. Especially Swiggy, which has reported https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/tech/startups/swiggy-q4-losses-nearly-double-to-rs-1081-crore-despite-45-revenue-growth/articleshow/121052306.cms?from=mdr a net loss of Rs 1,081 crore in the fourth quarter of FY25, with a revenue growth of around 45%. 

And it’s not just that. Swiggy has also been in the news for the shut down of its hyperlocal peer-to-peer courier service Genie. Yet another sign that the company is slowly cutting down on experimental projects and choosing to focus on its core food-delivery vertical to crack profitability, as The Ken wrote last year https://the-ken.com/story/swiggy-used-to-be-a-playground-for-innovation-now-its-a-graveyard/?searchTerm=swiggy%20playground. 

https://the-ken.com/?post_type=trade_tricks&p=1582440?utm_source=ro_hdr&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=View in Browser https://the-ken.com/?post_type=trade_tricks&p=1582440?utm_source=ro_hdr&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=

In a recent interview with The Economic Times, Swiggy Food CEO Rohit Kapoor gave a hint as to what Swiggy might choose to focus on instead—food deliveries in under 10 minutes with its new unit Bolt, which already contributes 12% of its revenue. 

“Bolt fits into the way people live today. You’re hungry, you want something now, and you don’t want to compromise. We built Bolt for that moment. Seeing it scale to 500-plus cities in just a few months has been incredible. And this is just the beginning.”

Rohit Kapoor, CEO, Swiggy Food Marketplace

An interesting choice, especially given rival Zomato recently decided to wind up its own 10-minute food offering, Zomato Quick. 

Dmart in dire straits

And to round off this week’s round-up, there’s Dmart, which just recorded its lowest profit margin in three years in the fourth quarter of FY25. Navil Noronha, CEO of Dmart, attributes this to the cut-throat competition prevalent in the FMCG sector now, apart from the increase in entry-level wages and the company’s ongoing investments to enhance service efficiency. 

As we’ve written multiple times in this newsletter, and even discussed on the Two by Two podcast https://the-ken.com/newsletters/two-by-two/dmart-versus-the-challengers-at-the-gate/, Dmart is facing challenges on many fronts as India’s retail market changes in fundamental ways. And its results for the latest quarter are an indication that these problems are coming home to roost. 

To quote from a Trade Tricks piece https://the-ken.com/tradetricks/navil-noronhas-sterling-legacy-at-dmart-hangs-in-the-balance/ published early this year: 

…the strongest criticism of Noronha’s stint is that he was perhaps too sanguine about Dmart’s resistance to the e-commerce threat. 

In the initial years after Avenue’s listing, Noronha managed to parry analysts’ questions about the company’s lukewarm approach https://the-ken.com/tradetricks/dmarts-e-commerce-bet-has-gone-from-counterintuitive-to-obsolete/ to e-commerce. Sure, online-grocery shopping was growing, but no one was close to making money. And Dmart was quite good at constantly becoming a better version of its frugal, highly profitable self. 

So investors thought Dmart knew something they didn’t. But quick commerce’s emergence against all odds has made them rethink that assumption. Dmart, too, knows that it needs to act. 

In the December quarter, the supermarket operator sacrificed some of the profits it holds so dear at the altar of growth. It ramped up discounts to persuade shoppers spoiled rotten by 10-minute delivery to keep visiting its stores. Sales in stores that existed a year ago grew 8%, but margins dipped.

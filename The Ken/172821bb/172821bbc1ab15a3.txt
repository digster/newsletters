Is it just me, or are these pandemic-induced changes just hard to keep up with? I mean, just a few weeks ago, Zomato, a food delivery company, said it wanted to deliver groceries. And now, it has realised it's a bad idea and wants to shutter it (PS: You read it first here). Then, there is industrialist Gautam Adani. Last year, he wanted airports; now, he doesn’t. But one has to keep trying. That’s why, don’t be surprised if you see edtech firms offering dancing lessons on Swiggy. Or if you find yourself a spouse, even if jobless, in this lockdown.

Zomato doubles down on grocery, then shuts it 

Abinaya 

India’s foodtech unicorn Zomato is shutting its grocery business, Zomato Market, just two months after launching it in 80 cities https://www.livemint.com/industry/retail/zomato-begins-grocery-delivery-in-80-cities-11586271221937.html. This was confirmed to The Ken by two people—a senior executive currently working for Zomato, and a former executive. Zomato has decided the grocery business is not scalable, they added. With food orders slumping 70% since India’s lockdown started in late March, grocery was a way for both Zomato and its rival Swiggy to utilise their idle delivery fleet and find an alternate revenue stream. 

While Zomato stopped short of calling it a closure, the company said it is going to focus on its core business—food delivery. This is what a Zomato spokesperson said in an emailed reply to The Ken (emphasis added):

Zomato Market will continue to operate and service users who need on-demand delivery of essentials. However, as the country opens up basis Unlock 1.0 relaxations, we are going to spend a large proportion of our time making our food delivery service the safest, and the most loved one in town.

Zomato and Swiggy were late to the grocery game, which is a punishing business with low margins, just like food delivery. Hyperlocal grocery also has low average order values, along with the added disadvantage of having no control over inventory. It relies on the stores to have adequate items and this needs to reflect on the app to avoid unavailability of products. 

So despite the fact that Zomato is food-adjacent, it is essentially a delivery company, with a model that is not optimised for grocery. But it’s not just Zomato. 

Only companies selling essentials were allowed to remain open during the lockdown. This saw a sundry of companies jumping on the grocery bandwagon. Real estate platform NoBroker, social commerce firm Meesho, and fintech startup Perpule are just some of the companies that rushed towards grocery after the lockdown was imposed. 

This, even as pure-play e-grocers Bigbasket and Grofers were struggling to fulfill orders during the lockdown. As India slowly opens up and services resume, it will not be a surprise to see many of these new entrants abandon their grocery ambitions. 

Then again, could Zomato’s renewed food delivery focus have anything to do with the newest player in town https://the-ken.com/story/amazon-food-india/?

Billionaires, airlines, and airports

Rohin

“If you want to be a millionaire, start with a billion dollars and launch a new airline,” said Richard Branson, the maverick founder of Virgin Airlines.

It’s possible Gautam Adani, the billionaire chairman of the Adani Group, never read those words. Or perhaps he thought that it wouldn’t apply to airports. Because last year, he emerged out of nowhere to aggressively bid and win contracts https://asia.nikkei.com/Business/Companies/Indian-billionaire-Adani-snaps-up-6-pricey-airport-contracts to all six airports India was offering to privatise. He won the right to run them for 50 years.

At a stroke, Indian billionaire Gautam Adani has positioned his mines-to-energy conglomerate to become one of the country's leading airport operators -- despite a conspicuous lack of experience in the sector.

This week Adani Group snapped up six contracts for modernizing airports in smaller cities, beating out established operators and fellow newcomers such as GMR Group and Kerala State Industrial Development Corp. The clean sweep raised questions not only over the bid process, but also over how Adani Group will fund the huge operations.

Indian billionaire Adani snaps up 6 pricey airport contracts, Nikkei Asian Review

Adani’s aggressive bidding played a factor.

Industry observers and competitors have been particularly critical of the high bids that Adani Group submitted.

A source in one of the rival bidders questioned the viability of the per-passenger revenue share that Adani Group has promised the airport authority. The company offered to pay 177 rupees per passenger for the most lucrative Ahmedabad contract, compared to GMR Group's 85 rupee bid.

As did his clean sweep of all six airports on offer.

"Adani has no experience in the airport sector and the only thing that is known to all is that he knows [Prime Minister Narendra] Modi quite well. The entire bid process appears to be mysterious. If not, how can a single person win all the five bids," said Pinarayi Vijayan, the chief minister of Kerala state, on Feb. 25 after Adani won the contract for the Thiruvananthapuram airport. For the same the airport, Kerala State Industrial Development Corp. had bid 135 rupees, while GMR Group offered 63 rupees for the same contract. The sixth contract was awarded on Feb. 26.

Yesterday, news broke that the Adani Group is trying to wriggle out of three of the airport contracts, citing “force majeure” https://www.business-standard.com/article/companies/adani-group-says-can-t-take-over-3-airports-cites-coronavirus-impact-120060400171_1.html.

The Adani group has expressed its inability to take over three airports it won after aggressive bidding last year, dealing a blow to the government’s privatisation plan.

The group has cited uncertainty in the aviation sector due to the coronavirus pandemic for seeking at least six months to take over Lucknow, Mangalore and Ahmedabad airports, sources told Business Standard. It has written to the Airport Authority of India (AAI) to invoke the force majeure clause to postpone the takeover and it has appointed consultants to re-evaluate the viability of the business.

Adani group seeks more time to take over 3 airports, cites coronavirus, Business Standard

If the Adani Group pulls out of these three airports, the ripple effects will be felt by the Indian government. It wanted to privatise another six airports this year.

Sachet learning meets premium online real estate. The results are...odd?

Olina

People consume sauce and shampoo sachets that often come free with a higher-value item. Now, can online classes also become that sort of a perk?

Swiggy surely thinks so:

What is Shaw Academy? It's an online marketplace for courses on photography, finance, and design, among others, priced at a reasonable subscription of Rs 600 (US$8) a month.

Before the pandemic, edtech companies were keen to bundle all-inclusive subscription packages to be sold at a premium. Now, companies are happy to offer them individually—sachet classes—for a few hundred rupees. The only problem to solve was distribution at a reasonable customer acquisition cost.

Enter Swiggy. Or even home services startup Urban Company. Cross-selling of health and nutrition products on food delivery apps makes sense. It's in the broad ballpark of ingesting things. But why cross-sell dance classes? 

Because Swiggy, Zomato, and Urban Company are few apps that can be habitual even during a pandemic. Infact, they’ve become habitual because of the pandemic. Even though food delivery transactions are down, with a switch to groceries, food delivery apps have become essential services. 

This in turn makes them unparalleled online real estate, whether you’re an aggregator like Shaw Academy, or an individual tutor hoping to be listed on Urban Company. 

How many people out shopping for electrical services will sample an online class, though, is still a question.

If you’re Unacademy, though, and you continue to raise https://tech.economictimes.indiatimes.com/news/startups/unacademy-eyeing-unicorn-club-as-it-talks-for-100-150m/76201302 millions of dollars hand over fist, pandemic or not, you’re looking at a slightly bigger marketing canvas. Featuring cricket and Bollywood stars.

Beyond the Covid-19 health scare

Savio

World Health Organization (WHO) director general Tedros Adhanom Ghebreyesus has warned that an old problem could be made worse due to the pandemic. The increased use of antibiotics to treat Covid-19 patients will strengthen bacterial resistance and ultimately lead to more deaths during the crisis and beyond, he said. But that was not his only concern.

Highlighting inappropriate usage, he said there was an “overuse” of antibiotics in some countries, while in low-income states such life-saving medicines were unavailable “leading to needless suffering and death”.

WHO warns overuse of antibiotics for Covid-19 will cause more deaths, The Guardian https://www.theguardian.com/world/2020/jun/01/who-warns-overuse-of-antibiotics-for-covid-19-will-cause-more-deaths

WHO’s 155-country survey showed the prevention and treatment of non-communicable diseases (NCDs) had been severely disrupted. The most common reasons for this were cancellations of treatments, reduced availability of public transport, and reassigned health workers. Patients with NCDs, like heart disease, cancer, diabetes and chronic lung disease, are at higher risk of Covid-related deaths.

About 53% of the countries reported partially or completely disrupted services for hypertension treatment, the survey showed. The figure was 49% for diabetes, 42% for cancer, and 31% for cardiovascular emergencies. Rehabilitation services were disrupted in 63% of the countries. But again, it was a question of affordability. 

Globally, two-thirds of countries reported that they had included NCD services in their national COVID-19 preparedness and response plans; 72% of high-income countries reported inclusion compared to 42% of low-income countries.

COVID-19 significantly impacts health services for noncommunicable diseases, WHO https://www.who.int/news-room/detail/01-06-2020-covid-19-significantly-impacts-health-services-for-noncommunicable-diseases

India’s numbers, therefore, came as no surprise to health experts.

In India, 30 per cent fewer cardiac emergencies reached health facilities in rural areas compared to the previous year, the WHO said in a presentation describing the findings. 

[...]

An independent study by a senior WHO researcher had found last month that India’s number of tuberculosis patients registered weekly had dropped by a precipitous 75 per cent during the first three weeks of the lockdown.

How coronavirus hit rural health system, The Telegraph https://www.telegraphindia.com/india/how-coronavirus-hit-rural-health-system/cid/1777769

Then, there’s polio, not an NCD but a serious health issue just the same. India’s pulse polio immunisation drive has come to a halt, ThePrint reported https://theprint.in/health/polio-immunisation-falls-casualty-to-covid-as-fear-staff-shortage-halt-drive-across-india/433842/?utm_source=izooto&utm_medium=push_notification&utm_campaign=ThePrint. This, despite it being an essential service and pushback from the Ministry of Health and Family Welfare (MoHFW).

Despite orders to start again, it is still not fully functional,” Dr Naveen Thacker, executive director of International Pediatrics Association told ThePrint.

“However, it should start on a priority basis and the government must analyse the risk versus benefits. We cannot afford another epidemic at the cost of one epidemic,” said Thacker, who is among the top influencers for the fight against polio according to UNICEF.

Polio immunisation falls casualty to Covid as fear, staff shortage halt drive across India, ThePrint

When jobless, wed

Durga

It seems Indians have found a particularly unique coping mechanism to deal with Covid-19. Or, possibly, the loneliness that comes from social distancing and the various lockdowns—India had a particularly confusing set of lockdows between March and May—further reducing human interaction.

I’m not just rambling. Classifieds company and stock market darling Info Edge has released a document http://www.infoedge.in/pdfs/COVID-Q4-Results-IR.pdf to disclose Covid-19’s impact on companies under it. And it turns out that while the job market is crashing and burning, with absolutely no one even attempting to poke the real estate bear, one sector is doing pretty darn well—the marriage market.

For job portal Naukri and real estate portal 99acres, Covid-induced lockdowns meant a 0% growth.

Here’s data for the fourth quarter of the financial year 2020 (they’ve included Jan-April 2020) straight from the Info Edge presentation on ‘Disclosure of material impact of COVID–19 pandemic.’

Naukri is down 54% year-on-year.

99acres is down 86% year-on-year.

But the marriage portal under Info Edge, Jeevansathi, is up 11% year-on-year.

You know, when they said, “If jobless, get married,” they were kidding. At least half-kidding.

That being said, I see perks. This would be a great time to wed without feeling the societal pressure to serve the best biryani at the most lavish hotel with at least 10X the number of guests your neighbour invited. If that’s what is driving this Covid-time marriage brokering, well, at least some part of our showy wedding culture has been corrected.

So, if you’re looking for a partner on Jeevansathi or any other marriage portal these days, cheers, and a very happy low-budget wedding to you.

Incentives versus disincentives

Rohin

It’s not that most people have spent the better part of the last two months locked up in their homes. It’s that they’ve been “going to work” and “hanging out with friends” in shorts, track pants, t-shirts, and slippers.

Semi-formals? Ha. Formals? What are they?

If you’re an apparel brand, how do you jolt customers out of this endless torpor and convince them to buy some new clothes? How do you induce them to get out of their homes and head to stores? By offering them serious discounts.

But if you’re an apparel retailer, how do you ensure customers who visit your stores don’t violate social distancing norms? How do you ensure that your store isn’t overwhelmed by customers? By not offering discounts. 

Malls are where incentives and disincentives will collide https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/industry/services/retail/retailers-avoid-discounting-to-meet-social-distancing-norms/articleshow/76172070.cms.

When malls open next week, it’s unlikely that apparel and lifestyle retailers will offer discounts – even after being saddled with unsold inventory – for fear that rebates could lead to a surge in footfalls and jeopardise social distancing norms.

“We expect only serious shoppers to visit malls, discounts could attract a larger set of consumer base. Overcrowding is the last thing we want from a safety standpoint,” said J Suresh, chief executive officer at Arvind Fashions, which manages over two dozen brands including Calvin Klein, Tommy Hilfiger and Arrow.

Retailers avoid discounting to meet social distancing norms, The Economic Times

Sports sponsors in limbo

Ben

Formula One has announced the start of its delayed season, with eight https://www.channelnewsasia.com/news/sport/formula-one-unveils-eight-race-schedule-in-europe-from-jul-5-12796722 closed-door races. But some of its venues have raised concerns over the viability https://www.todayonline.com/singapore/singapore-f1-still-talks-whether-race-can-go-sept-chan-chun-sing of a closed-door race. It’s uncertain what effect this will have on sponsors, who have poured money to gain visibility among the millions who watch the live races.

Organisers have turned to online simulator races in a bid to retain fan and sponsor interest, but the experience is not the same. Formula One’s online races lack one of the best drivers in the sport, with current world champion Lewis Hamilton not taking part. In other online races, drivers themselves appear to be not taking things seriously. In one race, two drivers were taken out in deliberate accidents https://slate.com/technology/2020/05/indycar-virtual-race-controversy-crash-norris-pagenaud-ferrucci-askew.html, which wouldn’t happen in real life. In another, a driver brought in a ringer who helped him finish third, prompting his racing team to fire him https://sports.yahoo.com/formula-e-driver-says-he-was-fired-from-real-racing-team-for-cheating-in-virtual-racing-event-185905097.html. 

Brands often pick and choose the sports events and teams they sponsor, and associating with e-sports comes with its own risks https://www.adexchanger.com/mobile/doritos-to-sponsor-twitch-tourneys-embracing-live-gaming-risk-and-reward/. While televising closed-door events may still pull in some visibility, broadcasters want to terminate https://digiday.com/marketing/the-bundesliga-offers-sponsors-and-broadcasters-a-sanitized-glimpse-as-to-how-sports-will-restart/ their contracts with sports leagues. All of this is leads to lower visibility for sponsors as smaller online platforms snap up some of these broadcasting rights.

Brands have to decide if sponsoring sports events—offline or online—is a worthwhile venture.

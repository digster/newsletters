Nowhere on the planet can one run a small dialysis business sustainably. Nephroplus founder Vikram Vuppala knew this from the start. That he built a Rs 755 crore business that has now filed papers to go public shows that he’s put his market wisdom to good use, devising an effective business model that works in India and overseas.

Dialysis in India is a tough business, and the lack of adequate infrastructure despite high—and increasing demand—only underscores the challenges involved in building a sustainable and profitable model (Nephroplus made a Rs 67 crore profit in FY25) that can scale. 

Founded fifteen years ago, Nephroplus is one of the few companies that appear well on the way. It is already India’s—and Asia’s—largest dialysis service provider. And it is expanding, both inside and outside the country’s borders, for which it wants to put the Rs 353 crore it plans to raise from its IPO to good use. 

But before it could begin thinking about foreign (and more lucrative) shores, it first had to survive and thrive in the crucible that is India’s dialysis market. 

As a testing ground, there are few that are more taxing.

Hard training

“India has the lowest price point (for dialysis) in the world.”

Vikram Vuppala, founder, Nephroplus

Demand, like I mentioned earlier, is not the issue. According to Frost & Sullivan estimates, some 280,000 patients with end-stage renal disease (ESRD) were on dialysis in India in 2024, a number that’s projected to increase to 520,000 within the next four years. These patients typically require three dialysis sessions a week. Add in patients who require less frequent treatments and there are over a million people who need dialysis in the country every year. To serve them, we have around 5,500 dialysis centres; industry experts estimate we need at least 15,000. 

That there is a huge gap to be filled is obvious. 

The challenge is in the economics.

India is one of the cheapest countries in the world for dialysis treatments, though even this is quite pricey—patients on average pay around Rs 1,500–4,000 ($25–45) per session. 

But then, it is also very low-margin. 

Procurement costs are high, and the quality requirements stringent. Aside from dialysis machines, there are consumable supplies that need to be managed on a daily basis. So for small standalone clinics in the unorganised sector, which comprise a large portion of the country’s dialysis centres, profit margins could be as low as 3–5%. It is only at scale that the numbers start improving, to about 10%, though a few larger players may clock up to 20%. 

This makes the early days for any dialysis business quite tricky. Which perhaps explains why there are so few organised kidneycare chains here; those like Nephroplus, DCDC, Apex, Vituscare comprise just about a fourth of the market. 

Nephroplus operates a mix of standalone clinics and captive centres in hospitals, but it has also happened to lean heavily on a tactic that has helped it surmount this scaling hurdle. 

Public-private partnerships (PPP). 

Faced with the same challenges as private players, India’s government has been more than happy to “outsource” the actual infra-creation to private companies, notes Prabhat Shrivastava, founder and CEO of Vituscare, a growing kidneycare company that currently operates over 50 dialysis centers in north India. 

And unlike for many other treatments, it’s been willing to pay its PPP partners well—very close to market cost.

As the chart above shows, dialysis is the top procedure in terms of number of treatments under the Ayushman Bharat Mission (which offers Rs 5 lakh medical insurance cover for Indian families, fully paid-for by the government), and the second-highest in terms of money reimbursed.

So being able to dip into this pool has helped Nephroplus handily jump the scaling hurdle, and made procurement operations more cost-effective.

“PPPs in India have been really good for the taxpayers, and for Nephroplus as well, because here, one specialised domain has been outsourced by the government and operated very, very efficiently, as compared to the government running its own dialysis clinics.”

Vikram Vuppala, founder, Nephroplus

The company’s financials reflect this advantage. Its Ebitda margins have doubled from 11.11% in FY23 to 22.05% in FY25, according to its draft red herring prospectus (DRHP), and the cost of consumables has reduced by a third.

In the same period, its share of revenue through PPPs has increased from 22.4% to 32.6%.

Tested OK for export

Not all of this margin improvement can be attributed to PPPs and the scale they’ve brought the company, though. 

Because around the turn of the decade, Nephroplus also started seriously considering overseas opportunities. 

In 2020, when The Ken had spoken to Vuppula about Nephroplus’ first foreign foray—into the Philippines—he was quite cautious about the expansion. “I’m not putting the company at risk,” he told us, “India will still account for 75–80% of our total business.” 

Well, five years since, Nephroplus India business share is only 68% of revenue. The rest comes from markets like the Philippines, Uzbekistan, and Saudi Arabia.

No surprise then that in my conversation with him last week, Vuppula was a lot more optimistic. 

Out of its 490 clinics currently, only 43 are overseas, but these already bring in 31% of its revenue. And Nephrolus wants to significantly increase their number. 

The draw is how much more profitable foreign operations can be. India, as Vuppala mentioned earlier, has the lowest price point for dialysis in the world. The prices are quite a bit higher in other markets [see chart above]. 

So Nephroplus is now putting the expertise and resilience it has gained in India to good use abroad. In Uzbekistan, for instance, it won its PPP bid against major global dialysis chains such as Fresenius Medical Care and Diaverum.

Of course, Vuppula notes that there is “no single model for every market”. 

For example, in Uzbekistan, there are a limited number of suppliers for consumables, so sourcing during difficult times like the pandemic becomes challenging. This requires Nephroplus to maintain higher inventory to deal with possible supply disruptions, Vuppula said. “The quality of consumables or the availability of clinical staff are all very country-specific issues.”

Vuppula says it is NephroPlus’ experience with the “full stack of services” in India (across standalone clinics, captive centres in hospitals, and PPPs) that “gives us a platform to pick and choose which model works in which country and we adapt accordingly”.

“India is like the core engine, the core platform that is helping us write the overall efficiency.”

Vikram Vuppala, founder, Nephroplus

And efficiency is the driving force behind it all. 

Globally, most healthcare systems “just don’t have the scale nor the expertise” to make dialysis profitable, notes Shrivastava. 

But Nephroplus can do this because it has figured out how to make dialysis profitable in the world’s most low-margin market, and it believes it can help do the same for its partners in other countries. According to Vuppula, “the procurement power” it has developed across global suppliers is something it “can apply to select countries wherever possible”.

For example, in Uzbekistan, the PPP with Nephroplus costs the government a lower amount for treatments—$48 per session compared to $60 before the partnership. “Cheaper is not the right word… it’s a more cost-efficient way of operating dialysis clinics,” Vuppala says. 

And its overseas businesses are also helping improve its core India model. For one thing, many of these markets are far more stringently regulated, with bodies like Philhealth in the Philippines and the health ministry in Uzbekistan demanding “very strong clinical outcomes” with higher accountability and standards than India. 

Vituscare’s Shrivastava believes Nephroplus’ example is applicable to other Indian dialysis players too, because expanding into more margin-friendly foreign markets can get dialysis chains the capital they so desperately need to scale. 

In Indian healthcare—full of anaemic, VC-funded examples where profits, and sometimes even quality, are elusive—Nephroplus’ numbers provide a welcome relief. That’s not to say Nephroplus hasn’t attracted venture-capital or private-equity interest; according to Vuppula, it’s raised six rounds in the last 13 years. 

But this isn’t a space for the impatient. 

As Vuppala says, it’s a business for “founders sticking to their guns and not allowing any distractions or clinical quality compromises”.

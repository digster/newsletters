You should spare a thought for the new chief of India’s largest lender. Challa Sreenivasulu Setty, chairman and managing director of State Bank of India, is trying very hard to put some oomph back into deposits.

“Recurring deposits are the original systematic investment plans… Earlier, people would ask when their deposits would double. One product we are working on is Har Ghar Lakhpati—a goal of saving Rs 1 lakh by encouraging RDs. It is important to create this narrative. We want to create goal-oriented deposits.”

‘Har Ghar Lakhpati’: SBI’s new mantra for deposit accretion, Business Standard

A strong narrative around deposits is no longer optional for banks. For 30 months until October 2024, overall bank deposits lagged loans in growth. In the September quarter, SBI’s deposits expanded by less than 10% from a year earlier. Its credit grew 15%.

This trend increasingly appears irreversible. In the 11 years leading up to March 2023, the share of deposits in household financial wealth fell sharply. On the other hand, investments in the stock market—made both directly and through funds—grew their contribution by half.

With the Nifty 50, a benchmark index, gaining 40% since April 2023, it’s fair to assume that the plight of banks has only worsened. Indians can’t get enough of the stock market, with the number of demat accounts surging 4.5X since 2019 to almost 180 million. The fascination for equities is hardly a metropolitan affair; half the new investors in mutual funds are from smaller cities.

Shaktikanta Das, India’s central-bank governor, has more than once warned of “structural liquidity” issues resulting from the imbalance between credit and deposit growth, and banks relying on short-term, pricey sources of funding.

For years, deposits have been among the safest investment options. That’s exactly why generations have sworn by them. Young investors today, though, couldn’t care less for the conservatism of their parents. Financial influencers on social media dangle the possibility of juicy returns, not just from simple equity trades but also complex and very risky derivatives.

More than nine in 10 individuals trading in futures and options lost Rs 2 lakh (US$2,360) on average in the two years leading up March 2024, forcing the securities-markets regulator to intervene. One could argue that those singed by derivatives will be eager to park their money in something as safe as deposits.

But it’s hard to look past the price one has to pay for that compromise.

SBI’s term deposits—for periods ranging from seven days to 10 years—offer interest rates of 3.5–7.5% per annum. Meanwhile, investing in a mutual fund mirroring the Nifty 50 index can return 18% a year. Granted, the latter is skewed by a frothy market and deposits aren’t comparable with equities on returns.

But banks have a hard sell on their hands, which shows in State Bank of India’s plans to channel a part of the interest earned on deposits to mutual funds and the National Pension System, a government-backed retirement-savings scheme. In other words, the public-sector lender knows it can’t fight these investments, so it wants to join them. It’s a case of shutting the barn door after the horse has bolted.

Banks already sell insurance and mutual funds, and SBI’s new idea is merely an extension. This is a problem because banks need to continue financing homes, higher education, and vehicles, and if they can’t rely on low-cost deposits from individuals, it impacts their ability to lend. Non-banks have already increased their share of household debt in recent years.

Das has been prodding banks to innovate to draw customers back in. But how much novelty can lenders possibly inject into deposits? They are hamstrung by the returns they can offer and a possible rap on the knuckles from the central bank, or worse, should they be inspired by fintechs to offer a newfangled, disruptive investment product.

It doesn’t help that the experience of investing in deposits is still relatively lousy. Just compare the apps of a top bank and a large discount broker. Finding what you want on a bank app can often feel like navigating a maze. By contrast, stock trading has caught on partly because of how ridiculously easy it is.

As much as the stock market has gained at the expense of banks, it’s not immune to some pain from the latter’s struggles. Five banks have a combined weightage—an indicator of their importance—of 18% in the Nifty 50.

Investors in bank stocks may not be overly worried yet about their struggles. The Nifty Bank index is up over 14% over the past year, comparable to the Nifty 50’s 18%. But what threatens banks’ centrality in our lives will unfold gradually, then suddenly.

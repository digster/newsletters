Long and Short
Wed, 25 Jun 25
The story behind the numbers and motivations driving India's stock market..
Good Morning Ishan,
Life as a listed company is going swimmingly for Vishal Mega Mart.
In both the December and March quarters, the cut-price retailer of clothing, groceries, and household items increased its sales by roughly 20% compared to the same period the previous year. Its operating profit in the latest quarter surged by twice as much.
Investors are justifiably giddy. Vishal Mega Mart’s market value has increased by two-thirds to Rs 62,000 crore ($7.2 billion) since it began trading on the exchanges in December.
Despite its golden run, Vishal Mega Mart’s majority owners—private-equity firms Kedaara Capital and Partners Group—sold shares worth Rs 10,220 crore last week, or almost 20% of the company. With this, the promoter holding in the company is down to around 55%. Among the buyers were investment manager Vanguard Group, HDFC Mutual Fund, and SBI Mutual Fund.
The move clearly caught the market off guard, sending the small-town retailer’s shares down 8%.
It’s common for anchor investors to pare their holdings months into a company’s listing, after the lock-in period ends. But why would the promoters part with such a large stake, especially so soon after selling shares worth Rs 8,000 crore in the initial public offering?
“They wanted a valuation of Rs 50,000 crore in the IPO, but had to settle for around Rs 35,000 crore,” said an analyst tracking the company. By the time of the share sale, the go-go days of India’s stock market were a thing of the past.
The PE firms had bought the retailer from TPG Capital and Shriram Group for Rs 5,000 crore in 2018, and Kedaara’s investment in Vishal Mega Mart is through a nesting-doll-like structure.
| Source: IIFL Capital |
The second factor in when and how much Kedaara and Partners Group choose to sell is Gunender Kapur, Vishal Mega Mart’s chief executive. Kapur has been at its helm since 2011, when TPG and Shiram bought the struggling and debt-laden retailer from its founder for a mere Rs 70 crore.
Kapur, responsible for Vishal Mega Mart’s impressive turnaround, is 64 and may not run the company for much longer. In fact, he stepped down as CEO last year, only to be back in the job in three months, as the company’s IPO filing shows.
| Source: Vishal Mega Mart DRHP |
“They needed him for a smooth IPO,” said the analyst quoted earlier. “As long as he is around, there is blood flowing in every vein of Vishal.”
Kapur spent two decades at FMCG major Unilever, including as head of its Nigeria operations, and almost four years at Reliance Retail before joining Vishal Mega Mart in 2011. “Before Kapur moves on, the investors would want to cash out. With a new CEO, it’ll take 18–24 months for the market to be comfortable,” added the analyst.
What also explains the PE firms’ move is that they are financial—and not strategic—investors. They have to exit at some point, and it’s easier to do so in phases when the company in question is publicly traded. For instance, a consortium of PE funds has gradually halved its stake in Sapphire Foods, a franchisee of KFC and Pizza Hut, to 26% since its November 2021 listing.
The Kedaara fund that invested in Vishal Mega Mart is almost eight years old, and the life of a typical PE fund is 10 years, with a possible two-year extension. Interestingly, the Kedaara fund will turn 10 around the same time as the end of Kapur’s term.
Vishal Mega Mart’s other investors seem to have realised that their panic over the stake sale was overblown. Its shares rebounded and touched a new peak on Tuesday. The stock has run circles around those of its closest peers, V2 Retail and V-Mart Retail, this year.
Apparel contributes at least 80% of V2’s and V-Mart’s sales, which has made them vulnerable to competition from Trent-owned Zudio. Vishal Mega Mart, on the other hand, gets just over half as much of its revenue from clothes. What’s more, discount retailer Dmart is almost non-existent in the two regions where Vishal Mega Mart is the strongest: the north and the east.
So, to its investors, what Vishal Mega Mart’s promoters do with their stake right now is a minor distraction.

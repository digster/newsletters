Good Morning Ishan,
Of all the behavioural changes quick commerce has brought about, the anticipation-filled scrolling of the order-tracking screen takes the cake. For turning those few minutes into a treasure.
Advertisers believe the “undivided attention” they get on that screen is gold. And the shine is visible in the revenue that platforms are booking.
Now, do you get why you came for bread, but Blinkit says you also need a credit card?
Until a few years ago, most of the digital-marketing budget was divided between Google (50–60%), Meta (15–20%), and smaller channels taking the rest. But in just two years, quick commerce has carved out a 2–3% share of the budget. Reliable estimates suggest this could rise to 10% in the next two years.
Separately, a Goldman Sachs report estimates quick commerce will make 5–6% of its gross merchandise value (GMV) from ads in FY26. That’s as much as Amazon and Flipkart make.
For advertisers, spending on social media or e-commerce platforms comes with standard metrics for measuring success. On quick commerce, advertisers don’t know what success looks like.
Yet, they are game. For one, the highly trackable users from which advertisers get direct, transactional signals are a novelty. In [today’s story](https://the-ken.com/story/google-facebook-and-blinkit-the-new-battle-for-indias-ad-money/), Gaurav captures how quickly the tides are turning.

Global tech giants have invested a pile of money in projects aimed at adding the next millions and billions of users to the Internet. They are helping the unconnected in the most imaginative ways—offering WiFi at railway stations, beaming signals from hot air balloons, satellites, etc.
They did so because they can. The behemoths have been freely using their dominant position in one area to disrupt another. And the authorities can smell the coffee. In October, the Competition Commission of India (CCI) slapped [hefty penalties ](https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/tech/technology/google-fined-113-million-in-second-antitrust-penalty-this-month/articleshow/95080594.cms)on Google for abusing its market position in the mobile-OS market with its Android and for restricting app developers from using third-party payment processing services.
Now, the tech giant is taking baby steps to strike an agreement with the Tamil Nadu government to win access to its population health data. The southern Indian state is rolling out an unprecedented public-health programme called Makkalai Thedi Maruthuvam, wherein it plans to collect its residents’ blood samples, check their blood pressure, and screen them for cancer and kidney diseases at their doorstep.
Google, which has spent [billions of dollars](https://www.economist.com/business/2022/06/20/alphabet-is-spending-billions-to-become-a-force-in-health-care) on health ventures and is known for its lofty ambitions to integrate artificial intelligence into healthcare, is now helping the Tamil Nadu government build the population health registry (PHR), multiple executives and officials told The Ken.
It has already shelled out ~US$3 million in funding IT services company Tech Mahindra and a Delhi-based startup Lattice Innovations to help them create an IT architecture and mobile app for the PHR. But it’s up against other biggies like Microsoft, Amazon, and Apple to become a force to be reckoned with in the healthcare sector.

Tata Group’s Indian Hotels Company is a colossus standing astride India’s hospitality industry. Rooms, locations, revenue, profits, market value—on most major metrics, the company behind the Taj, Vivanta, and Ginger brands doesn’t have to worry about any competitor snapping at its heels.

But there’s a tiny hotel operator that is lately rivalling it for investors’ attention.

I’m positive the name Ventive Hospitality means nothing to you. It’s also very likely that many of you haven’t stayed at any of its properties, even if it runs hotels of global brands such as Marriott and Hilton. That’s because Ventive has a mere 11 operational hotels in just three locations.

But the company, which listed in December, has been among the best-performing hospitality stocks this year, rising 4%. Indian Hotels shares have lost as much of their value, and Lemon Tree, a budget-hotel company, has slid by over 10%.

So what draws investors to value Ventive at Rs 17,500 crore ($2 billion)?

For one, it makes up for its lack of size—it has over 2,000 rooms, compared with Indian Hotels’ 26,000—in squeezing more out of every room.

Kotak Institutional Equities, a broker, estimates Ventive’s revenue per available room—the average daily room rate multiplied by the occupancy rate—in FY25 to be close to Rs 13,500, or 40% higher than that of Indian Hotels.

Take any listed company in this business, and it’s instantly apparent who its typical guest is. EIH, which runs the Oberoi chain of hotels, targets the big spender with a taste for luxury. Lemon Tree Hotels is meant for those who don’t care for, or can’t afford, the trimmings of a ritzy hotel. It’s not that these companies are monolithic; Lemon Tree, for instance, runs hotels ranging from Rs 3,500–9,000 (US$41–107) on average per night.

But no one has quite taken it as far as Indian Hotels Company, whose market value recently crossed Rs 1 lakh crore. Famed for its Taj chain of top-tier hotels, the Tata Group enterprise is now as much about its cheaper variants, with none as prominent as Ginger, its budget-hotel brand.

With 70 hotels, Ginger is second only to Taj in footprint. Sooner than later, it’ll eclipse the latter.

A room at Ginger costs under Rs 4,000 a night on average. Staying at one of Taj’s palace-turned-hotels can set you back by over Rs 50,000. Two wildly divergent experiences. Not to mention a whole bunch of options in between. All offered by a single company. Whoever said you can’t be all things to all people?

Tata-owned Indian Hotels’ art of being all things to all people, The Ken

So brands like Ginger drag down Indian Hotels’ average room revenue. Ventive, on the other hand, is only in the business of top-tier hotels. But there is another, equally crucial factor behind Ventive faring better than Indian Hotels.

Three of Ventive’s eleven hotels are in the Maldives, the island state where it’s easy to find a room for a weekend that costs as much as India’s per capita income. Ventive’s other properties are in Pune and Bengaluru, and it’s now adding hotels in Varanasi and Sri Lanka.

Seen differently, Ventive needs its rooms to be pricey. Compared with hotel chains that operate largely in cities and cater to business travellers, Ventive’s dependence on the Maldives means it has to make do with the relatively low occupancy that comes with seasonality. Ventive’s estimated occupancy in FY25 is 63%, while Indian Hotels and Lemon Tree could both be looking at well over 70%.

Sure, Ventive has inherently high costs, thanks to the kind of hotels in its portfolio. But that doesn’t mean it isn’t a well-run operation.

Ventive’s Ebitda margin, a measure of operating efficiency, is behind only that of Lemon Tree, which has a Dmart-like reputation for keeping its costs in check, and the company behind the Leela chain of hotels.

If there’s one thing that might have made investors look askance at the company, it’s Ventive’s debt. In FY24, its net debt was over 4X its Ebitda, among the highest in the industry. But fixing that was pretty much the whole point of its IPO. Ventive said it would use nearly 90% of its Rs 1,600 crore fundraise to pare its debt, and has already halved its net debt-to-Ebitda.

In few sectors are public-market investors as spoilt for choice as in hospitality. After Ventive’s flotation, ITC Hotels was demerged from ITC and listed, and Apeejay Surrendra Park Hotels went public too. Lemon Tree has an IPO on the cards for one of its subsidiaries.

This means even more scrutiny of Ventive’s prospects, but its appeal is that it’s the antithesis of Indian Hotels. And that won’t go unnoticed.

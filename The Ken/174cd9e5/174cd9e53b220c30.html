Good Morning Ishan,
 
One of the heartiest bits of news in weeks for me has been the massive bureaucracy reforms—Mission Karmayogi. It felt like what Lenin said, "sometimes nothing happens for decades and some decades happen in days".
 
Two decades-old memories of a civil service aspirant came flooding back. Having failed to qualify for the Mains in the UPSC, it then seemed like the end of the road. I had declined banking and management job offers. There was no plan B. (Middle-class hang-ups, you know.)
 
Once my grieving was over, my academic parents tried counselling. Mum gently murmured something like, "this is not the only test of your merit." But dad was brutal. Bureaucracy was not for me, he said. "It's a lick-above-kick-below culture. With your kind of idealism, you'll be unhappy, and you'll make people around you unhappy."
 
Friends, meanwhile, argued I had three more attempts. Good fellows even showed stats to convince me that my subjects were not 'scoring subjects' and I could choose better next time.
 
More than any of the above, the exam itself felt like it was more a test of conformity; it could be gamed. I chose to quit.
 
Over the next 23 years in journalism, as I met all kinds of civil servants—neck-deep into missions; skin deep, waiting for the next posting; the garden-variety—dad's one-liner stayed with me.
 
I still wonder whether I took the right decision. Because as a journalist, one can only shine a light…
 
Well, we did shine a light on some great topics this week:
 
On Friday, Arundhati made a provocative case for scrapping external ratings for micro, small, and medium enterprises (MSMEs). The loan moratorium is over; the ratings will restart, and MSMEs will end up as roadkill. Some 35% of India's MSMEs already shut in June. A Supreme Court hearing tomorrow will decide if more businesses will meet this fate. (Interestingly, a Crisil executive calls this a 'babudom' vestige that exists only to save bureaucracy.)
 
Olina zoomed in on blue-collar jobs on Thursday. Using an app and a good matching algorithm, WorkIndia has flipped the recruitment model. Applicants call the employers; 90% of the latter are from SMEs. But the entire source-hire-manage process in recruitment has to be digital for real rewards. So far, the company has avoided anything anti-scale. Now it can't, even though it serves some big brands like Zomato, Swiggy, and Flipkart.
 
In her debut story on Wednesday, Bhumika wrote about the least-talked about factor in the ongoing edtech boom: teachers. Online teaching jobs are being added every day; there were at least 12K full-time jobs up for grabs as of June. But between pressure to bring in paid users and the need for edtechs to keep costs low, teaching online is anything but merely switching on the camera.
 
On Tuesday, Maitri and I looked at the National Digital Health Mission that was announced, rather hurriedly, on 15 August. Even as a bunch of startups and a think tank are racing ahead with building modules of the digital health infrastructure, there's much that needs to be in the public domain to fix accountability and build trust. First mover advantage for some and fringe benefits for others with citizens caught somewhere in the middle is not a blueprint for national infrastructure, we argued. It was also our free story of the week. So please share it widely.
 
In case you missed it, we began the week with Seetharaman who wrote how India is hit by a contagion—of super corporations. Companies today are transmitting their dominance from one sector to another. Think Reliance's heavy-weight entry into internet economy (after oil, petrochemical, and telecom dominance), or Google's into payments or Facebook's into social commerce. Unlike western regulators, however, the Indian regulator hasn't a clue to this contagion. But maybe it's time to rewrite the competition law.

On Saturday, our weekly newsletter The Nutgraf tackled the important topic of the Essential Commodities Act, which the Parliament of India amended last week. However, the story actually begins with a landmark court case in India seventy years ago, one that has its origins in the arrest of a couple for the crime of transporting cloth. 
 
That's it from us this week. Do check out our stories if you missed reading 'em. And enjoy your Sunday.
 
Warmly,

Good Morning Ishan,
“As long as the music is playing, you’ve got to get up and dance. We’re still dancing.” Chuck Prince, former CEO of Citigroup, dropped this gem just a few weeks before the global financial crisis of 2007, and it’s since become shorthand for reckless risk-taking in the financial markets—and the inevitable consequences. Profiting big from these consequences are daredevils on the other side of the trade, like the short-sellers epitomised by the oddballs in gems like The Big Short.
The more things change, the more they stay the same.
More than a decade and a half after that great tumult, the game of excess and consequence is now playing out in the Indian stock market. The music that once played almost non-stop after the pandemic has slowed sharply in the past few months, more so over the past few weeks. Stocks are tanking, and reckless risk-takers—there are many—are facing their moment of reckoning. At the other end of this trade, though, are not short-sellers but fund managers of a different variety. The ones who saw the moment of reckoning coming many months back by reading the valuation tea leaves and deciding to move before the tide turned.
Siddhartha Bhaiya, the founder of portfolio management service (PMS) provider Aequitas Investments, did just that. He sold a chunk of the stocks in the small-cap portfolio for tidy gains and bet big on cash. How big? Almost 90% of the portfolio. Yes, that big.
Too risky a bet? Just why? Does he expect the market to fall much more? Does this contrarian streak even make sense?
Turns out, Bhaiya is contrarian in many more ways. His clients, the ultra-rich of the country, are happy to play along with a limited-edition fund manager who is winning by, well, not playing.

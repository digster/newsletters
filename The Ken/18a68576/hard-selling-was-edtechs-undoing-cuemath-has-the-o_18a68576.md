Good Morning Ishan,
When times are tough, a company needs its sales force to step up and really work its magic.
But what if the sales force is partly the reason why the company is struggling?
That is the story of edtech in India.
A lot of hard selling and misselling eroded trust in an industry that once attracted hundreds of millions of dollars in funding.
So it’s not all that surprising that Manan Khurma is scripting a turnaround for his $400 million-worth math-learning startup, Cuemath, that’s light on selling and heavy on teaching.
To his colleagues, Khurma himself is a tutor first, then a founder. And he has now made it mandatory for every Cuemath employee to teach to help them understand its products and learners. “People who teach don’t see customers as numbers on an Excel spreadsheet or revenue accrued,” he told my colleague Alifiya, the author of today’s story.
Radical or gimmicky? Depends on whom you ask.
A few months ago, Khurma assumed charge once again as CEO of the company, after letting a former COO of Swiggy run the company for a year and a half.
“The problem is, does the company need this kind of approach now, or does it need a business leader?” said a former employee.

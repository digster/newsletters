Good Morning Ishan,

I hope you all enjoyed a reinvigorating first week of 2024. I don’t know about you, but the hot topic of conversation in my circles lately has been New Year’s resolutions. 

Personally, I don’t go through this exercise for myself. I believe that if one wants to make a change in their life or start working towards a goal, they can take the first step at any time. However, I do appreciate that turning resolutions into an annual ritual helps people consciously think about their ambitions and how to get there. 

Consequently, you learn a lot about one’s aspirations and what they see as obstacles to achieving these by just knowing their New Year’s resolutions. So, it’s rather fitting that all our stories this week touched on these very themes of self-improvement. 

On Monday, Seetharaman kicked off the year with the results of The Ken’s survey of over 1,060 employees and employers on workplace trends. Our survey provided unique insights into how companies and employees expect trends like salary hikes, working from the office, and generative AI to play out in the year ahead. 

Arundhati’s story on Tuesday was about what happens when failed startups return the capital that they raised from their backers. While founders believe giving money back is the most ethical route, they also have pragmatic reasons, like pursuing a fresh start with no baggage. What complicates matters is that their investors are often less than thrilled by such plans. 

Meanwhile, across the Bay of Bengal, Michelle explored the struggles of Unilever Indonesia, which is battling flattening topline growth and falling profits on one hand and a boycott movement on the other. Indonesian supporters of Palestine have shunned the consumer-goods giant for its perceived ties to Israel—a blow that comes as it enters a “sunset stage.”

On Wednesday, Alifiya delved into why Indian students’ dreams of studying in Canada are receiving a harsh but necessary reality check. While international students are an economic boon for Canadian institutions, the influx is reaching unsustainable levels due to a housing shortage, the rising cost of living, and a lack of job opportunities. 

Rounak and Nuha teamed up to report on US-based startup Atlys, which wants to disrupt VFS Global’s monopoly on visa services. Travellers are fed up with VFS’ poor service, which comes with long queues, tons of paperwork, and a hefty price tag. Atlys promises quicker, more consistent results driven by automation—though it’s up against a mighty opponent. 

In our international edition, Prima wrote about Joko Widodo, Indonesia’s outgoing president, who ushered in a transformative era for startups and the tech industry. The candidates seeking to replace him say they want to continue building the country’s digital economy. Yet, none of them seem to have a full-fledged vision of their own.

Narayanan closed out the week with a story on Chennai’s ecosystem of global capability centres, or GCCs. Once a top destination for multinational players’ technology centres, the coastal city has lost ground to other tech hubs over the decades. While the state government is pushing hard to revitalise the city, concerns about its lack of top-tier tech talent persist.

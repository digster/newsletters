Good Morning Ishan,

Hope you are indoors. Hope you are safe.

It’s week two of the lockdown for most of us here in India and, by now, some of us have found a working routine. Others have just given up all hopes of figuring one out and just muddle through the day. Personally, I am somewhere in between. I have good days where I accomplish a lot. I have bad days, where it’s 8PM and the house is a mess, the dishes are in the sink, and my To-Do list is unfinished, leaving me unsure what I did all day.

The purpose of this newsletter is to connect the dots and give you a larger picture of the events that happened through the week. That’s getting more challenging. The pace of events has accelerated, and things get outdated in days, forget a week (that’s also why we launched a new daily newsletter, Beyond The First Order—you can check it out here https://the-ken.com/bfo/).

So I decided that the best way for The Nutgraf to stay relevant is to connect recent events to something foundational, conceptual, and long-lasting, instead of drawing transient connections that probably get outdated in a week.

I am trying something different today. I hope you like it.

Let’s dive in.

Lepers to Plague to Covid-19

If you’ve been following the news in India, a few things have been happening, especially around how people who are under quarantine are being treated.

In Delhi, the state government is tracking people using their smartphones. The state government handed over https://www.hindustantimes.com/india-news/covid-19-police-told-to-track-mobile-phones-of-people-under-quarantine-says-delhi-cm/story-2sZ90oirpjMrKbjnG1KNRI.html 25,000 phone numbers to the police, with the express purpose of tracking the owner’s location to find out whether they leave their homes or come into contact with others.

Karnataka, on the other hand, did something else. First, they published a list of addresses https://www.deccanherald.com/opinion/covid-19-lockdown-public-sharing-of-home-quarantine-addresses-a-bad-idea-818130.html of everyone in the state who was under home quarantine. Within hours, through WhatsApp, this excel file found its way into apartment complex groups, and pretty soon, all hell broke loose. A few days later, the government decided that the best way to track everyone under home quarantine was to ask them to send hourly selfies https://www.livemint.com/news/india/selfies-every-hour-gps-verification-to-enforce-home-quarantine-in-karnataka-11585587801659.html using a mobile app. The geo-location tagged to the pictures was then checked against the global positioning system (GPS) location of their homes—all to ensure that home quarantine wasn’t being violated.

Karnataka and Delhi aren’t alone. Other states in India are doing their own thing to control the spread of the novel coronavirus. Nor is India alone in this. South Korea, Singapore, China, Israel, and Italy have all resorted to similar tactics, as summed up in this fantastic story https://www.nytimes.com/2020/03/23/technology/coronavirus-surveillance-tracking-privacy.html?referringSource=articleShare in The New York Times.

Now, one could reasonably argue this needs to be done. These are dangerous times with exceptional circumstances, and that justifies exceptional measures.

Sure. Maybe.

But I thought, this is a great time for some historical context. And some elementary political theory. How did we get here? What does it mean? And what next? For all of us.

It all starts with this person named Michel Foucault, a French philosopher, who chronicled what happened during the Plague in Europe in the 17th century.

And how it changed the relationship between the individual and the nation state forever.

The changing nature of power

From 1971 to 1984, Foucault gave a series of public lectures https://journals.openedition.org/lectures/18113?lang=en at the College de France. They were attended by thousands, and offered fascinating insights into multiple topics in political theory.

In one such lecture, Foucault talks about the difference in how society dealt with two threats to public health—lepers and plagues.

Lepers were handled like outcasts. They were forced to wear bells around their necks so others would be aware if they were around. They were segregated and sent to colonies. They were cast away. And this was “regularly accompanied by a kind of funeral ceremony during which individuals who had been declared leprous were declared dead [and which they themselves had to attend]”.

Excommunicated. Sent away. Gone.

When the Black Plague hit in the 17th century, all of this changed.

People afflicted with Plague were handled differently. Instead, here’s how it was handled https://www.3quarksdaily.com/3quarksdaily/2013/03/foucaults-plague.html:

When plague arrived, it was inconvenient and undesirable, if not impossible and ill-advised, to empty out an entire town. However, towns already provided an existing technology for the control of plague.

The solution was quarantine, and the technology was the urban form itself.

Known as quadrillage, the affected town was divided into smaller and smaller sections, with administrators for each section appointing delegates for each subdivision, and so forth. Streets were guarded and movement thoroughly restricted. A twice-daily roll call required each resident to appear at a prescribed window; anyone who did not do so was assumed to have taken ill with the plague, and a team would be sent into the house to remove the newest victim, before they could infect others. The results would be compiled into registers, which would be recompiled into larger and more comprehensive registers, ultimately reaching the main accounting apparatus in the city hall.

This was much more than a brutal, unsentimental game of waiting it out.

Foucault's Plague by Misha Lepetic, 3 Quarks Daily

Sounds familiar?

Foucault points out that this was unprecedented. This was a new manifestation of power—one that transcends exclusion. It was no longer the power to drive people away and to excommunicate them, but to keep them around, observe them, and to identify them. And to do this continuously.

This form of power requires a few things to make it work.

First, it requires a mechanism for surveillance.

And second, it requires broad, willing compliance.

To describe this mechanism better, Foucault uses an architectural concept called the Panopticon.

The prison with no doors

Imagine a prison.

Imagine that all the cells are arranged in a circular fashion, and that the guard sits in the centre. None of the cells can see each other, but the guard in the centre can see them all.

It looks a bit like this.

Got it?

Now let’s take it one notch higher.

Imagine that the guard could see all the cells but the prisoners in the cells couldn’t see the guard. Perhaps the guard is hidden behind some blinds. So a prisoner wouldn’t know if he was being watched or not.

That’s a Panopticon.

What does this mean, in essence?

Although it is physically impossible for the single guard to observe all the inmates' cells at once, the fact that the inmates cannot know when they are being watched means that they are motivated to act as though they are being watched at all times.

Thus, the inmates are effectively compelled to regulate their own behaviour.

Panopticon, Wikipedia

The Panopticon is the simplest concept of a surveillance system. At its core, the idea of the panopticon is that it’s the most ideal form of power. It’s also efficient and economic—a large number of people can be controlled and a small number of people are needed to operate it.

The idea that Foucault puts forward is that the Panopticon goes beyond just lepers and plagues. It operates in society in abstract, deep ways. He argues that this structure is why taboos exist. People hide shameful aspects of themselves, and conform to a “disciplinary society,” falling into line because of a fear that they are being watched. Not just by a state, but by the society itself.

Or something like that. That’s my reading. Foucault is complicated, and notoriously dense to read.

And if that’s true, by extension, this is what leads to society being willing to stay at home under surveillance, or to submit to hourly selfies, or bang their pans at a specified time in the balcony of their houses.

Here’s a part that struck a chord with me:

That is why discipline ﬁxes; it arrests or regulates movements; it clears up confusion; it dissipates compact groupings of individuals wandering about the country in unpredictable ways; it establishes calculated distributions.

It must also master all the forces that are formed from the very constitution of an organized multiplicity; it must neutralize the effects of counter-power that spring from them and which form a resistance to the power that wishes to dominate it: agitations, revolts, spontaneous organizations, coalitions — anything that may establish horizontal conjunctions

Discipline & Punish - Panopticism, Foucault

Oh, by the way, Atlas Obscura has a great photo essay https://www.atlasobscura.com/articles/panopticon-prison-cuba about a Panopticon prison in Cuba. It’s beautiful.

The coronavirus is testing India's federal structure 

Kerala, one of India’s southern states, is quite unhappy.

With whom? The Indian central government.

Kerala Finance Minister Thomas Isaac, in an interview with Business Standard, makes it very clear why https://www.business-standard.com/article/economy-policy/why-is-centre-so-stingy-with-spending-amid-covid-crisis-says-kerala-fm-120040101832_1.html:

National income will be in negative territory in FY21. World growth is also going to be negative. The US, being so conservative, has announced a $2 trillion package. Others are also spending. Why is the Indian government being so stingy about spending? I am certain the Centre will be forced by circumstances to change its position.

Thomas Isaac, in an interview with Business Standard

There’s some history here.

The relationship between the central government and some states have been tense for a while, especially after the implementation of the Goods and Services Tax (GST), which centralised tax collections at the Centre and distributed the state’s individual share back to them.

Except, of late, the centre has been… missing payments to some states. One of which is Kerala, which has been quite vocal about this in the past.

Last week, Kerala broke ranks. Its chief minister, Pinarayi Vijayan, announced that the state would start conducting https://scroll.in/pulse/957726/coronavirus-kerala-announces-rapid-blood-tests-icmr-says-it-was-not-consulted rapid antibody tests on those suspected to have Covid-19. That is unusual because the Indian Council of Medical Research (ICMR), which is responsible for India’s testing regime, has only used RT-PCR or reverse transcriptase polymerase chain reaction test—a different test that takes longer to give results but is more accurate.

Kerala says it has the approval from the ICMR. The ICMR says, umm, no, you don’t.

Everyone is confused. But Kerala seems to be plowing ahead.

Maybe Kerala is doing this just to stick it to the Central government. Or maybe... Kerala, a state that knows a thing or two about controlling epidemics https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2018_Nipah_virus_outbreak_in_Kerala, and has the second-highest number of coronavirus cases in India (as of yesterday), knows what it’s doing.

Plus, to add insult to injury, the Central government opened up its new charitable trust—the Prime Minister’s Citizen Assistance and Relief in Emergency (PM-CARES) fund—for donations https://www.deccanherald.com/national/pm-cares-fund-not-to-accept-financial-assistance-from-foreign-governments-820541.html from foreign individuals and groups to help fight the coronavirus.

While this isn’t a reversal in policy, as some media outlets suggest, one can’t help but remember that Kerala tried to get funds through the foreign donations route when it was struck by floods a couple of years ago, and was denied from doing so—by the central government, in accordance with a policy.

By the way, Vijay Parshad, in his phenomenal newsletter for The Tricontinental https://www.thetricontinental.org/newsletterissue/newsletter-14-2020-migrant-workers/, writes about Kerala, and describes it as a ‘window in a ghastly wall’, in the context of migrant workers who are on their way back home:

Kerala—the state governed by the Left Democratic Front—is a window in the ghastly wall. The government is opening thousands of camps for migrant workers in Kerala who would need accommodation. As of 28 March, 144,145 migrant workers had been housed in 4,603 camps, and more camps are being opened. The government is also building camps for homeless and destitute people—44 camps have been opened so far in which 2,569 people are staying. The state has opened community kitchens across the state to provide free hot meals; for those who cannot come to the kitchens, the food is delivered to their homes.

Please break the walls and build windows.

Vijay Parshad, The Tricontinental

Last Week in SoftBank

SoftBank has pulled out of a $3 billion tender offer it made for WeWork shares.

Why?

It says because “closing conditions were not met”.

I see.

So who loses the most from this? According to a press note issued by SoftBank, “Neumann [former CEO of WeWork], his family, and certain large institutional stockholders, such as Benchmark Capital, were the parties who stood to benefit most from the tender offer”.

I want to say that everyone got what they deserved, but I’m really not sure who deserves what anymore.

Tell me what you think.

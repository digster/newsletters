Good Morning Ishan,
It was the late 1990s and it was a veritable cornucopia in many Bengaluru colleges.
Mobile telephony had launched just a couple of years ago and operators were eager to sign up new subscribers. Almost anyone could get a new “postpaid” mobile connection with barely legible and often clearly forged identity and address proofs. The agents selling the connections didn’t care. The operators too hadn’t wisened up. People rang up calling bills reaching into tens of thousands of rupees, and when the bills became due for payment, just dumped the SIM cards and got themselves new ones. Names and addresses were changed as often as needed.
It would be a while till the operators realised what was going on (or perhaps they did, but were interested in racking up the number of “subscribers” before regulators and investors) and clamped down.
Two decades later (sigh, it’s almost embarrassing to recall how long it’s been since I was in college) there’s another cornucopia brewing in India’s colleges—instant online credit.
A student ID card and Aadhaar card gets you an instant loan ranging from Rs 3,000-6,000. Repayment tenures range from 3-12 months. Depending on the repayment record, more credit becomes available, now between Rs 10,000-60,000. Parents aren’t in the loop until predefined limits are hit. The “staff” responsible for scoping new borrowers, verifying them or chasing delinquents are also students.
The “fintech” startups that are lending to students are seeing *thousands of applications every day*.
It’s both an interesting as well as terrifying trend. On the one hand, there are over 30 million college students across India. All adults, all underserved by traditional credit. On the other, well, they’re hormonal, instant-gratification-driven college students. How great do you think their financial literacy is?
Let Ashish take you down this rabbit hole. He’s spent weeks chronicling this megatrend from the ground up, talking to students and lenders. Trust me, you won’t be disappointed.

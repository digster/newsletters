I. Stop feeling guilty

Guilt is a powerful emotion. It leads us to dark and narrow alleys in our brain out of which there seem to be no ways out. Except just one. 

 

Think of the number of times you ended up doing something simply because you felt you “owed” it to someone. Most of us would be able to think of examples from just a few days or weeks ago. Which is why, if I were to list down the top three factors behind poor decisions, guilt would be the first to enter the list. 

 

The second would be our discomfort at saying “no”.

 

When was the last time you replied with just the word “no” to a request from a colleague, family member, friend, or even a stranger? Chances are, unless they were asking you for something trivial or factual (“do you want sugar in your tea?” or “will you be in Bengaluru next week?”), we rarely say just “no”.

 

Instead, we elaborate and explain. “I have this family engagement.” “I would love to, but I’m hard pressed right now.” Or “can I take a rain check for now, but do this some time else?”

 

Our discomfort at saying just “no”, without having to explain ourselves, comes back to guilt too. What if the person who’s asking us for something feels bad at our response? How can we soften it for them?

 

Kunal Shah, the founder and CEO of CRED, thinks it's because we Indians take rejection personally.

Kunal Shah 

 

Let me ask this question very differently. You will see venture capitalists or angel investors find it very hard to say no to a founder versus ghost them. Okay. That’s a very interesting point to think about. Explicit no, as a society, we can’t take.

 

Rohin Dharmakumar 

 

We like to keep it hanging.

No, no, They would happily do that.

It’s rude.

 

Kunal Shah 

 

But I have seen founders who get abusive with you and like, like, make it personal, right? Because you said no. So what happens is…

 

Rohin Dharmakumar 

 

We take rejection personally.

 

Kunal Shah 

 

We take rejections personally, not realising that entrepreneurship is everyday rejection.

 

Like non-stop. It’s not going to end, right? So the thing is that we tend to not appreciate that. So as a society, what did we do? This is, let’s stay in touch. We like this, but maybe it’s too early.

And in this manner, we invert the dynamic of anxiety from the person making the request to the person receiving it. When did we transfer the power over our time, resources, and energies to others? 

 

It took me decades to observe myself doing this. And I’m still working on learning to just say “no”.

 

Saying “no” is important because decision-making is as much about declining to do things as it is about agreeing to do them. In fact, as one progresses through their lives and careers, deciding what you are not going to do becomes more important than what you will. Because we get better as we do the things we love.  

 

Recently, Lalit Keshre, the co-founder and CEO of Groww, told me how he had learned to overcome guilt in a very different domain—reading books. And in a very interesting way.

Lalit Keshre

 

The discovery process itself is very… I enjoy that process also. Going to reviews, looking at… somebody recommends something and then I will read about the book about the author. There’s a very, very big top of the funnel…I’ll start reading maybe 30 books in a month and then probably just drop them. Like the first chapter, second chapter and then there’ll be some books… maybe I’ll finish four books in a month.

 

Rohin Dharmakumar

 

How long did it take you to accept that it’s okay to leave a book after the first chapter or second chapter?

 

Lalit Keshre

 

So that is… I’m lucky. I left that guilt.  When I used to read physical books there was a lot of guilt, but I moved to Kindle long back. Uske baad toh koi guilt nahi hai (there is no guilt after that).

Accepting that it's okay to abandon a book after reading a chapter or two is a relatively newer habit for me. But it’s one that I am comfortable with now. And while I do love Lalit’s discovery that guilt is easier to leave digital or Kindle books unread, I still do love reading and owning physical books.


A useful mental model to avoid getting cornered by guilt or the discomfort of saying no appears in entrepreneur Derek Sivers’ book, which a friend sent to me earlier this week. The “Hell yeah!” rule?

Use this rule if you’re often over-committed or too scattered.

 

If you’re not saying “HELL YEAH!” about something, say no.

 

When deciding whether to do something, if you feel anything less than “Wow! That would be amazing! Absolutely! Hell yeah!” — then say no.

 

When you say no to most things, you leave room in your life to really throw yourself completely into that rare thing that makes you say “HELL YEAH!”

 

Every event you get invited to. Every request to start a new project. If you’re not saying “HELL YEAH!” about it, say no.

 

We’re all busy. We’ve all taken on too much. Saying yes to less is the way out.
Anything You Want by Derek Sivers

I remembered a variant of Sivers’ advice that I’d come across. It was from Kevin Kelly, the co-founder of Wired magazine. We had also reviewed his book in Edition 13 of the First Principles newsletter—Don’t fill categories (or checklists).

When you get invited to do something in the future, ask yourself: “Would I do this tomorrow?” Not too many promises will pass that immediacy filter.” 

 

Excellent Advice for Living: Wisdom I wish I’d known earlier by Kevin Kelly

Remember that the next time someone requests your time. Would you say, “Hell yeah!”? Would you want to do it tomorrow? And if not, do you want to feel guilty about politely declining the request?

 

Once we’re able to remove guilt from our decision-making, things become much clearer. The projects we decide to work on; the meetings we want to attend; the people we want to spend time with; and of course, the books we want to read.

II. Books

The first book recommendation this week comes from Praveen Krishnan, my colleague at The Ken and also the author of our Saturday newsletter, The Nutgraf. He read The Creative Act during a two-week vacation trekking through the Langtang Valley in Nepal. (The country has suffered two major earthquakes this weekend, taking over 157 lives. Our hearts go out to the victims, and if any of you have family or friends in the area, we hope they are well and safe.)

“The Creative Act is a book that does something special and elusive i.e it gives an all-encompassing answer to a question that some of greatest artists often get from the rest of us — “How did you come up with this?”

 

Written by Rick Rubin, a nine-time Grammy-winning music producer, whom Rolling Stone called ‘The most successful producer in any genre’, The Creative Act has been hailed by people such as Seth Godin, Jony Ive and JJ Abrams as a mandatory reading of the creative process. Rubin has collaborated with artists from Adele, Red Hot Chili Peppers, Beastie Boys Slayer, Johnny Cash, Kanye West, System of a Down to Jay-Z. And he brings his rich experience of observing and fostering creativity across decades into this book.

 

This is not a book about frameworks, hacks or methods to boost creativity. For most people, this book may even come across as hand-wavy, metaphysical and theoretical. Instead, it’s a book about how artists and the world itself. It’s about how creativity isn’t a process, but simply a way of life. On the Kindle, where I read the book, one of the popular highlights is this: “We tend to think of the artist’s work as the output. The real work of the artist is a way of being in the world.””

The second recommendation is from me. I don’t think I’ve read a jaw-droppingly more interesting, reported, and ridiculous non-fiction book than Unscripted: The Epic Battle for a Media Empire and the Redstone Family Legacy.

Written by two incredibly talented journalists—both Pulitzer winners—the book is a roller coaster ride through the (mostly final) life and times of one of the last remaining Hollywood media moguls, Sumner Redstone. Filled with first-person accounts ranging from the bedroom to the boardroom, it feels like you’re a fly on the wall to one of the most vulgar, ambitious, and cut-throat struggles for power and fame. The fictitious HBO drama Succession feels tame in front of this book and its reality. 

 

As a journalist, I could only marvel at the unimaginable levels of access, sourcing, and fact-checking that would have gone into the writing and publishing of this book. Every other page felt like a lawsuit waiting to happen. 

 

A common complaint in people’s reviews of the book is that the authors haven’t woven in discussions around ethics, business strategies, or lessons to be learned. But that would be a different story. This book is a tour de force of non-fictional and pseudo-biographic storytelling. Read it if you want to know about the deeply flawed and yet incredibly successful man that was Sumner Redstone, and the interesting retinue of family, friends, and colleagues he had around him.

IV. Habits

It's tough to be a fan of mental models and First Principles without also being one of Habits. Because, if mental models are the theory, then habits are the application.

“Habit is far more dependable than inspiration. Make progress by making habits. Don’t focus on getting into shape. Focus on becoming the kind of person who never misses a workout.” 

 

Excellent Advice for Living: Wisdom I wish I’d known earlier by Kevin Kelly

Every single founder and leader I’ve met is constantly interrogating and evolving their habits, whether at work, home, or play. And based on the emails and conversations I’ve had with subscribers, it feels like habits is a theme a lot of us are interested in and curious about. 

 

So, I decided to experiment with a temporary section of habits in our newsletter. I hope to introduce ideas, discussions, and criticisms of habits through this each week. Please write to fp@the-ken.com if you have changed your mind about any habits, old or new. Tell us why, what you learned, and what others can take away from your experience. 

 

Here are a few relatively newer habits I’ve started cultivating, primarily to increase the number of books I read. 

 
  • Concurrency: Instead of reading one book at a time, I’ve started reading two to three. I’ve realized that it provides me with more variety and less boredom than earlier.
  • Convenience: In order to make it easier to read multiple books simultaneously, I’ve started placing them in different parts of my house. So that there is always a book around when I want to read. Especially near bathrooms. 😀
  • Beauty: In order to make it easier to not just pick a book that’s lying nearby, but also to the page I left it at, I bought a bunch of lovely and colourful Mandala-themed bookmarks. Now, an unread book practically looks even more appealing.
  • Variety: And lastly, I’m consciously trying to read different genres at the same time. Fiction versus non-fiction. Business versus parenting. Self-help versus organisational culture. I’ve found this challenges my brain more and prevents me from switching to “default” mode.

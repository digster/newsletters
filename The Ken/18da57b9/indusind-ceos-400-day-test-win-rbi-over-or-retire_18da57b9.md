Good Morning Ishan,
Earlier this week, a statement from Amitabh Chaudhry, the chief executive of Axis Bank, the country’s third largest lender, piqued my interest. He said if the regulator permits, Axis would be open to working with the beleaguered Paytm*, since it was an important player in the fintech industry.
And why not? It’s a shot at getting Paytm’s merchant payment volumes worth $44 billion.
HDFC Bank’s top executive, too, expressed an interest. But if any bank gets dibs on Paytm, it should be Axis Bank, in my opinion. It’s the only bank that has a mini-Paytm in it, thanks to its acquisition of Freecharge. Remember Freecharge? The one that fintech entrepreneur Kunal Shah sold in 2015 to Snapdeal for Rs 2,400 crore (US$289 million). Two years later, the e-commerce company then sold it in a fire sale to the bank for Rs 385 crore (US$46 million).
At first glance, it seems like the bank messed up the acquisition. When it acquired Freecharge, it had 61.5 million users in 2017-2018. And that’s now down to just 1.4 million users, according to the bank’s annual reports.
But interestingly, with far fewer users, the fintech is processing much more—the value of payment transactions on Freecharge was Rs 20,000 crore (US$2.4 billion) last year, 10X more than what it was in 2017. It’s also making a tidy sum while at it—with Rs 400 crore (US$48 million) in revenue and Rs 40 crore (US$4.8 million) in profit in 2023. That’s quite the turnaround from the Rs 23-crore (US$2.7 million) revenue and Rs 81-crore (US$9.7 million) loss in 2017-18.
Sure, it took the company six years to get here. But given that acquisitions usually go nowhere, Axis Bank has managed a rare feat of extracting value out of this buy.
In today’s [story](https://the-ken.com/story/indusind-ceos-400-day-test-win-rbi-over-or-retire/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter), we look at how another bank and its CEO have to demonstrate the latter’s value to the regulator; and he has 400 days to do it.
Sumant Kathpalia or SK, as he is widely known, is the chief executive officer and managing director of Indusind, India’s sixth largest lender by market capitalisation. In his three years as the bank’s chief, he proved his mettle by getting the bank’s profits to go up by 350%. But the RBI is a tough crowd to impress. Kathpalia only got a two-year extension to his term, instead of three.
Rounak writes on the tasks ahead of Kathpalia in his remaining tenure. And if he has what it takes to put the bank among India’s top five, and win the RBI’s approval to do that.
*Paytm’s founder Vijay Shekhar Sharma is an investor in The Ken

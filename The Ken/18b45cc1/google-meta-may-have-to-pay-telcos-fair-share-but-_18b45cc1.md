Good Morning Ishan,
It’s almost like telcos in India were waiting for this moment.
On 7 July, the country’s telecom regulator, Trai, issued a consultation paper examining the need for a collaborative framework between telcos and OTTs, which includes addressing regulatory mechanisms and selective bans.
Seizing this chance, the telcos started building a case to get Big Tech to pay a network-usage fee. Telcos want the likes of Google and Meta, which use maximum network, to pay “fair” charges.
Not only does this allegedly nullify the principles of net neutrality—hard-won digital freedom, but it is also a curious demand given that it is this traffic that brings in most revenues for the telcos.
What telcos have going for them is precedent. In 2020, South Korea introduced a “Netflix law” mandating content companies with over 1 million daily users and 1% network traffic to engage with telcos and mutually decide on a usage charge.
If this suggestion turns into a law, internet giants’ India arms could potentially be shelling out millions of dollars.
This is going to be a protracted campaign that will play out over the coming months, and the government will likely make a move only after the 2024 General Elections, as per all the insights gathered by Pratap, the author of the story.

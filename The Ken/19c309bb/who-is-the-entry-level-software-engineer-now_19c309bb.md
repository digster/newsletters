An abridged, narrative version of the latest episode of Two by Two, The Ken’s premium weekly business podcast.
Good Morning Ishan,
Anthropic just announced that 100% of the code they write is now AI-generated.
Soon after, Kailash Nadh, CTO of Zerodha*, dropped a [blog](https://nadh.in/blog/code-is-cheap/) post titled “Code is Cheap, Show Me the Talk”, arguing that an engineer’s real value now lies in thinking and critically evaluating code instead of writing it line-by-line. This is the same Kailash who, just last year at The Ken event, said he’d never ask AI to write code for his team.
Something big has changed in the world of software engineering very fast.
This week on Two by Two, Praveen Gopal Krishanan and Rahel Philipose sat down with two experts who have very different takes on what’s happening to software engineers—[Arnav Gupta](https://www.linkedin.com/in/arnavgupta/?skipRedirect=true), a third-time guest and currently the head of engineering at Meta, and [Abhay Saraf](https://www.linkedin.com/in/abhay-saraf-5ba9a670/), founder of Bushel Technologies, a Nagpur-based software development company.
Throughout the episode, they debated and disagreed violently on the very fundamentals of this topic.
Arnav thinks the entry-level step of the ladder is disappearing. The jobs where you just translate technical specs into code, those are gone.
Arnav Gupta: Companies will shrink that headcount. All the large companies employing more than 1,00,000 engineers have been shedding headcount like anything for the last four years.
Abhay Saraf: Just because we invented calculators, we did not stop teaching multiplication and division in school. You have to teach the basics.
Arnav breaks down what a software engineer needs into four skills.
- Desire: Translating a vague human need into a product
- Trade-offs: Deciding which tool or approach is best
- System knowledge: Understanding how a computer actually works
- Syntax: Learning how to write code
These four skills map to seniority, for example, the CEO figures out what people desire, product managers handle trade-offs, senior engineers understand how computers work, and entry-level engineers handle syntax.
The issue arises because LLMs can do syntax now.
Abhay calls it the K-shaped career path, where one path, because of AI, benefits highly trained people, and another, where careers might end for the vast majority of developers.
Abhay: Most people become software engineers because it’s a way to make money and then do what they really want to do. The real world is very, very slow in adopting things.
Abhay says engineering should become a six-year or ten-year college program, as you can’t compress this knowledge anymore.
Arnav says the opposite and believes two years is enough. The curious ones will figure out the rest, while others will exit.
Arnav: When knowledge compression happens, you have to compress the teaching also. You can’t make people grind on fundamentals for long. Sounds very cruel, but capitalistic pressure will make this happen.
So what do you actually tell students?
Abhay: Learn typing—touch typing, 60 words per minute. When typing becomes automatic, your brain is free to think about the actual problem.
Arnav: Stop using ChatGPT like a consumer. Open OpenAI docs, make API requests, build an agent. If you want to be in the tech industry, go beyond using it as a power user. Actually, open the hood and look at how the engine is running.
But the real tension in the episode is around hiring. Multiple CTOs told Rahel they’ve stopped hiring freshers entirely. One fintech firm said 80% of certain teams used to be freshers, and now it is down to zero.
The episode goes into how both of them actually hire today and what they test for. Arnav talks about letting people use AI during interviews and watching how they prompt it, and how they fix bad code. Abhay talks about people using ChatGPT during remote interviews and getting caught because they can’t explain a single line.
There’s also this fascinating bit about art versus craft, which is Abhay’s framework for everything. The craft of coding is getting easier. But the art, the thinking about how systems should work, that’s not going away.
Abhay: The principal engineer was always a prompt engineer. His coders are just no longer carbon-based humans, but silicon-based agents.
Praveen rebuts, saying that the principal engineer got there by being carbon-based. So how do you get there now if the bottom rung is gone?

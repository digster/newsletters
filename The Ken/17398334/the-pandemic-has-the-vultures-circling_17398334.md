Vultures are often viewed as lowly scavengers but they play a crucial role in the balance of nature. The same is true in finance.
In the Philippines, Congress is [cooking up](https://business.inquirer.net/297882/bsp-proposed-fist-law-to-buttress-ph-banks-even-if-bad-loans-double) a meal… err, a plan that’s sure to attract a lot of [vultures](https://www.wsj.com/articles/vulture-investors-swoop-into-china-to-feast-on-soured-loans-11579602603)—the creation of special purpose vehicles (SPVs) that will acquire distressed assets from banks.
With the pandemic crippling businesses and slashing jobs, bad loans have piled up. Local banks’ current bad loan ratio—[2.3% as of April](https://www.pna.gov.ph/articles/1106386)—remains way lower than the [18.7% peak](https://www.philstar.com/business/2020/06/21/2022302/no-repeat-massive-loan-defaults-seen) seen during the 1997 Asian financial crisis. But the Philippines thinks it’s best to have a backup plan.
The proposed Financial Institutions Strategic Transfer law will allow banks to sell their bad assets to SPVs, or vulture investors (private or state-owned), as they’re generally known as. It will also allow them to sweeten the deals with fiscal incentives, including exemptions from payment of certain taxes like the documentary stamp tax and capital gains tax.
Just like the bird they’re named after, vulture investors swoop in at the last minute to take advantage of an ugly situation.
Here’s how it works. Investors will buy the bad debt—many secured by physical assets like houses, land, warehouses, and commercial properties—at deep discounts and profit from it via various means. They can choose to work out a deal with borrowers, forgiving a portion of the debt while recouping the rest of the payout. Or sell the real estate or resell the defaulted loans to other vulture investors.
In return, banks recover some of their money, freeing capital for new lending. Which, at this time, is crucial to avoid a severe collapse of the economy.
It’s not just in the Philippines; vultures are also circling around coronavirus carnage in markets like [China](https://www.reuters.com/article/us-health-coronavirus-china-badloans/coronavirus-thwarts-vulture-funds-efforts-to-finally-crack-china-idUSKCN2260CX), [Italy](https://www.wsj.com/articles/coronavirus-hits-europes-bad-loan-securitization-market-11591090201), [Venezuela](https://www.nytimes.com/2017/11/14/business/venezuela-debt-investors.html).

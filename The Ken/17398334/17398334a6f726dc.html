Good morning.

I’m sort of sick of saying data is the new oil. But wouldn’t you believe it, seeing as:
 
  • GAFA—the big four comprising Google, Apple, Facebook, Amazon—has been summoned, yet again, to untangle their unintelligible business models in front of the US Congress.
  • Facebook has decided to turn the data tables on the European Union (EU). 
  • The Indian government, now on high alert for foreign influence, is asking companies to reveal trade secrets. 
  • Garmin’s massive data breach might just spawn new spends for tech companies.
  • Data from sero-surveys in Delhi and Mumbai could hold some good news after all.

But first, vultures are circling the Philippines. They aren’t the flesh-eating kind. But on second thought, did someone say… subprime crisis?
The pandemic has the vultures circling
 
Jum
 
Vultures are often viewed as lowly scavengers but they play a crucial role in the balance of nature. The same is true in finance.
 
In the Philippines, Congress is cooking up a meal… err, a plan that’s sure to attract a lot of vultures—the creation of special purpose vehicles (SPVs) that will acquire distressed assets from banks.
 
With the pandemic crippling businesses and slashing jobs, bad loans have piled up. Local banks’ current bad loan ratio—2.3% as of April—remains way lower than the 18.7% peak seen during the 1997 Asian financial crisis. But the Philippines thinks it’s best to have a backup plan.
 
The proposed Financial Institutions Strategic Transfer law will allow banks to sell their bad assets to SPVs, or vulture investors (private or state-owned), as they’re generally known as. It will also allow them to sweeten the deals with fiscal incentives, including exemptions from payment of certain taxes like the documentary stamp tax and capital gains tax.
 
Just like the bird they’re named after, vulture investors swoop in at the last minute to take advantage of an ugly situation.
 
Here’s how it works. Investors will buy the bad debt—many secured by physical assets like houses, land, warehouses, and commercial properties—at deep discounts and profit from it via various means. They can choose to work out a deal with borrowers, forgiving a portion of the debt while recouping the rest of the payout. Or sell the real estate or resell the defaulted loans to other vulture investors. 
 
In return, banks recover some of their money, freeing capital for new lending. Which, at this time, is crucial to avoid a severe collapse of the economy.
 
It’s not just in the Philippines; vultures are also circling around coronavirus carnage in markets like China, Italy, Venezuela.
Garmin and the new post-breach reality
 
Rohin
 
With an annual revenue of $3.7 billion, Garmin was one of the world’s largest makers of GPS devices and connected wearables like fitness bands and smartwatches. Its GPS technology has been around for so long that it powers aeroplane navigation and landing systems.

Last Thursday, virtually everything Garmin did went down. 

Its GPS services went offline. Its smart devices went dumb. Even its call centres went down.

Because hackers had breached Garmin’s security and encrypted all of its data within its own networks.

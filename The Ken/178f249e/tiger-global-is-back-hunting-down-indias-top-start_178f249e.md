“Tigers will tentatively stalk their future meal until they’re within six to nine metres away. Once in range, tigers target their prey’s neck, which severs its spinal cord. Larger meals may also require a fatal bite to the throat to drag them to the ground… Once the struggle is over, tigers will move their catch under cover to enjoy in peace without interference from scavengers.”
This is a description of how tigers hunt from Howitworks.com—a website my science-loving kids have spent much of the pandemic reading. We don’t publish wildlife stories at The Ken, but the above very much applies to Tiger Global, the New York-based hedge fund that famously bet big—and then won big—on Flipkart.
After a quiet period in which its leader and driving force Lee Fixel left to start his own fund, the firm is back. That's a return that will delight founders and strike fear into other venture capital firms since Tiger lives up to its name:
- It moves fast: deals are often closed after one Zoom call with a founder
- It bites big: its cheques are in the hundreds of millions
- It eats in peace: it gives startups high valuations that put off price-sensitive investors
- Hunting season is here: Tiger has backed seven of the eleven new unicorns born in India this year
There's much more to come.
Tiger has closed a dozen deals in 2021 to date, with a dozen more reportedly coming. Indeed, while I worked on this email, news broke of its investment in fintech startup Chargebee at a US$1 billion-plus ‘unicorn’ valuation, of course.
Like a tiger, there’s vision in how the firm hunts:
“Tiger, in contrast, doesn’t bet on companies. It doesn’t even bet on markets. It bets on trends or megatrends that it knows for sure will come to fruition in some future version of the world. For instance, Tiger did not bet on Flipkart or even on e-commerce in India. Instead, it made a bet on the consumption patterns and retail aspirations of the Indian middle class,” Sumanth writes in today’s story.

If you’ve ever enjoyed playing chess, you know the importance of measuring every move in conjunction with how your opponent’s pieces are placed on the board.
In a 2002 episode of The West Wing—a political drama series set in the United States—President Josiah Bartlet engages in two concurrent chess games with his aides while negotiating with a sabre-rattling Chinese state threatening to turn war games near Taiwan into a full-scale war (sounds pretty familiar, doesn’t it?)
As Bartlet puts navy ships en route to the Taiwan strait, a visibly distressed aide asks him why he would risk a move that could put two superpowers at war.
“Are they in the Taiwan strait?” asks Bartlet.
“They’re on their way,” the aide replies. “Is that the same thing?” remarks Bartlet.
Some swift diplomatic manoeuvring combined with the threat of US involvement ultimately defuses the crises and also delivers a lesson that our team at The Ken holds close to our hearts: the importance of looking at the whole board and not just segments of it.
That pursuit was front and centre in the stories we wrote this week. They looked beyond the noise surrounding business moves and instead, distilled it all to tell us what was really going on.
Olina teamed up with contributor Jugal Wadhwani to write about the
[evolving angel investing landscape](https://the-ken.com/story/new-rules-engagement-india-angels/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter) in India. In 2021, angel investors poured around US$100 million into the Indian startup ecosystem, turning them into somewhat of a safety net for entrepreneurs who otherwise would be at the mercy of venture capital groups.
Even though
[sustainability](https://the-ken.com/story/everyone-wants-a-sustainability-leader/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter) is a major buzzword these days, there aren’t enough sustainability executives to guide companies in their pursuit of being more environment-friendly. As Shruti details in a sharp story, effectively pursuing sustainability will require paradigm changes that go beyond the band-aid approach taken by firms so far.
Inspired by the success of India’s homegrown payments system, UPI, the country now wants to replicate the model for healthcare by minting health IDs under a system called United Health Interface (UHI). But, as Maitri points out in her deeply reported piece, bettering health care won’t just work by creating IDs. Rather, it will depend on the much taller order of bringing out-of-the-fold doctors, hospitals, and clinics on board the mammoth exercise. This was our free story of the week, you can read it
[here](https://the-ken.com/story/govts-upi-for-health-plan-faces-tech-consent-hurdles/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter).
The public market debut of India’s largest insurer Life Insurance Corporation of India is perhaps one of the most anticipated market events of this year—it’s also India’s largest asset manager. As Seetharaman and Anand point out in their story, LIC’s assets under management outstrip India’s entire mutual fund industry by 8%. And while this is
[LIC’s magic potion](https://the-ken.com/story/ipo-bound-lics-130b-equity-holdings-are-its-magic-potion-and-its-achilles-heel/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter), it’s also, in a way, the state-owned firm’s Achilles’ heel.
[Twitter India](https://the-ken.com/story/twitters-bold-plan-to-make-india-its-growthengine/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter) has been under the spotlight for multiple reasons over the last year or so, but what’s lesser known is how central India is to Twitter’s success in the future. Facing concentration in markets like the US and Japan, Twitter has a keen desire to grow in the country. But, as Pratap writes, its revenues from the country have pretty much flat-lined. With its future dependent on success in the country, Twitter can’t really afford to take its eye off the ball in India as it has in the past.

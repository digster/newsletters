Good Morning Ishan,
Last Saturday, I found myself in a conflict-of-interest situation.
Full disclosure: I am a bargain shopping addict. This time I had my eye on a juicer—cold press, whole slow with guaranteed 10% extra juice. But the spouse would have none of it.
For the last decade, we have used variants of the same old hand-pressed aluminium juicer. He spends oodles of time and effort churning out one glass but won’t replace it. “No automated machine can give that taste,” he argues.
The same man versus machine debate.
Why are we so resistant to technology and why does it make some of us uncomfortable? Does it really improve our lives or complicate it at times?
Now, that’s the same argument I saw laid out in Nathan’s draft: on traditional education consultants battling it out with study-abroad startups. The former is backed by solid experience, and the latter, armed with technology and generative AI.
Education consultant Viral Doshi, a Cornell University alumnus, claims to have counselled 15,000 students in the past 19 years. But he doesn’t have a website, nor does he use Whatsapp. His only digital presence is an email address.
Meanwhile, Leverage Edu, a six-year-old startup that claims to have sent close to 80,000 students abroad in 2022 alone. It uses generative AI tools to not just match students with their colleges of choice but also offers an array of services – loans to forex to accomodation.
But what the education consultants miss out on in numbers, they recoup in moolah. Doshi charges up to Rs 6 lakh (US$7,223) for a three-month consultation process. Meanwhile, startups’ average cost is education consultants’ lowest price.
Naturally, not everyone can afford the price of a consultant. As Red Pen’s sales and marketing head, Kavita Sonawala, put it: “We cater to the Belgian-chocolate-eating audience and not those who eat Cadbury milk chocolate.”
Amid man versus machine, niche audiences versus mass markets, who really does own the future?

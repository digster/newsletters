Let me start off with a confession. I have never played any kind of fantasy sport in my life.
Don't get me wrong. I have no problem with sports. Take cricket, for instance. Watch it all the time. Even Test matches. And then regret it five days later. It's the fantasy part that eludes me. I am not sure why. Perhaps it's because fantasy sport adds a layer of competition while I am watching a contest that's already in progress. It's one carefully-constructed, make-believe thing upon another...carefully-constructed make-believe thing, and that can be disconcerting. That's also why I don't watch TV news anymore.
Then I read Sidhartha's story. His story was about a company named Dream11, India's largest fantasy sports platform. In 2016, Dream11 had 2 million users. Two years later, it now has 20 million. It's not just users. Even their revenues increased from Rs 82 lakh to Rs 61 crore in two years. That's 75X. In two years. And it holds 90% of the market share today. Rumours are that now Tencent, the Chinese conglomerate giant, is looking to invest.
Suddenly I was intrigued. I was always under the impression that fantasy sport was one of those things that would thrive in the West but would never take-off in India. Like Golf, mayonnaise or Priyanka Chopra's career. Clearly, I was wrong.
The success of Dream11 appears even more incredible if you consider that gambling laws are quite strict in India, and a fantasy sport app flirts dangerously with the legal definition of whether it's a game of skill or luck. There's more. It's not listed on the Google Play Store. Imagine that. Twenty million users. It really begs the questions: How on earth did they do it? Who plays this? Is it sustainable? What risks lie ahead? Is it even in the larger public interest?
Today's story tries to answer all these questions. Partly through robust, deep-reporting and analysis which we do every day. Partly through something called the 'fish and shark funda'.
What's that?

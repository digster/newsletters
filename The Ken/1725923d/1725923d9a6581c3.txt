Comic book aficionados will recognise that as a quote by the character Dr Octopus in one of the Spider-Man films, when he holds aloft a newly-created mini fusion reactor, pulsing with power.

India's next big step in its clean energy ambitions is somewhat similar: storing solar and wind energy to be used when required.

India has made great strides in renewable energy. Its installed capacity has nearly tripled over the past five years to top 87 gigawatts (GW); the target is an ambitious 275 GW by 2027. That makes the fate of a couple of projects awarded in this year incredibly crucial as a testing ground for energy storage.

First off, Goldman Sachs-backed ReNew Power, India's largest clean-energy producer, bagged a 300 megawatts (MW) wind-and-solar project in January. That project specifies the need for energy storage to meet peak demand every morning and evening. And ReNew has chosen to store power in lithium-ion batteries. Yes, the same ones that power electric vehicles.

On the other hand, Greenko Energy, funded by Singapore's sovereign wealth fund GIC, won a 900 MW project in January. Greenko uses PSH, or pumped-storage hydropower, to store power. PSH is the dominant electricity storage tech globally. But has its drawbacks, like high capital costs and topographical challenges.

The sharp drop in the average price of lithium-ion batteries—about 87% between 2010 and 2019—seemingly gives ReNew the edge over Greenko in the race for the most viable form of energy storage. But then, India's "vocal for local" campaign could mean higher import duties on Li-ion cells. Then what?

And that's without accounting for the fickleness of state governments (in India, electricity is a state responsibility, not a central one), which try to renegotiate contracts with older projects to buy power at lower prices. That's a massive risk for ReNew, which has nearly 60% of its capacity tied up in state-owned utilities.

Storage ambitions are all well and good, but is it commercially viable for developers like ReNew and Greenko, and the debt-ridden utilities that buy power from them?

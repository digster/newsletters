New acquisitions, old tactics. The rumors about Unacademy’s buyout follows a familiar tune
By Olina Banerji
6 min read
View in browser

Good Morning Ishan,

You are reading our subscriber-only newsletter Ed Set Go—a weekly commentary on Indian edtech and education

Last week, I had a real sense of déjà vu. It was 2020 all over again. Edtech “giants” were head-to-head in the race to buy the newest, hottest startup in the space. Founders were getting generous buyouts. The long game was that Byju’s would acquire you, and your invention.

Five years later, in a sector I barely recognize anymore, the attempts to acquire continue. Except that some of the players are different. More importantly, the power dynamics have changed.

All this started, of course, with the news that Allen Career Institute is eyeing a US$800 million takeover of Unacademy. The very same Allen that low-key threatened Unacademy when the latter allegedly lured tutors away from the offline test prep giant. Whatever hurt that offline test-prep companies like Allen took during the Covid drought has subsided. Allen has come back.

The hunters have become the hunted.

Power dynamics aside, though, the “news break” about the potential acquisition follows a pattern well-established in edtech.

Cue the tug-of-wills

Rumors of an Unacademy buyout have been afloat a while. First, there was Byju’s, when it had the capacity and balance sheet to absorb a rival business that had the execution chops that it, frankly, lacked.

Curious things happened, allegedly, when Byju’s had their eyes on a business. Sources have told me Byju’s would arrive at a price they wanted to pay for a company, and many times, that valuation would eventually make it into media discussions about a potential buyout. Like what happened around the time Byju’s was trying to buy doubt-solving edtech Doubtnut:

Here’s an excerpt from a piece I wrote in 2020:

For a company like Doubtnut, say two sources The Ken spoke with, Byju’s would have used a similar service—like China’s Zuoyebang—to benchmark Doubtnut’s value. Zuoyebang, a homework app, also began as a doubt solving product and had racked up 50 million registered users in 2015, a year into its existence. Currently, it has over 170 million monthly active users. However, Zuoyebang is part of Chinese tech giant Baidu and was initially grown within the company before being hived off.

“Byju’s would have benchmarked response time by apps like Zuoyebang. Byju’s would’ve also tracked how quickly Doubtnut was growing in comparison to it, and then offered up a value,” says the source mentioned above.

Byju’s aggressive push to acquire Doubtnut didn’t pay off, eventually. It’s believed that the price Byju’s set for the business—between US$125 million and US$150 million—wasn’t enough to convince Doubtnut’s founders that they needed to sell. Doubtnut, of course, did eventually sell for a fraction of that valuation to Allen.

If Allen’s playing the same game that Byju’s allegedly did, of setting a public price for their targeted company, they’ve given Unacademy’s valuation a sizeable haircut—from US$3.4 billion in 2021 to US$800 million now.

But Unacademy isn’t one to acquiesce easily. At the helm is an audacious Gaurav Munjal, who’s managed to stay somewhat relevant to edtech even as his peers of the 2020 boom have either folded or are facing uphill battles in court. 

Munjal has rubbished the acquisition rumours on social media platform X. He says Unacademy has sufficient runway, that both Airlearn and Graphy, two of its non-tutoring businesses, are doing well, and that it’s going to be Unacademy’s “best year ever”. There’s also the declaration that its offline coaching center business grew by 30%, with a “significant” improvement in its unit economics.

With these business metrics out in the open, Munjal could be doing two things—one, he may be signaling to Allen that Unacademy’s assets are worth more than the price tag put on it. Or two, he’s signalling to other investors that Unacademy’s still a viable business to invest in.

What’s in it for Allen?

Allen has the opportunity to take out a rival business from the center-eat-center world of Kota’s coaching business. Overall, Allen too has struggled—with a 44% decline in profits in the last fiscal year even as revenues bounced back after Covid. In this situation, Allen doesn’t want to cede any money, tutors, or brand recall to Unacademy.

The question is, what would Allen do with the non-tutoring parts of Unacademy’s business. Products like Graphy and Airlean are where Munjal and Co., flex their innovation muscles. Graphy is a platform that primarily helps tutors launch their own websites, and Airlearn is a Duolingo competitor that’s focused on teaching people how to read and write languages like Spanish.

Allen’s interest, however, may be limited to the manpower of the physical coaching centers. Unacademy’s other hustles may be less appealing, and thus, less valuable. 

But Allen’s hands could be tied. It’s at that point of its investment cycle where its own investors may be pushing the test-prep giant to grow quickly.

I found this excerpt from an Entrackr story particularly telling:

“Allen’s PE deal in some ways marked the peak of the edtech boom, as the last of the large firms that had held out until then before taking the plunge. It is showing signs of the same, with pressure to spend their way to some sort of leadership, even at the cost of margins that the firm always had before the funding. The Unacademy deal, if it works out, will be yet another investor-backed deal no doubt, to beef up the balance sheet size. Will that really be the solution the firm is looking for to combat future risks? One has to wonder, considering just how fast the market is evolving, and the challenges of integrating such a firm within the Allen culture.”

The piece raises an important point about culture fit, which is obliquely connected to the issue of Graphy and Airlean, projects built to fill gaps in markets that Allen has never played in. Would Allen want to keep them going?

Now that I’ve spun a whole web out of well-placed rumors, and fallen for the exact strategy I laid out just a few paragraphs ago, I’d like to share more evidence that competition is alive and well in the edtech space. 

Scholars Den, a coaching edtech, has been restrained by the Delhi High Court from putting up any more hoardings that seemed to take a dig at its well-known competitor, Physicswallah. The marketing is funny, if too on the nose. The managing director of Scholars Den claims no Alakh Pandeys were hurt in the making!:

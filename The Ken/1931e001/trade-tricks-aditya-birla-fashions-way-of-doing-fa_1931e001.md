Trade Tricks
Tue, 12 Nov 24
A weekly newsletter about the biggest changes in commerce—focusing on shifts that matter to you..
Good Morning Ishan,
It’s Aakriti for Trade Tricks today!
Last week, Aditya Birla Fashion & Retail Ltd (ABFRL), one of India’s largest fashion retailers, reported [tepid growth](https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/markets/stocks/earnings/abfrl-q2-results-firm-posts-net-loss-of-rs-215-crore/articleshow/115074680.cms) for the September quarter. But what came a day later at its investor presentation was a fair bit more interesting—new details fleshing out its plans to demerge its legacy menswear brands.
I want to tell you a little story, however, before we get into those details.
A day after Aditya Birla Fashion’s announcement, I went to Chandni Chowk, Delhi’s biggest wholesale market, to do some shopping. And as a respite from all the tiresome browsing and bargaining, I decided to take a break and hog on some street food. On one side of the road was Natraj Dahi Bhalle Wala, which has just two things on its menu: dahi bhalle and aloo tikki. On the other side, opposite Natraj, was another chat corner (the name of which I don’t remember) offering every possible street food you could imagine. Chole bhature, Gol gappe, Palak chaat, Bhalle papdi… you name it, they had it.
Natraj is an eight-decade-old brand, but it sells a plate of dahi bhalle for Rs 70 (US$0.80). Its rival across the street sells the same for Rs 100. It’s wedding season now, so there was quite a large crowd at Chandni Chowk, but only one of those two shops had a ridiculously long queue in front of it. And it wasn’t the one offering everything under the sun.
The fashion retail empire of Kumar Mangalam Birla, though, seems to want to take the opposite tack.
ABFRL is positioning itself as a house of brands: it wants to sell everything from fast fashion (Style Up) to casual wear (Bewakoof, Wrogn), innerwear (Van Heusen) to shoes (Reebok).
In the [investor deck](https://www.bseindia.com/xml-data/corpfiling/AttachHis/115ec256-c32a-4221-8860-bf8ee85ffc7d.pdf) it published last week, the company said it wants to house its “legacy brands”—which it’s sold for nearly three decades—under one business unit, and demerge the rest, with higher total addressable market (TAM) and growth potential, into a separate arm.
The half of the business housing the legacy brands will be called Aditya Birla Lifestyle, with labels such as Louis Philippe, Reebok, Allen Solly, Peter England, and Van Heusen, each of which is a Rs 1,000 crore-a-year brand. This unit recorded sales of Rs 1,975 crore in the September quarter.
The other half of the business—the demerged Aditya Birla Fashion—will house new-age, high-growth brands in categories such as luxury, ethnic, digital-first, and value fashion, covering the likes of Sabysachi, House of Masaba, Wrogn, The Garage Company, Style Up, and of course, Pantaloons. This half recorded Rs 1,838 crore in sales in the same quarter.
| Source: Investor presentation, ABFRL |
The investor presentation cites Wazir Analysis to peg the TAM for the demerged business at Rs 7.6 lakh crore, i.e., bigger than the TAM for its legacy business Aditya Birla Lifestyle (formerly Madura), which is pegged at Rs 6.1 lakh crore.
But according to analysts that track the stock, the demerger does not appear to be a fundamentally positive move. Brokerage Kotak Securities, for instance, says the demerger by its own “does not create any new value, given challenges being faced by both ABLBL (weak growth) and ABFRL (losses)”.
Why demerge at all?
ABFRL has hardly seen much in the form of growth recently, especially compared to some of its rivals like Trent Ltd.
The legacy brands business has been seeing slowing growth over several quarters, including the latest one ended September, where sales grew by only 4%. According to Kotak Securities, the poor growth was due to a lack of wedding dates during the period and the resultant decline in the sale of higher-priced suits/shirts. And even though the entity housing the newer brands saw its sales jump 31% during the quarter, this was largely driven by its acquisition of Aurelia and W, the low base of the new businesses, and more store openings for brands like Tasva, an ethnic menswear label.
Trent, on the other hand has seen substantial growth in sales as well as stock price, spearheaded by Zudio, its fast fashion brand. Competitors like ABFRL’s Style Up have not shown nearly the same kind of traction, a second analyst tells me.
The value fashion category is a Rs 3.7 lakh crore market, and the competition can be palpable if you visit any mall, with fashion brands jostling for space on every floor. And when ABFRL first made the demerger announcement in April, my colleague Seetharaman [wrote](https://the-ken.com/story/whats-aditya-birla-fashion-without-brands-like-allen-solly-and-louis-philippe/) that it seemed to be driven by the retailer’s need to do something to show it still mattered.
As Trent ran circles around ABFRL with the frenetic expansion of Zudio, which inspired Style Up, investors began to lose whatever little patience they had with ABFRL. In each of the past four quarters, Trent grew its operating profits between 25% and 95%. ABFRL, on the other hand, posted a decline in all but one.
It was running out of moves to show it mattered, hence the spin-off. But cleaving Madura Fashion from itself means ABFRL is letting go of the one thing that has sustained it all these years. Madura is responsible for the majority of ABFRL’s cash flow, according to Nihal Jham, an analyst with Nuvama Institutional Equities, a broker. That has helped fund ABFRL’s acquisitions.
So, without Madura, ABFRL will look even less appealing than it does now.
To be fair, the demerger plans have helped woo back some of the lost investor interest. After all, according to the analyst I quoted above, the move would give them the option of holding “only Madura stock” (aka the legacy brands business) once it is demerged and listed—a unit that has constantly generated value.
Since the demerger announcement on 1 April, ABFRL stock has risen 40%—far higher than the 7.5% of the broader Nifty 50 index. But then, its rival Trent’s stock has moved up even further during the same period, by 70%.
The demerger is expected to be completed by the end of this financial year. But once that is done, Aditya Birla Fashion will have to raise capital to sustain itself. This is because a lot of its brands like TMRW (which houses digital-first brands) and Style Up (its fast fashion brand) are still not profitable. And analysts don’t expect these businesses to generate enough cash flow on their own for at least the next two years.
| Source: ABFRL |
In the past, the company raised funds from varied sources—it raised from the likes of Flipkart, it raised debt, it raised through qualified equity investors. Now, going forward, says the analyst cited above, raising from the equity markets “will be a hard thing to do”. It should also be noted that the company’s promoter group has decreased its shareholding from 56% two years ago to 49% as of September 2024.
“TCNS is hardly ABFRL’s sole unprofitable acquisition. In December 2022, ABFRL paid Rs 200 crore (US$24 million) for the direct-to-consumer brand Bewakoof, which is incurring losses, too.
…ABFRL doesn’t have the cash to fund its purchases all by itself. It borrowed Rs 750 crore (US$90 million) to finance the TCNS deal. Kotak Institutional Equities, a broker, estimates its net debt to have swelled by two-thirds in a year to roughly Rs 2,500 crore (US$300 million) by March. After the demerger, the “lion’s share” of this will be on ABFRL’s books.”
“The company has been known to have a very complicated structure, and the management believes that this will bring some method to the madness,” noted the analyst. “But in my view, the structure will become even more convoluted.”
I know selling street food is definitely not the same thing as selling fashion. But perhaps there’s a thing or two to be learnt from Natraj Dahi Bhalle Wala’s ‘do few things, but do them right’ playbook.

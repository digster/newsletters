[How Tata landed a Punch on Maruti and also buried Nano’s ghost](https://the-ken.com/story/tata-punchs-rise-buries-the-ghost-of-nano/?utm_source=weekly_email&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=weekly_recap_card)
Nano failed miserably. So, Tata Motors went back to the drawing board and came back with the safer, affordable, and feature-rich Nexon and Punch
Nano failed in the market despite being labelled the cheapest car. But Tata Motors found success in Punch by balancing affordability, desirability, and safety. Unlike how Maruti Suzuki launches multiple cars in the same category, Tata Motors launched Punch in a smaller and cheaper category than Nexon, ensuring minimal competition between the two. While Punch gained first-mover advantage, the company also kept upgrading its features as and when Hyundai’s Exter and Maruti’s Fronx upped the ante. With India’s automobile market growing more competitive and electrification on the rise, experts believe Punch’s success may be short-lived. [Summer is coming. For India’s AC makers, winter is coming](https://the-ken.com/story/summer-is-coming-for-indias-ac-makers-winter-is-coming/?utm_source=weekly_email&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=weekly_recap_card)
Demand is soaring, but supply is still mostly imported. Factories are running, but compressors are missing. And until the industry makes all the parts it needs, every summer will be a race against shortages
Scorching summers have pushed air conditioner demand to new heights, and with it, a supply crunch—cue the push for local manufacturing. But even with government initiatives like the PLI scheme, local manufacturing of key components like compressors is dragging, with over 60% still imported. The problem deepens as delays in BIS recertification for Chinese suppliers collide with a lack of tech partnerships, leaving Indian companies unable to produce compressors locally. Multinational giants have the upper hand, sourcing in-house components, while Indian firms juggle the dual challenge of acquiring tech and fighting off imports. As global fashion giants move towards smaller order sizes and quicker turnaround times, the Tiger Global-backed B2B startup may have found a way to avoid its peers’ pitfalls
The global fashion industry is shifting dramatically. Brands like Zara that once ordered a minimum of 6,000 pieces per style are now ordering just 600, bringing small manufacturers into the picture. In this changing scenario, Groyyo built its Rs 470 crore business by focussing solely on small and medium textile manufacturers—a segment other B2B platforms avoided. It offered end-to-end solutions for apparel brands, covering design, manufacturing production, quality, and logistics. Serving over 60 international brands, including Next and Mango, 90% of its business comes from exports.

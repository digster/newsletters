HUL and Disney+ Hotstar are at war over IPL ads

What happens when a big spender feels it’s not getting what it signed up for during the largest streaming event of the year?

The times of TV and print ads were simple and inefficient. Ads were seen by everyone, and advertisers couldn’t choose their audience. Then came digital. The promise was far more customisation, including targeted advertisements for users. Brand marketers mutually decided that no user should see an ad more than thrice. And the focus should be on reaching out to more users. But in the run-up to the IPL, HUL found that the digital ad promise has fallen flat. And that they had only been wasting money. Dr Agarwal’s vs Dr Agarwal’s: a Rs 12,600 cr listing and a namesake’s puzzling rise

The 14-year-old holding company of the 67-year-old listed subsidiary is a canny story of private-equity money birthing a market leader. Dr Agarwal’s Healthcare debuts on the stock market today

Indian healthcare is abuzz with private-equity funds. Today’s listing can set an example of high returns. Or it can re-set the expectations of investors and temper private valuations. In either case, Dr Agarwal’s holdco is an interesting example of corporate restructuring and value creation. The subsidiary stock went through a dream run and is now crashing. The level it seeks and the merger it gets will be stuff of healthcare lore. [Read the full story]

How Tata landed a Punch on Maruti and also buried Nano’s ghost

Nano failed miserably. So, Tata Motors went back to the drawing board and came back with the  safer, affordable, and feature-rich Nexon and Punch

Nano failed in the market despite being labelled the cheapest car. But Tata Motors found success in Punch by balancing affordability, desirability, and safety. Unlike how Maruti Suzuki launches multiple cars in the same category, Tata Motors launched Punch in a smaller and cheaper category than Nexon, ensuring minimal competition between the two. While Punch gained first-mover advantage, the company also kept upgrading its features as and when Hyundai’s Exter and Maruti’s Fronx upped the ante. With India’s automobile market growing more competitive and electrification on the rise, experts believe Punch’s success may be short-lived. [Read the full story]

Stories you don’t want to miss

Summer is coming. For India’s AC makers, winter is coming

Demand is soaring, but supply is still mostly imported. Factories are running, but compressors are missing. And until the industry makes all the parts it needs, every summer will be a race against shortages

Scorching summers have pushed air conditioner demand to new heights, and with it, a supply crunch—cue the push for local manufacturing. But even with government initiatives like the PLI scheme, local manufacturing of key components like compressors is dragging, with over 60% still imported. The problem deepens as delays in BIS recertification for Chinese suppliers collide with a lack of tech partnerships, leaving Indian companies unable to produce compressors locally. Multinational giants have the upper hand, sourcing in-house components, while Indian firms juggle the dual challenge of acquiring tech and fighting off imports. [Read the full story]

Groyyo rewrote the rules of apparel-making. The very ones that put Fashinza out of business 

As global fashion giants move towards smaller order sizes and quicker turnaround times, the Tiger Global-backed B2B startup may have found a way to avoid its peers’ pitfalls

The global fashion industry is shifting dramatically. Brands like Zara that once ordered a minimum of 6,000 pieces per style are now ordering just 600, bringing small manufacturers into the picture. In this changing scenario, Groyyo built its Rs 470 crore business by focussing solely on small and medium textile manufacturers—a segment other B2B platforms avoided. It offered end-to-end solutions for apparel brands, covering design, manufacturing production, quality, and logistics. Serving over 60 international brands, including Next and Mango, 90% of its business comes from exports. [Read the full story]

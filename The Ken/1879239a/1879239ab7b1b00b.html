The ~Rs 26,000 crore ($3 billion) production-linked incentive (PLI) scheme for the automobile and auto components industry is a lucrative proposition for both electric-vehicle makers eyeing juicy subsidies and the central government looking to stimulate domestic private investment to create jobs. 
 
But more than a year after its launch, not a single company selected under the scheme has qualified for the incentives, let alone received them. Even standard operating procedures (SOPs)—essential for evaluating the claims of companies looking to avail these subsidies—are not in place. As The Ken found out, a draft SOP still under discussion has become a bone of contention between the government and the manufacturers. 
 
The government’s scepticism seems to be rooted in the bad press received by the second phase of the Faster Adoption and Manufacturing of Hybrid and Electric Vehicles (FAME) scheme. Like the PLI scheme, companies selected under FAME-II received subsidies only if they sourced a significant portion of their components domestically. As it turned out, a bunch of manufacturers allegedly misappropriated subsidies by masquerading their imports as local procurement. Subsidies to players like Hero Electric and Okinawa Motors were suspended.
 
So, once bitten, twice shy? Not exactly. The government wants companies in this PLI scheme to make several additional disclosures that would make it harder for them to declare imported components as locally procured. But companies want none of that. 
 
In today’s exclusive scoop, Anushka captures intricate details of this tug-of-war between some of India’s most powerful corporations and the government. Who blinked first? Who will have their way?

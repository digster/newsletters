Nykaa still can’t get a handle on fashion. Even as its beauty business pulls further ahead

Looking at this chart, it’s easy to forget how badly investors wanted to get their hands on Nykaa’s shares during its listing in late 2021. 

The demand for the stock was 82X what was on offer. Few companies of the e-tailer’s size have since had such a frenzy around their share sale. Nykaa began trading at almost twice its issue price. 

There were two simple expectations responsible for investors’ perkiness: Nykaa had built a profitable business selling beauty products and was primed to scale that further, and the company would repeat its success in its fledgling fashion business.

But only one of those played out.

This is what I wrote in January 2023, when the stock hit its all-time low:

Fashion, by its very nature, is a thorny proposition for Nykaa—something that it may not have experienced in the BPC [beauty and personal care] business. “I do believe that fashion is the ultimate discovery problem. Fashion has so many products, far more than beauty. So, how do you really show the right thing to the right person at the right time? And that remains a focus for us,” [Nykaa Fashion CEO Adwaita] Nayar said during the same call.

Oddly enough, two-and-half years later, the story isn’t very different. In the quarter ended March, Nykaa grew its beauty sales by a quarter over last year to nearly Rs 1,900 crore ($XX million). The fashion business, despite being much smaller, expanded by a tenth to Rs 160 crore. 

Beauty is pulling ahead on another key metric. In the March quarter of 2024, it had 4X the number of customers who had made at least one purchase in the previous 12 months as fashion. A year later, the gap had widened to 5X. 

The bad news doesn’t end there. 

Ebitda losses, a measure of operating efficiency, in fashion inched up, while beauty’s profitability improved further. 

Nykaa’s shareholders are obviously not chuffed. On Monday, the first trading day after the company announced its financials, the stock slumped almost 5%. 

What explains Nykaa’s struggles in fashion? I believe what an industry longtimer told me in January 2023 still holds true.

E-commerce is still a tough nut to crack for apparel priced above Rs 2,000 (~US$25), according to Subrata Siddhanta, a former senior executive at apparel retailers such as Trent and Shoppers Stop. “It works on touch and feel. That’s why 65-70% of apparel sold online is priced less than Rs 1,000 (US$12.4).”

There are no verifiable numbers for how well Nykaa’s fashion rivals, including Flipkart-owned Myntra and Reliance Retail-run Ajio, are doing. But what is obvious is that most brick-and-mortar apparel retailers have seen better days. Reliance has closed 3,650 stores in the past three years, including in grocery and fashion. Aditya Birla Fashion and Retail has shuttered 50 Pantaloons stores in the past 15 months in an attempt to focus on big cities. 

A couple of weeks ago, I wrote about how Manyavar, once the envy of retailers, has lost some of its sheen because of rising competition in ethnic wear. And there’s little you can take for granted about Manyavar’s mainstay—wedding clothes. 

Vedant Fashions’ lukewarm performance is hardly limited to a single quarter. Even for the year ended March, its profits slumped even as it struggled to grow its sales.

Part of the company’s appeal is the widely held belief that weddings are immune to consumption slowdowns. But Vedant Fashions’ financials have driven a truck through that assumption. 

Nykaa Fashion’s pain isn’t going away anytime soon. JM Financial, a broker, now expects the business to break even next fiscal instead of the current one. All those Nykaa investors betting on a disruption in fashion will continue to be unlucky contrarians for now.

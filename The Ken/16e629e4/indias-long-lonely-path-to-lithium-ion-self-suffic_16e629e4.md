"It's 8:05 am. You are asleep. The doorbell rings. Standing outside are your parents, who came to have breakfast. In the fridge, you have bread, milk, juice, and a jar of jam. So, what do you open first?"
I'm sure you've come across this riddle somewhere considering it has been around since 2013, also known as the Jurassic age. In the context of today's story, this riddle has plagued us since before Niti Aayog, the Indian government's policy think-tank and self-appointed EV cheerleader, was formed (in 2015).
Niti Aayog's latest plan is to launch 4-5 gigafactories in India that together will produce 50 gigawatts (GW) of power by 2024. Up from the current production of 0. That will, per plan, put India on the global map and lower its reliance on Chinese imports, which account for 90% of the country's batteries.
But there's a catch (because of course there is), says Olina, the writer of today's story. Both global battery powerhouses like Panasonic and BYD and local manufacturers like Amararaja and Exide are giving the policy a wide berth.
"It's a funny situation. The government has laid out an elaborate incentive structure, but no one's beating down their door to claim it," says one industry executive.
Why? Because, like "you" in the puzzle, Niti Aayog has to open its eyes first. To a reality that is stark to companies and industry executives.
The reality of a three-pronged problem: 1) anaemic growth in EV demand in India, 2) the government's soft spot for a widely used but first-gen battery tech, and 3) a dearth of raw material that could as much as halve the 'Make In India'-subsidy on offer.
These hurdles, writes Olina, will have a far-reaching impact on India's long, lonely path to lithium-ion self-sufficiency.

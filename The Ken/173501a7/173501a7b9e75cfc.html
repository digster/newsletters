Ride-sharing companies are hard at work thinking of clever ways to get passengers on board. And, eventually, the driver out. Airlines have the same problem. They’ve cut salaries amid the travel slump, and pilots are taking the brunt of it.
 
In India, a bid to offer faster internet speeds to premium customers has been blocked. Another bid that’s been blocked? The Philippines’ largest broadcast network’s franchise renewal. Meanwhile, Singapore has opened a travel bubble with Malaysia.
Three tricks in a pandemic
 
Olina
 
Urban mobility is stuck between a rock and a hard place. Ride-sharing platforms that had declared “peak car” status for thriving local markets in India and parts of Southeast Asia, are now eating humble pie. 
 
There have been a fair number of pivots. We wrote about how companies like Bounce are moving to “rent-to-own” options. Other mobility models—like bike taxi company Rapido—have partially pivoted to a pick-up-and-drop service for small and medium enterprises.
 
It’s clear that pull factors, like access and affordability, are less important now than the organising principle of low touch versus high touch.
 
Stage 1: The curtain falls (and rises)
 
Car-sharing platforms like Uber and Ola have laid down extensive guidelines for driver partners to sanitise their cars every 48 hours, put up plastic sheets, and keep the air conditioning off. This makes for a hot, stuffy ride. But the real problem is that passengers don’t trust that cabs are exactly “low-touch”.
“Out of the seven rides I have taken so far, I found only three drivers wearing a mask at all times. Most of them leave it hanging around their neck or chin. The seats still have cloth covers that are difficult to clean. Some drivers said they have been given masks and sanitisers by the firm, but protective screens have been installed only in company-owned vehicles,” he said. 

Manoj, a driver who works with both Ola and Uber, revealed that all that he has received is a mask and a bottle of hand sanitiser.

“Since I have my own vehicle, I have to install the screen myself but that is difficult since my earnings have been low. Uber provides sanitisation only at the airport. But what about the other times?,” he questioned.
Caution, bumpy ride ahead in cabs during COVID-19 pandemic, The New Indian Express
Stage 2: Outside the box
 
Smaller form factors may fare better. For one, passengers aren’t boxed-in on a scooter. Long-term rentals can work here. On-demand ride-hailing, though, will still be a significant challenge to overcome. After all, these businesses can’t have hundreds of employees spraying every scooter with sanitiser.
 
Stage 3: The disappearing act
 
What if you didn’t have to touch anything at all
“Elon Musk’s Tesla Inc. is “very close” to developing fully autonomous vehicles and could work out the basics of that technology as soon as this year, he said in a prerecorded video played during the World AI Conference in Shanghai.

Musk reiterated that the electric vehicle maker has solved most of the essential challenges toward achieving Level 5 autonomy, or a fully self-driven automobile that needs no human behind the wheel.”
Musk Says Tesla Is ‘Very Close’ to Developing Fully Autonomous Vehicles, Bloomberg​
Ironically, the pandemic has slowed down the very technology most suited for these times:
“Tesla is racing against the likes of Alphabet Inc.’s Waymo and General Motors Co.’s Cruise to attain the pinnacle of the industry: the first 100% driverless car. The coronavirus pandemic has both strengthened the case for robot drivers—by making social distance essential—and shuttered labs and factories where the technology is being refined.”
If all three were options (and didn’t have surge pricing), which one would you choose?
Indian telecom’s unique internet socialism
 
Pranav
 
Last week, Bharti Airtel, India’s second-largest telecom company in terms of subscribers, said that it will give its “platinum customers” more “preference”. And by preference, Airtel meant faster 4G speeds.  
 
Technically, there’s nothing wrong with providing better services to customers who are willing to pay. Platinum customers are postpaid customers who pay more than Rs 499 a month. India’s broadband service plans across the country vary depending on the quantity and quality (speed) of data. This move doesn’t breach the net neutrality clause. The practice isn’t that different across the world either. 
 
India’s third-largest telecom operator Vodafone Idea too launched a similar service in November last year for its “RedX” users. 
 
However, India’s telecom regulator doesn’t seem to agree. On Sunday, Trai asked both Airtel and Vodafone-Idea to stop selling its respective premium services to new users. The regulator believes that these plans may impact the quality of service for other customers.

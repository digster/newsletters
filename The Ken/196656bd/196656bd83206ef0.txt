A Gujarat high for fintechs. Is a successful GIFT City just a boozy GIFT City?

What do Indian fintechs really need? 

Depending on who you are and what you do for a living, you could say more capital; less regulation; better talent; more fees; less competition… the list is long. And if you were one of those who said Indian fintechs need to loosen up, guess what? Gujarat heard you. 

The western Indian state is doubling down on investments and offering more sops in an area where it has long lagged behind the rest of India. 

Booze. 

Gujarat International Finance Tec-City, or GIFT City, is just a few kilometres away from the state capital of Gandhinagar, and is Gujarat’s biggest bet on turning itself into a global financial powerhouse. The state government, which has maintained a nearly six-decade-long ban on alcohol production, consumption, and sales, first began allowing alcohol for people working in GIFT City in 2023. 

The objective was to make the place a more attractive destination for professionals. But the process of getting a permit still seemed as onerous as securing a banking licence from the Reserve Bank of India. Employees needed a “recommendation officer” from their company to submit a permit form to GIFT City officials, and the permit card they were issued could only be used to take up to five people for a drink in a few designated areas. 

Two years on, the Gujarat government seems to have realised this does nothing to portray the state as the free, fun-loving paradise it thinks talented young folks would want to live and work in. 

So this month, it rolled out a few more relaxations https://www.hindustantimes.com/real-estate/gift-city-liquor-permit-rules-eased-real-estate-developers-say-move-to-attract-investments-101745252152484.html to its GIFT City alcohol policy. Employers no longer need to provide recommendation letters, and employees can directly apply to government authorities for a liquor permit valid for up to two years.

But to be honest, alcohol or not, Indian fintechs have been eyeing GIFT City as a home base for a while now. Especially firms involved in cross-border businesses.

Already, some 100 fintechs have set up shop https://www.thehindubusinessline.com/books/reviews/General/fintech-hub-at-gift-city-to-become-functional-by-january-2025/article68576420.ece there.

One founder told me he is considering moving his fintech there because it would make conducting business a lot easier—you can onboard NRI customers with far simpler KYC requirements; get incentives such as a 10-year tax holiday, and escape GST and capital gains tax if you serve foreign customers; and raise money from foreign investors a lot easier. 

But the biggest attraction for fintechs is that you get to escape having the RBI as your regulator. Since GIFT City is like a foreign jurisdiction within India, it’s not the RBI but the International Financial Services Centres Authority (IFSCA) that is the big daddy in the city. 

“They are like a startup regulator, so they are flexible, fast, and easy to work with,” said one fintech executive. Of course, any flows from GIFT City to India will need to comply with RBI regulations. 

But all this, even combined with the alcohol relaxations, doesn’t seem to have convinced a lot of financial talent to move in.

In 2023, my colleague Rounak explored https://the-ken.com/story/14-years-later-is-indias-gift-city-ready-to-take-on-hong-kong-and-singapore/ GIFT City’s pervasive quality-of-life shortcomings. Here are a few excerpts:

The Ken met several employees working here who are contemplating leaving, just like Agrawal. The lack of physical infrastructure isn’t the only reason. “Unlike Mumbai and Gurgaon, Gujarat isn’t traditionally a hub for financial-services companies. I’m concerned about my future job prospects,” said one of them.

[…]

“We are almost in the middle of the wilderness. The nearest grocery store is a 5 km walk. The area becomes desolate in the evenings. I and several others are leaving,” he told The Ken.

Property prices have sky-rocketed, and only a few of the 20,000 professionals working in the SEZ area can afford these houses. 

[…]

Every IFSCA official The Ken spoke with began by talking about an underground utility tunnel, an automatic waste-collection mechanism, potable water from all taps, etc. But when it comes to food, GIFT City has just two eateries. And only one of them was operational during The Ken’s stay.

“Zomato and Swiggy have also only recently started delivering. The options are limited. Some of the companies arrange sandwiches and tetra-packed juices. Most of us carry our food,” an employee at JPMorgan Chase, a global financial-services company, tells The Ken. They did not want to be named as they are not authorised to speak with the media.

Fintech folks I spoke to for this edition were a lot more succinct in their assessments. 

“This is a punishment posting for the finance folks in Bombay,” said one fintech executive. 

“Gandhinagar can be a very sleepy town,” added the founder.

Turns out, it’s the city that is the buzzkill, not the lack of alcohol-laced incentives.

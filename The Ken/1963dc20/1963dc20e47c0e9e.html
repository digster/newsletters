Hello Ishan, 

 

I graduated with an engineering degree in 1998 and got a bottom-rung job as a software developer with a second-tier IT services company. Not wanting to start at the bottom, I decided to pursue an MBA instead so I could start my career a few rungs higher. The job I landed two years later was with a technology consulting startup, as a business analyst.

 

Today, 24 years later, what I do is nothing like what I envisioned back then.

 

It’s not just me though. Here are three other examples.
 
1. The Professor: Sourav Mukherji teaches organisational behaviour and human resource management at IIM Bangalore. He earned an engineering degree from IIT Kharagpur and worked in product and marketing roles at IBM and Oracle. After getting a PhD in management from IIM Bangalore, he worked for BCG as a consultant for a year, before joining IIMB’s faculty in 2003. He’s been teaching since. 
 
2. The Leader: Vasuta Agarwal is InMobi’s chief business officer. She too earned an engineering degree, from BITS Pilani, and then worked for Intel as a chip designer. After a few years, she got an MBA from IIM Bangalore and then joined McKinsey as a consultant. She joined InMobi and became a member of the founder’s office in 2012. She’s been there since. 
 
3. The Founder: Harshil Mathur is Razorpay’s co-founder and CEO. After getting an engineering degree from IIT Roorkee, he joined Schlumberger for his first job. He then quit in less than a year to start Razorpay with his co-founder, Shashank Kumar. They’re still running the company.
 

Three very different people. Three very different sets of choices. Three very successful careers.

 

If you wanted to discuss how best to think about your career ladder, I don’t think you’d find a set of professionals as diverse, experienced, or qualified as them. 

 

Except, that would be a waste. Because career ladders are dead.

 

That’s right. The concept of a traditional “up-or-out” career ladder has been silently crumbling for a long time. 

 

“Millions of people are caught in a perpetual career catch-up. They gain a certain degree of experience and expertise in a seemingly safe job category (‘They’ll never outsource this!’), only to find that, in fact, they can outsource this, and their final weeks are spent training their overseas replacements,” wrote Joanne Cleaver in her 2012 book, The Career Lattice.

 

Cleaver is clearly writing from the US perspective, given the risk of your job being outsourced to a lesser-paid professional in a foreign country. Today, the risk is that your job might be outsourced to an even lesser-paid entity—AI-powered automation.

 

They say human beings, as a species, are unable to accurately perceive and react to events that play out over lengthy periods—over lifetimes and generations. As professionals, we may be making the same mistake while traditional careers are being disrupted.

The 40-year career that young professionals took for granted as they entered their first job has been crumbling for many years. Today, even a 10-year career arc is getting harder to predict and plan for. The career ladder is gradually disappearing in organisations as roles get eliminated, hierarchies get flattened, and work gets automated with AI. As funding cycles, competition, innovation, disruption, growth, and failure all happen in faster cycles for organisations, wouldn’t careers unfold differently, at a brisker pace than ever before?

How do professionals, young or old, plan their careers in such an environment? What are the new “constants” they can make plans around? How do they balance professional growth, stability, and career satisfaction?

 

If companies search for “product-market fit”, what is the way for professionals to seek “career-life fit”?

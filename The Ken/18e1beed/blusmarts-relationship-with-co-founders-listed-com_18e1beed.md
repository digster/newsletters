Good Morning Ishan,
We have a fascinating story in our India edition.
The relationship between Blusmart Mobility, the ride-hailing platform valued at $134 million, and $450 million-valued publicly listed Gensol Engineering goes deep. For starters, they share the same promoters.
Nearly two-thirds of Blusmart’s 6,000-strong fleet of cabs is rented from Gensol, a leasing and EV manufacturing company. Blusmart, too, accounts for over half of Gensol’s assets under management in leasing. So, naturally Blusmart is a “great customer” for Gensol, and by virtue of that, it gets a great deal on pricing.
But what does that mean for Gensol’s stakeholders? Shaswata goes into [great detail](https://the-ken.com/story/blusmarts-relationship-with-co-founders-listed-company-could-prove-costly/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter) on how the two companies are joined at the hip.
In our international edition, Katrina brings you a turnaround story on Sea Limited.
Southeast Asia’s largest tech firm by market capitalisation recorded quite the shift in investor sentiment after its Q4 2023 earnings call. Its gaming arm Garena showed green shoots after a challenging year. While the worst is definitely over for Garena, what are its new avenues of growth? [Read on](https://the-ken.com/story/seas-gaming-arm-bounces-back-with-esports-move-despite-still-missing-india-boost/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter) to find out.
What happened to Google and Apple this week shows…
While Margrethe Vestager may be the European Union’s dreaded competition commissioner, she may as well double hat as the antitrust commissioner for India.
For years, Indian apps and Google have been at loggerheads about in-app purchases.
Last week, Google delisted apps of Bharat Matrimony, Shaadi, Naukri, among others. It took this step to penalise the companies for not paying Google Play Store its 11-26% fees in exchange for letting the firms’ users buy services within the apps.
But after pressure from the Indian government, on Tuesday, Google said it will temporarily reinstate all the apps.
Indian government’s push may have played a part, but what happened to Apple in Brussels, EU’s HQ, also ought to have swayed Google’s decision to relent in India.
On 4 March, a day before Google reinstated all the suspended apps in India, the EU fined Apple $2 billion or 0.5% of its global turnover. It came down on Apple for promoting Apple Music, its own music streaming service. The EU ruled that the big tech knee-capped rivals like Spotify by forbidding it from informing its own users on how they could pay for cheaper subscriptions outside of the App Store.
Sure, Apple is set to appeal the fine. But for Google, the EU’s decision must have set off alarm bells.
After all, just last year, it paid the full penalty of Rs 1,338 crore ($161.7 million) after India’s antitrust regulator said Google exploited its dominant position with Android. While making the payment, Google said that the anti trust regulator copy-pasted parts of a European Union’s judgement without examining evidence in India.
Ironically, it’s the EU’s dominance in shaping antitrust regulations that is a problem for big tech.

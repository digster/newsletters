When the regulator and the regulated form a cosy club, there’s little good that comes out of it. Indian medical education is a fair but sorry example. On how a corrupt regulator, inept machinery and an apathetic government have dragged their feet on a gaping issue: the shortage of specialist doctors.
The regulator, Medical Council of India, has been dissolved three times in the past eight years. But on 2 January, the doctors’ association stood up to the latest, September 2018, dissolution. It wants the government to stay out of regulation.
Now, you wonder, why would a profession fight to save its (corrupt) regulator. Well, the MCI is the ace in IMA’s hand. Ruhi tells you how. She brings a 360-degree view of what the government has done, by not tabling the National Medical Commission Bill (which won’t happen in the foreseeable future either), and instead, has taken a backdoor approach to set up a Board of Governors (BoG).
It’s not funny that everyone agrees on the shortage but majorly disagrees on the approaches to addressing it. The answer may lie in how we got here—from the time when 19 out of 20 medical colleges in India were government-run in 1951 to today when more than half the medical colleges are privately-owned. Most of these colleges (and most of the big hospitals) don’t find it viable to train more specialists. (It takes $70-75 million to set up a medical college, 4X the amount needed for an engineering college, with similar 10-12% returns.)
For now, the BoG may have shifted the balance of power from doctors to an alliance between the regulator and private hospitals. However, it is far from solving the actual problem at hand as it doesn’t directly do what the doctors' association wants it to do—build more medical colleges that offer affordable post-graduation.

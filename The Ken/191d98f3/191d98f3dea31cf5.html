Blinkit and Instamart have the government in their corner. Unlike Amazon. Pricing, profits, ownership—they all have a role to play
By Seetharaman G
6 min read
View in browser

Good Morning Ishan,

Every year, Amazon does its own version of “It’s time to tell you how good we are for India”. This time, it happened right after the government launched one of its periodic—though not always prudent—attacks on the e-commerce colossus. 

Union commerce minister Piyush Goyal took no prisoners when he got up on the podium at an event last month:

When Amazon says ‘we are going to invest a billion dollars in India’ and we all celebrate, we forget the underlying story that these billion dollars are not coming for any great service or investment to support the Indian economy,” said Goyal.

“(Do the losses) not smell of predatory pricing to any of you … what was that loss for? They are not allowed to sell to consumers (directly),” he added.

Then, a few days ago, Amazon issued a press release talking up the scale of Indian merchants on its marketplace selling to overseas customers. And it made sure to name-check “Made in India”. 

Amazon—and, to a lesser extent, Flipkart—have repeatedly irked the government because of their ownership (Walmart bought Flipkart in 2018); deep discounting; and years-long practice of prioritising some sellers over others. This is Goyal in 2021, without naming Amazon or Flipkart:

“A number of these large ecommerce companies have come into India and very blatantly flouted the laws of the land in more ways than one,” he told a virtual event late on Saturday.

“I’ve had several engagements with these large companies, particularly the American ones, and I can see a little bit of arrogance,” he said.

The government’s displeasure with these two have been a function of, or at least accompanied by, mom-and-pop retailers taking issue with them. 

Their ire is nothing new, and it is now being directed at quick-commerce apps such as Zomato-owned Blinkit, Swiggy Instamart, and Zepto. But Blinkit and Instamart preempted this by stating that they are not eating into kiranas’ business. 

The government is reportedly looking into this, but I’m not sure how it can establish beyond doubt the impact of quick commerce on neighbourhood grocers, especially at scale. It’ll perhaps get a consultant to visit a few-dozen stores across a handful of cities and extrapolate that data to arrive at some grand conclusion.

Regardless of the inference, the powers that be have reason not to view instant-delivery apps with the same level of scepticism as they do conventional online marketplaces. The equivalent of third-party sellers on Amazon and Flipkart are the sellers that run the small warehouses from which quick-commerce orders are dispatched. It’s hard to say if the economics of running these warehouses will make sense once growth tapers off and rapid-grocery services wade into each other’s territory. 

But that isn’t a dealbreaker for the government. Franchising isn’t a newfangled idea in India, as retail consultant Arvind Singhal pointed out on a recent episode of The Ken’s first premium podcast, Two by Two. In everything from fast food to fashion retailing, franchising is a tried-and-true model.  

What does infuriate Goyal, though, is that Amazon and Flipkart don’t mind mounting losses as long as they are able to pad their sales with rock-bottom prices. 

But quick commerce is not about discounts. Sure, you may get a good deal on certain products, but convenience invariably trumps value. Tata-owned Bigbasket makes the distinction clear while letting you choose between its two services.

Forget predatory pricing, you could end up buying groceries at the maximum retail price—which is no different from shopping at a kirana—and pay a delivery fee on top of that. 

As a result, quick-commerce platforms aren’t bleeding so badly that profits will always remain elusive. In fact, Blinkit almost broke even in the June quarter. 

Then, there’s the question of ownership. It’s not that they don’t have foreign owners. Dutch tech investor Prosus owns almost one-third of Swiggy, and two little-known American investment firms control one-fifth of Zepto. But none of them has a retailer like Walmart or Amazon calling the shots, as Singhal observed. 

The government would have even less of a problem when the company in question is publicly traded, with thousands of retail investors. What’s more, Zomato kick-started the rush to list among VC-funded tech ventures, and its shares are scaling new heights almost entirely because of Blinkit.

Swiggy will soon go public too, followed by Zepto. They both, of course, want in on the investor obsession with quick commerce. But there’s also no better insurance against trigger-happy policymaking.

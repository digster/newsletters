Good Morning Ishan,

Mphasis has had a worse run in the past few quarters than its peers. But the IT-services company is sanguine about its prospects.

Much of the confidence seems to be a result of private-equity giant Blackstone’s 55% ownership in the company. “Think of it this way—Blackstone is the largest commission-giver on Wall Street, they give many commissions to investment banks through mergers and acquisitions. And via that, Mphasis gains these clients as well,” an analyst told my colleague Abhirami.

What’s more, Mphasis wants to dance to the beat of its own drum on GenAI and finds it pointless to make announcements about how many of its 17,000-strong workforce are being trained in it.

Blackstone may have succeeded in furthering an “entrepreneurial bias” in how projects are taken up at the US$5.2 billion-market cap company. But it’s been eight years since its investment, which means Blackstone will have to find someone to take Mphasis off its hands.

Has Reliance got quick commerce backwards?

Reliance Retail-owned e-tailer Jiomart is reportedly launching its rapid-delivery service in 7–8 cities next month. It’s tempting to say Reliance is late to the party, considering how popular Blinkit, Instamart, and Zepto have become.

But that isn’t necessarily a deal-breaker if you have a bottomless pit of cash and a legendary disdain for thinking small. Nor is its decision to stick to delivering under 30 minutes, unlike the 10–15 minutes preferred by the incumbents.

What’s puzzling, though, is that Reliance will apparently stay away from the lifeblood of quick commerce: small warehouses in high-order-density neighbourhoods. It will instead rely on its own supermarkets and large warehouses used to deliver products in a few hours.

If this is true, Reliance will once again succumb to the allure of something that’s terrific on paper but messy in reality. In 2020, when it rolled out Jiomart, Reliance thought it would use kiranas as an extension of its own warehouses, except that it often had no idea how many units of a particular product the kirana had at any given point.

Reliance may be better off with its supermarkets, but it has to design them to be convenient both for shoppers scouring the aisles and for workers who have to pack a quick-commerce order in a few minutes. In our space-starved cities, that’s a high-wire act.

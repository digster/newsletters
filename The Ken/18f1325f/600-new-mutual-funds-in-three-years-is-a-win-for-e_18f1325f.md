Good Morning Ishan,
India’s US$640 billion mutual fund industry is on a roll.
The bull market we are in has sent mutual fund houses into a tizzy, and they have launched about 185 new fund offers—a process where a fund house offers units of a new scheme to the public—in the 2024 financial year.
This is the third straight year in which mutual fund houses are offering over a hundred such subscription offers; from industry giants like SBI Mutual Fund to relatively newer and smaller players such as Whiteoak Capital, most of India’s 40-odd fund houses have launched NFOs since the start of 2023.
The distributors of these schemes are a happy lot making a fat commission. So, surely this rush means investors must be raking it in, too, by investing in these schemes, right? Right?
Read Akshat’s and Anand’s authoritative story on [what NFOs have done for investors](https://the-ken.com/story/600-new-mutual-funds-in-three-years-is-a-win-for-everyone-almost-everyone/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter).
The Reserve Bank of India, in a surprisingly detailed order, came down on the country’s fourth largest private bank, Kotak Mahindra Bank.
It asked the bank to stop onboarding new customers through its online and mobile banking channels and barred it from issuing fresh credit cards. The regulator pulled up the bank saying it found a slew of IT issues at the bank for over two years now. On top of it, the bank was also “significantly non-compliant” with the corrective action plans issued by the regulator.
This scathing order came three months after the new MD and CEO, Ashok Vaswani, took charge of the bank.
Call it a freak coincidence or a very deliberate warning to incoming CEOs of these banks, but in 2020, a similar story unfolded at HDFC Bank.
The country’s largest private lender appointed Sashidhar Jagdishan in October 2020 to the helm, and in just a little over a month, the RBI asked the bank to stop all digital product launches and sourcing of new credit card customers. This was after the bank had issues with its primary data centre that led to frequent outages in its mobile and internet banking systems.
The diktat on Kotak Mahindra went one step further than in the case of HDFC Bank as it also barred onboarding of any customer through digital channels for Kotak. That must hurt Vaswani who incidentally was a “digital evangelist” in his previous stint at Barclays.
Talk about trial by fire.

Here's a quick fact. There are about 100 days left for the year to end. As Ashish would say, let that sink in.
 
A meme I recently encountered said, "Sooo, what have you done this year?" The answer to that question, for me at least, has been, "I'll fix this. I can fix this. Totally fixing this."
 
Now, one way to fix this, to feel a sense of achievement as a minuscule unit of a massive mediascape, as I understand it, is to have more people read oneself. Which is how we come to this all-important weekly exercise.
 
We've had a good week at The Ken. There has been a wedding (a marriage of convenience), a funeral (RIP) and more. Let's get to it.
 
Amazon and Shoppers Stop's alliance is not very different from a marriage that wasn't originally meant to be, but worked out anyway. Vivek had the story out on Thursday. Why would e-commerce behemoth Amazon need a retail chain like Shoppers Stop? he asks. Read on.
 
The funeral we speak of was that of Tapzo's. Sumanth's story on Wednesday, the last of the Ouroboros trilogy, looked into why Tapzo shut down. Will it find redemption in a new avatar or is this the definitive end? It's free, check it out.
 
The other free story this week, of course, was Roshni's TKOW piece on Sunday. She wrote about donation shaming, especially in the aftermath of the Kerala floods. It's always good to give, but how much? Do you talk about it? What if you're doing it to make yourself look good? Really, is there a right way to donate? These are some of the questions she addresses.
 
A story I quite liked this week was Salman's delivery personnel piece on Tuesday. Indian foodtech companies are looking to corner the market. But to do so, they need to scale their fleets. There are tens of thousands of jobs on offer, but delivery personnel aren’t coming cheap. Is there a solution?
 
Gayathri's story on Monday took a close look at Cure.fit and if a sum of many parts such as Mukesh Bansal's company could actually pull it off. Bansal is betting he can fit four different businesses together and assemble a healthcare empire on a scale no one's attempted anywhere in the world. His investors seem to buy it. Should we? she asks.
 
The last story of the week came from Ruhi on the dialysis chain NephroPlus. The chain has gone from a handful centres in 2010 to 149 today. In doing so, it has become India’s largest network of dialysis centres. But NephroPlus isn’t done growing. Can it maintain its quality of care while expanding rapidly?

Good Morning Ishan,

“Skin in the game” is a beautiful and simple aphorism, coined by someone on the continuum between William Shakespeare and Warren Buffett. Like all great aphorisms, it is simple, powerful and universal in its applicability. Put simply, you are likely to be more invested in the success (or failure) or something if there are personal stakes involved. 

“We are giving money without any capital risk,” says one of the founders of an online lending website from today’s story by Arundhati. If you can build a platform that takes in roughly 7% of every loan disbursed through it “without any capital risk”, you’ve hit business model nirvana. 

It gets better. We’re talking about “P2P” lending or person-to-person lending. The borrowers are people who have poor or non-existent credit scores. The lenders? Well, they are attracted by lending rates of 12-36%. 

“…without any capital risk...”

It was only in October last year that India’s reserve bank, the RBI, formally created a new category for P2P lenders. Yet there’s only one with a license—Faircent. For Faircent, Arundhati identified a group of 18 lenders — mostly businesspeople and savvy investors — who had between them lent Rs 4.3 crore ($600,000) to nearly 2,500 people. 

26% of their borrowers had defaulted.

“…without any capital risk...”

To put this in perspective, let’s consider personal loans, which are lent without collateral much like P2P loans. According to bankers, these have an average default rate of 4%, with peak defaults still only around 10%.

Yes, it’s still very early days for P2P lending. But read Arundhati’s reports from her trip down the rabbit hole to understand how far P2P lending platforms must go before they are trusted by all participants.

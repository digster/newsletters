Trade Tricks
Tue, 03 Feb 26
A weekly newsletter about the biggest changes in commerce—focusing on shifts that matter to you.
Good Morning Ishan,
In August, the co-founder of an investment firm told me that Swiggy was just a couple of quarters away from raising yet more money.
I was aware of the food-and-grocery-tech firm’s struggles, but I didn’t realise then how astute he was. After all, it had been less than a year since Swiggy’s Rs 11,300 crore initial public offering, 40% of which had gone to the company.
But exactly a month after my chat with the investor, JM Financial, a broker, said Swiggy should consider raising [Rs 4,400 crore](https://www.moneycontrol.com/news/business/startup/swiggy-should-consider-raising-over-500-million-to-support-quick-commerce-ambitions-says-jm-financial-13565774.html) to stay in the quick-commerce race.
“Swiggy is fast depleting its cash balance and there are growing competitive threats, it needs to take cues from its larger peer, which recapitalised itself last year despite no visible signs of balance sheet concerns,” the analysts said in a note released on September 20.
In December, Swiggy raised more than twice as much in a share sale to institutional investors.
Last Thursday, it announced its first quarterly results since the fundraise. In contrast to their enthusiastic response to the fundraise, which was subscribed 4.5X, investors punished Swiggy for its bigger-than-expected losses in the December quarter, wiping out 6% of its market value in a day.
There weren’t any curveballs in food delivery, whose total order value expanded the most in three years. The business has also been profitable for a while.
Quick commerce, on the other hand, continued to be Swiggy’s Achilles’ heel. Sriharsha Majety, Swiggy’s co-founder and group CEO, tried to redirect investor expectations in his [shareholder letter:](https://www.swiggy.com/corporate/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/Q3-FY2026-Shareholder-letter.pdf)
Amidst irrational competition, our recent investments into lower consumer-side monetization have not yielded the desired incremental order-growth, especially at the bottom of the AOV-pyramid; and are being reviewed. We have consciously chosen not to participate in deep-discount-driven, purely-volume-focussed growth that sacrifices AOVs and margins.
This has to be read with Majety’s assertion, earlier in the letter, that the company now has a “rock-solid balance sheet”, thanks to the fundraise and a Rs 2,400 crore stake sale in ride-hailing firm Rapido.
The claim is not merely a boast. With almost Rs 16,000 crore in cash, Swiggy has considerably closed the gap with Eternal, which has over Rs 17,800 crore. For context, Swiggy’s September cash balance of Rs 4,600 crore was just one-fourth of Eternal’s.
Swiggy knows it cannot turn to investors one more time to shore up its balance sheet. And it knows just as well that they aren’t going to wait too long for Instamart, its quick-commerce business, to turn a profit, when rival Blinkit, owned by Eternal, broke even in the December quarter.
So, is not keeping pace with “irrational competition” an option, when the “category is only 25% done”, by Majety’s own admission? No.
For what it’s worth, Eternal is as [dismissive](https://b.zmtcdn.com/investor-relations/Eternal_Shareholders_Letter_Q3FY26_Results.pdf) of steep discounts as Swiggy:
We don’t believe you can build a strong quick commerce business on the back of heavy discounting. The tougher answers to growth are the only ones that last.
But Blinkit’s quick-commerce orders in October–December grew 9% over the previous three months. Instamart’s was flat.
When every company in this business could deliver in 10–15 minutes, the next logical fight was bound to be about value. I’m not sure how much Swiggy can avoid this. Even [Eternal](https://b.zmtcdn.com/investor-relations/Eternal_Shareholders_Letter_Q3FY26_Results.pdf) is happy to let its profitability take a hit.
If these [heavy-discounting] tactics start impacting our business, we would need to respond and that might impact our margins.
Quarterly profitability therefore, is not guaranteed… We will continue making sound long-term decisions and let margins land where they do. That’s what got us here so far.
What’s unsaid here: “We aren’t giving up on growth.”
So Swiggy has to maintain a delicate balance between growth, profitability, and a “rock-solid balance sheet”. An impossible trinity of sorts.
Even if you have a rudimentary interest in economics, you might be aware of this concept. I don’t want to bore you with the [details](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/t/trilemma.asp), but the impossible trinity, or the “trilemma”, refers to a country’s inability to have all of these at once: a fixed exchange rate, free capital flows, and an independent monetary policy.
Swiggy’s predicament is similar. Pursuing growth doesn’t necessarily worsen the bottom line, provided economies of scale work the way they are supposed to. But what is more probable is that spending big on marketing and customer acquisition will push profitability by a few quarters. What is likelier still is a depletion of Swiggy’s cash pile. This is highly problematic for a company that raised Rs 14,500 crore in a year.
Viewed differently, obsessing over its cash reserves will hurt Swiggy’s sales and market share when rivals are in no mood to slow down. But this strategy doesn’t guarantee profits.
Should Swiggy put profitability first, the move could once again be detrimental to growth. But it can at least protect its balance sheet from erosion. Eternal has so far enjoyed the kind of backstop not available to Swiggy. Partly a function of raising funds [before](https://the-ken.com/story/zomato-is-flush-with-funds-why-is-it-still-raising-rs-8500-crore/) it needed to, Eternal earned three times as much from its investments in the December quarter as Swiggy.
Swiggy’s investors aren’t oblivious to the bind the company is in. But all they really care about is how soon Swiggy can get out of it.

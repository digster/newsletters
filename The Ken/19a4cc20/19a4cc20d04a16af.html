When you are talking to a friend over the phone, you can usually tell what’s really being said. A pause, a sigh, a shift in tone… she may be describing her breakfast, but you know she is actually mad at you.

That’s the idea behind Pradhi, an Indian AI startup that’s teaching machines to detect emotions in conversations. To read the unsaid. Who knew algorithms could be taught something so “human”?

Pradhi is one of 900-odd AI startups in India, by Nasscom’s count. And most of them are built on top of Big AI—Google, Anthropic, OpenAI—as domain-specific AI wrappers. That’s well and good for now, but what happens if Big AI pulls the plug? Or worse, enters the ring as a competitor to the likes of Pradhi?

In theory, that’s when the Competition Commission of India (CCI) steps in. Practically, the antitrust watchdog is far from ready.

The regulator, often seen as a bunch of “retired half-dead guys”, has only one active office across the country compared to Sebi’s 22; its Director General’s office has been stuck at seven members since 2009, against a sanctioned strength of 20; and its case disposal time has shot up to six to eight months, from three earlier. None of this is encouraging.

CCI itself seems to understand its unpreparedness. Its latest market study on AI suggests companies should simply “self-audit” their algorithms. But what happens when India’s first AI-related collusion case comes knocking?

In today’s story, Inderpal takes a hard look at how AI companies are moving faster than the watchdog meant to keep them in check.

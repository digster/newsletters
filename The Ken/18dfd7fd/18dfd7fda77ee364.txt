We finally got confirmation about Jio’s acquisition of Disney+Hotstar’s India operations last week.

Mukesh Ambani-owned Reliance Industries, Viacom18 Media and The Walt Disney Corporation on Wednesday entered an agreement to form a joint venture that would merge the television and digital streaming businesses of Viacom18 and Star India and create an entertainment giant in India. A joint statement pointed out that the value of the JV at Rs 70,352 crore ($8.5 billion) was on post-money basis, excluding synergies.

As part of the transaction, the media undertaking of Viacom18, a RIL group company, will be merged into Star India Private, a Disney-owned firm, through a court approved scheme of arrangement. RIL would invest Rs 11,500 crore ($1.4 billion) into the JV to fund its growth strategy post-closing. Nita M Ambani will be the chairperson of the venture while media veteran Uday Shankar will be the vice-chairperson.

While the news hasn’t really come as a surprise, what is somewhat interesting is the decision to “merge” the businesses. Like I’ve written before https://the-ken.com/the-nutgraf/disney-hotstar-is-not-a-streaming-service/ several https://the-ken.com/the-nutgraf/hotstar-must-die-for-disney-plus-to-live/ times https://the-ken.com/the-nutgraf/disney-leaves-jio-the-keys-to-the-kingdom/, the writing was on the wall for Disney+Hotstar in India for a while now. In fact, in my last piece about this in July last year, I described this scenario as Disney handing over the keys of the kingdom https://the-ken.com/the-nutgraf/disney-leaves-jio-the-keys-to-the-kingdom/ to Jio. 

So what does this new empire look like? 

Well, I can think of three implications—one from Jio’s perspective, the other from the advertising side, and the third from cricket’s standpoint. 

Jio can now segment the market better than anyone

From a product standpoint, one of the big problems with OTT is that it’s much harder to create offerings that target specific consumer segments. I’ve written about how Netflix and Hotstar have both struggled to do this in India, and eventually, how Netflix found a narrow, viable path that led to them laddering their offerings https://the-ken.com/the-nutgraf/netflix-climbs-down-india-ladder/ through distribution. But it took Netflix years (and several missteps) before they finally got this right. Disney+Hotstar didn’t and treated all of India’s markets as one blob, and well, we know where that got them. 

Arguably, the ability to segment India’s massive market is key to an OTT’s success, mostly because the ones who can (and do) pay for subscriptions are restricted India’s California users at the top of the pyramid, while the rest of the nation have nothing to offer (beyond their eyeballs and attention). To make matters more complicated, most of India’s brands also have their own objectives—they either want to access just the top of the pyramid, or the large, massive base. In the past, brands didn’t really have much a choice, even if their product wasn’t really relevant for the large chunk of the market, they simply had to advertise to everyone, because OTTs couldn’t really give them an option to pick and choose their segments (see: CRED’s ads during IPL 2022). 

Now all that changes.

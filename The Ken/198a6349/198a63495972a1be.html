In early June, just a day before the Reserve Bank of India’s (RBI) scheduled monetary policy meeting, I wrote a piece in Ka-Ching! headlined: Can depositors beat RBI’s rate-cut blues?

As it turned out, the RBI ended up rolling out a “jumbo” rate cut of 50 basis points (bps), or 0.5%, during that meeting, bringing the repo rate down by a whole 100 bps since the start of 2025.

“The repo is the rate at which the RBI lends to other banks in the country. So, when the RBI hikes or cuts the repo, commercial banks follow with their interest rates for customers (or are at least expected to). These cuts, or hikes, then cascade through the economy.”

Can depositors beat RBI’s rate cut blues?, Ka-Ching by The Ken

Well, the deposit rate cuts began as a trickle but quickly turned into a flood. Depositors who moved swiftly then would have been able to lock on to the 8%-plus yearly interest rates being offered by a few small finance banks. (As we had written.) But that window has now closed.

The best rates offered by almost all banks now are below 8%, across deposit periods. Suryoday Small Finance Bank is perhaps the only one that still offers an over 8% interest rate, and that only for five-year deposits. But going overboard with deposits in just one or two banks opens a whole other can of worms, especially if they are small finance banks.

Thankfully for depositors, the RBI has paused its rate-cut spree, making no changes to the repo rate in its monetary policy announcement last week. Otherwise, banks would have gone deeper with their cuts.

Even so, bank deposits now are far less attractive than even just a couple of months ago. And as I wrote then, depositors can and should look beyond banks too.

The first port of call, if you haven’t been there yet, can be the tax-efficient small savings schemes offered by the government—like the Public Provident Fund (PPF) and the Sukanya Samriddhi Yojana (SSY). The PPF currently offers 7.1%. Middling, on the face of it. But the interest accrued on PPF investments is exempt from tax, making its post-tax returns far superior to that of bank deposits for many investors. The SSY, at 8.2%, also gives tax-free interest, which is a great deal.

But both PPF and SSY come with restrictions—an investment cap of Rs 1.5 lakh annually; long lock-in periods (15 years or more); and no regular payments because they’re designed to let the interest compound. Besides, the SSY is only open to parents or guardians of girl children.

The Senior Citizens Savings Scheme (SCSS) also gives 8.2% currently, with an all-time investment cap of Rs 30 lakh per investor. But it is also restricted, open only to those aged 60 and more. And while the interest is paid out every quarter, it is taxable.

There is one instrument, however, that still offers good rates without too many restrictions, even if it has some hitches.

Say hello to the FRSB, short for Floating Rate Savings Bond.

Best of all worlds… almost

Introduced in July 2020, the FRSB has much going for it. It is issued by the RBI on behalf of the government, so it is as safe as it gets. It’s open to all. Any investor can invest any amount in the FRSB without restrictions. Its deposit tenure of seven years is longish, but not as long as the PPF. And the interest is paid out every six months, which is good for those who seek regular income.

Perhaps most importantly, the interest rate it currently offers—8.05%—is much better than what most banks give.

A possible downside, though, is evident in its name. The FRSB’s interest rate is a “floating” rate, pegged to the prevailing rate on the National Savings Certificate (NSC), another small savings scheme offered by the government.

The current FRSB rate breaks down like this: the NSC’s prevailing 7.7% plus 35 bps (0.35%). This mark-up of 35 bps is fixed, but the NSC rate can change every quarter depending on what the Union government decides.

So, if the NSC rate dips to, say, 7.5%, the FRSB rate will fall to 7.85%. And if it rises to 8%, the FRSB rate will be 8.35%. You get the idea.

This floating interest rate on the FRSB is reset every six months—in January and July every year—based on the NSC rate at the time.

So… can the government take a cue from the RBI and cut the NSC rate in the near future?

Nothing can be ruled out, even though the government has kept most small savings rates, including that on NSC, unchanged for more than two years now.

Small savings rates are supposed to be market-linked and based on a formula—i.e., the prevailing rates on government securities of a similar tenure plus a mark-up. Except, the government follows this formula more in the breach, as I have written earlier. Cutting small savings rates can be a political hot potato, as we saw in April 2021, when the government was forced to back off from rate cuts overnight due to protests.

Even if the NSC rate were to be cut, though, I think it is unlikely to be very deep. The government would be cautious, going by past experience. And even at something like 7.4% for the NSC, the FRSB rate will still be 7.75%—much better than most bank deposits today.

Besides this double edge, the FRSB has other drawbacks. The interest it pays out every six months is taxable. During its seven-year tenure, the bond is neither tradeable in the secondary market nor transferable, so investors are locked-in. And the compulsory interest payout means that the FRSB is not a compounding wealth-generator for those who seek such an option; they will need to reinvest the interest on their own every six months in another FRSB, or another instrument that offers similar rates.

But for now, on balance, the FRSB’s pros outweigh its cons—making it an oasis in a desert of drying deposit rates.

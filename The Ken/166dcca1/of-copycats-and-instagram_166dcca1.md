Let me just start off by saying that if there's one subject I'm hopelessly out of depth talking about, it's fashion. I'm happiest when the topic is, say, tech, cooking, books, business or even public transport (I'm that weirdo who when he asks you how it is you commute, it isn't just making small talk).
I can do a pretty good job of winging it with politics ("Hard to say if the opposition has a chance, unless anti-incumbency, you know?") and sport ("New batsman is good, but not a scratch on Sachin"), but not fashion.
And so, naturally, I found myself in the interesting position of having to edit a story on... fashion. More specifically, on how plagiarism is playing out in Indian fashion today.
Copying, says Chandni, the writer of today's piece, is nothing new in the industry. But while in days gone by, public criticism of "inspired' designs would be far and few in between, in the age of social media, things are changing fast. Take something called Diet Prada, a once-anonymous Instagram account that takes pleasure in cheekily calling out some of the biggest designers for copycat work. Everyone's been talking about it in global fashion circles for at least a couple of years now—needless to say, this was news to me.
What social media offers is a platform for the little guy—small designers, independent labels—to speak out against larger brands who occasionally cannibalise their work. The great equaliser and all that. And the trend has already hit Indian shores (and there's even a local version of Diet Prada, called Diet Sabya).
But there's a lot more to it. For one, there's a fine line between plagiarism and coincidental similarities. For another, it's hard to say whether anything we do is ever really original.

There’s no going back on the use of artificial intelligence, or indeed, technology in education. Parents and educators can try to create more friction in the process—supervised or limited time spent on devices, for instance—but it’s going to be too difficult to detach kids from tech now, whether that’s at home or in school. Partly because adults won’t practice this detachment themselves.

Plus, it’s now ridiculously easy to generate study material, quizzes, and question banks online. Stuff that teachers would spend hours on earlier. A large language model can, in a matter of seconds, give you five different ideas on how to plan your next class. This kind of ease shouldn’t be taken away from educators, even if it should be monitored for accuracy.

The area of education where AI actually stumbles is learning. 

Hear me out. AI can be harnessed as a tool, or as an assistant that makes the process of learning easier. Alternatively, it can just straight up give you the answer. But it can’t emulate the struggle of learning something. AI is not good at reasoning out why certain things are the way they are.

It’s a problem that LLMs aimed at the education market have been trying to tame. When Khanmigo, the AI chatbot developed by Khan Academy and Open AI, was launched, Khan Academy founder Sal Khan touted the “socratic” abilities of the chatbot to push students to actually think about the question instead of just giving them the answer. But students can still circumvent that system and go to another LLM to get the solution.

Can the LLM reason out an answer by itself, though? Can it “show the work”?

Claude for Education, Anthropic’s latest offering, claims to do just that. Built atop the Claude 3.7 Sonnet mode, the LLM offers to take students through its own thinking process. It’s a hybrid model—students can toggle between quick-hit answers or choose a more reasoned response. This is how tech journal Venture Beat describes how it works:

It takes time to analyze your prompt before delivering its answer. That makes it great for math, coding, and logic. You can even fine-tune the balance between speed and depth, giving it a time limit to think about its response. Anthropic positions this as a way to make AI more useful for real-world applications that require layered, methodical problem-solving, as opposed to just surface-level responses.It takes time to analyze your prompt before delivering its answer. That makes it great for math, coding, and logic. You can even fine-tune the balance between speed and depth, giving it a time limit to think about its response. Anthropic positions this as a way to make AI more useful for real-world applications that require layered, methodical problem-solving, as opposed to just surface-level responses.

On 2 April, Claude for Education announced a partnership with a handful of higher education institutions, like the London School of Economics and Northeastern University, for campus-wide access to its technology. 

When Claude 3.7 was asked to explain the Monty Hall problem—a classic probability question involving cars and doors and animals—the LLM’s “extended” mode took the user through its thinking, even pausing to output stuff like, “wait… that doesn’t seem to match my earlier hypothesis…” It’s sort of like working through a problem with a teacher or a peer, and feeling your way through to finally understanding how the solution works.

Turning on a “thinking mode” might be the next big step for AI chatbots as they try to make a dent in the edtech market. Teachers still greatly fear that students are just using AI to cheat or get quick shortcuts. The extended or thinking mode, if schools choose to use it, can emulate some of that struggle that teachers hope happens when students are trying to grasp a new concept.

There are still things to iron out though, as a recent evaluation of all LLMs showed. When tested against six questions from the US Math Olympiad and asked to show rigorous proofs behind their mathematical calculations, all LLMs, including Claude 3.7, failed to come up with justifiable reasoning behind their proofs.

The challenge, said the researchers, is that LLMs think they’ve solved the problem correctly, even when they haven’t:

The most frequent failure mode among human participants is the inability to find a correct solution. Typically, human participants have a clear sense of whether they solved a problem correctly. In contrast, all evaluated LLMs consistently claimed to have solved the problems. This discrepancy poses a significant challenge for mathematical applications of LLMs as mathematical results derived using these models cannot be trusted without rigorous human validation.

[…]

Another important observation is the lack of creativity in the models’ reasoning. Each model often attempted the same (and wrong) solution strategy across all attempts, failing to explore alternative approaches.”

Everyday maths may not need the level of rigorous proofs demanded by an Olympiad-level answer. Still, if we’re going to look to AI for the future of edtech, LLMs need to be able to reason, and reason correctly. There’s only so much space for more “tools” in the market. “Thinking” LLMs that grapple with the human condition of learning, as much as real humans do, need to become the next frontier in online learning. And they need to arrive at the correct answer, the way humans eventually do.

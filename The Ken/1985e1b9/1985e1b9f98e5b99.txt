IPO-bound Groww’s Hail Mary. The mass-market discount broker needs to do a lot to justify the valuation it’s seeking. And it seems to think one answer is a user segment it’s ignored all this while—high-volume traders

Groww is in a bit of a pickle. 

The discount broker, long a poster child of India’s stock trading segment, made its bones by bringing in millions of young participants into the stock market—many of them first-timers. The craze over futures & options (F&O) trading went a long way in facilitating this rise. 

But F&Os are in the regulatory doghouse now, with discount brokers across the board—not just Groww, but others https://entrackr.com/news/groww-maintains-lead-over-zerodha-and-angel-one-amid-user-base-decline-in-june-9477898 like Zerodha* and Angel One too—starting to lose “hobby users” who were predominantly lured by the prospect of seemingly easy money offered by F&Os (they were wrong, of course). 

Now, having already filed for an IPO through the confidential route, with an asking valuation that is expected https://the-ken.com/story/growws-ipo-pitch-we-are-more-than-a-discount-broker-investors-really-show-us/ to be around $7–8 billion and reportedly looking to raise nearly a billion dollars, Groww finds itself in a bind. 

Its top business—around 90% of its topline comes from broking—is no longer as lucrative or high-growth as it was before the regulatory squeeze https://the-ken.com/story/nses-monopoly-was-unshakeable-until-now/ on F&Os. And it doesn’t really have much else to show its prospective IPO investors. 

Which explains why it has spent the last few months desperately trying to diversify into segments it has ignored thus far. Like wealth management, for which—as The Ken wrote https://the-ken.com/story/growws-ipo-pitch-we-are-more-than-a-discount-broker-investors-really-show-us/ this May—it has signed an agreement to acquire wealth-tech startup Fisdom for $150 million, and is planning its own wealth-management unit focused exclusively on the well-heeled, called “W”. 

And now, as noted by business writer Jayant Mundhra in a recent Linkedin post https://www.linkedin.com/posts/jshilanjanm_deepdive-its-nice-that-groww-is-finally-activity-7352895821505286144-JAPm/?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_ios&rcm=ACoAAAAYqnoBkNTKwzrl29sr5b1QlRYuaHPBArI, Groww seems to be foraying into a market segment it has been happy to ignore for years.

Servicing high-frequency, high-volume traders. 

In search of high-quality users

Earlier this month, news broke https://www.moneycontrol.com/technology/ipo-bound-groww-to-launch-trader-focused-915-terminal-to-drive-revenue-diversification-article-13276023.html that Groww is looking to launch a new trading terminal exclusively aimed at this segment. Code-named 915, the terminal will reportedly be hosted outside Groww’s app (a first for the platform and a clear indication that the product is not aimed at its existing user-base) and feature an array of capabilities that high-frequency, professional traders look for, including customisable dashboards. 

The company did not respond to The Ken’s queries on the matter. 

Groww’s desire to break into this market is understandable, even if its tardiness is not. 

Even before the F&O squeeze, Groww’s average revenue per user (ARPU) was far, far behind its major competitors, and this had to do with the kind of users it attracted to its platform. Quoting from our story in May: 

Unlike some of its peers who have more deep-pocketed clients and power traders, Groww’s clients are mostly young, first-time investors. The kind who generally don’t have big spending and staying power, especially in volatile market conditions.

In FY24, for instance, Groww’s average revenue per user (ARPU) came in at under Rs 4,000. Angel One raked in twice as much. And Zerodha and Dhan reported thrice the number.

In contrast, its valuation was among the highest in the segment, ahead of the listed Angel One (Rs 27,240 crore or $3.2 billion) and not too far behind the unlisted Zerodha (Rs 85,000 crore or $10 billion). At $7 billion (the valuation it’s likely seeking in its IPO), Groww would trade at about 20X its FY24 sales, compared with Zerodha’s 9X and Angel One’s 6X.

That would likely be a hard pill to swallow for any public market investor, and a tough sell for Groww. 

In sharp contrast to Groww, though, is another discount broker. One with a far smaller user base, but with an ARPU many times higher—Dhan. 

Founded in 2021 by ex-Paytm Money executive Pravin Jadhav, Dhan has spent these last four years focussing on “power traders” (a segment once seen as being firmly in the pockets of Zerodha) and actually making a lucrative business out of it. In March, when I wrote https://the-ken.com/story/why-is-private-equity-suddenly-chasing-dhan-a-stock-broking-underdog/ about Dhan, around 85% of its user base were such high-volume traders, or “super traders” as one analyst put it. 

Dhan’s offering might have been a bit too much for the average amateur Groww user, but was close to everything professional traders wanted. And it seems to have worked. Not only has Dhan added a significant number of users, its ARPU is just shy of Zerodha’s, the leader in the space.

Groww probably thinks replicating this is one way it could convince prospective investors that it is a good buy. 

And give it one more card to add to its financial supermarket deck. 

Of course, it took Dhan and Zerodha years of concerted effort and focus to build an attractive platform for “super traders”. 

Groww is just starting.

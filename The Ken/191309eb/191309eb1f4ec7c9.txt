Gimme my grandpa too. Debt fund investors will be well justified in seeking a relaxation on long-term capital gains (LTCG) tax, similar to what has been handed to real estate investors

Good Morning Ishan,

“Be careful what you wish for,” I wrote https://the-ken.com/kaching/be-careful-what-you-wish-for/ a couple of weeks ago, after finance minister Nirmala Sitharaman delivered a googly in the Budget for the current fiscal year. The Budget made big changes in response to demands to simplify capital gains, but not quite the way many wanted.

Here’s what I wrote:

…the sting in this rehaul lies elsewhere: in the new tax rates and the removal of a key benefit—indexation.

[…]

The real standardisation—and one that has left many reeling—is in LTCG tax. Now, the LTCG tax on most assets, including stocks, equity mutual funds, listed bonds, gold and real estate, is the same 12.5%. Earlier, for assets like gold and real estate, the LTCG tax rate was 20%. That means the tax for those categories has been cut, right? What’s not to like? 

Here’s what. Earlier, the LTCG tax rate of 20% came along with the benefit of indexation. Now, the 12.5% is without indexation. The benefit of indexation has now been removed across all asset classes. 

That’s a seismic shift—especially in big-ticket real estate deals. 

Indexation essentially allowed you to increase the cost of acquisition of an asset to account for inflation over the years. This led to lower recognised capital gains and, thus, lower tax outgo.

The scrapping of indexation benefits on long-term capital gains (LTCG) in real estate deals in particular resulted in a firestorm of protest, including on social media https://x.com/dmuthuk/status/1815665677094854916 and in the Indian Parliament https://x.com/AbhasHalakhandi/status/1816469121632014519.

Firmly on the backfoot, the government made a concession yesterday in its amendments https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/wealth/real-estate/capital-gains-tax-calculation-changes-to-bring-big-relief-for-homeowners-12-5-ltcg-tax-or-20-tax-with-indexation-which-will-reduce-tax-outgo/articleshow/112344642.cms to the Budget. Real estate investors have now been given a choice—of ‘grandfathering’ their long-term capital gains. Grandfathering essentially means that a rule change is not applicable or mandatory on transactions done before the date on which the rule changed.

Grandfathering is a fair principle, and I want to talk about that (and why some other classes of investors will be looking for the same) in today’s edition. We also have a piece on why some income-tax filers have been pushed into seeking personal loans and a quick round-up of other interesting developments in fintech and finance.

Where’s my grandpa?

Here’s what The Hindu BusinessLine reported yesterday morning:

According to officials, one of the key amendments set July 23, 2024, as the date of grandfathering. “In the case of transfer of a long-term capital asset, being land or building or both, by an individual or HUF (Hindu Undivided Family), which is acquired before July 23, the taxpayer can compute his taxes under the new scheme [@ 12.5 per cent without indexation] and old scheme [@20 per cent with indexation] and pay such tax which is lower of the two,” a source explained.

Like I just mentioned, grandfathering is fair—taxpayers should not be compelled to pay up for decisions they made when the new rules weren’t in force. Many real estate investors would have bought property earlier on the premise, and the promise, that their LTCG on sale would be taxed at 20% with indexation. So, it’s only fair that, on earlier transactions, they should be allowed the choice of LTCG tax at 12.5% without indexation or 20% after indexation—essentially giving them the choice to select whichever one works best for them. Indeed, LTCG tax at 12.5% without indexation would work better if the rise in property value has been significant and has outpaced inflation. 

For property bought after the new rules were announced, i.e., on or after 23 July, 2024, the LTCG tax is still a uniform 12.5% without indexation. No choice needed here; investors will walk in with their eyes open.

But the amendment, while doing the right thing by real estate investors, seems to have given the cold shoulder to investors in other asset classes such as debt mutual funds and gold. There is no mention of ‘grandfathering’ of LTCG tax in asset classes other than real estate.

1. Most Favoured Employee

Earlier this week, an article was going around in which Brian Chesky, Airbnb CEO, defended bosses having favourite employees.

Bosses need to do that to be effective leaders, Chesky told Fortune last month. Those people can be top performers who set the quality of work standards on their teams, or quickly find and solve problems, he said.

“The favorites have to be [picked] on fair criteria … but if you can’t say this is a high performer and this is what excellence looks like, then you’re going to be in big, big trouble,” said Chesky. “That is just not good leadership.”

I won’t lie. My first thoughts were, yes, of course they do. We’re all human. Don’t we have favourite friends, favourite colleagues, favourite teachers, or favourite bosses?

Of course we do.

But it is also a reality that one of the reasons people leave companies is perceived favouritism. Searching for answers, I turned to a book that helps explain why employees leave companies.

Favouritism is a problem, but not that big.

View in Browser

The above figure is from the wonderful book, “The 7 Hidden Reasons Employees Leave“ by Leigh Branham. The book is based on 19,700 exit and current employee surveys conducted by Saratoga Institute (a part of PwC) from 1999 through 2003.

Most managers have built comfortable and satisfactory relationships with some employees, but have also experienced the opposite as well—relationships with other employees that never got off on the right footing, or went from bad to worse. Perhaps it is because we simply like some employees better than others, or we favor those who are most similar to us, but it is a common phenomenon to place a halo on the heads of some employees, and see horns growing on others.

[…]

The vast majority of bosses favor some subordinates, treating them as part of an in-group, while consigning others to an out-group. The manager may either totally ignore those in the out-group or over-supervise them to such an extent that they stop giving their best, stop taking the initiative, and become automatons, sending the clear message back to their managers,

‘‘Just tell me what you want and I’ll do it.’’ This is the very definition of disengagement.

The effects on those in the in-group may also be highly negative. If the manager loses faith in a performer he perceives as weak, he may start overloading those he considers stronger performers, creating resentment on their part and eventually burning them out.

So, I switched to a different book to see things from the POV of engaged and happy employees, “Work Happy” by Jill Geisler.

She argues, correctly, that treating everyone the same is actually bad. Because we are all unique.

Fallacy Number One: You help employees succeed by teaching them to work just like you.

Fallacy Number Two: You must treat everyone the same.

I know those ideas seem to make sense. You did a lot of things right when you were the “doer,” and it got you promoted, so why not have people copy you? And how in the world can you be known as fair-minded if you don’t manage everyone the same way?

The answer is simple: Every person is different, just like the instruments in an orchestra. What they have in common is that they aren’t you. If you try to make them your clones, they’ll flounder. And if you treat each person identically, you’re inviting your Evil Twin to undermine you. Instead of being seen as the Manager Who’s Mighty Fair, you’ll be seen by many as the Manager Who’s Making Me Fail.

How do you learn to treat employees differently but still be true to your goal of fair play? You get smart about the fascinating and frustrating array of differences among perfectly normal people.”

So we do want to be treated differently, not equally.

“It is not a naïve platitude to point out that complaints are just negatively stated solutions. People complain of poor management when what they want is good management. They complain of favoritism when what they prefer is an even playing field.”

Leigh Branham explained it very well. We don’t mind favouritism, as long as we have an equal chance at becoming a favourite too.

Somewhat like the concept of a “Most Favoured Nation” when it comes to world trade. The MFN status is actually one of the foundations that underpinned the World Trade Organisation.

Under the WTO agreements, countries cannot normally discriminate between their trading partners. Grant someone a special favour (such as a lower customs duty rate for one of their products) and you have to do the same for all other WTO members.

This principle is known as most-favoured-nation (MFN) treatment.

MFN does not mean a country is favoured more than others, but it means it is treated as fairly as the best favourite.

Extending this to the workplace, I would love to hear your thoughts on the concept of a “Most Favoured Employee”, or MFE.

Meaning, you can demand to be treated as fairly as the person you think is a favourite. I love this thinking, because it implies that an employee wants more opportunities, more mentoring, more growth.

I’ve often seen that angst against high-performers in workplaces (and they are usually also the favourites of one or more bosses) rarely comes from those who seek to become high-performers themselves. In my (limited) experience, such employees will either demand opportunities and growth, failing which they simply switch off and quit after a while. The best bosses and organisations can spot such talent before it reaches that state. Of course, I am assuming good intentions overall here.

We’ve digressed a bit. But my point is that we have to explicitly want that MFE status for ourselves. We should want the same mentoring, opportunities, and growth that someone else might be getting. And we should be unafraid of asking for it.

It is also incumbent on bosses to encourage and support those who do want MFE status. I recently created a “User Manual” for myself, so my colleagues would have a better idea of how to work with me and “use” me in their careers. I shared it with everyone at The Ken.

Here are a few points from it that I hope will convince more of my colleagues to apply for MFE status with me. 🙂

“I am partial to ambitious, self-driven and self-aware professionals. I like helping them find clarity, helping them achieve their goals, helping them come unstuck, and helping them succeed. But I cannot be the one to insert ambition into you.”

[…]

“I support people who take risks in pursuit of ambition. If that leads to mistakes or failures, I will fully support you (provided you were diligent). My own professional life has been shaped and moulded by “mistakes” and “bad choices” and “failures”.”

From How to work with Rohin (A User Manual)

2. Thank God it’s Christmas!

Hello, Ishan, it’s Hari here with this year’s First Principles Christmas Playlist. Before we hit play on the lovely Christmas tunes, I just want to thank all of you, and my colleagues at The Ken, for helping put it all together. In fact, I even took the title for this section from a document packed with recommendations shared by my colleague Raveena. 

Christmas playlists need to walk a fine line. They are, undeniably, about warmth, home, longing, love and family. A good playlist offers us comfort and familiarity, with a hearty dash of sweetness, much like cake, spiced chocolate or mulled wine. But great ones also push the boundaries and edges just a little bit. They nudge us ever so gently into a newer direction, without pushing us. We all need that for the new year ahead.

What we have for you today is a perfect blend of all those many things.

3. Beyond just vacations

Hello and welcome to another edition of First Principles Silent Sunday, Ishan. It’s Jonathan here as always. 

Why, for many of us (dare I say, most of us), does photography remain a ‘vacation’ activity? 

I know the most common answer is obvious enough that it shoves my question rather unceremoniously into the realm of rhetoric. 

“When else do we have the time, Jonathan?!” I can hear you splutter. 

I understand. And I agree. The hustle and bustle of daily life offers little space for anything beyond the mundane. I have trouble enough with my daily search for matching socks without adding non-essential side quests to my checklist. 

It is an entirely understandable choice. And a reasonable one. But, of course, if photography is more than just an occasional diversion for you, relegating it to just vacations does come at a cost. Perhaps a small one, but a cost just the same. 

I was reminded of this a few days ago when one of my colleagues, Shristi Achar, sent in a photo for this week’s Silent Sunday.

Lalbagh, Bengaluru (Shristi Achar)

View in Browser

“From Lalbagh in Bengaluru, for some local options,” Shristi captioned. [You can send in your own photos for the Silent Sunday section using this link.]

I’ve been in Bengaluru for a couple of years now, and have visited Lal Bagh quite often. But try as I might, I couldn’t find anything noteworthy from my visits to the garden in my photo gallery. Neither, when I started looking, could I find anything from the hundreds of walks my wife and I have taken around our local lake in C V Raman Nagar. 

Not because there wasn’t anything I found worth photographing, but because taking a minute or two to click a picture never even occurred to me. I realised I had, subconsciously, assigned the ‘vacation activity’ tag to photography. And because of that, I was missing out on exploiting some great frames that existed right next to me. 

So yesterday, when I was at the lake, I made it a point to whip out my phone and capture a frame that I’ve always found intriguing. Serendipity ensured that a lovely crescent moon honoured me with her presence too.

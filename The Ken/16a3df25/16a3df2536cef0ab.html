Good Morning Ishan,
 
This is a story from almost a decade ago. A time when I stayed up all night, smoked more than I should have, and had a stunning lack of prescience. It is this last trait that led me to make a nightly expedition, usually at around 2AM, to buy cigarettes from a roadside vendor stationed a kilometre from my house.
 
His name was Anand, and while I didn't realise it the first time we encountered each other, he was as sharp a businessman as I'd ever met. It started with just chai and the usual assortment of tobacco products—the staples of any late-night Bangalore vendor. And then he added biscuits because, of course, what goes better with chai? And then coffee since not everyone drank chai. And then sponge cakes to go with the coffee. And chewing gum to avoid smoker's breath. Rolling papers, even though he only had a tenuous idea of what they were. Eye drops would eventually make an appearance, too. And a host of other things I never got around to trying.
 
Over several journeys to Anand's makeshift stall, he explained his approach. If more than a few customers asked for an item he didn't have, he stocked it the following day. There were about six late-night vendors in the vicinity. His job, he explained, was to make his stall the most attractive. It worked, I walked past two vendors every night for weeks to get to his stall. I wasn't alone. Anand had several regulars, from IT professionals on their way home to prolific alcoholics who were just getting started. I was somewhere in between these extremes.
 
The lessons from that tea stall all those years ago embody a very fundamental aspect of success. The need to add value. Constantly. Especially when the going is tough, but also when it seems like the going is good. We saw examples of this in the stories we covered this past week.
 
Like our contributor piece on Tuesday, for example. Srikanth Rajagopalan, a veteran of the payments space, wrote about how debit cards are potential casualties in the digital/cashless revolution. If they are to remain relevant, he argues, banks and traditional card networks need to start trying new things. This was also a free read, so do share it with anyone you think would be interested.
 
The modern retail sector understands the importance of adding value. As Rozelle wrote in our Monday offering, modern retailers in India are increasingly turning to private labels in their quest to increase their margins.
 
Our Thursday piece also touched upon something similar. Vandana wrote about Gurugram-based e-grocer Grofers. A master at ditching old, failing models for newer, promising ones, Grofers' many pivots have ensured that the business is still alive and raising funds today. Now, the six-year-old company has embarked upon a new path, one it believes will finally lead to its moment in the sun.
 
We also had Sumanth chime in this week. On Sequoia India's new accelerator, Surge. What value does this add to Sequoia and the Indian startup ecosystem? What value does it add to the 17 startups in Surge's first cohort? Sumanth unwraps all of that.
 
Our final offering for the week was an absolutely lovely read from Roshni, who spent her time writing about... well, time. About IST, specifically. About the men who think it's rubbish and needs to be tweaked. About the value this could add, both economically and socially. About the bureaucrats who don't really think it makes enough of a difference for them to care. It's also a free read, so share it far and wide.
 
Oh, and as for Anand, he added more value than even he knew. See, I wasn't the only person who saw him grind. Among the various IT professionals who frequented Anand's stall, one owned a business. He saw Anand's hustle. He hired him and trained him. Anand now works in IT. 
 
Always add value.

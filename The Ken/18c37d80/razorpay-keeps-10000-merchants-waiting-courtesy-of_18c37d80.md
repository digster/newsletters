Good Morning Ishan,
Nine years ago, twenty-something-olds Harshil Mathur and Shashank Kumar convinced large investors to give them millions in funding to start a company.
Then, they convinced thousands of employees to join their startup, Razorpay.
The duo even convinced hard-nosed banks and 10 million merchants to partner with them. All of this led to Razorpay becoming one of the largest companies in the payments space, processing $100 billion in transactions.
They still have one more party to convince—India’s banking regulator, the Reserve Bank of India (RBI)—and make it see why the company is worthy of a payment-aggregator licence.
It’s been nearly a year since Razorpay, as the second-largest payment aggregator, onboarded new merchants for its payments-gateway service.
The Y combinator-incubated fintech is facing an RBI embargo along with India’s other major payment aggregators like Payu, Cashfree, and Paytm*. Together, they account for nearly 80% of total new merchant onboarding and are waiting for the regulator’s final nod for a licence.
At first, Razorpay didn’t seem to mind the embargo, but now, as the months drag on, not being able to onboard new clients is hurting it. Especially since Razorpay’s claim to fame was easy onboarding for merchants, particularly the small ones. Now, it has over 10,000 merchants just waiting in the wings.

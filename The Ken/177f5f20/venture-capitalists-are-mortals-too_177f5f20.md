It’s easy to believe that venture capital (VC) firms are at the top of the food chain.
In many ways, they are. They have the power to launch companies with investments; the muscle to keep the dream alive by doubling down with more money; and, in many cases, when it all plays out right, they’re rewarded with outsized financial returns.
Accel, for example, is reported to have made a profit of US$1.1 billion from Flipkart’s sale to Walmart. That’s the archetypal home run: a single exit that returns the total value of a VC’s fund and much, much more.
But those events are rare, and the reality is that VC life isn’t all that different to the very startups that they back:
-
VCs themselves must raise money—that comes from limited partners (LPs)
-
They must execute—that comes from their portfolio companies being sold or listed
-
They must attract and retain talent—that comes from doing both of the above well
But you'd never know it. We hear many of the VC success stories—for example, around Flipkart—but precious little of the reverse, despite startups and founders increasingly talking of the acceptance of failure and telling their own stories of failing.
Today’s story from Sumanth, however, flips the switch and looks at what happens when a VC firm falls from its lofty perch.
Kalaari Capital—his subject—was once India’s premier e-commerce firm but its last decade features all the twists and turns of a startup roller coaster ride. A charismatic founder forges a promising reputation but a pivotal moment goes the wrong way leaving them paddling against the current.
There’s even a highly-relatable financial bailout from Reliance, India’s digital white knight-in-chief via its big tech-funded billions, which has bought into numerous startups already.

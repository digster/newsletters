Good Morning Ishan,
 
Today's story reminds me of a scene from one of my favourite books - Watchmen, by Alan Moore.
 
If you haven't read the only graphic novel in Time's list of 100 Best Novels since 1923, you should. It's an amazing story about a group of superheroes. Except, unlike every Marvel movie where everybody puts aside their misunderstandings and band together by the end, in Watchmen...that never happens. Anyway, there's a scene in the book when seven superheroes meet after forming a group called the Crimebusters. Everything goes well at first. Then disagreements are voiced. Accusations fly. Some stay silent. Others walk out. Finally, something unforgettable happens. Captain Metropolis, the leader, left alone, makes a plea to the departing heroes:
 
'Please! Don't all leave...Somebody has to do it, don't you see? Somebody has to save the world...'
 
In India, there were seven people who organised themselves into a group called the Internet Freedom Foundation. More famously known by their previous name - Save The Internet. Their goal was to get rid of Facebook's Free Basics in India. And they did. As Ashish writes, "Together, this motley group created the largest, most significant internet activism movement this country has ever seen. Together they created Save The Internet. Together they created hope. That, in a country where millions of lives will be impacted by the Internet, there were people who cared. Who would fight for your rights. Unafraid of the powers that be. Together they created a great story of human willpower and ambition. One worthy of believing. One worthy of telling."
 
Someday they will make a biopic about it. Today's story isn't about that, though. It's about the fallout after the credits rolled. It's complicated, with lots of claims and counter-claims. And disagreements. On structure. On operations. On personalities. But mostly on how to handle Aadhaar. While Indians across the country were receiving repeated, insistent text messages urging them to link everything to Aadhaar, one group was tearing itself apart because they couldn't agree to do so. To me, today’s story is fascinating. Because it pits the organisation which spearheaded the creation of one of the strongest Net Neutrality regulations in the world against the forces of human nature and ego that led to its dissolution. 
 
As I said, it’s complicated. 
 
Oh also, it has not one, not two, but three whole disclaimers.
 
Luckily, there are few writers better than Ashish who can unpack a complicated story. And it's free. So take some time and read the story here: https://the-ken.com/story/indias-save-the-internet-breaks-up/
 
After that, if you get a chance, go read Watchmen. Trust me.

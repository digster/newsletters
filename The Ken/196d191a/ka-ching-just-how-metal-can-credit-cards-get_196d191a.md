Ka-Ching!
Thu, 15 May 25
A weekly newsletter about how finance is getting supercharged by tech in India, and how you can make money work for you..
Good Morning Ishan,
I want to start with a few questions.
First: do you have a car? If yes, does it have a sunroof? How many times have you ever used it?
Second: do you have an iPhone Pro Max? If yes, how many of those fancy extra features do you regularly use?
I think you’ve figured out what I’m getting at by now.
Vanity features are great… for the companies that sell them. Being a boring, tightfisted spender who doesn’t see much, if any, value in purely cosmetic add-ons, I’ve always found them entirely pointless.
But it seems that a larger number of fellow Indians than I would have expected disagree. And they’re going for something that—if you ask me—is even more useless than sunroofs or fancy electronic features.
Metal credit and debit cards.
Last week, I got my hands on a white paper by Composecure, a 25-year-old US-based fintech. The same paper that, according to two Delhi-based bankers I spoke to, was used to support ICICI Bank’s and HDFC Bank’s plans for their new retail credit cards. Both banks have apparently decided to ramp up the sales targets for metal cards, despite the fact that they cost nearly 5X to produce compared to plastic ones.
“But why?” was my first reaction.
After all, hadn’t Onecard, the fintech unicorn, done exactly the [same thing in the past](https://the-ken.com/story/onecard-sailed-past-rbi-hurdles-only-for-banks-to-hold-it-back/) and received, well, only a middling response? Also, why would anyone want to buy a metal card that has the same benefits as a cheaper plastic one?
But apparently, a lot of Indians are ready to do just that.
According to Composecure, which surveyed 21,250 customers across 17 countries for its report, 92% of Indian consumers surveyed are willing to pay an additional premium for a metal payments card—debit or credit—surpassing the global average of 73%. And nearly 80% of global respondents and 90% of Indians are ready to choose a metal card over a plastic one even if both have exactly the same features. In fact, an increasing number of Indians are converting their plastic cards into metal, the two bankers told me.
None of the reasons respondents gave in favour of metal cards were anything out of the ordinary—“It’s durable. It’s sustainable. It gives a premium, high-end feeling.” And for all that, they’re willing to shell out as much as Rs 5,000 extra every month.
Bankers, of course, are quite happy with this. One of the two I mentioned above told me that internal market research has found that metal cards instil more confidence among consumers in the brand. So, more metal cards would only result in a handy uptick in brand perception.
I guess that answers why banks would want to get their metal card sales up. But I still found it very difficult to square the reported consumer demand with how little metal cards actually offer. So I spoke to two users who have over 20 credit cards between them, many of which are metal. One is a Mumbai-based credit-card connoisseur and the other is a Delhi-based chartered accountant, but neither wanted to be seen commenting on the cards publicly.
Banks presently offer several metal cards in India—there’s HDFC Bank’s Infinia, ICICI Bank’s Emerald, SBI Card’s Aurum, American Express’ Platinum, among others. The chartered accountant, who shells out around Rs 15,000 a year more just for metal versions of plastic credit cards, says “metal cards usually tend to have better benefits and rewards systems”.
But it’s actually the other way around. Premium cards have better benefits, and most metal cards happen to be premium ones. In fact, one of the bankers acknowledges that it would be difficult to scale metal cards with no extra features.
For those willing to shell out more for just the metal, though, it all seems to come down to the one thing we started this piece with.
Vanity.

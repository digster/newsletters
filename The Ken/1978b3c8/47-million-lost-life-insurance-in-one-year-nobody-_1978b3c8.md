Good Morning Ishan,
We are all still processing the tragic air crash in Ahmedabad last week. The headlines have now shifted to the insurance covers for the lives lost.
In all that, one other piece of news got lost. As many as 47 million Indians lost life insurance in the just-concluded financial year. One of the steepest declines ever, and yet no major insurer is surprised: not HDFC Life, not ICICI Prudential, not Bajaj Allianz, not even the state-owned SBI Life.
Insurance veterans believe such a dramatic collapse in coverage is usually caused by something big, like a pandemic. This time, there’s no apparent trigger. (There’s a lingering pandemic correlation, though.)
It’s a build-up in the microfinance industry, a self-inflicted crisis, a bubble popping.
For a country that has spent the last decade proudly chasing financial inclusion—Pradhan Mantri Jan Dhan Yojana now has over 552 million accounts—it’s a moment of reflection. Of these accounts, 368 million are in rural or semi-urban areas, and the missing 47 million life covers fall in that quadrant.
The rising load of death claims is now close to 75% of pre-pandemic peaks, and insurers are happy to look the other way to decouple insurance sales from lending. And the central bank?
In a year where so many millions got pushed out of coverage, few decision-makers seem surprised.

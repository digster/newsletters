More than 10,000 unemployed pilots, but airlines won’t hire. Rather, can’t hire.

One may shrug off the job-candidate mismatch as typical of a populated country like India. (Degree holders are everywhere, yet employers often fret over their employability.)

But when pilots die of fatigue, when flights are frequently delayed, when airlines see soaring air travel, and have huge expansion plans in the wings, it’s no longer a question of just another airline-pilot-not-seeing-eye-to-eye story.

In 2022, the DGCA issued the highest number of commercial pilot’s licenses (CPLs) in a year in the last decade. Some 36 Flight Training Organisations train 1,000+ pilots every year. It takes close to Rs 1 crore (US$120,800) and more than three years to be equipped with a slew of licenses and certifications as a commercial pilot.

Elite job, great perks, and assured tenure, right?

In her wonderfully reported debut story today, Nuha Bubere tells a paradoxical tale of pilots’ training, a career that often fails to take off, and an increasingly antagonistic relationship with their airlines.

Such is the pilot crunch today that airlines are not just brazenly poaching but even using the idea of poaching to tame their own pilots. Some believe by 2024 end, things will stabilise. But in the turbulent aviation business, much more than financial stability is required to address what pilots are facing today.

An internal chat, accessed by The Ken, revealed Indigo pilots’ fatigue. They considered a newspaper ad but rejected the idea, suggesting an influencer make a video about the challenges.

Good Morning Ishan,

At work, some of us have a post-lunch ritual.

We walk up to a nearby cafe and grab a coffee, to go. A cold coffee tonic brew is my go-to beverage. Experimental coffee and being close to our workplace are what keep us going to this coffee chain every day.

Turns out, these reasons seem very important for Filipinos, too. Important enough that such chains can even become larger than Starbucks.

Coffee to Filipinos is what chai is to Indians. Four in five Filipinos drink over two cups of coffee daily.

And their taste in coffee is changing, thanks to the 2.5 million Filipinos deployed overseas for work, who are experiencing sophisticated and even experimental coffee.

That’s why even though American coffee players like Starbucks, Dunkin’, and The Coffee Bean & Tea Leaf are prominent in the Filipino market, there are also significant regional chains coming up, such as Zus. The Malaysian coffee chain’s store count more than doubled to 478 in 2023 alone, surpassing Starbucks in its home market.

You should read Isa’s story to know how Zus is drawing out a new league of coffee drinkers.

Meanwhile, in India, a Rs 15 discount is at the heart of discontent brewing at the Indian Railway Catering and Tourism Corporation (IRCTC), the e-ticketing arm of Indian Railways.

But you won’t be able to spot any signs of this.

The company posted its highest-ever operating revenue of Rs 4,270 crore for the year ended March 2024. Its profitability, too, is on the rise and its financials had nearly all the ingredients for what analysts would call “an exceptional performance.”

But still, the ticketing behemoth is hurting. All thanks to a five-year-old government mandate to promote digital transactions.

The government told the Railways arm to forgo a third of the revenue it earns per ticket whenever a customer chooses UPI for payments. As a result, it ends up offering a Rs 5 and Rs 10 UPI discount on a non-AC and AC ticket, respectively.

IRCTC was on board with this initially. But now, the rising volumes of UPI payments are squeezing it.

On average, the company receives 1.3 million ticket bookings a day. From one in every three users paying for their tickets through UPI during the year ended March 2023, two in five people do so now.

India’s zeal for incentivising UPI has cost so many businesses, from banks to payment companies to, now, even the government’s own IRCTC.

The ticketing vertical is IRCTC’s prized jewel, as it is the second largest revenue stream and rakes in a lot of profit. So, UPI is hurting it not just financially but also in other ways.

“We expected the government to understand this [the repercussions] once UPI’s share in e-tickets hit 40%,” a disappointed IRCTC official told Rounak, the author of today’s story. It’s a free read so do share it widely.

There’s almost always more than one way to do any task. Take today’s edition of Ka-Ching!. We could, of course, do the usual: I inform, you read, I conclude. 

Or I could tell you a story. 

Because I’ve already got a plot in mind, we’re going to go with the second option (heh!). 

It’s 2055, a space mission has just found evidence of ample potable water on Mars, and humans on Earth can’t wait to ship out to a new home. Because, you know, overpopulation and turning the planet of your birth into a choking oven can make you feel that way. 

So the world’s governments decide to help people settle on the tan rocks of the red planet. But first, to get things ready, they send in a bunch of entrepreneurs. Why entrepreneurs? Well, who’s going to set up all those must-have goods and services, right? 

Our protagonists, Bred and Broww, are among these select few pioneers. And they choose two very different ways of establishing their bases. 

Bred only wants to cater to the richest 1% and sets up a nice swanky base in southern Mars. Residents here will get fancy features and services, he says, like this app that categorises all their expenses, helps them pay bills, takes care of their vehicle needs, luxury travel escapades, etc, etc. 

Broww, on the other hand, isn’t really looking to attract the already affluent crowd. Instead, he wants the people eager to accumulate wealth. So he sets up the perfect base for driven, young professionals, with resources and tutorials that can help them invest and grow their wealth. All using his own awesome app, of course. 

Both bases, and businesses, start growing. Bred has relatively fewer occupants, but they are all quite rich. And Broww’s amenities, which help his residents make more money (in the short term, at least), attracts lots of immigrants. 

After a while though, both entrepreneurs start getting interplanetary letters. It’s the VCs from Venus. (Why are they from Venus? Ask Shubhankar Bhattacharya, who wrote a book in 2016 titled VCs are from Venus, Entrepreneurs are from Mars. For the purposes of my story, I’m just going to take him literally.)

The VCs want Bred and Broww to start cranking up their profits, because what they’re doing now isn’t enough. And just like in our real world, popular wisdom says one way you could do that is to start lending. So that’s what Bred and Broww do—they start offering loans to their residents. 

Initially, people expect Bred’s richer residents to have low demand for loans. And even when they do need a loan, they’d likely prefer going to a legit bank, right? But Bred persists, makes a bunch of assurances, offers an end-to-end experience with on-time reminders and other fancy features. And after some resistance, he starts finding takers. 

On the contrary, most people think Broww’s less wealthy residents would have a large appetite for credit, meaning better cashflow for the business. 

Except, it doesn’t quite turn out that way. 

And some of that, at least, is due to the way Broww built his base in the first place.

The right customer

I’m going to leave Bred and Broww to their fictional (and totally unbelievable) existence on Mars and switch back to Earth. Our real Earth. 

The stories of Indian fintechs Cred and Groww are quite similar to the one I just told you, including where they find themselves right now, according to two fintech executives I spoke to. 

Last month, Cred reported a 66% jump in revenue to Rs 2,473 crore (US$294 million) for the financial year ended March 2024, with its operating losses falling to Rs 609 crore (US$72.5 million) from about Rs 1,024 crore (US$122 million) in the previous financial year.

Lending has had a big role to play in this.

“Cred has scaled its revenue mostly because of lending. After initial difficulty, it has found a way to sell loans to its customer base.” 

First fintech executive

Meanwhile, Groww reported a net profit of Rs 297 crore (US$35.4 million) in the year ended March 2024—a 300% increase—while its overall revenue more than doubled to Rs 2,899 crore (US$345 million).

But to keep its profits ticking and growing, Groww is trying to ramp up its lending too. The fintech had first started offering loans in April 2023.

“The company is now turning to loans to keep the growth sustainable. It has accumulated a customer base of nearly 11 million, which is huge. Selling them loans will be a win-win for the company as that will lead to sticky customers and more revenue.”

First fintech executive

But the customer base Groww has so painstakingly built is actually turning out to be a bit of an issue. 

Both executives I spoke to say that while Zerodha, Groww’s closest competitor, focussed on attracting professional traders and investors, Groww bet on customers new to trading and investing. It carried out surveys and meetings across the country to understand the needs of young and new investors, and created engaging content to hand-hold them as they started out. As the numbers show, it definitely worked.

But now, Groww wants to ace the loans segment. And its average user doesn’t quite fit the bill:

“Groww’s customer base is new to credit. Cash flow is slightly irregular. [Average income per user is lower than Rs 50,000 per month.]”

Second fintech executive

Groww, according to my sources, is trying to fortify its lending business by investing in collection operations as well. This way, it can provide a full suite of services to partner banks and non-banks, who’ll actually be the ones extending loans to Groww’s customers. But even lenders are now weary of short-term, low-value borrowing originating via fintechs, most of which are personal, unsecured loans, inviting the spectre of India’s banking regulator too. 

So the “irregular cash flow” ascribed to Groww’s average user profile isn’t its only problem. 

But hey, is Groww anxious to make its lending plans work? 

The answer is likely both yes and no. Yes, because the market regulator has been squeezing F&O trading for a while now, and that segment was responsible for nearly 70% of Groww’s revenue. So finding an alternate is important. 

But at the same time, Groww is operationally profitable, so it has some time to figure out a way.

Long and Short
Wed, 26 Feb 25
The story behind the numbers and motivations driving India's stock market..
Good Morning Ishan,
Last Friday, Jio Financial Services—controlled by Mukesh Ambani, the country’s richest man—got a big bump-up. An intriguing promotion with big implications not just for itself but for millions of India’s mutual fund investors, too.
The country’s dominant index provider, NSE Indices, announced the stock’s entry into the Nifty 50 index by the end of March. The Nifty 50 is India’s premier large-cap bellwether representing the top 50 companies. Billions of dollars ride on it.
The power move has “big” written all over it.
Except, Jio Financial Services is still small.
But I am running ahead of myself.
First, some context.
Jio Financial Services isn’t walking alone into the exalted Nifty 50 club; it has food-delivery platform Zomato for company. On their way out to make way for these two are state-run oil refiner BPCL and biscuit-maker Britannia Industries.
These rejigs happen twice a year on the Nifty 50 and other indices; and at play here is something known as the free-float market capitalisation. Essentially, the total value of a company’s shares held by everyone except its promoters.
Here’s what NSE Indices says about these two inclusions and two exclusions.
“Zomato Ltd. (average free-float market capitalisation Rs 1,69,837 crore) and Jio Financial Services Ltd. (average free-float market capitalisation Rs 1,04,387 crore) have been included in Nifty 50 index as the 6-month average free-float market capitalisation of these companies within eligible universe is at least 1.5 times the 6-month average free-float market capitalisation of the smallest constituents i.e. Bharat Petroleum Corporation Ltd. (average free-float market capitalisation Rs 60,928 crore) and Britannia Industries Ltd. (average free-float market capitalisation Rs 64,151 crore) respectively.”
Only stocks that are available for trading in NSE’s futures and options segment are eligible for inclusion in Nifty 50.
Turns out, Jio Financial Services crossed this hurdle when the NSE included it in the F&O segment in November 2024.
Until now, Jio Financial Services and Zomato have been a part of the Nifty Next 50 index (also known as the Nifty Junior) that houses the top 51–100 stocks on the NSE, again on the basis of free-float market cap. The move next month to the Nifty 50 will mean a lot more money flowing into these stocks.
Several active funds (where fund managers try to outperform the index) and more importantly, a large number of passive funds (whose portfolios mirror the index they track) use Nifty 50 as a benchmark. If a stock forms part of an index, passive funds that track the index have to own the stock in the same weight. More passive money tracks the [Nifty 50](https://www.business-standard.com/finance/personal-finance/aum-of-index-funds-crosses-rs-2-lakh-cr-nifty50-dominates-with-70-share-124072600754_1.html) than any of the many other Indian indices.
Jio Financial Services could see buying inflows of Rs 3,128 crore and selling outflows of Rs 1,152 crore, according to IIFL Institutional Equities. Net-net, a positive that could see the stock get a leg-up.
But wait. Does Jio Financial Services even deserve to be part of the Nifty 50? It’s a curious case.
One, the company’s main business is not driving its market capitalisation, at least not yet. Rather, a chunk of its value flows from the over 6% stake it holds indirectly—through group entities—in its parent firm Reliance Industries, which incidentally is the numero uno on Nifty 50.
In a masterclass in deal structuring and financial engineering, that The Ken previously [wrote](https://the-ken.com/story/the-secret-to-jio-financials-13x-net-worth-boost-in-the-reliance-demerger/) about, Reliance carved out Jio Financial Services from itself in 2023 and got it listed that year. The benevolent parent had given its newest child a cool $12 billion (about Rs 1 lakh crore) gift in treasury shares—essentially, its own shares that Reliance earlier held in itself—to kickstart Jio Financial Services’ business.
As it stands, these shares currently have a value of over Rs 1 lakh crore compared with Jio Financial Services’ total market cap of about Rs 1.45 lakh crore. It follows that the treasury shares would have significantly bumped up the stock’s free-float market cap, too.
Two, Jio Financial Services, while it has big plans, is still small—at least relatively. In the December 2024 quarter, the company’s top line came in at about Rs 450 crore. But that pales in comparison to the revenues the current Nifty 50 constituents posted. The lowest revenue among these companies in the quarter was about Rs 4,500 crore, and the median revenue was about Rs 19,000 crore, according to data from market-information provider Screener.
Besides, nearly half of Jio Financial Services’ revenue in the quarter was from non-operational sources. The company and its related entities “are engaged in the business of investing and financing, leasing, insurance broking, payment bank, and payment aggregator and payment-gateway services,” said the notes to accounts. But these still seem fledgling. These businesses currently do not satisfy the quantitative thresholds laid down under the accounting standards on “operating segment”, the notes added.
Sure, the company’s earnings-call transcript [claims](https://www.bseindia.com/xml-data/corpfiling/AttachHis/160289af-6709-4cab-866d-9ddc128f6ecd.pdf) its lending business is growing, the insurance-broking business is seeing traction, and the payment bank is adding customers. That’s on a low base, though. Its mutual-fund joint venture with Blackrock, meanwhile, is awaiting a final nod from the market regulator to get going.
Interestingly, the company had no update on the [Rs 36,000 crore](https://the-ken.com/story/who-stands-to-gain-the-most-from-jio-financials-us4-3b-lease-deal-hint-not-jio-financial/) buy-and-lease telecom gear deal involving group companies Reliance Retail and Reliance Jio Infocomm. This deal announced last year was supposed to be executed over two years ending March 2026; one year is almost through.
The combination of a high market capitalisation (mainly due to the Reliance Industries treasury shares holding) and a low profit means that the Jio Financial Services stock trades at a princely price-to-earnings ratio of over 90X. This is much higher than the median 27X of the Nifty 50 companies now. Seems safe to say that the inclusion of Jio Financial Services, and the even pricier Zomato (that trades at a PE of over 300X) in the index would [peg up](https://www.livemint.com/market/stock-market-news/zomato-jio-financial-services-entry-to-make-nifty-50-more-expensive-here-s-why-11740376699637.html) this valuation metric by a notch or two.
Passive funds and their investors would likely end up buying a costlier index.
So, should indices like Nifty 50 open spots for stocks like Jio Financial Services? Shouldn’t index providers look beyond the free-float market cap criterion to decide on inclusions and exclusions? Shouldn’t the size of a company’s business be a factor, too?
Nearly four years ago, my colleague Seetharaman and I [wrote](https://the-ken.com/story/nse-bse-and-the-benchmark-blues/): “With the rise of passive funds, indices dictate where billions of dollars go. But the way they are designed could do with more rigour, even as Sebi is yet to make up its mind on regulating them.” A senior executive with a large fund house had argued that index providers should couple their metrics with qualitative analysis. Another senior executive with an index provider argued against it because “it’s not scalable and you want to institutionalise the process.”
Sebi did come out with a [regulatory framework](https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/markets/stocks/news/sebis-regulatory-framework-for-index-providers-comes-into-effect/articleshow/108397025.cms?from=mdr) for index providers last year.
But it likely didn’t factor in curveballs like Jio Financial Services.

Good Morning Ishan,
Every few months, healthcare in India is caught in one of its recurring deadlocks—between hospitals and insurers over who pays for what. Cashless services are suspended, disgruntled pronouncements are made, and insurance holders silently suffer through the fallout.
But what if hospitals and insurers were to sit on the same balance sheet?
That’s what the hospital group Narayana Health is attempting with its latest integrated offering of healthcare and insurance, Narayana One Health. It’s essentially a subscription-based service that offers access to a combination of neighbourhood clinics, insurance, and virtual doctor consultations.
It’s also the reason Viren Shetty, vice chairman of the group who is steering the integrated business, can so easily say, “Our plan is never to dispute ever. We don’t do claims adjudication; we just pass the bill.” A phenomenon almost unheard of in Indian healthcare.
But a year-and-a-half into the launch of Narayana One Health, the group is in for some brutal reality checks. “We had thought lakhs and lakhs of people would sign up, whereas we have just about 75,000,” says Shetty. The truth is that the group may understand healthcare, but they don’t know how to sell. And in insurance, the latter makes all the difference.
Is Shetty a naive innovator, a hubristic outsider, or just an idealist? And how far can idealism take him in a brutally cynical industry?

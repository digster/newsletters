"Dude, where's my customer?"
The $55 billion Indian auto industry grappled with this question for months before the pandemic. Last year saw the worst decline in sales in two decades. And the lockdown, imposed on 25 March, drove the sector deeper into a sinkhole.
Stores were shuttered, and inventory piled up. Dealers were saddled with 700,000 two-wheelers and 12,000-13,000 passenger vehicles they couldn't sell because these vehicles did not comply with the new emission norms that came into effect on 1 April. And even when the showrooms reopened later, potential buyers were wary of stepping into one.
So automakers and dealers had to find immediate digital fixes to get back on the road. After all, 65-70% of customers expect to finalise their car before visiting the showroom now, compared to 50-55% pre-Covid, according to a report dated July 2020 by Boston Consulting Group and Facebook.
Maruti Suzuki, India's largest passenger vehicle maker, identified 28 "touch points" and digitised between 17 to 21, depending on the dealers. Its rivals, such as Hyundai and Tata Motors, also unveiled portals that allow buyers to complete most steps of the purchase online. Hyundai's website, launched in April, got 20,000 enquiries and 1,900 bookings by July.
Dealers are also taking hitherto unexplored roads to gauge customer interest. They are listing their vehicles on classifieds platforms such as CarDekho and CarTrade, best known for used cars.

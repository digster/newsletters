What China’s cheap AI model tells us about India’s future. Research is a lottery. But is India even playing?

Here’s a hypothetical question. 

Could a startup of, say, three Indian engineers, with funding of, say, US$10 million build a large language model that’s as good as Open AI?

A couple of years ago, at an event https://www.youtube.com/live/AiE7FsdRzz8?si=lANs0yAl1H6TUfNp&t=2743 in Delhi, Sam Altman, CEO of Open AI was asked this exact question. 

Even by Altman’s usual standards, his answer was annoyingly disdainful. He responded in four sharp sentences, almost instantly.

“The way this works is we’re going to tell you, it’s totally hopeless to compete with us [Open AI] on training foundation models [and] you shouldn’t try. “

Then he switched to being patronising. 

“And it’s your job to try anyway. And I believe both of those things.”

There was awkward, embarrassed laughter in the room. Then finally, before he put down the microphone, Altman went back to being annoying.

“I think it is pretty hopeless”

Fun.

Anyway, here’s the big news from the world of AI last week.

A Chinese-built large language model called DeepSeek-R1 is thrilling scientists as an affordable and open rival to ‘reasoning’ models such as OpenAI’s o1.

These models generate responses step-by-step, in a process analogous to human reasoning. This makes them more adept than earlier language models at solving scientific problems and could make them useful in research. Initial tests of R1, released on 20 January, show that its performance on certain tasks in chemistry, mathematics and coding is on par with that of o1 — which wowed researchers when it was released by OpenAI in September.

“This is wild and totally unexpected,” Elvis Saravia, an AI researcher and co-founder of the UK-based AI consulting firm DAIR.AI, wrote on X.

China’s cheap, open AI model DeepSeek thrills scientists https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-025-00229-6, Nature

And how much did it cost to make this model that’s on par with Open AI?

DeepSeek hasn’t released the full cost of training R1, but it is charging people using its interface around one-thirtieth of what o1 [Open AI] costs to run. The firm has also created mini ‘distilled’ versions of R1 to allow researchers with limited computing power to play with the model. An “experiment that cost more than £300 with o1, cost less than $10 with R1,” says Krenn. “This is a dramatic difference which will certainly play a role in its future adoption.”

R1 is the part of a boom in Chinese large language models (LLMs). Spun out of a hedge fund, DeepSeek emerged from relative obscurity last month when it released a chatbot called V3, which outperformed major rivals, despite being built on a shoestring budget. Experts estimate that it cost around $6 million to rent the hardware needed to train the model, compared with upwards of $60 million for Meta’s Llama 3.1 405B, which used 11 times the computing resources.

Deepseek has become a global sensation. Altman came to India and loudly dismissed the notion of building an Open AI competitor with US$10 million as “hopeless”. A Chinese startup needed just US$6 million in training budget to come within striking distance of it. It’s one thing to beat the world’s biggest technology companies with a fraction of the resources available to them. But what makes the achievement even more staggering is the fact that Chinese startups like Deepseek could do it despite facing US export controls that limited their access to cutting-edge AI chips. Deepseek has also open-sourced considerable parts of its model, and it’s quite clear that they have pulled off something revolutionary. 

Last week, the world came to the realisation that it’s possible to build AI companies from scratch with a fraction of the investment that US tech companies have led us to believe. So either these tech companes know less about AI than they think, or they’ve been lying to us all along. 

One view suggests the former. Anonymous posts by insiders suggest that Deepseek’s achievements have forced a wave of inquiries at Silicon Valley tech companies about the actual resources required to build AI models. As one poster wrote https://www.teamblind.com/post/Meta-genai-org-in-panic-mode-KccnF41n/44121111, “every single ‘leader’ of a Gen AI organisation is making more than what it cost to train Deepseek v3 entirely, and we have dozens of such leaders”.

The least charitable view is that US tech companies like Open AI, Microsoft, Meta, and Google have pulled the wool over our eyes by pretending that building AI models from scratch was, in Altman’s words, “hopeless”. 

Maybe building cutting-edge AI systems is cheaper than we believed.

And this is why a lot of people are turning their eyes towards India.

It isn’t just Altman who believed that it wasn’t worth building foundational models in India. One person who is strongly against it is Nandan Nilekani, co-founder of Infosys. In December, just weeks before Deepseek released its model, he said, “Foundation models are not the best use of your money. If India has US$50 billion to spend, it should use that to build compute, infrastructure, and AI cloud. These are the raw materials and engines of this game.” 

It looks like Nilekani is wrong. 

India’s position with AI has always been, “Well, we are too late and this is expensive, so let the big boys build all the core stuff, and we’ll build the applications.” If you know nothing about AI, here’s an analogy—this is like choosing not to invest time and effort to build an operating system for smartphones, but taking Android and building apps on top of it. I get the temptation. Building use-cases is much less risky, and it’s easier to justify an investment. Nilekani is someone who has built India’s software service industry. He understands how to create value. 

However, now that Deepseek has shown us that things have changed, will India change its mind? 

I doubt it. 

And it’s not from a lack of trying. Here’s Aravind Srinivas, CEO of Perplexity, explaining what India got wrong.

The next day, Srinivas made an offer of US$1 million and five hours of his time a week to any startup that wanted to build a Deepseek like product from India. 

Personally, I have my reservations, both about Srinivas’s motivations and his methods. The barrier to building Deepseek in India is neither access to capital, nor technical expertise. An AI startup can raise a several million dollars over a cafe latte at 12th Main in Indiranagar, Bengaluru any day of the week. It can also easily access all the best talent both in the Valley and in India. Srinivas’s offer is buzzworthy, but I highly doubt it’ll amount to anything material. 

A long time ago, I used to be a research engineer building products using machine learning, so I have a point of view here borne out of firsthand experience. 

The thing that prevents India from building a Deepseek is how research is viewed, and consequently practised here. Socially and professionally, research projects are neither prioritised, funded, nor lauded. Deepseek’s success is considerable, but it’s probably one of the dozens and dozens of AI research projects that are going on in China. India has chosen to go into use-cases because researchers constantly face the question—“but what’s the point of doing this? What’s the application?”—all the time. So it’s much easier, and tempting professionally, to choose to build something that has some outcome and application than to spend time building something that may not go anywhere. 

Frontier models are built by those who are truly at the frontier. And those people aren’t the ones that work at VC-funded startups, but researchers. Research is uncertain by design. Researchers have no easy answers to questions like, “What’s the ROI of doing this?” There are no hockey stick curves. There’s no product-market-fit. It’s just a group of hardened people staying at the frontier, and the rest of us sending them supplies regularly, trusting that they’re doing the best they can.

I just don’t think we are ready for that, yet.

Last week, Thomas Fenn was in Bengaluru, sitting next to us at Spacebot Studios in Indiranagar. He had a giant instant camera with him—the Polaroid sort—with which he likes to “document” interesting moments in his daily life. He took a few photos of the group.

Oh yeah, the group. Along with Thomas in the studio was another restaurateur, Gaurav Saria, founder of Infinitea, India’s first exclusive chain of tearooms and stores. “A chef for life!” is what his Linkedin bio reads. The Bengaluru crowd will know him well.

Our third guest was Ramchander Raman, who was previously President of Cafe Coffee Day, handling its entire F&B business and also co-founder of Nucleus Kitchens, a startup which helps automate cloud kitchens.

We were all there to discuss 10-minute food delivery, specifically by Zomato and Swiggy through their new apps Bistro and Snacc. The discussion was pretty animated. Thomas is not just the co-founder of Mahabelly, a restaurant serving Kerala Cuisine, but he’s also part of the National Restaurants Association of India (NRAI) which is reportedly planning legal action against Zomato and Swiggy for launching these apps.

We spoke for nearly two hours, and right at the end, Gaurav glanced at his phone and held it out to the rest of us. He’d gotten an email from Deepinder Goyal, CEO of Zomato, which I’m guessing was sent to many of Zomato’s restaurant partners. Because Thomas got it too.

Gaurav proceeded to read out the letter live.

Tell me. I’d love to know. 
 
Meanwhile, in today’s edition, I thought I’d focus on something that has largely slipped by unnoticed over the last few months—India’s economy, and what lies ahead. 
 
Let’s dive in.
The developer's dilemma, by Kuznets
Sometime in the 1950s and the 1960s, an economist named Simon Kuznets developed a hypothesis. 
 
In its simplest form, Kuznets suggested that as countries develop and industrialise, a rise in income is accompanied by a rise in inequality. This increases to a point, after which nations continue to develop and inequality reduces. If you make a graph, it looks something like this.
Kuznets went even further, describing why this happens. His hypothesis was that developing countries attempt to address two goals: 
 
  • Countries want to do structural transformation because their economies move from agrarian-based economies to manufacturing or other high-productivity means of production. This leads to growth and increases income. 
     
  • However, this structural transformation leads to another effect— it increases inequality, all other things being equal. This is something that developing countries want to address, but any attempts to do this will bring down growth. 
 
Kuznets called this the developer’s dilemma—there’s a tension between growth and income inequality. And developing countries need to grapple with this deftly until they crossover and become a developed economy. 
 
Sometime during the week, I came across a fantastic working paper by Saon Ray and Sabyasachi Kar, who analyse this tension with a historical context in India. In it, they look at two distinct episodes of India’s post-independence history. Here’s what they found: 
 
  • The period from 1960 to 1980, when India emerged from colonial rule, was characterised by a reduction in income inequality, but with poor productivity.
     
  • The period from 1980 to 2010, when India went through liberalisation, saw incomes go up, but so did inequality. 
     
Ray and Kar call the first period ‘inclusion without growth’ and the second one as ‘growth without inclusion’. It’s a fascinating paper, and it goes into multiple factors, such as political factors, economic ideologies, and many more.
 
This got me thinking—ten years later, where does India stand today on both these aspects? What’s the tension like today? And who is winning?

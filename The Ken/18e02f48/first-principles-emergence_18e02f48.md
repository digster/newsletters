“Have you ever injured your right ankle?”
It was 2021 and I was at a new physiotherapist’s, frustrated at my chronically injured or inflamed left shoulder that would force me to suspend weight training every three-six months. My earlier physio was both well-known and friendly, but he had been unable to “permanently” fix my shoulder.
I was stunned at the question. Because I’d just spent the last 20 minutes explaining how my left shoulder and rotator cuff had been troubling me for years, to no respite.
After patiently listening to me, [Badri](https://www.linkedin.com/in/badrinathrao/) asked me to walk up and down his studio a few times. And then stand facing away from him at a distance.
And then he asked, “Have you ever injured your right ankle?”
Why yes I had, I told Badri. But years ago. Maybe seven-eight years ago when I twisted my right ankle badly while playing basketball. It had taken me nearly six months to heal.
But how on earth had Badri known to ask about my right ankle when I’d gone to him for help with my left shoulder? I felt like I was in an episode of House.
Because everything is connected, he replied. My earlier physiotherapist hadn’t done that great a job of restoring my right ankle to full health post injury, which left me with a slight imbalance that was imperceptible. But that small variation over weeks and months and years ended up creating an imbalance in my hip, which over years ended up creating an imbalance in my shoulder, which was the cause of my rotator cuff issues.
I was floored. But also in incredible agony as Badri proceeded to start fixing things by first “restoring” my right ankle’s mobility by virtually resetting all the muscles and ligaments in the lower part of my right leg.
“The force is there in this one,” he smiled and told his colleague after he was done and I did everything I could in my power to not scream out loud or punch him, due to the pain. “Well done, Luke,” he told me.rohin
“Thanks, master Yoda,” I muttered out through my pain. We still address each other as “Luke” and “Master”.
I used the example to illustrate the concept of a “system”. Because the bones, joints, muscles, and ligaments in my body are all interconnected and part of a complex system, an injury in my right ankle would manifest as an injury in my left shoulder in five years.
It was around the same time that I became fascinated with the subject of [Systems Thinking](https://medium.com/disruptive-design/tools-for-systems-thinkers-the-6-fundamental-concepts-of-systems-thinking-379cdac3dc6a).
“A cloud masses, the sky darkens, leaves twist upward, and we know that it will rain. We also know the storm runoff will feed into groundwater miles away, and the sky will clear by tomorrow. All these events are distant in time and space, and yet they are all connected within the same pattern. Each has an influence on the rest, an influence that is usually hidden from view. You can only understand the system of a rainstorm by contemplating the whole, not any individual part of the pattern.
Business and other human endeavors are also systems. They, too, are bound by invisible “fabrics of interrelated actions, which often take years to fully play out their effects on each other. Since we are part of that lacework ourselves, it’s doubly hard to see the whole pattern of change. Instead, we tend to focus on snapshots of isolated parts of the system, and wonder why our deepest problems never seem to get solved. Systems thinking is a conceptual framework, a body of knowledge and tools that has been developed over the past fifty years, to make the full patterns clearer, and to help us see how to change them effectively.”
The Fifth Discipline: The Art & Practice of The Learning Organization—by Peter M. Senge
And one of the most fascinating concepts of Systems is Emergence, best explained by the late theorist and futurist, Buckminster Fuller: “There is nothing in a caterpillar that tells you it’s going to be a butterfly.”
You could spend a lifetime studying an individual water molecule and never deduce the precise hardness or slipperiness of ice. Watch a lone ant under a microscope for as long as you like, and you still couldn’t predict that thousands of them might collaboratively build bridges with their bodies to span gaps. Scrutinize the birds in a flock or the fish in a school and you wouldn’t find one that’s orchestrating the movements of all the others.
Nature is filled with such examples of complex behaviors that arise spontaneously from relatively simple elements. Researchers have even coined the term “emergence” to describe these puzzling manifestations of self-organization, which can seem, at first blush, inexplicable. Where does the extra injection of complex order suddenly come from?
How Complex Wholes Emerge From Simple Parts, [Quanta Magazine](https://www.quantamagazine.org/emergence-how-complex-wholes-emerge-from-simple-parts-20181220/)
Take our conversation right now. There was nothing in August 2022 (when I released the [very first episode of the First Principles podcast](https://the-ken.com/podcasts/first-principles/kabeer-biswas-dunzo/) with Kabeer Biswas) that would lead me (or you) here in March 2024.
And yet, here we are.
This is emergence. And the System is all of us. Me, you, The Ken, the numerous founders and CEOs I spoke with for my podcasts, and the hundreds of thousands of other readers and subscribers.
In 2022, when I launched the First Principles podcast I did not know I would also launch a First Principles newsletter.
I did not know in May 2023 when I wrote [the first edition of this newsletter](https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/a-new-sunday-newsletter-from-the-ken/) that we would also end up having sections on Silent Sunday photos, books, music playlists, or habits.
I did not know that through the simple act of exchanging emails with many of you, a community would evolve.
I did not know that we’d start using the scale and diversity of our community to make this a truly many-to-many learning and discovery project on Sundays, instead of just one-to-many. [Quests](https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/first-principles-introducing-fp-quests/) is just one aspect of that.
And I admit, I do not know what we will be doing six months from now, or in two years. I’m happy to let that reality “emerge”.
But if emergence is unpredictable and random, what is even the point of discussing it, you might be wondering. You have a point.
Emergence, by its very nature, cannot be planned and created. But we can create systems that are conducive to emergence. Conversely, when you’re planning something new and ambitious, place it in the context of the larger systems it operates in.
- Interconnectedness—how is it connected to other elements (people, teams, products, etc.) of the system? What does it draw from others and what does it provide?
- Feedback loops—what feedback loops have you built that will allow you to frequently understand how things are going? So you know how and what to tweak.
- Self-organisation—does the success of your project depend on you or a few other people, or are many others adequately incentivized and empowered to come together and help in its success?
- Experiments—is it possible to do smaller experiments and learn from them, before scaling them?
- Collaboration—are you incentivizing the elements of the system to collaborate? Or are you merely hoping and praying they will?
- Sustainability—it’s easy to launch new products but far harder to sustain them over time. How are you ensuring that the system will sustain
Another way to think about healthy systems that are conducive to emergence is via the 4Ps I mentioned when we started.
“In fact, the principle that might be closest to the core of the Media Lab’s mission isn’t listed here at all, though you will find it running through every chapter of the book. It’s the notion of putting learning over education. Learning, we argue, is something you do for yourself. Education is something done to you. The pedagogical spirit of the Media Lab owes much to Mitch Resnick, whose mentor, Seymour Papert, helped start the Lab. Resnick runs the Lifelong Kindergarten research group, and his dedication to what he calls the “four Ps” of creative learning—Projects, Peers, Passion, and Play—animated much of the spirit behind this book. We firmly believe that for the principles to find a fertile soil in the years ahead, our educational system must embrace some of the same philosophy.”
From Whiplash: How to Survive Our Faster Future, by Joi Ito
Projects, Peers, Passion, and Play are very strong elements of The Ken’s culture. Not because we knew these starting out, but because we made enough (and more!) mistakes along the way that told us lasting success would not be possible without most of these.
For instance, if we find that a product or project is being helmed by someone who does not enjoy it, or is not seeking to build expertise in it, we would rather kill such a project. We’ve done it [numerous](https://the-ken.com/bfo/) [times](https://the-ken.com/podcasts/cost-to-company/a-closing-note/).
Similarly, we “force” collaboration on people and projects, so that it simultaneously increases their chances of success while also strengthening the bonds that make up our system.
When healthy systems evolve, whether they be our bodies, our careers or our organisations, they often give rise to unpredictable magic. Sometimes we call it serendipity.

Hi everybody, and happy Sunday! This is Anushka again—whose messy, all-over-the-place bookshelf has debuted on Silent Sunday this week. Frankly, a younger version of me might have baulked at my bookshelf now; she liked arranging her books by genre, height, and even the colour of the spines. But now, I like to keep the unread books right up top, so that even a cursory glance at the shelf reminds me: GET THROUGH YOUR READING LIST!
Speaking of reading lists, this is an interesting one. Last week, Rohin was telling us that his way of getting through his TBR list (to-be read, for the non-Goodreads crowd) is to add books that he wants to read to his Amazon cart on the regular, and then, when he opens his cart to buy something, he can either buy the book or discard it altogether. A very do-or-die approach to a reading list, in my opinion, but truly effective (or so he’s convinced us).
I have friends who have long TBRs in excel sheets, but still find it hard to complete them. And then, there are folks who simply pick up a book after a light scan of the blurb (without checking any ratings or reviews!) and get through it, essentially creating and demolishing their TBR as they go.

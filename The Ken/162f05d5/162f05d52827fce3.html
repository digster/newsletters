The future for them never looked brighter in India. But the more things look different, the more they seem the same
Good Morning Ishan,
 
Here’s wishing you a great Monday right out the gate. If YOLO is the 4-letter acronym that describes the millennial generation, the equivalent one among businesses is FOMO. Fear Of Missing Out.
 
It’s a powerful motivator. You may be coasting along just fine - profitable, stable, innovative - but then come along a bunch of competitors, sorry, “disruptors”. Many of who might be loss-making, cash burning and confused. But they’re all confident that there’s a massive Internet-linked pot of winner-takes-all gold at the end of the tunnel.
 
That’s how FOMO starts.
 
Visa and Mastercard have been in India for over 20 years. From 1996 to 2016, they managed to issue 600 million debit cards (and 30 million credit cards). But only 10% of those were active, and just 0.5% of the user base was incrementally using it every year on a regular basis for making payments.
 
Then comes along Demonetisation. With 86% of India’s currency invalidated overnight, many inactive debit card users (naturally) turned active overnight. That 5% number jumped to 14%. Visa and Mastercard were ecstatic. Their HQs, up till now lukewarm to India as a market (only 4% of their revenue comes from India), became believers.
 
But from the ashes of Demonetisation’s dust was also born UPI - a free peer-to-peer payments competitor promoted by the government and supported by all banks. UPI is doing nearly a billion transactions a year now.
 
So now, the two companies are stuck in the unenviable space of wanting to cement their unexpected overnight growth while staving off the impending irrelevance of “cards” in a world of digital money. Woe is them.

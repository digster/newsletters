Good Morning Ishan,
 
1. To survive. 
 
Even though nobody thought BSNL would or should survive. India’s 4th largest telco has been bleeding market share, customers and money for nearly a decade. Its accumulated losses are over Rs 90,000 crore (~$13 billion). Last year its telecom revenue dropped 22%, and losses climbed to $2 billion. But even after that, it has 117 million mobile subscribers, 9.8 million fixed-line and 8.51 million broadband subscribers.  
 
2. Because he who survives lives to fight (a duopoly) another day.
 
And the government really, really, really wants BSNL to fight another day. Because the rapid implosion of India’s telecom sector, spurred on by the imposition of a massive $14 billion backdated license fee, risks turning it into a duopoly with Reliance Jio as #1 and Bharti Airtel as #2. Vodafone-Idea at #3 could decide to fold up if it sees no path to profits in the future. Unlike Softbank-funded companies, telecom companies must make profits. In such a scenario, the Indian government wants BSNL to become a viable #3 player.
 
3. That means selling your family silver.
 
In BSNL’s case, that means selling land and buildings across the length and breadth of India. While its own valuation of these assets is close to $10 billion, experts value it at close to $28 billion. BSNL’s staff values just the land it owns in India’s metro cities at $43 billion. Problem solved?
 
4. Not in a bear market.
 
Real estate developers are struggling to sell the properties they’ve already built and service the massive debts they’ve already raised. So even if BSNL can sell some of its land assets, the prices may not be what it expects.
 
5. Turning around loss-making organisations is hard. Turning around loss-making and state-run organisations is hard^2.

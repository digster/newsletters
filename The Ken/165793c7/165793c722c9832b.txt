Good Morning Ishan,

Who doesn’t want to lose weight? And who doesn’t know keeping it off is hard? I mean, how often in a day do you go, ‘I can resist anything but temptation’?

I do it all the time. I think we are in this together. After all, 1 in 5 Indians, nearly 250 million people, are considered obese or overweight. A lot of it is self-inflicted, but an awful lot is beyond one’s control, perhaps needing intervention. (Many years ago, an Indian doctor, now in Pune, CS Yajnik, discovered the ‘thin-fat’ Indian baby phenomenon—for the same or lower birth weight, Indian babies have more fat than Caucasians.)

But it’s Johnson & Johnson (J&J) which gets credit for creating awareness. Since 2004, J&J has jumped through one hoop after another to create a market of 20,000 bariatric surgeries conducted each year in India. This, from next to nothing.

A few years ago, they struck gold when a panel of experts convinced the health ministry to include bariatric surgery in the Central Government Health Scheme (CGHS).

Next was insurance. A few insurers have bitten the bullet; many more are evaluating it. The insurance regulator held a meeting this month to consider if this surgery should be included in insurance policies or not.

The patient selection criteria under CGHS reduced the fear of its misuse, but the data emerging after 10 years of use in India sends mixed signals. What’s more, if weight loss surgery was an insurance against renal/diabetes complications, finance minister Arun Jaitley’s surgery four years ago, followed by a renal transplant recently is bad publicity.

Ruhi’s story today is about how J&J created this market in India, surgeon by surgeon. The multinational may be in the eye of a storm today over faulty hip implants, but weight loss is a different ballgame altogether.

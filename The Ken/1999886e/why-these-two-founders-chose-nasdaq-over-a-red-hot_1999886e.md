Good Morning Ishan,
India might have the hottest IPO market right now, with investment bankers assigning an “India premium” to stocks, but that doesn’t mean companies are rushing to domestic bourses.
There’s a rule that prevents promising companies from going public in India: pre-revenue businesses are not allowed to list, even if they have strong intellectual property. Just ask SS Innovations International, a medical-technology developer that delivers a robot-assisted surgical system every week for as low as one-third the cost of the premier Da Vinci setup. It’s been listed on the Nasdaq since April, eschewing India’s stock exchanges.
The same goes for 15-year-old biotech firm, Vyome Therapeutics, which completed a reverse merger with a US-based healthcare company in August. Those in charge at Vyome had even considered planting the company’s flag on a bourse in India, but investment bankers talked them out of the notion because it wasn’t clear that Vyome held appeal among public investors.
Seema spoke with the founders of both SS Innovations and Vyome, as well as others who explained why some Indian companies are heading overseas. It’s a counterintuitive situation, given the torrent of IPOs in India right now.

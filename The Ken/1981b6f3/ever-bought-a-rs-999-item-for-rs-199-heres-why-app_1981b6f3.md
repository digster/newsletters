Good Morning Ishan,
In 2017, the ‘nudge theory’ got global spotlight. That was when Richard Thaler won the Economics Nobel for his work on this quirky strand of behavioural economics. The theory says that human beings often need encouragement or intervention—a nudge—to do what’s best for themselves or for the greater good. Among the most cited examples of a nudge is etching the image of a fly in men’s room urinals at Amsterdam’s Schiphol Airport to “improve the aim” and reduce cleaning costs.
It was not just airports, governments, and bureaucrats that took notes. Consumer-tech platforms across the world, including in India, drew their lessons too. Just that these platforms used the idea for their own good, blurring the thin line between nudge and manipulation. And the “dark pattern” became mainstream.
From clickbait to guilt trips to fake scarcity to hidden charges, dark patterns are now baked into India’s tech platforms. If you bought a Rs 999 item for Rs 199, you might just have walked into the dark pattern parlour. It’s not a bug any more, but the feature. And almost everyone—from Zepto to Swiggy to Amazon—is in the game. Consumer platforms can’t help themselves. As Farheen, the contributor-author of today’s story writes, dark patterns are built into their unit economics. A necessary evil, if you will, amid tough business conditions and big losses.
But the government, amid pressure from consumers, is no longer in a mood to ignore or countenance this. It recently read these platforms—including the likes of Zomato, Ola Electric, Google, and Amazon—the riot act, warning them to mend their ways by September. But will anything change? Can it even? Read Farheen’s [sharp story](https://the-ken.com/story/ever-bought-a-rs-999-item-for-rs-199-heres-why-apps-cant-stop-using-dark-patterns/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter) today to find out whether there will be light at the end of the dark-patterns tunnel.

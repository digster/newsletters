Universities in India are gradually losing state funding.

This is how severe it is: India’s University Grants Commission wields Rs 1,000 crore less than it did 10 years ago, a 23% drop. It’s a consequence of moving away from grants and issuing loans through the Higher Education Financing Agency instead.

That means even when schools receive grant money, a portion of it is used to repay the principal amount. In some cases, what’s left isn’t enough to support the original goals, like constructing new buildings as part of campus expansions.

To keep the cash flowing, universities have a Plan B.

Most IITs have set up limited companies and hired full-time staff to raise funds from affluent alumni and other donors, then allocate that money into hedge funds, private equity, venture capital, and other asset classes. Other institutions are hopping on the bandwagon too.

We already know that this works. In the US, institutions like Harvard, Yale, and MIT each maintain multi-billion-dollar endowments. These amounts can be so large that they divide out to be worth seven figures for each enrolled student.

The downside is this doesn’t work for every school. Institutions with name recognition manage to build large endowments, but others have trouble getting started.

Atul’s debut story with The Ken explores how India’s institutions of higher education are adapting with Section 8 companies. Check out his article to see how your alma mater might be faring.

India’s sovereign wealth fund, NIIF, just made its biggest exit. It sold Ayana Renewables to a joint venture of state-backed companies. Which sounds like a normal private-equity exit, except the buyer is also the government. So is this an actual exit, or just the state moving money from one pocket to another? ONGC, which knows a lot about oil but not much about solar power, now owns a green-energy platform. What will it do with it? Meanwhile, JSW Energy wanted to buy Ayana but lost out—was that just business, or was the deal always meant to stay in friendly hands?

In this week’s Long and Short, Seema breaks down the deal, the valuation puzzle, and what it really means for the sovereign fund in this exit.

Welcome back to this morning habit we share. Like you, all of us at The Ken are grateful to have made it to the other side. I hope the trying Covidian times, the whiplash news cycle, and the radical uncertainty are behind us.

Schools and colleges have begun to reopen. However, education won’t be the same again. It wasn't just the VC-backed edtechs, which had a breakout year in 2020. Even state-backed education broke out of its mould and inertia to experience an acceleration in its make-shift digital classrooms that mustn’t decelerate now.

Online education went from being mere remedial ‘supplements’ to the main fare for over 285 million students in India. Edtech companies raised an impressive $2.2 billion; most of them adding millions of free users.

We saw release after release on the countless ‘minutes’ students spent on these platforms. Important as digital education has proven to be in the pandemic, a more important question must now be addressed: Does online learning actually work?

Consumers haven’t asked this. VCs will never ask this because downloads, views, and user experience are the metrics they value. Companies, for their part, have chased the proxy metric—engagement. But engagement ≠ learning.

Who then will bell the cat?

A motley group of non-profit organisations, state education departments and government teachers have been running a free, low-tech, and public version of edtech with support from Google. Its viewership numbers even rival those of Byju’s or Unacademy. In collaboration with Jameel Action Poverty Lab (J-PAL), an international research organisation, and via randomised controlled trials, the public edtech is trying to ask the inescapable question: Does online education impart learning?

Olina has been tracking edtech with sage- and sleuth-like dedication. She argues that maybe, just maybe, this could be the start of a phase when customers would finally ask for the right metrics before they pay up to download edtech apps.

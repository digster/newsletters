I don't think anyone enters journalism with ambitions of being on the edit desk. I certainly didn't. For me, the allure of journalism was reporting—the sexiness of uncovering hidden truths, questioning the system, and a dozen other cliches that come from watching too many movies and TV shows.

Some journalists, I'm sure, consciously shift to editing. It's a great move career-wise if you eventually want to head a newsroom or a media house, even. I transitioned for a reason that's far more base, though no less pertinent. Reporting is exhausting. Don't get me wrong, editing is tiring, invisible, and often thankless work. But it's also stable—the work comes to you each day.

Reporting, on the other hand, involves plunging headlong into the unknown, week after week. Finding a story, gathering sources, convincing them to speak, corroborating their information, parsing documents, and finally having your story scrutinised by thousands of readers, many of whom are hardwired to assume the worst about you.

Yeah, hard pass.

Every week, though, I'm amazed as our writers voluntarily enter this whirlpool, digging deep to tell some amazing stories. This week was a great example of how that hard work pays off.

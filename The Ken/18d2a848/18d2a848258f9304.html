Good morning. While much of north India continues to experience bone-chilling cold weather, we down south are already transitioning out of winter. The days have started to get pretty warm. I shudder to think what summer will be like this year, especially because we don’t own an air conditioner (AC). 

 

It used to be that Bengaluru didn’t need air conditioning. That, alas, is no longer true. But it is true that peak summer in Bengaluru is roughly 45 days. So, if you can get through that without an AC, you can get through the entire year without an AC. 

 

Each year I wonder, will this be the year that we capitulate? Or will we add another year to our unbroken no-AC “streak” (currently 23 years). Personally, I’d love to get to the quarter-century mark, but our teenager would vehemently disagree. 

 

“No air conditioning builds character,” I tell him. “I have enough,” he tells me.

1. 10- to 20-year journeys

 

One of my favourite mental prisms with which to see the world is “goals versus journeys”. When we’re young, whether as individuals or as organisations, our default prism is the goal.

 

The next job title. The latest car. The fancy house. The next fund-raise. The next valuation.

 

But, as we grow older, most of us begin to realise that immediate goals lose their charm after a while. There are only so many times we can motivate ourselves by climbing another rung on the ladder that we’ve been on for years.

 

And as we realise this, we begin to wonder if there really was something to that most cliched of interview questions, “What do you want to be in 10 years?”

 

It’s another matter to dole out a prepared answer to an interviewer in the hopes of landing a better job, but we can’t fool ourselves as easily.

 

Thinking about what we want to be in 10 years is hard. Because when time spans get lengthened to decades, goals lose their meaning.

 

If you multiply your salary by, say, 10, will that be enough to convince yourself of the future that is 10 years ahead? No. Like I said, numerical goals lose their meaning over longer time periods.

 

Then, there is also the fact that we as human beings are terrible at being able to visualise growth over the long term. We underestimate it. While overestimating the importance of the short term.


You might have heard of this bias either as “Amara’s Law” or “Gates’ Law”.

“Most people overestimate what they can achieve in a year and underestimate what they can achieve in ten years.”

 

It’s unclear exactly who first made that statement, when they said it, or how it was phrased. The most probable source is Roy Amara, a Stanford computer scientist. In the 1960s, Amara told colleagues that he believed that “we overestimate the impact of technology in the short-term and underestimate the effect in the long run.” For this reason, variations on that phrase are often known as Amara’s Law. However, Bill Gates made a similar statement (possibly paraphrasing Amara), so it’s also known as Gates’s Law.


Gates’ Law: How Progress Compounds and Why It Matters, Farnam Street Blog

There is also the fact that we, as human beings, are also bad at visualising or imagining exponential growth. Blame evolution.

 

This is why we struggle to visualise 10- to 20-year horizons in terms of goals. And that’s okay.


But what if we viewed it as a journey instead? I once asked this question to Naveen Tewari, the co-founder and CEO of two companies valued in the billions, Inmobi and Glance.

Me: You’re 44 today. What advice would you give to a younger 20-something you that might possibly have allowed that 20-something to get to where you are faster?

 

Naveen: It’s an interesting question. Would I start earlier, maybe. Would I have started on this journey a little earlier, maybe I would say that, would I change much about this journey?

 

There is a part of me which wants to change some of these things where I failed.

 

But I also know those are the reasons why we get to where we are today.

 

I fretted too much on success, you know, being better than the other company around me, etc. etc. etc. to begin with. I don’t do that anymore.

 

This is not a game of competition. This is a thing about being generational. So you can be faster, slower, it doesn’t really matter. If you do it correctly, you will create a gigantic impact in the world.

 

Who cares about three or four years here and there, in this whole picture. So I think we are being very myopic with time as a factor, because the world of startups is driven by venture capitalists who have a life cycle of 7 years.

 

So we think in those, you know, 7 to 10 years lifecycle. But for people who are building good companies, you don’t have to do that. You can just be at it.

 

Me: By 7- to 10-year cycles, you mean that’s the time they (VCs) have to deploy their money and to get an exit and to return that money. And therefore, the companies that they invest in have to operate at a subset of that cycle?

 

Naveen: That is correct. Therefore, they generally are not sitting there and saying, “Hey, can you build for 20 years?”

 

Because when you think about building for 20 years, when you are a 25-year-old, or a 30-year-old…

 

Me: It does not process.

 

Naveen: It does not process. You have no idea, like, if to a 30-year-old me, you know, somebody said, “Hey, build for 20 years and here’s how you should think about it”.

 

That would have been so different. And it doesn’t process because there is no one who is sitting you down and telling you that. And you know, for example, most of my conversation with people today is to say, grow slow.


Inmobi founder Naveen Tewari gets candid about survival, innovation, and playing the game by changing the rules, Episode 4 | 22 September, 2022

When we think about our future as a pleasurable and rewarding journey instead, we start optimising for the right things. Who we are. What gives us joy. What makes us better. The people—friends or colleagues—we choose to spend it with.

 

Getting to the destination becomes a mere by-product.

 

I asked Naveen about goals and journeys too.

Me: Are you a goals- or a journey-motivated person?

 

Naveen: Used to be goals, and now it’s journey.

 

Me: What were some of your earlier goals like and what does your journey look like now?

 

Naveen: You know, I actually didn’t know the first goal was to raise a lot of money. You did, and then you realise that, wait a second, that I achieved it, but why am I not happy?

 

Then you go to the next one and you achieve that and you go to the next one. So you keep achieving these goals and you realise you’re on a treadmill. And so you’re never happy with any goal.

 

And one could say, look, not being happy with any goal is a great motivation to continue to create bigger and bigger goals, but not living happily is not a great place to be at.

 

And so today, I think the journey puts you in a very good place to be able to make decisions without fear of failure, without fear of being judged, without fear of proving yourself.

 

And therefore you actually, on the contrary, make bigger decisions because you’re just part of this journey and you’re not trying to get to a goal because when you try to get to a goal, you optimise very differently.

 

The human mind works to reduce failures versus increase the chances of success. And I think over a period of time as that has shifted to our journey, it’s far more pleasant and apparently it’s far bigger.

Being journey-motivated does not mean you have to give up on goals. Most of us will still have goals in the short- to medium-term.

 

But when we frame the next 10-20 years of our career or life as a journey, we no longer have the pressure to attain something in an unreasonable time frame. We can give ourselves six months or two years or even five years.

 

Thinking about our future 10-20 years is a good counterbalance. “Is this the path I want to travel or am I merely on it?"

4. #FPPlaylist—“The Zone”

 

We have a new, energizing playlist for you this Sunday, curated by suggestions from the First Principles Community and our colleagues at The Ken. The theme is “The Zone”—you know, the feeling of being in a completely and utterly focussed mindspace? Very often, especially when immersed in a creative activity, this is also referred to as the “flow state”. Read: fully energised and involved in the activity you’re doing, and most importantly, enjoying it. 

 

I always imagine a writer at a typewriter, stuck on a page, and then suddenly finding the words flowing out of them to the beat of the typewriter’s click clack click clack.

 

When you’re in it, “The Zone” is incredibly beautiful. But it’s equally difficult to get into, which is why a lot of us have a routine—a particular place, a particular time of the day, and of course, a particular kind of music—to get into it. This playlist, I think, is a perfect starting point if you’re looking for the right music.

Everyone here has a style. Mine's rarely nostalgia.

But it would be disingenuous to not give in today, on Diwali, only one of the biggest festivals in India.

The truth is, attending a north Indian school filled with Hindu kids meant it was either Diwali or Holi that one swore by. Yeah, despite my name, I always looked forward to the festival of lights when my namesake goddess wrapped up her festivities in the very Bengali neighbourhood I grew up in.

Holi got too rowdy for my liking most years. But Diwali—and we've celebrated a silent, cracker-free one at ours for years—meant family time, and a pushy grandma who wanted us (my sister and me) to stay home and light all diyas before we went gallivanting with our respective friends.

I'd like to believe the festival means something different to everyone—to my dear sis it meant dread for it often clashed with her birthday. To me, it meant families, often busy with work and other responsibilities, brought together with food, light, laughs and wrapping/unwrapping gifts.

I'll circle back to gifts, but let me quickly do the recap I'm here for.

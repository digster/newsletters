Once upon a time, I was in Gurugram meeting the founder of a large company in his large, fancy two-storied buzzing office. The rectangular meeting room had a white-board and comfortable chairs. After exchanging pleasantries, the founder got up and drew a straight line on the board. At one end, he wrote onion, at the other end, a house. And then he said something like, from Rs 10 a kg to Rs 1 crore, everything will be sold online. Starting today and in the next ten years. I’m solving for starting at Rs 10. And then he began adding, milk, washing powder, soap, cola to the line...a lot of water has flown under the bridge since then, and that founder is doing his best to save whatever remains of his business.
 
Delivering grocery to your doorstep at a price where a business can make money is tough business. Simply put, the last four years have seen a wave of businesses high on venture capital money, hoping to steal you away from your nearest kirana store. They’ve tried every trick in the book, and they are still at it. But early this year when Amazon entered the business, with Amazon Now, there was hope that hmmm, maybe Amazon will be able to bring in some semblance of a strategy.  
 
That’s not how it has panned out. My colleague Payal who writes on e-commerce says the Amazon team has been told to rationalise expenses. Today’s story talks about the myriad challenges of the grocery delivery business and also how Amazon sees this business going forward. 
 
You can read Payal’s piece here: https://the-ken.com/amazon-now-maybe-much-later/
 
I found this bit from Saurabh Srivastava, director of consumables and FMCG at Amazon India very interesting. “We do not want to restrict Amazon Now to grocery. It is about urgent shopping needs and we want to grow it to Prime Now service in the US.”

The chance to right the lopsided economics around medical education
Edition #40. Monday, 25 May 2020
Someone sent you this? Sign up here
Good morning,
 
Nothing like a pandemic to boost our medical vocabulary, huh? Well, today’s edition sticks to that theme—of narrative violation. Or more precisely, the chance to right the lopsided economics around medical education, especially as an imminent baby boom is set to increase the healthcare burden and wreak havoc on women’s rights. All while the ill effects of our current sedentary lifestyle accumulate.
Narrative Violation 

Seema
 
It has a flavour-of-the-season ring to it. Narrative violation was 2019’s “VC bingo” buzzword.
A narrative violation…is a way to express an idea that goes against conventional wisdom. It is a friend of “well, actually,” and a distant cousin to the devil’s advocate. It has been used as an investment framework, a conversational shortcut and, in at least one case, a defining personal identity trait.
What makes a narrative violation, The New York Times
In 2020, it’s the catchphrase to describe how India must overhaul its healthcare. 
 
On 23 May, think tank Niti Aayog, India’s policy vending machine, brought out yet another (quick) fix:
The NITI Aayog has asked states to accelerate the process of setting up of medical colleges on PPP model and augmenting district hospital facilities with private partners. In a communication to chief secretaries of all states, a NITI official has also advised them to use the facility of Viability Gap Funding (VGF), available for social sectors, for this purpose.
Speed up medical colleges under PPP model, The Indian Express
Even before the pandemic blew a hole in India’s decrepit health infrastructure, a High Level Group (HLG) on healthcare to the 15th Finance Commission (FFC) had been in place. It made many recommendations. Last Thursday, the group reconvened.
“There is an immediate need to reassess the requirement of health manpower (medical and paramedical) and estimated requirement of resources for 2021-22 to 2025-26 in relation to the health infra-gap (hospital infrastructure, medical equipment, PPE, etc.). Further, the mechanism to fund these requirements will need to be critically examined, including the enhanced role of the private sector," the FFC said in a statement.
Covid-19: Finance Commission asks for recommendations from health expert group, Mint
After that meeting, FFC chairman Nand Kishore Singh didn’t say much, except that the “outlay for the health sector has to be raised significantly [from the current 0.9% of GDP]”, besides increasing availability of doctors and paramedics in rural areas. Whatever transpired during the Thursday meeting will go into the Commission’s report due in October, which will become the foundation for healthcare reforms. The HLG already has suggestions for the supply side—more doctors, more hospitals (3,000-5,000), shorter courses for doctors, and so on. More of the same? (The demand management needs a tectonic shift, but that’s another story altogether.)
 
Seat skews
 
For a very long time, India has allowed artificial scarcity in medical human resources to loom over its healthcare delivery, driven in equal parts due to poor regulation, poor resource allocation, and over reliance on private colleges. Medical professionals are produced not by the actual need but by the job market diktat. 
 
Look at the plight of dentists. The number of permitted seats in bachelors programmes has only increased by about 1,280 in the five-year period from 2013-14 to 2018-19.
Source: Factly.in
For some years, a state like Bihar in the north had ONE dental seat in MDS (Masters in Dental Science), according to recent graduates.
 
It’s a skew economists have long warned about, advocating that healthcare cannot be left to market forces alone. 
 
If dentistry is at one extreme of the demand curve, radiology is at the other. A masters or an MD seat in Radiology in a private college or a deemed university today costs Rs 3-4 crore. Imagine a medical graduate who pays this kind of money to get a master’s degree, what would be her goal after two years of education? 
 
To serve patients or recover that investment? Which is why the first thing many of them do is set up X-ray or imaging centres. Which is why X-rays are prescribed left, right, and centre in urban India. Which is why you undergo a front and back chest X-ray for a dry cough when a throat swab can do the job in many cases. It’s economics.
 
Ironically, if there’s one discipline that is being hugely aided by artificial intelligence, it’s radiology. Technology, therefore, must disrupt radiology education. But it doesn’t in the real sense. Young doctors say radiology is coveted because you don’t have to deal with patients, you only look at images. 
 
I hope what Dr K. Srinath Reddy suggests in his book “Make Health in India” reaches the Finance Commission. (Dr Reddy is also a member of the HLG).
Source: Down to Earth
He suggests the “creation of a National Commission for Human Resources in Health to provide overarching help to all categories”.
 
Fixing the supply side also needs a reduction in the cost of medical education. A narrative violation that the global Massive Open Online Courses (MOOC) movement has missed so far. 
 
Something simple like digitising medical school textbooks, which have always been awfully expensive, hasn’t been attempted. There is no reason why students should buy these and sit in the classrooms to read them. As a well-meaning hospital owner said to me recently:
 
“You can keep all this online, make it free, and let the training happen on ground at the hospital. This has immediate value. It’s also good value for technology, something that the country needs.”
 
Digital health companies may not see a business model in this, but the FFC can surely find a workaround.  
A campus divided

Nadine

After months of lockdown, the National University of Singapore plans to reopen its campus in August. And nothing will be the way it once was
“To minimise intermingling of students, it intends to divide up its campuses into different zones. Students and faculty will have to keep within their designated zone for all activities, including attending classes and eating at canteens.” 
NUS plans to keep students within zones on campus, The Straits Times
Dorms will also be assigned to one zone, which means students will live, eat, and learn only within a certain perimeter and interact only with people of their own faculty. In addition, NUS plans to introduce a mix of online and offline learning to prevent overcrowding in large classes.

If strict zoning at university campuses continues for several months, one key aspect of university life is lost: interdisciplinary work and the random flow of ideas across faculties.

The benefits of open campuses are obvious if you’ve had the privilege of studying on one. In my own university experience, many friendships or spontaneous collaborations wouldn’t have been possible if our media studies faculty didn’t share canteens, libraries, and open spaces with the rest of the student body. Journalism students documented the work of those in the fine arts department. Business students teamed up with members of the design faculty to form a student-run creative agency, and so on.

Multidisciplinary research is linked with innovation and technological breakthroughs, so it’s a real loss for universities if strict segmentation becomes the new standard for campus life. If health concerns outweigh the benefits of open campuses, universities need new tools and practices to encourage the flow of ideas.
The Covid-patriarchy one-two punch 

Olina

India, the second most populous country in the world, is going to add another 20 million babies to its roster. Across the world, about 116 million babies will be born in the shadow of Covid-19 since the World Health Organization (WHO) declared it a pandemic on 11 March.

The second order effects of this, quite chillingly, are going to “recast motherhood”:
Millions of mothers all over the world embarked on a journey of parenthood in the world as it was. They now must prepare to bring a life into the world as it has become - a world where expecting mothers are afraid to go to health centres for fear of getting infected, or missing out on emergency care due to strained health services and lockdowns," UNICEF Executive Director Henrietta Fore said.
India will see post-quarantine baby boom with over 20 mn births after coronavirus was declared a pandemic, The Economic Times
For poorer countries, this baby boom could trigger an insurmountable health burden, where neonatal mortality and maternal mortality rates are already high. But the health hit doesn’t just start when the baby is born. Covid-19, it turns out, has hobbled contraception and abortion services as well.
 
A policy brief released by the Foundation for Reproductive Health Services (FRHS), a Delhi-based think tank, estimates that over 25 million couples will have no access to contraception methods, resulting in millions of unintended, and often unsafe, pregnancies. From condoms to intrauterine devices, it’s taken decades to build awareness and access to these contraception methods. Not to mention, India’s family planning goals will also take a 20% hit. FRHS has summarised what this lack of access—to sexual and reproductive health services—might result in. 
Source: FRHS
Contraception and abortions are health measures. But access to these is a gendered issue. In an interview with Business Standard, V Chandrashekar, chief executive officer of FRHS, makes it plain that a patriarchal system, combined with Covid-19 as a stressor, can actually wreak havoc on women’s rights over the health of their own bodies:

“Our clients haven’t been able to access these services for two months now...It’s become hard for them to access their choice of contraception, while patriarchy has always made it hard for them to say no to their husbands…”
 
A paternalistic view on health is a global scourge. In the US, it’s cut off access to safe abortions for women in Texas, with the pandemic a convenient excuse. In fact, the UN is now being accused by the US government for “promoting abortion” under the guise of extending essential status to include reproductive and sexual health.
No masking this 

Arundhati

Police in India are more overwhelmed than usual. Just doing their jobs of maintaining law and order is fraught with danger.
The Hebbagodi station in Karnataka is operating at half-strength as 16 personnel were sent to institutional quarantine after two men they interrogated and arrested, tested positive, for allegedly stealing iron rods.
Whom to arrest and how: Coronavirus dilemma for Karnataka cops, The Times of India
As it is, India's police force is among the weakest in the world. 
According to Mint’s calculations, there are only 144 police officers for every 100,000 citizens (the commonly used measure of police strength), making India’s police force one of the weakest in the world. India’s police-to-population ratio lags behind most countries and the United Nations-recommended ratio of 222.
India’s police force among the world’s weakest, Mint
So naturally, the police have to make a choice on what crimes they need to pursue and when to make arrests. According to The Times of India, police personnel across Bengaluru have been told to only make arrests in cases of heinous offences and emergencies. 

But who defines what is heinous? Is dowry harassment heinous? What about stalking?

The other problem that police will soon encounter is trying to identify perpetrators via CCTV cameras. If everyone is masked, how do you identify criminals? 

Like with everything else, the onus of staying safe—from both Covid-19 and criminals—is likely to fall on citizens.
Farming in the city centre

Shreedhar

Covid-induced lockdowns meant the food supply chain has been disrupted globally. 

But demand wasn’t down. For Cycloponics, an urban farming startup in Paris, the time was ripe to step up. It took advantage of reduced car usage and the city’s empty underground parking lots, turning them into farms
Source: BBC
Covid is set to overhaul use of prime real estate in India as well. Some companies are making working from home permanent. Malls and theatres in city centres have been shut for more than two months now, and some of them may not recover.
 
Could this be an opening for India’s own urban farming startups.
Covid isn't the only health worry

Seetharaman

A lockdown to arrest the spread of the coronavirus may save many lives. But its unintended health consequences will linger long after the restrictions are lifted. 

Confined to our homes, we are getting a lot less exercise than before. A poll of 2,000 people in the UK found that since the country was placed under lockdown on 23 March, their physical activity dropped by around 25%. A third of them have put on weight, with the average increase being 2.7 kgs.

Between 29 March and 3 April, Canadians aged 15-49 spent 66% more time watching TV and 35% more time playing video games. 

This is clearly not good for countries’ fights against lifestyle diseases like obesity and diabetes. And India, under lockdown longer than any other country, is in danger of going down a similar path, even if there isn’t much data available at this point. 

The impact of lockdowns on children is even more worrying. 

Studies have shown that summer vacations are when children in the US pile on the kilos. Now, with 55 million children having twice as much time away from school, the potential for weight gain only goes up. And obese children are likely to be plagued by weight issues well into their adulthood.
 
These effects, combined with Covid-19’s impact on mental health, show that governments’ measures to contain the virus will continue to cast a shadow over our well-being even after we have a vaccine.
What’s in a name?

Savio

As with anything new and novel, there is a competition of sorts to coin the right terminology to describe the biggest economic crisis since the Great Depression. The International Monetary Fund calls it the Great Lockdown, while there are two candidates to fill the abbreviation GCR—Great Covid-19 Recession and Global Coronavirus Recession. Other monikers include Great Virus Crisis, Pandession, and Corona Crisis.
 
But the New York Times had little trouble giving the crisis a name. Or 1,000. The front page of its Sunday edition listed the names of 1,000 people who have died of Covid-19 in the US. The tribute comes as the US death toll nears 100,000, the highest in the world.

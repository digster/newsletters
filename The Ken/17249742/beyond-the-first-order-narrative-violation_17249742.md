Narrative Violation
Seema
It has a flavour-of-the-season ring to it. Narrative violation was 2019’s “VC bingo” buzzword.
|
A narrative violation…is a way to express an idea that goes against conventional wisdom. It is a friend of “well, actually,” and a distant cousin to the devil’s advocate. After that meeting, FFC chairman Nand Kishore Singh [didn’t say much](https://link.the-ken.com/ls/click?upn=sy6ExvMQ-2BqIqW2Qsf-2FVG5vXPs2Fx9e-2BN7JzIZIg7-2BOorgOGLjDse7qn-2FNWLoVi-2F6-2BpLReAAPEuQiM2bkivwcVaeX9EQY1GErE-2Fv-2F5H32pA6-2F5rgrv-2FyouCAPOoEQUKd7LyRY4S7ghPj-2BBd-2FAKzoUwK8jE7jKYL8Vs-2FzMTekG-2Bi-2BcPvBooRt52uVae-2BgntIS2rEnG_dmDs81p1OM5gBGYvHfsMFvgp9PcMB6iZkwowmLx-2FVhwlHXbL5m6csiptGuneRwktW27c6ZwIBsjV67BZcfkOlPppVavmb9GVjXF4V-2FTqNpUBwYt91f9uY-2FF2bGExyNH68wdnKVchcVZT3nk9unZC8zjsiGaOeZ1tRJqBWjo8X6tlo95PJNUk5B3DuzDykvdwEVEILE96kwgO4Od5tw-2FLt7Yc1AiMBNnBh-2FlScGlFS-2Fz6rCtNWxA3uG9H3g8-2FUzqTy6E6GooWVWsfa-2FhIP9S1MiHVIveIOSQBU74jXl2J3m7y0lBy1e7I0wC3JtQjFaXlwW0NmiQVO3aQE8LzuJEfckAvwIyGB7UraPMms9fpMkTOHXEjVqcxYqga96k2oTBtI3KI-2B7Q1tdx2E3viZ-2BxLfLd8KalbwGHSbGJ7ngWeRVY-3D), except that the “outlay for the health sector has to be raised significantly [from the current 0.9% of GDP]”, besides increasing availability of doctors and paramedics in rural areas. Whatever transpired during the Thursday meeting will go into the Commission’s report due in October, which will become the foundation for healthcare reforms. The HLG already has suggestions for the supply side—more doctors, more hospitals (3,000-5,000), shorter courses for doctors, and so on. More of the same? (The demand management needs a tectonic shift, but that’s another story altogether.)
Seat skews
For a very long time, India has allowed artificial scarcity in medical human resources to loom over its healthcare delivery, driven in equal parts due to poor regulation, poor resource allocation, and over reliance on private colleges. Medical professionals are produced not by the actual need but by the job market diktat.
Look at the plight of dentists. The number of permitted seats in bachelors programmes has only increased by about 1,280 in the five-year period from 2013-14 to 2018-19.
|
|
|
For some years, a state like Bihar in the north had ONE dental seat in MDS (Masters in Dental Science), according to recent graduates.
It’s a skew economists have long warned about, advocating that [healthcare cannot be left](https://link.the-ken.com/ls/click?upn=sy6ExvMQ-2BqIqW2Qsf-2FVG5m8vVQilb-2FixmP6unIAEKfWioulMkdeIDKaBHFeNkjqOGbkK6FU-2B2ypsGoNPpAA-2FPg-3D-3DW39O_dmDs81p1OM5gBGYvHfsMFvgp9PcMB6iZkwowmLx-2FVhwlHXbL5m6csiptGuneRwktW27c6ZwIBsjV67BZcfkOlPppVavmb9GVjXF4V-2FTqNpUBwYt91f9uY-2FF2bGExyNH68wdnKVchcVZT3nk9unZC8zjsiGaOeZ1tRJqBWjo8X6tlo95PJNUk5B3DuzDykvdwEVEILE96kwgO4Od5tw-2FLt7eUvegNt2IXa-2FHCdObsfGyX1h3ppZm2wjXGteVAdgj71KFMZ6mbrJYgnbu6SmyruBC-2BUT3iyvxh5KsLMexxW5b9-2BSJOw8Hur6uy-2FVJDvmFi9RDdcNyMV5-2BN1-2Fy-2BRPtASGYgoX42jFxVGqvVrMFUzcqY27clcFq9HwwKn1YA17SIT4M07aBMmgrHnQN5owS8k-2BQLAcIAbrVXuW-2F0DvxzUZ4-3D) to market forces alone.
If dentistry is at one extreme of the demand curve, radiology is at the other. A masters or an MD seat in Radiology in a private college or a deemed university today costs Rs 3-4 crore. Imagine a medical graduate who pays this kind of money to get a master’s degree, what would be her goal after two years of education?
To serve patients or recover that investment? Which is why the first thing many of them do is set up X-ray or imaging centres. Which is why X-rays are prescribed left, right, and centre in urban India. Which is why you undergo a front and back chest X-ray for a dry cough when a throat swab can do the job in many cases. It’s economics.
Ironically, if there’s one discipline that is being hugely aided by artificial intelligence, it’s radiology. Technology, therefore, must disrupt radiology education. But it doesn’t in the real sense. Young doctors say radiology is coveted because you don’t have to deal with patients, you only look at images.
I hope what Dr K. Srinath Reddy suggests in his book “Make Health in India” reaches the Finance Commission. (Dr Reddy is also a member of the HLG).
|
|
|
|
He suggests the “creation of a National Commission for Human Resources in Health to provide overarching help to all categories”.
Fixing the supply side also needs a reduction in the cost of medical education. A narrative violation that the global Massive Open Online Courses (MOOC) movement has missed so far.
Something simple like digitising medical school textbooks, which have always been awfully expensive, hasn’t been attempted. There is no reason why students should buy these and sit in the classrooms to read them. As a well-meaning hospital owner said to me recently:
“You can keep all this online, make it free, and let the training happen on ground at the hospital. This has immediate value. It’s also good value for technology, something that the country needs.”
Digital health companies may not see a business model in this, but the FFC can surely find a workaround.

A campus divided
Nadine
After months of lockdown, the National University of Singapore plans to reopen its campus in August. And nothing will be the way it once was
|
“To minimise intermingling of students, it intends to divide up its campuses into different zones. Students and faculty will have to keep within their designated zone for all activities, including attending classes and eating at canteens.”
|
|
|
Dorms will also be assigned to one zone, which means students will live, eat, and learn only within a certain perimeter and interact only with people of their own faculty. In addition, NUS plans to introduce a mix of online and offline learning to prevent overcrowding in large classes.
If strict zoning at university campuses continues for several months, one key aspect of university life is lost: interdisciplinary work and the random flow of ideas across faculties.
The benefits of open campuses are obvious if you’ve had the privilege of studying on one. In my own university experience, many friendships or spontaneous collaborations wouldn’t have been possible if our media studies faculty didn’t share canteens, libraries, and open spaces with the rest of the student body. Journalism students documented the work of those in the fine arts department. Business students teamed up with members of the design faculty to form a student-run creative agency, and so on.
Multidisciplinary research is [linked](https://link.the-ken.com/ls/click?upn=sy6ExvMQ-2BqIqW2Qsf-2FVG5kfurTdEWMvpo-2BRQqAMIFkCHbwj47m1njINbtsbF3tEbLggRw30-2BRWstKdeMm-2FFGu8vKLjO6FBKldzbY76sz2-2FSp-2FLbYlTZbWWQi8vQ8PrP3Gm8ijD5XpLXd1IsOYFHA2Us0IUtmaoSRGegox-2BWXE1YitswpZsy1KclSNCMdu6vKjCsk2rxg07930r-2FwNNkxPA-3D-3Dat07_dmDs81p1OM5gBGYvHfsMFvgp9PcMB6iZkwowmLx-2FVhwlHXbL5m6csiptGuneRwktW27c6ZwIBsjV67BZcfkOlPppVavmb9GVjXF4V-2FTqNpUBwYt91f9uY-2FF2bGExyNH68wdnKVchcVZT3nk9unZC8zjsiGaOeZ1tRJqBWjo8X6tlo95PJNUk5B3DuzDykvdwEVEILE96kwgO4Od5tw-2FLt1-2F1Z5TinSacfl1bsO3WjfQKOXLbujIK8IzL3608nKKwBgqJaqa48abvX95yy-2BcrYXK6oWkxY-2F-2FOiiStinCI4zzTGONXWT7BwLHmJNNuK3kTc-2BseSoopcNO97CV0R3FSe-2BzDD4BNz1BKNfveYtZGvPQ5sSP3BXTUOADfy0kvL0frEVM9PaBUdQGTjO0M1DJovL8hUS3ywEZc4XfkpjuaM2Q-3D) with innovation and technological breakthroughs, so it’s a real loss for universities if strict segmentation becomes the new standard for campus life. If health concerns outweigh the benefits of open campuses, universities need new tools and practices to encourage the flow of ideas.

The Covid-patriarchy one-two punch
Olina
India, the second most populous country in the world, is going to add another 20 million babies to its roster. Across the world, about 116 million babies will be born in the shadow of Covid-19 since the World Health Organization (WHO) declared it a pandemic on 11 March.
The second order effects of this, quite chillingly, are going to “recast motherhood”:
|
|
Millions of mothers all over the world embarked on a journey of parenthood in the world as it was. They now must prepare to bring a life into the world as it has become - a world where expecting mothers are afraid to go to health centres for fear of getting infected, or missing out on emergency care due to strained health services and lockdowns," UNICEF Executive Director Henrietta Fore said.

India will see post-quarantine baby boom with over 20 mn births after coronavirus was declared a pandemic, [The Economic Times](https://link.the-ken.com/ls/click?upn=sy6ExvMQ-2BqIqW2Qsf-2FVG5kw7IY1VwD2tS0-2FnHHAnZJB67Duhi0siT-2BbAJGa4xXmigz5ypSnY-2Bnz-2BNsoRHYuX8vzy1BoXrx8cvp59gGXWkzoPTN2-2F0zZw7Kwuw6GPppqom0XKxxW23CN7HCH4rQjOzJkh78vDxZPAw7mqBXG1xADKtZnEDTllLV-2FpgzgiX-2FBeNSIQ-2FG-2BDB9Wx-2FVXmhNO40RbsVD1LTG57sRxURIBU3oj5M6SZ0h6H31Qo5dNGPVykyuVQ_dmDs81p1OM5gBGYvHfsMFvgp9PcMB6iZkwowmLx-2FVhwlHXbL5m6csiptGuneRwktW27c6ZwIBsjV67BZcfkOlPppVavmb9GVjXF4V-2FTqNpUBwYt91f9uY-2FF2bGExyNH68wdnKVchcVZT3nk9unZC8zjsiGaOeZ1tRJqBWjo8X6tlo95PJNUk5B3DuzDykvdwEVEILE96kwgO4Od5tw-2FLt4YKnAVIbwgL9kkcq3-2BqWV8VNf0MFTxJSxhvICkNV2BcZ1ZeoXNYm35HX9CHzYEXs31q7mMPEF1W5jpPV4Z6JKiCQOcArgL6xxkaPjOH2n1yHsn2gluXoNxkxHfBCQHVqWxU3OOUYvCWvJWgjIzL1UObGnR8U-2BRyFKj45YCdwHmA02F-2F4T3nNbrqAzPKV0uNQ8ZMGzjN1A123vqhZNMxcxQ-3D)

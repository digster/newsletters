The dream to be immeasurably rich.
Despite all the ways in which six billion humans are different from one another, if there is one dream that every person has wishfully aspired for at some point or the other, it is the dream to be rich beyond measure. It is a dream that has built kingdoms and empires, nations and civilizations.
Yet, the one thing that I have heard from every obscenely wealthy person that I have ever encountered is that being rich is not all that it is cracked up to be. Sure, there is a sense of joy and comfort, but it is a feeling that wears thin very quickly.
So, what does a rich person do after acquiring cars/houses/property et al?
Inevitably, they dream of creating a legacy—something that gives a sense of personal fulfilment and something that the world will remember in the ages to come.
Take Rohini and Nandan Nilekani, for example.
After earning billions of dollars through Infosys, Rohini and Nandan are now playing their second innings through stints that will serve as their legacies beyond the narrow considerations of commerce. Today, Rohini is India’s most prolific woman philanthropist, while Nandan is known more for being the architect of Aadhaar, which now covers 1.26 billion Indians, than for being the founder of India's original IT startup.
In today's interview, Rohini and Nandan have a deep conversation with Rohin, talking at length about their motivations, imperatives and ambitions. They also answer tough questions around regulatory capture and crony capitalism that nobody else had dared to ask them hitherto.

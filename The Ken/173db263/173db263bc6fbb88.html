The market knows
 
Nithin
 
Economic indicators are alarming. India is set for its first contraction in more than four decades. Yet, the share prices of companies—a reflection of the economy—are trending up.
“Any market activity without supporting fundamentals will not sustain,” said C. J. George, chief executive officer at Geojit Financial Services Ltd., a brokerage backed by BNP Paribas SA. “We are yet to see the fundamentals improving in the country.”
India has the worst disconnect between stock rally, gloomy economy, Business Standard
Source: Bloomberg and Business Standard
 
This doesn’t make sense. But, it’s the same everywhere—the US, South Africa, Malaysia, Pakistan and India. And over in Iran, even the authorities are denying a stock market bubble.
 
While headlines tell you that markets are expensive and it’s a bad time to buy stocks, the market exuberance has its reasons.
 
1. Economic indicators come with a lag and tell you what has already happened. The stock markets like to look ahead. This chart shows that right after the Global Financial Crisis (GFC) in 2008, the US stock markets recovered much before the actual earnings of companies grew. 
Source: Ambit Private Client Group
 
So, today the market is saying that, yes, we know many companies will have a tough year, but we need to have an eye on the future.
 
2. Looking at the level of the Sensex, a stock market index with 30 of India’s largest stocks, in isolation doesn’t signal anything. At 38,000, what does it mean? Instead, it needs to be adjusted against the earnings of companies in the index. This creates something called the price to earnings ratio (P/E). A measure commonly used by most investors to determine how cheap or expensive a company or the index is. 
 
So if you divide 38,000 by the collective earnings per share of the 30 companies, you end up with a P/E of 26 times. Now, investors compare the P/E to that of a previous stock market peak, which was 28 times in 2008 before the GFC. So, it looks like the market today is indeed expensive and at a peak.
 
But, from 2003 to 2008, when the Sensex went from levels of 3,000 to 20,000, and the P/E rose from 11 to 28, the earnings grew by 20% every year too. This type of earnings growth isn’t sustainable and when the 2008 recession hit, earnings tumbled.  
 
Today, we are near the bottom of an economic cycle and a low point in the company earnings which has grown below 7% every year for the past few years. If we assume that the economy recovers due to the global and local central bank actions, the earnings will recover and this P/E will fall. 
 
The market will not seem expensive anymore.
 
3. Stories make a difference.
 
In the Indian markets, the shares of Reliance Industries are up by 47%  in the past six months, due to its mammoth fundraising which has come at a time when everyone else is struggling. Britannia Industries, an Indian food products company whose revenues are driven by its biscuits division, is up by 25% in the same period. On the other hand, a lender like Bajaj Finance, which is at risk of bad loans ballooning, is down by 27%. Even amidst all this chaos, there is some rationality.
 
In the US, the rally of the broad based and diversified S&P 500 Index has been driven by tech stocks which are some of the biggest beneficiaries of the shifting business dynamics. Take them out and the index hasn’t done too much.
Source: Goldman Sachs report
 
In an environment where investors are operating with a very high degree of uncertainty, the story could be more important than the numbers. 
 
Yes, the market looks expensive and you may disagree with where it is, but respect it you must, for its collective wisdom.

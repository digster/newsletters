Trade Tricks
Tue, 05 Nov 24
A weekly newsletter about the biggest changes in commerce—focusing on shifts that matter to you..
Good Morning Ishan,
Amazon and Flipkart have no competition in selling everything from dog hoodies to electric scooters on a single website.
But there’s one company that has come a long way hawking a number of products you associate with the e-commerce giants. And it talked up a milestone last week by taking aim at its considerably larger rivals.
In a [blog post](https://www.meesho.io/blog/meesho-becomes-first-horizontal-e-commerce-company-to-generate-positive-cash-flow-in-fy24-with-33-yoy-growth-in-operating-revenues-2) announcing its financials for the year ended March 2024, Meesho said it had become the “first horizontal e-commerce company” to generate positive cash flow.
The numbers are commendable at first glance.
| Source: Meesho |
But that doesn’t mean Meesho’s growth is self-explanatory.
In its early years, there was considerable scepticism about Meesho’s desire to be an e-commerce marketplace for small-town India. Was that market big enough to bet the future of an internet company on?
But it has since become clear that Meesho had every reason to choose the buyers it did. An Indian Institute of Management-Ahmedabad [survey](https://www04.iima.ac.in/rtc/wp-content/uploads/2024/02/Digital-Retail-Channels-and-Consumers-1.0.pdf) of nearly 36,000 consumers in 2022 revealed that tier-2 consumers’ median spend on their last online purchase was 70% higher than that of their tier-1 counterparts. [Almost half](https://www.meesho.io/blog/meesho-mega-blockbuster-sale-2024-fueled-by-bharats-demand-orders-surge-40-yoy-with-145-crore-customer-visits) the shoppers in Meesho’s recent flagship sale were from tier-4 cities.
As much as 10-minute delivery has caught on in large cities (roughly 90% of Blinkit’s sales is from India’s top eight cities), there’s still a sizable online-retail market to go after in smaller pockets of the country.
So, to whom Meesho sells doesn’t raise a lot of questions. But what and how it sells certainly do.
The Softbank-funded marketplace, last valued at US$5 billion, puts front and centre the kind of unbranded products and little-known brands that form the “long tail” of its rivals. Chances are, you’ll rarely come across these items on Amazon or Flipkart. They exist solely to give you the illusion of unending choices.
But on Meesho, they are all you see. You can buy a wristwatch for as little as Rs 125 (US$1.50), and an “Airpod Pro with Silicon Case” for just twice that.
The company is said to be working on fixing counterfeit issues with algorithms but is careful not to be too aggressive against sellers of fake items. Doing so could hurt its order numbers and top line, said the first former employee.
And this is rubbing brands the wrong way.
Meesho wasn’t doing enough to ban those selling counterfeits of the products manufactured by the personal-care brand that quit the platform, claimed the expert quoted earlier.
Meesho wants big brands in its cart. But unbranded products are baked into its DNA, [The Ken](https://the-ken.com/story/meesho-wants-big-brands-in-its-cart-but-unbranded-products-are-baked-into-its-dna/)
For someone with not a lot of money to spare, these are deals too good to pass up. But thanks to the dubious provenance of these products, it’s quite possible that Meesho has to deal with a large number of orders being returned. I couldn’t find a reliable number for the same, but it’s reasonable to assume that the return rate is higher than that of branded goods on Amazon or Flipkart.
What’s more, the category that is Meesho’s mainstay isn’t as exciting as it used to be. My colleague Praveen has [written](https://the-ken.com/the-nutgraf/the-great-wall-of-fashion/) about the struggles of online sellers of apparel. It’s not just e-tailers, even fashion stores have found the going hard. The one notable exception is Tata Group’s Zudio.
With an under-Rs 1,000 price tag, the value retailer has expanded far and wide. It has over 600 stores in close to 170 cities—a lot of which are presumably home to Meesho users. So Meesho has to count on other verticals to do for it what fashion, which was half its business last year, has done all these years. In its blog post, Meesho said home and kitchen, beauty and personal care, and baby essentials were its fastest-growing categories in the year ended March 2024.
The other part of Meesho I haven’t been able to get a handle on is its pitch to consumers that they can’t just shop on the cheap, but also make money by “reselling” to others. Meesho says you can set up your own business by just choosing certain products, adding a margin on the price, and then promoting them among friends and family.
This sounds very warm and fuzzy. But I wonder why someone, after their first purchase from a Meesho “entrepreneur”, wouldn’t just go to the app directly to buy what they want. Even given Meesho’s prodding of resellers to consistently share products with their network at least once a day and create “the urgency factor”.
Do the people these resellers target place such a premium on curation?
Meesho has over the past year added a number of well-known brands in personal care, footwear, and smartphones. But in doing so, it has also played right into the hands of Amazon and Flipkart, which offer a wider assortment in each of these categories.
I don’t begrudge Meesho its growth and almost-profitability. But the reasons for its rise aren’t as obvious as one would imagine.

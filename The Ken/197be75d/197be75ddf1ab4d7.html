You are reading our subscriber-only newsletter Ed Set Go—a weekly commentary on Indian edtech and education

In 2021, The Ken launched its first-ever newsletter to keep up with the barrage of news that the pandemic would generate. The world, as we knew it, had changed forever, and we needed an apt medium to keep you informed. From that initial successful experiment, we realized there was a need—and demand—for more products that packaged sectoral news for you in breezy, weekly capsules. 

I was tasked with creating Ed Set Go, India’s first and only education-focused newsletter because, as we say on our landing page, “the powers behind education are shifting”.

The opportunity was both a gift and a challenge–would anyone read a weekly digest about Indian education? Was there enough news to write about? What if the powers stopped shifting? (To be clear, I still ask myself these questions when I sit down to write an edition.)

And while the newsletter had to be synced to the current news cycle, I also wanted to write about things that personally interested me. There was news aplenty in 2021 about edtech startups ballooning to a tenth of their original size, rapid mergers and acquisitions, and newly-minted education entrepreneurs. But there were unexposed corners too–like how climate change is shaping the contours of a school day, how budding study influencers made Youtube money online, and how a vast majority of Indian students study on Whatsapp.

There were, of course, big moments to capture too. Byju’s rapid rise and fall, Unacademy’s attempts to buy out both online and offline rivals to narrow its competitive field, Physicswallah’s breakout moment—from Youtube star to investor favourite.

A lot has happened, but there’s an irony to where things stand now. When I started writing on edtech for The Ken, the sector had few players, rules of the game were still being established, and people were enthusiastic about the potential of tech and business models to create new types of educational formats. It was like a provincial town, where everyone knew everyone else’s business. 

We’re at a similar stage now: few companies, looking for the next big idea, with a new set of rules to establish. But the players look very different, and everyone carries the faint whiff of being scorched by an intense battle.

There are many things that are clear to us now—that upselling old content isn’t an edtech model, that hybrid can work if done well, that Youtube is a formidable distribution channel, but conversions can still be hard. That there’s still a struggle to establish a robust edtech business model at the grassroots level, but delivery mechanisms like Whatsapp can make things easier. 

I could go on about what I’ve learned about education, and more crucially, our country, through this process of crafting this newsletter for you every week. I firmly believe that when it comes to education, India is the best testing ground to try crazy and bold ideas. Companies can thrive in niches, or in the mainstream. Parents are still willing to spend a considerable amount of their income on education. Entrepreneurs are still welcome, as long as they don’t try to turn into messiahs. And it’s time now to put a new lens on that coverage–a job that I know my colleagues at The Ken will excel at.

Over the last seven years of covering edtech, I’ve heard a lot of bets and predictions about the sector. Yet one sticks with me. In his heyday, Byju Raveendran would always say at conferences and in interviews: “I believe the world’s biggest education company should come from India.” There’s a lot he wasn’t right about. But on this, Raveendran is right on the money.

I hope, one day, I’ll be bringing you that story, when we meet again on The Ken’s pages.

Until then, signing off with love and gratitude for everyone who has read, shared, and sent me feedback over this five-year journey!

Regards,
Olina

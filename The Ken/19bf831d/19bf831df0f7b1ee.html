Today, India celebrates becoming a republic, where power—at least in theory—rests with the public. It’s also a fitting day to look at a company about to hand itself over to public shareholders. So, pop quiz!

Question 1: One company processes 49% of India’s UPI payments. Another processes 6%. Which one should be worth more?

Question 2: If company A was last valued at $12 billion, and company B (the listed rival) is valued at $7.9 billion, is company A expensive… or is company B just a bad reference point?

Question 3: Does market leadership in payments actually matter when payments are low-margin, and regulators keep changing the rules mid-game?

Bonus question: How much should the market punish a sector after one very public IPO went wrong before clawing back?

Phonepe is about to find out.

The Walmart-backed payments leader has filed for an IPO that lets its investors exit but leaves public-market investors to decide what dominance is really worth in 2026. The company looks bigger and safer than Paytm* did at listing. But it’s entering the market just as some of its most lucrative revenue streams are disappearing, and just as investors are starting to become far more allergic to stories without profits. Meanwhile, Paytm, once the cautionary tale, is slowly pulling ahead on monetisation, even as its stock still trades well below its IPO price.

So when bankers pitch Phonepe at a premium, what exactly are investors buying? Market share? Governance? Walmart’s halo? Or the hope that this time, it’s different? Arundhati’s incisive story today breaks down why Phonepe’s IPO could become a referendum on what and how much India’s public markets have really learnt since Paytm.

*Paytm founder Vijay Shekhar Sharma is an investor in The Ken.

This is an excellent article, brings out the complex mechanism of operation of educational institutions in a simple language, well wrtten. The fact that such details have become available due to publicly shared documents as a result of the PE deciding to list the entity should not take away the fact that this structure has always existed and this is not a new phenomenon created by a PE fund. Ideally the capital should come from an endowment which continues to use the accrued cash to expand education by reinvesting it in the school or expanding it, with no person taking out the surplus for personal use – there are several of them in the country but not enough. So where does the capital come from and what do the people/organization/PE-fund make as returns on capital deployed? Is that as exorbitant as it is coming out when we look at percentage of revenue going out as rent or management fee? Can you also find out the capital deployed by the PE for this acquisition? That would help calculate what is the net IRR for the investment. And if it is in the range of 8-10% mentioned in one of the comments, one may not worry so much about profiteering. Then the question would pivot to the cost of acquisition itself – is that too much? On the other hand if the capital deployed is making some insane IRR of 30% types then it would turn out to be profiteering in true sense.

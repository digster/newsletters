Good Morning Ishan,
Today, India celebrates becoming a republic, where power—at least in theory—rests with the public. It’s also a fitting day to look at a company about to hand itself over to public shareholders. So, pop quiz!
Question 1: One company processes 49% of India’s UPI payments. Another processes 6%. Which one should be worth more?
Question 2: If company A was last valued at $12 billion, and company B (the listed rival) is valued at $7.9 billion, is company A expensive… or is company B just a bad reference point?
Question 3: Does market leadership in payments actually matter when payments are low-margin, and regulators keep changing the rules mid-game?
Bonus question: How much should the market punish a sector after one very public IPO went wrong before clawing back?
Phonepe is about to find out.
The Walmart-backed payments leader has filed for an IPO that lets its investors exit but leaves public-market investors to decide what dominance is really worth in 2026. The company looks bigger and safer than Paytm* did at listing. But it’s entering the market just as some of its most lucrative revenue streams are disappearing, and just as investors are starting to become far more allergic to stories without profits. Meanwhile, Paytm, once the cautionary tale, is slowly pulling ahead on monetisation, even as its stock still trades well below its IPO price.
So when bankers pitch Phonepe at a premium, what exactly are investors buying? Market share? Governance? Walmart’s halo? Or the hope that this time, it’s different? Arundhati’s incisive [story today](https://the-ken.com/story/should-phonepe-be-worth-more-than-paytms-7-9b/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter) breaks down why Phonepe’s IPO could become a referendum on what and how much India’s public markets have really learnt since Paytm.
*Paytm founder Vijay Shekhar Sharma is an investor in The Ken.

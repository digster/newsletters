Matcha is a rising star with an Achilles heel. For the Japanese tea product taking over Indian cafés and Instagram feeds, demand is hardly a problem. But supply could be

Last month, I found myself in a Bengaluru café staring at a menu that seemed to have transformed overnight. Once a simple line-up of cappuccinos and cold coffees, now half the items sported various shades of green. Matcha latte, matcha smoothie, matcha ice cream, matcha cheesecake—it was like someone had taken a green highlighter to the entire board.

The barista eyed me when I asked for a vanilla latte. “You haven’t tried matcha yet? It’s our best seller,” she said. Behind me, a group of college students were taking pictures of their matcha orders, angling their drinks in the window light just the right way, looking for that perfect Instagram shot. 

Well, I succumbed and bought a matcha latte (only to instantly regret it), but ever since, I’ve been noticing it everywhere. Social-media feeds, quick-commerce apps, supermarket shelves, restaurants… it seems the centuries-old Japanese tea ceremony staple is turning into India’s hottest beverage trend. 

And matcha businesses are popping up all around. 

Last month, actress Sanya Malhotra partnered with the founders of clean-label wellness brand Essenzaa Nutrition to launch Bree Matcha. Glow Glossary, another matcha brand, made its way into Shark Tank India this April. More importantly, major café chains such as Starbucks and Blue Tokai have started incorporating it into their menus in a big way, offering new drinks combining matcha with ingredients like coconut water, strawberry, and mango. 

Clearly, there is no dearth of demand. 

Supply, on the other hand, is a whole different matter. 

Matcha mania

Tencha—established in 2020 as one of India’s first matcha brands—has ridden this green wave almost from the beginning. In 2022, it sold around 500 kg of matcha products. In 2023, that figure rose to 652 kg. But 2024 was the breakout year—the brand’s sales skyrocketed sixfold to 3,700 kg. 

“The product itself hasn’t changed,” says Ashish Kumar, Tencha’s founder. “What’s changed is the presentation and visibility.” 

Matcha is basically just ground up green tea leaves. But unlike most green teas, where you strain out the leaves or powder before consumption, matcha is intended to be drunk in its entirety. 

The result is a far more powerful cocktail of some of the active components. Matcha contains a significant amount of caffeine—up to 176 mg per cup, depending on its concentration, compared to approximately 100 mg in a standard cup of coffee. But it also contains a lot more L-theanine, an amino acid in green tea that promotes relaxation and focus without the crash that caffeine usually brings with it. And if you look at antioxidants, matcha is supposed to pack about 137X more than normal green tea products. 

Kumar says the skincare industry was the first to jump on the matcha wave, before Covid. Brands such as Quench, Nykaa Skin, Mamaearth, and many others now all offer matcha-based masks, scrubs, and serums. 

Tencha initially went the same direction and explored skincare, because Kumar did not think Indians would like the taste of matcha. But competing against the big brands wasn’t very easy. 

Social media, however, changed things. Here’s a Forbes piece from last year: 

Social media has been instrumental in propelling matcha’s popularity. Influencers and health enthusiasts frequently captivate their millennial and Gen Z followers—generations known for their affinity for natural, plant-based products and wellness trends—by showcasing matcha drinks and food as integral to a healthy lifestyle. This resonates perfectly with their desire for unique experiences and holistic health, driving matcha’s surge in cafes and restaurants globally.

Indian Instagram feeds weren’t immune. And once Tencha shifted to matcha as a beverage and started selling pure matcha powder, the business hit the fast track. Kumar says his company “nailed” the marketing, creating terms like “ceremonial grade” and “culinary grade” to help Indian customers understand quality differences. “But they’re not traditional Japanese classifications,” he notes. 

That’s not to imply that there aren’t real quality differences: premium matcha has a smooth, umami-rich taste and uses young leaves, while cheaper variants are bitter and coarse, better suited to baking. According to Kumar, cafes pay anywhere from Rs 3,500–15,000 per kilogram for matcha, depending on quality. 

Right now, about 35% of Tencha’s revenue comes from supplying matcha to cafés and other brands (35%), while sales through Amazon (35%) and quick-commerce (20%) apps are also significant. Last month, it hit Rs 1 crore sales in Delhi alone, accounting for 40% of Amazon’s matcha market, Kumar claims. 

Out of stock

The soaring demand and exploding sales, though, have revealed a weakness. 

“We’re facing shortages along with everyone else in the industry,” Kumar says. 

Here’s the thing about matcha—almost all of it is grown in Japan, and the process is pretty specialised. About 90% of what we drink in India is imported, for instance. 

A tea garden in Upper Assam gave matcha a shot as early as 2016, and there are now other growers in India, but Kumar says the quality isn’t very good and the expertise and infrastructure needed just isn’t there yet. It takes years of training to perfect the harvesting of matcha. 

“Authentic matcha can only be made from ‘tencha’ leaves. The process involves steaming, drying, and freezing the leaves. Harvesting only happens in May, June, and July.”

This is a problem. What happens when Japanese supply is impacted? 

In 2024, for instance, prices of Japanese matcha rose sharply as temperatures broke new records and farmers saw sharply lower yields. And while growth in matcha production is high—Japan produced 5,336 tons of matcha in 2024, up 2.7X compared to a decade ago—it’s struggling to keep up with demand. 

Back in India, despite all the supply challenges, Kumar isn’t interested in using Indian or Chinese matcha. 

“The matcha story is tied to its Japanese origins,” he says. 

That might preserve authenticity, but it could also start dragging down growth.

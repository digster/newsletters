Biotechnology has been somewhat in the shadows of information technology, particularly in Karnataka. Where IT contributes over 65% to the state’s gross state domestic product, biotech accounts for just 10%. 

But that ratio is poised for change. The numbers released at the end of February by the industry body, Able, show that Karnataka’s bioeconomy reached $39.2 billion in 2025, growing over 26% from 2023. Separately, at the national level, the industry grew 9.7% to $166 billion in 2024, contributing 4.3% to India’s GDP, compared to IT’s share of about 7%. 

It was then fitting that the Biotechnology Industry Research Assistance Council (Birac) received one of the first allocations from the Centre’s recently announced Rs 1 lakh crore Research, Development, and Innovation (RDI) Fund. The biotech agency has been given Rs 2,000 crore to “accelerate investment in India’s R&D and innovation ecosystem, with a focus on strengthening private sector R&D through up to 50% co-investment”. 

Union minister of state for science and technology, Jitendra Singh, made the announcement on 14 February, but until 6 p.m. on 27 February (when I complained to an industry veteran who consults for Birac), there were no details about the RDI fund or its call for applications. This is when the deadline for the first phase of applications is 31 March. 

But I mention this tardiness as a minor point in the broader context of Birac’s unique status as a government agency. After a great start in 2011, Birac, which was set up to evaluate and support market-driven ideas in an “un-government” like fashion, slipped into a safe grant-giving mode. (I’ve written about it before.) 

People in the industry tell me that as much as a fifth of the RDI fund, i.e. Rs 20,000 crore, is earmarked for biotech. That’s a lot of money, given that the Department of Biotechnology’s (DBT) entire annual budget for FY26 was Rs 3,446 crore, the only department among its peers to have seen a reduction in its budget since 2021...

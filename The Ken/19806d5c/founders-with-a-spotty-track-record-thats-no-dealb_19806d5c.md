Good Morning Ishan,
If you have ever wondered how many times someone can screw up and still get funded, today’s story is for you.
You can flame out. Be accused of mis-selling drugs. Selling snake oil to parents. Setting up a phantom EV factory staffed by three confused men and a dog. And still—still!—venture capitalists will throw fresh money at your next company.
That’s not hyperbole.
Last month, three Pharmeasy founders (yes, that Pharmeasy—the one now worth less than a decent SaaS retainer) raised a fresh round for their new interiors startup. Bessemer Venture Partners backed them. Karan Bajaj, the guy behind Whitehat Jr, which aggressively marketed coding classes for six-year-olds, raised $16 million for a new startup for cancer patients. Seed-stage funding. At unicorn-stage valuation.
You’d think VCs, now operating in a market with the funding velocity of a coma patient, would be more risk-averse. Instead, they are just desperate.
The thing is, India’s startups are not full of hot new ideas. And when you’re a VC sitting on a dry powder keg and your limited partners are getting better returns in the stock market, you don’t have the luxury of scruples. You just need to not be the one left holding the bag.
Seetharaman’s [piece today](https://the-ken.com/story/founders-with-a-spotty-track-record-thats-no-dealbreaker-for-vcs/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter)—with jabs as sharp as the insights—walks through how India’s VCs are backing founders with “spotty” track records. It features caviar-rich exits, IPO gymnastics, and at least one company that raised millions while pretending to build two-seater EVs.

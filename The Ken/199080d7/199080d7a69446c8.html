When I interviewed Sudhir Sitapati, chief executive of Godrej Consumer Products, last month, I was quite curious to understand if venture capital-funded companies like Udaan and Elasticrun have had any impact on the way fast-moving consumer goods are distributed. 

Sitapati was categorical in his response. 

No, not materially or in the last 60 years. The revolution that happened in FMCG was in the 1950s, when Hindustan Lever moved away from wholesale selling—which we still do, for example, as Godrej Consumer in Africa—to appointing exclusive redistribution stockists whose salesmen would go from shop to shop to sell.

And that was a revolutionary change because Southeast Asia and others moved to the distributor model. It effectively solved the problem of last-mile distribution for new products. This didn’t happen, for instance, in China and is possibly one of the reasons for the e-commerce boom there. 

Central to these startups’ purported disruption is the idea of “direct reach”. It’s a term FMCG companies tout in their presentations and annual reports. It’s nothing but the number of kiranas, or mom-and-pop stores, they can supply through their own distributors. Unlike wholesalers, who work with multiple FMCG companies, distributors are exclusive to a single manufacturer. 

Distributors are FMCG companies’ feet on the street, visiting kiranas once a week to make sure they haven’t run out of any products. Wholesalers, on the other hand, typically expect retailers to come to them with their requirements. And they tend to push the wares of the firm with the highest margins and the most liberal credit terms. 

Wholesalers are a necessary evil, though. 

Consider ITC, India’s second-largest FMCG company by sales. Its products are sold at 7 million stores, according to ITC’s annual report for FY25. But its own distributors only cater to one-third of those. ITC needs wholesalers for the rest. Hindustan Unilever, the 800-pound gorilla of Indian FMCG, directly services a similar number of outlets, despite over 9 million shops stocking its brands.  

FMCG firms would love for their distributors to supply as many stores as possible. The result is a relationship that’s more than transactional, not to mention rich data on customer behaviour that a wholesaler couldn’t care to share with the company. But for manufacturers, direct reach can quickly become counterproductive. 

To justify being an exclusive partner to an FMCG company, a distributor needs a cluster of kiranas. For context, a sales representative visits over 200 stores a week in a big city. The math obviously changes in less populous cities. A distributor in a tier-2 or tier-3 town would cover stores over a wider radius and not meet their owners as often as in a metropolis. 

But the density of stores really begins to thin beyond the most urban 1.5–2 million kiranas, as any FMCG executive would tell you, and no distributor would want to stake their income on a single company. 

So far, so good. Except that the long-held assumptions about kiranas in cities are beginning to fall apart.

What I’m realising is that the top 20% of Indians are now shopping in modern trade [supermarkets] or quick commerce. They have moved [from general trade, or kiranas]. So when you create a product of Rs 100 or more, general trade is not that relevant anymore. You have no option but to launch on quick commerce or modern trade. 

Godrej Consumer’s Sudhir Sitapati on selling to India’s top 20% during a demand slump, The Ken

In the year ending March, kiranas’ FMCG sales by volume in metros fell 2%, while e-commerce, driven mostly by instant-delivery apps, was up 4o%, according to research firm NielsenIQ. Even adjusting for e-grocery’s relatively low base in the previous year, the growth was substantial.

E-commerce contributes 5% of FMCG’s overall urban revenue and 13% of sales in India’s top eight cities, which account for the lion’s share of quick-commerce apps’ business. Even as the FMCG sector has struggled, Blinkit, Instamart, and Zepto have grown by drawing customer spends away from kiranas and supermarkets. 

This leaves FMCG companies in a bind: the small grocery stores in large cities, which their distributors can service best, will become far less relevant than they traditionally have been. But it’s still financially unviable for the likes of ITC and Godrej Consumer to cater to the majority of the country’s kiranas directly.

Why should FMCG makers worry if their customers are merely switching to a newer channel to make their purchase? For decades, these companies have had the upper hand because they deal with millions of shopkeepers, and thousands of distributors, who are inconsequential on their own. 

Ever so often, bodies representing these distributors and store-owners make certain demands and get their way. But make no mistake, FMCG companies are used to setting the terms. 

The arrival of supermarket chains such as Dmart and Reliance Smart didn’t change the status quo much, thanks to the resilience of kiranas. For several FMCG firms, Blinkit is already the second-largest conduit to consumers, behind Dmart, according to an industry veteran. The stonking rise of quick commerce shifts the balance of power in a way FMCG firms never saw coming.

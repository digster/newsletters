Is the party over for small-cap and mid-cap stocks? There are many caution signs, and investors must be wary of blind spots
By Anirudh Somani
5 min read
View in browser

Good Morning Ishan,

You are reading our subscriber-only newsletter Ka-Ching!—a weekly commentary on fintech and personal finance

One of the most damaging investor tendencies—what Charlie Munger called “massively stupid”—is our habit of extrapolating the past. We assume what has worked will continue, often ignoring valuations, capital cycles, and mean reversion, where returns eventually move toward the long-term mean.

That’s precisely the concern today with India’s mid- and small-cap stocks, referring to the top 101–250 stocks by market capitalisation, and those ranked 251 and below. Until recently, investors were pouring money into funds that track these segments, driven by their outperformance. In the year ended December 2024, for instance, the roughly 25% rise in the Nifty mid-cap and small-cap indices far exceeded the 11% rise in the large-cap-focused Nifty 50 index. 

But does the past behaviour of these stocks justify their current valuations? Mr. Market doesn’t think so. Since September, while the large-cap index has slipped by about 12%, the mid-cap and small-cap indices took much steeper dives—by roughly 17% and 23%, respectively. There could be more pain ahead.

Some market mavens have sounded the alarm.

Sankaran Naren, ICICI Prudential AMC’s executive director and CIO, didn’t mince words. His recent speech at the IFA Galaxy Summit in January, aptly titled “What Are We Worried About?”, was blunt.

Naren called mid- and small-cap valuations “absurd”, and urged investors to redeem their holdings.

“It is a clear time to take out lock, stock, and barrel from small- and mid-caps.”

Sankaran Naren, executive director and CIO of ICICI Prudential AMC

To understand Naren’s concern, we can first look at two benchmarks’ price-to-earnings (P/E) ratio, which can tell us when valuations are high. As of 18 February 2025, here are the figures:

  • The NSE Midcap 150’s P/E ratio stood at nearly 35X, compared with its three-year median of less than 27X
  • The NSE Smallcap 250’s P/E ratio was nearly 27X, compared with its three-year median of less than 25X

This, after the fall since September.

Naren isn’t alone.

Siddhartha Bhaiya, the managing director and CIO at Aequitas Investments in Mumbai, manages over Rs 5,000 crore, a large portion of which normally goes into small- and mid-cap stocks. Over the past year, he moved around 90% of Aequitas’ PMS portfolio into cash and equivalents, and much of this into gold recently.

His reasoning is that “Indian markets are terribly expensive”, noting that 90% of NSE 500 companies have a P/E multiple of 36X. Furthermore, earnings growth has slowed sharply—his analysis of 500 companies’ Q3 earnings shows just 4% topline growth in India, trailing listed firms in US and European markets.

Bhaiya follows a simple philosophy: watch what the promoters do. Look in their direction and you’ll see that promoters have been aggressively selling.

Promoter stake sales in 2023 and 2024 exceeded Rs 2.3 lakh crore, surpassing the total from 2019–2022. Private-equity and venture-capital exits through IPOs and block deals in 2023 and 2024 together have crossed Rs 2.3 lakh crore, according to data on markets information provider Prime Database.

Despite the correction since September, market-wide valuations seem stretched. Motilal Oswal’s February edition of the Bulls & Bears monthly report highlights that all major global markets, barring the US, were trading at a discount to India as of the end of January:

The Buffett Indicator, which is the total US stock market value divided by GDP, was at 132% in December 2024, well above its historical average of 85%.

This caution isn’t new. In December 2024, a panel of fund managers warned that small- and mid-cap stocks were overheating:

“Right now, while certain segments of the market are fairly priced, nothing can truly be considered cheap. The real challenge may lie in investor sentiment. Many investors are entering the market with unrealistic return expectations.”

Sailesh Raj Bhan, CIO of Equity at Nippon India Mutual Fund

Foreign institutional investors (FIIs) aren’t thrilled either. FIIs pulled out $8.4 billion in January 2025, the second-highest monthly outflow ever, following a record $10.9 billion monthly exodus in October 2024.

However, comparing current P/E multiples to historical averages or global markets without considering factors like return on equity (ROE), growth rates, and competitive intensity can be misleading, some argue.

DSP Mutual Fund’s December 2024 Netra report notes that nearly one-third of Indian companies in the Nifty 50 have maintained a 10-year average ROE that is above 20% as of October 2024, second only to the US. Adjusted for cost of capital and growth rates, India fares better than China, Brazil, Mexico, and European markets.

And then there’s the long view.

Naren said last month at the IFA Galaxy Summit that short-term valuations may not matter for anyone who stays invested for two decades.

Naren has 35 years of experience in the investment industry. He pointed out that “very few will be 20-year SIP [systematic investment plan] investors”, and that they remain rare in the fund house’s well-known ICICI Prudential Value Discovery Fund too.

There’s yet another catch: most fund managers and investors haven’t seen a prolonged bear phase or a redemption cycle. Around three-quarters of fund managers have less than three years of experience.

It’s not that small- and mid-caps started correcting after Naren’s speech; they were already on a downward slide. 

Are mid- and small-caps still overvalued? Maybe. Does India’s growth story justify the premium? That’s anyone’s guess. But when valuations stretch, promoters offload, and investor sentiment runs hot, it’s worth considering whether we are drinking the Kool-Aid.

Regards,

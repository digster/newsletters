The Reserve Bank of India (RBI) probably thought it was doing something radical by creating a new category of lenders called small finance banks (SFBs) in 2015. 

The idea was that these banks would cater to low-income families that often fall through the cracks in commercial banking. There was ample validation for RBI's proposal—72 financial institutions applied for the first set of SFB licenses, of which 10 were chosen. 

But six years on, the idea of SFBs is just not as appealing.

Only four entities sought its nod to become SFBs in April this year. Most non-bank lenders and microfinance institutions (MFIs), the ideal candidates for SFBs, are nowhere near as excited as they once were. And Paytm Payments Bank and Airtel Payments Bank passed on the opportunity after considering applying.  

This is despite SFBs growing their loan book and deposits to $15 billion and $11.5 billion, respectively, in the year ended March 2021. 

So what's repelling potential applicants? 

For one, SFBs can't rely on deposits from the kind of people they lend to, simply because those people don't have enough money to put away. So SFBs have to go after commercial banks' customers by offering them higher interest rates. 

They are also shackled by RBI's mandatory listing requirement, which forces them to go public before their business is mature enough. As a result, they are valued poorly—something RBI should have anticipated.

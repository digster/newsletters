Imagine walking into a café every morning, grabbing your usual chai, nodding at the cashier, and strolling out without paying. No one stops you. No one asks for money. After a while, you start thinking—maybe chai is just free now?

That’s been the story of UPI in India. For years, you’ve been tapping, scanning, and paying without a second thought. But someone was always covering the bill for those transactions. And now that someone just decided it’s time to put the wallet away.

With UPI subsidies slashed by over half for FY25, the payments industry is in a bind. Phonepe, Paytm*, and the rest have been burning cash to keep transactions free, and in FY24 alone, payment processors may have collectively lost up to Rs 2,700 crore. The government’s new MDR policy introduced some charges on select transactions, but it’s like using a bandage to fix a broken dam.

So now, we have a standoff. Either merchants start paying, or the system starts cracking. But no one wants to make the first move—because the second someone slaps a fee on UPI, businesses might just run back to cash. And then what?

Sure, UPI is too big to fail—it’s processing over 18 billion transactions a month. But it can’t run on fumes forever.

Ateesh breaks it all down with some cold, hard math. If the government keeps resisting fees, it’s essentially asking the banking and payments ecosystem to subsidise India’s payments forever—losing crores in the process. And if fintechs don’t find a revenue model, their UPI-driven growth is a house of cards. The question isn’t whether UPI should charge fees but who should pay, how much, and how soon. Are we in the “enshittification” phase of Indian consumer tech?

From cross-selling insurance for a product that absolutely doesn’t need it to trying to charge more for a subscription that you’ve already paid for—companies are trying everything under the sun to extract more from customers for not-so-different and sometimes unsatisfactory experiences.

In the latest episode of Two by Two, hosts Rohin Dharmakumar and Praveen Gopal Krishnan discuss the enshittification of Indian consumer tech products. How has the Internet, which was supposed to be our respite from the problems of the real world, ended up with products mirroring the same behaviours? Joining them for the discussion are Aditya Suresh, head of India equity research at Macquarie, and Abhishek Madan, ex-VP of Product at Paytm.

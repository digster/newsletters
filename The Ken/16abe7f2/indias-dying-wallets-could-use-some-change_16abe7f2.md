There’ve been many analogies of the shrinking half-life of modern tech companies, but none as provocative as what Benedict Evans, a partner at the VC firm Andreessen Horowitz, tweeted earlier this week.
“Tech monopolies tend not to fall like Rome. They fall like Venice. They’re still there, and no-one actually invades them, but the trade routes moved, the things that gave them power and wealth stop mattering, and they become just another city, and then a backwater.”
Before you shoot angry emails to me on the history of Rome and Venice, remember it’s an analogy. One that helps understand what has happened to digital wallets in India. Soon after their meteoric rise with demonetisation, the government’s Unified Payments Interface brought stagnation. (Punitive regulation didn’t help.) Today, digital wallets face an existential challenge.
In order to drive merchant acceptance, wallet operators agreed to ruinously low merchant discount rates. On the customer side, even after spending hundreds of millions of dollars of venture capital on acquisition, they earn next to nothing. Though they have tried everything under the sun, none of those guarantee any kind of sustained revenues, let alone profit.
“Paytm has just announced its intention to issue Visa debit cards as well. It’s unlikely to do anything for Paytm as these will be distributed free of charge and will not generate any serious earnings. In fact, Visa will want to tokenise the cards and Paytm will end up paying incremental fees for this as well.”
That’s Ateesh Tankha writing in today’s story. But for wallets, he argues, there may yet be a way out—by focusing on the B2B market, provided they learn from their past mistakes.
It’s hard to miss the irony that Ateesh, who spent nearly 10 years at the Citigroup (in the cards division), is writing on a day when the largest wallet company, Paytm*, launched a credit card with Citibank.
It’s a free story. Read, share and debate if wallets are getting Venice-d.

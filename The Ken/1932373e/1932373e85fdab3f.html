Tata-owned Indian Hotels’ art of being all things to all people. Five years ago, the story couldn’t have been more different
By Seetharaman G
4 min read

A hotel company must’ve really screwed up not to have taken advantage of the persistent demand for business and leisure travel over the past couple of years, after the Covid slump. Little surprise, then, that hotel chains across the board have had an enviable run.

There have been quarters in which some of them even doubled their business. But that’s not why I chose to write this edition.

Take any listed company in this business, and it’s instantly apparent who its typical guest is. EIH, which runs the Oberoi chain of hotels, targets the big spender with a taste for luxury. Lemon Tree Hotels is meant for those who don’t care for, or can’t afford, the trimmings of a ritzy hotel. It’s not that these companies are monolithic; Lemon Tree, for instance, runs hotels ranging from Rs 3,500–9,000 (US$41–107) on average per night.

But no one has quite taken it as far as Indian Hotels Company, whose market value recently crossed Rs 1 lakh crore. Famed for its Taj chain of top-tier hotels, the Tata Group enterprise is now as much about its cheaper variants, with none as prominent as Ginger, its budget-hotel brand.

With 70 hotels, Ginger is second only to Taj in footprint. Sooner than later, it’ll eclipse the latter.

“The potential of Ginger in India is anything between 500 to 1,000 hotels over the next 10 to 15 years. It’s a high margin business because it’s built more on a variable cost model than a fixed cost model. So, it’s more crisis resistant. You can invest, you can do revenue share, you can do lease. It’s not like blocking your capital in one big asset,” [Indian Hotels MD & CEO Puneet Chhatwal] added.

Ginger & Spice: Tata-backed IHCL’s new business has growth flavour, The Economic Times

A room at Ginger costs under Rs 4,000 a night on average. Staying at one of Taj’s palace-turned-hotels can set you back by over Rs 50,000. Two wildly divergent experiences. Not to mention a whole bunch of options in between. All offered by a single company. Whoever said you can’t be all things to all people?

But Ginger didn’t always spark joy for Indian Hotels.

Launched in 2004 with Rs 1,000 rooms, Ginger struggled for years to find its footing. In the 2010s, aggregators such as Oyo* and Treebo began to poach Ginger’s consumers with cut-throat pricing. I remember swearing off Ginger after staying at a couple of its hotels on reporting trips.

When Puneet Chhatwal landed the top job at Indian Hotels in late 2017, fixing Ginger was among his immediate priorities. It’s incredible how commonsensical his diagnosis of the problem was.

“Customers don’t care what the room size is in the value segment, and they don’t want to pay for a banquet hall if they are not using it. However, a key expectation is a seamless broadband experience and we are working towards providing that in all Ginger hotels. We need to make Ginger a bit more fun by playing with colours, style of furniture, lighting, and by offering WiFi connectivity.”

Ginger Hotels to don new avatar as Taj Group battles losses, Moneycontrol

The “lean luxe” strategy for Ginger worked.

In FY23, after seven years of losses, the chain turned the corner. In the latest September quarter, Ginger’s top line grew by more than half, and its margins moved up considerably. The period was great for Indian Hotels as a whole, too, with its revenue up nearly 30%, and profits almost 50%, from the year-ago levels.

Not surprisingly, Indian Hotels’ occupancy rate has been among the highest in its peer group in recent quarters.

What’s more, the Indian Hotels stock has run circles around those of its competitors, surging nearly 80% over the past year. The company trades at 58X forward earnings, considerably higher than Lemon Tree’s 40X. What’s interesting here is that Indian Hotels is counting not just on its pricey hotels to shore up its bottom line. Ginger isn’t far behind them on profitability.

At the same time, growth isn’t merely restricted to Indian Hotels’ affordable variants. Four out of the 10 rooms the company plans to add by March 2027 will be in Taj hotels.

Hotel chains are often too sanguine while the going is good, and they end up with far more rooms than they need during leaner times. But their near-death experience during the pandemic might have made them change their ways.

The compact has been broken
Rohin
"It's not that the compact has been broken. The compact will be honoured," [said](https://www.businesstoday.in/current/economy-politics/fm-sitharaman-govt-pay-states-share-gst-compact-hasnt-been-broken/story/391661.html) India’s Finance Minister Nirmala Sitharaman in December last year. She was speaking at an event in New Delhi hosted by The Hindustan Times newspaper.
The compact she was referring to was struck between India’s central government and its states in 2017, when they collectively agreed to the creation of a unified value-added tax called the ‘Goods and Services Tax’ (GST). Prime Minister Narendra Modi called it the [“Good and Simple Tax”](https://www.hindustantimes.com/india-news/midnight-gst-show-rings-in-india-s-biggest-tax-reformr/story-HAzhTBqE7HF2J7QVbum0vK.html) as he launched it into action with the press of a button at midnight inside India’s Parliament. He had opposed the GST when he was the chief minister of the state of Gujarat.
“GST is a tribute to the maturity and wisdom of India’s democracy,” said India’s then President Pranab Mukherjee at the launch.
The GST put an end to a 14-year turf war between India’s states and its central government over 20 different taxes that businesses needed to pay across states.
The reason it had dragged on for so long was because states had to voluntarily agree to giving up their right to tax goods and services sold within their borders so that there would now be just one tax, the GST. The ice was broken when the central government [agreed](http://gstcouncil.gov.in/sites/default/files/GST-Compensation-to-States-Law.pdf) to compensate the states till 2022 for their loss of future revenue from their own taxes.
In their collective wisdom, the center and states agreed that the compensation would be [based on](https://www.prsindia.org/node/511253/chapters-at-a-glance) an assumed annual growth rate of 14% of states’ tax revenues, starting from 2015-16.
That assumption would turn out to be very rosy even before India got struck by the coronavirus.
By December 2019, several states were [threatening](https://www.businesstoday.in/current/economy-politics/fm-sitharaman-govt-pay-states-share-gst-compact-hasnt-been-broken/story/391661.html) to take the Centre to India’s Supreme Court for long delays in being paid their due compensation.
|
Kerala had last week threatened to drag the Centre to the Supreme Court over delay in compensation to states. Notably, the Centre is yet to pay states' GST share due for the past four months. The finance ministers of Kerala, Delhi, Rajasthan, Punjab, Chhattisgarh and Madhya Pradesh had even met the FM in this regard this week.
|
|
|
With states having signed away their individual rights to tax, they were [dependent](https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/news/economy/finance/the-pushback-over-delays-in-gst-transfers-by-the-centre-is-the-latest-instance-of-states-growing-assertion-on-the-fiscal-front/articleshow/72623644.cms) on the Centre. And its promise to compensate them.
|
“It is demeaning and embarrassing,” says Manpreet Singh Badal, the finance minister of Punjab. “We signed away our financial sovereignty but are now being denied the compensation promised to us.
|
|
|
|
States do not even have the ability to borrow themselves, because fiscal prudence rules limit their borrowings to 3% of their Gross Domestic Product (GDP). Beyond that, they need permission from the Centre.
Meanwhile, by December 2019, India’s GDP growth rate had already [crashed](https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/news/economy/finance/view-come-up-with-a-fair-distribution-mix-for-tax-proceeds-b/w-centre-and-states/articleshow/72390545.cms) to half of what the Centre had forecast.
Then came the coronavirus pandemic.
What was merely a slowly growing economy turned into one that will contract. Assumptions of 14% GDP growth per annum must now confront the reality of a [-6.1% GDP contraction](https://www.thehindu.com/business/Economy/indias-gdp-to-contract-61-in-fy21-nomura/article32149819.ece).
Last month, the Centre started releasing trial balloons to escape from its obligations to the states. It was an [“act of God”](https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/news/economy/finance/gst-mop-up-hit-by-covid-act-of-god-centre/articleshow/76380282.cms), said its unnamed officials to the media.
But it turned out that there was no provision of such a cop-out (a “force majeure”) in the Central Goods and Services Tax (Compensation to States) Act, 2017.
So, the Centre went and asked for the legal opinion of its top law officer, the Attorney General of India. And he said, [the compact does not exist](https://www.business-standard.com/article/economy-policy/centre-not-obliged-to-pay-for-shortfall-in-gst-compensation-fund-ag-120073001207_1.html).
|
The Attorney General has opined that there is no obligation on the central government to pay the GST compensation shortfall to states and the GST Council has to decide on ways to make good any shortfall, sources said.
After the GST Council's meeting in March, the Centre had sought views from Attorney General K K Venugopal -- who is the chief legal officer of the government -- on the legality of market borrowing by the council to make good any shortfall in the compensation fund.
The AG in his view said there is no obligation on the central government to pay the GST compensation shortfall, according to the sources.

Saving for a rainy day
Ben
Local banks in Singapore have been advised by the Monetary Authority of Singapore to cap their dividend payout for the year ended March 2020 at 60% of dividends from the previous financial year. This is an effort to conserve capital while [balancing](https://www.channelnewsasia.com/news/business/mas-local-banks-cap-fy2020-dividends-conserve-capital-12973564?cid=FBcna) the interest of shareholders. Scrip or new shares in the bank was also offered as an alternative to paying out dividends, even as the banks’ capital positions are considered “strong” by the MAS.
Banks traditionally pay out dividends in two tranches, and some may have already given the first interim dividend. Banks who have may have to either cut down the next tranche of dividends, offer scrip instead or not give the tranche at all to meet the 60% cap
|
|
All the same, MAS wants to ensure the banks’ capital buffers remain ample in the face of significant uncertainties ahead, so that they can sustain lending to the economy. We have carefully calibrated the restriction on dividends, taking into account the needs of investors who may rely on this income.
|
Ravi Menon, managing director, [MAS](https://www.mas.gov.sg/news/media-releases/2020/mas-calls-on-local-banks-to-moderate-fy2020-dividends)

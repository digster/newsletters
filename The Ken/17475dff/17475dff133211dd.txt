What comes to mind at the mention of an electric vehicle?

Price? Speed? Range? Model? Tesla (stock price)? Or battery?

Battery, I’d bet, is the last thing. Simply because it’s integral to an EV; without it, an EV is just a metal frame. In fact, buying an EV without a fitted battery sounds bizarre.

Yet, that’s what India's Ministry of Road Transport and Highways permitted in August with the stroke of a notification. EVs can be sold and registered without batteries, which, in turn, will be sold and registered independently.

A small policy twist has divided the players: EV manufacturers like Mahindra Electric, which have built-in batteries in their vehicles, are unhappy; fleet owners like Ola Electric and Battery-as-a-Service (BaaS) companies like SUN Mobility are delighted.

Some see it as the government keeping its promise to reduce the cost of EV ownership. Especially at a time when auto sales have nosedived, leaving only tiny green shoots in EVs and personal mobility options.

Detaching the battery from the EV halves the upfront cost of an electric two- or three-wheeler. The upside is the unlocking of new business models and leaving the choice of a battery to the vehicle owner. (The latter is contentious.)

For EV makers, though, the challenge of providing a safety and performance guarantee with an external battery is non-trivial. It outweighs the uptick they might see in sales from lowered pricing.

Between OEMs and BaaS providers, two different types of business models and incentives are at loggerheads. One may look at Taiwan or China for ideas, but there are some local and thorny issues—including the all-important goods and services tax—to deal with, says Olina in her story today.

Good Morning Ishan,
 
It feels inopportune, on this Sunday, to bring up the “Wednesday Society”. The society was a group of psychologists who would meet on Wednesday evenings at Sigmund Freud’s home to discuss their profession over tea and cakes, heralding the beginning of the entire psychoanalytic movement.
 
Although it is largely associated with Freud and his disciple, Carl Jung, I recently learnt about a third giant in the field—one who would often disagree with Freud—called Alfred Adler. His teachings are distilled in a book called The Courage to be Disliked, which argues that all your problems, with being yourself and doing what you want to do, are because you don’t have the courage to be disliked.
 
Being okay with being disliked grants the freedom to do whatever you truly believe in.
 
The concept struck me because journalists have always had to deal with it. Whatever one writes finds both lovers and haters, but neither, in any amount, is a sign of a job well done. Instead, during my short time in our newsroom, I’ve seen writers draw comfort from their conviction—in the facts they unearth and the arguments that tie them together—whatever may be the repercussions of publishing them. 
 
Thanks to a unique subscriber base that values exactly these traits, this gives us the freedom and the courage to produce consistently high-quality business journalism.
 
On Monday, Munsif made his debut at The Ken with a fabulous story on the nebulous content moderation practices at ShareChat and Moj. A few subscribers expressed their dislike for some of its points in the comments section, but it only brought about a diverse set of views and counterviews.
 
Our next story, by Bhumika, was about Inshorts’ short video app called Public. Although Public is well-liked by investors and over 50 million users, it must still confront rivals with more diversified revenue streams.
 
On Wednesday, Arundhati wrote about India’s small finance banks. They might be lending in amounts that are pocket-change for bigger banks, but make up for it with large volumes. Still, they are held back not only by a complex regulatory environment but also by their own design.
 
With India’s second wave proving to be an acid test for healthcare institutions across the country, Ruhi analysed the situation through a new lens in her story: how are hospitals in both large and small cities preparing to survive? Despite predictions for a third and fourth wave, they might have to diversify beyond covid-related treatments.
 
Our last story of the week dissected JioMart’s performance since its grand launch in May last year. Despite a funding war chest amassed by parent Reliance Industries, JioMart hasn’t made much headway in its ambition to become ‘Desh ki nayi dukaan’. But even as it stumbled in its plans to woo kirana (mom-and-pop) stores, it has made significant inroads into online grocer. 
 
Praveen wrote a fitting, detailed follow-up in this week’s Nutgraf about another Jio struggle—how, despite building a telecom network that conquered India and topping it up with phones and custom apps, it is still stuck in its search for a display driver that usually sells for $1 in the market. 
 
This week in our daily newsletter, Beyond the First Order, Anand wrote about the jackpot that Adar Poonawalla has hit, courtesy vaccines and financial services; Rohin dug up Huawei's PR crisis in the UK that could set a precedent for other countries; and Nithin crashed the crypto party to understand what the hype bubble popping could mean.
 
We hope you enjoyed this week’s stories, newsletters, and emails. Do write in with your feedback, both good and bad. And if there is something holding you back, do you think reading The Courage to be Disliked would help? I might pick it up—not that I need any help with being disliked—so do write in with your thoughts!

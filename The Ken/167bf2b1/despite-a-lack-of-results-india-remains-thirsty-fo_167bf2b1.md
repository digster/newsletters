Weather modifications elicit strong responses. Everywhere. If deteriorating conditions denote human failure, modifying them denotes the triumph of human abilities.
The atmospheric science specialist at IIT-Kanpur, who’s all set to run a two-day cloud seeding experiment to assuage Delhi’s air pollution woes, believes in the latter. “When you have a fever, you’re given antibiotics. Not chastised over why you fell ill,” he says, in response to whether air pollution requires preventive, not mitigative solutions.
SN Tripathi is proud this would be the first truly indigenous cloud seeding programme, with everything from aircraft to C-band radars being Indian. While it still sounds knee-jerk, like several earlier attempts for state-wise rainfall, at least it’s not on borrowed plumes this time.
Cloud seeding is imprecise, even though chemists and atmospheric scientists at General Electric set the precedent as early as the late ‘40s and through the ‘50s. (Kurt Vonnegut’s short stories capture this well, not the least because his brother worked at GE.) Some 56 countries have tinkered with clouds since. To enhance rain. To increase snowfall. To suppress hail. To dissipate fog. To reduce air pollution.
But the results are often clouded.
In 2017, Pune’s Tropical Meteorology institute began a $35-million (Rs 250 cr) experiment to quantify the feasibility of hygroscopic seeding. In an ideal scenario, this experiment could give a definitive thumbs up or down to cloud seeding in 2020. But few want to wait that long. Fairly so, but are they equally knee-jerk as the politicians have been in the past?
The Uttar Pradesh government, which is in talks with Tripathi for cloud seeding to combat drought and air pollution in five Bundelkhand districts, is watching closely.

Why do my parents refuse to buy new clothes? There’s a chasm between generations in how they spend money
By Rounak Kumar Gunjan
7 min read
View in browser

Good Morning Ishan,

You are reading our subscriber-only newsletter Ka-Ching!—a weekly commentary on fintech and personal finance

Do you feel broody, reflective, and contemplative as temperatures start to cool down and the year draws to a close? I do. It’s like an annual ritual: I spend the second half of December pondering the year gone by and plan for the next one.

A couple recent events have only made me think more deeply.

For instance, this weekend, while folding my summer clothes so they could be stored away, I noticed a sad pattern: I have more items of clothing than my parents, and a lot of them are newer than what my parents have in their wardrobe. Invariably, mine are more expensive. But how did this come to be? And… why?

I wanted an answer. That night, when my air-fried tofu and their chapati and veggies were on the dinner table, I asked about this.

“Why do you not buy clothes more often? Instead, why do you keep offering to buy more for me?” I asked them.

My mother kept her eyes locked on her plate, smiled, and continued eating—she knew what my father was about to say.

“We have enough…” he said.

I was expecting this answer. I’m sure you were too. The conversation went on for about 15 more minutes. The conclusion: they believe it’s their responsibility to spend on me, and it’s also my responsibility to spend on me. Both make them happy.

I didn’t agree, but vocalising that would have killed the quiet, wintery vibe. I just nodded, then spent the rest of the weekend ruminating and browsing online to pick out new outfits for them.

Save or spend

Cut to Tuesday. I met a senior banker in Gurugram. He will be retiring next year, after spending more than three decades working for lenders like HDFC Bank, ICICI Bank, and other institutions. Something he said gave me a new perspective on money-related behaviours.

“Frugality as a value has fewer takers among the younger generation. More importantly, younger folks don’t mind spending on themselves,” said the 59-year-old.

It sort of explained my parents’ behaviour. But it was still a generalised observation. I asked him to be more specific and drive his point home.

“Think of retirement. Until recently, parents did not have a solid retirement plan. They spent all their money on their children’s upbringing, education, their marriage, buying them a house, and so on. Their retirement plan was their children,” he said.

The difference in spending patterns between my parents and me was starting to make more sense. The senior banker had more to say:

“Why do you think FIRE [financial independence, retire early] as a concept became famous in India only in the last 5–7 years? Apart from rising disposable incomes and finfluencer marketing, it’s a change in mindset. Younger folks are much better at prioritising themselves.”

He was making sense but I wanted more feedback, so I reached out to an old connection—Mausumi Manna. She was my microeconomics professor at Calcutta University. Apart from nurturing fantastic students (count me out), she studies evolving spending patterns. She agreed with the banker and had more to add.

Security or adventure

Parents of millennials, Manna said, view money as a means of security. “Especially in India, splurging wasn’t even an option because of the financial situation of the masses. Spending on oneself was considered to be self-centered and greedy. That has stayed in the minds of older folks,” said Manna.

To people who are my parents’ age, money is meant to create safety nets. “More clothes” simply isn’t a real choice.

Millennials were brought up with similar ideals, added Manna. They were taught to save first and then spend. But there was a mismatch between what they were told to do and the situations they experienced. By the time millennials left school and started earning their own incomes, a lot had changed.

First, social media flattened the way we consume information. Fads, trends, and ideas from all over the world flashed across smartphone screens in India. Millennials around the country watched their overseas counterparts spend on things that might seem like a waste of money to their parents.

“What percentage of older folks own an iPhone? Far fewer than millennials, right? Not because they don’t have enough money or are not tech-savvy. It’s because they don’t feel that the expenditure serves enough utility,” said Manna.

Having said that, Manna added that millennials are still cautious about money because of their upbringing. Spending Rs 30,000 on a shirt would still make most millennials hesitate for a beat. For their parents, that price tag is simply outlandish. There are, of course, exceptions.

“But—” Manna let out a soft laugh. “Gen Zers and their children wouldn’t mind spending at all.”

And why would they? The world around them is one of relative abundance and fewer financial restrictions. There is much broader variety in the products they can buy, they can make payments with a tap of the finger, and “buy now, pay later” schemes make impulse purchases all the more gratifying. Phew!

An economic adviser at EY, DK Srivastava, used an example to sum up the spending patterns of different generations.

Most older folks in India have never thought of owning an expensive car. It wasn’t really an ambition for them, he said.

Millennials, on the other hand, want to own a luxury car. It is a motivational factor for them. Some have achieved that goal, then moved on to seek new experiences.

Gen Zers believe owning a luxury car is the norm, so they don’t really seek experiences; they expect them. Spending money on new experiences is an extension of their collective outlook.

It’s no wonder, then, that Indian brands are doing everything they can to get on the good side of Gen Zers. This generation influences 43% of household consumption, contributing US$860 billion to India’s economy, of which US$200 billion comes directly from their own earnings, according to a joint report by Boston Consulting Group (BCG) and Snap Inc.

It isn’t just brands. Banks understand this pattern as well. The Gurugram-based banker shared that the marketing plans for products like loans and credit cards also take age into account.

“For instance, home loans are usually for the entire family. The new house comes with a sense of security for the borrower, so these loans are for the older folks,” he said. “But co-branded credit cards, personal loans—these are mostly used by the younger folks who want to spend on themselves.”

Having spoken with Manna and the banker, I think I’m better equipped to convince my parents to add a few new items to their wardrobe. I’ll let you know if they budge in the next edition.

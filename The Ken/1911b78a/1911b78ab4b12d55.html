In the spotlight

Poonawalla-backed Wellness Forever was unrivalled as a half-pharmacy, half-grocer. Then came quick commerce

The rise of Blinkit and Zepto may have queered the pitch for the one-of-its-kind Indian drugstore chain

With a mere 400 stores, Wellness is far behind Apollo Pharmacy and Medplus Health and is restricted to just four states. But unlike its peers, Wellness gets half its revenue from non-pharma products and has for years served as a quasi-supermarket in parts of Mumbai. This distinct positioning is now under attack from the 10-minute promise of quick commerce. Wellness’ revenue expanded 8% in FY24, which was half the growth it had seen the previous year. Cars24 wades deeper into used car loans when even banks play it safe 

As Cars24 chases profit with its non-bank arm, its key business of buying and selling used cars has taken a back seat

Cars24 initially set up its now-profitable financing unit as a lever to sell more used cars. Partnering with the likes of Bajaj Finance, it adopted an aggressive growth strategy for its non-bank arm. With the core business now slowing down, the company is now planning to offer new financial products under Loans24. However, it still needs to fix the quality of its own books, which have been strained by defaults and subprime customers. [Read the full story]

We need to have a chat about Zepto 

No one saw the young upstart grocery delivery company, led by founders barely out of their teens, as perhaps the biggest disruptive force emerging in Indian retail. Or is Zepto merely overhyped?

Many vastly underestimated Zepto’s true disruption potential. A company that came out of seemingly nowhere and became a phenomenon that everyone was trying to decode. One started by two literal teenagers because they wanted stuff delivered in 10 minutes, which is now worth $3.6 billion and painting targets on the backs of rivals and categories alike. Is Zepto for real?

The real reason behind Ola Electric slashing its IPO valuation in a booming stock market 

No, it has nothing to do with leaving money on the table for investors

Ola Electric’s IPO, which opens today, pegs the electric two-wheeler leader’s valuation at US$4 billion—25% less than what it was less than a year ago. Overpromises and under-delivery of rosy past projections in previous private fund-raises have come home to roost for the company. In a booming stock market, there’s the rich irony of a downround IPO. Calling it an “attractive” price for new investors is making a virtue out of necessity. Ola Electric, in need of a quick cash boost, is settling for a valuation cut. Even then, the offering falls short for investors. [Read the full story]

Meet the ex-Tata CEO with a plan to make up for India’s lost time in chipmaking 

After more than 40 years in the chip industry, Raja Manickam has founded a company and devised a business model which, he believes, is the only way to scale semicon products in India

A fab in India is a few years away but P Raja Manickam of iVP Semiconductor is buying wafers and making chips. But not in the conventional way. The world is his design house and Taiwan, his foundry. Projecting US$70–100 million in revenues in the first 3–4 years, it’s the fastest route for iVP to market and scale its bet. Maybe like Morris Chang, who founded TSMC at 55, Manickam is old enough, and knows the industry well enough, to ride the chip disaggregation wave. [Read the full story]

Hire the wounded: a Philippine CEO’s strategy to build a new digital bank

After laying strong foundations for the Philippines’ biggest e-wallet, Albert Tinio is back at it again. The co-head of Gotyme shares his secret to convincing millions of people to bank digitally

Filipinos traditionally prefer to keep cash at home rather than in banks, but key institutions are changing this. One of these is Gotyme, a digital bank in the Philippines rapidly gaining users—adding a quarter of a million new users per month. Gotyme is co-headed by Albert Tinio, former CEO of the country’s largest e-wallet, known for onboarding previously financially excluded Filipinos. The Ken spoke to Tinio about his strategy leading a digital bank. His tactics are unconventional but notable, as Gotyme is on track to reach 5 million users within two years of its launch. RBI dug a pit for Razorpay. Razorpay just built a ladder 

The last quarter was the company’s best in two years as it scrambles to onboard merchants and tries to make the most of a year-long regulatory ban

In the seven months it came out of RBI’s embargo on onboarding new merchants, Razorpay has bagged some big names in its portfolio. Navigating the embargo period was tough. And the company had to undertake significant restructuring to grow while staying afloat. Even though the company didn’t cut any jobs during the period, its staffers feel the environment at the firm has changed. Raking in more revenues and seeing through a domicile flip are just a couple of Razorpay’s priorities before it can go public. [Read the full story]

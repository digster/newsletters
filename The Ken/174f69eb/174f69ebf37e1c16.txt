Good Morning Ishan,

It sounds too good to be true. Perhaps it even is.

10K surgeries in just over a year, 17X growth in centres in 11 months, 10X growth in outpatient consultations, 12X growth since Series A, 3X growth since Series B.

Valuation at the end of September? $250 million, following the latest $11 million funding round.

That’s Pristyn Care for you. The new Indian healthcare startup on the block, it has sprinted to such metrics—some in reality, some on its investors deck—in just 24 months. How? By renting spare capacity and surgical infrastructure of hospitals to perform surgeries for their “own” patients, via their “own” surgeons. 

Utilising excess capacity is a proven model. Before Uber and Airbnb took the world by storm, SETI@home was a pretty successful academic model, using spare computing in the world to search for extra-terrestrial intelligence.

Taking that idea to perform surgeries at scale and speed is interesting. Nearly 85% of surgeries in India happen in small (unorganised) hospitals. Many of them have unutilised capacity because they lost out to the trend of patients flocking to bigger, super specialty centres.

Pristyn burst on to the scene in August 2018 with its asset-lite model. It offered a bouquet of surgical procedures at rates most corporate hospitals couldn’t match—up to 40% cheaper. All of this is backed up by a relentless marketing and sales machine, funnelling prospective patients into clinics and operating theatres (OT). An employee calls it a “sophisticated tele-caller factory”.

If not dialled to high fulmination, this model can be effective. But to add a new layer of digital marketing to healthcare, where patients are counselled by non-medical staff, is an ethical grey zone.

For her story, Maitri visited a few centres to bring you a rich account of what’s going on. A few copycat startups have sprung up as well. Pristyn, backed by $27 million from Sequoia and other investors, may not be a curious creature for long.

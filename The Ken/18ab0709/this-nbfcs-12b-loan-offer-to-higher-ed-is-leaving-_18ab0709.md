Good Morning Ishan,
There’s something called the “Stamp’s Law”. It’s named after the British civil servant, statistician, and economist Josiah Stamp who warned that no matter how much a government may enjoy amassing statistics, “raise them to the nth power, take the cube root and prepare wonderful diagrams”, at the end it comes from an official “who just puts down what he damn pleases”.
I was reminded of this when I saw what the Indian government promised in 2017 when it set up a non-bank to finance higher-education institutions in India. It said loans up to $12 billion would be disbursed by 2022.
I don’t want to hazard a guess as to how that number was arrived at. Because calculating the eligible institutions’ payback ability must not have been that difficult.
The idea of a non-banking financial company (NBFC) funding capex at higher-education institutions through a loan without doing a simple (or compound) interest calculation also looks puzzling. To put it in perspective, a Rs 500 crore ($60 million) loan would eventually cost the government nearly double the amount over a 10-year period.
In 2023, four years after the first loan was given out by Hefa, the government’s cash budget dropped to zero. And the NBFC’s only customers, institutes and universities, are shying away from taking loans because they can’t bear the financial burden.
Now, the new education policy requires college enrolments to go up from 27% in 2020–21 to 50% by 2030. Who’s going to fund the larger hostels, classrooms, and laboratories?

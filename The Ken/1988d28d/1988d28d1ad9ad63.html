A few weeks ago, I asked subscribers of The Nutgraf to tell me how they are using AI at their workplaces. To find these answers, I created a (long!) survey with 20 questions—several of which were open-ended. 

The survey itself was pretty detailed because I asked about how people were using AI, what kind of tools they used, and what they used them for. I also asked questions about which teams are really adopting AI in organisations and which ones are reluctant. And finally, I asked some deeply personal questions about how and whether AI has changed core beliefs about people’s roles, jobs, and their careers.

Over 500+ subscribers took the survey, and well, let’s just say they poured their heart out. In fact, if you were at The Ken’s most recent event where we discussed product development in the age of AI, you’d have caught a glimpse of these responses.

There are many themes that emerged from the survey. For today’s edition, I’ll focus on how using AI is making people feel, rather than the specifics of how they’re using it—I’ll save that for another edition.

Which subscribers took the survey?

Broadly speaking, the respondents who answered the survey are representative of the median subscriber of The Ken—middle to senior executives, leaders, and founders who work in established companies and successful startups. Some of these people work at tech companies, others in tech-adjacent organisations. 

Unsurprisingly, these are the same people who are facing deep, existential questions about their careers thanks to AI. 

Over the last several editions of The Nutgraf, I’ve written about how AI is affecting these people in various ways. 

To begin, we had a nearly equal number of respondents who work at product or tech organisations and those who don’t.

Now that we know who these 500+ respondents are, we can break them down to see how they feel about using AI at work. Primarily, for this, I asked a simple question: Do you feel pressure to use AI at work? And for those who answered yes, I encouraged them to share more details. 

So what are the insights from these respondents?

Well, three things.

Everyone is feeling the heat from AI, and not just those in product and tech companies. 

Unsurprisingly, the pressure to use AI at work is much higher among those who work at product or tech organisations, but the numbers are closer than I expected. Around 47% of those who work at product or tech companies say they face pressure to use AI at work. For non-product and -tech organisations, that number is 35%. 

This seems like a big divergence—but one in three people who work in non-product/tech organisations face AI pressure, which I think is pretty significant. 

The bigger the company, the more the pressure to use AI at work. 

This is also interesting, because I expected smaller startups to feel the heat more than larger behemoths, but the numbers indicate otherwise. The numbers trend up as the company gets bigger, and sort of plateau at the 45% mark. 

View in Browser

Global companies are far more anxious about AI than their Indian counterparts

This is the most counterintuitive finding from this survey. If you work at a global company, you are far more likely to face pressure to adopt AI than if you are at an Indian one. 

Interestingly, this finding holds regardless of whether the company is public or private, which leads to one inescapable conclusion—global companies are much more paranoid about making their employees adopt AI than Indian ones.

Product managers and designers are feeling the most pressure to use AI  

At the recent event at The Ken, one of the recurring themes that kept popping up was the role of the product manager (PM) in this new world of AI. Many people felt the PM role might evolve into something more akin to sales. Others felt that it would move closer to engineering.

This anxiety is translating to roles too, with PMs feeling the maximum pressure to adopt AI in their role.

Here are some of the most interesting comments I received from subscribers about how they are feeling pressure to use AI at work. 

“I started a new job two months ago. In true startup fashion, I was given access to a bunch of things and left to figure out for myself how things work. This process would have taken 5–10X as long without using AI tools. Even the time taken to learn to use the AI tools for the first time is insignificant compared to the time saved.”

“So much hype around AI. Plus, we being the Consulting and IT services company, are expected to be ahead of the curve.”

“Upper management directive. We’ve been explicitly told by our line manager that using AI is a choice now but will not be one in the future so get used to it right away, which was a pretty stern warning.”

“There is a lot of chatter and noise around AI in the office and otherwise. Senior leadership is excited and wants the team to use and achieve results. Awareness and lack of imagination on the use cases is more at the middle-management level for driving efficiency and speed.”

“Because every Product Manager worth their salt is expected to be proficient in it and we have 15% of our roadmap dedicated to AI.”

“We are an AI company, so there is pressure to prove to ourselves and clients that we are the first to adopt internally. I don’t feel too much ‘pressure’ myself, since I’ve always been an early adopter of new tech.”

“FOMO amongst some of the management that we are missing a ‘transformative trend’ and we have hired an AI Head. However, SME leads who are tech-savvy (not in an IT coder way, but those who have used tech for research and project management work for decades) are unconvinced, especially given the very sketchy and unreliable results of AI queries. Also, IPR and original content is a major element of research, and AI fully blurs the lines of hygiene, especially when junior staff like research analysts indiscriminately and inefficiently use AI.”

“As PMs, there is a lot of content out there saying how the role will be eliminated or how everyone is vibe coding a new SaaS product and how the role will evolve. So even though my company does not put that sort of pressure, the feeling of FOMO based on how other PMs post about what they are doing forces me to upskill and learn using these AI tools.”

“Constant reminders that AI usage is not optional, weekly newsletters on how AI is being used in different teams, regular trainings/hackathons on AI usage.”

“It’s an evolving technology. There are levels to it. 1. Top management—They don’t want to be left behind. 2. Middle managers—Optics or personal brand management plus some level of work efficacy. 3. Lateral colleagues—Acceptability and quality of life. Even a mediocre AI generated result/presentation is perceived as a good output, higher returns on time and ease of work.”

“Our organisation has a public dashboard tracking individual level AI usage by day and tool.”

“There is pressure from investors and leadership to prove that we are not behind the cutting edge in tech. The AI departments have to push for numbers for their existence.”

“People using AI are much quicker. I’m convinced using AI is the only way to keep up, even though I personally don’t want to.”

“Performance evaluation criteria now mandates use of AI to improve productivity.”

I’ll end on a final statistic. 

The pressure to use AI at work is real, and organisations are using both the carrot and the stick approach—almost in equal measure. 

And employees are feeling this from both ends, but one is slightly stronger than the other. 

About 29% of all respondents said their organisation has provided some form of formal training in AI.

And 35% of all respondents believe that hiring has stopped or slowed down due to AI in their organisations. 

In a future edition, I’ll cover other insights from the survey, such as which tools are being used by employees, how exactly they are using it, and some of the most surprising and interesting things they discovered about themselves after using AI. 

But for now, the bottom line is that AI at work isn’t just optional anymore, more so for some than for others.

Good Morning Ishan,
Knee-jerk price control is a thing in India. Thanks to it, the poor are subsidising the rich. At least two leading hospital owners admit this.
First it was cardiac stents, then knee implants, and now cancer drugs. In March, the government capped the trade margins of 42 cancer drugs, reducing many brand prices by up to 87%. India’s largest cancer care chain of 26 hospitals, HCG, has challenged this decision in the Karnataka High Court.
For a surgeon entrepreneur of a listed company to battle government like this mustn’t have been easy. But when your house is on fire— and more single-specialty houses might be gutted if this trend continues—you take to extremes.
Like in stents, where it’s been hard to gauge if price control benefitted the patients since hospitals simply shifted the margin from one bucket to another, in cancer, the price cap will benefit chemotherapy patients but hurt those needing surgery.
In her story today, Suraksha says government-funded hospitals and, to an extent, multi-specialty corporate hospitals will not bear the brunt of this decision. The margins of manufacturers or importers are also untouched. It’s specialist cancer centres that will be forced to take drastic steps if HCG loses the case.
A policy to soften the sticker shock of drugs (and devices earlier) will have unintended consequences. Revealingly, some hospitals pre-emptively raised cancer procedure charges and capped medicine margins at 30% last year.
HCG founder and CEO Dr BS Ajaikumar is both hopeful and defiant. “I treat oral cancer patients and yet I am working with farmers to explore alternative crops for them to grow. I am ready to go out of business if I don’t get cancer patients. But is the government ready to ban, let’s say, cigarettes?” asks Dr Ajaikumar.

Good Morning Ishan,

Nvidia, the red-hot chipmaker, will announce its financials for the November–January period today. The company, whose graphics processing units are powering the artificial-intelligence (AI) boom, is within striking distance of a US$2-trillion market cap.

Right now, only three other companies are worth more: Microsoft, Apple, and Saudi Aramco.

But there’s one thing weighing on Nvidia investors’ minds: the 70% market share it has in AI chips is no longer unassailable. It’s not just rivals like AMD that want to whittle away at Nvidia’s dominance; Google reportedly spent US$2–3 billion last year creating its own AI chips, and Microsoft is testing its first.

But, as The New York Times pointed out last month, “this work is a balancing act between competing with Nvidia while working closely with the chipmaker and its increasingly powerful chief executive, Jensen Huang”.

A similar dynamic is at the heart of our Big Story today.

Until a few years ago, billionaire Mukesh Ambani’s e-commerce game was so weak as to be virtually non-existent despite owning the country’s largest retailer. So, Reliance Brands, which has licensed luxury labels such as Versace, Salvatore Ferragamo, and Balenciaga, heavily relied on Tata Cliq Luxury—an e-tailer from a storied group with which Reliance competes in several retail businesses.

Then, the pandemic made Reliance understand the importance of having its own Tata Cliq Luxury, which is exactly what Ajio Luxe is. Reliance allowed Ajio Luxe to find its feet, and then last year—in typical Reliance style—it decided it no longer needed Tata Cliq Luxury. Reliance’s top 20 brands accounted for half the value of goods sold on the platform in FY23.

To recover from a blow like this, Tata Cliq Luxury will need nothing short of a miracle, as Nuha writes today.

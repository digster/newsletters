As for this week’s episode of Two by Two, are you considering buying a car right now? If you are, I’m sure you’ve noticed you have a pretty panel of choices—one certainly far larger than even a handful of years ago. One reason is the recent profusion of electric models, a fair number of which whiz past us every day. 

But it’s also becoming clear that this is not the EV revolution we were promised.

Growth in sales of electric cars have slowed, as my colleague Nathan Narde reported last year, except for a recent bump during the festive season. And slowing adoption isn’t just an Indian phenomenon; 2024 saw sales slow in mature markets too. For instance, Tesla’s sales dipped 1.1% during the year, and other companies have downsized initially massive EV plans. 

Two by Two hosts Rohin Dharmakumar and Praveen offered one hypothesis during this week’s podcast for why EVs haven’t taken off as expected in India’s car market—“a narrative collapse”. A flaw in how EVs were sold to customers:

Electric is the future.

Electric will be cheaper in the long run.

It’s silent.

It’s cleaner.

All good arguments. But there is one really important thing that’s missing. 

To quote Dr Amitabh Saran, our first guest on the podcast and founder and CEO of Altigreen, the Bengaluru-based three-wheeler manufacturer:

Amitabh: Look at Tesla. 

Everyone talks about that. They talk about winged doors. Arey kya farak padta hai? (What difference does it make?) But the first vehicle that has to come out has to be such that people say, dude, ludicrous mode of driving! Oh my God! 

But who used something like that in India?

No one.

In India, we were saying ‘don’t drive like this’, ‘don’t drive too fast’, ‘avoid water and potholes and puddles’ and all these things.

So you set the narrative. You make it something that says this is a desirable vehicle.

Rohin: Nobody’s doing that, right? You make a very valid point. Who’s arguing for EVs? Who’s the EV champion among Indian car makers today?”

In short, EVs in India just don’t seem aspirational enough. In fact, most electric cars we see on the country’s roads today are built on the same design platforms as earlier ICE equivalents. And even setting aside the narrative flaws, there are other concerns if you are a prospective electric car owner, the biggest of which still remains range anxiety. Are you comfortable with the charging options along Indian roads right now? 

Our second guest, Awadesh Jha, executive director of Glida India, formerly known as Fortum Charge and Drive India, had much to say on that subject:

Awadesh: 300,000 cars would have been sold in the last two-and-a-half or three years. Now these 300,000 cars would require charging in public places or at home, right? 

Now, in India currently, we have roughly 10,000 to 12,000 charging points.

So if you have 10,000 to 12,000 charging points against 300,000 cars, it means you already have a 1:25 or 1:20 ratio, which is as good as the European countries. So in terms of numbers, we don’t have fewer (charging points).

Second, you know, when the people say that ‘no, charging infrastructure is a major concern’, at least globally, that’s… narratives which are being played by the vested interested parties, right? 

When 80% of charging happens at home, you drive 30 kilometers, 40 kilometers a day… How can you make public charging infrastructure a culprit? A villain?

[…]

Indian OEMs are always saying that, ‘Oh, public charging is missing. That’s why we are not able to sell the car.’ But at the same time, you are cannibalizing the public charging by selling your own chargers at home.

So you give a consumer a charger at home, and then you expect a private CPO to set up the public charging, and nobody will come to that public charging (station), then how would (CPOs) be profitable?

And all this was just in the first few minutes of a more than hour-long discussion, which branched into several other factors that have stalled the Indian EV growth story.

Here are a couple of other excerpts of subjects we covered.

Two- and three-wheelers chart a different path

An interesting point that came up was how electric two- and three-wheelers don’t seem to be suffering from the sluggishness affecting electric cars. Two-wheeler sales have been soaring, but perhaps that’s partly due to the kind of market India is.

Both two- and three-wheelers are high-utility vehicles for large sections of the country, and the attractive economic benefits of electric models have driven fast adoption. 

But do two-wheelers also need an aspirational pitch? This is perhaps a good place to share an excerpt from episode 12, featuring Ather Energy’s Tarun Mehta:

Bikes are the passion thing, right?

It’s the scooter guys who are really the commoditized segment.

It’s the commuter bike guys who are the commoditized segment.

If you’re buying a Splendor, you really don’t care about what you sit on.

But then you go meet a Splendor buyer.

And when they go to buy the Splendor, even today, they’ll go with the entire family, 12 people. It’s not just a little tilak ceremony that they will do. They will so passionately care about what they bought.

And the simple proof of that is that Hero gets to make something like 30% gross margin on Splendor, which, if you were an international observer, you would say, listen, that’s the most commoditized product in India, in bikes, right?

Ather Energy was a pioneer. Can it also be a leader?

Hybrids might stay a while

Amitabh: I do believe that hybrids will be adopted for a longer period of time in the emerging markets of the world. And by that, I mean India, South Asia, Africa, South America… I do believe that that is going to happen.

If you look at electric vehicles today, even though charging infrastructure is important, we say you should do home-based charging. In most of Delhi, though, you park on the street. 

These could be high-rise buildings, and you know, it’s the only parking you will have because they want to maximize selling floor space. How will you charge there?

Number two, you know, the whole idea of ‘this is actually a charging point for my vehicle’. A security guard is not supposed to take it out to charge his phone and then forget to plug it back in. Reality, I’m telling you.

Number three, on average, we drive 35 kilometres a day. When you buy an EV, the conversation is more like…

‘How much is the range in this?’

‘250 kilometres.’

‘That’s it. Only 250 kilometres?’

‘Sir, where do you want to go?’

‘Oh, I want to drive to Goa.’

‘Sir, when did you last drive to Goa?’

China, too, came up during the conversation. And both guests had a lot to say about why they strongly believe EVs are the future and how they’re much safer than ICE vehicles, among other things. 

Listen to the full episode for a thorough breakdown of what the future of EVs might look like in India. We’ll be back next Thursday with a new episode of Two by Two.

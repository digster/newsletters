Good Morning Ishan,
 
When Covid's second wave broke the back of India's healthcare system, there was nowhere to hide. Each day, we saw patients line up outside all types of hospitals, begging for assistance that  hospitals were struggling to provide. The immediate impact of the pandemic has been brutal on these hospitals. But there's an underlying, structural problem that's unfurling for major names like Narayana Healthcare, Manipal, and Apollo.

Covid has turned hospital math on its head. Hospitals in bigger cities like Delhi and Mumbai are struggling to get to pre-pandemic revenues. Simply put, there aren't enough people traveling from other countries (or even smaller cities) for complex surgeries and tertiary care. Combined with Covid-capped prices, big hospitals are scraping through. Their loss, though, has been someone else's gain. Patients who would usually gravitate to big-city facilities are instead making do with smaller, local hospitals for fear of traveling during the pandemic. As Ruhi reports in her deeply nuanced story, in some cases, small 50-100 bed hospitals have even ramped up to 130% their normal capacity.

The wrecked bottom lines might take a while to recover. But the bigger hospitals are now trying out a different mix of services to attract patients from far and wide. Teleconsulting, naturally, is an avenue they are keen on. Hospitals in tier-2 and -3 cities are learning new tricks too—e-ICUs, a critical care facility that connects patients digitally to a specialist in a bigger city—might be the new post-pandemic normal.

Right now, there's no guarantee that a third or fourth wave won't be as devastating as the second, especially with India falling drastically behind on its vaccination goals. But hospitals, big and small, have to think about survival.

Good afternoon Ishan,

I cannot remember when or how it started. I just started calling him. 

On my way to the airport. Early mornings. In the cab, sitting behind, I’d just call his number and he’d pick up. 

A scratchy, long drawn hello. And me, “Good morning Dadu (Grandfather).” And then we’d talk. About this and that. My work. His health. My salary. His son’s whereabouts (that’s my dad, of course). He’d ask me where I am going, for how long. I’d tell him about the story I am chasing, the people I am going to meet. We’d chat for five, ten minutes or so, in the pregnant, quietness of a still rising metropolis, in a black & yellow with windows rolled down, eager to reach its destination. 

I still do this. Every time. Minus, the black and yellow. 

It is like a drill. My grandpa knows what’s going to happen, but he has never asked me why I’ve called, or why at that particular odd hour only. I’ve often wondered myself. Why? 

I suspect I know the answer. It has been 15 years since I left home in search of a career. The calls help me keep in touch with something, someone, anything at all, that feels like I’m not too far away. Not forgotten. Not unloved. Early mornings are simply convenient. I imagine my grandfather making tea, and us chatting. Just like the good, old days, when he’d wake me up to study in the morning, after he’d made his tea and I’d take whatever’s left, and we’d chat. After all, not even the superhuman can have tea and study at the same time. 

In those 10 minutes, I imagine, I’m home. 

For the longest time, human beings have been travelling across towns, cities and countries in search of living and employment. Leaving behind a place that was once home. The idea of Going Home, a series of personal essays, is to send writers to the places they grew up in. To capture change from the writer’s lens. How the place has changed (or not changed) over the years from how they remember it. How the writers themselves have changed over the years to become what they are today. And even what "home" means to them. 

Going Home is part travelogue, part nostalgia, part reflections, part insights. To tell these stories, we’ve picked some of the best writers in the country. One each for East, West, North, South. 

Lathika George will be writing on Mumbai. She lives in Kodaikanal. 

Prem Panicker will be writing on Chennai. He lives in Bengaluru. 

Aditi Rao will be writing on the Kullu Valley. She lives in Delhi and Shimla. 

Janice Pariat will be writing on Shillong. She lives in Delhi.

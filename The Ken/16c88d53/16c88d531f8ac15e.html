Good Morning Ishan,
 
Growing up in Bangalore, the ubiquitous south Indian breakfast—dosas or idlis—has been a cornerstone of my existence. Access, undoubtedly played a large part. Having a dosa was as easy as visiting your nearest 'sagar' (south Indian breakfast joint), with one never further than a hop, skip and jump away.

In Delhi, not so much. Especially if you want a good dosa, or idlis that aren't salted pebbles. The alternative then, is to make them yourself. But have you ever tried making dosas or idlis from scratch? It isn't easy.

Mixie grinder or stone grinder? Iodised salt or rock salt? The careful mix between rice and lentils. Adding water to get that perfect consistency (assuming you even know what that is). Watching the temperature during the grinding process. The overnight fermenting.

Or, you can buy a ready-to-cook packet of dosa batter and be done with both cooking and eating in about 5-10 minutes. 
 
The sheer convenience this brings has rocketed iD Fresh, today's protagonist, to revenues of Rs 210 crore—double what it was some four years ago. It's now present across 30,000 stores in India alone, and that's despite the fact it has limited itself to south and west India. Clearly, if there's one thing people love more than dosas, it is convenience.

But iD now wants to cover all of India—after all, it has successfully entered both the US and UAE. North India, however, may be its biggest challenge yet. Because north Indians don't get south Indian breakfasts. Making them switch from paranthas and kulchas (Indian breads) to iD's ready-to-cook batters will be an uphill task.

Is iD worried? Not quite, write Sharath and Abinaya. Starting from a 50-square-feet kitchen in Bangalore to targeting Rs 1,000 crore in revenues within the next five years, you can bet PC Musthafa, iD's founder, has a few tricks up his sleeve: https://the-ken.com/story/id-fresh-north-india/ (12-minute read)

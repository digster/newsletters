"India is one of our priority markets."; "India is strategic for us."; "We’re investing aggressively into India."; "We’re very bullish on India."
 
These are some of the standard PR-created quotes visiting CEOs of international companies give the Indian press when they visit the country. Rarely do journalists press them on actual revenue or profits out of India.
 
Since today’s story was about LinkedIn, I did a quick DuckDuckGo search to see if CEO Jeff Weiner had made any such quotes. Of course he had.
 
"India continues to be one of our most important markets as our business continues to grow."; "India is one of our best-performing markets globally across a whole host of important consumer metrics."; "It is a unique market and of strategic importance to us."
 
Weiner gave those quotes in 2016. In today’s story, Vandana and Seema ask the questions that need to be asked. How exactly is India one of LinkedIn’s “best-performing markets globally” if its annual net profit a year ago was shy of $6 million, for a company whose annual revenue was $2.3 billion? How is India of “strategic importance” to LinkedIn if it hasn’t really created any truly localised products for a country that accounts for more than 57 million users? Even the pricing for many of its products is in US dollars. How is India one of LinkedIn’s “most important markets” if its annual growth was 19.3% compared to 130% for LinkedIn overall? Worse, that growth rate has been falling—46% to 24.4% to 19.3%.
 
Vandana and Seema explain why LinkedIn is stuck between aggressive players like Naukri, Facebook and Google on one side, and a very different workplace and hiring culture on the other. Here’s their story: https://the-ken.com/story/india-is-testing-linkedins-talent/ (12-minute read)
 
LinkedIn didn’t make any of its executives available to us for the story, but sent us emailed responses.

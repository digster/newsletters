Good Morning Ishan,

CRED is one of India’s most storied and envied startups. Not many startups can boast of raising seed investment of $30 million even before having a product to show. Fewer still go on to raise $120 million in a Series A round in short succession. 

And the best part? CRED doesn’t really sell anything of its own to customers.

Instead, it sells desire. The desire to maximise. The desire to multiply. The desire to play.

You see, CRED offers a credit card rewards programme. It presents a simple proposition to credit card users with high credit scores: use our app to pay your credit card bills. And in return, we’ll give you reward points that you can use to buy other products and services at a discount. 

For a while, things were good. The $150 million in the bank allowed CRED to rapidly bulk up to 200 people and build a beautiful looking app that touted “neumorphism” (no, it has nothing to do with either Neo, Morpheus or The Matrix). Then, a global pandemic struck.

And people stopped behaving.

What I mean is that CRED’s USP was to incentivise two key behaviours—credit card purchases (bills of which you’d pay via CRED in return for points) and subsequent impulse purchases (using the points you got from CRED). Alas, both behaviours fell off a cliff and are unlikely to return to normal levels any time soon. It’s a double behavioural whammy for CRED.

Which is probably why it’s pulling out newer use-cases for its users from its venture fund hat. Like an instant credit line for $6,600 or the ability to pay your monthly rentals via its app.

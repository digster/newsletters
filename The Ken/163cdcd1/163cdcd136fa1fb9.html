"What if Microsoft decides to build what you’re building?” was the question legions of venture capitalists posed to multitudes of desperate entrepreneurs eager to convince them about the future of their brilliant business ideas. Till Google dethroned Microsoft to become the new bogeyman. 
 
“What if Google decides to build what you’re building?”
 
And then Facebook.
 
“What if Facebook decides to build what you’re building?”
 
Billions of dollars of valuation have been wiped out simply because a Microsoft, Google or Facebook decided to enter a new market by building a newer product. Tens of billions of dollars of wealth were probably not created because just the fear of competing against a seemingly unstoppable and cash-rich player was enough to dissuade VCs and entrepreneurs.
 
In India, that bogeyman is the NPCI. On the face of it, it is just a "not for profit" association comprised of 56 banks. But behind the scenes, it is the Microsoft/Google/Facebook of India’s fintech ecosystem.
 
Because there’s just no telling when it will decide to become a competitor.
 
18-year-old BillDesk, long considered one of the most no-nonsense, execution-driven success stories of India’s fintech space, is the latest one to feel NPCI’s heat. Because NPCI has not only come up with a bill payment platform, Bharat BillPay, that will create an alternative to BillDesk; it is also mandatorily inserting itself into the ecosystem through regulation.
 
BillDesk must now get a license to connect to Bharat BillPay. Every biller that it currently offers services to must connect to Bharat BillPay. That’s not all. BillDesk must dismantle all its individual agreements.
 
For a company that was processing over $40 billion in payments annually and was valued at over $1 billion, the future isn’t looking all that rosy for BillDesk.

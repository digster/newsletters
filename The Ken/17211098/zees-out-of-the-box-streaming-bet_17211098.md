Indians can’t get enough of online videos.
A 2019 survey covering nine countries found that Indians on average watch 8.4 hours of online video content every week. We are surpassed only by Americans, with their 8.6 hours.
This year, 16 million users are expected to pay Rs 4,150 crore ($551 million) for video subscriptions in India, according to a report by industry body FICCI and consulting firm Ernst & Young.
And this is just a pre-lockdown estimate.
So it’s hardly surprising that streaming services are throwing a plethora of movies and shows at the Indian viewer. But a company’s past success as a television network or a movie studio is hardly enough to get people hooked to its over-the-top (OTT) platform.
Zee Entertainment Enterprises Ltd has learnt that the hard way, with its first OTT ventures, Ditto TV and Ozee, coming a cropper. But with Zee5, launched in early 2018, the company is hoping the third time’s the charm. And crucial to making it work is a box that will help users stream content on Zee5 without paying for data. (They still have to get a Zee5 subscription.)
In April 2017, Zee paid around Rs 70 crore ($9.2 million) for an 80% stake in the company that owns that technology, sold under the brand name SugarBox. Last month, it doubled down on the company by announcing a further Rs 522 crore ($69 million) investment.
But the company has its share of woes. One, it may not be able to match its larger OTT peers, like Disney+ Hotstar, Netflix and Amazon Prime Video, in content. Two, Zee slipped from its founder Subhash Chandra’s control last year after his ill-timed investments in infrastructure forced him to drastically pare his stake in the company.
Prasannata, in today’s story, deconstructs Zee’s bet to understand if SugarBox—whose underlying tech is not as unique as its makers would have you believe—can make Zee5 a serious contender in the streaming arena.

Good Morning Ishan,
 
If you are a frequent flyer in India and do not actively shun technology, you probably already belong to the rarefied group of ~3.4 million people who are using Digiyatra.

A biometric-based boarding system, it is meant to streamline air travel—by making it faster and more convenient. As you read this, it is deployed across at least eight government and privately owned airports.

The service may not be fast or convenient yet—as many who’ve experienced this say—but it’s certainly a piece of novelty to free up passengers’ time inside the airport. More free time on their hands means higher revenue earned by the airport operators.

It’s now run by a not-for-profit company, but the idea for Digiyatra came from the airports themselves, according to multiple individuals closely associated with its implementation. Five airports own a 74% stake in this company, and the rest is held by the Airports Authority of India (AAI).

Today, “Digiyatra Buddies” may be coercing passengers to enrol at specific kiosks, but you know, it’s early days. All such new technologies have targets.
What’s interesting is that from a proof of concept run by the Bengaluru airport in 2017 for facial-recognition-based passenger entry, it has evolved into a government-backed initiative for improving user experience and revenues. All airlines haven’t bought into it fully yet—and they have their reasons—but they won’t have much choice.

AAI is awarding sizeable contracts for its implementation, and there’s a long list of use cases.

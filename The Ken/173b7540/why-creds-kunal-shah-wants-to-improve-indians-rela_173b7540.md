An MBA dropout, a philosophy major, a super successful first-time founder—Kunal Shah’s relationship with money, rather his understanding of people’s relationship with money, is distinctive. He’s built his second startup CRED on that insight.
A members-only credit card payments platform, CRED intends to build its trust and profit pool by improving the financial literacy of card users. India has one of the highest Return on Assets on credit card margins. Not because credit card companies are trying to be evil. A basic understanding of money is missing.
When Shah exited Freecharge in March 2015, $1.8 billion was being spent on 18 million credit cards. In March 2020, $8.2 billion was being spent on ~50 million credit cards. Nearly 18 months since its founding, CRED processes 14% of all credit card bill payments in India.
That’s an eye-popping reach, but how does CRED make money? Why did Shah, despite a $25 million in angel round (with $120 million in the subsequent round), believe he was only taking a knife to a gun fight? Why was being an angel investor in 30+ companies, alongside top notch VCs like Sequoia, not enough for him? What sort of company is Shah building that he hires people with performative skills?
In The Interview today, Sumanth gets down to asking some of the most incisive questions that Shah must have ever fielded. It’s a conversation where the questions are as illuminating as the answers.
In early 2016 when I was closing my book on biotech and Biocon, in my final interview, I asked Kiran Mazumdar-Shaw some forward-looking questions. She perked up, “Come on, Seema, I can’t tell you my whole strategy”. Fair restraint for a promoter of two listed companies.

Can venture capitalists do no wrong? Some changes seem inevitable as VCs get back to investing after a "small reset"
By Rohin Dharmakumar
5 min read
View in browser

Good Morning Ishan,

You are reading the Two by Two newsletter—a weekly commentary on the most important business stories around you

For the last two years, the default way in which startups were exposed to venture capital and its effects has been, in many ways, paused. We went from the ZIRP-era ‘a unicorn every three months’ norm to layoffs, company shutdowns, and down rounds—there’s been a slowdown. Venture capital funding for the first nine months of 2024 is down 7% year-on-year, according to Tracxn.

But not because of any particular shortage of capital, which is abundant. It’s just not getting invested at nearly the same rate.

Founders who had burned through their raised funds to capture market share have had to reconsider their strategies at a time when AI and LLMs are making building and scaling companies a fair bit cheaper. 

VCs have had to sit tight and think things through before signing off another cheque. 

And the biggest change—there’s a focus on being profitable.

As the year progressed, though, we have seen big fund raises return; Zepto, Rapido, Meesho being cases in point. But overall funding is nowhere near the earlier highs. 

And now, as the world of venture capital inches back to “normalcy”, is it time for some introspection? Should VCs want to be a bit more cognizant of their past mistakes in encouraging the “grow or die” mentality among founders? 

I’ll say this, VCs aren’t the most optimistic of folks—that’s the job of founders and founding teams. VCs have to hedge multiple bets simultaneously, hoping and believing that at least a few of them will result in the outsized returns that keep the lights on, and the funding flowing for other promising ideas and people.

So this week on the Two by Two podcast, when my cohost Praveen Gopal Krishnan and I sat down to discuss the role VCs play in the world of startups, and what they need to be doing differently, we roped in two people who are intimately familiar with both sides of the coin. People who have been in the shoes of a founder, and so understand the responsibilities that come with taking money from someone willing to bet on you, and the pressures of running your company. But also people who have transitioned into being full-time VCs. 

Manav Garg is the founder of Eka Software and co-founder of Together Fund. Listeners of the First Principles podcast would remember Manav from an earlier episode, where he spoke at length about the operator-led fund. And Rajiv Srivatsa is a co-founder of Urban Ladder, and now a founding partner at Antler India.

A matter of governance

Fifty-eight times! That’s how often the word governance came up over the course of our conversation. Startups are not particularly known for having great governance—which is a fair assessment given how many have busied themselves with growing at all costs. But Manav kept coming back to this point. 

Startups and their boards, he said, need far sharper focus on governance if things are to remain in check. Most big conglomerates remain in a position of strength because they have this.

Manav: I think we as an ecosystem (should) build a narrative on governance. 

The companies that you spoke about—Reliance, Tata—are very high on governance and ethics, right?

And giving back to society, right?

So, they have created that fabric, where governance is a board discussion. Governance is a day-to-day life in their companies that they build.

[…]

I think growth and governance has to go hand in hand.

It doesn’t have to be just “grow or die”

Rapid growth is the essence of building a startup, especially if you are one that is open to taking venture capital. This is one of the reasons venture capital flows into the companies it ends up in—VCs know there are risks, but they’re willing to make a bet for the pay off. 

We could use some change here, though. VCs currently operate on a shorter cycle of maturity for their investments, say 8–10 years, but as we discussed in our conversation (and a few of our earlier episodes), it takes time sometimes for things to take off. And that’s where funds with a long-term vision come into play, because they can help businesses build sustainably, rather than just hopping on to the fast lane.

Manav: Typical venture capital cycle is 10 plus two. But companies in India take typically 15 years, or sometimes more, which is fine.

But then, VCs will need to do the job and convince their LPs saying we are in an early stage of the India market, where outcomes or exits may take a little bit more time. So it is okay to have a company giving returns over a 15- or 20-year period, instead of 10 plus two. 

[…]

I am investing in a seed stage… a very early stage. And sometimes, founders may take more time to find product market fit, or market forces may require the company to exist for a longer time before it can get to product market fit.

Therefore, it may take 15 years to really give a return on that.

So, people have to adjust for a longer period of holding the company and not try to do things in a very short time.

Mistakes will happen. It’s better if they are new ones

There have been massive mistakes over the years which have resulted in the destruction of capital. But the general outlook, especially with the emergence of more operator-led funds that index heavily on a founder-first mentality, is that founders and VCs have to start having honest conversations whenever the need arises.

Rajiv: So, the market view. 

One, India’s GDP is very different from the US or China, I think all of us have to agree. And as much as a lot of us—founders, VCs—try to increase it, it is going to increase only at a particular pace, which has a lot of other factors going into it. 

Two, the VC industry in India is 15 years old, like Manav was talking about. In the US, it is 50–60 years old. 

You can understand that when an industry is in different maturities across two different markets, which have very different GDPs, you have to account for certain learnings which will be different.

Three, if you see historically, the lifespan of companies has been reducing drastically. What used to be 50–60 years in the middle of the last century is now becoming 15–20 years.

If you just simplistically overlay these three concepts, I think as an industry that is so nascent, in a market whose GDP is so much lesser, you will have to allow for all the learnings to happen on all the mistakes.

Otherwise, you are putting undue pressure, which is just not right in terms of even plain commonsensical comparison. Because you are asking for the maturity of a US. And even the US, as Manav said, has had an FTX mistake.

Now, why should an FTX mistake happen when an Enron has happened? 

It happens because it is a new mistake.

As long as we make new mistakes, and this is what we tell founders, that is fine. We should not make the same mistakes.

There was, of course, a lot more we covered in our conversation, and both Manav and Rajiv pushed back on multiple things that both Praveen and I had to say about how VCs function.

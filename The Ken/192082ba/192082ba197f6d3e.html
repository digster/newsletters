There’s a whodunnit underway in India: the case of the missing UPI mandates in the Bajaj Housing Finance IPO. It has far-reaching implications for the Indian stock-market ecosystem and what it reveals about its plumbing.

The numbers stacked up by this blockbuster IPO are staggering. The Rs 6,560 crore offer attracted bids worth Rs 3.14 lakh crore, setting a record. Also, it got the highest number of applications—about 9 million—for any IPO in India so far. But, and there is always a but, one in every six of those bids was disqualified—sparking much hand-wringing and outrage among the unlucky deprived of their lottery to double the money on listing.

A headline screamed, “Technical glitches rob many of chance to make money in Bajaj Housing IPO”. A vast majority of these rejected applications were due to missing mandates on UPI, India’s instant payment system that is often elevated as the greatest thing since sliced bread.

So, who is responsible for this mess? Brokerages? Exchanges? NPCI, the manifester of UPI? Fingers are being pointed in all directions. Aakriti, the intrepid reporter of today’s story, puts on her Tintin hat to find out that the culprit is not among the usual suspects. And the plumbing, she finds, needs much fixing. From across the ocean, contributor Jeff Hutton has a fascinating story about an Indonesian coffee chain that’s now planning to open shops in India and the Philippines. And if Kopi Kenangan’s success in the archipelago nation and other Southeast Asian countries is any indication, incumbents like Starbucks and Blue Tokai on foreign shores better watch out.

From just one kiosk in 2017, Kopi Kenangan has gone from strength to strength, boasting revenue of US$37 million in 2024. Along the way, it picked up the coveted US$1 billion valuation, making it Southeast Asia’s first retail food-and-beverage unicorn.

Its secret cuppa? For starters, it’s anti-Starbucks in its approach—with modest pricing to keep its cup of joe accessible to everyone. But Kopi Kenangan offers much more than that, including unique concoctions with whimsical names and a differentiated sales model. Can it replicate its winning recipe in distant lands?

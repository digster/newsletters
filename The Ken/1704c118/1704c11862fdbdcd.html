Good Morning Ishan,
 
When I joined The Ken a month ago, I imagined a typical work day would be less frantic than some of the other online newsrooms I've been a part of. I had always worked for publications that published dozens of stories in a day, including news reports, features, live blogs, opinions...the works. Speed and currency were key—we were in a race with numerous other publications to get the story out first. 
 
And so, when I joined The Ken, I thought getting out just one story a day would be easy-peasy lemon squeezy. No pressure.
 
How wrong was I. It's not even funny.
 
This last month at The Ken has been... eventful, to put it mildly. I had a mini-meltdown in my first week itself, as I struggled to close my first edit. I had totally underestimated the time, patience, and effort that goes into producing a story for The Ken. And this includes not just the editing, which is done by me and my team, but also the ideating, reporting, visualising, and marketing, done by my colleagues. After finally managing to close that first edit following five days of pure hell, the sense of accomplishment was second to none in my short career. 
 
I've been a reader of The Ken right from when it launched a little over three years ago but my respect and admiration for the process, the people, and the product has grown manifold over the last month. And so, it brings me great pleasure to take you through the five excellent stories we published earlier this week:
 
Shreedhar kicked off the week on Monday with a David vs Goliath story about how Mint turned the tables on The Economic Times by changing its focus completely to mobile. Mint was struggling on the digital front till 2018, before it overhauled its content strategy and technology. It's a fascinating read about a bizarre, unconventional journey.
 
On Tuesday, Pratap wrote a (free) story that should have us all worried—about how the India's National Crime Records Bureau has drafted a Bill that could turn the country into a surveillance state. If passed in its current form, the Bill will provide legislative backing to collect all types of biometrics—from palm prints to vein patterns—from not just prisoners and arrested persons but also individuals summoned for interrogation. Think the all-seeing eye of Sauron in Tolkien's world. 
 
Arundhati and Sumanth then teamed up to provide a cracker of a story on Wednesday, about Flipkart co-founder Sachin Bansal's second innings. After exiting the e-commerce giant, Bansal now wants to disrupt the Indian fintech space via his new startup, Navi, which wants to make financial services simple, accessible, and affordable.
 
On Thursday, Ruhi delved into rural India's access-to-healthcare problem. The Indian government has set aside a viability gap funding (VGF) under the "world's largest health insurance scheme", called Ayushman Bharat, to finance building hospitals in small towns. However, private hospital chains have still faced tough odds in these areas, despite having the money. 
 
On Friday, Pranav Shankar wrote about how a niche cafe chain called Third Wave Coffee Roasters is aiming to beat the odds in India's troubled coffee retail sector. The Bengaluru-based company aims to expand from its current 15 stores to 100 by 2021. But ambition only goes so far in an industry where even the world's biggest cafe chain, Starbucks, has struggled. 
 
That's it for this week. Let me know what you thought of our stories, and have a great Sunday.

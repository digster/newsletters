A weekly newsletter about how finance is getting supercharged by tech in India, and how you can make money work for you.
Good Morning Ishan,
The crazy turmoil that US President Donald Trump has thrown the world, its trade, and stock markets into takes me back to a Ka-Ching! edition I had written about a year ago. It was on a question many retirees grapple with—how much should you withdraw from your retirement portfolio each year?
This super-important question has become all the more relevant and urgent now, thanks to the stock-market crash engineered by Trump’s tariffs. Retirement portfolios across the world—including in India—will now be experiencing something called ‘sequence-of-returns risk’, at a level not seen since the pandemic. It’s a big risk that retirees need to be aware of and manage, if they don’t want to run out of money in their silver years.
But I am getting ahead of myself.
First, the answer to the original question. As I [wrote](https://the-ken.com/kaching/how-much-should-you-withdraw-from-your-retirement-portfolio-each-year/) earlier, the key is an esoteric-sounding term called safe withdrawal rate (SWR). But it’s actually a pretty simple concept. SWR, as its name suggests, is how much you can safely withdraw from a retirement corpus each year without exhausting it prematurely—i.e., quicker than the expected life-span after retirement.
Here’s the example I used to explain the concept in my earlier piece:
For instance, simplistically speaking, if a person retires at the age of 60 with a retirement corpus Rs 100, and expects to live another 30 years, the annual SWR would be around 3.3% (100/30). Basically, you would be able to withdraw Rs 3.3 every year from the age of 60 until 90. If instead a higher amount, say Rs 4, is withdrawn, then the portfolio will be wiped out before you get to 90. Not a nice situation to be in.

The widely accepted SWR is 4%. But it’s based on studies in the US and the experience there, and is flawed for India. Simply because India is not the US and our playing field is vastly different. SWR calculations are complex and have to consider many factors, such as the mathematical and statistical interplay of expected inflation rates; the mix between asset classes in the portfolio and their correlations; and the expected returns from the portfolio. These assumptions and numbers have to be run through a large number of simulations to estimate the SWR.
In 2022, Ravi Saraogi, a registered investment advisor and co-founder of financial planning firm Samasthiti Capital, authored a [paper](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=4216077) on SWRs in the Indian context. It found that for an average Indian investor, an SWR of 3% is suitable.
Then, in February 2024, Rajan Raju and Saraogi [published](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=4697720#:~:text=The%20often%20cited%204%%20rule%20is%20not,particularly%20for%20withdrawal%20rates%20greater%20than%203.75%.) another paper on SWR. Raju, an IIM-Ahmedabad alumnus and ex-banker, runs Invespar Pte, a single family office in Singapore. This paper—with different methodologies, more realistic assumptions, and an increased scope—had good news. It figured that the SWR had risen from 3% in the earlier study to 3.5%. So, Indians could now withdraw more each year for a 30-year post-retirement period.
This required a balancing act with gold, though. Instead of the usual 40:60 equity-to-fixed deposit asset allocation, this retirement portfolio would need to have a 30:10:60 equity-to-gold-to-fixed deposit distribution.
Fast forward to this January, and Raju has published yet another updated and expanded [study](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=5114252) on SWRs in India, based on new data over a longer time period, updated tax policies as per Budget 2025, and enhanced analysis.
There’s more good news for some, and bad news for others. The SWR for lower income retirees, as per this latest study, is now higher—between 3.5% and 4.2%. That’s thanks to the Budget 2025 increasing the annual tax-free income threshold to Rs 12.5 lakh, essentially making income from fixed deposits tax-free. In fact, this was one of the key takeaways from the earlier study for policymakers—that reducing the tax on fixed deposits can increase the SWR for retirees. It has aged well.
But for those who pay 20% tax, the paper still arrives at a conservative SWR of 3–3.5%. And for those in even higher tax brackets, the SWR has fallen to between 2.7% and 3.2%. This is due to higher taxes on long-term capital gains on equity, among other factors. Win some, lose some, I guess.
Raju’s latest study, which considers a 50:50 equity:debt mix, does not have gold as part of the portfolio. But adding some gold would offer diversification benefits and increase the SWR for retirees, Raju told The Ken.
Importantly, the new paper reiterates one of the key findings of the old one. Moderate equity allocations (20–50% of the portfolio) offer the highest SWRs, while excessive equity exposure increases risk. While equity exposure can potentially enhance portfolio returns, the SWR cannot be increased by increasing the share of equity in the retirement portfolio beyond an optimum level. The inherent volatility of equity then starts playing spoilsport, and SWRs fall.
This is an outcome of what I mentioned earlier—increased sequence-of-returns risk.
This arises because returns from equity can vary widely year to year, even if the average return over a long-time horizon is healthy. And negative returns in the initial years of retirement could end up hurting retirees badly.
Raju says: “Sequence of returns has a critical impact on portfolio sustainability during retirement. Early negative returns, combined with regular withdrawals, can deplete a portfolio much faster than a scenario with early positive returns. This occurs because withdrawing funds from a diminished portfolio leaves less capital to recover during subsequent positive return periods.”

So, for instance, if someone had retired in 2008 and faced the great stock market crash the same year, the diminished equity component in their retirement portfolio early on would have hurt them badly. This would have had a cascading effect in later years too, because the corpus would be used even as it was diminishing.
Something similar is happening right now for recent retirees. The stock market crash due to Trump’s actions will result in a high sequence-of-returns risk. And a big equity exposure would only exacerbate this.
Is there a solution?
Besides its recommendation of moderate equity exposure, Raju’s new paper also offers some hacks to retirees and their financial planners.
The usual ‘fixed real’ withdrawals, in which retirees withdraw a fixed, inflation-adjusted amount every year from their portfolios, won’t really help in getting around sequence-of-returns risk. Instead, dynamic spending strategies such as forgoing inflation adjustments after losses could help mitigate the impact. There is also the ‘declining spend’ approach as time progresses, and strategies such as ‘guardrails’—where withdrawals are dynamically adjusted based on portfolio performance.
These, however, come with tradeoffs like lower cash flows during difficult years.
But I guess that’s just the price for Trump’s tantrums.
Everyone is having to, or will have to, make tough trade-offs.

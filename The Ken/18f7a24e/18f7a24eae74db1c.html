Good Morning Ishan,

“Lending is easy, collecting is hard.”
“There is a huge unmet demand for credit.”
“We have a great tech stack.”

If I had a dollar for every time a lender said these statements, I would never need to take a loan.

But as common as these statements are, it’s also hard to find a consumer-lending fintech that can pull off all of this and build a profitable business.

Of the 100-plus startups in this space, few fintechs have grown the way PE-backed Kreditbee has. Backed by the likes of behemoths like Advent International, Mitsubishi UFJ Financial Group, TPG Newquest, and Azim Premji-backed Premji Invest, Kreditbee saw a 140% jump in loan disbursals to about Rs 14,000 crore (US$1.6 billion) in the year ended March 2023.

But unlike other lenders who typically make money from the interest generated on the loans they provide, US$700 million-valued Krebitbee made over half of its total income of Rs 1,275 crore (US$153 million) for 2023 from non-interest income sources like processing fees.

Read Anjali’s debut story on the fascinating rise of eight-year-old Kreditbee.

When losing is winning

Zomato, this week, gave up its ambitions to be a payment aggregator. A payment aggregator’s job is to offer merchants all kinds of payment options. Given the huge volumes the food aggregator processes, it may have initially thought that it could do a better job of controlling payment failures and saving on costs if it builds this internally instead of relying on the likes of Payu and Razorpay.

But given that the regulator has also handed numerous other companies such as Pinelabs, Phonepe, Worldline, and others licensees, the competitive intensity to offer discounts on an already cheap service just got more intense.

Also, payment aggregators are awaiting more rules from the regulator which can shake things up for those in this space.

Zomato can now both browbeat payment aggregators into giving the lowest price and also not invite RBI’s scrutiny.

Sometimes, giving up is the best business outcome to hope for.

Good morning Ishan, and welcome to the last time we’ll speak in 2025. It has been a “full” year for me. In that it has been satisfying, demanding, rewarding, and frustrating in equal measure.

The First Principles newsletter has, in so many ways, been my stable anchor through it all. Committing to write every week for such a large, diverse, and discerning set of readers such as you will do that. “Defragmenting” is the analogy that comes closest, and one that I’ve used earlier.

Defragmenting used to be a periodic task we performed manually on our Windows PCs, allowing the operating system to rearrange scattered files and bytes stored on the hard drive in a way that they become more contiguous and easier to access. These days, it’s mostly an automatic, background process.

Writing the First Principles newsletter each Saturday is my way of defragmenting a week’s worth of thoughts, ideas, and work—both conscious and unconscious—so I can emerge clearer-headed by Sunday.

It’s hard for me to see these patterns play out during the weeks and months, because proximity often inhibits our sense of perspective. But now that we’re at the end of the year, was there some grand pattern or undulating wave that shaped what I wrote?

Now that I’m—we’re—in the calm, reflective, and unhurried last week of December, I think some patterns are starting to become clearer.

I started the year with a decided emphasis on careers.

January kicked off with Edition #78, which explored the end of 40-year careers and questioned traditional career trajectories. I followed this up with editions on careers as “lattices rather than ladders”, whether professionals should be willing to take pay cuts for meaningful work, and ended with a piece on the importance of “working in public”.

February continued this thread with “Changing your mind and enjoying it too,” arguing that consistency is overrated across careers. The month also introduced writing as a decision-making tool, something that would repeat throughout the year. There were also the editions on seeing careers as “tours of duty” and breaking out of to-do lists, budgets, and scarcity mindsets.

By March, I’d softened a bit and shifted towards authenticity and pattern-breaking, arguing how authentic human actions can bypass conventional filters and how empathy could be a superpower. We also discussed ergonomics!

On balance and reflection, this is how most years start out for me. Cold weather, new year. Ambitious, high-agency, and work-focused.

I mellowed as the seasons changed to spring and then summer, focusing more on our individual agency, uniqueness, and personal habits.

April explored contrarian thinking, habits and streaks, the importance of friction in our lives, and “mental time travel” as a human superpower.

May marked the entry of AI, with my “(Unformed) Thoughts on AI”, a reflection that LLM-powered AI threatens to “subsume and erase the very essence of what makes us human”. That edition set the stage for the year’s ongoing tension between technology and humanity. I also spoke about the “editor’s mindsets” and the difference between “not failing” and succeeding.

June was largely focused on health and wellness, where I asked you about your contrarian views on fitness. This was followed by a live event on building healthier lives. One edition also talked about the importance of a diverse community, for which we now have a wonderful new colleague at The Ken, Vishnu!

As the rains put summer behind us, I realise I shifted to decision-making frameworks and workplace collaboration. Was I trying to “integrate” the careers and individual-agency threads from earlier? Who can tell.

July had me talking about byproduct values and bricolage, triangulated decision-making, and the “Yes or No” framework (forcing every node in decision trees to be binary to reach places where “only subjectivity lies ahead”).

In August we talked about rubber ducking, refactoring and entropy, and another human “superpower”—attention (especially as a counterbalance to AI and distraction). It also featured a significant meta-narrative: “Putting my First Principles money where my mouth is,” in which I discussed refactoring and reimagining The Ken for its second decade. We got hundreds of really talented and sharp readers and professionals apply to us in response to that, of which many are now my colleagues!

September was thematically richer and more varied. In “The Great Unravelling,” we spoke about how a mere college degree no longer guarantees a career. Then, in “Psychological Runway,” we discussed the concept of mental pacing for long-term success, and in “Dreams of retirement,” the anxiety around career endings. We also had the wonderful Coffee Survey!

Was I getting diffused as the year went by, or just more open to possibilities? It seems like I started the year with a narrow “beam” focused on careers, which expanded to include various other aspects of our lives and work as the year progressed.

As autumn set in, my themes became more reflective and integrative. Synthesis?

October featured the second edition of our gifting survey and a semi-synthesis of many earlier themes into a broader philosophy around work satisfaction, “Living a life you like while solving poorly defined problems”.

In November, we talked about a few more synthesising concepts, like reframing reality as a leadership skill, homeostasis as a framework for organisational health, and The Infinite Game perspective on long-term thinking. We also paid homage to the great Warren Buffett as he wrote his last letter to the shareholders of Berkshire Hathaway.

And here we are, in December. In many ways, “A Fourier analysis of our lives and careers” is very much an integrative view of all the various strands that hum in parallel in our lives.

Through all of these, some themes stayed close to me.

The Careers Crisis: We talked about it throughout the year, but the framing evolved from crisis (“40-year careers are ending”) to opportunity (“find poorly-defined problems you enjoy”).

Community: I’d said at the beginning of the year that “Community” would be 2025’s theme. I think it played out through surveys, live events, and reader contributions. First Principles is today as much yours as it is mine. With my colleagues Tanim, Sakshi, Uddantika, and Vishnu, I hope to make this even more so in 2026!

Humans in an age of AI: Rather than obsessing over AI, I realise that human uniqueness—attention, mental time travel, authentic connection—is more interesting.

The Whole Us: Over the years, this newsletter has deliberately and subconsciously blurred the lines between personal essays (about dogs, coffee, seasons) and professional insights, because our careers and lives are increasingly inseparable.

Seasons: I’m thankful for living in Bengaluru, a city that has nurtured and supported me for decades. Perhaps one way I recognise it is by weaving in weather and nature observations from Bengaluru into most editions. It helps me keep a sort of a “clock” of passing time over the months and years, and acts as a thread that connects my life with my experiences and thoughts.

See you all in 2026!

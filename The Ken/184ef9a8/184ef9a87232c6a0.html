Good Morning Ishan,
 
One of my earliest assignments as a journalism intern, back in June 2016, was to report on then RBI governor Raghuram Rajan’s address at an event, Resolving Stress In The Banking System, in Bengaluru. That was the first time I was introduced to non-performing assets. I must admit, it took me a while to wrap my head around the extent of the crisis.
 
Non-bank lender Five Star Finance, which until recently was backed by those like Sequoia, Matrix, TPG Partners, did not seem to be contributing to the mess.
 
In the last five fiscal years, the 38-year-old NBFC reported just 1% bad loans, placing them adjacent to India's largest private lender, HDFC Bank. Despite lending to mostly single-shop owners, who are likely to have high default rates given the irregularity of their cash flow.
 
What sticks out though is that, among its peers, Five Star has one of the highest percentages of borrowers who have skipped paying one to two EMIs. In the year ended March 2022, its ‘stage two’ assets—when people have not paid an installment for 30-90 days—was about 16%.
 
How does this figure magically shrink to 1% for customers who’ve not paid an EMI for more than 90 days?
 
A one-year-old rule by India’s central bank that forces non-banks to take their stressed accounts more seriously, and bring due date discipline among borrowers has sent them scrambling.
 
And in that time, Five Star Finance did something that is unheard of in lending circles. Arundhati dives deep into the mysterious model of this mid-sized NBFC. Read it here: https://the-ken.com/story/tpg-matrix-backed-five-star-finances-face-off-with-its-stellar-bad-loans/ (10-minute read)

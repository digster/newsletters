India and the US might be at the opposite ends of the public healthcare spectrum, but they’re both close to their tipping points. India spends an abysmally low 1.5% of its GDP on public health, while the US spends a ridiculously high 18.5% of its own GDP. "If they reach 20%, the bubble will burst,” says the subject of today’s story, CS Pramesh, the lead architect of India’s National Cancer Grid. NCG is a loosely associated network of 177 centres across India that see over 700,000 new cancer patients each year. 

700,000 patients is 60% of new cancer patients each year. The WHO estimates that by 2020, one in three households will be affected by cancer. And for a disease that’s increasingly treatable and even curable, only 12.3% receive early treatment. NCG wants to change that. Partly by creating stratified guidelines that ensure treatment budgets are spread as widely among those who need it, and to those most deserving. 

Also by truly understanding the efficacy of breakthrough new drugs and adapting them for Indian conditions. For instance, NCG was able to expand access to Herceptin, a breast cancer drug that costs Rs 10 lakh ($14,535) per treatment cycle, from 4% to 50%. How? By observing clinically and through multi-year trials that taking the drug for just nine weeks was almost as effective as taking it for a year. 

The NCG is now partnering with the Indian government on Ayushman Bharat, the ambitious universal health insurance plan that currently covers 40% of India’s population. And through that, they’re driving down prices and driving up outcomes.

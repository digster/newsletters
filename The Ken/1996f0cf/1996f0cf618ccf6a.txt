When will the RBI allow payments banks to grow up?. With little space to expand, and little opportunity to level up, payments banks remain stranded on the ground floor

Just about a month ago, one of the Reserve Bank of India’s (RBI) banking innovations came of age. AU Small Finance Bank received an in-principle approval https://www.reuters.com/world/india/indias-au-small-finance-bank-gets-first-universal-banking-licence-decade-2025-08-07/ from the banking regulator to transition into a universal bank—the first small finance bank (SFB) to do so, and the first full-fledged banking licence issued in a decade. 

The development was a pleasant milestone. Both for AU, which will now be able to significantly expand its business and offerings, and the RBI, which introduced small finance banks in 2014 as an innovation and has now seen it mature enough for an SFB to advance into the more high-stakes world of universal banking. 

You can hardly say the same thing for the other banking innovation the RBI introduced that year. 

Payments banks have, for years now, struggled. 

Their numbers have dwindled. Their bottomlines are negative. More than one has run into regulatory trouble. 

And certainly none have been able to level up into an SFB, though many would clearly love to do so. 

The RBI introduced both payments banks and small finance banks in 2014 with the same general goal—they would expand banking coverage to the underserved and unbanked, and promote financial inclusion in an era of hyper-specialisation. They would each do it little differently, and at slightly different levels. 

But while SFBs have emerged as a relatively successful, profitable segment in Indian banking (they already accounted for 1.18% of total banking assets as of March 2024), payments banks have not. 

According to the RBI’s most recent report https://rbidocs.rbi.org.in/rdocs/Publications/PDFs/0RTP261220247FFF1F49DFC04C508F300904A90C7439.PDF on Indian banking trends, SFB net profits grew at nearly 50% cumulatively year-on-year in FY24, coming in at Rs 6,219 crore, and their deposit bases and advances have grown steadily. In contrast, payments banks registered a net profit of just Rs 94 crore, at a 3% growth rate. From 11 payments banks in operation at their peak, just six remain now: Airtel Payments Bank, Jio Payments Bank, Paytm Payments, India Post Payments Bank, Fino Payments Bank, and NSDL Payments Bank. 

At the heart of the problem is a faulty design that offers little chance for growth, and no easy route up the banking ladder. 

A flawed model?

R S Sayers, a renowned British economist and banking historian, captured the essence of traditional banking in his seminal book Modern Banking (1938):

“The essential characteristic of a bank is that it takes deposits from the public and creates credit; it is a dealer in debt—its own and other people’s.”

Nothing about this has changed in the intervening century, and yet, payments banks seem designed to operate on a diametrically opposite principle. They are only authorised to accept deposits and are prohibited from lending, which is the primary revenue-generating activity for any bank. 

As anyone familiar with banking would agree, real profits lie in lending, not merely in holding deposits, and so structurally, this leaves payments banks with very limited avenues for revenue generation. And they are constrained from the deposit-taking perspective too—payments banks are not allowed to accept deposits beyond Rs 2 lakh per individual customer, which means they cannot function as a customer’s primary bank for high-value transactions or savings. 

Essentially, payments bank accounts are just an upgraded version of digital wallets, with a slightly wider set of use cases and higher deposit capacity.

When the payments-bank model was first introduced, the idea was straightforward: give wallet-issuers and other eligible entities a bigger role in India’s digital payments ecosystem. Digital-wallet issuers were among the first categories to be allowed to apply for a payments bank licence, because, back in 2014, wallets were the “hero product” of Digital India. Think back to demonetisation in 2016 and how Paytm*, Mobikwik, and Freecharge wallets became almost household names. 

But 2016 also brought something else—the launch of the Unified Payments Interface (UPI), which completely rewired India’s payments landscape. 

Overnight, everyone wanted to build financial products around UPI. Even credit cards like Rupay are now enabled for UPI payments. 

And so wallets lost their sheen. 

UPI made it possible to transfer money directly from a customer’s bank account, so why would anyone maintain a separate wallet balance? And, by extension, why would they want to open a secondary bank account with the respective payments bank? 

The payments bank regulatory framework was built for a pre-UPI world, but it has hardly evolved as the rest of the ecosystem leapfrogged ahead. 

Moreover, in recent years, much of the other value propositions offered by payments banks have been taken over by fintechs without payments-bank licences. Through neo-banking models, many fintech startups now “rent” regulatory access from licensed banks, using their infrastructure to offer seamless customer-facing services. In these setups, the neo-bank acts as the interface, with banking transactions routed through a partner bank in the background.

Similar utility. No payments-bank licence needed. 

Not to mention the fact that the one big success story in the payments bank space—Paytm Payments Bank—ended up being so full of https://www.thehindubusinessline.com/money-and-banking/rbi-bars-paytm-from-offering-banking-services-effective-march-2024/article67797411.ece regulatory holes https://www.thehindu.com/business/Economy/the-paytm-payments-bank-debacle-explained/article67826703.ece that the RBI barred it from onboarding new customers and accepting fresh deposits.

No advance

With such limited revenue-generation opportunities, do payments banks at least have a clear path to transition into more advanced banking? 

RBI guidelines say they do—any payments bank can apply for an SFB licence after completing five years of operations. And several like Paytm Payments Bank, India Post Payments Bank, and Fino Payments Bank have already expressed keen interest in moving up the ladder. 

But the RBI seems to be in no hurry to allow anything of the sort. For instance, Fino Payments Bank applied for an SFB license in 2023. Yet, almost two years later https://www.financialexpress.com/business/banking-finance/fino-payments-bank-eyes-sfb-license-for-sustainability-and-growth/3917970/, little seems to have moved. 

So, what then? 

It is understandable that the RBI cannot allow just anyone to start lending. When it first introduced the payments-bank model, the intention was never to turn these institutions into full-fledged lenders. 

But without the ability to engage in any meaningful credit activity, the future of these banks appears uncertain.

A potential middle ground could be to grant payments banks limited lending powers, rather than a blanket ban on all credit. Perhaps allow co-lending arrangements with larger financial institutions, where the primary credit risk and responsibility remain with the bigger partner. This would enable payments banks to participate in the credit ecosystem without undermining regulatory safeguards. Alternatively, the RBI could also consider permitting them to offer small-ticket loans targeted specifically at the microfinance segment—a sector that continues to be underserved. 

Interestingly, while small finance banks were originally created to serve low-income and underprivileged borrowers, some reports show that many of them have shifted to high- and middle-income borrowers. This gap could be filled by payments banks, which are well-positioned through their digital infrastructure and wide reach to cater to such customers.

There is also an argument for raising the current Rs 2 lakh deposit limit, which severely restricts payments-bank accounts from becoming the primary account for most individuals. If the RBI were to raise this cap, it would open many more use cases—for instance, parents could open a child’s savings account with a payments bank; others could use them to manage salary inflows and recurring payments. 

All of which could make payments banks more functional and relevant without compromising on the regulatory intent behind their limited license.

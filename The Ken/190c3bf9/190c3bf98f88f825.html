Now, onto our stories for the day.

When Apple CEO Tim Cook visited India in April 2023, he was paving the way to bring the Apple Card to the country. That meant partnering with a reputable financial institution that knew the lay of the land. Taking a step to make that happen, Cook met with HDFC Bank CEO and managing director Sashidhar Jagdishan.

For now, Apple’s titanium credit card isn’t in the wallets of Apple fans in India, but the company has had a partnership with HDFC Bank, the largest private-sector lender in the country, since 2019.

It involved cashback offers and affordable financing. But the prestige of being one of Apple’s partners was proving to be too costly. One credit card executive who spoke to The Ken said HDFC Bank was spending “1.5–2 times more” on Apple product offers compared to other brand partnerships. The two have now parted ways.

Apple found replacements. SBI Card and ICICI Bank now offer cashback to buyers but at lower rates than HDFC Bank. Consequently, a nationwide retailer experienced a dip in sales of Apple products.

We think of partnerships as mutually beneficial arrangements, but that wasn’t the case with Apple and HDFC Bank. In today’s story from India, Arundhati explores the saga of Apple’s rotating collaborators to find out who needs the other the most.

Over in Manila, Katrina spent the past weeks speaking with 15 men and one woman who work behind the wheel for Grab. Her goal was to find out more about the “boundary system”, where wealthier individuals purchase cars and rent them out to drivers who can’t afford vehicles of their own. Grab encourages this system because it feeds drivers onto the platform.

The problem, however, is that those drivers hardly earn enough to cover their car rental fees and fuel even when they work a full shift, so anyone who drives for eight or nine hours will struggle to earn even the minimum wage. They only get take-home pay if they stay on the road for much longer spans—the driver at the centre of Katrina’s story today drives for 15 hours a day, six days a week. That’s hardly sustainable.

The boundary system is a far cry from the situation that Grab advertises to recruit drivers onto its app, and it’s reasonable to expect drivers to eventually quit the platform to work for competitors. In small pockets, that’s already happening.

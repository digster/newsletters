If you had to skate where the puck is going to be, I’d prepare to kiss goodbye to one of the largest use cases for credit cards.

Last week, the Reserve Bank of India said card networks should stop processing credit-card payments made between businesses via fintech intermediaries. The regulator has a problem with a payment product offered by the likes of Visa, on top of which fintechs have built businesses. These fintechs allow companies to utilise the free 45-day credit on their corporate credit cards to transfer money into their vendors’ bank accounts.

But if the regulator does not like this, I’d think all those companies that let credit-card users like you and me pay rent and school fees using credit should re-examine this feature. After all, here, too, fintechs allow credit-card users to transfer credit into another person’s bank account. Moreover, such usage has grown by leaps and bounds, so much so that it is now one of the largest use cases for credit cards.

And by now, we know if something grows that quickly, trouble follows.

Even the subject of today’s story, footwear maker Campus Activewear, would agree. It’s the brand that lets one buy Nike-like shoes, but at Bata prices.

The Indian sneaker brand sold 24 million pairs in the financial year 2023—more than any other rival. Two years earlier, Campus had even pipped Puma, the largest global sportswear brand in India, in footwear revenue.

But its stock, which investors once loved, is now finding new lows as its profits are being wiped out. Around the time of its listing in May 2022, even its worst critics couldn’t have anticipated how dramatically its fortunes would change. Seetharaman has a great Monday morning read on why Campus is now losing ground.

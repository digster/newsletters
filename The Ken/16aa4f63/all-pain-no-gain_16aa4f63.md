Good Morning Ishan,
"A blizzard of prescriptions."
That was the prediction of Richard Sackler, a member of the family that owns US-based Purdue Pharma. It was the mid '90s, and Sackler was speaking at the launch party for Purdue's opioid-based painkiller, OxyContin.
Even then, over two decades ago, Sackler saw the potential of the drug. Potential that his family and company would maximise by lying about its addictiveness and potential for abuse. At the time, Sackler stated that the "blizzard" would bury the competition. The real victims, though, ended up being many of OxyContin's users. Between 1999 and 2017, the US recorded over 200,000 deaths due to prescription opioids. Oxycontin had kicked off and fuelled the US opioid crisis.
But even as America's opioid epidemic was beginning, India's opioid usage had fallen to an all-time low. Morphine, the cheapest strong opioid available, saw its usage fall 97% percent between 1985 and 1997. On account of stringent laws India implemented as it joined the global war on drugs.
I know what you're thinking at this point—crisis averted, right?
Wrong.
Yes, opioids have tremendous scope for abuse, but they're also the gold standard for pain management. And in India, just 1% of patients who need them—especially the terminally ill—have access to them. And here's the kicker, we're one of the largest producers and exporters of opium in the world. Irony just died, and it didn't even have access to pain management in its final days.

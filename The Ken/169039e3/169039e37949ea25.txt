I could keep that as a souvenir. The question: “What’s in it for me?”

A director at a private equity fund, with close to $8-10 billion in investments in India, recently asked me this when I said he should talk to my colleagues when they report on sectors he invests in. He had a point. If people like him can’t talk up their investee companies on record, then why engage with journalists.

Sometimes plain sharing of knowledge, without a spin, is worth much. At least the reporter will go back and write a factually correct story. On sectors where you invest. Precious in post-truth times, no? I think I spun a yarn, though not sure if it was effective.

“What’s in it for me?”

Do you ask this often? When Olina heard that in 2019 India was re-entering the global ranking system, PISA, she asked, what’s in it for India? Because India had flunked, straight-up: ranking 73rd out of 74 countries.

It’s interesting where this line of questioning took her. While the re-entry itself, according to ministry insiders, is instigated by Shanghai’s strong run in the PISA rankings, it’s the outcome one should be mulling over. India’s decade-long boycott of PISA overlapped with its intensifying love affair with edtech. The sunrise sector claims to drag India’s learning standards into the 21st century with an emphasis on conceptual over rote learning.

But how do we know if these apps are actually doing what they say they do? A second question, and more damning: do the edtechs care? 

It’s too complex for the common man to unpack the edtech diagnostics kit; the consumer market is not that discerning, agree most edtech founders. The products are getting slicker and more persuasive. Will they be able to scrub some of the sins of India’s last PISA outing? That’s Olina’s story today.

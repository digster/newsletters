Roughly a month ago, four of my colleagues—including two of our co-founders, Seema and Rohin—got the kind of email we don't often get from public relations (PR) agencies.
It proposed an "affirmative" article about a company it represented. The email even had a table for us to fill in how much we'd like to charge for pageviews, ranging from 100,000 to 1 million. But this was the most important part: the article had to have no sponsored tags and had to be written in such a way that it was "less PR and more news-like".
If the PR agency had bothered to even have a cursory look at our website and stories, it would have realised The Ken does not run advertisements, so forget about advertorials. (Advertorials are ads that resemble editorial pieces to make them look less like ads.)
But that's not the case with YourStory, a 10-year-old digital media publication that was started with the objective of helping "entrepreneurs who are not yet superstars tell their story”. For a fee, that is. Nearly 80% of YourStory's impressive revenue of $3.4 million in the year ended March 2020 came from advertorials.
On the face of it, this should not be a problem, as long as it's clear to the reader that the article has been paid for. But is that always the case?

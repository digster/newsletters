The Securities and Exchange Board of India, or Sebi, is clearly basking in the glow of its crackdown on Jane Street Group. 

When you go toe-to-toe with one of the world’s most profitable and secretive trading firms, global news outlets are bound to come calling.  

First, Bloomberg profiled Ananth Narayan, a whole-time member with the capital-markets watchdog and former derivatives trader who helmed the investigation into Jane Street’s alleged manipulation of the world’s largest options market. 

Then, Financial Times interviewed Tuhin Kanta Pandey, chair of the regulator. “I know these guys [Jane Street] are brilliant mathematicians and PhDs, but we can have PhDs from our side. We are not constrained,” he said. 

There are investors who believe Sebi waited too long to throw the book at Jane Street. “Everyone in the market knew for a long time what was happening,” the co-founder of a fund for the wealthy told me. But anyone who has read Sebi’s 105-page order knows the complexity of the issue is possibly unlike anything the regulator has dealt with in the past. 

If you’re not familiar with the details of what went down, you should read my colleague Anand’s two meticulous stories on the issue. In fact, Anand blew the lid off it in January.

Sebi briefly banned Jane Street last month, until the trader put Rs 4,840 crore ($552 million) of allegedly ill-gotten gains in an escrow account whose fate will be decided on the results of the watchdog’s ongoing investigation. 

Last week, yet another high-profile investigation—on front-running at Axis Mutual Fund—had a consequential development. Viresh Joshi, a former chief trader with the firm whom Sebi had barred from the securities market in 2023, was arrested by the Enforcement Directorate. 

The swift rise of India’s capital markets—the benchmark Nifty 50 index has almost tripled over the past decade—has forced Sebi to get better at sniffing out any behaviour that’s beyond the pale. In FY14, Sebi completed 120 investigations, according to its annual report. A decade later, that figure had jumped to almost 200. 

But nailing market miscreants has become harder. In FY14, the Securities Appellate Tribunal ruled in Sebi’s favour in four out of five appeals. In FY24, it only did so in two out of three appeals. But Sebi was more successful with cases that had reached a High Court or the Supreme Court, winning nine out of 10. 

The numbers tell an incomplete story, though. For a regulator, perception is just as vital as performance. And Sebi hasn’t drawn as much flak for any issue lately as it did in the Adani Group investigation. 

The Ken was among the earliest to flag, in April 2021, the odd foreign portfolio investors (FPIs) whose fortunes were almost entirely tied to Adani companies. For four such investors, 90% of the value of their stakes was in Adani Group. Two months later, the junior federal minister of finance, in response to questions from an opposition lawmaker about the shareholdings, said Sebi and the Directorate of Revenue Intelligence were investigating some Adani companies. 

I’m not sure what the Sebi investigation led to, because even in August 2023, months after short-seller Hindenburg Research’s explosive report alleging stock-price manipulation and accounting fraud in the Adani Group, some of these FPIs still owned more than 1% of a few Adani companies. And most of their net worth was still in the group, as we pointed out in a story, even if they subsequently pared their stakes.

Journalist Sucheta Dalal was unsparing in her criticism of Sebi on The Wire after Hindenburg released its report. 

Remember, SEBI is among the most empowered regulators in the world today, with powers of search, seizure, raids, and arrest. It also has an expensive surveillance system to catch suspicious trading activity and price manipulation on a real-time basis. What explains the regulator’s silence through different political regimes?

The answer is simple. Over the past two decades, SEBI has either operated on the directions of the finance ministry (especially during the second term of the United Progressive Alliance) or its activities have flown below the radar of any public or political scrutiny.

The regulator finally swung into action in late 2024. At this point, few expected anything dramatic to unfold, and a sense of ennui had settled over the probe.

SEBI has charged Adani Enterprises, Adani Ports, Adani Energy, and Adani Power of wrongfully categorising certain shareholders as public, according to financial statements filed by these four companies.

Twenty-six other Adani groups and related entities have been charged with categorising shareholdings by three Mauritius based offshore funds as public shareholdings when these funds were linked to Vinod Adani, brother of Adani group Chairman Gautam Adani, the source said.

Adani settlement pleas delayed by India regulator’s review of processes, Reuters

The Supreme Court may not have found Sebi “lackadaisical” in its investigation, but Sebi’s reputation took a beating. The Jane Street order, however commendable, isn’t redemption enough.

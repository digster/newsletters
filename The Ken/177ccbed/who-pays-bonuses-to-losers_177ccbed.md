In quite the incredible coincidence, I taught my 11-year-old son how to play rummy yesterday evening. We watched a YouTube video and then played each other, sans wagers. We learnt the value of observing each other’s body language, the cards we picked and discarded, and even the rough probability of finding the cards we wanted on the next turn.
Our harmless rummy game was a coincidence because today’s incredible story from Arundhati is about the online version of rummy that’s taking over India.
Indians spent about $5.5 billion playing online rummy this financial year. Rummy gaming sites like Rummy Circle, Junglee Rummy, and Ace2three allow users to bet as little as Rs 10 ($0.14) to as much as Rs 5,000 ($68.95). Yet, it isn’t gambling.
Because gambling has been banned in almost all Indian states for nearly a century and a half.
The online version of rummy that millions of Indians play each day is, instead, a game of skill. India’s courts have held that playing rummy in clubs and online are games of skill, not chance. Well, Google and Apple still won’t allow these games on their app stores, but India’s courts do.
This has turned online rummy into a promising high-growth sector for startups and VCs. Heck, Tiger Global is an investor in the space.
But how exactly do these sites operate? How do they attract users? How do they get their apps on smartphones without being allowed distribution via app stores?
Best of all, why on earth do they pay bonuses to losers?

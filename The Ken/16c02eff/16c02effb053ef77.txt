You know the Infinite Monkey theorem, right? Given enough time, monkeys typing out random characters on a keyboard will eventually produce something that isn’t gibberish. Or even the complete works of William Shakespeare. Welcome to the forecasting version of it: given enough time, dart-throwing monkeys will eventually produce forecasts that turn out to be accurate.

In 2009, famed management consultancy McKinsey forecast that India’s Pharma sector would grow to somewhere between $55 billion and $70 billion by 2020. In reality, the sector may end up just short of $42 billion by next year.

Never mind that, because McKinsey has a new forecast. It now expects the Pharma sector to grow to $120-130 billion by 2030. Okay.

Seema and Pranav in their story today don’t just dissect McKinsey’s projections, but also lay bare the sectoral headwinds and trends that have held India’s Pharma sector in check. Even better, they analyse the opinions of public market investors on pharma company stocks and come to the conclusion that, perhaps, the market and investors are seeing something McKinsey isn’t? In the short-term, the stock market is a voting machine; in the long-term it’s a weighing machine, said famed American investor Benjamin Graham. Seema and Pranav argue that the market has begun behaving like a weighing machine. The management of most Indian pharma companies, say industry watchers, don’t have an answer about how they’d transform their companies. “That’s where the challenge is. They’ll start trading at a lower multiple, people will kind of rationalise it even in the future,” says a stock analyst they spoke to.

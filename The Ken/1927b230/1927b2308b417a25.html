Taking on workplace stress. The WHO has chosen a high-stakes conflict to address in this year’s edition of Mental Health Week

The hardest part about telling any story is finding the right conflict. 

There are infinite ways in which you can write the second act of a typical romance movie. But are all of them equally engrossing? What if there was no stern mustachioed father standing in the way of love, say? Or no misunderstandings? No natural disaster that tears the couple apart. No confusion. No cold feet. No rival love interest. Basically, what if there was nothing in the middle? 

It doesn’t make for a good story, let alone a great one.

View in Browser

But what do you put in that second act that would make your movie a blockbuster? It’s a question storytellers compulsively obsess over, because if you get it right, that middle—that bridge—will carry your story from beginning to end. 

Successful organisations do the same. They diagnose a problem. They figure out a solution. And they build a good solid bridge to get them there. Often, when it comes to public campaigns, that bridge is a narrative with a powerful conflict at its centre—one that can best achieve your end goals. This week, on World Mental Health Day, the World Health Organisation (WHO) gave us a pretty illustrative example of this by telling a story whose central conflict is both high-stakes and widely relatable: 

Steady work can be one of the best ways to improve and preserve mental health. But status-quoist workplaces are among the most common sources of mental stress.

The Workplace Conflict

I’ve written quite a bit about mental health in this newsletter before, and the world’s current crisis in this space rests largely on three aspects. 

First, as I wrote a couple of years ago, while mental health is highly prevalent globally, resources allocated to deal with it are disproportionately small. The result is that most patients cannot access mental health services at all. This is the case even now, as data collated by the WHO shows.

View in Browser

And in the most extreme cases, left untreated, several mental health conditions can lead to increased chances of suicide, as the WHO notes: “The link between suicide and mental disorders (in particular, depression and alcohol use disorders) and a previous suicide attempt is well established…”

Second, we have a severe shortage of mental health professionals, which is also partly due to the paucity of resources allocated to this segment. India, for instance, has only 0.7 psychiatrists for every 100,000 people. The recommended number is three. 

Third are insurers, who still aren’t very keen on providing cover for mental health conditions.

Despite India’s insurance regulator mandating mental health cover since 2018, the practical coverage lags much behind in targets, leaving patients unable to continue therapies that would get them out of the trough. Things haven’t changed despite the regulator reiterating the directives in 2022. 

Does your health insurance cover mental health? What are the exclusions?, The Indian Express

The result is a mammoth gap between patients and the treatments and services available to them. 

And top of all this, there is now the fact that work—which can be an asset in mental health intervention—is a major source of mental health problems. 

Research strongly indicates that steady decent work is “critical” for mental health. To quote from a 2020 academic paper: “Unlike most mental health treatments, employment engenders self-reliance and leads to other valued outcomes, including self-confidence, the respect of others, personal income and community integration. It is not only an effective short-term treatment but also one of the only interventions that lessen dependence on the mental health system over time.”

But the World Economic Forum (WEF) says that for a huge number of people, workplaces do just the opposite.

Workplaces can protect mental health by providing financial stability, an inclusive community, building confidence and a sense of achievement, and creating a routine. But work can also be a source of risks to mental health, from job insecurity to discrimination, unsafe working conditions and excessive hours.

In the UK and the US, nearly half of employees (47%) and two-thirds (66%) of CEOs say most or all of their stress comes from work, according to the 2024 Workforce State of Mind report by mental healthcare organization Headspace. And this stress is affecting workers’ personal lives, with more than three-quarters saying it had negatively impacted their physical health and 71% saying work stress caused a personal relationship to end.

View in Browser

There are already attempts to solve and build a business around this need. Like Singapore-based mental health app Intellect, which raised US$10 million round a few years ago by focusing on providing mental health services to the enterprise market, and other newer applications of technologies such as AI.

But as the WHO, the WEF, and the UN clearly indicate in their campaigns and theme for this year’s mental health day on October 10, a big reason for workplace mental stress is the inertia of systems that are already in place. The status quo in most workplaces even now does not include proper mental health assistance.

Unhealthy conditions including stigma, discrimination, and exposure to risks like harassment and other poor working conditions, can pose significant risks, affecting mental health, overall quality of life and consequently participation or productivity at work. With 60% of the global population in work, urgent action is needed to ensure work prevents risks to mental health and protects and supports mental health at work.

It is essential for governments, employers, the organisations which represent workers and employers, and other stakeholders responsible for workers’ health and safety to work together to improve mental health at work. Action to address mental health at work should be done with the meaningful involvement of workers and their representatives, and persons with lived experience of mental health conditions.

That is a major conflict—one that involves high stakes, is deeply complex, and if solved, will take the human mental health story that much closer to a happy resolution.

The Spotlight by Shivani Verma
Staff Writer

Good Morning Ishan,

Usually I’m an early riser. But then there are a few weekends when I want a slow start—have a cup of joe, read a book, and then, hit the gym. I had similar plans this weekend, too.

But I woke up early on Saturday after getting about three phone calls—no, not from my mother but the painter. My flatmate and I were excited about our house finally getting a makeover.

While I was shifting my stuff outside the room, I noticed this tiny, creative book lying in a corner. Art Matters—that’s the title of the book. Written by Neil Gaiman, it is all about art, writing, and imagination, and has some striking illustrations by Chris Riddell.

I’d recently finished reading that book. And my favourite part is where Gaiman says whatever discipline we are in—writing, singing, designing, or any other—we have one unique thing: the ability to make art. And that can be an ultimate lifesaver for so many of us.

“It gets you through good times, and it gets you through the other ones,” he writes.

“Husband runs off with a politician? Make good art.
Leg crushed and then eaten by mutated boa constructor? Make good art.
Cat exploded? Make good art.
Somebody on the internet thinks what you do is stupid or evil or it’s all been done before? Make good art.”

And with that, let me run you through all the good art pieces from my colleagues this past week at The Ken.

monday

Experimentation has been a crucial part of operations at the nine-year-old foodtech giant Swiggy. But a change in attitude to pursue profits has resulted in the company gradually distancing itself from this ethos—turning its playground for innovation into a graveyard, writes Gaurav.

Swiggy used to be a playground for innovation. Now, it’s a graveyard

tuesday

The Adani Group, Coal India, Vedanta, and Jindal have reportedly shown interest in the lithium mine discovered in the central Indian state of Chhattisgarh. The precious battery material has helped miners across the globe amass wealth. But this ground report by our contributor, Prateek Surisetti, paints a different picture.

Inside the lithium rush: can bidders strike gold in the race to mine?

wednesday

India’s largest retirement-funds manager, the Employees’ Provident Fund Organisation (EPFO), rejected nearly 2.5 million final settlement claims in the year ended March 2023. What’s more startling is the rejection rate of one in every three claims. The reason? Peculiar and damning all at once, writes Anand in this excellent piece.

The long-brewing EPFO crisis behind rising claim rejections

Singapore can’t get enough of seasoned startup founders. A once vibrant startup community filled with new, aspiring entrepreneurs is calling on experienced heads to lead the city-state towards newer tech. Taro explains this shift.

Why Singapore can’t get enough of seasoned startup founders

thursday

Credit card holders don’t typically opt for card networks such as Visa or Mastercard. But that’s set to change soon. The Reserve Bank of India (RBI) wants to change that, and one card-network firm, Rupay, is set to gain a significant market share, writes my colleague Rounak.

RBI’s shot in the arm for Rupay is a shot in the foot for co-branded credit cards

friday

I’d been a Nescafé fan for years. For a change, I tried Continental Coffee, and like many others, never switched back. From a negligible share about five years ago, CCL Products—the parent company of Continental Coffee and one of the largest contract coffee manufacturers globally—now commands 2.5% of the pan-India market. And in some pockets, 8–10%. The company manufactures coffee for brands like Reliance Retail and Strauss Coffee, but is now determined to scale its B2C business, writes Aakriti.

The unlikely upstart challenging Nescafé, Bru’s hold over instant coffee

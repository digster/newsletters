Undercutting your competition is one of the oldest ways of business there is. You identify a market, figure out the pricing, lower yours, and bam! A toehold in a new market.
That has pretty much been Skillshare’s strategy to conquer India since it launched here two months ago. The US-based upskilling giant is offering a Netflix-style all-access annual subscription for less than $25. That’s almost one-fifth of what it charges in the US and almost the same as what celebrity-led Indian incumbent Frontrow charges for a subject-specific monthly subscription.
Pretty nifty, if you ask me. Skillshare is banking on its vast library of pre-recorded online classes across genres like arts, creativity, design, entrepreneurship, lifestyle, and productivity. The platform allows anyone to sign up and teach a class, essentially making it a skills marketplace. India is already one of its top three markets outside the US; it now wants a significant slice of India’s $15 billion edtech industry.
But a rising tide lifts all boats; and Indian platforms, with nearly two years of post-pandemic experience under their belts, have been anticipating Skillshare’s arrival. They’ve tightened their belts, reworked their offerings, added more courses, and, most importantly, have built a community of learners with a mix of live classes and interactive features on recorded ones. BitClass, for instance, saw one of its classes—a short 1.5-hour one—drag on till 2 am in the morning. That sort of engagement is hard to beat.
Still, Skillshare isn’t a company one can underestimate. So far, it has raised over $108 million in venture funding. It also has a formidable reputation in the upskilling industry. Arpit, in today’s sharply-reported story, analyses how Skillshare’s India entry could upset the upskilling apple cart:

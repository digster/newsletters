Dear Ishan, good morning. Today’s is a special edition, in that our lead story is a Summer playlist!

It’s been a while since we put out a First Principles playlist. The last one was Spring https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/belief-actions-conflict/, in mid-February. And boy, do we have a huge playlist today. We’ve gone from 18 songs in our very first playlist to 21, 24, 50, and today, 91 songs!

Why did that happen? I think it’s because summer is our most evocative and memorable of seasons. 

Think about it. What seasons or times do you remember most from your childhood? From your college days? From your first days working in a new city? Which season is associated most with holidays and vacations? And partying and dancing? Or just bumming it out with no purpose? 

For me, and I think for most of my colleagues at The Ken, it’s summer. That’s why when we started crowdsourcing today’s playlists inside our company Slack, the songs came flying. 

“Summer wedding + sweaty Margaritas and Micheladas + good food. This song brings back the memory of a May 2022 day,” said Raveena, one of our Desk Editors, for “A Todos los Rumberos”.

Nathan, a staff writer with us, said he always tries to listen to the song “Beqaboo” on the Mumbai Sea Link. “It’s the perfect song for cruising speeds.”

I envy folks like Raveena and Nathan who are able to not just remember specifics from their past so well, but are also able to correlate it to songs and moods. 

My own summer memories span decades. The 1980s were hot Delhi summers, three-day train journeys, Kerala backwaters, mixtapes and MTV (launched 1981). The word I’d probably use to describe it would be torpor—“a state of physical or mental inactivity; lethargy.”

The summers of the 1990s for me were about exams, college life, hostels, new cities (I met and fell in love with Bengaluru in 1994, met Kolkata in 1999), new friendships, and newfound independence. Summers were either the energetic dance-y kind (think Bally Sagoo’s “Aaja Nachle”, Shaggy’s “In the Summertime” or the still-incredible “Urvashi Urvashi” from A R Rahman) or the languid and chill types (think Lucky Ali’s “O Sanam” or Silk Route’s “Dooba Dooba”).

I won’t bore you with more decades, but you get the picture. One of our youngest colleagues, Hari, who’s my partner for First Principles, put it best. “They could be songs that you were listening to on repeat on vacations, or on the days spent on the beach, it could be the needle drop from that summer blockbuster or TV show, or in my case, simply from one of the movies broadcast on repeat on cable TV.”

Our playlist today spans musical genres, languages, decades and even moods. There’s English and Hindi of course, but also Tamil (huge hat tip to Arundhati, our deputy editor, for reminding me of how viral “Telephone Manipol” was in the late 1990s), Nepali, Malayalam, and Spanish. 

There’s Reggae, dance, pop and disco, but also rock, electronic (I loved “Pashto Dance” from Tony Quattro, recommended by Seetharaman, our deputy editor), alternative and world music. 

Take Sergio Mendes’ “Mas Que Nada”, which for Indrajith, our analytics lead, “evokes memories of messing around with just a football and friends during vacations.” (If an Indian usually has memories of playing football during vacations, it’s safe to assume they’re either Malayali or Bengali. Indra is the former.)

Florence & The Machine’s “Dog Days Are Over” “reminds me of watching reruns of Glee during the first pandemic summer. It made me feel better about the second wave.” That was Ruhi, who leads our learning vertical. 

And from our resident electronic guru and (former) DJ, Rajiv, who’s also our sound engineer, come recommendations to “Strandfeest” (Bakermast) and “Daydreamer” (KC Lights). They’re “upbeat and yet tropical. Can imagine a beach party with these tracks during sunset.”

For such a large list, I struggled to “organise” it the way I usually did with our earlier playlists. So the best way to listen to this one is randomly. Let the music surprise you, and maybe take you back to some evocative summers.

1. Summer prints

Hello everyone! Welcome to another issue of Silent Sunday photographs. It’s Jonathan here as always and we have a beautiful collection of community contributions for you today. 

I’ve always been fascinated by how incredibly diverse feelings about seasons are. And sure, a lot of that is pretty obvious—if you’re far enough away from the tropics and trying to avoid becoming a popsicle for half the year, the return of warmth is something to celebrate I guess. Just like relief from the life-sapping heat is what the tropics ache for during the other half. Even if you take similar geographies, feelings about seasons vary wildly though. If you asked school-going children in India, I’m sure the 40-degree-weather pales in comparison to the couple of months of vacation time that comes around at the same time. For their parents, with soaring electricity bills to pay and work to do, not so much. 

You’ll find that subscriber contributions for the summer special today are pretty illustrative of this phenomena. 2. Your next book should be set in summer

Writers love summers. 

In fact, it may be argued that writers love all seasons, because what are seasons for a writer but a device? Writers can tell me that a character is sad, desolate, gloomy… Or they can place them in a small apartment during a rainy winter. They can write about change, hope, and love… or let their character arcs quietly curve into the spring months. Seasons have meaning. 

So, what do writers see in summers?

Me? I think writers love summers because it can be anything. 

Summer can be a blazing, pulsing heat. Still air, reddening sky. A power, as if descending directly from a sun god. Gabriel Garcia Marquez, Ray Bradbury, Perumal Murugan—and many more—have all placed their characters in a scorching heat, as unbearable as their own lives. I can’t help but always think about summer through this lens, simply because I grew up in the kind of dry Gujarat summers that prick your skin—during our worst weeks, we heard doctors on our local radio warning us not to leave home between 1 pm and 4 pm! 

Our community recommendations this week are replete with such meaning. Check out what our recommenders have to say about them in our on-site edition https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/summers-memories-moods-and-music/. 

You can also send us your own book recommendations here https://theken.typeform.com/FPBooks.

Our first recommendation, We Were Liars by E Lockhart, from Keya Nowak, is set in almost a sinister summer. A summer of “a dysfunctional family, grief and mourning.” 

Keya says: “I think First Principles listeners or subscribers might love this book because, despite the summer undercurrent running through it, it brings to light several things we wouldn’t associate with this season: a dysfunctional family, grief and mourning. The author also provides several plot twists by establishing a foundation for the story, and then reversing everything, so that we feel like we’re not quite so sure about anything any more. It’s a fascinating read that is not only a page-turner but also makes you reconsider the world you live in.”

But writers—especially in the western world, where summers are more of a relief than a pain—also love summers for their delicious freedom, youth, and a tempting suspension of belief. No doubt it comes from our collective nostalgia of summer vacations and the freeing memories of it.

Our second recommendation, People We Meet on Vacation by Emily Henry, from Nupur Agrawal, makes the most of this. I’ve read this one—it is truly breezy, a book you want to read through a lazy weekend. 

Nupur says: “This book is about two best friends who follow a ritual of visiting a new place every summer. Poppy and Alex are both writers who had a fallout last summer. The book transports you to the Californian summer where cocktails and beaches are the ultimate heroes. The heat between two friends who deeply care about each other, in the backdrop of a dreamlike summer vacation is nothing less than a respite if you are spending a lot of time indoors.”

And our last book recommendation, Karna’s Wife by Kavita Kane, from Divya Jyothi, may not be about summer, but she suggests that this is the best book to start the season with. A book full of conversations, hope, and dilemmas. 

Divya says: “Uruvi is Karna’s wife. Most people wouldn’t know her name. She is the sane, strong and sensible voice throughout this book. The author beautifully captures the moral dilemmas a woman in her place would feel in the Mahabharat setting. The most fascinating conversations happen between the women – between Uruvi and Draupadi for instance. This book got me interested in Karna and let me to the poem Rashmirathi – my english can’t do justice to the beauty of this poem. I cant think of a better book to start off this genre for summer 🌸”

3. Middle management might save your company…

…if you are to believe Alok Mittal, the co-founder and CEO of online business loan platform Indifi. 

Here’s what he says in our most recent episode of the First Principles Podcast: an organisation is held together not by its leaders, but by its middle management. 

That may be surprising to you, it certainly was to me, but not once Alok explains it. Middle management, he says, is the translation layer between “what the leadership wants to get done and what actually gets done.” 

Alok was a venture capitalist long before he was a founder, and it plays well into Indifi’s business. When you land on the company’s website, you see one sentence: “We help small businesses thrive.” And this is through assessing and calculating business risk before disbursing loans. The spidey sense that a VC develops for risk is quite useful for Alok the Lender, too. 

In his conversation with Rohin, Alok weaves in and out of these two personas—talking about organisational culture and leadership, successful careers, digitisation, raising money. Alok is a numbers guy and you can tell. He pulls out patterns, anecdotes, examples, and breakdowns in this episode—it’s perfect for a relaxed but informative Sunday listening session.

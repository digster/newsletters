Good Morning Ishan,
 
Ask anyone who’s been through the Covid wringer and they’ll have the same thing to say—it’s a rough ride. I got diagnosed two weeks ago. I was symptomatic, and seriously so. From high fever and nausea to headaches that put chronic migraines to shame, I needed a doctor’s attention.

But I couldn’t go to the hospital. I had one parent already admitted and another had comorbidities severe enough to require monitoring at home. Luckily for me, I’m privileged enough to live in a metro with hi-speed internet and doctors who are all right with looking at lab results through email and sending prescriptions via WhatsApp.

Of course, other, more formal versions of this last-minute patchwork of telemedicine already exist. Private teleconsultation platforms such as DocPrime and Practo had a rough go of mainstreaming and reaping the rewards of telemedicine.

Then there was an alliance of startups (including Practo) that came together at the height of the pandemic to create and operate an open-source telemedicine platform, but ultimately vanished without a trace.

Flying under the radar, though, has been the government-run platform e-Sanjeevani, connecting doctors to patients from hundreds of kilometres away. The platform was originally meant to connect doctors at government medical colleges with workers at local health centres for the purpose of training. It was retooled as a free telemedicine platform just as Covid’s shadow touched Indian shores.

The platform has facilitated a whopping three million consultations in around a year, of which around 2.1 million were sought directly by patients. Impressively, eSanjeevani now holds 35,000 consultations pan-India on a daily basis.

Crown jewel in the Indian government’s treasures? Not yet. From doctor shortages and connectivity issues to problems of accessibility—there are poor, illiterate patients who have no idea how to use the app, says one official—e-Sanjeevani has its share of hurdles to overcome. Even so, its potential is inarguable.
 
Little wonder then that the government has ambitious plans for the platform, including linking the telemedicine service with its upcoming unique patient health ID programme.

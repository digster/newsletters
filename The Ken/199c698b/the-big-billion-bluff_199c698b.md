Ka-Ching!
Thu, 09 Oct 25
A weekly newsletter about how finance is getting supercharged by tech in India, and how you can make money work for you.
Good Morning Ishan,
Every year, Flipkart’s Big Billion Days sale promises jaw-dropping discounts and deals.
And this time around, I finally thought I’d found one I wanted to take advantage of. The price of the new iPhone 16 and 16 Pro had dropped by almost Rs 20,000 on the platform. That’s definitely a “great deal”, I thought, adding it to my cart with a small feeling of triumph.
Then I looked closer.
Hidden among the fine print and checkout details were fees totalling Rs 627.
Not too much in the larger scheme of things, but they came in many creative forms: an offer handling fee, a payment handling fee, a protect promise fee, a platform fee, and a few more I’ve never heard of before. I am not bluffing; take a look at this checkout screenshot for a different order below.
And it did not stop there.
As I started comparing prices for different listings, I realised how many layers are built into Flipkart’s pricing structure right now—there are “special discounts”, “extra discounts”, “other discounts”… The same product shows a “listing price”, a “selling price”, and a “special price”. And which is the final price? Well, none of those.
It feels less like shopping and more like solving a puzzle.
Take a simple example. If you are buying a phone that costs more than Rs 50,000, you might naturally expect free delivery. After all, most platforms offer that on purchases above Rs 300. But Flipkart’s breakup showed a shipping fee of Rs 575 and a “secure packaging” fee of Rs 149. That is Rs 724 extra just for logistics.
The same product listed on Flipkart Minutes—Flipkart’s quick-commerce service—has a different fee structure, and ends up costing Rs 120 more.
Take a look.

Such inconsistencies are rife all over the platform. For instance, if you trade in your old phone, you are charged a Rs 199 pick-up fee. However, if you exchange a washing machine that weighs half a quintal or more, the pick-up fee is only Rs 100. Try making sense of that.
The issue has not gone unnoticed.
On October 3, Pralhad Joshi, India’s Minister for Consumer Affairs, Food, and Public Distribution, announced that the consumer affairs department would be initiating an investigation on such additional charges. Joshi’s response came after a viral thread on X exposed the charging of a special fee for cash-on-delivery orders.
Soon after Joshi’s tweet, many of these mysterious fees disappeared, leaving only the “secured promise” fee intact. Nevertheless, the Central Consumer Protection Authority (CCPA) has already initiated its probe.
This is not the first time Flipkart’s billing model has raised eyebrows.
Back in August, the Directorate General of Goods and Services Tax Intelligence (DGGI) started probing whether Flipkart’s confusing pricing structure was being used to manipulate GST charges. The concern is that if certain “fees” are charged lower GST rates than the one charged on the product, then a platform might be able to shift the taxable amount in a way that reduces its overall tax liability. In other words, the numbers might not add up.
The DGGI even [issued](http://www.gstclub.in/news/2315/Flipkart-under-preliminary-tax-probe-for-allegedly-recasting-marketplace-fees-as-transport-charges#:~:text=The%20Directorate%20General%20of%20Goods,%2C%22%20said%20a%20DGGI%20official.) a show-cause notice to Flipkart and other e-commerce platforms last month for the same.
Last year, Flipkart’s Big Billion Days sale generated Rs 19,400 crore in gross merchandise value (GMV) for the platform over six days, making it India’s largest e-commerce sales event. Around 89 million orders from 67 million customers were [processed](https://findly.in/flipkart-sales-statistics/), and 8.9 million new users were recorded on the platform.
This year’s complete numbers are yet to be public, but so far, Flipkart has [recorded](https://www.business-standard.com/companies/news/flipkart-festival-sale-2025-premium-demand-growth-125092401282_1.html) 26% year-on-year growth in premium product purchases across mobiles, televisions, and refrigerators, while Flipkart Minutes processed 4.5 million visitors, with order volumes doubling compared to regular days.
According to e-commerce market tracker Redseer, the first 11 days of the festive season this year have already seen India’s e-commerce players [surpass](https://redseer.com/articles/e-commerce-festive-1st-leg-11-day/) Rs 60,000 crore in GMV—nearly 3.5X the usual non-festive season level. About 90 million shoppers have already taken part, spending an average of Rs 7,000 each. The surge has been fuelled by the early start of the festive season, coinciding with Navratri, and the rollout of new GST rates that encourage spending.
Flipkart and its competitor Amazon are said to have transferred Rs 300 crore worth of GST rate-cut benefits to consumers. But with all these hidden fees and confusing charges, who can really tell?
Online shopping right now, even if many products do come at real discounts, has been turned into a test of awareness. Very often headline prices are designed to lure you in, but the final bill can tell a different story. In many cases, it is only a few popular flagship products that are sold at significant discounts. For other products, advertised discounts may not [save you much](https://www.newindianexpress.com/business/2025/Sep/23/flipkart-big-billion-days-2025-offers-are-real-or-not-detected-by-this-ai-tool#:~:text=PUMA%20Cassey%20Revamp%20current%20price,got%20the%20best%20value%20possible.), if they save you anything at all.
A very similar thing happened with Zepto earlier, something The Ken has also [reported](https://the-ken.com/story/ever-bought-a-rs-999-item-for-rs-199-heres-why-apps-cant-stop-using-dark-patterns/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter) on. But the platform eventually ended up [committing](https://www.threads.com/@dealztrendz/post/DOduQMrkZFX/zepto-finally-removed-all-hidden-chargesnow-only-handling-charges-gst-on-fee?hl=en) to no hidden charges.
As shoppers, the least we can do is stay alert. Check every line on the invoice. Question every new fee. Report the ones that make no sense.

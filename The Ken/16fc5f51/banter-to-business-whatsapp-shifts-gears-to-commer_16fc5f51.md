Good Morning Ishan,
“India runs on chai.”
That’s the slogan of a popular tea chain in India. It’s catchy—and probably true—but it also reminds me of another everyday adage for the country.
“India runs on WhatsApp.”
This second claim has hard stats to back it up. WhatsApp has 400 million monthly active users in India. That’s estimated to be 80% of all internet users in India, which, unsurprisingly, makes India the largest WhatsApp user base on the planet.
While it is already true that WhatsApp is the number one app for texting or calling in India, Facebook—WhatsApp’s parent company—wants more. It literally wants India to run on its app, per my home-made slogan above. That'll mean you can order taxis through WhatsApp, buy your daily groceries inside the app, use WhatsApp to refill your cooking gas, etc., etc.
This strategy of conversational commerce—a phrase that describes transactions made using chat interfaces—is the key focus for WhatsApp this year, and it has become of critical importance to Facebook.
Every tech giant worth its salt is keen to crack India—be it Netflix, Amazon or Google—but, unlike its peers, Facebook’s problem is different. It has reached the mainstream through WhatsApp, and now it needs to make money.
That hasn’t happened yet.
Despite those incredible user numbers, Facebook grossed less than $1 million in revenue from WhatsApp during its last financial year. That’s a modest return for any startup, let alone a $630 billion-valued company that sees $55 billion in annual advertising revenue.
With Facebook’s plan to add payments to WhatsApp stalled at the hands of the government, the US firm is putting all its might beyond this new strategy. Arundhati’s story today looks at the chances of conversational commerce taking off, how it has taken cues from China and how—with rivals like TikTok waiting in the wings—WhatsApp needs to step on the gas.

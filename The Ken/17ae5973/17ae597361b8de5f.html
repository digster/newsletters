Has Zomato set new benchmarks for internet companies to beat?

Nithin Sasikumar

In April (BFO#269), as the number of unicorns in India ballooned and reports emerged that many of India’s new-age internet companies were planning to go public (some in India), we wrote this:

Of course, promoters benefit in a market like this. Who wouldn’t want to sell a part of their stake and pocket some money when the going is good? Investors benefit a bit; they’re getting the chance to participate in and (hopefully) multiply their wealth from a wider set of companies, especially the tech ones.

But someone else benefits as well. By pocketing a nice, fat fee for these transactions. Investment bankers.

Now that the first in the line of these unicorns—foodtech company Zomato—has gone public in India (skipping an alluring US Nasdaq listing) and even completing its listing on Friday, let’s look at how these ‘parties’ to an IPO have fared.

The company

The stock markets are at all-time highs, and every day people are worried that it’ll finally crack. Just last week, the US markets experienced a tumble (it recovered the following day). Everyone laid the blame on surging coronavirus cases. So you can imagine that when you’ve worked so hard at setting your company up for an IPO, you want to get ahead of any bad news. 

Instead of the typical time of six days to listing after closing the IPO applications, Zomato completed it in four days. Which means that the company’s investment bankers and everyone else in between really sped up all the paperwork to get ahead of the deadline.

How much money the founders made

While there’s a lot of chatter on how Zomato’s founders own a very small part of the company (in effect making them ‘employees’ of their investors), owning a small part of what is now a very big company hasn’t worked out too shabbily for them:

[Deepinder] Goyal is worth $650 million based on his current 4.7% stake in Zomato, according to the Bloomberg Billionaires Index. He also owns more than 368 million options that vest over the next six years, which if exercised would almost double his stake in the company, which now has a market capitalization of $13.3 billion.

Zomato founder now among India's super rich after stellar listing, Livemint

How much Info Edge, one of their early backers, made 

Shortly before the IPO, Info Edge realised that the whole of India(!) wanted a bite of Zomato’s IPO. So it decided to halve the number of shares that it had initially planned to sell. Keep more shares, pocket more money. 

And as one of the early backers of Zomato, Info Edge has shown that all it takes is for one company in your portfolio to become a multibagger, and you’ll forget all the other bad investments you made.

Source: Twitter

How much retail investors made

Zomato IPO listed at Rs 116 (US$1.56) with 52.63% premium above its issue price of Rs 72-76 (US$0.97-1.03). It closed at Rs 125.30 (US$1.68). 

All those jokes about, “I spent Rs xxx ordering from Zomato in the past few years, and I’m investing in the IPO to make it all back”, have probably come true for some of the investors. 

Even those who weren’t lucky enough to be allotted shares during the IPO could’ve pocketed some neat gains with the stock rising nearly 10% yesterday. 

How much the investment bankers made

Rs 229 crore (US$30.77 million). This puts it at the higher end of payouts to investment bankers who’ll be loving it that Zomato didn’t list in the US.

And why so much? Well, they seem to have worked hard. 

“While the fee may look large, one should keep in mind that this was a first-of-its-kind IPO in the Indian market of a leading tech company. It needed substantial effort from advisors in preparation of the prospectus and working with the regulator to get approval for the deal as well as extensive marketing in overseas markets to get marquee investors, as is clearly visible from the anchor book of the IPO," a person aware of the fund-raising effort said requesting anonymity.

Zomato IPO delivered record fees for bankers, Livemint

Source: Livemint

So far, everyone seems to be a winner in this game. 

Even the stock markets 

In his story titled, Will Zomato bring “less sense, more sex” to the Sensex?, Sumanth Raghavendra wrote on 16 July:

bfo quote

Rather than the public markets changing the way Zomato works, Zomato’s effects on public markets could be more profound—bringing the inflationary forces of the startup world to public markets. Until now, public markets in India have largely been untouched by the drivers that are propelling startups to unprecedented heights. That may be about to change. […]

Zomato has a chance of heralding a new paradigm for public market investments that are driven more by sentiment than by spreadsheets.

WcMgcq

Has the benchmark been set on all counts—multiples, exits, fees, speed of listing—for the other internet companies who’ve lined up to list in India this year?

Keeping an eye on the kids

Ka Kay Lum

While every country is scrambling to inoculate its citizens, a group of people have been left out of vaccination programmes in most countries—children. And as the delta variant and other mutations of Covid-19 rage on, especially in Southeast Asia, the death rates among children are soaring. 

In Indonesia, hundreds of children have died in recent weeks because of the pandemic. Doctors are attributing this to vulnerabilities from malnutrition, obesity, and the country’s overall low vaccination rate—only 16% of Indonesians have received one dose. Indonesia struggles with a high rate of stunting, stemming from poor nutrition and hygiene. For the archipelago, it’s a double whammy as Covid has also pushed back efforts on its general vaccination programmes for children. 

Other countries in Southeast Asia, including Malaysia and Singapore, have also warned that there could be a spike in Covid cases among kids. So far, only Singapore has started vaccinating children with the Pfizer-BioNTech vaccine but even then, it’s only for those between the ages of 12 and 17. Many of the deaths of children happening in Indonesia are under the age of five. 

In June, the Malaysian government also approved the Pfizer vaccine to be used to vaccinate children above age 12. But vaccination for adolescents is likely to only begin later this year as priority is given to inoculating adults and senior citizens in the country. 

Kids may have been considered a low-risk group at the beginning of the pandemic, but are quickly becoming one of the most vulnerable groups.

Our fears were not merely to do with how vulnerable kids were to contracting Covid; we were also worried about what kind of damage a year of mostly being cooped up at home could inflict on their bodies and minds. And with the threat of a third wave looming, those concerns haven’t abated.

Tobacco companies have different ideas about the future

Nithin Sasikumar

India’s favourite meme stock—courtesy its languishing share price—tobacco major ITC, just announced its first quarter results (FY22). Overall its revenues and profits fell compared to last quarter. But the important thing is that it fell less than what analysts predicted. Even though analysts frequently get their estimates wrong, when a company does better than what analysts expect it to do, investors let out a cheer. And the stock price jumps a bit.

Now ITC is not just a cigarette company. It has business interests in hotels, agriculture, paper and packaging, and more importantly fast moving consumer goods (FMCG). It believes that FMCG could well be its future. In March (BFO#244), we wrote about how ITC was diversifying its FMCG into sugar-loaded treats (because if not cancer, there’s always diabetes) as a way to counter a decline in tobacco sales:

A report published in August 2020 by the World Health Organization (WHO) showed that cigarette consumption in India has been on a steady decline.

Also, many value-conscious investors don’t really like tobacco companies. Because they’re not helping anyone’s health. Smoking, including secondhand smoke, kills roughly eight million people a year, according to the World Health Organization. They also don’t like them because the over six trillion cigarettes produced every year have an environmental impact—water and land use, and toxicity.

ITC is not quitting its cigarette business though. It’s still a cash cow and contributes to over 70% of revenues. A push towards FMCG is just a way to continue to ensure it remains a giant in some form in the future. But while FMCG is supposed to be relatively recession-proof, rising raw material costs coupled with incomes being hit due to the pandemic has meant that both tobacco and FMCG have been downers for ITC this quarter. 

Over in the UK, the plans for the future are slightly different for another tobacco giant, Philip Morris International (PMI) which manufactures the famous Marlboro brand of cigarettes.

Last week, PMI’s CEO appealed to the UK government to ban cigarettes within the decade. Just like the plan to ban ‘petrol’ cars. Because alternatives to both tobacco and petrol are better. So it wants to become a “healthcare and wellness company”, “unsmoke the world”, and make sure that for those goals to be achieved, its executives get paid based on this metamorphosis. 

One way to go about this “wellness transformation” is to keep the addictive nicotine, but cut out the disease causing residue of tar. 

Tobacco firms have been moving into cigarette alternatives such as vapes and e-cigarettes. PMI is pushing the IQOS, a cigarette device that heats tobacco to deliver nicotine without the smoke and tar that cause diseases including cancer.

A move that could be fueled by the UK government’s “support” of alternatives like vaping. Public health experts had even called the World Health Organization’s (WHO) claims of the ill-effects of vaping as “blatant misinformation.”

With that support, you can see why PMI thinks the future is in “wellness”.

Back in India, in 2019, the Indian government went the other direction and banned e-cigarettes instead. A risk to India’s youth, they said. But, the government still owns 28% of ITC, directly and indirectly through the Life Insurance Corporation of India 🤷. So they can’t quite ban regular cigarettes yet. 

Has the future path for Big Tobacco been laid down by government actions more than anything else?

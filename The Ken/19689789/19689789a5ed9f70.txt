In the world of financial and capital markets regulation, most tech-first proposals die quietly in committee meetings. 

But the Securities and Exchange Board of India (Sebi) is now preparing to actually test the waters when it comes to fractional ownership of shares. 

Not just in theory, but as a pilot. On blockchain. 

Granted, this is part of the capital-market regulator’s Innovation Sandbox, and the pilot will be run on past data. But according to sources close to the development, Sebi has already formed an internal working committee to oversee the project and conduct the pilot in phases—one asset class at a time.

Neither fractional ownership of assets nor a tokenised market infrastructure based on blockchain is unprecedented. Fractional ownership has been popular in the US since the ‘90s, and other security regulators such as those in Singapore and Hong Kong have been running pilots around asset tokenisation for a while. 

But for the most part, all this has remained frustratingly absent from India.

Why “frustratingly”? Well, because of what fractional ownership allows retail investors to do. 

Say you have an eye on some company’s stock. Right now, the smallest unit you can buy is one share. But if that company is something like an MRF, Bosch, or Shree Cement, which are among the most expensive stocks in India, that one share could cost you tens of thousands of rupees. (Or over Rs 1,30,000, in MRF’s case!)

Fractional ownership would let investors grab a stake for far less by dicing up that one share into many little fractions. Can’t afford a Rs 5,000 price tag? How about a tenth of a share for Rs 500? 

Sounds simple, I’m sure. But… it’s not. 

And it’s not just Sebi’s opinion that could make or break the scheme.

Little strides towards big change

Sebi isn’t the only authority tinkering with blockchain and tokenisation. In February, another Indian regulator—International Financial Services Centre Authority (IFSCA), which oversees operations in GIFT City, Gujarat—released a consultation paper https://ifsca.gov.in/Document/ReportandPublication/ifsca-consultation-paper-on-regulatory-approach-towards-tokenization-of-real-world-assets03032025111644.pdf on creating digital tokens that would represent ownership of real-world assets and financial securities such as funds, bonds, stocks, commercial real estate, IPs, etc. And a meeting with startups in this context was concluded less than a week ago, on 25 April.

In 2021, Sebi had also asked depositories to use blockchain to record and monitor security creation as well as covenants of non-convertible securities. This is because blockchain, even then, had proven potential to create far more resilient systems than traditional centralised databases. Tokenisation based on blockchain’s distributed, decentralised tech can drastically improve how assets are recorded, traded, and settled. 

India has already been experimenting with optional T+0 settlements (or same-day settlements) for the top 500 listed stocks, but tokenisation promises near-instantaneous settlement https://www.weforum.org/stories/2025/03/tokenization-and-on-chain-capital-markets/ as default, not a privilege. 

That’s a powerful value unlock, especially in volatile markets.

Then, there’s the inclusion angle. India’s equity participation stands https://www.indiabudget.gov.in/economicsurvey/doc/eschapter/echap02.pdf#:~:text=The%20government%20has%20also%20achieved%20significant%20progress,64.2%20at%20the%20end%20of%20March%202024.&text=As%20of%20December%202024%2C%20the%20Indian%20stock,geopolitical%20uncertainties%20and%20election%2Ddriven%20market%20volatility%20challenges. at a modest 4–5% of the population. Some of that might be due to the high price barriers for certain stocks. A stock price of Rs 5,000 per share may not sound like a lot, but even that may be out of reach for a large chunk of the population. 

Fractional ownership, though, will allow investors to buy even 0.1% of an equity without having to invest in mutual funds or basket products like Smallcase, according to people close to the developments. And voila, just like that, the capital markets are opened to a whole new class of retail investors.

The plan right now is for Sebi’s pilot to be carried out in multiple phases. 

Phase one will be purely experimental. Think dummy trades using historical data, simulated for 3–4 months across various asset classes. Based on those results, the team, alongside Sebi’s internal working group, will pick one asset class to pilot in the live market. Initially, this will likely happen in high-value equities, which are ideal candidates for fractional ownership.

From there, the pilot will run for another six months in a restricted, low-volume environment. During this phase, real investors will be able to buy tokenised versions of those high-value equities, including fractional units, which will exist in parallel with regular demat shares. 

Same security, different wrapper. And it will be up to the investor to choose which version they want—standard or tokenised. But only the latter will allow fractional units.

Now, I wouldn’t blame you if this sounds like just another shiny fintech toy. 

But the real impact is structural. 

Fractional ownership will invite more investors into the market, increase liquidity in expensive stocks, and pave the way for broader use of smart contracts in capital markets. But more than anything, it signals that SEBI no longer wants to just watch the tokenisation wave from the shore—it wants to ride it.

And that’s great for the markets. 

But Sebi’s isn’t the only head that needs to nod for fractional ownership to become a reality, because there is a legal barrier. As referred to by my colleagues Anand Kalyanaraman and Seetharaman G in their June 2023 story https://the-ken.com/story/why-mrf-nestle-and-other-pricey-stocks-are-happy-being-exceptions/, the Companies Act 2013 simply does not allow investors to hold less than one share.

Last year, though, Sebi recommended https://www.moneycontrol.com/news/business/markets/have-asked-government-to-allow-fractional-ownership-of-shares-in-india-says-sebi-chief-12757950.html to the Indian government that the Companies Act be amended to allow fractional ownership.

If that happens, it could well be India’s biggest capital markets upgrade in decades.

But the operative word, of course, is if.

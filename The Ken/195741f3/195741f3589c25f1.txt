Everything Everywhere Ola at Once. Bhavish Aggarwal encounters the biggest, baddest, supervillain yet—irrelevance

Four weeks ago, I started a fun experiment because some subscribers wanted me to read this newsletter to them instead of reading it themselves. I never asked them why. Maybe people are lazy. Perhaps my voice is sexy. Or reading is going out of fashion. Who knows?

Anyway, for the last four editions, I dutifully complied. And The Nutgraf podcast was born. It was weird at first to read something for 15 minutes into a microphone, but now I’m starting to enjoy it. It reminds me of radio plays I did back when I was back in school (GenZs please go ask your parents).

So now, I feel much more confident making The Nutgraf into a paid podcast. In many ways, this parallels the evolution of The Nutgraf newsletter. For a while, I kept it free, until I figured out what I was doing. And once I got the hang of it, I made it into a paid product. When I started writing the newsletter, it was just gifs and memes. Today, it’s evolved into something bigger, from interactive visuals to community editions about quick-commerce, product management, and much more.

And just like how the newsletter evolved, I expect The Nutgraf podcast to change too. It’s going to be fun. I’m looking forward to it.

The one thing I’ll say about Ola is that they inspire me to come up with colourful metaphors, analogies, and wordplay. A couple of years ago, I came up with one to describe https://the-ken.com/the-nutgraf/ola-electric-sprinter-marathon/ Ola Electric’s maniac focus on short-term wins in a market where winning was only possible over the long-term. I called the company “a sprinter that is running a marathon”. Last year, when Krutrim, the AI company founded by Bhavish Aggarwal, received a US$50 million investment to become a unicorn, I wrote https://the-ken.com/the-nutgraf/krutrim-is-whatever-ola-wants-it-to-be/ that “it’s almost as though unicorns are imaginary animals”. Then a few months back, when Aggarwal was locked in a dumb Twitter war with stand-up comedian Kunal Kamra, I wrote an edition of this newsletter with the headline https://the-ken.com/the-nutgraf/how-ola-electric-blew-its-lead/ “How Ola Electric blew its lead”—a fabulous pun that I was extremely proud of that worked at multiple levels, but also one that nobody noticed, not even Jonathan, my editor. 

Anyway, now I’m here with yet another analogy to explain what Ola is going through in 2025. 

Everything Everywhere All at Once is one of my favourite movies of all time. If you haven’t watched it yet, please keep that information to yourself unless you want me to ridicule you for your poor taste to half a million registered subscribers in next week’s edition. The premise https://www.imdb.com/title/tt6710474/ of the movie is simple—Evelyn, a middle-aged Chinese woman (played by the wonderful Michelle Yeoh) is “swept up into an insane adventure in which she alone must save existence by exploring other universes and connecting with the lives she could have led”. In some universes, Evelyn is rich, successful, and famous. In another, she runs an ordinary laundromat. In another, she’s an expert in martial arts. You get the picture. If you don’t, go watch it. 

Most startups in India are content to exist in one universe. Some bold ones like Swiggy and Zomato exist across a couple of universes like food-delivery and quick-commerce. Others are bolder still, and try their hand in four or five universes, like Paytm* or Phonepe. In some universes, each of these companies are rich, successful, and famous. In others, they are exceptional at one thing, or ordinary, or an insurgent. 

Ola is the only company that exists across nearly every single universe in India. I’m not exaggerating. Here’s a non-exhaustive list of universes where Ola the company exists today: Ride-sharing. Electric Vehicles. AI. Quick-commerce https://www.livemint.com/companies/ola-to-join-quick-commerce-business-to-compete-with-blinkit-and-zepto-report-11723092378223.html. Food delivery https://www.hindustantimes.com/cities/bengaluru-news/ola-set-to-launch-10-minute-food-delivery-services-starting-from-bengaluru-report-101734504036678.html. Lending https://www.financialexpress.com/money/ola-users-can-now-apply-for-personal-loans-of-up-to-rs-10-lakh-directly-through-ola-app-3584599/. Credit card https://www.olamoney.com/olamoney-credit-card/index.html. Chip Manufacturing https://www.olakrutrim.com/silicon. Maps https://maps.olakrutrim.com/. Battery Manufacturing https://www.olaelectric.com/battery-innovation-center. Cloud services https://www.business-standard.com/companies/start-ups/ola-founder-s-latest-bet-on-cloud-and-free-offer-shakes-tech-industry-124051500754_1.html. Insurance https://www.business-standard.com/article/companies/ola-financial-services-to-expand-insurance-business-internationally-121110700213_1.html. Financial services https://www.olamoney.com/index.html. I’m pretty sure I’m missing some, but maybe somebody will build an AI research product that’ll do better than my google searches. 

For a long time, just like Evelyn, Ola was rich, famous, and successful in some universes. And just an ordinary player in others. 

But this year, something changed. Ola has encountered pretty big setbacks across the multiverse. 

You could almost say that it’s being threatened by a scary supervillain that’s attacking Ola’s everything, everywhere all at once. 

Let’s start with the original ride-sharing business. Ola Consumer, the company that houses the ride-sharing and the financial services businesses, reported a decline https://www.business-standard.com/companies/news/ola-consumer-turns-ebitda-profitable-revenue-declines-21-in-fy24-125021401261_1.html in revenue and operations last year. The fall wasn’t insignificant. Two years ago, Ola Consumer had a revenue of Rs 3,000 crore. Last year, that fell to Rs 2,300 crore. Sure, the business also reported https://www.moneycontrol.com/news/business/ola-consumer-achieves-profitability-in-mobility-financial-services-ebitda-jumps-to-rs-271-crore-in-fy24-12939794.html a modest profit, but that number “excludes discontinued operations” (whatever that means). Three weeks ago, Bhavish Aggarwal proudly announced this as an achievement, but I’ve been reading stories about Ola’s imminent profitability from as far back as 2018 https://inc42.com/buzz/with-profit-on-every-ride-ola-ready-to-be-profitable-in-2018/, so it’s hard to assess if something has fundamentally changed or not. 

There are other signs that Ola Consumer isn’t that attractive a company to others. Late last year, one of Ola’s investors, Vanguard, valued https://techcrunch.com/2024/11/04/vanguard-cuts-ola-valuation-to-2-billion/? the company at around US$2 billion, which is a pretty big cut from the US$7.3 billion valuation from back in 2021. And in January, there were reports https://auto.economictimes.indiatimes.com/news/aftermarket/bansal-is-in-talks-to-sell-usd-100mn-investment-in-ola-at-a-premium/117007845 that Sachin Bansal, one of Ola’s investors, was looking to exit his holding in the company at a valuation of US$4 billion. Seen from the outside, Ola’s existing investors certainly don’t seem to have much conviction in its future prospects. 

The one thing I’ll give credit to Bhavish Aggarwal is that he probably saw this coming a while ago, and thus, he moved quickly to create a hedge against it—by getting into electric two-wheelers. For several years, Ola Electric zoomed ahead at a searing pace and captured a significant market share. Last year, fueled by this performance, it went public. 

Well, here’s what Ola Electric’s stock price looks like today. 

In just six months, Ola Electric’s valuation has practically halved. There are perfectly good reasons why investors are running away from the company. Ola Electric’s early advantage in market share in two-wheelers has dissipated, and others have caught up with it. There’s also the problems with quality and service. As I wrote six months ago: “Aggarwal’s somewhat petty quarrel with Kunal Kamra has changed all that. Now the focus has shifted away from Ola Electric’s products, profits, and sales to its service. No company wants to talk about its service, because companies can almost never win the service narrative. Sales is benchmarked against the company’s past performance. Service is benchmarked against a flawless, ideal performance, i.e., success is when the company makes all customers happy. Even one dissatisfied customer keeps the narrative going.” This is exactly what happened. No matter how hard it tries, Ola Electric is unable to shake itself out of the perception that its products have poor after-sales service.

As a company, it has also attracted unwanted attention which has placed more pressure on its stock price. Just last week, Ola Electric’s battery subsidiary received a notification https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/markets/stocks/news/ola-electric-shares-in-focus-after-getting-govt-notice-for-missing-out-on-pli-target/articleshow/118723371.cms?from=mdr from the government for missing out on its PLI target. 

Then there’s Krutrim. 

Look, quite honestly, I have no idea what’s going on with Krutrim. I’m tempted to research it a bit and tear it down with specific explanations, but why bother? In general, it’s hard to separate the hype from the substance in AI, but Krutrim takes the dissonance to a point of absurdity. At one level, Krutrim just looks like someone https://www.financialexpress.com/life/technology-olas-krutrim-faces-fresh-scrutiny-after-indian-ai-chatbot-says-it-was-made-by-openai-3734627/ slapped a UI on top of ChatGPT. I’ve lost track of all the magical stuff that Krutrim can do, and how it’s India’s answer to Open AI, Nvidia, Microsoft, and Google all in one. Strangely, all of these claims seem to be made only by the company. Most self-respecting and serious AI experts and publications seem to be studiously ignoring Krutrim. If Bhavish Aggarwal comes out tomorrow and claims that Krutrim can solve unsolvable problems like cancer, interstellar space travel, or Bengaluru traffic, who’d be surprised?

Anyway, all of this is to point out one inescapable truth—Ola is facing significant setbacks all across its old world to its new world. This is what a perfect storm looks like. 

All superhero movies have a moment of crisis. We’ve seen movies like this all the time. For a while, everything is fine. And then, suddenly, a scary supervillain appears, portending the end of all the worlds. And our heroes need to team up and find a way to fight together to save the multiverse. 

This one is similar, but there are also significant differences.

The first big deviation in Ola’s situation is what failure looks like. In movies, our heroes are fighting to prevent the destruction of the world (usually multiple worlds). Nothing creates bigger stakes than knowing that if you lose, you and everything and everyone you know and love will get wiped out of existence. But businesses are not superhero movies. Like I’ve written before, companies that lose don’t just disappear overnight. Instead, they fall into an irrevocable, slow, death spiral of irrelevance. They lose cache. They lose currency. Companies don’t burn out. They fade away. A few decades ago, IBM used to be the most powerful company on earth. Today it’s around, but nobody really thinks about them much. Snapdeal used to be a major e-commerce company that threatened to outcompete Flipkart. It’s still around, but much more modest. The list of companies that slowly got eclipsed is a long one. Micromax. Rediff. Dharma Productions. This is the future that awaits Ola if it doesn’t find a way out. This is what it’s fighting against. Not some dramatic bankruptcy that happens at the snap of a finger. But irrelevance. And in many ways, irrelevance is a worse fate than death.

The other thing that’s markedly different is the supervillain itself. Ola isn’t fighting some big purple monster with a tragic backstory. Like all great superhero stories, Ola is fighting itself, and a changing world. Over the last several years, Ola, and Bhavish Aggarwal have aggressively positioned Ola as a company that’s willing to do anything, no matter how ridiculous it sounds, as long as it can hop on to the bandwagon. When food delivery became attractive and cool, Ola started doing food delivery. When quick commerce became the rage, Ola announced it would do quick commerce. When AI became sexy, Ola became an AI company. Over time, Ola’s constant stream of announcements became the corporate version of what happened with the news media when Trump became President. After a while, everyone loses track of things. They lose track of all the big claims about fixing service quality in bikes. They get immune to your press releases that announce the numbers. There’s only so much the business media, users and even consumers can take before they become numb to it. The shock and awe of launches and announcements is a drug that people get resistant to after a while. “We’ll make electric bikes” became “we’re announcing electric motorcycles” which became “we’re now going to make electric cars”. 

Then there’s Krutrim, which has announced everything from “building https://www.businesstoday.in/technology/news/story/olas-krutrim-partners-with-lenovo-to-build-indias-largest-supercomputer-plans-massive-ai-model-467073-2025-03-07 India’s biggest supercomputer” to steadily adding zeros to the tokens in its models, and nobody has any idea what it means anymore. For all of Krutrim’s claims of superiority, it’s telling that the only person who is actually putting money in it https://www.moneycontrol.com/technology/bhavish-aggarwal-pledges-more-ola-electric-shares-for-krutrim-personal-pledge-now-8-article-12958434.html is Bhavish Aggarwal himself. And this is an AI company we’re talking about. Masayoshi Son, CEO of Softbank has no problems, investing https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/tech/artificial-intelligence/softbank-openai-launch-500-billion-ai-infrastructure-project-masayoshi-son-named-chairman/articleshow/117442393.cms?from=mdr US$500 billion in AI infrastructure with Open AI in the US, but still hasn’t touched Krutrim—despite investing in Ola and Ola Electric earlier. What signal does that send to others?

People, even business analysts, journalists, and billionaire investors have work, family, and other things to attend to, and until they see or use something that’s demonstrably distinctive, they respond to all the announcements with fatigue, which morphs to studious indifference. 

This is the first step towards irrelevance. 

Those are just the internal problems. Ola is also fighting a bigger battle. Bhavish Aggarwal has modelled himself closely on Elon Musk, to the point where, as one Twitter user pointed https://x.com/sarayupani/status/1896614771899949070 out, “the unoriginality really runs deep”. He’s not the first CEO to model himself on Musk. This was great when Elon Musk was cool, hip, and desirable. But Elon Musk today isn’t the Elon Musk of five years back. Today, the Musk brand is undesirable, and consumers are voting https://in.mashable.com/tech/90928/tesla-sales-are-reportedly-falling-globally-how-bad-it-is-and-where with their wallet. This is not a good time to cosplay Elon Musk. Musk, at least, gained proximity to power to counteract his fall in perception. Bhavish hasn’t gotten his gains, but seems to have inherited the disdain.

So how does one beat the big supervillain—fatigue—and avoid the scariest fate of all—irrelevance? 

How will Ola emerge out of this crisis that threatens to consume its multiverse?

To be honest, I don’t know. There’s no easy answer here. 

I guess the only answer I can offer is what Evelyn did in Everything Everywhere All at Once to win in the multiverse. It’s this theme that makes this movie exceptional, and elevates it to something beyond a superhero trope.

Here’s Gamerant https://gamerant.com/empathy-biggest-new-movie-superpower/ which summarises it best: 

“But of course, there’s no greater film from 2022 that encapsulates this trend better than Everything Everywhere All At Once. The film’s plot spans numerous genres and countless universes, but at its core, it’s a story about the importance of empathy and kindness. 

Throughout the movie, the major characters are all confronted with the vast, cruel, uncaring nature of reality, and deal with it in their own ways. Some embrace despair, while others try in vain to control the chaos of existence. 

However, it’s mild-mannered Waymond Wang (Ke Huy Quan) who delivers the answer that saves everything: compassion.”

Regards,

Praveen Gopal Krishnan

The rise and impending fall of weight loss drugs. We are starting to see cracks in the facade
By Ruhi Kandhari
4 min read
View in browser

Good Evening Ishan,

You are reading our subscriber-only newsletter Inciting Incident—a weekly commentary on brand narratives and storytelling

At least one in five Indian adults are now overweight or obese. And one in ten children and one in twenty teenagers are expected to be obese by 2030. This trend is true for rural and urban areas, high-income and low-income households, and women and men. The obesity crisis, associated with the rising prevalence of diabetes, cardiovascular diseases, and cancer, is severe and growing. 

But I am not just counting the numbers, as most publications did this week on account of World Obesity Day. Paralleling the rise of growing obesity these past several years has been the emergence of an extremely popular narrative: “weight loss drugs are the most potent way to arrest obesity.” Even as early as 2023, this had spawned a global market worth US$4.5 billion—a number that is expected to cross US$30 billion in the next five years. 

We are starting to see the first cracks in this edifice, though. Touted as “miracle” solutions, the rising accumulation of research into outcomes is now starting to threaten this image. I’ve already written about the first such shift in this newsletter:

And finally, they really don’t do much to fix the proverbial bullet hole. In fact, they’re not even as good as a bandaid—because the real villain behind obesity is a more systemic problem that is becoming increasingly impossible to solve. It’s the food we eat—the high-salt, -sugar, and -fat packaged foods that are increasingly becoming a part of diets across the world. Those with their eyes on this ball want such junk food to be taxed, monitored, and controlled. And for more nutritious low-calorie foods to be available in supermarkets, catered events, and restaurants. And they are all sceptical of the new wonder drug.

[…]

The solution to obesity is a systemic overhaul of the entire food industry and the modern-day sedentary lifestyle. But fixing the system is a tougher problem to solve.

But this realisation didn’t initially do much to halt the substantial grey market that had grown around such drugs. Indians had even managed to find copycat drugs supplied via Bangladesh, and online portals and doctors unofficially started administering them. The Ken wrote about how—for many—this seemed like the magical solution to obesity. Even when the drugs were not approved for sale, and often not prescribed. 

People wanted them because they did something no other weight loss solution had done so far—they attacked the second-biggest villain after packaged foods in the obesity story. 

Food cravings.

It’s not the first time a diabetes drug has shown secondary weight-loss properties, according to Vaman Khadilkar, a Pune-based paediatric endocrinologist, who also practises in South Mumbai. Khadilkar is the lead author of the Indian Academy of Pediatricians’ latest guidelines on the management of childhood obesity. “It is the most effective drug right now acting on cravings, though.”

[…]

For a month’s supply, a box (that will come with a valid bill) that costs about Rs 60,000–70,000 (US$700–840) can be imported at double the cost, including shipping charges and tax—with a minimum of 10-day waiting period for delivery, Singh added. “But a doctor’s prescription is mandatory.”

Fast forward to last month and Nature Medicine’s study of over 2 million people who had used these drugs. It identified benefits, yes, but also side effects. Use of such drugs, the study found, was associated with increased risk of gastrointestinal complications, arthritis, hypotension, and a host of other conditions. (Here’s a non-exhaustive list of side effects: abdominal pain, nausea, and vomiting, GERD and gastritis, sleep disturbances, arthritis, arthralgia, tendinitis and synovitis, interstitial nephritis, and nephrolithiasis.)

And it isn’t just research. 

There is growing disillusionment among users too. 

Even as India’s pharma industry is working on rolling out generic versions of popular weight loss drugs, Times of India reported last month that many Indian users were now dissatisfied by the high costs and harsh side effects of such “miracle pills”. Enough that they are turning to bariatric surgery instead, according to a trustee at Obesity Surgery Society of India quoted in the story.

That’s a wrap for this week.

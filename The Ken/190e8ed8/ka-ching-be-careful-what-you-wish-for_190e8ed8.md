Be careful what you wish for.
For a long time, many folks including yours truly have been asking for a simplification of the capital-gains tax regime in India. In fact, in January 2023, before the Budget for the year was announced, I [wrote a story](https://the-ken.com/story/why-its-time-the-government-bit-the-bullet-on-simplifying-capital-gains-tax/) arguing why it was about time the government rationalised capital-gains tax and started encouraging investments on merit instead of just doling out sops.
At that point, the plethora of permutations and combinations possible in capital-gains tax computation made it a veritable madhouse, even for dyed-in-the-wool practitioners of the craft.

Well, finance minister Nirmala Sitharaman finally bit the bullet—a year and a half later—when she got up in Parliament this Tuesday to present the Budget for the current fiscal year.
The wish has been granted all right, but not quite the way many wanted.
First things first, and credit where it is due, the capital-gains tax structure has now indeed been simplified. By a great deal.
For capital gains to be classified as long-term, and hence eligible for concessional tax treatment, you need to hold them for a particular period of time. Until the year ended March 2024, there were three different holding periods for various asset classes. Now, that’s down to just two—12 months for listed financial assets such as shares and equity mutual funds, and 24 months for unlisted financial assets and assets such as real estate and gold. Nice.
But the sting in this rehaul lies elsewhere: in the new tax rates and the removal of a key benefit—indexation.
Short-term capital gains (STCG) on listed financial assets like stocks and equity mutual funds will now be taxed at 20%, up from 15% earlier. Also, long-term capital gains (LTCG) on these assets will now be taxed at 12.5% against 10% earlier. The consolation here is that LTCG tax on stocks and equity mutual funds is now exempt for up to Rs 1.25 lakh (US$1,490) a year, up from Rs 1 lakh (US$1,200) earlier.
The real standardisation—and one that has left many reeling—is in LTCG tax. Now, the LTCG tax on most assets, including stocks, equity mutual funds, listed bonds, gold and real estate, is the same 12.5%. Earlier, for assets like gold and real estate, the LTCG tax rate was 20%. That means the tax for those categories has been cut, right? What’s not to like?
Here’s what. Earlier, the LTCG tax rate of 20% came along with the benefit of indexation. Now, the 12.5% is without indexation. The benefit of indexation has now been removed across all asset classes.
That’s a seismic shift—especially in big-ticket real estate deals.

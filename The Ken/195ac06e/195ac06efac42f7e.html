Haldiram’s vs Bikaji just got spicier. Haldiram’s stake sale to Temasek will make Bikaji’s dream run hard to sustain
By Seetharaman G
5 min read

I don’t remember a stake sale being reported by the business media for quite so long as that of Haldiram’s. It’s easy to lose track of all the suitors eager for a piece of the snack giant synonymous with bhujia. 

But last week, Haldiram’s Snacks Food reportedly sold 10% to Singapore’s state-investment company Temasek Holdings at a $10 billion valuation.

A piece in a financial daily called this a “risky bet on India’s consumer market”, not because Temasek paid too much but because the investment will run into health-conscious urban Indians. 

If only that were true.

If you want to understand why Temasek will not be losing sleep over calorie-counting city folk, look no further than Bikaji Foods. Between the quarters ended December 2022, when the company went public, and December 2024, Bikaji’s sales grew 40%. Its market value has more than doubled since the listing to Rs 17,000 crore ($2 billion). In the same period, the Nifty-500 index has only risen by a third. 

Interestingly, Bikaji’s founder is the grandson of the man who founded Haldiram’s in Rajasthan’s Bikaner in the 1930s. 

Over the years, his [Gangabhishen Agarwal] descendants spread out to other metros to establish themselves. After a pitched battle in the 1990s, they marked their turf within India and overseas. But [grandson Shiv Ratan] Agarwal knew that division by region would become a barrier to growth. He decided to branch into a new brand, ‘Bikaji’, named after Raja Bika who had founded Bikaner

Bikaji Foods: Taking the taste of Bikaner global, Forbes India

Agarwal’s three brothers ran their businesses from New Delhi and Nagpur, and a cousin operated out of Kolkata.

Shiv Ratan was free to expand Bikaji as he saw fit, unlike the rest of his family. Bikaji is now sold in every single Indian state and Union territory. Plus, it has quadrupled the retail stores serviced by its own distributors—instead of brand-agnostic wholesalers—in under three years.

But the Haldiram’s-Temasek deal was the culmination of a move that could chip away at the advantage Bikaji had. The Delhi and Nagpur businesses merged in 2023, and the combined entity is the one Temasek has bought into.

The new company had sales of Rs 12,800 crore in FY24, far higher than Bikaji’s Rs 2,330 crore. Bikaji’s concerns will extend far beyond the gap in revenue. Across several states, Haldiram’s will now be able to use a single supply chain and a stronger distribution network.

What’s more, Bikaji’s one big hedge against the might of Haldiram’s seems to have come a cropper. 

Western snacks, which Pepsico and ITC dominate, are a bigger market than Indian snacks, so Bikaji and Haldiram’s have been trying hard to persuade consumers to give their chips and cookies a shot. But the share of western snacks in Bikaji’s sales, already in the single digits, has slipped further in the past two years. 

The decline was thanks to Bikaji slashing production as a result of rising potato and oil prices. Without the brand equity it enjoys in Indian snacks, Bikaji may not be able to pass on the rising costs to consumers without alienating them. So it’s better off deliberately selling less chips. 

But there’s no such respite in its mainstay, with Temasek’s investment in Haldiram’s likely tied to aggressive expansion. 

Even as private-equity giants and sovereign-wealth funds circled Haldiram’s, foreign portfolio investors continued to lap up Bikaji’s shares. Their stake in the company has more than doubled since the listing to 7.7%. Their domestic counterparts have raised their holdings, too. 

But they may not continue to be so enamoured of the company. After outperforming the Nifty 500, Bikaji has fallen more than the index this year. 

The days of Bikaji punching above its weight may be over.

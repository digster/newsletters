Good morning Ishan,  it’s nice to have you back again this Sunday. In Bengaluru, “proper” winter has set in a few weeks earlier, thanks to some low-pressure weather around Sri Lanka, which has led to fog, light rains, and chilly weather for us temperate city folk.

Gabru refuses to sleep on the floor now, seeking out indoor foot mats around the house to cosy up on. Bribo has found himself a discarded cotton mattress on the periphery of the nearby lot for the nights.

As the weather gets cold, I keep thinking how he—and all the other street dogs—manage to survive on their own. They must find food, befriend humans, socialise with other dogs, navigate traffic, find water, safeguard their territories, nurse their injuries, and so much more. And yet, I’ve never seen Bribo be anything other than curious, happy, and animated.

It’s hard to reconcile your imagined, human view of what a street dog ought to be feeling versus how they actually seem to be doing at times. I keep struggling with it.

I wonder if I should get Bribo one of those sweaters or fleece jackets for dogs. I know Gabru would tear it off within minutes. Have any of you tried outfitting your community dogs against the winter?

1. The infinite game and the player

As opening lines of books go, this one made me sit up and do a double take.

“THERE ARE at least two kinds of games. One could be called finite, the other infinite.

A finite game is played for the purpose of winning, an infinite game for the purpose of continuing the play.”

These are the opening lines from the 1986 book, Finite and Infinite Games: A Vision of Life as Play and Possibility. Written by James Carse, an American academic and religious scholar, the short book is a set of matter-of-fact statements that progressively build upon themselves as the book goes on. The statements are separated and bound by sequentially rising numbers, going from 1 to 101 over the book’s 98 pages.

101: “There is but one infinite game.”

The numbered sections are then split up across seven self-contained, postulate-ish chapters:

1: There Are at Least Two Kinds of Games

2: No One Can Play a Game Alone

3: I Am the Genius of Myself

4: A Finite Game Occurs Within a World

4: Nature Is the Realm of the Unspeakable

6: We Control Nature for Societal Reasons

7: Myth Provokes Explanation but Accepts None of It

This isn’t a business or self-help book, mind you. But it is a deeply reflective and philosophical one that could take you to the former places.

Using spheres like sports, politics, society, politics, culture, and even war, Carse argues that the best “games” are those where the play is enjoyable, not because winning is rewarding.

He calls these “infinite games”—games whose rules, boundaries, and participants all keep evolving as play happens.

“The rules of an infinite game are changed to prevent anyone from winning the game and to bring as many persons as possible into the play.”

When I read “bring in as many persons as possible into the play”, my first thought was you! Yes, you!

Do you realise we’re playing an infinite game with these newsletters? It’s been over two and a half years since I began writing First Principles. There was no game, and the only player was me.

And look now. We have so many of you writing in with book reviews and recommendations each week, curated and helmed by Tanim (and before her, by Hari). We have photos from all over the world, curated by Sakshi (and before her, by Jonathan). None of them—you, Tanim, Sakshi—were asked or required to play.

“It is an invariable principle of all play, finite and infinite, that whoever plays, plays freely. Whoever must play, cannot play.”

Why do you—we—do it?

An infinite player does not begin working for the purpose of filling up a period of time with work, but for the purpose of filling work with time. Work is not an infinite player’s way of passing time, but of engendering possibility. Work is not a way of arriving at a desired present and securing it against an unpredictable future, but of moving toward a future which itself has a future.

Infinite players cannot say how much they have completed in their work or love or quarreling, but only that much remains incomplete in it. They are not concerned to determine when it is over, but only what comes of it.

For the finite player in us freedom is a function of time. We must have the time to be free. For the infinite player in us time is a function of freedom. We are free to have time. A finite player puts play into time. An infinite player puts time into play.

What is the purpose of the First Principles newsletters? When will they stop? What sections can they contain? Who can write or submit to it?

I have no clear answers for any of these questions. Because I too, spent many hours through the week and over the weekend going through 123 album and song recommendations sent by so many of you last week. Remember, I’d asked you?

I’m planning to discover more such artists and their music by cutting through the forest undergrowth that is algorithmic recommendations. Seriously, have you tried to avoid them altogether?

Come to think of it, it’s been a while since we created a community playlist. So, let’s create a playlist composed of albums!

What I mean is that you have to suggest one album that you really love, but also your favourite song from that album. That way, we can create a playlist of songs that is also a starting point to jump off into great albums.

One more point–while we impose absolutely no guidelines (much less restrictions) on languages, genres, or decades, we will look for album suggestions that aren’t too popular or cliched. So, please, no The Life of a Showgirl.

And here are just some of the recommendations.

Rooh – aswekeepsearching

One of the only post-rock fusion bands in India. I came across them in 2019 and this album still has me captivated! – Dipankan Bandopadhyay, Kolkata

Blue Lines – Massive Attack

Apart from being their first album, it gives us a new kind of music, which later began to be categorised as lo-fi and trip hop – Sanjeev Mehra, Bengaluru

Moon Safari – Air

It’s the perfect blend of psychedelic, pop, rock, and electronic music that not many people have listened to. – Tapan, Bengaluru

Just As I am – Bill Withers

The songs are a collection of a man’s core memories. Memories of self-destruction, hope, and deep impact nostalgia. – Narendra S, Delhi

Fetch the Bolt Cutters – Fiona Apple

Came out during pandemic, this album captures the claustrophobia of that time & plays with themes of isolation, feminism, friendships, etc., as well as innovates by producing sounds from objects like utensils.

Expanding to One – Phi-Psonics

Wonderfully meditative and calming. Reminds me of a slow, winding afternoon. – Janhavi, Mumbai

Tabula Rasa – Béla Fleck, Pandit Vishwamohan Bhatt and Jie-Bing Chen

The combination of Banjo, slide guitar, erhu and mridangam blends bluegrass, Hindustani classical and traditional Chinese music in a way that provides a unique listening experience. The artists, all maestros with their instruments, have rendered beautiful soundscapes with this album. – Jayasekharan Unnikrishnan, Bengaluru

Buxtehude: Trio Sonatas, Op. 1

Have you ever wondered who inspired Bach? Well, Buxtehude is said to have been a great influence on Bach and Handel. Therefore, in someways, he is the original – Karthik, Gurugram

Sifar – Lucky Ali

Simple yet deep lyrics, all tied to travel. Lyrics full of wisdom as if written by a 70 year old man, but interestingly written by Lucky Ali’s friend in late teens—Syed Aslam Noor. The unsung hero of Lucky Ali’s early and purest form of work. Not to take away any credit away from Lucky Ali’s voice and modern (at least for that time) yet rooted music. – Abhijit B, Raleigh, NC

Babel – Mumford and Sons

The indie folk rock vibe of their band comes out so harmoniously and naturally in the entire album. Every song naturally leans into the troughs and crests of their mood – Parth Shah, Mumbai

Lost in the Dream – The War on Drugs

Lost in the Dream stands out to me because sonically, it feels very voluminous, and richly-produced, harnessing in particular the electrical guitar (so many incredible hooks and riffs), the drum machine, and the synthesiser, amongst various other instruments.  Bert McCoy once said, “from great suffering comes great art” and that is probably true in the case of this album. Adam Granduciel, the lead singer of this band and a general musical savant, battled clinical depression and paranoia during the making of this album. Listening to this masterpiece when it came out in 2014,  I could instantly tell that it wasn’t a culmination of an artist trying to fulfil a commercially-driven agenda, but someone who was seeking catharsis through art. – Amar Chandramohan

Run it’s the Kid – Run it’s the Kid

The band describes its music as ‘moody waltz’ and it’s exactly that. You sway through the highs and lows of each song and somehow relate to each and every word sung by Shantanu Pandit. – Angad Virk, Chandigarh

Mutter – Rammstein

Pioneering Neue Deutsche Harte album which paved the wave for the band to become the most popular ever German band and get worldwide fame despite being fully sung in German – Siddharth Chaudhari, Mumbai

Elwan – Tinariwen

My love for blues music has made me fall in love with the music from West Africa and this album is my first introduction to this band. – Mridula, Udupi

Desert Rose – Mame Khan

Amazing flavour of Rajasthani genre with modern touch in instruments. – Abhishek Soni, Pune

How to name it? – Ilaiyaraaja

A Bach inspired carnatic fusion album from the 80’s by Ilaiyaraaja. You don’t need another reason. For the uninitiated, this album gives a peek into the Maestro’s mind. – Kabilan, Chennai

Once – Nightwish

This was the apex of Nightwish’s symphonic metal prowess. – Udhay Shankar, Bengaluru

Mockroot – Tigran Hamasyan

It is a concept album so there are recurring musical motifs across tracks and each song is just an absolute pleasure to listen to. This is some really fine avant garde jazz that sits at the intersection of jazz and progressive metal – Abhishek Suresh, Chennai

Pappo’s Blues Vol.1 – Pappo

Argentina’s best blues & rock guitarists. Icon. It’s hard, heavy, gnarly, and terrific blues tinged rock. Will stack up with any of the great 70’s Brit or US bands. And this album is a good entry point into Latin American blues rock rabbit hole. – Harish, Bengaluru

Islands – Bear’s Den

Folk Album which comes alive when listening at night, alone in solitude. – Aatman Shah, Ahmedabad

Les Retrouvailles – Yann Tiersen

The album is a collection of disparate compositions, some instrumental, some with vocals, each with a different tempo invoking different emotions. Yann Tiersen uses vocals by his friends here and there, artists who are accomplished in their own right where the recording of the vocals give you the feel of an independent home studio recording. Listening to the album transports you mentally to a quieter place where you are able to access emotions without disturbance. – Snehit Vemulapati, Bengaluru

Five Leaves Left – Nick Drake

The whole album is moody and folksy. It takes me to my “self”. I love the feel of Nick Drake’s music. The fact that his success is entirely posthumous is sad, but adds to the allure. – Sujay, Bengaluru

Scenery – Ryo Fukui

Brilliant exposition of Japanese piano Jazz by an acclaimed master – Boban James, Singapore

Concierto – Jim Hall

Captures a blues / jazz vibe that is deeply evocative and tells a compelling story of discovery of oneself and the other in a brief 65 minutes – Shimal Kapoor

Thick As a Brick – Jethro Tull

A parody on how society takes a bright questioning child’s mind and forces it to dogmatic rigidity. – Vikram Sakhuja, Delhi

Departure songs – We lost the sea

The entire album is basically post-rock/indie genre. There are no lyrics. It’s just beautifully composed sounds that move you and take you through a journey. It’s a shame it’s not listened to by more people. Each song is an ode to a person or event in history that marked courageous expeditions in humankind – but which also ended in tragedy. There are songs based on the Challenger mission, Capt. Lawrence Oates, and one more on a deep sea diver. – Tarusri Raja, Bengaluru

Dafan – Aarlon

Metal as a genre is fairly underrepresented in India with lots of artists (who, by my subjective opinion, are at par with their European and American counterparts) continuing to dodge the spotlight they deserve. What makes this album in particular special is that I believe it has something for everybody. Even if you happen to be the sort of person who doesn’t like “screaming” (a staple of most metal songs), Dafan has you covered with melodic (and soothing) tracks in between the more “heavy” ones. This makes the album like a roller coaster of emotions and it is one of the few albums in my library which I can listen from start to finish, with each song complementing the others. The lyrics are in Hindi and in the band’s own words are supposed to take you on a journey “through the gamut of emotions a human being can experience in their lifetime shackled in the cages of a capitalist society”. Dafan also happens to be the band’s debut album. – Atulya, Mumbai

4. Pick of the month: My friends by Fredrik Backman

Hello Ishan, it’s Tanim with your FP Books pick of the month. 

This month’s pick is a tearjerker. If you have read Bridge to Terabithia or All the Bright Places and liked them, then you would fall head over heels in love with this book. Even if you weren’t a fan but thought they were decent, you would still love the book because I fall into that category. 

It’s called My friends by Fredrik Backman.  A melancholic melody that makes your heart squirm on every other page. If I could annotate the whole book, I would. 

The premise of the book is: a world-famous artist dies, leaving his most famous painting to an orphaned 18-year-old. Honestly, this is not something that would make me pick up this book. But I did, because, as the novel says, “Art is context.” And the context is what made me choose it.

I had heard of Fredrik Backman but I had never read any of his books. Because of what I had heard earlier and because it was a fairly recent book, I dived in without reading anything else about it. The book describes the importance of context in art with an example that makes you feel blue. 

If a homeless man in the street had tried to sell the painting of the children on the pier, it would have been worthless, but once it was hanging on a white wall in a beautiful gallery, it cost a fortune. When sufficiently wealthy people want something bad enough, it becomes invaluable, because then art isn’t experienced through the eyes, but by the ears, they’re not paying for a picture but for its name and history. In their world it isn’t the artist who should be admired, it’s the owner, because only something which has a price can have any value. That’s why the children on the painting are so important that they’re protected by guards, but the children on the pier in real life could die without anyone even caring.

I sampled a few other books before I settled on picking this one for this month because I wanted something that captured my interest in the first five minutes. I saw that it was released eight months ago, so I decided to give it a try. I fell in love from the first paragraph itself, but then, as I read more, I realised that there were far better things to come. 

Backman solemnly and very empathetically handles the thread that connects art and lived life. It is both a privilege and a necessity. 

And she’s particularly angry about rich people buying art, because rich people are the worst sort of adults, and the worst way to vandalize art is actually to put a damn price tag on it. That’s why rich adults hate the sort of thing that Louisa paints on the walls of buildings, not because they love walls, but because they hate the fact that there are beautiful things that are free.

It’s not all doom and gloom, though. After all, the characters are sometimes children and sometimes adults, and the author tries to paint the beauty of childhood friendships, such as in this conversation which made me laugh out loud:

Once he heard someone on television talking about “organic meat” and asked the artist what it meant, and the artist suggested that maybe it meant the pigs got better food and were allowed to live outdoors, and maybe be happier? “So they only murder the happy pigs? Isn’t that fucking worse?” Joar wondered angrily. The artist had no reply to that. It was hard to argue with Joar’s logic, however flawed.

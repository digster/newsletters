Coming clean is always the best option.
 
That’s what India’s top drug companies must be thinking right now as the news of their alleged involvement in the massive generics price-fixing conspiracy ripped the pharmaceutical industry just last month.
 
As many as 45 states in America are party to the antitrust investigation which alleges companies, including eight from India, indulged in “artificially inflating prices and unreasonably restraining trade throughout the US”. Cumulative sales of the 13 drugs listed in the lawsuit are estimated to be around $1.2 billion.
 
The agreements between companies were kept off the books, inked at industry dinners, girls’ nights outs and working lunches, according to the complaint. Heritage, the American subsidiary of Pune’s Emcure Pharmaceuticals, was the ringleader.
 
While the two senior executives of Heritage have pleaded guilty, the lawyer representing generics companies says the antitrust case deals with a limited number of products that have a limited amount of sales. “So, it is like comparing the elephant to the elephant’s toenail. The antitrust case is about the elephant’s toenail.”
 
Today’s story is not about whether this is Price Fixing 101 or not. As some analysts have said, “proving price collusion in a court is easier said than done”. Granted, but the reputation of firms has already taken a beating. And state authorities say their investigation is just beginning; they will now widen their net.
 
For the first time, the detailed complaint allows a reconstruction of alleged price-fixing among generics players in America. Our new contributor, Gayathri Vaidyanathan, gives you a peek into how it went down.
 
Last week, the Competition Commission of India began investigating an allegation of price fixing of a diabetes drug involving two Indian and two multinational companies. Emcure is one of them.

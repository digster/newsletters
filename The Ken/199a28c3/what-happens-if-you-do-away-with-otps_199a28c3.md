Good Morning Ishan,
If you are done with one-time passwords (OTP) taking over your life, you should thank the Reserve Bank of India (RBI) for its latest notification. But I’d suggest waiting until the end of this piece before you start celebrating.
Ever since 2009, when the banking regulator mandated that all payments must have two-factor authentication (2FA) to protect consumers, OTPs have taken on a life of their own. Back then, all the RBI said was that banks should put in place a system for additional authentication and validation based on information that was not visible on cards for all card-not-present transactions.
That additional authentication ended up becoming SMS-based OTPs.
What started as a way to authenticate payments, however, has now infiltrated every part of digital life.
Want to login into an app? You need an OTP.
Want to accept an online delivery? OTP, ma’am.
Want to use government services like Aadhaar or Digilocker? OTP, please.
Now, Indians get almost 2.5 billion OTPs in a day, estimates Bhavik Koladiya, who runs OTPless, an authentication and access management platform. But more importantly, OTP frauds have become all too common. The RBI has repeatedly cautioned people not to share their OTPs, but fraudsters are good at wheedling them out of their victims.
The regulator recognises by now that it can’t put this genie-turned-demon back into the bottle, but it also knows OTP-based authentication needs to change.
So, on 25 September it sent out a [notification](https://www.rbi.org.in/Scripts/BS_PressReleaseDisplay.aspx?prid=61282) saying it was now “encouraging the introduction of new factors of authentication by leveraging upon technological advancements”. And as if it were wary of being blamed for breaking things with its circulars, it hastily added that it wasn’t calling for the “discontinuation of SMS-based OTP as an authentication factor”.
But in typical RBI-style, the way it lays out further [directions](https://www.rbi.org.in/Scripts/NotificationUser.aspx?Id=12898&Mode=0) for using 2FA ends up effectively throwing out OTPs as an option.
That’s because it has now laid down two important principles.
The first is about what counts as two-factor authentication. The RBI wants it to be a distinct mix of at least two of these three things:
i. “something the user has”, like a sim card or device.
ii. “something the user knows”, like a password or pin.
iii. “something the user is”, like a fingerprint or any other form of biometrics.
If you consider this framework closely, online card transactions verified by SMS-based OTPs do not satisfy these principles. Because, for transactions like online credit-card payments, you currently check for “something the user knows” twice, says Koladiya. For instance, to finish a transaction, you give the card’s CVV number and then an SMS-based OTP.
As things stand now, only UPI’s 2FA meets the new principles, since one factor of authentication is something the user has (the device that’s bound with a sim) and the other is something the user knows (the pin).
But even UPI will have to change the way it authenticates payments if you consider the second principle that the RBI is insisting on—which is that one of the factors of authentication has to be dynamic and unique to that transaction. And since it’s the same pin that is in use for every UPI transaction, there will need to be some changes there too.
The RBI has said all payment-system providers, including banks and non-bank entities, should comply with these new guidelines by 1 April, 2026. Those in the industry expect the deadline to be extended, but they also know they’ll need to brace themselves for the aftermath, since this will require a massive overhauling of existing systems.
Users, merchants, banks, and payment companies already got a taste of what such new rules can mean in 2021, when the RBI mandated a seemingly simple change to recurring payments. It said banks would need to send a notification to customers about auto-debits at least 24 hours before the renewal date, along with the option to cancel the subscription. It did this to give users greater control over recurring payments.
But that fundamentally changed the subscription business, which was never the same again.
A world without OTPs could potentially be even more disruptive.
“This will be an infra-level change that every participant will have to make. It will cut through every single one, from banks to payment aggregators to merchants,” says Koladiya.
And it’s bound to change the customer journey for anything we buy online.
There is no doubt OTPs are problematic. But it won’t be long before we get to know how users, banks, and merchants truly feel about them, when things we’ve taken for granted start breaking without OTPs.

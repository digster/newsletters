Good Morning Ishan,
 
If earlier this week you felt the earth tremble in your cities in India, it was likely caused by the shifting of vast amounts of money—into shell accounts, junk status, or simply being frozen in their tracks as Yes Bank went dark.
 
The impact of Yes Bank screwing up its loan book on the digital ecosystem was widespread. Payments companies like PhonePe and BharatPe, which had Yes as a partner bank, found their services stalled. Many others using Yes APIs, such as food delivery company Swiggy and payments intermediary Razorpay, were all seriously affected.
 
Despite Yes Bank’s financial stability being questioned publicly for months now, companies like PhonePe didn’t plan for business continuity. In fact, most fintechs took Yes Bank’s APIs for granted and never had a backup plan.
 
For its smarts, PhonePe managed to switch to ICICI Bank with amazing swiftness. But the whole incident raises some difficult questions. Does PhonePe store customer data and was Yes Bank just a business partner? How the hell did Yes Bank become such a dominant backend intermediary in a sector where banks aren’t particularly known for their software? Why did fintechs flout the directives of the quasi-regulator, the National Payments Council of India (NPCI), on business continuity? On 2 March, NPCI reminded all its third-party apps to migrate to a multi-bank model but, curiously, that circular did not have a compliance date.
 
A mature company like Google signed up ICICI and HDFC banks as its partners when it entered payments. But startups, with hustle in their DNA, did not take NPCI seriously. Because NPCI is not RBI.
 
In her three years of tracking fintech, Arundhati has seen it all but she’s still whipped up a riveting account of how in taking down Yes Bank, RBI scrambled India’s digital space. She tells you about five second order effects that will follow. One is, NPCI will (should?) now have a regulator.

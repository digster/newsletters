How one critical sell report destroyed the career of a respected equity analyst. And how he still won't quit.
Good morning Ishan,
 
Truth is the absolute defence against libel, but not in India. Thank the British, but blame us Indians.
 
Defamation became a crime in the mid-1800’s in India, primarily by criminalising criticism to protect the interests of India’s erstwhile colonial rulers. Over 150 years later, it continues to meet its purpose. Ask Nitin Mangal.
 
Mangal is the subject of today’s massive feature story that took Ashish nearly 2 months to fully report. An enterprising chartered accountant from small town India, Mangal stumbled upon equity research as his calling quite serendipitously. From Ashish’s 8,500-word story (yes, 8,500!):
 
"After all, this had been the dream. To write a differentiated report, something which can shake up the status quo. The language. The research. The data collection. The deep understanding of what a promoter is actually trying to do, not just a buy or a sell report. It thrilled him to bits.”
 
Till, on 8 August, 2012, Mangal ended up committing a “crime” that would destroy his career and life. He co-authored a critical sell report on a large and powerful company, Indiabulls.
 
Retribution was swift, unforgiving and unrelenting. Cases were filed against his firm and him in multiple jurisdictions, including 2 criminal cases too. He was briefly arrested and put in jail. Today, 5 years after that event, he is only a shell of his former self.
 
The repercussions of what happened to Mangal though, are being felt throughout India's equity research industry. In the words of Saurabh Mukherjea, CEO of Ambit Capital and one of the most respected names in equity research:
 
"In India, we are still a family, feudal society. There are invisible hierarchies, meant to stay within and follow. High-quality research, whenever it upsets a vested interest, challenges a pre-set hierarchy. This hierarchy usually forms a clique. And they are using the legal system to put the offender in his or her place.”
 
As an example consider the Rs. 1000 crore (~$150 million) lawsuit filed this August by cigarettes-to-hospitality conglomerate ITC against IiAS, an advisory services firm. The crime? A critical research report.

Changes are afoot in not just what the FMCG major sells, but also how. Smart TVs offer a clue
Inadequate job and income growth, combined with high commodity prices, have dealt a serious blow to India’s FMCG firms. But the top 20% of consumers, or 60–70 million households, is experiencing a boom, unlike the rest of the country. This is reason enough for Sudhir Sitapati, Godrej Consumer’s MD and CEO, to increasingly focus on this group—with products like dog food and laundry liquids. Selling to these consumers, many of whom swear by streaming services on their smart TVs, allows Godrej Consumer to market its products more efficiently. [Private equity’s priority for Indian schools: pickleball courts over teacher training](https://the-ken.com/story/private-equitys-priority-for-indian-schools-pickleball-courts-over-teacher-training/?utm_source=weekly_email&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=weekly_recap_card)
Falling budgets, lower salary hikes, and shrinking student numbers—this is what PE acquisitions bring to Indian schools
PE-backed acquisitions have led to diminished budgets, reduced salary hikes, teacher attrition, and declining student numbers at once-prestigious Indian international schools. The Western-style cost control and standardisation don’t fit India’s volatile teacher market or founder-led school culture, causing trust and quality to fray. Further, operators sometimes prioritise visible upgrades, such as sports courts, over essentials like teacher training and salary hikes. While a lot of acquisitions struggle, long-term focused buyers like Cognita have shown that PE investment can preserve culture and improve governance if adapted to Indian conditions.

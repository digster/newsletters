Good Morning Ishan,
 
Investopedia defines ‘New Economy’ as “new, high-growth industries that are on the cutting edge of technology and are believed to be the driving force of economic growth and productivity”.
 
That stack of books you once ordered online in the early years of this decade? That was the new economy speaking. That time when your Amazon package went to the wrong address, and you either had to get it picked up via Dunzo or order an Uber to get it? Yep, new economy.
 
As the 2010s wound down, more and more elements of this new economy slowly intertwined with the big, old economy. Buying groceries via WhatsApp from your neighbourhood mom-and-pop store is now normal. I should know; my mother regularly tells me about her adventures with online shopping. There are a lot of ‘WhatsApp shops” that have mushroomed around our centuries-old local market, she says.
 
As the new economy turned a new page and came of age, so did our stories in its breadth and scope. In fact, many elements of this new economy are already dated—or will soon be dated—as you will find out from this week’s stories. We have a diverse set of tales from quantum science to WhatsApp shops.
 
My personal favourite was Seetharaman’s story on Reliance Retail and D-Mart, which coincided with the swashbuckling annual general meeting of Reliance Industries. He looked at how India’s two big successful retailers have grown very differently, under very different circumstances. The coming together of Reliance’s online grocery venture, JioMart, with Reliance Retail, will force the likes of D-Mart to up its e-commerce game.
 
Seema told us the fascinating story of quantum computing startup QNu Labs. Quantum computers can crack open encrypted messages that would take 10,000 years for existing supercomputers to crack. China and Israel are already in the lead, and others are playing catch up. India is just beginning its quantum computing journey; QNu labs is the only Indian member of the Quantum Alliance Initiative that comprises countries such as Canada, Australia, the US, South Korea, Japan, and others.
 
Mid-week, Olina told us yet another story from the burgeoning ed-tech space. Swayam, a government-funded ed-tech platform, could have been the go-to for millions of students who could not get access to education at this time. Instead, it is still grappling with rather fundamental problems. It's as much a "what could have been" story as it is a "what can be" story. This was also our free read for the week, so hit that share button.
 
My mother could have contributed to the next story I'm going to talk about. Prasannata wrote about how WhatsApp is being used by businesses more than ever before. But of course, all is not what it seems. WhatsApp Business may be the version of the app that makes money for parent company Facebook. Retailers, however, prefer using the regular old chat app.
 
At the close of the week, Pratap wrote about how those responding to BSNL’s 4G network tender are having to twist themselves into knots over this. The tender forbids Chinese vendors. And those that BSNL is relying on to apply—IT service companies like TCS and Tech Mahindra—already work with global telecom companies. So what happens to these relationships once they also become competitors?
 
That’s all from us this week. Stay safe. If you’re able to do so, shop online, and practice social distancing.

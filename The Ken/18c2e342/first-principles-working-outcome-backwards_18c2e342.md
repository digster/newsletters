It’s a good thing our theme today—working outcome-backward—is related. (My favourite counter question as a manager when someone lists out their achievements at the end of a year is, “But is this what you wanted to achieve last December?” It’s not an easy question for me to answer too!)

I. Working outcome-backwards 🔮
|
|
“I’m a hustler. It takes a lot of work to get me to slow down or switch off,” says Smita Deorah.
Smita is the co-founder and co-CEO of LEAD School, an “edtech” that is somewhat different from most others we’re used to. It provides curriculums, infrastructure, and training to schools with an aim to improve learning outcomes for children.
Smita and I were making small talk on Friday evening before delving into mental models. I was telling her that December is usually a slow and reflective month for me and The Ken. In contrast, the months of November to January are LEAD’s busiest months, replied Smita. Because that’s when schools are making key decisions about their curriculums, pedagogies, and training programs for the next year.
I ask Smita how she manages to slow down or switch off, in spite of herself.
“Travel,” she replies. “It helps me zoom out, so I try and do it as often as possible.”
As it turns out, it was a vacation that changed Smita’s outlook on the way she planned her own life and career.
Roughly twenty years ago, she recalls, she and her husband Sumeet (who is also Smita’s co-founder at LEAD) were wondering where to go for their next holiday.
“Let’s go to Antarctica!” said Sumeet, probably half on a lark. Smita was taken aback and asked, “Who goes there? Can we even go?”
To which Sumeet replied, “I don’t know. But if we want to go, we’ll figure it out.”
The two spent the next year planning their Antarctica vacation. To date, says Smita, it was her best vacation.
What does that have to do with lives and careers, you must be wondering. I did too.
Smita says that is when it struck her that we can make things happen once we decide that is the future we want. She calls it outcome-backwards. It is also her favourite mental model as a leader.
Most of us think and operate “today-forward” in contrast, she says. Meaning, we make decisions based on our experiences in the present, instead of from the future we desire.
“As human beings, we have the power to create what we want. To put what we want to on a blank canvas, and then build it,” says Smita.
She recollects the time she and Sumeet used to coach high schoolers with their career choices. “Some kids would say, I am good at math, hence I want to… and I would tell them it wasn’t merely about what you are good at today. Instead, I would ask them to imagine themselves at age 25. We would paint a canvas. To get to 25, what should they have done at 20? 15? The next year. It changed the way the kids thought about their future,” she says.
“My own initial career choices were ‘today-forward’. I hated science for a few years in school, so I took up commerce as a reflex. In hindsight, it was a bad choice. Because I am fundamentally a builder. And accounting and finance wasn’t something I ever liked,” she recollects.
Why is outcome-backward better than today-forward, I ask Smita.
It’s because our thinking always depends on the context, she says. When we work today-forward, our context is the present. And that means all the messy constraints, realities, and frustrations that surround us. Our imagination and thinking, thus, tends to become a reflection of that: transactional, conservative, iterative.
Imagining a path backwards from a future outcome frees us. It makes us more creative and unconstrained.
An organisation that is known to “work backwards” outstandingly well is Amazon. One of its simplest and most powerful methods to help employees do this is to get them to [write a future press release](https://coda.io/@colin-bryar/working-backwards-how-write-an-amazon-pr-faq) for something they want to build.
I recommend this book for anyone who wants to dive deeper: [Working Backwards: Insights, Stories, and Secrets from Inside Amazon](https://www.goodreads.com/en/book/show/53138083), by Colin Bryar and Bill Carr.
At LEAD School, Smita says there is no vision statement. Instead, they have a one-page “vision note” written by her and Sumeet. “For any new person who joins us, it paints a vivid picture of what we want the future to be, and how we want to build it. We reference it in our annual planning exercises and figure out how we can work backwards from it to decide what we will do the next year. When we do that, often, we don’t have the capabilities or the knowledge on how to do things. But then, we decide how to do it,” she says.

Working outcome-backwards isn’t just for founders or executives. As a professional, one of the most powerful ways to do this with your own career is to write your future resume.
What will your dream resume one year from now look like? What achievements will it list? What skills will it highlight? What responsibilities will it play up?
What is stopping you from making those into reality?

Unleashed: The Unapologetic Leader's Guide to Empowering Everyone Around You by Francis Frei and Ann Morriss
I got on to this book after reading an earlier one by the same authors, Uncommon Service (reviewed in [Edition 20](https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/edition-20-minimise-your-regrets/)). I took away a single powerful insight from the entire book, and thus, I will excerpt that.
|
“The practical definition of leadership we use in our work is that leadership is about empowering other people as a result of your presence—and making sure that impact continues into your absence.
Your job as a leader is to create the conditions for the people around you to become increasingly effective, to help them fully realise their own capacity and power. And not only when you’re in the trenches with them, but also when you’re not around, and even (this is the cleanest test) after you’ve permanently moved on from the team."
|
|
|
|
Try drawing your own leadership performance curve. Recall a team you’ve been on, one where you’ve spent some real time (no less than three months). What happened to the slope of other people’s performance after you showed up? Did it go up or down? If your curve generally looks like the one in the figure, then you (likely) helped to create the conditions for other people to succeed. But if it’s flat or negative or simply not as steep as you know was possible, think back to the choices you made as a leader and teammate. What could you have done—big or small—to improve your team’s performance?”
|
|
|
And an extension of this into an everyday event—meetings.
|
|
“...change your objective from getting what you need in the meeting to making sure everyone else gets what they need. In other words, take radical responsibility for everyone else in the room. Share the burden of moving the dialogue forward, even if it’s not your meeting. Search for the resonant examples that will bring the concepts to life, and don’t disengage until everyone in the room understands. Note that this is almost impossible to do as long as texting or checking email is an option, so put away your devices (everyone knows you’re not taking notes on their good ideas).”
|

It’s rare to come across deeply argued essays on leadership that are contrarian and full of insight.
This essay made me think as a founder, professional and parent. Unsurprisingly, this essay dates back to 2010 because originality never gets old.
[Solitude and Leadership](https://theamericanscholar.org/solitude-and-leadership/) by William Deresiewicz in The American Scholar (March 2010)
|
“The chance to find yourself.” Now that phrase, “finding yourself,” has acquired a bad reputation. It suggests an aimless liberal-arts college graduate—an English major, no doubt, someone who went to a place like Amherst or Pomona—who’s too spoiled to get a job and spends his time staring off into space. But here’s Marlow, a mariner, a ship’s captain. A more practical, hardheaded person you could not find. And I should say that Marlow’s creator, Conrad, spent 19 years as a merchant marine, eight of them as a ship’s captain, before he became a writer, so this wasn’t just some artist’s idea of a sailor. Marlow believes in the need to find yourself just as much as anyone does, and the way to do it, he says, is work, solitary work. Concentration. Climbing on that steamboat and spending a few uninterrupted hours hammering it into shape. Or building a house, or cooking a meal, or even writing a college paper, if you really put yourself into it.
“Your own reality—for yourself, not for others.” Thinking for yourself means finding yourself, finding your own reality. Here’s the other problem with Facebook and Twitter and even The New York Times. When you expose yourself to those things, especially in the constant way that people do now—older people as well as younger people—you are continuously bombarding yourself with a stream of other people’s thoughts. You are marinating yourself in the conventional wisdom. In other people’s reality: for others, not for yourself. You are creating a cacophony in which it is impossible to hear your own voice, whether it’s yourself you’re thinking about or anything else. That’s what Emerson meant when he said that “he who should inspire and lead his race must be defended from travelling with the souls of other men, from living, breathing, reading, and writing in the daily, time-worn yoke of their opinions.” Notice that he uses the word lead. Leadership means finding a new direction, not simply putting yourself at the front of the herd that’s heading toward the cliff.

This next one just reinforced my existing views on “AI” and all that it is leading us to.
[AI and the Rise of Mediocrity](https://time.com/6337835/ai-mediocrity-essay/) by Ray Nayler in Time (November 2023)
|
The problem for AI is that creative work is not predictable. It is not about statistical likelihood or simply mashing up the familiar—it is about leaps in logic and counterintuitive juxtapositions. It is about the unique experience of the individual, and seeking to do what has never been done before. It is about the least predictable next word or pixel. So the danger is not that AI programs will write the next great novel or create the next great painting, successfully replacing human inventiveness: they never will. The greater danger is that they won’t need to create great writing or art.

And the final recommendation comes from subscriber Karan Kothadiya, a policy researcher in the climate space.

|
Children have two advantages. First, they’re new to the world and constantly confused by it. So they ask about everything. Several years ago, the psychologist Michelle Chouinard listened to recordings of young children spending time with their parents. On average, the kids asked more than two questions per minute. Nearly a quarter of those questions sought explanations; the kids wanted to know how or why. Second, they aren’t afraid of getting things wrong – or seeming silly. Kids are wrong all the time. And silly is their main line of business. That makes them fearless thinkers, ready to share their thoughts, even if they don’t know that they’re right.

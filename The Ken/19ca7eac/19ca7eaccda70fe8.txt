1. What three skills?

Some First Principles readers might remember a January 2024 edition https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/first-principles-horror-vacui/ in which we first talked about values worth inculcating. We talked about curiosity and belief.

Of the many values that parents hope to instil in their children, curiosity is easily among my top three. Another would be believing.

Because belief is the precursor to action. If you don’t believe in something, I can guarantee you will never enjoy it, much less excel in it. 

Conversely, the absence of belief is almost always one of the factors behind things that fail, whether they be products or careers.

[…]

Because each time our lack of belief or conviction is replaced with someone else’s belief or conviction, we slowly give up the need to believe. I mean, why believe in what you’re going to do if everyone around you already expects you to believe it, right?

This is a guarantee for stagnation and frustration. I’ve seen this play out numerous times, including in my own career.

We followed that up a week later https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/the-third-value/ with “the third value”—a bias for action.

And the most logical outcome for a belief is an action, for me. Actions are where beliefs are put to test.

Don’t like the way your work role is structured? Want to pick up the electric guitar? Fancy your chances as an artist? Not impressed with your Chess.com score? Wonder if that brilliant business concept you came up with is going to succeed? Agonised about the cruelty inherent in raising meat for food?

Do something about it.

Everyone has ideas and beliefs. Only a fraction do anything about them.

This being, well, First Principles, we ended up doing a community survey on what might be the top three values https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/values-for-life/ we desired to live our lives by. Two-hundred and seventy-seven of you took part.

A lot of water has flowed under our respective and collective bridges since then, no? I don’t just mean the two years, but the explosion of AI models and the greed of their makers.

I was reminded of this when I came across a recent interview. Shane Parrish, the founder of the Knowledge Project, interviewed Nicolai Tangen https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zyvuM3J9QqQ&t=560s, CEO of the $2 trillion (yes, that’s trillion) Norwegian Sovereign Wealth Fund. Part of their interesting conversation had this bit where Tangen talks about the human skills that will still hold value in a “post-AI” world.

The ability to talk to people, to listen to them, and to empathise with others.

Because if you’re not curious you’re not going to listen. So I think it goes hand in hand with that. I mean generally you should listen twice as much as you speak. That’s why you got two ears. A lot of people think they’re listening but they’re not actually listening. They’re just processing stuff and can’t wait to get to the next questions themselves.

I spoke to this guy called Saul Pearlmutter, a scientist who got the Nobel Prize in Physics. He said, “Nikolai, we are now at the stage in the world where we, for the first time, can solve all the problems. We  know how to solve the climate problem, we know how to feed everybody in the world, we have the technology, we have the science. It’s just that we don’t talk to each other.

I saw empathy pop up again, this time from Nvidia CEO Jensen Huang https://www.instagram.com/reel/DTxvCsaDZ3F/.

In addition to empathy, Huang talks about the ability to anticipate problems and to be able to “see around corners.”

Seeing “empathy”—a word that’s been derided in recent times by the likes of Elon Musk—appear twice as a valuable skill from two very different people made me think.

Are leaders just talking it up because the rest of humanity desperately wants to hear something, anything, that humans will still be good at?

What else do they talk up?

Taste. Paul Graham https://x.com/paulg/status/2022604692178522562.

“Pursuit of mastery” and a “sense of purpose and meaning”. Brené Brown https://fortune.com/2025/10/29/author-brene-brown-human-skills-ai-era-leadership-next/.

Sacrifice. Again, Jensen Huang https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YNshj2oOr3E.

It’s hard to take these at face value, because it’s also hard to see how having them leads to great professional careers for someone today.

Perhaps we should, as a community, try to arrive at our own answers. What skills do we believe will be valuable in a post-AI world?

Remember, LLMs (the current underpinning of AI) are trained on human knowledge. Which is why they can write code, find patterns, search for information, generate essays, reproduce art and even mimic conversations so well.

This week, we talk about “The People.”

***

Here’s a test. Close your eyes and think of the last film that really got you.

I’d bet you’re not remembering the edit. You’re not thinking about the production design or what the sound mix sounded like. You’re thinking about a face. A voice. A moment when an actor looked at another actor, or perhaps at nothing, and something in you shifted.

Reaction videos on Youtube are my guilty pleasure watches (no, not all of them, I have my go-tos), and one of my favourites is “Cinema Therapy,” where two friends—a licensed therapist and a filmmaker—watch movies and give their takes.

In one of the episodes https://youtu.be/Z3KCVrBhFps?si=khafjRQ6b4ZOAFT5&t=871, while reacting to Robin Williams’ park bench scene, the filmmaker says, “The best special effect in movies is the actor’s face.” What a line!

Acting, when done right, is like a direct line between the film and you. It’s also paradoxically, the hardest thing to judge.

So what does “best” even mean when you’re comparing a woman playing grief in a Norwegian family drama to someone playing a wealthy CEO who is kidnapped because she is taken for an alien?

You can’t. But there has to be a winner. That’s the fun, innit?

So let’s look at this year’s nominees:

(I’ll add my thoughts about the nominations, if any. But as always, all critiques are personal and subjective, please.)

Best Actress in a Leading Role

Nominees (L-R): Jessie Buckley (Hamnet), Rose Byrne (If I Had Legs I’d Kick You), Kate Hudson (Song Sung Blue), Renate Reinsve (Sentimental Value), Emma Stone (Bugonia)

The Best Actress category has always had more personality, more history, more drama per square inch than any other.

In 1985, in what is now an iconic Oscars moment https://youtu.be/u_8nAvU0T5Y?si=7PytfEUxAw9QnKuz, when Sally Field won this category for Places in the Heart, she walked up and said: “I haven’t had an orthodox career, and I’ve wanted more than anything to have your respect. The first time, I didn’t feel it, but this time I feel it. And I can’t deny the fact that you like me. Right now, you like me!”

This went on to become one of the most parodied https://www.vanityfair.com/hollywood/2020/01/sally-field-you-like-me-oscars Oscar moments—with even Field herself joining in on the fun. But it’s the rendition by Jim Carrey (in The Mask) that takes the cake for me.

Sorry, I digressed. 

Back to this year’s nominees:

Jessie Buckley—Hamnet. The frontrunner, and one of those actors who makes you forget you’re watching acting, which is the highest compliment I know how to give.

Emma Stone—Bugonia. Okay, here’s a wild stat: Emma Stone is now a five-time Oscar nominee, and three of them are for Yorgos Lanthimos films. Lucky charm much?

Rose Byrne—If I Had Legs, I’d Kick You. First nomination. An A24 film about a mother in crisis. Byrne has been criminally underrated for years. She’s been excellent in everything from Bridesmaids to Platonic, and the Academy finally looked up.

Renate Reinsve—Sentimental Value. She broke out with The Worst Person in the World (which I loved!)  in 2021. Now she’s back with the same director, Joachim Trier. Four years from breakthrough to Oscar nominee. That’s a trajectory!

Kate Hudson—Song Sung Blue. Her last nomination was for Almost Famous, 25 years ago!

Who’s your pick? Here’s the voting link https://theken.typeform.com/to/VN5ffFCi for your picks for all categories in this week’s Parallel Oscars.

Takes 90 seconds, covers all four categories.

Okay, moving on.

Best Actor in a Leading Role

Nominees (L-R): Timothée Chalamet (Marty Supreme), Leonardo DiCaprio (One Battle after Another), Michael B. Jordan (Sinners), Ethan Hawke (Blue Moon), Wagner Moura (The Secret Agent)

The most fun race this year.

One thing I’ve always noticed about this category is that it almost always goes to the one who looked at the script and thought, “Okay, what if I went completely insane with this?”

It’s probably why Daniel Day-Lewis is a three-time winner. It’s why Matthew McConaughey starved himself for Dallas Buyers Club. It’s also probably why it took Leonardo DiCaprio six nominations and getting mauled by a CGI bear in The Revenant to finally win.

The Academy was waiting for him to suffer enough 🙂 

Which brings us to this year: Timothée Chalamet for Marty Supreme, Leonardo DiCaprio for One Battle after Another, Michael B. Jordan (and Michael A. Jordan) for Sinners, Ethan Hawke for Blue Moon, and Wagner Moura for The Secret Agent.

Honestly, it’s a packed lineup. And there are a lot of stories here: Michael B. Jordan’s first-ever nomination, Leonardo DiCaprio in a PTA film, Hawke’s quieter but devastating performance, and Wagner Moura marking Brazil’s entry into the Best Actor conversation.

But the story I can’t stop thinking about is Chalamet’s.

At 30, he is the youngest actor to earn three acting nominations since Marlon Brando (which is an insane stat). That too, for acting in an objectively least cinematic sport imaginable. When it’s time, it’s time, I guess.

Best Supporting Actress

Nominees (L-R): Elle Fanning (Sentimental Value), Inga Ibsdotter Lilleaas (Sentimental Value), Amy Madigan (Weapons), Wunmi Mosaku (Sinners), Teyana Taylor (One Battle after Another)

Here’s another piece of Oscar trivia for you:

When the Supporting categories were introduced in 1936, the winners didn’t even get an Oscar statuette. They got a plaque.

A plaque! Like a participation award at a school sports day.

It took until 1943 for the Academy to upgrade them to the same trophy as everyone else. It’s a small thing, but it tells you something about how the industry used to think about supporting work.

But since then, the category has spent the last 80 years proving that wrong. Ingrid Bergman, Robert De Niro, Christoph Waltz, Heath Ledger, Viola Davis—some of the most iconic performances in cinema history have come from people technically playing second fiddle.

This year is no exception, especially for Amy Madigan.

Her first nomination was in 1985, for Twice in a Lifetime. Her second nomination is this year, 40 years later, at 74 years old.

The longest gap between nominations for an actress in Oscar history, and if Madigan wins, it will be the most earned standing ovation of the night.

Actor in a Supporting Role

Nominees (L-R): Benicio Del Toro (One Battle after Another), Jacob Elordi (Frankenstein), Delroy Lindo (Sinners), Sean Penn (One Battle after Another), Stellan Skarsgård (Sentimental Value)

Like all the other categories, this one too is rich with stories: Sean Penn and Benicio Del Toro ‘battling’ it out for the same movie, Delroy Lindo’s 50-year career getting his first nomination at 74, Skarsgård’s 50-year wait for an Oscar nomination, Jacob Elordi’s first nomination at just 28 years old!

But the stat that really caught my attention is that across the 20 acting nominees this year, 11 are receiving their first-ever Oscar nomination. That’s more than half.

Looks like it’s gonna be the year of the firsts and careers being made.

3. Stories from the land of sports

Hello Ishan! It’s Tanim here.

Last week, I got to share an engaging interview https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/a-veteran-sports-journalist-on-playing-the-long-game/ with Sundeep Misra, who spoke about the status of sports literature in India. 

To follow up on that, we have community recommendations of books related to sports. 

Meanwhile, in case you missed, the International Booker Prize Longlist 2026 https://thebookerprizes.com/the-booker-library/prize-years/international/2026 has been announced. Is there anything you have read from the list that you would like to recommend to the community? Drop in your suggestions here https://theken.typeform.com/FPBooks?typeform-source=the-ken.com. 

Community recommendations:

Kirti Manian who is a mom, runner, and climate advocate, shares her thoughts on Driven by Susie Wolff:

“Driven is a first hand account of a woman making it in the extremely male-dominated F1 world. Susie is absolutely iconic and her book really illustrates the struggles and what she put herself through to get to the pinnacle of racing aka F1. Doesn’t hurt that she is married to Toto Wolff, Principal of Mercedes but that happens in a much later stage of her life. Read the book from start to finish in one go. I would recommend it especially if you are into F1 racing and understand the nuances.”

Photo from Kirti Manian

Sagar Keswani, who is learning how to play tennis, has an apt book to share with us: The Inner Game of Tennis by W. Timothy Gallwey:

“It’s an incredible read for anyone trying to learn the art of learning. FP readers I assume are a bunch of learn-it-alls. Through the medium of the painful journey of learning tennis, this book simplifies the explanation of how our minds are in constant dichotomy, and what we can do to bring the two inner selves in us in harmony to unlock a natural learning experience. I feel I am not doing justice to the book with this explanation but it is indeed a complex topic which the book has been able to simplify.”

Raghav S, who loves reading biographies, has one to recommend too—Searching for Novak: Unveiling the Man Behind the Enigma by Mark Hodgkinson

“The book breaks down what first principles define a world champion like Novak. We’ve read a lot about Novak’s antics on and off the court. It’s more about what couldn’t stop a kid from war-torn Serbia from conquering the courts and winning the highest number of grand slams ever. It’s not about supporting someone who didn’t take vaccines, but it is about how one fought the mental battles to come out of a prison-like situation during the Australian Open. Every biography has its pros and cons, but it’s about what we take out of it.”

Harsh Mer thinks that The Winning Way by Anita Bhogle and Harsha Bhogle is a good read if you are interested in life lessons from sportspersons and how they orient their mind towards achieving their goals.

Photo from Harsh Mer

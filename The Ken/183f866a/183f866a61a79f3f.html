Much like fintechs, real-money-gaming (RMG) companies are getting a taste of what it's like to catch the government’s attention.
 
The biggest afterclap came last month, when India's tax body served one of the largest tax-evasion notices, amounting to $2.6 billion, to Gamekraft. It was nothing like these companies had seen before.
 
The four-year-old firm is a leading RMG company and among the most-profitable startups in the country. RMG products, like fantasy sports and online card games, are wildly popular in India: operators like Gameskraft, MPL, Dream11, and Games24x7 claim user bases running in the tens of millions. 
 
These companies rake in hundreds of crores in profits each year, something that even prominent startups, such as edtech giant Byju’s and food-delivery firm Swiggy, have not achieved so far.
 
So when faced with a notice that was nearly 11 times its cumulative revenue, Gameskraft did what any company would do. It tried to rally support from its peers to fight the government's tax department. But help did not come immediately. 
 
In fact, for a while now, the RMG companies have been at odds with each other. It made business sense to be on opposing sides. Even the industry groups meant to help these companies have been divided, with all the big firms part of different groups. 
 
Soumyajit, the author of today's story, looks at what kept companies in this money-spinning industry at each other's throats, and if the tax notice is bound to change all of that.

Alternative investment funds, or AIFs, may be quite exclusive in a lower-middle-income country like India. But even with a minimum-investment requirement of Rs 1 crore, AIFs have grown tenfold in number in as many years, to over 1,740.

The committed capital in these funds has risen over 50X, to Rs 15 lakh crore, in the same period.

AIFs, especially the kind that can invest across listed and unlisted companies, real estate, and private credit, have been catnip to India’s wealthy in recent years. Even if such funds, which charge a steep fee, have had their fair share of detractors, owing to returns that are hardly eye-popping.

So what makes the well-heeled so giddy, especially when a series of unrelated developments, including new norms by the banking and markets regulators, have begun to chip away at the allure of AIFs?

Aakriti Bhalla spoke with 15 fund managers, family offices, and wealth managers, among others, to piece together her story today.

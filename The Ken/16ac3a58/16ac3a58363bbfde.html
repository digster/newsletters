Good Morning Ishan,
 
A few minutes into my first day at The Ken, my colleague Ruhi swivelled around in her seat and very matter-of-factly told me I should invest in headphones. Noise cancelling ones. Almost as if to prove their effectiveness, she put her headphones on and proceeded to ignore my question—Why?

The reason soon became obvious. Our workplace at the time—a co-working arrangement in south Delhi—would give your average Sunday market a serious complex. Ruhi even linked me to her preferred brands of headphones, but between distraction and self-inflicted poverty, I chose the former.

Not for very long though.

Eventually, I bit the bullet and ponied up for headphones. Not a fancy JBL, Bose or Sennheiser, mind you. My middle class moorings would sooner see me lose my hearing. 
 
Instead, I opted for one of the mid-range brands. Cheap enough for me to still eat three square meals a day, but costly enough to convince me they wouldn't explode. I could care less about its aesthetic, and if you've met me, you probably already know that. But many other equally price-conscious customers do. And they form the market that boAt, the protagonist of today's story, has made its money from.
 
Seemingly from out of nowhere, boAt has taken the Indian audio accessories market by storm. In the financial year ended March 2018, it earned more than German audio stalwart Blaupunkt. It sponsors three major Indian Premier League franchises, including the Chennai Super Kings and Mumbai Indians—this year’s finalists. It has cricketers, actors and singers as brand ambassadors. And claims to sell up to 6,000 units daily.
 
Oh, and did I mention it’s scarcely three years old?

BoAt is an underdog story. But underdog stories are usually short-lived, one-off successes. BoAt plans to keep its explosive growth up. In today’s story, Vandana delves into how boAt got here, and how it intends to thrive in the face of cut-throat competition, both from legacy brands and ambitious Chinese competitors.

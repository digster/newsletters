Good Morning Ishan,
 
If you were a small business in search of a loan, which door would you knock on first? Banks won't lend to small fry like you. You could always try a top-tier non-banking financial company (NBFC). But squeezing a disbursement out of them is another matter.
 
There’s another option in between the two though—the business-to-business (B2B) lending platform, a lending instrument that slides perfectly into this gaping hole. There are, after all, over 300,000 SMEs with financing needs.
 
OfBusiness knew this quite early on. And so the B2B raw material procurement platform, today valued at $5 billion, spun out an NBFC called Oxyzo in 2017. The company quadrupled the size of its loan book in three years and ended up raising an impressive $200 million, becoming a unicorn in the process.
 
In fact, Oxyzo became only the fifth subsidiary of an Indian unicorn to earn that tag for itself, sitting at the table with the likes of electric two-wheeler maker Ola Electric and lock-screen platform Glance.
 
But Oxyzo will be damned if it's just a plain-vanilla NBFC with VC funding.
 
So far, it has lent on the straight-but-narrow lending path, given its NBFC origins. And while Oxyzo might have leaned heavily on companies in OfB’s catchment—paying over 20,000 suppliers on behalf of 2,500+ SMEs—it’s keen on carving its own path as a go-to SME lender for a wider variety of financing needs. This includes companies with warehousing needs, EV companies, and even direct-to-consumer brands.
 
Oxyzo has a head start because it can basically access cheaper capital than its rivals. That's a boon as it steps into the chaotic and crowded world of B2B financing, with formidable rivals like Capital Float and CredAvenue. Seetharaman's story today delves deep into this question: is the low cost of capital enough to carve a unique niche for Oxyzo? Oxyzo is definitely counting on it to execute a perfect Swiss-knife strategy.

Even before the world woke up to AI’s insatiable electricity needs, India was adding renewable energy capacity at a fast clip. Now, it’s aggressively building standalone battery storage systems, relaxing rules for states to approve projects and fund them. 

The result is a mad rush to lower the tariffs. 

This movie has played before—in solar and wind energy, where India checked the box on rapid installation but failed on building local manufacturing. But no one is watching the rerun. 

Last week, Rajasthan awarded contracts for standalone battery energy storage systems through a tender process that set a new “lowest tariff” record at Rs 1.77 lakh per megawatt (MW) per month. That’s 15% lower than what the state awarded a year ago. Earlier, Andhra Pradesh had set the previous record at Rs 2.08 lakh per MW per month. After the most recent award, the Rajasthan government claimed that it was a “milestone” that reflects its “robust policy ecosystem and proactive facilitation”. 

The Ken has learnt that Maharashtra is sending out feelers to developers for an upcoming battery energy storage tender, and indicating that it expects lower tariffs than Rajasthan’s. 

What was meant to lead to price discovery and spur innovation has led to market distortion. 

In Rajasthan, say industry insiders, there were 50 bidders. This kind of interest, from anyone and everyone, has not been seen in renewable energy before. Eleven bidders eventually won the tender, but it’s evident this could become a race to the bottom.

Relying on China, and spreadsheets

“You know it’s a problem when state governments try to get in a bidding process,” said a senior executive from the energy storage industry, referring to how states are using the flexibility in viability gap funding to influence pricing. 

Firstly, the tender document is designed to encourage new players. And understandably so, because it’s a new sector and more competition is welcome. Secondly, the construct of standalone projects is such that anyone who can muster up some capital can become a battery storage builder. 

“Due to the viability gap funding scheme, the government funds 16% of your project cost. You are given land for free, you are given the power to charge your batteries, you have guaranteed offtake, and it’s a 12-year contract. So, it’s almost a risk-free business,” the executive noted. Bidders know that they just need to keep the asset at the distributor companies’ premises for 12 years, after which they can take it back. 

“The story is simple, and it offers a 11–13% internal rate of return (IRR),” said the executive, who has no competing interest in these projects.

But there’s a reason established players such as Renew, Serentica, Avaada, JSW, and Indigrid have stayed out of these tenders. The newcomers are “forward bidding” in the hope that lithium prices will continue to fall. They are also quoting such low prices because buying full battery systems from China is cheaper than undertaking any local manufacturing.

“It can be 25-30% cheaper,” said a power producing company’s chief executive. “Importantly, many are going to tier-2 or tier-3 suppliers in China for their batteries, to players who haven’t proven their product under Indian conditions.”

India and China are shaking hands now after a few tumultuous geopolitical years, but the supply chain risk isn’t mitigated. 

“When you bring in batteries from China, everything is just on a spreadsheet; nothing is proven on the ground [here]. How those batteries will perform in the high temperatures of Rajasthan or Gujarat is not known,” said the above executive. He and many others are also concerned that new or aspiring battery storage builders rely heavily on Chinese manpower to install these batteries in India.

“We have enough manpower working on excel sheets and presentations. But we don’t have skilled manpower to install and operate these batteries on the ground. It is scary that we are relying on external manpower for such high-voltage batteries.”

Chief executive of a power producer

In solar and wind energy too, Indian power producers and contractors relied on Chinese imports to bid and win tenders. Over the past few years, India’s solar-module manufacturing ecosystem has expanded significantly, even though the cells mostly come from China. Incidentally, defect rates in Indian solar modules—at 22%—are among the highest globally. 

To correct the skew in cell manufacturing, the government said in September that it would procure power from projects only if they use solar cells from approved domestic manufacturers. 

Ironically, none of the solar or wind turbine lessons are translating into better project designs for battery storage. What’s worse is that battery storage guidelines from 2020-21 are being copy-pasted to invite tenders in 2025, when the technology has advanced to the next generation. 

“We are devising power purchase agreements for 12–15 years. This is based on 2020 guidelines, when the battery life was around 10 years; today, it is 20 years. The cell capacity then was ~300 milli amps, today, it is nearly 1,000 milli amps. Why are we not taking these into account?” asked the first executive quoted above

The short answer is lazy, blinkered policymaking. 

Standalone battery storage is the future of agile energy supply, and governments can fix the lacunae whenever they find them. 

“I am all for promoting new players, but it’s necessary to put entry barriers so that bidders bring minimum value to the table for executing these projects,” said Debmalya Sen, president of the Indian Energy Storage Alliance (IESA). “You need some kind of construction background, if not power sector experience, to be able to deliver well.” 

Secondly, he added, India needs to safeguard supply. “We do not have cell manufacturing capacity today, but at least we can ensure that bidders do not bring in the finished product from China. Because that way, you are not developing a complete value chain locally.” 

India is already in a ditch vis-à-vis clean energy supply-chain security. It should stop digging further.

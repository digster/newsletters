Good Morning Ishan,

I’m so glad to be back in your inbox again! Did you miss me?

I spent the last month in various parts of north India, drinking many cups of chai, and wielding my Google Pay like a superpower. It was good to be back in the country!

It wasn’t hard to keep an eye on what’s happening in Indian edtech, even on my break. Most newsrooms now seem to have what I would call a dedicated Byju’s breaking news reporter, whose diligent reporting has exposed the absolute operations bedlam within the company. If you’d like to learn more about that, join me and the hosts of The Ken’s excellent podcast Daybreak next week as we talk about the ups and downs of the once celebrated edtech.

In this edition, I thought I’d alert you to a new development from the dynamic world that creates and confers “alt-MBAs” to young people in search of jobs, degrees, and an IIM-like stamp on their resume. 

There are a number of shops now that offer a chance at this learn-work-network opportunity, packaged into a few months rather than the years-long traditional MBA model. One of those to make a big splash, specifically, has been Stoa School, which recently made a hard pivot from being the next-best-thing-to-IIMs to an AI-driven enterprise platform called Zeus (pivoting hasn’t dampened any ambitions, if we are to pay any heed to the new name). 

The other big fish was, of course, Masters’ Union (MU). 

Started by Pratham Mittal, MU was supposed to be the Gen Z alternative to the Indian School of Business. The school claims to pair hands-on experience with expert mentor advice and, most crucially, aims to place its graduates at a faster and more reliable pace than the traditional MBA schools, which have struggled in the recent past to hit their placement targets. 

For a while, things seemed to be rosy for MU. They even sent out a marketing email in December 2021 about how they had beaten the IIMs and ISB at their own game—MU’s highest placement offer, at approximately Rs 30 lakh (US$35,700) per annum, was higher than what the others had managed. We’ve already written about MU’s decision to be transparent with their placement report.

The candour, of course, worked when there was good news to report. But as my colleague Alifiya wrote this July, MU was struggling to place over half its cohort this year. If the value of alt-MBAs, like traditional MBAs, comes down to placements, then MU’s bet on a Gen Z version of higher education isn’t paying off.

But it seems that doesn’t mean the business model isn’t worth trying—just that it needed a different, more international context.

Enter Tetr

Look. I love new-age, two-syllable start-up names just as much as any other wannabe millennial. But the Tetr College of Business isn’t a completely unfamiliar entity. Let me explain.

I was scrolling through my inbox once I got back to the US when I chanced upon a marketing email from Tetr. The email talked about how this business school was the “first of its kind”, and how it was going to roll out an “educational journey” across 45 countries, including the US, UK, Japan, and even Argentina. The kick-off for the inaugural program would be in Dubai.

I’d almost scrolled past the email before I truly registered its first line. Tetr was founded by a familiar name. Could it, should it, dare it be the same Pratham Mittal?

This venture doesn’t seem like something Mittal’s just lent his name to. Across Tetr’s website, Mittal is fronting the conversations and messaging about what Tetr’s goals are. Which, by the looks of it, seems like the international version of what he’s trying with MU. 

Except, there’s no obvious mention of MU anywhere except for Mittal’s one-line bio. One would assume that the alt-MBA schools would stick together, grow side-by-side. Heck, even trade students for a semester or two. For now, though, Mittal seems to have decided to keep these two ventures separate.

Tetr, on its own, has an impressive opening cast of advisors and educators from the likes of Harvard, Cornell, Stanford, and MIT. A brand-new school, understandably, needs to draw on established names. Tetr also has a unique pedagogical model, which seems like a lot of international executive business courses stitched together in one course. The first course on offer is ‘Bachelor’s in Management and Technology’, spread out over four years and seven countries. The syllabus is purely based on what the founders are calling “experiential learning” and consists of a set of project modules—from creating a dropshipping business in Dubai to piloting a NGO in Ghana—that ratchet up to an internship in the last semester.

With Tetr, Mittal’s target is even younger students than the MU crowd; the eligibility criteria to apply to Tetr’s course is just Grade 12. 

Maybe it’s time for an alt-bachelor’s degree, and creating an international bachelor’s degree means that Mittal may not have to jump through all the accreditation hoops that a UGC would insist on in India. Currently, Tetr has partnered with the United Business Institutes, or UBI Business School, which in turn is associated with Middlesex University London in the UK. Students who are enrolled now in Tetr’s first batch will have to see who finally stamps their degrees.

It’s also an interesting way to get out of the chicken-and-egg placements issue that most new-age education businesses run up against. If the degree is diffused over so many courses, experiences, and mentors, who do you ultimately hold responsible for your level of employability when you graduate? Is it Tetr? Or Middlesex University? All this to say, Mittal has a different set of stakes with Tetr than with MU.

In the future, MU might tag-team with Tetr to take its graduates in as MBA aspirants. Maybe the job market should get ready for freshers honed purely on experiential learning, and much less on rote-learning. This could be the high-temple of higher education. Or it could surface problems no one’s thought of yet.

Exciting times.

Cheers!

Good Morning Ishan,

You are reading our subscriber-only newsletter Ed Set Go—a weekly commentary on Indian edtech and education

I don’t know what you’ve heard, but in the latest round of cuts, President Donald Trump’s administration has defunded and almost unstaffed the Department of Education in the United States. The move will leave public schools poorer—in terms of resources, vital data, and protections against the protracted culture wars being waged around them.

The move has, however, opened channels for education businesses to think about opportunities now open to them if more money flows into independent or private schools, which is a stated policy objective for the Trump administration. Private schools aren’t subject to the same checks and balances as public schools, and while they track student scores, they don’t have to provide the same level of transparency in terms of how they run and who they enroll.

Edtechs could easily wedge their way into this ecosystem, especially if these schools decide to provide at least part of their instruction online.

This potential shift got me thinking about school-focused edtech in India, which doesn’t have the star appeal of famous tutors or marathon Youtube prep sessions, but has chipped away at making schools in India more efficient and effective. From the curriculum to the tech to study it on, scheduling and training teachers, and keeping track of outcome date–these edtechs have tried to modernise Indian schooling.

The challenge is that school-based edtechs can’t focus on the same metrics as test-prep players in the space. The latter can flaunt enrollment rates, online reach, immediate validation through NEET or JEE results. The turnaround time for impact is quick.

When dealing with schools, though, test results can take a long time to come. Teacher effectiveness can only be measured over years. A school’s data tracking may work better because of you. But how do you translate that into VC speak for results?

LEAD has been straddling that space for some time now. Sumeet Mehta, LEAD’s co-founder, has been a strong advocate for edtech in schools, rather than after or outside it. His simple rationale is that most learning happens in the 6–7 hours kids spend in school. So why should edtech innovation happen outside it?

So, LEAD has taken the slower route of creating curriculum and training teachers, but they’ve also acquired and raised funds like any other edtech in the space. LEAD’s acquisition of Pearson India’s school business in 2023 also expanded their scope and access to a more premium consumer base.

Mehta has said he’d like to take LEAD to over 12,000 schools in the next few years. The tally currently stands at 9,000, a drop in the ocean of the approximately 1.5 million government, private, and affordable private schools in the country.

Mehta has an interesting approach to growth, which struck me as the antithesis of the monopolistic, competition-killing type thinking that some other edtechs have espoused.

The need to adapt to a fractured ecosystem

“There is no doubt in my mind that if we have to transform, we need three to four players,” Mehta told Yourstory in an interview about a month ago.

“Why don’t we try a public-private partnership like partnering with LEAD to manage 500 schools. The government spends between Rs 30,000-50,000 per student annually. I am saying you give me two-thirds of it–Rs 20,000. I’ll definitely be able to deliver 2X outcomes. It’s a very good ROI.”

Throughout the interview, Mehta advocates carving up the vast territory of Indian schooling through “public-private partnerships”: the government spending a part of its money on private players to improve services in schools. 

His test-case scenario is that the top 10 school-edtech players get 500 schools each, and once they show results, expand to 10,000 schools each. He also advocates for a penalty if the provider doesn’t meet the benchmarks for success. Mehta says a group like DAV could take up these solutions and spread them through their network. Or a competitor like SchoolNet, a much older brand in the school enrichment space, which predates the edtech boom.

Mehta also has ideas on how to situate this model within the current, complex network of schools in India:

“To not cause any concerns for government teachers, start with a 100-school public-private partnership model under a distinct name, similar to Kendriya Vidyalayas or Jawahar Navodaya Vidyalayas. Call it a model school programme. There are 100,000 schools with fewer than 50 students. Select some of these schools and have private players adopt the buildings and run them. The private partner would be required to allocate 25% to 50% of seats, depending on the economics, for government-quota students, while managing the rest independently.”

He thinks this model should spread slowly, and that school-based edtechs shouldn’t be in a hurry to show test-prep-like metrics. Test-prep companies like Physics Wallah have also partnered with schools, but it’s more to push their own pipeline of test-prep solutions into a school-based setting.

Mehta’s recommendation is a model that goes much deeper in terms of engagement and solutions.

It’s a noble thought, especially if school edtechs can bring more equitable access to technology for students from low-income households. It’s one thing to watch a lecture on Youtube, it’s quite another to learn the skills needed to integrate technology in everyday learning, or teaching.

Yet, there’s one aspect of this model that’s unclear to me. Test-prep solutions, ultimately, can’t escape the glare of a national result. If you’re “good”, it means your graduates have secured all All-India Rank or breached an equivalent score ceiling. (Just to be clear, I’m not saying that test-scores are the only way to mark academic achievement. It’s the market.)

But to understand if school edtech is delivering the solutions they promise, what are the benchmarks? Are we doing controlled experiments on student scores, where one set goes to a school supported by an edtech and the other doesn’t? Do we track how long students spend on their homework? Or should the metric be the effectiveness of teachers, pre- and post-training?

Also, who sets these benchmarks? The school? The edtechs? Or the government?

There’s still only one way to judge how well a school has performed at scale, and that’s through exam scores. Many of our parents picked schools because of how well their students did in Class 12 board exams. 

Maybe school edtechs can create their own metrics, as they’ve created their own markets. The challenge will be to get an universal nod on them, especially from the government.

Driver: Drop location?
Customer: [names a place]
Driver: Oh super.
Customer: Are you coming?
Driver: No.
This is an actual chat between an auto-rickshaw driver and a customer on a ride-hailing app in India a few days ago. I saw it on social media, where the customer had posted a screenshot of the conversation and saw the funny side of the driver's savage response. And it's not hard to believe because such denials are pretty common in the big cities, where traffic dictates where the drivers want to go.
But as India tries to cope with the second wave of Covid-19, customers have no choice but to endure such cancellations and pray that they find an accommodating auto driver. With fewer physical touchpoints and better air circulation than cabs, autos are perceived as a comparatively safer way to get around.
Autos have become the prime battleground for the two ride-hailing giants in India—Ola and Uber. This is ironic because, when the two companies came to India a decade ago, their air-conditioned cabs offered a means to escape the heat, dust, and bumpy rides that autos brought with them.
Now, though, in a world with a pandemic, everyone's scrambling to onboard more autos. Ola, Uber, and even bike-taxi player Rapido, which quietly launched its three-wheeler service in October 2020. And there's enough room for everyone to grow.
According to the chief of Ola and Uber cab drivers association of the southern Indian state of Karnataka, the two companies have so far registered only 60,000 autos out of around 150,000 running in Bengaluru. Rapido has onboarded more than 100,000 drivers in 25 cities and aims to add up to half a million drivers in the next six months.
Ola has the head-start, having started its auto service in 2014, while Uber properly got into the game only four years later. The Indian ride-hailing unicorn even has a superior algorithm to match drivers with customers. That's not to say Uber or Rapido are out of the race.

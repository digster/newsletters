Good Morning Ishan,
 
I remember that people at Housing had no clue about Jason Kothari. Come on! Don’t tell me you’ve already forgotten Housing. I haven’t. It is clear as a bright, sunny day in my head. SoftBank invests $100 million in a young, online real estate company in India. The young co-founder Rahul Yadav blows up, fights ensue between the investors and the co-founder, Yadav gets thrown out, Jason Kothari comes in as SoftBank’s man, fires 80% of the company, shuts everything down and Housing is then sold to PropTiger, the News Corp backed online real estate company.
 
It is true now as it was true then, who in the whole wide world is Jason Kothari? What’s with his whole brooding front? Is his special skill firing people and shutting things down? Housing was a startup for all reasons, what’s with Jason staying at a five star hotel throughout? People talk...
 
...You know what, I am getting ahead of myself. Let me continue with my story.
 
So, Jason didn’t disappear after Housing. I thought he would. Because Housing wasn’t a turnaround; it was a clean up operation. And I’ve always believed that any industry places a huge premium on a professional who can turnaround things. People who do clean ups build a certain reputation and they don’t go very far. Plus, there are professional agencies to do this job. Clean ups are simple. Being a person who everybody hates because you’ve taken away their job is simple. Remember, my comparison is between turnaround and cleanup.
 
Of course, Jason didn’t disappear. He joined as CEO of Freecharge, a subsidiary of Snapdeal, also a company with SoftBank as a majority shareholder. You’ve read our stories on Freecharge and also Snapdeal. So you know what happened there. Jason comes in, Freecharge gets sold to Axis Bank. Jason doesn’t disappear still. Next, he joins Snapdeal. As chief strategy and investment officer. Well, that looks important. What’s also important are these two questions: who is Jason Kothari? And what does he do?

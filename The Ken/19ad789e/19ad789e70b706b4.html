When business leaders don’t work, they do the next best thing—talk about the number of hours they work, or the number of hours others should be working.

And why not? Working hours are a barometer for productivity and growth. But how many hours make for “productive” hours, and how much over that is “counterproductive”, has been left in the hands of policymakers.

The new labour codes, announced last week, simplify India’s labyrinthine labour laws and break them up into four snackable codes around wages, social security, workplace conditions, and industrial relations.

And in the codes on workplace conditions, the labour gods imposed an eight-hour limit a day or a 48-hour limit a week. Even though this was the central government’s decision, it may not have been thrilled about it.

Its real feelings about working hours were on display in the 2024-25 Economic Survey, where it said working-hour restrictions hamper economic growth.

“There are instances where labour regulations designed to protect workers’ rights perhaps inadvertently hinder the growth of firms, especially small and medium enterprises, by restricting their ability to expand operations when needed compared to global peers. In doing so, it dampens employment generation as well,” the survey said.

But for now, it has decided to toe the line of having defined working hours. And if employees consent to working anything over those hours, companies have to pay an overtime premium of twice the wage.

When it comes to overtime hours, the new code leaves a tiny window open for companies.

In the older Factories Act, overtime was capped at 75 hours a quarter. But the new set of labour codes, codified back in 2020, removed uniform caps, leaving it to states to decide on them.

Since then, seven states—Maharashtra, Haryana, Himachal Pradesh, Odisha, Punjab, Karnataka, and Uttar Pradesh—have already increased the ceiling of overtime hours up to 144 hours in any quarter, potentially increasing earnings for employees in those states.

While the government has been liberal with overtime hours, what needed looking into was the overtime rates. Because the high rates may deter employers from asking employees to work overtime. This will eventually end up hitting those who want to work more to earn more.

As the Economic Survey pointed out: “Regulations also hurt workers by discouraging job creation, limiting wages, and encouraging informal employment. For example, Indian workers cannot formally work overtime because the law requires employers to pay at least twice the regular wage. Indian workers accept informal employment to receive overtime pay.”

So, on one hand, high overtime wages are pushing employees towards informal employment. On the other hand, the informal workforce includes gig workers—the likes of food-delivery personnel and rideshare drivers—who work 10–14 hours a day, and are not eligible for any overtime pay.

In India, gig workers are not recognised as employees and are outside the employee-employer relationship. They earn per task, and that flexibility allows them to work as many hours and do as many tasks as they please.

While this is true in principle, some courts around the world are seeing merit in recognising that gig workers are working long hours not out of choice. As a result, they are given “employee” status and the benefits that come with it.

Last week, New Zealand’s top court said Uber drivers will be recognised as employees, following similar rulings in the Netherlands and the UK.

The court in the UK, for instance, found that long hours for gig workers were not so much out of choice, but a function of the system. The UK’s Supreme Court, in its judgment, said:

  • Uber set the fare which meant that they dictated how much drivers could earn
  • Uber set the contract terms and drivers had no say in them
  • Request for rides is constrained by Uber who can penalise drivers if they reject too many rides
  • Uber monitors a driver’s service through the star rating and has the capacity to terminate the relationship if after repeated warnings this does not improve

The court determined that drivers were in a position of subordination to Uber where the only way they could increase their earnings would be to work longer hours.

— Uber drivers are workers not self-employed, Supreme Court rules, BBC

India’s new labour codes don’t go that far, but it has made a start.

The country’s 12 million gig and platform workers are now eligible for social-security benefits like insurance, provident fund, and maternity benefits, it said.

So, what gig workers can use is a little more structure as employees, and employees can use more flexibility like gig workers.

The bottom line is that working hours should be about productivity. In 2023, Indian employees’ hourly output was worth $8.1, about one-tenth of Germany’s, which has similar working hours to India. The difference? It gives long-term flexibility in work hours. That is, if German employees’ daily working time does not exceed eight hours in six months, their workweek can extend to 10 hours a day. Indian employees, meanwhile, are expected to work eight hours a day all through the year.

Now, with many states yet to lay down their implementation of the codes, the one way left to beat the working-hour glitch is to see if states’ own labour codes can undo some of the rigidity of the centre’s codes.

After all, you can only fight code with code.

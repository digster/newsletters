Make India Competitive Again
Mon, 30 Jun 25
What India is doing, will do, and should do—to not just survive but thrive in the chaos unleashed by Trump..
Good Morning Ishan,
Around 2014 and 2015, something magical happened in India’s banking ecosystem. In dusty villages and bustling towns, long queues formed outside bank branches. Farmers, daily-wage workers, homemakers… all lined up with their Aadhaar cards and a hope—of getting their first bank accounts opened.
For some, it was about saving money safely. For others, it was their first brush with a formal financial identity. This was part of the Government of India’s Jan Dhan Yojana Scheme, where millions of zero-balance bank accounts were opened free of cost.
On the very first day, 28 August 2014, 15 million bank accounts were [opened](https://www.downtoearth.org.in/environment/jan-dhan-yojana-how-the-government-started-15-million-bank-accounts-in-one-day-46077). That number rose to 30 million by the end of the month, and by the time 2015 wound to a close, it was an enormous 250 million—10X the entire population of Australia. As of 18 June, the scheme’s dashboard lists over 556 million beneficiaries.
It is a massive project, with a transformative impact on the Indian economy. By enabling direct benefit transfers (DBT), these accounts have allowed government welfare schemes to actually reach their intended targets. MGNREGA wages, LPG subsidies, insurance payouts, pensions—all straight to beneficiary bank accounts without a single sarkari babu taking a cut.
The opening of bank accounts at such a massive scale has also acted as a catalyst for the digitisation of India’s financial economy. A State Bank of India report noted as early as 2021 that India had already [surpassed](https://www.thehindu.com/business/Economy/india-now-ahead-of-china-in-financial-inclusion-metrics/article37387465.ece) China in financial inclusion metrics. Account ownership among adults [more than doubled](https://www.epw.in/engage/article/towards-inclusive-finance-india-comparative) from 35% in 2011 to 77% in 2021. This has played no small part in feeding India’s mobile and internet banking revolution; it’s what has made projects like UPI the successes they are.
But with the Jan Dhan Yojana’s scheme well into its eleventh year, a new challenge is rearing its head.
According to the Reserve Bank of India’s (RBI) [KYC Master Direction, 2016,](https://rbi.org.in/commonman/English/scripts/notification.aspx?id=2607) banks are required to carry out periodic re-verification of KYC (Know Your Customer) information for every “normal-risk” bank account at least once every 10 years. This means millions of Jan Dhan bank accounts are becoming due for re-KYC. (In simple terms, KYC is just the bank’s way of verifying your identity by looking at a certain set of listed documents such as Aadhaar, PAN, etc.)
But reports are already starting to emerge that re-KYC is not going as well as it should be. Many accounts coming up for re-KYC are not passing muster, and so banks are freezing them. Just a few days ago, in Sonbhadra, Uttar Pradesh, an elderly man named Raman [died](https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/opinion/et-commentary/kyc-unlock-kar-diya-jaye-customer-id-verification-framework-is-making-life-difficult-for-the-poor/articleshow/118186588.cms?from=mdr) while standing in a bank queue. According to a local Jan Chowk report, Raman had been trying for several days to complete the KYC formalities required to unfreeze his bank account.
Even the RBI has acknowledged the seriousness of the issue in a recently released [discussion paper](https://www.fidcindia.org.in/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/RBI-DRAFT-UPDATION-OF-KYC-DIRECTIONS-23-05-25.pdf), where it cites a sharp increase in customer complaints and distress arising from re-KYC-related disruptions.
For its part, earlier this month, the regulator tried to address persistent re-KYC failures by introducing amendments to its KYC master directions.
But the recent changes don’t really seem that… transformative.
Especially considering what’s at stake is a critical part of India’s financial inclusion project.

Jan Dhan accounts already start with a handicap—banks have little incentive to keep them up and running.
Typically, the average cost for opening and maintaining a bank account is in the [range](https://www.moneycontrol.com/news/business/eight-years-on-banks-struggle-to-make-jan-dhan-accounts-cost-effective-viable-8621681.html) of Rs 3,200 to Rs 3,500 per year. Most Jan Dhan bank account-holders fall into the lower-income segments of society, and usually maintain very low balances. According to an August 2024 government press release, the [average balance](https://www.pib.gov.in/PressReleasePage.aspx?PRID=2049231) in a Jan Dhan account is approximately Rs 4,000. And nearly 20% of Jan Dhan accounts were [inactive](https://www.businesstoday.in/latest/economy/story/pmjdy-20-of-accounts-inoperative-with-rs-12779-crore-funds-says-mos-finance-410063-2023-12-19) as of the end of 2022, according to a Parliament reply, meaning they had no customer-initiated transaction in the past year.
For banks, these low-balance, low-activity accounts do not generate interest income or fees that can help recover the cost of servicing them. Further, banks are not allowed to charge account-maintenance fees or minimum-balance penalties on Jan Dhan accounts, as they are meant to be zero-balance by design.
Given the demographic profile of Jan Dhan account-holders, the potential for cross-selling higher-margin financial products—such as credit cards, personal loans, insurance policies, or fixed deposits—is also extremely limited. This is one of the key reasons why Jan Dhan accounts are overwhelmingly concentrated in public-sector banks. In fact, only about 3% of Jan Dhan accounts have been [opened with private banks](https://www.business-standard.com/article/finance/private-lenders-contribution-to-jan-dhan-only-3-115011300031_1.html), which largely view them as commercially unviable.
In fact, way back in 2014, then RBI Governor Raghuram Rajan [advocated](https://www.ndtvprofit.com/business/jan-dhan-yojana-a-burden-on-public-sector-banks) for compensation and incentives for banks handling Jan Dhan accounts. However, the government believes that in “public interest”, banks ought to fund these activities themselves.
We have already seen this scenario play out in the case of UPI, where both the [industry](https://www.business-standard.com/finance/news/fintech-industry-seeks-upi-merchant-discount-rate-levy-on-large-sellers-125061701099_1.html) and the [RBI](https://rbidocs.rbi.org.in/rdocs/Publications/PDFs/DPSSDISCUSSIONPAPER5E016622B2D3444A9F294D07234059AA.PDF) have consistently called for a merchant discount rate (MDR) that is actually commercially viable, or compensation for financial institutions currently footing the bill.
The government hasn’t budged, though, and the result has been a deterioration in service quality. In March and April this year, UPI services went [down](https://www.moneycontrol.com/technology/why-was-upi-down-for-a-few-hours-over-the-weekend-article-12995316.html) four times, highlighting the strain on infrastructure due to insufficient financial support.
Thus hobbled, banks really have no reason to go the extra mile to ensure Jan Dhan accounts remain active.
Which brings us to the RBI’s [new amendments to its KYC rules](https://www.cnbctv18.com/business/finance/rbi-new-kyc-rules-extended-timeline-june-2026-fresh-norms-for-banks-and-customers-19620107.htm), which appear largely clarificatory rather than transformative and stop short of delivering the regulatory injection that re-KYC urgently needs.
For instance, one of the four changes proposed allows banks to use business correspondents (BCs) to assist customers with KYC updates. BCs are contractual agents of banks who deliver basic banking services in rural and remote areas, meant to enhance last-mile financial inclusion.
However, there was never any prohibition on using BCs for re-KYC. As per [RBI’s guidelines](https://rbi.org.in/scripts/NotificationUser.aspx?Id=6017&Mode=0), a BC can undertake any activity which is part of the bank’s normal course of business, provided it is conducted outside the bank’s premises or ATMs.
Another change mandates that due notice be given before suspending an account due to KYC non-compliance. This, too, is not a novel requirement—banks were [already](https://www.ikigailaw.com/article/631/kyc-ya-later) issuing such notices. The only real addition is a defined frequency for such communication.
The RBI has attempted to provide immediate relief to account holders by granting a one-year extension for bank accounts classified as “low risk”—one of the three risk categories (low, medium, and high) that banks are required to maintain under KYC norms. However, the likelihood of this benefiting Jan Dhan account-holders remains uncertain.
Most Jan Dhan accounts belong to individuals in the informal sector—daily-wage earners, migrant workers, or agricultural labourers—whose incomes are irregular, undocumented, and marked by limited transactional activity. These accounts typically have low balances and sporadic deposits, making them unlikely to be classified as “low risk” under banks’ risk assessment systems. And even if some Jan Dhan account-holders do benefit from this extension, it merely delays the inevitable disruption.
The RBI needs to reexamine the approach it has chosen and address the actual pain points impacting re-KYC at the ground level.
For instance, it should be working collaboratively with banks to define clear criteria for when a customer can be asked to visit a branch for KYC completion—especially when, in today’s context, digital KYC options are both available and legally acceptable. Currently, the KYC master directions grant broad discretion to the banks in this regard. This discretion, often exercised at the branch-level, not only disrupts the account-holder’s daily life but also delays the process. The problem becomes even more severe when the account-holder is a senior citizen or people belonging to vulnerable sections.
There is also no standardised format for the self-declaration form required for re-KYC. Each bank has developed its own version. In some cases, under the guise of re-KYC, banks are [collecting additional](https://www.moneylife.in/article/kyc-nightmare-how-banks-are-asking-for-rekycs-illegally-and-in-the-process-collecting-additional-data-from-customers/75585.html) data from customers, raising concerns about the potential misuse of such data.
Although the RBI and its Governor have issued [advisories and recommendations](https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/industry/banking/finance/banking/avoid-calling-customers-repeatedly-to-submit-kyc-docs-rbi-governor-sanjay-malhotra-to-banks/articleshow/119125850.cms?from=mdr) on improving the re-KYC process, it is now past time to translate words into action.
Perhaps most importantly, the RBI must set a strong precedent by taking action against banks that violate KYC norms or cause unreasonable inconvenience to customers. After all, it has the full authority to do so under the [Banking Regulation Act, 1949](https://www.indiacode.nic.in/bitstream/123456789/1885/1/A194910.pdf).
Thus far, such actions have only been taken in cases of large-scale systemic failures—such as with [Paytm Payments Bank](https://www.ikigailaw.com/article/610/aftershocks-from-the-paytm-payments-bank-debacle)—but consistent violations at the operational level also deserve regulatory attention.
All of this will go a long way in loosening the stranglehold re-KYC is snowballing into. But that’s only half the Jan Dhan problem. For the other half, the RBI has to work with the government to develop a financially viable model that actually incentivises banks to keep Jan Dhan accounts active.

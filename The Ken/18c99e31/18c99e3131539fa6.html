Good Morning Ishan,

I recently had the jarring taste of being locked out of the system.

Let me be more specific by setting the scene. A new contemporary art museum opened in Shenzhen, with its debut exhibition featuring Chiharu Shiota, a Japanese artist who makes installations using spools of thick yarn. It was worth seeing, I felt, so I made the journey over.

It was a bright and shiny Saturday morning. And I realised I hadn’t been in Shenzhen since the pandemic’s onset—when the city was already under a partial lockdown and felt uncharacteristically dead.

A lot has changed since then. Most cars on the road are now electric or hybrid; the air was cleaner than I had ever experienced there, and things generally moved more smoothly than before.

With real anticipation, I marched up to the museum’s entrance and encountered a couple of smart gate systems. As it turned out, visitors had to register through a Wechat mini program—an embedded application that functions within the only real super-app. Then, they had to scan their ID card to gain entry. It was automated convenience! There was neither a ticket office nor staff on site to help anyone who wasn’t a local. The lone security guard told me to buzz off.

The photograph above shows one of Chiharu Shiota’s artworks at an exhibition, A Tide of Emotions

Feeling defeated, I left for a late, consolatory breakfast, but the first two eateries I walked into only accepted payments through Alipay and Wechat Pay. I don’t use either.

As you can tell, the trip was a bust. But I wasn’t disappointed. If nothing else, it reinforced in me a sense of where things are headed in one of the most technologically advanced countries. It’s still an awkward mix of great infrastructure and sketchy implementation. That can only change when the folks in positions of authority develop a feel for users’ needs, values, and limitations.

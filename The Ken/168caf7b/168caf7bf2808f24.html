Bhavin Turakhia is a xillionaire. As in, calling him a millionaire seems trifling. But he isn’t a billionaire either. So let’s call him a xillionaire.
 
Turakhia is a charmed man. Not yet 40, he’s worth somewhere around $500 million, thanks to two massive hits - a $900-million exit of ad tech company Media.net to Chinese investors in August 2016, and a $160-million exit of web hosting company to a US company in 2014. Both companies were bootstrapped by him and his brother, Divyank Turakhia.
 
A third exit would make it a hat trick for the xillionaire.
 
And what better way to do that than to launch three new companies? Between 2014 and 2015, Turakhia launched an international calling app called Ringo, a digital payments and meal vouchers business called Zeta, and the third, Flock.
 
What was Flock? It was a Slack clone. But one in which Bhavin announced personal investments totalling $45 million, with a goal to turn it to a global leader.
 
Four years later, Slack has started the process for an IPO, albeit a non-conventional one, but one valuing it at a reported $7 billion. Its open source competitor, Mattermost, just raised $20 million in a venture round.
 
And Flock? Well, it's flapping its wings. Perhaps even flailing.
 
What happened? Sumanth digs into Flock’s publicly reported numbers, the brutality of the digital collaboration market it chose to fight in, and the disadvantage of trying to build a SaaS business out of India that isn’t focused on revenue generation.

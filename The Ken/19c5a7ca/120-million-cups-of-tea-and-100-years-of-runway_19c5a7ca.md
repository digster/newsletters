Good Morning Ishan,
There’s a scene in the first season of The Bear that goes like this: Chef Carmy is strung out, yelling orders in the kitchen, his team barely keeping up with what’s going on. His sous chef, Sydney, tries and fails to wrangle everyone who’s supposed to be assembling their establishment’s Italian beef sandwiches or serving cakes. Meanwhile, the whir of a chit printer never stops. Orders are coming in… and coming in… and coming in. A good thing turns into a nightmare. Altogether, it’s [two minutes of television](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=1K3z62yoiOA) that raises your cortisol to traumatic levels if you’ve ever worked in a commercial kitchen.
A version of that happened in real life this week, when orders flooded into takeout beverage shops across China. Alibaba gave away free drink orders to users of its Qwen app, which can do everything you’d expect from an AI assistant. It was the first phase of a campaign to dole out vouchers worth RMB 3 billion, or $435 million, all to give people a reason to download and interact with Qwen—ask the assistant to locate a bubble tea shop, ask it for suggestions, customise your order (sugar and ice levels), and let it handle everything else from there.
The scheme worked: 10 million orders were placed in the first nine hours. Tea shops made so many drinks that they ran out of cups. Food delivery drivers had more requests than they could handle, and Qwen shot to the top of app stores, leaving the offerings of Bytedance, Tencent, Baidu, and others in the dust.
It would be easy to dismiss Alibaba’s move as similar to giving away Perplexity Pro or releasing a free version of ChatGPT, but something else is going on here…

Hi! This is Vidhatri, the producer of Zero Shot, chiming in once again. I have a small observation to make.
Over the last year or so, I have been hearing from lawyer friends about AI within their firms—demos and, in some cases, in-house tools. There was the occasional piece of news about court records being digitised and the judiciary trying to use AI to decrease its workload. Not to forget the stories about model hallucinations and AI-powered fake citations. All in all, AI has arrived in the legal field—but we are not talking about it enough.
We decided to change that in this week’s episode of Zero Shot. Hosts Praveen Gopal Krishnan and Brady Ng were in conversation with Nikhil Narendran, a partner at Trilegal, one of India’s biggest law firms.
Nikhil is part of Trilegal’s telecom, media, and technology (TMT) practice, and is often in rooms where great tech is being developed and deployed. He also heads Trilegal’s digital innovation efforts and is responsible for making sure the firm is as tech-forward as it can be.
In this fascinating conversation, Nikhil took us through Trilegal’s journey of AI adoption. They incubated a legal AI platform called Lucio back in 2024 and have now reached a point where lawyers are vibe coding on Fridays.

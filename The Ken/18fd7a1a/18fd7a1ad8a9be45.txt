Dear Ishan, good morning. Getting older is something we look forward to as kids and then get to ignore as adults. Not forever though.

We now know that even getting older—aging—has facets to it. There’s our chronological age of course, which we calculate by the birthdays we celebrate. But most of us also know there’s biological age—how healthy the cells, bones, muscles, and tissues of our bodies are. You see that mentioned on an increasing number of health check reports as “Actual Age”.

I’d argue that there is also something called mental age, or how old we feel.

I first “felt” middle-aged a few years ago when I turned 45, and it was all of a sudden. After a few years of low-grade shoulder pain that came and went with the intensity of my workouts, things got worse. I suspected a rotator cuff tear and decided to go see an orthopedist instead of a physiotherapist. The doctor recommended I get an MRI.

The MRI report said I had “mild to moderate degenerative arthritis” in my left shoulder’s ACL joint (anterior cruciate ligament), a low-grade “tendinosis” in one of the rotator cuff muscles, and a partial tear in another.

The tear didn’t bother me as much as the word “degenerative arthritis” did. (It was only a year or so later that my physiotherapist told me it was both common as well as the lowest grade of joint and tissue damage).

But on that day, I suddenly felt middle-aged. And mortal.

I won’t lie. It took me the better part of a month to accept the fact that my brain was out of sync with my body. My mental age and biological age met and said hello to each other that day.

Though I’d been injured playing or working out more than a few times, I’d always recovered. I even took recovery for granted.

But it was at 45 that I first felt that a switch had flipped with my body. Thinking of it as a car, I realized I just couldn’t hit the accelerator to the floor when I felt like it. I had to make this “car” last another 40-50 years.

I had to learn how to drive my car in “maintenance mode”, not “push-it-to-the-limits mode”. It was the day the meaning of Fitness changed significantly for me.

The Meaning of Fitness

Fitness means different things to different people. Even for the same person, it means different things at different stages of their life. What does fitness mean for you?

Perhaps you’ve forgotten, or perhaps you never took our Fitness Quest Survey. But that was the very first question we asked. These were the other questions:

1. Has the definition of meaning of fitness changed significantly for you over the years? How would you say it has changed?

2. Most of us try to measure or live up to our own ideals of fitness using some metrics. For instance, resting heart rate, HRV, BMI, or even daily steps. Do you have a favourite? What is your favourite fitness metric? And why?

3. What would a great community-driven *Fitness Quest* be for you? What are we searching for? What answers should we seek?

4. If you had to recommend one fitness habit to the rest of the First Principles community, what would it be? And why?

5. Is there a book that has made a significant impact on how you understand and practice fitness? Why?

6. Would you recommend a product (no subscriptions or services) that you feel has significantly helped you become more fit?

7. If I were to ask you to name a person whose opinion you value when it comes to fitness, or perhaps someone who embodies fitness for you, who would that be? And why?

Three hundred and seventy-nine of you took the survey and the vast majority answered most of those questions. 

Which means I was dealing with a few thousand qualitative responses. My wonderful colleagues Jonathan, Anushka, and Hari helped sort and make sense of the recommendations. I then roped in my colleagues in The Ken’s editorial design team led by Adhithi to help present some of the results in graphical form.

But I was still left with the question of how to present close to a thousand answers in a newsletter! So, I decided to go through each one of them and see what patterns emerged. And they did! And in order to stay true to the community aspect of this Quest, I’ve decided to quote verbatim responses at length. Reading other’s views on fitness in their own words has a raw and authentic quality. I’ve also decided to not carry names next to each comment, because I don’t think it matters. Besides, I’m sure many of you gave made-up names (and that’s okay). 

A “Community Quest”, I realized while parsing all the survey responses and writing today’s newsletter, should be focused on what the community thinks and says. So, don’t expect the typical narrative you’re used to. Instead, grab a cup of coffee or tea, switch on some music, and read on. 

I. “Realization”

Like how my own definition of fitness changed when I was 45 thanks to an MRI scan, so too was it for many of you. You realized what fitness ought to mean in your own ways. “The inflection points that cause us to change how we think about fitness,” as a subscriber pointed out. For instance, something as trivial as climbing stairs.

“Not panting while running up the stairs”
“To not pant when I climb up a flight of stairs”
“Climb up four floors without losing my breath”
“Climb steps and more importantly climb down steps”
“Breathe normally after climbing steep slope”
“Being able to run up a flight of stairs, or jump over a fence easily”

“What percentage of people like us can do household tasks like lifting a gas cylinder, carry their own check-in luggage for 100m, climb 200 stairs without feeling strained, walk two km, say, when there’s a transport strike, etc?” asked a subscriber.

Many of us develop newer ideas for fitness when we’re confronted with children, including our own. 

“To run and swim and jump with my daughter. To get old but never get tired.”
“Being able to lift my five-year-old whenever and trek with him for a couple of hours if I need to.”
“My son can almost catch up with me running”
“Kicking my son’s ass in badminton 10 years from today. Follow that up with 18 rounds of golf the next day without any niggles.”
“Being able to play with my niece, who is a toddler, without wincing every time I have to bend over.”
“Fitness means not running out of breath when carrying my 3-year-old daughter for more than 500 meters.”
“Having the energy to go through the day and still have energy to run around with my 5-year-old.”

Hiking and trekking came up a lot too. I guess it’s one way to get our bodies to tell us if they’re up to it.

“I can hike up the mountain in my village with the least effort.”

“Being able to manage the stretch of walking holiday or a light trek.”
“Ability to explore new places, ability to trek hills!”
“Ability to do things without constraint, eg. Walk 15 kms, climb up a hill etc without having to worry about back ache, leg ache, etc.”
“This could be enduring endless meetings or a hike through the forest on a weekend.”

II. Acceptance

If you found those definitions of fitness interesting, then the way we explained how that has changed over the years is even more revealing. At least it was to me.  

Somewhere, the changes signaled a sort of calm acceptance to me. That we’re now mature enough to accept that fitness isn’t a static concept, but one that evolves with us. 

“Earlier it was to keep my body in shape for others to appreciate, now it is to keep my inner health in shape.”
“Earlier it used to be “break the metal” mentality in the gym. Now it has evolved to taking care of the body.”
“Earlier, it was about a certain body shape or size, but now it is more about how I feel about my body and my mind”
“I earlier used to equate being fit to looking good. These days I’ve realised that it’s more about feeling good.”
“Earlier it was all about being thin and fitting into clothes, now it is about wellness and feeling good in your body”
“From looking good >> feeling good + looking good”
“More focus on wellness and happiness”
“Fitness meant maintaining weight, now it means feeling fit.”
“Initially it used to be about how I look on the outside (eg- toned abs). Now it is about being actually healthy on the inside.”
“Focus only on the outcome, e.g., more muscle, less fat (earlier) vs. Enjoying the process, e.g., finding the fitness class which I like!”
“Went from what others said it should be [slim, athletic] to how I feel it should be [feeling positive about overall well being]”
“Changed from focus on the physical aspects only to fitness as a driver of happiness”
“Earlier it was about how slim you are. Today it is about how you feel every second of the day!”
“It used to be about personal bests and body image. It’s not about joy and functionality and progress.”
“Earlier fitness was working out at the gym and building muscles, while now it’s a general feeling of well-being”
“I don’t necessarily want to look a certain way, but feel a certain way”

We view it more holistically now. 

“Earlier, fitness meant going to the gym. Now it means a combination of mobility, flexibility, strength, balance, and rest”
“As one ages, there is a gradual, but very palpable shift from doing strenuous, strength building or aerobic things like running long distances to more sustainable forms like Yoga and cycling.”
“Initially I thought that people who go to the gym or are in sports are really fit and care about it. But as I have grown up, I realised fitness is not just weight or muscles, but health in general. It’s how you are treating your body like with food, moving around and good sleep.”
“Now it’s more holistic. At a point it meant thin”
“With age one needs to change… for example, instead of jogging for 30 minutes I will walk for an hour”
“Earlier I used to think it was about muscles and biceps, triceps and being buff. Now I don’t see it as that important”
“I used to co-relate weight with fitness and probably also muscle definition. After completing a couple of triathlons, I realise fitness looks different on different people. Though I have lost weight, I am still overweight, but incredibly fit (according to me)”
“I was not into fitness, and now I realise how it’s a way of being—and not something ‘extra’ that one does in life”
“Earlier it was only limited to weight loss and an attractive body. But now, I have understood that it’s important to be healthy from internal.”
“I used to care more about being a certain weight and looking a certain way. That’s changed to being actually healthy, having good heart and liver and kidney health, good skin and bones.”
“Earlier it was about achievement, now I prefer balance and equanimity”
“It was purely about looks when I was younger, but with years it has also become a way to de-stress and get a balance in life”
“Earlier: it meant only physical fitness and exercise. Now: I understand it’s about both mind and body; balance of rest and activity and means full range of movement vs just exercise”
“The type of questions related to fitness have changed—from how to lift to how to meditate, from just gyming to pursuing different forms of fitness”
“At first I thought running a mile is enough but now for me fitness is growth”
“In my teens, I saw fitness as a recreation, a break from the incessant hours of studies. All kinds of sport was my goto. In my 20s, It became a FOMO and very goal oriented thing to be done against all odds. Now in my 30s, I see fitness as something you should get at without thinking, no goals to be chased, it’s a self internal growth.”

Naturally, all of this means that fitness means much more to us today than earlier. 

“It has shifted from fitness being one of the top three priorities in my life while earlier it always took a backseat”

“It’s come to the forefront of my conscious thought”

Many of us now realise that we want to be strong.

III. Exploration (Community)

So what do we want to do about our interest in fitness, as a community? This was the hardest bit. Honestly, I appreciate all the directions here, but don’t have a very real sense of what we should or shouldn’t do. The Ken isn’t a fitness-focused publication, hence whatever efforts we may put in to create a community about fitness, it will be niche and small.

So, I won’t try and steer these answers into promises or solutions just yet. Instead, let’s just read what we want from a community.

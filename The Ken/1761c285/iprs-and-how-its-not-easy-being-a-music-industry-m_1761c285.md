When Pakistani singer Akhtar Chanal Zahri was approached by a certain Dr Israni to sing for a Bollywood film—
Phantom, directed by Kabir Khan, whose blockbusters
[mint money](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bajrangi_Bhaijaan)—he was curious.
His song, Afghan Jalebi,
[one of the two](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=84aQ8hSIcQ0&ab_channel=T-Series) versions in the film, became a massive hit even though not many would remember the film itself. Except Zahri was not paid his due.
He was told he's recording a "
[trial version](https://tribune.com.pk/story/1026293/akhtar-chanal-zahri-claims-payment-for-afghan-jalebi)" and was paid Rs 15,000 ($202) for a trip to Lahore.
Zahri's story got publicity because he reached out to journalists in both Pakistan and across the border, in India. One of them was in the newsroom I worked in at the time—in 2016. Most artistes don't have the confidence to challenge an industry this big.
How big? Well, Indian film music constitutes over 80% of the total revenue for the country’s music segment. The total market size of the Indian recorded music industry stood at $181.4 million as of 2019.
The reason Zahri could put forth his case, though, was the Copyright (Amendment) Act of 2012, which gave artistes rights to their music, and not just the producer or the music label.
The organisation that tends to mediate in such cases—namely the Indian Performing Rights Society or IPRS, a copyrights society since 2017—has around 6,000 members under its wing. But it has been accused of playing favourites, of being biased towards labels. It is often them who put forth a case, after all.

Good Morning Ishan,
 
What was your first phone? If it was at the turn of the millennium, it was most likely a Nokia, perhaps a 3300. Because you had no choice.
 
And when you did get a choice, you probably shifted models. Was it a Samsung? Or a Motorola? The Razr perhaps, when flip phones were all the rage. If you had an office phone, it had to be a Blackberry, which was synonymous with security. 
 
Then Apple threw the biggest twist into the market and removed the keypad. Your choice then was kidneys or Apple. In a few years every phone looked the same, and you perhaps obsessed over the software specs.
 
That too got standardised, and the differentiator became the camera. The selfie generation was calling the shots, and even Apple’s fabled annual launches focussed on the camera—how many lenses, megapixels, zoom, image stabilisation, etc.
 
In 2020, we are progressing to a new choice, a new question. It moved from “who made the phone?” to “where was it made?” And the answer should be “in India”. Or, to put it another way, “not in China”.
 
India’s border stand-off with China, followed by a ban on Chinese apps, has Chinese smartphone companies spooked. With their 80-plus percent market share at stake, they are pulling out all stops to prove their Indianness. They’re touting investments and job creation and everything remotely Indian, even the company leadership if they can.
 
The good news is that this might mean Chinese smartphone companies may help speed up the component ecosystem moving to India. To be fair, some already have made sizeable strides, writes Vandana in today’s story.
 
But the real question is whether Indians are clamouring for “made in India” phones, which mean decimated brands like Lava and Micromax can aim for renewed glory. Or is the demand for “not made in China”—something Samsung will be rooting for.
 
We don’t have the answer to that, but Vandana lays out how the combatants in the three-way battle are gearing up—whatever the question— now that the rules have changed. Read it here: https://the-ken.com/story/smartphones-anti-china-wave/ (12-minute read)
 
Warm regards,
From he who owns a Blackberry,

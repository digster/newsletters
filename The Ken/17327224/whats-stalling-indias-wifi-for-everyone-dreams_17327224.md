In today's world where everyone seems to have a cellphone, it's strange to think of the days of the phone booth.
Rifling through one's pockets for not just change but the right denomination of coin. The usually battered handset, privy to thousands of hushed and hurried conversations. And the inevitability of a call ending, not because a conversation was finished, but because you fumbled as you tried to pop a coin in the slot.
Gradually, phone booths were superseded by cyber cafes, retreating first into a corner, before being forgotten altogether. But phones eventually got their revenge. They got smaller, smarter, and more powerful, rendering cyber cafes obsolete by putting the internet in people's pockets.
The next step should have been the evolution of internet access—cheap, fast, and ubiquitous public WiFi. Instead, Reliance Jio happened. The telecom subsidiary of India's most valued company cratered data tariffs, making public WiFi seem... well, pointless.
Except, it really isn't.
Look at more developed nations. Public WiFi didn't disappear as their telecom coverage expanded. Instead, it mushroomed—taking the load off stressed telecom networks and allowing seamless browsing on the go. Technology like the Internet of Things could take root and spread.
In India, however, while a lot is said about public WiFi, not enough is done. The country has around 100,000 hotspots. The government wants 10 million by 2022. Not surprising, given that India accounts for 1 in 6 mobile subscribers worldwide.
We're probably not getting to 10 million anytime soon, but if India's telecom regulator has its way, we'll definitely be further along than we are now. It has a plan for an unbundled, distributed, interoperable public WiFi system. Inspired by India's monstrously successful payments innovation, the UPI.
For that to happen though, it must first sway a stubborn Department of Telecom, which first dragged its feet for years, and then threw a spanner in the works in May. It must also persuade telcos and internet service providers to play ball. And, finally, convince entrepreneurs that there's money to be made in public WiFi.

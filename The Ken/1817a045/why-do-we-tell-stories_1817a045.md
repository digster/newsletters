There is something weird that happens to journalists and writers (even artistes) when they get too involved in their work. The events outside that we read and write about day in and day out start to become a part of us. The story and the storytellers become inseparable. The dancer becomes the dance. Yes, I am writing this recap email from a rainy Delhi and an existential mind space. Please bear with me.
The last fortnight has been heavy with the news of a slowdown, layoffs, and rising Covid cases. If you have been reading the stories my talented colleagues have been writing, you know that the stories have also been extremely exciting.
Amidst these contrasting sentiments, I have asked myself why, after all, do we tell stories?
Journalists don’t tend to be a happy bunch. We aren’t particularly liked, either. We tend to be cynical (often paranoid), and are used to judging the moral compass of how leaders, governments, and companies behave (to our friends and family we often sound like annoying know-it-alls).
Even worse, we can be so detached from our surroundings that we see everything as a story. Life can appear full of conflicts, dialogues, and scenes, and it is hard to be grateful for the rich emotional landscape that life has to offer. I will try to answer this question as I recap the stories we published this week.
One of the more fun reasons for telling stories for a living is camaraderie. On Tuesday, three of my colleagues, all with different areas of expertise—Jaspreet writes about crypto, Arundhati writes about fintech, and Olina writes about edtech—found themselves working together on one of the most deeply investigated stories
The Ken has ever published. If there is one thing you do today, read the story about how the
[Byju’s juggernaut](https://the-ken.com/story/an-audit-reckoning-the-22b-byjus-juggernaut-is-yet-to-get-deloittes-approval/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter) is yet to get Deloitte’s approval. Jaspreet shared the story behind the story with all of us and it not only made for a great all-hands call, but it also reminded us how much fun working together can be.
The second reason, I believe, is an addiction to nuance. For a journalist, the devil and the angels both live in the details. Also, it is a source of pride for a reporter when they can answer the detailed questions that their editor asks. The detail makes stories chaotic but it is a great relief when we get to simplify them. Like Pratap’s story about the power politics behind the
[text messages that Amazon sends](https://the-ken.com/story/us101-million-the-steep-sms-price-tag-amazons-fighting-in-india/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter) to its users. The story promises a detailed but simple and profound account of a unique setting.
Further, stories connect the dots between events and processes that are often difficult to see. Making those connections intuitively and discovering something new is a eureka moment for us. Just like the story in which Anand and Srikant wrote about the implications of the massive growth in India's
[mutual fund industry](https://the-ken.com/story/retail-investors-are-the-new-stars-in-indias-479b-mutual-fund-show/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter) that no one is talking about.
Finally, as storytellers, we get to feel good because we tell the stories of other people. In the process, we get to feel their emotions and experiences, and provide them with some catharsis by telling their story. Like Arundhati’s story about
[layoffs in the Indian startup sector](https://the-ken.com/story/is-your-job-safe-this-startup-layoffs-season/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter) and Yunindita’s story on
[live-commerce enablers](https://the-ken.com/story/how-upmesh-shoplus-and-co-are-filling-metas-live-commerce-gap/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter) helping thousands of merchants in Southeast Asia.
Why we tell stories is an open question with evolving answers. However, as far as my feelings this week are concerned, I feel that, fundamentally, stories help us do more with less. Good stories magnify the landscape of reality and help us go deep into its rhythm. The effect of telling and listening to stories is that when one acts, one acts with complete conviction in the direction that they know is right. A good story eliminates confusion, simplifies the complex, and aligns the heart with the head.

Today’s article is atypical, in that it wasn’t written by one of our staff writers. Instead, it was written by a practising software engineer. Sidu Ponnappa heads technology recruiting and data engineering in India for GO-JEK, the Indonesian motorbike on-demand and logistics startup that is also a “unicorn”.
 
The idea for this story came up months ago at a Starbucks when I asked Sidu why, in spite of being the country that gave the world IT giants like TCS, Infosys and Wipro, India never managed to replicate that success when it came to large software-powered Internet businesses?
 
Sidu’s take was that it was in large parts because the Indian IT industry viewed software as a project management challenge. One meant to be broken down into clear requirements, delineated stages, sequential development, large teams and project managers. And over time, this outlook permeated into the people entering this sector. The career goal everyone strived for was becoming Project Manager.
 
Today’s article takes off from that thought and sheds light on the contrasting methods by which today’s start-ups create software. The coming years for Indian IT are not going to be easy. Not because they won’t win business (TCS alone won close to $6 billion in just the last month), but because their people-powered model of software development will gradually make way for more of automation and AI.

Good Morning Ishan,
Around 2020, Peter Biskind, lauded for his propulsive chronicles of Hollywood, set out to write a book on “peak TV”. But by the time he finished it—three years later—peak TV was history. So the book, Pandora’s Box, is as much about the swift decline of highbrow shows as about their unexpected rise.
A review of the book summed it up quite well: “How Ted Lasso killed Tony Soprano”. Ted Lasso, for all the awards it has won, is nothing but Hum Aapke Hain Koun with football.
Indian shows on Netflix, Amazon Prime Video, and Disney+ Hotstar have never measured up to the likes of Transparent and BoJack Horseman. But there was a brief period—during the pandemic—when there was respectable fare, such as Scam 1992 and Paatal Lok.
If writers, directors, and actors thought they could finally cut loose, they were mistaken, as Nathan argues in [his story](https://the-ken.com/story/jiocinema-hotstar-wont-deliver-the-thrills-netflix-and-amazon-once-did/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter) today.
India is not immune to streamers’ preference for crowd-pleasing content that doesn’t break the bank. They have slashed budgets by half for new seasons of popular local shows. Most worryingly for the creative community, the platforms are making far fewer shows. “Barring a handful of big directors and actors, most of the industry has been sitting at home for the last two years,” said actor Aahana Kumra.
What happens when you add the game-changing Jiocinema-Disney+ Hotstar merger to the mix?
Let’s [find out](https://the-ken.com/story/jiocinema-hotstar-wont-deliver-the-thrills-netflix-and-amazon-once-did/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter).

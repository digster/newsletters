Good morning, dear Ishan, another weekend, another conversation! After a few weeks of virtually daily rain in Bengaluru, we had a mostly dry, sunny, and cool week. I was happy for all the street dogs, including Bribo. But then, on Friday evening, the skies opened up, just 15 minutes after I’d fed him dinner. I think there was enough rain to make up for the rest of the dry week earlier.

I’ve truly loved rain—the heavier, the better—all my life. And yet, these days when it rains, my first thoughts are how Bribo and his fellow street indies must be feeling. That “switch flipped” the day I let Bribo and his mother Svelty into my life. Svelty tugged at my heart strings to get me to adopt her and Bribo (so thin he could barely get up the first time I fed him).

But, because the switch flipped, I now strangely feel connected to street indies and their lives. When it’s raining, I wonder if they’ve managed to find some cover. When Bengaluru nights turn cold, I wonder if they have found some warmth. When I hear sounds of dogs howling or barking nearby, my heart sinks. Was it Bribo? What kind of trouble are they in? What must they be feeling?

I cannot imagine what Bribo, who’s now vaccinated and neutered, would go through if someone were to forcibly take him away and put him in a shelter with dozens of other scared, anxious, mistreated, hurt, and hungry dogs. Who will he look to? What will he tell himself?

Thankfully, India’s Supreme Court has course-corrected https://www.thehindu.com/news/cities/Delhi/supreme-court-modifies-earlier-directive-allows-release-of-strays-from-delhi-shelters-after-immunisation/article69963533.ece on its earlier order to forcibly relocate all street dogs to “shelters” in Delhi. Yes, it’s true that dog attacks and rabies deaths are a problem. But the solution must be compassionate and community-driven too.

The more disconnected and disinterested we are from any “problem”, the more we want it just taken care of anyhow. But once we get involved, we want to be part of a sustainable solution.

ABC—animal birth control—works. So does vaccination. Except, most city administrations don’t stick to them seriously enough. Bengaluru, fortunately, is a city where both the administration and citizens do try their best https://www.thehindu.com/news/national/karnataka/a-dogs-life-in-the-city/article69958726.ece.

A shout out to India Animal Fund https://indiaanimalfund.org/, an organisation I came to know about via Parag Agarwal, its founder and board member. The fund wants to bring about systemic solutions to animal welfare. “Two billion animals are in our daily systems. Zero animals are on our dashboards,” it says. How true.

Meanwhile, Bribo’s new home for the past few months has been a discarded sofa that his caretakers have salvaged from somewhere. A week ago, a cotton mattress got added to it, giving him a cozier and warmer bed for the increasingly colder Bengaluru nights. I’m glad he has others who care for him too.

This was him, all happy and eager, coming back home after spending a month at the hospital recovering from a car driver running over his hind leg. How can you not love him?

1. “Attention is all you need”

If you had to trace the root of the current LLM-driven AI bubble-wave to a single sentence, that would be: “Attention is all you need.”

That was the title of the landmark 2017 research paper https://arxiv.org/abs/1706.03762 co-authored by eight Google scientists.

“We propose a new simple network architecture, the Transformer, based solely on attention mechanisms, dispensing with recurrence and convolutions entirely,” they said.

I find it triply ironic that the core technological breakthrough that enabled the creation of a new class of AIs that mimic human thinking is, well, human attention.

“Attention mechanisms are inspired by the ability of humans (and other animals) to selectively pay more attention to salient details and ignore details that are less important in the moment. Having access to all information but focusing on only the most relevant information helps to ensure that no meaningful details are lost while enabling efficient use of limited memory and time.”

“What is an attention mechanism? https://www.ibm.com/think/topics/attention-mechanism” by Dave Bergmann and Cole Stryker (IBM)

Meanwhile, us human beings are losing our ability to focus and provide attention. Because we’re overwhelmed with information and choices, much of which is generated by AI models inspired by, well, our ability to command attention.

In spite of, or perhaps because of, these, I strongly feel that the secret ingredient that will enable the most talented, creative, ambitious, or hard-working humans to become even better at what they do will be attention.

Our ability to focus our attention, to be in the moment and to bring our whole self to the tasks that matter, will be our superpower.

“In the future, there will be two kinds of people in the world: those who let their attention and lives be controlled and coerced by others and those who proudly call themselves ‘indistractable’”, wrote Nir Eyal in his 2019 book of the same name. (In many ways, his second book was him atoning for his first, Hooked!)

We’ve talked about the concept of “Flow” in past editions. But here’s Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi (also now deceased) explaining the concept of “attention as psychic energy” from his book, Flow.

“Some people learn to use this priceless resource efficiently, while others waste it. The mark of a person who is in control of consciousness is the ability to focus attention at will, to be oblivious to distractions, to concentrate for as long as it takes to achieve a goal, and not longer. And the person who can do this usually enjoys the normal course of everyday life.”

[…]

“Because attention determines what will or will not appear in consciousness, and because it is also required to make any other mental events—such as remembering, thinking, feeling, and making decisions—happen there, it is useful to think of it as psychic energy. Attention is like energy in that without it no work can be done, and in doing work it is dissipated. We create ourselves by how we invest this energy. Memories, thoughts, and feelings are all shaped by how we use it. And it is an energy under our control, to do with as we please; hence, attention is our most important tool in the task of improving the quality of experience.”

Though I don’t think anyone could’ve put it better than the late poet Mary Oliver https://guideposts.org/inspiring-stories/stories-of-faith-and-hope/mary-olivers-instructions-for-living/.

Instructions for living a life:

Pay attention.

Be astonished.

Tell about it.

Ironically, I realised this even more as I started to use LLMs in my work.

The biggest change or “delta” that I’ve realised is an increase in my ability to pick up and complete “0 to 1” tasks. By that I mean things that were merely one- or two-line items on some calendar or list or plan somewhere, languishing because I did not have the time to take them up. Perhaps it was conceptualising a new role or researching an idea for a story or understanding newer ways to categorise First Principles editions over the years.

AI tools allow me to cross the 0-to-1 chasm very fast, across numerous items.

Once I do, though, I realise which of them are worth spending more time on to take from “1 to 10”, so to speak. There, AI tools are less valuable as I need to devote much more of my own time and attention.

Which in turn makes me realise I’ve spread myself too thin across too many projects. I need to safeguard my time and attention better so that they can be spent on things that either give me more joy or The Ken more value.

To do that, though, I realise I need to become stricter about where to spend my time and attention. I should be figuring out ways to “save” them from relatively lower-value tasks.

Perhaps the best way to do that is to spend some time automating them with AI tools.

Attention, like time, seems to be a flat circle.

But do you see how the first and second “passes” of AI are significantly different? One focused on pending tasks, the other on repetitive tasks.

3. To write is to imagine

Hello Ishan, this Wednesday, I found a surprise on my third-floor balcony: a small plant growing stubbornly in the corner. Such resilience—it reminded me of the “tree of heaven https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ailanthus_altissima”, which rather famously “grows on neglect”. 

To thrive despite being overlooked is a powerful story, and it’s one that is perfectly captured in Betty Smith’s classic, A tree grows in Brooklyn https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/14891.A_Tree_Grows_in_Brooklyn.

The book is a classic coming-of-age tale (a künstlerroman, if you fancy some German) of Francie Nolan, the gritty, observant daughter of immigrants who dreams of being a writer. She can’t even finish school, but Francie fights her way toward a better life—a mirror of Smith’s own journey. 

What struck me the most about this story was how Francie was introduced to writing; it’s after she is caught in a lie and her teacher tells her:

“In the future, when something comes up, you tell exactly how it happened but write down for yourself the way you think it should have happened. Tell the truth and write the story. Then you won’t get mixed up.”

It got me thinking. 

Whether we are writing fiction or nonfiction, we must first imagine before we can put words to paper. Sometimes, this is an almost simultaneous process. But oftentimes, I, at least, sit and stare at nothing trying to make sense of what I want to say—stewing in my own thoughts, getting the picture right, helps me articulate it that much better. 

There are several places in the novel where imagination is seen as a necessity, not just for writing but also for survival. For example, every Saturday, Francie pours out her coffee just to feel, for a moment, like someone rich enough to waste things. 

And then there’s this exchange between her mother and grandmother:

“Because,” explained Mary Rommely simply, “the child must have a valuable thing which is called imagination. The child must have a secret world in which live things that never were. It is necessary that she believe. She must start out by believing in things not of this world. Then when the world becomes too ugly for living in, the child can reach back and live in her imagination.”

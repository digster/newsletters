"According to Darwin's Origin of Species, it is not the most intellectual of the species that survives; it is not the strongest that survives; but the species that survives is the one that is able best to adapt and adjust to the changing environment in which it finds itself." - Leon C. Megginson
The pandemic has forced us humans, social creatures in happier days, to adapt to physical distancing. India is halfway through a 21-day nationwide lockdown to enforce this. That's left the country's startups to adjust to a weird environment—one where demand is unpredictable, and supply is slammed. One where the government is omnipotent and omnipresent.
From multi-billion-dollar enterprises to bootstrapped companies, everyone ground to a halt. Except if the government decided you have, or can adapt to get, the perfect product-market fit for "essential services".
It's a whole new order for businesses to operate in India. One that is best described as three concentric circles, write Arundhati and Abinaya in our story today. The outermost ring is for the outcasts—startups that don't deal in essentials nor have adapted. The middle circle is for those selling essentials. Vending veggies? Got milk? You can stay open. Delivery? Well umm … figure it out, adapt or level down.
It's the innermost circle, though, that is the most fascinating. It houses the chosen few, the ones who have adapted, chameleon-like, to become the official lockdown product or service.
I won't give you names for two reasons. 1) Because, though told through an Indian lens, this new order of doing business is not limited to Indian shores. Which is why we are sharing this story with our readers in Southeast Asia.

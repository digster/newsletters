For a Valley unicorn that hits the headlines almost daily for its imminent IPO, fraught relationships with civic authorities, or its hosts making big bucks, Airbnb is conspicuous in India by its quietness.
 
Except, of course, when company CEO Brian Chesky walked the ramp two years ago in Delhi for the launch of its new product, Experiences. Its arrival fed the notion that Airbnb would make travel a transformative experience, allowing people to discover communities, and eventually shake up Indian hospitality. The number of Experiences in this time has swelled to ~20K globally, but it stands at a puny 100 in India. Imagine, Kolkata doesn’t have any listed Experiences despite being famous for its rich history and culture.
 
Internet companies in India—in payments, dating, e-commerce, music, search, etc.—have had formidable competition from multinationals. Travel, however, remains a distant success. Even Booking.com hasn’t had it easy.
 
Make no mistake, Airbnb hasn’t done poorly in India. In tune with its global financial performance (unlike unicorn peers like Uber or Netflix), it’s profitable in India, too. Its country head, Amanpreet Bajaj, says listings in India have grown 115% over the past year, with 40K listings across the country. But that accounts for just 1% of Airbnb’s 4 million listings across 191 countries, less than what it has in Thailand and on par with South Korea's.
 
The company is hitting the skids on several fronts in India. It also has well-funded hospitality unicorns like Oyo and online travel agencies like MakeMyTrip, Yatra and others snapping at its heels. For some reason, Airbnb has been slow and non-aggressive in India; it’s crossing the river by feeling the stones.

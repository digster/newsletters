Some stories become clearer with time. In the case of India’s environment, they also get sadder with time. Earlier this month, the Center for Science and Environment (CSE) published the latest edition of its State of India’s Environment Report—a tradition of keeping tabs on India’s environment they began four decades ago.
CSE, which also produces Down to Earth, a fortnightly magazine covering the politics of environment and development, has since captured India’s environment from the ground—with regular snapshots and news stories, but also the big picture through their annual reports and books. In this edition of Inciting Incident, which is all about how stories are told and evolve, I thought we’d take a quick run through how India’s environment story has changed over the years.
Starting with CSE’s first report in 1982. Let me quote from a Cambridge University Press [review](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/environmental-conservation/article/abs/state-of-indias-environment-1982a-citizens-report-centre-for-science-and-environment-807-vishal-bhavan-95-nehru-place-new-delhi-110-019-india-iii-190-pp-illustr-285-21-15-cm-rs-125-1982/E259C38A4B9B7080DFE6242F40EDF6AB) that says each of the 11 chapters in the report is preceded by half-a-dozen highlights “calculated to chill the blood of anyone who cares about the future of India and its people”.
Water – Seventy percent of all the available water in India is polluted. About 73 million man-work days a year are lost owing to water-related diseases. In a 158-km stretch of the Hoogly, the average annual yield of fish in the unpolluted zones was 719.25 tonnes as opposed to only 124.94 tonnes in the polluted zones.
Atmosphere – India’s premier pollution research institute claims that 60% of Calcutta’s residents suffer from respiratory diseases because of air pollution.
‘Habitat’-India’s urban population has doubled in the last 20 years. In the 30 years from 1951 to 1981, Calcutta and Hyderabad have doubled their populations, Greater Bombay, Madras, and Ahmedabad, have tripled, while Delhi and Bangalore have quadrupled.
Health – Every third person who dies in India is a child below the age of five-a victim of a vicious combination of poverty, malnutrition, insanitary environment, and unclean drinking water.
Energy – Firewood scarcity in rural and urban areas is increasing dramatically. In mountainous Garhwal, women walk at least seven hours, three out of every four days, to bring back 25 kg of wood each time. In many cities firewood prices have nearly doubled in the last six years.
About a couple of decades later, in 2009, the story had changed, but only for the worse. CSE’s [2009 report](http://www.indiaenvironmentportal.org.in/files/StateofEnvironmentReport2009.pdf) goes into more detail than its first avatar, but the essentials? Land was degrading, biodiversity was in free fall, air quality in cities was deteriorating due to growing vehicle use, industrial pollution was impacting health on a massive scale, clean water was scarcer.
And it was finally also crystal clear that climate change and energy security were both major concerns.
Well, it’s been another couple of decades since, and I don’t think you’d be surprised by what CSE’s State of India’s Environment report 2024, [released last week](https://www.cseindia.org/page/aaddialogue2025), says. The headline of the press release: The year the world changed. 2024, it is reported, was the warmest year on record. The next generation will grow up in a world which will be far hotter and climatologically changed.
The State of India’s Environment report quotes the European Union’s Copernicus Climate Change Service, which says that 2024 was the first calendar year with a global average temperature “clearly exceeding” 1.5 o C above the pre-industrial level.
The year 2024, in fact, was the warmest year on record, with the average global temperature at 1.60 o C above the pre-industrial era (1850-1900) average. Says Kiran Pandey, programme director, environmental resources at CSE: “2024 will be remembered as the year that divided the pre- and post-climate change eras.” She adds: “Given the fact that atmospheric moisture levels rise by 7 per cent for every one degree rise in global average temperatures, this is a perfect brew for planetary disruptions in terms of extreme weather events.”

There is more bad news, according to Sunita Narain, director general of CSE.
Speaking at Anil Agarwal Dialogue 2025, an annual conclave of Indian journalists who write on environment and development issues, Narain highlighted how Americans have essentially voted against the climate, how internal displacements due to weather-related disasters have surged worldwide, and more pertinent to us, how India’s environment management system is broken—working neither for industry nor the environment.

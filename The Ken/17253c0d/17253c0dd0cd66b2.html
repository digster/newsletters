Today, we’re going beyond the first order, beyond the happenings on Earth. Yes! Barter is making a comeback. But then, so are locusts and Crocs.
 
Strap in.
Nasa to Isro: Look, I did it; you can, too
 
Seema
 
After hitching a ride on Russian rockets for nine years, Nasa is finally sending its astronauts to space on an American rocket, from American soil. Built by SpaceX, the reusable rocket Crew Dragon will fly two astronauts to the ISS on Wednesday afternoon.
 
The space race is largely synonymous with countries trying to outdo each other's outer space ambitions. In 2011, Nasa unleashed a private space race between Boeing and SpaceX, which the latter seems to have won. Today’s launch is the culmination of a decade’s work between Nasa and SpaceX. If successful, it’ll mark the launch of a new age in commercial crewed spaceflight.
The program has many goals, but its biggest is this: have private companies, not NASA, create the next generation of spacecraft that can take US astronauts to orbit. The hope was that these commercial vehicles would be cheaper than what NASA could make, saving taxpayer money. Once the spacecraft were complete, the companies that built them could then turn a profit by selling seats on their vehicles to paying customers.
 
In essence, NASA wanted to bring capitalism to human spaceflight. “It’s Capitalism 101,” Lori Garver, the deputy administrator of NASA under President Obama, tells The Verge. “The government is just not good at that stuff. We have been launching for 50 years, and the industry was doing all the work. Why not let them take it over and we buy the services.”
How the SpaceX Crew Dragon Mission could shape the future of commercial space, The Verge
This is a goal the Indian Space Research Organisation (Isro) has had in mind for years. It was articulated once again last week when the Indian government went on a reforms spree. Space policy was one of them:
While praising the work done by ISRO, the finance minister pointed out that the private sector is also doing a lot of work in developing space technology. She also acknowledged that the existing regulations prevent private entities from using or even testing their products.

Therefore, to level the playing field, the government “will make a provision for the private sector to benefit from the assets which are available to ISRO and for India (in general) to benefit from.” The minister also said the new reforms would allow the private sector to play an active role in “satellites, launches and space-based services”.
India’s Space Programme: A role for the private sector, finally? The Wire Science
While the Nasa-SpaceX mission today is an inspiration in itself, for Isro it must act as the much-needed thrust to set in place practices that ensure more transparency and accountability.
The Crew Dragon. Source: From Space With Love
 
Over the last decade, Isro has become a non-open, if not opaque, organisation. As an ageing government agency, it must understand that it needs a dash of energy and imagination from the fledgling private sector as much as they need Isro to chart their commercial course.
Isro should step up to share its non-critical/classified intellectual property with the industry. Not ask the private sector IP to rest with Isro, which is one of the bones of contention today in the new space policy. This is sad because last week, Isro called for private companies to help with technology development in its own manned mission, Gaganyaan.
 
A government has to be large-hearted. It is, in fact, the hero of innovation, if you look at some of the most useful and popular technologies of our time: from GPS to the internet, the iPhone to Tesla’s battery tech to Google. “Every major technological change in recent years traces most of its funding back to the state,” says leading economist Marianna Mazuccato in her book The Entrepreneurial State: Debunking Public vs Private Sector Myths.
 
That said, open-ended goals hardly fructify in the Indian public sector. Isro should keep aside a publicly known set of IPs/technologies and a certain percentage of its facility usage/occupancy for the private sector. Create a team and task them with this goal. Otherwise, given how packed Isro’s production and launch calendars are, the Indian private sector will rarely get a chance.
 
Nasa’s success with this mission should hugely encourage Isro to be more collaborative with the private sector, mostly in its own interest.
Pondering house arrest
 
Nadine

Like in many countries, Indonesia has a problem with overcrowded prisons. Covid-19 made that a health hazard too serious to be ignored.
 
Almost a quarter of the prison population are actually detainees, which means they’re held in official custody, but aren’t yet sentenced.
“The country’s 524 penitentiaries and detention centres hold a total of 232,795 inmates and detainees, almost double the facilities’ total capacity of 131,931, according to Law and Human Rights Ministry data from May 24. The country’s 55,089 detainees account for about 24 percent of the total prison population.”
Alternatives to detention must be part of ‘new normal’, govt official says, The Jakarta Post
There are now calls for reform, aimed at keeping detainees out of prison. The alternative is to put them under house arrest or city arrest.

The Attorney General is hesitant in implementing these practices, writes The Jakarta Post.
"If defendants are placed under house arrest, are there any guarantees they will show up to their trials and not run away?”
That’s a real dilemma; one that makes fertile ground for advocates of surveillance tech. Electronic bracelets, location tracking, and facial recognition tech could provide answers here, but it would require Indonesia to move further down the road of implementing government surveillance tech, especially surveillance cameras.
 
So far, Indonesian cities haven’t made the list.
The heralds of a plague

Savio
 
India’s farm sector, which employs nearly half the population, has been a “beacon of hope” and is poised to grow at least 3% in 2020-21, the country’s officials have said. Nonetheless, farmers have little to cheer about, with wholesale prices crashing during the lockdown. Already on a weak footing, they now face a fresh problem. Swarms of desert locusts.
 
India fought off the first wave of swarms earlier this year quite successfully. But the second wave has not only come earlier than expected, it has also spread wider in search of food given the low availability of vegetation in summer. Swarms have been seen in the states of Rajasthan, Gujarat, Punjab, Haryana, and even Madhya Pradesh (for the first time since 1993).
Source: Twitter
 
It’s not only India. The swarms have already infested multiple countries across East Africa, the Middle East, and South Asia. But what caused the swarms and why is it so difficult to fight them off this time around?
“Indeed, the locust eruption is another reminder of how climate change poses a clear and present danger,” said WRI Vice President and Regional Director for Africa Wanjira Mathai.  “The locust outbreak is further compounded by the challenges of COVID-19, which have hampered preparation efforts to fight and control the pests. It’s urgent that we build resilience in our food systems so that external shocks like COVID-19 and locusts are not as devastating.”
Which Countries Are Most Vulnerable to Locust Swarms?, World Resources Institute
It isn’t a massive, wide-spread problem yet, but it could become one in June when monsoon rains spur rice, cane, corn, cotton, and soybean sowing. Already, Moong cereal crop worth around Rs 8,000 crore (US$1.05 billion) is under threat in Madhya Pradesh.
 
But, it’s not as if India was caught off-guard. The Locust Warning Organization (LWO), which is controlled by the Centre, had already warned of another attack in May-June. However, the LWO centres are crippled by staff shortages and obsolete equipment. Rajasthan, which bears the brunt of the attacks each year, has asked for an upgrade.
 
As such, the forecast by the UN’s Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO) is grim.
Source: FAO
 
So what is India doing now? Besides reaching out to Pakistan to deal with a common enemy, it has placed an order for 50 mounted pesticide sprayers with the UK and green-lighted the use of drones to spray insecticide. But insecticides and drones are hardly the answer.
Officials say standard containment plans for such pest attacks have undergone a rethink as better irrigation facilities have increased the green cover in Rajasthan. Indiscriminate aerial spraying of insecticides is ill-advised as this can damage crops as well as affect human settlements.
 
One of the strategies being considered now is to use fixed-wing aircraft for ultra-low volume spraying of chemicals. The state government may request for such aerial sorties by the air force. Drones have limited capabilities as they can carry only about 20 litres of insecticide at a time, making them effective only over small areas.
Locust attack: Rajasthan’s new worry, India Today
What did other countries do? Iran promised to deploy its military, China sent 100,000 ducks, while the Kenyan police fired tear gas and used machine guns.
 
Then there’s always banging utensils.
An odd pair of economic crashes
 
Jon
 
The 2008 financial crisis nearly wiped out Crocs and its love-it-or-hate-it foam footwear. Its share price dropped below US$2 in October 2008, down from nearly US$70 the previous year. Crocs narrowly avoided bankruptcy a year later and was reborn over the next decade thanks to a strategy that included tightened operational costs and a focus on fun designs.
 
“Crocs are hot again,” wrote CNN Business in August 2018.
 
That was until Covid-19, another global economic disaster that, this time, included widespread residential lockdowns that hit fashion brands hard.
 
Crocs braced for impact. It reported end of year financials on 27 February with a warning that it had lost up to US$30 million in China already. And as the virus spread worldwide, it would suffer further still. Investors were spooked. The stock fell 16% immediately after that report and closed February down 31%.
 
Then, something unexpected happened. Crocs held up. In fact, it thrived.
 
Global sales revenue in Q1 dropped just 3.3% on a constant currency basis, even with the outbreak in China.
 
Remarkably, business in the US was spiking. Sales grew 14% in the first quarter of 2020, according to market research firm NPD Group. Crocs stood out as the only footwear brand tracked by the firm to post positive numbers in March, according to NPD analyst Beth Goldstein.
Source: Twitter
 
With staying in becoming the new going out, Crocs was well placed to weather this storm. It may have also gotten a boost due to demand from frontline healthcare workers, though the firm has donated more than half a million pairs of its foam shoes during the pandemic.
Source: Google
 
Crocs is not out of trouble yet, though. It has furloughed employees, cut salaries (right up to boardroom level), closed retail outlets, and had its supply chain disrupted—but its share price has bounced back to some extent and there’s optimism this time around.
What are the unforeseen consequences of Covid-19 pivots?

Rohin
 
Just a year ago, tour and activities platform Klook raised US$225 million in a round led by Softbank, bringing the total amount it had raised to US$521 million. The Hong Kong-based platform primarily targets travellers in China, Taiwan, and the Southeast Asia region.
“The tough part of our business is we cover so many services [tours, activities, attractions, train tickets, dining experiences, et cetera] that no one size fits all. You can create an airline booking engine and apply it to all airlines, same goes for hotels or ride hailing. The inconvenience of our business is it is fragmented not just in merchants but in category of services. So we pick our battles, for example, we digitize Japan rail while with Euro rail it could be about something as basic as the user experience with Wi-Fi connectivity,” he said.

Klook will focus on scaling up demand and service offers in Asia this year. Asia will remain its key market but Europe has strong growth potential.
 
“The upcoming Tokyo Olympics will result in travel from west to east, which is a market we’re going after. As the network effect grows stronger, we will also be valuable for European inter-regional travel. In fact, we’re already seeing a promising upward trend on Europeans consuming our services within Europe,” he said.
No one could have foreseen what Covid-19 would do to these growth assumptions.

Now, Klook is pivoting from outdoor to indoor activities.
Klook, a world-leading travel activities and services booking platform, announced today the launch of a new home-based experiences initiative, Klook Home. The new initiative is the first phase of Klook's global multi-stage recovery approach and has been rolled out across 14 markets. The debut signals the company's commitment to kick-start recovery and tackle the new normal of travel, combating the impact of COVID-19 in the travel industry. There are almost 200 home-based experiences on Klook Home such as Do-It-Yourself (DIY) craft and cooking kits, online workshops, as well as free virtual tours.
Now this is admirable. But given the number of large unicorns that are rapidly pivoting to anything they can do, I wonder what the longer-term effects will be.
 
How many pivots will create lasting competitive advantages? How many will distract organisations from their original USPs? Will they need to re-establish product-market fit all over again? How many employees who joined to pursue a different vision will stick on when they have newer opportunities? And when things start returning to normal, will organisations be able to unwind their pivots and revert to their original missions?
 
No easy answers.
What’s older than cash? What’s better than brands?

Rohin

Bartered essentials. From the province of Iloilo in the Philippines.
In the Iloilo Barter Community on the web, members conduct transactions online, by posting photos and videos of items they wish to trade and products they want in exchange. If a deal is agreed on, the participants will then discuss how and where they can meet up in person for the swap.
The community that was created is so lovely because branded things aren't as valuable anymore to people in Iloilo. Rice, face masks, alcohol, fruits, vegetables and basic needs people used to take for granted now have greater value.

If you think Chinese apps TikTok and WeChat had it bad after being banned in the US and India, Huawei—the Chinese firm that sells smartphones and mobile network equipment—is on a different level.
A relentless wave of US sanctions have left it flying like a plane "riddled with bullet holes," to use the company's own words.
With the US leading the charge, other countries are joining a growing boycott of its 5G equipment. India has already barred Huawei from a $1 billion tender floated by state-owned BSNL. A 5G ban is also a possibility. Singapore has kept Huawei out of its 5G networks, though not through state action. Germany has blocked Huawei from the core of its network—where all the sensitive data is processed. The UK has banned it from 5G network deployment altogether.
For a company that was hoping to lead the charge in 5G, it seems like death by a thousand cuts. But, as today's story shows, Huawei might have the last laugh. It has declared almost 1,000 5G patents more than its nearest rival, effectively ensuring that its future and the future of 5G networks are inextricably intertwined. The cause of its ostracisation—China—is also lobbying hard to control the 5G narrative.

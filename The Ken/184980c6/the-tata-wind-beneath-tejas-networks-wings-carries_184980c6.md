For tech journalists of a certain vintage, Tejas Networks was once a very happening company. It did innovative things, freely spoke with reporters, and dominated the tech-news cycle in the 2000s.
Business papers those days didn’t report breathlessly like today, but they did give Tejas consistent coverage. After all, it was one of its kind: an internet protocol (IP)-led telecom-tech company with some illustrious backers.
So when it finally listed in 2017, many of us expected it to do very well. But it didn’t, relatively speaking. In the 5+ years, the stock has appreciated 150%. (Tech stocks globally did much better.) A middling market cap made it hobble, especially in taking an inorganic growth path.
That streak was broken when Tata Sons acquired a majority stake in Tejas. The stock has rallied ~2,000% since May 2020 lows—with much of the gains coming in after the Tatas stepped in last year.
The market cap of Tejas already stands near Rs 10,000 crore ($1.2 billion). After a few loss-making quarters, the company posted a profit—of just Rs 1 crore ($123,000)—in the quarter ended September 2022.
Why, then, are the stock valuations off the charts with a price-to-earnings multiple of more than 2,000X?
In his story today, Anand says other numbers are equally striking. Tejas can’t be directly compared with its global peers like Ericsson and Nokia or domestic ones like HFCL and Sterlite; still, the rally in its stock is inexplicable. A class of investors has exited, and a new class has come in.

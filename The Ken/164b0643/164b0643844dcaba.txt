Good Morning Ishan,

Anti-trust regulators everywhere would have taken note of the sharpest criticism of Google yesterday when the European Union competition chief slapped a record €4.34 billion fine on the company. Within hours, Google made it public that it’d appeal the decision, which came after eight long years of investigation. 

The clash of titans will continue, but the message is unmistakable: regulators must do more to ensure fairness in the digital market.

A computer scientist I know, who has engaged with a few UN agencies, speaks highly of the European regulators at large, of their desire to truly understand how tech is evolving and upending businesses and social lives. (Perhaps also because the traces of European history are replete with injustices and extremes of violence.)

As for the knife-fights between the internet companies in India, they are often whodunits that never get a proper investigation. Our story today is a timely read on how India’s Competition Commission is placed to direct the future of competition in the online economy.

“These online network markets are very nuanced in the way they work. Big tech giants can exploit inherent loopholes in the implementation of the law. The truth is that they’re getting away with it. By the time the CCI asks them to respond to anti-trust allegations, these companies are already lawyered up.”

While this quote from the story may look bleak, and by their very nature, online businesses today escape the traditional safety nets of the Competition Act, India’s quasi-judicial regulator is finally gearing up to face the forces of the new economy.

Can the old horse teach itself some new tricks?

Good Morning Ishan,
There is a legion of marketers who have built their careers over decades. We expect the most experienced and effective individuals to naturally become CMOs, leading teams to execute the marketing functions of the companies they work for.
The nature of the job, however, has changed. “CMOs with a techno-commercial mindset are prioritised now,” as one human resources consultant told The Ken. In fact, some major companies in India, like Nykaa, no longer have CMOs, recognising that it makes more sense to use specialists who focus on specific channels with different marketing functions.
Look at the top FMCG companies and D2C brands in the country, and you’ll see that many of them have marketing heads for specific business units but no CMO that oversees the whole operation. Firms like Nestlé India and Tata Consumer Products, as well as Purplle and Licious, have all decentralised their marketing endeavours.
By scrapping this C-suite role altogether, marketers are in an awkward position—their diverse functions still need to aim at a common goal, but it’s less and less likely for a team to have a leader that brings it all together. In Nykaa’s case, it’s the CEO who makes the relevant decisions rather than a CMO.
More tactical and less strategic than ever, individuals in marketing leadership roles are increasingly compelled to work toward short-term outcomes. That doesn’t always work well for the companies they work for.

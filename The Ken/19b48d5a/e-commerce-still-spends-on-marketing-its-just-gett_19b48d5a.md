A weekly newsletter about the biggest changes in commerce—focusing on shifts that matter to you.
Good Morning Ishan,
Something is up with how e-commerce spends on marketing. The budgets haven’t vanished—but they are no longer being poured into expensive, do-everything software. They are now being directed more towards tools that deliver quicker growth.
Swiggy’s numbers show the shift. The company continues to spend heavily on advertising and promotions—[Rs 605.3 crore](https://www.storyboard18.com/advertising/swiggy-supercharges-ad-spend-scaling-up-13-2-to-rs-605-3-crore-in-q2-fy25-49273.htm) in Q2 FY25 alone—but that spend hasn’t ballooned indiscriminately year after year. Instead, it has stayed broadly consistent even as Swiggy pushed into newer bets like Instamart—suggesting tighter targeting rather than bigger spends. Even in its IPO filings, where optimism usually runs high, Swiggy avoided the usual brand-heavy language. Marketing spend was described less as a growth-at-any-cost play and more as a tool to drive repeat usage and back businesses that were already proving themselves.
The distinction matters. This isn’t a scorched-earth breakup with legacy systems. It’s e-commerce maturing enough to understand where they can chase growth realistically.
This shift is landing at an awkward moment for global SaaS. Growth is slowing, and not in a polite, cyclical way. Western enterprise software and bloated marketing platforms are feeling it first: budgets are tighter, sales cycles longer, and patience thinner. An [analysis](https://www.financemarketresearch.com/post/saas-companies-revenue-slowing-down-yoy?) of several publicly listed SaaS companies shows revenue growth falling from a once-comfortable 21% to 12% in 2024. And by the first quarter of 2025, SaaS wasn’t just slowing—it was shrinking, with sector-wide revenue growth at -2%.
This is where customer engagement platform Moengage enters the picture—not as an exception, but as a tell. Companies like Swiggy, Flipkart, Myntra, Oyo,* Makemytrip, Bookmyshow, Dream11, and Lenskart are shopping for its tools because it sits right on the revenue nerve. Its $280 million [Series F](https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/tech/funding/saas-startup-moengage-closes-extended-280-million-round-chryscapital-dragon-fund-schroders-join/articleshow/126031380.cms) raise in December 2025 came from selling the kind of software e-commerce teams use on a Tuesday afternoon: the push notification that pulls you back into an app, the in-app nudge that reminds you an item is still in your cart, the Whatsapp ping that quietly converts a lapsed user into a repeat orderer.
This SaaS startup lives in the messy middle between an app being built and money being made. As a source at Lenskart told me, “When users stop opening, clicking, or buying, this is the layer teams poke first.”
This isn’t a David-versus-Goliath story. The giants are still very much alive. Salesforce, Adobe, and their peers aren’t going anywhere. What’s changed is the terrain they’re being asked to fight on. And that shift—quiet, operational, and driven by how e-commerce actually works today—is where smaller, sharper tools like Moengage have found room to grow. The company saw a year-on-year revenue growth of [27%](https://inc42.com/buzz/moengage-bags-100-mn-from-goldman-sachs-alternatives-a91-partners/) in 2024.
The legacy SaaS playbook favoured one giant global platform, sold top-down through CIOs, justified with “digital transformation” promises and sold features—dashboards, modules, shiny capabilities you might get around to using someday. For example, in Swiggy’s case, that tells you who orders what, when, and how often.
And in e-commerce, that translates to tools plugged straight into the daily grind—retention, reactivation, conversion. Best-of-breed tools are picked for specific jobs, deployed fast, and judged faster—often by growth and marketing teams, not IT. Here, ROI isn’t a promise for next year; it’s something you’re expected to prove in weeks.
This isn’t a philosophical shift. It’s what happens when scrutiny rises and tolerance for “eventually useful” software wanes.
Which is why, in a market where patience is running thin, Moengage isn’t winning by being bigger—it’s winning by being harder to ignore. And while its closest peers—Clevertap, Webengage, Netcore, Insider—are focusing on consolidating their existing market, it is the outlier that’s still attracting capital. In a time when broad-based SaaS platforms are no longer an obvious bet for venture capital the way they were a few years ago. Not because the startup promised more software, but because it promised usefulness, fast.
After all, when margins are thin and users are fickle, the only software that matters is the one that brings them back.

Good Morning Ishan,
Stock markets love a good bull run. They climb, roar, dazzle, and for a while, everyone feels invincible. But when the music stops and the lights flicker on, someone’s stuck holding the tab. Spoiler: it’s usually the retail investors.
Since the pandemic, Indian retail investors have been on a buying spree, snapping up shares of micro-cap companies promising outsized returns. These are the smallest of the small caps—companies with market capitalisation up to Rs 5,000 crore, some even under Rs 100 crore (US$12 million). The smaller they were, the more retail investors loved them. In September, their stake in small-caps hit a 15-year high. But love, as it turns out, was delusional.
The market is now feeling gravity, and micro-caps are crashing. About a quarter of these stocks have dropped over 20% since September, some even losing 60% of their value. Meanwhile, the promoters of these companies—the folks who actually know how their businesses are doing—have quietly been cashing out.
Retail investors now own up to 80% of the stock in some of these companies. And their promoters? Vanished. It’s as if the hosts of the party left early and turned off the lights.
This isn’t even just a blip. A lot of these micro-caps were wildly overpriced, to begin with, trading at ridiculous valuations despite minimal profits—or no profits at all.

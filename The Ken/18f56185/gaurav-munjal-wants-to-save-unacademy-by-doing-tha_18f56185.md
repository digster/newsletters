Good Morning Ishan,
The Unacademy that exists now may not be the one that you were familiar with a year or two ago.
The new parts of its current sales pitch are straightforward: 500 students are enrolled at a centre, compared to competitors’ locations that might have up to 3,000. Unacademy’s in-person class sizes are smaller, and the teachers can give each pupil more attention. It even looks like a better deal.
But look closely and the coaching centre isn’t just a new vertical for the company. Sources told The Ken that Unacademy’s online business is just half of what it used to be. The transformation to offer more conventional tutorial services is keeping the company afloat. In fact, it accounts for half of the company’s revenue.
Observers in the edtech space suggest Unacademy’s latest move isn’t sustainable unless there are significant adjustments.
For today’s story. Alifiya spoke to 14 people, including current and former staff as well as investors and experts, to map out how Unacademy is making a transition from an edtech firm that stuck to its playbook and scaled quickly, to a business whose offline services will take time to mature.
More broadly, Alifiya’s article is a look into how Unacademy is adapting after India’s edtech sector flatlined. Check out her story [here](https://the-ken.com/story/gaurav-munjal-wants-to-save-unacademy-by-doing-that-very-thing-he-once-hated/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter).
In before you made it
You all know about the headwinds that startups around the world are facing. The results are intermittent layoffs, a funding crunch (for most sectors), and a lot of soul-searching.
Add one more worry to the bag: pre-IPO share sales.
The proliferation of marketplaces that deal in the buying and selling of startup shares is causing concern in Silicon Valley, according to an article published by The New York Times on Monday. The companies whose shares are changing hands are some of the most recognisable names. The rosters typically include AI developers, large Web3 companies, and a few popular platforms like Discord.
Skimming through several of these marketplaces shows that there are at most a couple dozen sell orders for each startup’s shares. But this can already make things difficult for the firms. With more shareholders comes more obligations (as well as complications) when complying with securities laws.
It’s an interesting phenomenon, particularly at a time when some of the world’s biggest startups are in no hurry to go public. The question is this: if you had the chance to own a piece of OpenAI, Stripe, Consensys, or SpaceX before these companies go public, would you go for it? The bet here is that enough people would say yes.

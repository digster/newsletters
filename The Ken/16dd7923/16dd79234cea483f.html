India is a country with a large population that worships cows.

India is also, as our school textbooks taught us in the "white revolution" chapter, the largest producer of milk.

Adds up.

But what doesn't add up is that despite this, India imports most of its whey protein. Whey is essentially the stuff that comes out as residue while cheese is being manufactured.

Some might call it cheese waste.

As it turns out, India’s total dairy imports jumped to $43.8 million in 2017 from $23.5 million in 2006. Much of that was due to burgeoning imports of whey - to 52.56% of dairy imports in 2017, from 18.41% nine years back, official government data shows.

One big reason for this is that while India is big on milk, it consumes very little cheese compared to the rest of the world—about 700 gm per capita consumption in Indian cities as opposed to the global average of 7 kg per annum.

And without cheese, there's no whey.

India is also a highly protein-deficient country, with 80% of the population lacking adequate protein intake, a gap whey protein supplement makers claim they can help bridge. But due to the low production of whey in India, these guys are also heavily dependent on imports.

Indian dairy majors like Amul and Parag, which produce both cheese and whey, are lobbying to increase import duty and feed the whey demand in India.

India had already hiked this import duty on whey from 30% to 40% last year. With the possibility of a 60% import duty on the horizon, can smaller dairy players rise up to the production challenge?

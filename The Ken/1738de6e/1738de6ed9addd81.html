Good morning,

Grab your coffee and buckle in. We’re taking you into the Kill Zone, that belt of stifled innovation that forms around all-too-powerful tech platforms. What exactly is the halo that appeared around Jio Platforms? After the long read, we’re off to Indonesia, where small towns long for a renaissance. Lastly, find out why Apple stores, in any city, are the first to close.
Big Tech and Kill Zones. Jio and Luck Zones 
 
Rohin
 
Four of the most powerful men in the world were to have testified today before a US Congress committee. The men are the CEOs, respectively, of Amazon, Apple, Google and Facebook. They are being called because of an ongoing antitrust investigation by the US Congress into “the dominance of a small number of digital platforms and the adequacy of existing antitrust laws and enforcement.”

Author and professor Scott Galloway offered some questions for the investigating panel and its experts for each of the four CEOs. It’s in his characteristic voice, of course. 

Q: Mr. Zuckerberg, you are in sole control of a platform whose algorithms select and deliver content (news and entertainment) to 2.6 billion people. Your algorithms are opaque to government agencies. You are the principal source of information for a cohort larger than any country or religion — a population greater than that of China and the US, combined. You cannot be removed from office, and will likely control these algorithms for the next 5-7 decades. So, we’d like to get to know you better. You know, get a feel for the real Mark Zuckerberg. A couple of questions:

Are you a pathological liar?

Do you display a lack of remorse or guilt?

And again…

Are you a pathological liar?

No one ever faulted Galloway for mincing his words. And in his defence, not that he needs it, his blog is called “No Mercy/No Malice.”

One of his many questions related to the “Kill Zone” that surrounds these 4 companies.

Q: Small business formation is at a multi-decade low. The fastest-growing sectors receive scant funding from investors. Why should someone invest in a search engine right now, or a music streaming business, or a social media platform, or an e-commerce firm, given the sizes of your companies?
What is the “Kill Zone”? It’s the relative absence of healthy competition, innovation and funding in market segments where each of these big companies operate or even make acquisitions. 

“Kill Zone”

Here’s how three researchers from the University of Chicago’s Booth School of Business  explained it in their April 2020 paper called, well, “Kill Zone.”

“We study why high-priced acquisitions of entrants by an incumbent do not necessarily stimulate more innovation and entry in an industry (like that of digital platforms) where customers face switching costs and enjoy network externalities."

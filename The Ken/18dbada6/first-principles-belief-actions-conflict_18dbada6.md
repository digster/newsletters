Its iridescent new leaves can be easily spotted alongside roads, next to lakes, and inside parks all over Bengaluru. They’re the ones with the ridiculously bright, waxy and gossamer green leaves simultaneously reflecting and channelling sunshine. I’ve always had a thing for bright spring foliage.
The Pongamias will start blooming in a few weeks, and then roughly a month or so from now, literally carpet the ground around them with fallen blossoms.

You might remember our “[Horror Vacui](https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/first-principles-horror-vacui/)” edition, where I postulated that belief is a key trait to succeeding in our careers, and indeed, in our lives.
Because just like nature does not allow vacuums to exist, modern organisations do not allow lack of beliefs to exist. In the rush to get on with work, belief voids are quickly filled in with the views and beliefs of others (subordinates, peers, bosses, customers, etc.). But for projects to succeed, this void cannot be filled up by others. Especially “HIPOs” (highly paid person’s opinion), to borrow Chargebee CEO Krish Subramanian’s pointer.
Because each time our lack of belief or conviction is replaced with someone else’s belief or conviction, we slowly give up the need to believe. I mean, why believe in what you’re going to do if everyone around you already expects you to believe it, right?
In a subsequent edition, “[The Third Value](https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/the-third-value/)”, we went one step further, and said that “actions” were a logical outcome of beliefs.
None of us, regardless of our age or experience, have a full view of the world with all its complexities and wonders. It is curiosity that drives us to know more of it. To explore. To hypothesise. To understand.
As we do, our understanding of the world shifts. And with it, our perspective too. From making sense of the world given to us, we begin to form beliefs that emanate from within us. Our locus of control shifts from external to internal. It is belief that creates in us the urge to create.
And the most logical outcome for a belief is an action, for me. Actions are where beliefs are put to test.
If beliefs should lead to actions, what could actions lead to?
Conflict.
That’s right. As counterintuitive and uncomfortable that sounds, I do believe that strongly held beliefs often lead to conflict. And that is good.
Allow me to back track.
I view the standard state of most of us as professionals (or even as individuals) as “at rest”. By that, I don’t mean to imply we’re stagnating or resting, but that we aren’t compelled to overcome our natural inertia on various topics. Most of the time we’re okay continuing along the path of least resistance defined by someone else’s beliefs.
But often, when we truly believe in something, that changes. Our beliefs lead us to newer actions. And those actions end up taking us to places and people at odds with our beliefs.
Perhaps your colleague disagrees with the strategy to grow a product that you spent the last week deliberating over. Or maybe your parents disagree with your decision to put off further studies for the next few years. Could be your partner disagrees with your decision to quit your job and write a book.
These are all made up situations, but they all lead to something most of us view as negative or harmful: conflict.
If beliefs are the force that move us from rest and take us in newer directions, conflict is what happens when we come up against the constraints of growth and discovery. The “feedback loop” provided by conflicts allows our beliefs and actions to be tested, honed, and finalised.
As writers and journalists, we’re taught to work hard to identify the conflicts in a story right at the onset. It’s what propels the best stories, whether fiction or nonfiction.
Conflict is as important in our workplaces too.
Better work outcomes. When you and your coworkers push one another to continually ask if there’s a better approach, that creative friction is likely to lead to new solutions. “Conflict allows the team to come to terms with difficult situations, to synthesize diverse perspectives, and to make sure solutions are well thought-out,” says Liane Davey, cofounder of 3COze Inc. and author of You First: Inspire Your Team to Grow Up, Get Along, and Get Stuff Done. “Conflict is uncomfortable, but it is the source of true innovation, and also a critical process in identifying and mitigating risks.” And there’s rarely a fixed amount of value to be gained in a disagreement. If you and your colleague are arguing about the best way to roll out a new initiative — he wants to launch in a single market first and you want to enter several at one time — you’ll be forced to explore the pros and cons of each approach and ideally find the best solutions.
Opportunities to learn and grow. As uncomfortable as it may feel when someone challenges your ideas, it’s an opportunity to learn. By listening and incorporating feedback, you gain experience, try new things, and evolve as a manager. When a peer chews you out after an important presentation because you didn’t give her team credit for their work, the words may sting, but you’re more likely to think through everyone’s perspectives before preparing your next talk.
Improved relationships. By working through conflict together, you’ll feel closer to the people around you and gain a better understanding of what matters to them and how they prefer to work. You’ll also set an important precedent: that it’s possible to have “good” fights and then move on. My 10-year-old daughter knows this intuitively. She once came back from a sleepover with her close friend, and when I asked her how it went, she said, “Great. We fought the whole time.” I pressed her about how it could’ve been fun when they were arguing. She said, “Because we got over it and now we’re BFFs.”
Why We Should Be Disagreeing More at Work by Amy Gallo, [Harvard Business Review](https://hbr.org/2018/01/why-we-should-be-disagreeing-more-at-work)
I am aware that [avoidance of conflict](https://www.nbcnews.com/think/opinion/ghosting-quiet-quitting-re-avoiding-conflict-s-not-healthy-rcna64709) is itself one of the markers of intergenerational conflict between, say, baby boomers and Gen X versus Millennials and Gen Z. Yet, my advice to my younger colleagues (I am sadly Gen X) is to consciously seek out and embrace conflict for projects or matters where they have strong beliefs.

That was Aneesh Reddy, the co-founder of Capillary Tech, on the First Principles podcast last week. Aneesh talked at length about starting up, designing a SaaS business model, and expanding Capillary Tech—across a conversation that spanned two episodes, [Part 1](https://the-ken.com/podcasts/first-principles/aneesh-reddy-of-capillary-tech-on-his-wins-mistakes-and-a-breakout-saas-model/) and [Part 2](https://the-ken.com/podcasts/first-principles/part-2-aneesh-reddy-of-capillary-tech-on-how-being-called-a-bully-led-him-to-be-a-better-leader/?utm_source=web&utm_medium=nl&utm_campaign=fp_pod2).
If you listen closely, you’ll notice that Aneesh has a tendency to imagine the life of his company in cycles, or phases. He dreams in instalments, he says, even compares starting up to running a marathon: you begin with momentum, you slow down, and then speed up when the finishing line is close.
It is one of those more difficult phases of Capillary Tech and Aneesh’s life as a founder that he breaks down in our latest episode. A time when both his co-founders had left. A time of success and excess. But every winter promises to thaw into spring, and Aneesh explains his own slow emergence from the cold into warmth.
There were two crucial interventions that he decided to make to get himself there: executive coaching and vipassana. Welcome to another issue of #SilentSunday photographs, it’s Jonathan again.
These past few months, I’ve tried my hand at being an amateur gardener, only to become something of a professional serial killer instead. Two Jasmines, two Neems, one Rose, countless Coriander seedlings, two Palms, and even a trencher full of Mint—which many gardeners consider a weed, and so very difficult to kill. Apparently not for me though, because I had them brown and wilting in less than two weeks.
This has been, to say the least, a bit depressing. And to place blame where it’s due, it’s almost entirely because my wife and I are new to balcony gardening and just didn’t know or research enough before buying any of them. How much to water each plant, what kind of soil they preferred, how much to fertilise, should you fertilise… we’ve been learning all of it on the go. Unfortunately for the plants, this game of Russian roulette hasn’t been a healthy one, and as the middle of January rolled around, I was certain none of them had survived—the distinct lack of leaves was a pretty good giveaway, I thought.
But I am, if anything, stubborn, so I didn’t stop watering those leafless sticks. For three weeks, I kept at it, until this Monday, I was rewarded with this:
To be clear, this is the only plant in our balcony that’s put out new shoots so far; I think my bumbling around has been fatal for the rest. But still, I don’t think I’ve ever been happier to see a leaf before.
That little shoot, to me, symbolises everything Spring.
Harsh cold, hard soil, hare-brained caretakers—all pale beside life’s natural tendency to grow. All it needs (and this applies to us humans, too) is for the right conditions to come around again. And voila: new growth!

Millennial productivity and the chicken-and-egg dilemma

Arundhati

Millennials have been held responsible for killing everything sacred, from soap to sex to sitcoms. (Here is the entire list https://www.marketwatch.com/story/here-are-all-of-the-things-millennials-have-been-accused-of-killing-2017-05-22). But there is a heavier cross for this crowd to now bear. The global working-age population has been held responsible for killing entire growth rates of economies. 

Columnist Vivek Kaul zeroes https://www.livemint.com/news/india/growth-is-falling-and-it-has-a-lot-to-do-with-the-youth-11595776626171.html in on why the youth are responsible for slowing global growth since 2008.

Between 1961 and 1990, the global working-age population (comprising people in the 15-64 age group) grew by 2.05% every year on average. In fact, in 25 of the 30 years, the working-age population grew by 2% or more. The economic growth during this era averaged 4.11% per year. In 1991, the growth of working-age population fell below 2% and has stayed like that ever since. This has primarily caused the slowing down of economic growth over the last three decades. The growth in the working-age population fell to 1% in 2017 and has stayed as is, since.

The growth in the working-age population between 1991 and 2019 has averaged 1.53% per year. During this period, global economic growth slowed down to 2.83% per year. Since the global recession of 2008, the working-age population growth has dropped to 1.21% per year with the economic growth slowing down to 2.5% per year.

Growth is falling and it has a lot to do with the youth, Mint https://www.livemint.com/news/india/growth-is-falling-and-it-has-a-lot-to-do-with-the-youth-11595776626171.html

The argument here is that if more people in the working-age group entered the workforce, they earn, spend, and keep the wheels of economies turning. 

But what if there aren't enough working-age people entering the workforce because there are simply not enough jobs? 

Andrew Van Dam of The Washington Post calls this a generation shaped by the recession. So much so that despite the gains made with better education, millennials come nowhere close to having the wealth or assets like homes that their previous generations did. 

A few brutal months of the coronavirus set the labor market back to the turn of the millennium. In April, the economy bottomed out with about as many jobs as in November of 1999. The economic regression to the Y2K era is a fitting symbol for a generation that — more than any other — has been shaped by the recession. Things improved in May, but the improvement just means we’re back to December 2000 levels of employment. For millennials who came of age then, it’s as if all the plodding expansions and job recoveries of their namesake epoch evaporated in weeks.

The unluckiest generation in U.S. history, The Washington Post

“The story here is not just that it’s a bad recession, and that it’s hitting young people more, but that it’s hitting people who have already been hit,” Gray Kimbrough, an economist at American University told The Washington Post.

“I have Rs 2 crore worth of inventory sitting with me because the buyer backed out. That is going to affect my topline, and in turn, affect my rating.”
This is a Tirupur garment manufacturer’s existential dilemma—borrow he must to keep the business going, but a downgraded rating will either raise the interest rate for him or limit his access to credit altogether. More than 50 million enterprises are grappling with this today.
Credit rating is overrated. Even more so for micro, medium, and small enterprises (MSMEs) in India. When the Supreme Court hears a case on the loan moratorium on 28 September, along with the plea to suspend interest on interest for the moratorium period, MSMEs are also seeking to stop their credit ratings from being downgraded.
Before Covid-19 killed 35% of MSMEs in June, mandatory credit ratings from external agencies had already pushed these businesses down a vicious spiral: Small size—> no money to seek ratings—> no formal credit—> ineligibility for the moratorium.
Which is why only ~300K of ~60 million MSMEs opted for the moratorium. And only 17% of $12.2 billion of bank credit disbursed in July went to MSMEs—a sector that accounts for 29% of the country’s GDP and half of India’s exports.
The pandemic is exacerbating historic inequalities. Could this be an opportunity to correct this historic aberration?
Arundhati, in her story, makes the case that external ratings for MSMEs are pointless. A loan reviewer at SBI, India’s largest public bank, was more with her: “The ratings are garbage.”
The regulator, SEBI, had asked rating agencies to not downgrade companies during the moratorium so they don’t end up with a default rating. But as the moratorium lifts and ratings re-start, MSMEs’ fear they’d be doomed to lower ratings.
Millions are looking forward to the Supreme Court’s ruling on Monday. Will the judgment give ratings the place many think they deserve—akin to just a ‘review by a movie critic’?

Habit is stronger than reason — George Santayana
 
In recent years, the European Union has caught up with the United States to emerge as India's largest trading partner. But, it has remained the third-largest market for the country's IT sector. It has been stuck at this rung for over a decade.
 
Even in third place, the EU accounted for only 11% of India's total IT exports for the year ended March 2020. And like many surprises the pandemic has sprung upon humans and businesses, some of the largest IT companies have figured that during the current quarter, revenue from Europe grew on constant currency whereas it remained flattish in North America.
 
For Indian IT, doing business with America has been a matter of habit. Most of them invested little organisational resources in building businesses beyond US' shores. Now, the American market poses challenges from multiple sides, says Kunal in today's story.
 
The managed services market in the US is shrinking. Most big businesses are moving to the cloud, where Indian IT faces off against giants like AWS and Azure (Microsoft). Instead of offshoring to Indian IT, large American companies are setting up their own captive centres in India. Importantly, President Trump's calls for protectionism don't seem to be empty rhetoric.
 
Indian IT needs to break its habit. The European market may be small; its countries more culturally diverse; its language barriers tall, and labour laws stringent. It also requires more commitment to workers' rights as compared to the US. Yet, it offers long-term potential.
 
Those like TCS and HCL have been ahead of others in their EU habit formation. In the early days of TCS, its sales team in European countries used to be 80% Indian and 20% local.
 
Today, it's 80% local. Around 35% of its on-site team are locals too. Unsurprisingly, TCS claims to be having more mature discussions with enterprises there and says it has found greater acceptance "to moving things offshore".

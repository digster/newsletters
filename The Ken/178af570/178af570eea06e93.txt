Good Morning Ishan,

Beauty is a beast, and the Korean beauty (K-beauty) space is especially humongous. Globally, K-beauty exports fetched a total of US$7.57 billion last year.

It is not hard to imagine why. Known for its ‘10-step’ skincare routines, one usually buys K-beauty products by the battalion—from cleanser to toner to sheet mask—to achieve the prized ‘glass-skin’ look. Even the ‘no-makeup’ trend is earning skincare companies some pretty pennies (the battle cry: “Moisturise, moisturise, moisturise!”)

What happens when the beast that is K-beauty meets the largest market in Southeast Asia, Indonesia? Instant chemical attraction.

K-beauty behemoths like Amorepacific (which runs Laneige and Innisfree) is salivating after the youthful Indonesian market that is already spending big on makeup, especially after South Korea’s diplomatic fallout with China (we wrote https://the-ken.com/sea/story/k-pop-took-centre-stage-in-se-asia-but-how-long-can-it-hold-it/ about how this affected the Korean craze in Southeast Asia previously). The country’s cosmetics and toiletries space is expected to be a US$7 billion industry by 2023.

Indonesians, on the other hand, are on board the 'chok-chok' train—that’s K-beauty lingo for a dewy complexion. The import of K-beauty products in Indonesia rose 12-fold in a span of five years, while local makeup brands like House of Hur and Mireya battle a war of K-beauty mimicry. Investors, meanwhile, are piqued.

Dita’s story today explores the true colours of an industry with an identity crisis. It is a lucrative market contoured by “local products are trying to capture the K-beauty market; the K-beauty brands are tapping into the halal segment; all the while the number of brands continue to grow, with very little differentiation to offer against existing products.”

Despite that, as one local brand put it, “the industry is looking very sexy”.

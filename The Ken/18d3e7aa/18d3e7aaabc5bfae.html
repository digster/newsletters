Back in 2020, speculation was rife that Flipkart would lead Indian internet companies’ entry into public markets with an IPO in the US.

Just as the e-tailer had made it possible for a lot of them to, dare I say, even exist. It’s hard to imagine India’s startup scene without Flipkart’s emergence in 2007.

But Flipkart is still privately held, even as younger tech peers like Zomato, Paytm, and Nykaa capitalised on the bull run in the stock market in 2021 to take their shares public.

That said, a lot of what the Walmart-owned company expects of its employees is still informed by its desire to expand its business and cut costs to make that much-awaited listing happen.

Those expectations have proved to be too much for many, though. Across product categories, Flipkart grew 20% in 2023. But that was still only half of CEO Kalyan Krishnamurthy’s target.

To make matters worse, for mid- and senior-level employees used to pay raises, sometimes even three times a year earlier, not getting one for two years has been hard to stomach.

But not as hard as being laid off, which is reportedly in the offing for 5-7% of Flipkart’s 22,000 employees.

As a category manager put it, Flipkart was a “dream workplace” in 2019. “Now, a war is brewing. They say everything is fair in love and war. When you go into war, you only come back after winning it.”

What’s implied is there will be casualties, too.

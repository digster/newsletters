What is arguably the “most successful” edtech in India?

No, not that one. Or that other one that raised billions and bought everything that moved. This one is less conspicuous; so much so that most people outside medical colleges have never heard of it.

It’s called Daily Rounds, and its main product, Marrow, helps students prepare for Neet PG. Just that. It’s not for UPSC or JEE, which millions fight for every year. It’s not even for “learn AI in six weeks”. It’s just one exam that gets about 200,000 candidates every year.

And somehow, that’s enough.

In FY25, Daily Rounds claims to have made roughly Rs 340 crore in profit on revenue of Rs 590 crore. That’s nearly a 60% margin in edtech, a sector where profits are usually discussed in the future tense. Physicswallah has managed profitability once; others like Unacademy’s Prepladder, even at over Rs 100 crore in revenue, are still losing money. So, what’s going on?

The short answer: it made a set of deeply unfashionable choices, and they worked. For instance, it relied on the most powerful marketing channel: seniors telling juniors, “Use this.” It also helps that Daily Rounds had an earlier hit that built trust and a massive user base even before it could roll out its latest model.

But the model only works as long as Neet PG does. And that’s no longer a given. The government is actively pushing a more practical, clinically oriented replacement exam. One that could shift preparation back into medical colleges and, in the process, make coaching apps far less central and far less valuable.

Atul’s story today looks at how Daily Rounds avoided the usual edtech traps and at how policy, not competition, could be what finally breaks this model.

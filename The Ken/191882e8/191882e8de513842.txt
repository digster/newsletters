1. Be anti-TAM

I’ll start with a confession. The Ken did not have a clue about its “TAM https://www.cognism.com/what-is-total-addressable-market”—Total Addressable Market—when we started out. 

TAM is the ultimate metric that helps entrepreneurs, businesses, and most importantly, investors figure out “how big” you can grow

Nearly eight years later, we still don’t know what our TAM is. But perhaps more importantly, we don’t care. And we’ve been both lucky and privileged to find multiple investors along our entrepreneurial journey who didn’t care too.

In creative professions and startups, you could have all the world as your TAM, but if you do not have the people, passion, and ambition, it’s all quite pointless. TAM is not just useless for us, it’s actively harmful. Because it creates this illusion of opportunity to chase. 

Instead, we realised after the first 3–4 years (paved with numerous mistakes and significant costs) that we were never a top-down company, but instead a bottom-up and inside-out one.

From individuals.

Looking back at the things we’ve done over the years, I’ve realised that the biggest determinant of success was the interest of the individual driving a product.

Did they like doing it? Were they challenged by it? Did they want to learn and improve themselves in the pursuit of success in the direction of the product? Did they enjoy it? Did they want to stick with it till they succeeded? Did they believe in it more than their colleagues? Do they have a point to prove to the world?

When you start with individuals like these, it’s virtually impossible for rivals to compete. Because it doesn’t matter how much money or how many people you throw at the problem, individual interest, passion, and ambition are virtually unbeatable over the medium to long term.

It starts small, but over time it compounds.

In the startup space, perhaps the best articulation was provided by Y Combinator co-founder Paul Graham in a seminal 2013 essay called “Do things that don’t scale https://paulgraham.com/ds.html”.

One of the most common types of advice we give at Y Combinator is to do things that don’t scale. A lot of would-be founders believe that startups either take off or don’t. You build something, make it available, and if you’ve made a better mousetrap, people beat a path to your door as promised. Or they don’t, in which case the market must not exist.

Actually startups take off because the founders make them take off. There may be a handful that just grew by themselves, but usually it takes some sort of push to get them going. A good metaphor would be the cranks that car engines had before they got electric starters. Once the engine was going, it would keep going, but there was a separate and laborious process to get it going.

Do things that don’t scale https://paulgraham.com/ds.html

Another wonderful anti-TAM essay, and the starting block of many great startups or entrepreneurs, is Kevin Kelly’s 2008 essay, “1000 True Fans https://kk.org/thetechnium/1000-true-fans/”

Perhaps some of you are thinking this reminds you of the Japanese concept of Ikigai https://www.japan.go.jp/kizuna/2022/03/ikigai_japanese_secret_to_a_joyful_life.html? Aligning your interests and skills with your profession? Specifically, this Venn diagram?

I hate to tell you this, but not only is this approach wrong, it’s not even Ikigai https://ikigaitribe.com/ikigai/ikigai-misunderstood/.

Like TAM, this Venn diagram is also a top-down way of looking at this. “If you can situate yourself at the intersection of these four circles, then life will be good.”

But that isn’t Ikigai https://ikigaitribe.com/podcasts/podcast06/.

Starting small

Ken mentions in his book the five pillars of ikigai: starting small, releasing yourself, harmony and sustainability, the joy of little things, and being in the here and now.

The first pillar is “starting small”, and Ken associates it with the concept of kodawari, an approach where craftspeople take extraordinary care of the fine details of their work — people can achieve their goals by taking small steps.

Japanese people do understand that there is no way to attain perfection, but they still appreciate the effort that one nevertheless puts in to always striving to be better. The important thing is the process — trying to attain their goal and learning from their attempts to do so.

[…]

The joy of the little things

The fourth pillar is “the joy of little things.”

“No matter where you are in the world, if you make a habit of having your favourite things sooner after you get up, for example, chocolate and coffee, dopamine will be released in your brain, reinforcing actions getting up prior to the receipt of the reward.”

Ken proposes starting the day with some ikigai and getting into the habit of using the little things to create pleasurable, rewarding experiences that facilitate contentment. With these embedded in one’s routine, it doesn’t matter if people get recognized for their performance; no matter what, they have still experienced moments of happiness.

Rock Star Neuroscientist, Ken Mogi’s 5 Pillars of Ikigai https://ikigaitribe.com/podcasts/podcast06/

It keeps coming back to you. To your interests. To what makes you tick. To what makes you, you.

Your interest is a very reliable lead indicator of your chances of success.

Therefore, instead of the classic innovation funnel that starts wide and narrows down to find focus over time, we prefer at The Ken to implement a reverse funnel.

One that starts very narrow, usually from just a colleague wanting to do something that excites them.

We encourage them to wear blinkers and ignore things that are in the future. We let them build things that may not look scalable, or perhaps don’t have an obvious business model.

Almost always, they expand their horizon and ambition as they go along. TAM be damned.

First Principles https://the-ken.com/newsletters/first-principles/ is just one example of that at The Ken, but we have many more like Visual Stories https://the-ken.com/visual-stories/, Learning https://the-ken.com/learning/, Ka-Ching! https://the-ken.com/ka-ching/stories/, Daybreak https://the-ken.com/podcasts/daybreak/, and so many yet to be born.

5. Abstraction ⇔ Ambiguity

Hello Ishan, it’s Jonathan here with another issue of First Principles Silent Sunday, your weekly treat of photos served up entirely by the First Principles community. 

I’m a sucker for abstracts. At least, I’ve become one these past few years. When I was about a decade or so younger, all the squiggly lines and the blurred subjects and the (to me) incomprehensible treatment of everyday things just came across as pretentious. 

“Sure, it’s pretty. But what’s the point?!” my art-critic voice would go in my head. 

I guess you’re just so darn certain about everything in your teens and your early twenties that something whose entire raison d’etre is to throw ambiguity in your face feels like an attack. So I punched back. 

“These modern-art chaps just draw a couple of lines and some funky dots to con rich people who don’t know what to spend their money on.”

That was when I was feeling wordy. Other times, it was “seizure-inducing”. Or just “meh”.

Well, my teenage self has been eating crow these past few years because all it took was some time out in the real world for my certainties to be shown up as immature and lazy. Very few things in life are certain, after all. 

By now, I’ve learned to embrace the ambiguities around us. I’ve even learned to love them, for all the myriad possibilities they hide behind their mystifying facades. 

I now adore abstract art for the same reason I hated it before—it makes me stop and ponder. 

Let me just quote from psychologist, writer, and photographer John Suler’s book on photography and the human psyche:

People who truly love abstract images will often tell you that figuring it out is not the objective. In fact, they may not even try or want to know. They take delight in its ambiguity. They immerse themselves into the purely perceptual experience of the colors, textures, and patterns. They like to immerse themselves into the visceral sensations, moods, and primeval ideas aroused by the image. They don’t “think” about the image per se, but rather use intuition to sense its meaning and impact. Does it create sensations of power, mystery, love, loneliness, grief, pain, joy? Is it the essence of sharp, hard, smooth, brittle, hot, cold. sweet, bitter, loud, or soft? It touches them and moves them on a purely intuitive level that includes what they taste, touch, smell and feel, as well as see. It’s not about a particular person, scene, or thing that is brittle, lonely, mysterious, or cold, but rather about the pure abstract idea and the resulting gut-level experience of those qualities.

Piet Mondrian, a famous non-representational artist, said that “Every true artist has been inspired more by the beauty of lines and color and the relationships between them than by the concrete subject of the picture.” Psychological research on people in altered states of consciousness, most notably psychedelic experiences, similarly reveals how they draw away from reality as we usually perceive it, and find themselves thrown into a joyful realization of the colors, patterns, and textures underlying reality. They get a glimpse into the pure essence of things. That’s what good abstract photography is about.

Photographic Psychology: Image and Psyche by John Suler

The reason I decided to talk about abstract photography in today’s issue is because I found a really beautiful example among our community contributions. It’s our top pick this week, from Shaurya Tyagi.

In any case, in 2024 it is possible to eat delicious food you didn’t make yourself, watch movies that have recently come out in the cinema, buy all manner of clothes, tools and fripperies, do the food shopping, speak to friends and family and earn a wage – all without ever leaving the house. Living a real, physical life outside the home is good because humans need friction. It’s time to retire the term “user” https://www.technologyreview.com/2024/04/19/1090872/ai-users-people-terms/ (April 19, 2024) – Taylor Majewski

People have been called “users” for a long time; it’s a practical shorthand enforced by executives, founders, operators, engineers, and investors ad infinitum. Often, it is the right word to describe people who use software: a user is more than just a customer or a consumer. Sometimes a user isn’t even a person; corporate bots are known to run accounts on Instagram and other social media platforms, for example. But “users” is also unspecific enough to refer to just about everyone. It can accommodate almost any big idea or long-term vision. We use—and are used by—computers and platforms and companies. Though “user” seems to describe a relationship that is deeply transactional, many of the technological relationships in which a person would be considered a user are actually quite personal. That being the case, is “user” still relevant?

[…]

Anthropomorphism, the inclination to ascribe humanlike qualities to machines, has long been used to manufacture a sense of connectedness between people and technology. We—people—remained users. But if AI is now a thought partner, then what are we?

Well, at least for now,we’re not likely to get rid of “user.” But we could intentionally default to more precise terms, like “patients” in health care or “students” in educational tech or “readers” when we’re building new media companies. That would help us understand these relationships more accurately. In gaming, for instance, users are typically called “players,” a word that acknowledges their participation and even pleasure in their relationships with the technology. On an airplane, customers are often called “passengers” or “travelers,” evoking a spirit of hospitality as they’re barreled through the skies. If companies are more specific about the people—and, now, AI—they’re building for rather than casually abstracting everything into the idea of “users,” perhaps our relationship with this technology will feel less manufactured, and it will be easier to accept that we’re inevitably going to exist in tandem.

What do I think is ambition (a good trait) but is actually envy (a terrible one)?

Are there things going well in my life today that I will look back on and wish I had quit while I was ahead?

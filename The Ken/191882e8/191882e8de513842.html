Dear Ishan, good morning. I’m so wedded to the concept of writing to you on a Sunday morning that I find it hard to accept that most of you read it at a different time. And, as our subscriber community has gotten more global, we aren’t just inhabiting different time zones, but also climate and weather zones.

This makes it harder for me to write. For instance, the last week in Bengaluru has seen some of the brightest and clearest skies, with the sun just shining down on you, literally.

I took this photo on Friday morning. I cannot describe how blue the skies were, how cool the air, how calming the sun’s rays, or how magnificent the airplane’s trail.

Yet, I also realise that most of you will be in different places, times, and seasons. I know this, but I also struggle to visualise or imagine it. If you have 30 seconds, tell me when you read First Principles.

Some of you might remember the mama-son dog duo I took to feeding some months ago. The pair has been inseparable since the time she had her last litter. I think she had three puppies, of which the two stronger ones went off on their own. The third was the runt of the litter, as they say.

One of his rear legs is smaller and less developed than the others, giving him a limp. Maybe that’s what prevented him from striking out on his own. Or maybe it’s because his mama decided to be around to help him survive.

Whatever the reason, the pair have been inseparable for the last 9–10 months. Here are the two of them from a few weeks ago (the white one is the mama).

View in Browser

Well, the mama got pregnant again, and she hasn’t been around for the last few days. I have a hunch she’s gone off somewhere nearby and safe, in the post-rain undergrowth, to give birth. And so the cycle starts again.

Meanwhile, her kid is now lonely for the first time in his life. The guy who’d quickly polish off his own bowl of food before trying to also eat out of his mama’s bowl, isn’t hungry enough to finish his own bowl. He looks into my eyes (yes, I know it’s the super evolutionary trick dogs mastered) as if asking me, will she come back?

I’ve been heartbroken each night on the way back home, after feeding him.

I hope the mama dog comes back. And this time round, I will try and call local volunteer organisations like CUPA to get her spayed and her son neutered. Even though I feel it would be a betrayal of their trust.

The last time, I kept thinking about the right window to get it done, afraid of what them being separated for a few days during the procedure might do to their bond.

If you have gotten your local street Indies spayed, please let me know what’s the ideal time or method. I’m confused because young puppies will once again be in the picture.

1. Be anti-TAM

I’ll start with a confession. The Ken did not have a clue about its “TAM”—Total Addressable Market—when we started out. 

TAM is the ultimate metric that helps entrepreneurs, businesses, and most importantly, investors figure out “how big” you can grow

Nearly eight years later, we still don’t know what our TAM is. But perhaps more importantly, we don’t care. And we’ve been both lucky and privileged to find multiple investors along our entrepreneurial journey who didn’t care too.

In creative professions and startups, you could have all the world as your TAM, but if you do not have the people, passion, and ambition, it’s all quite pointless. TAM is not just useless for us, it’s actively harmful. Because it creates this illusion of opportunity to chase. 

Instead, we realised after the first 3–4 years (paved with numerous mistakes and significant costs) that we were never a top-down company, but instead a bottom-up and inside-out one.

From individuals.

Looking back at the things we’ve done over the years, I’ve realised that the biggest determinant of success was the interest of the individual driving a product.

Did they like doing it? Were they challenged by it? Did they want to learn and improve themselves in the pursuit of success in the direction of the product? Did they enjoy it? Did they want to stick with it till they succeeded? Did they believe in it more than their colleagues? Do they have a point to prove to the world?

When you start with individuals like these, it’s virtually impossible for rivals to compete. Because it doesn’t matter how much money or how many people you throw at the problem, individual interest, passion, and ambition are virtually unbeatable over the medium to long term.

It starts small, but over time it compounds.

In the startup space, perhaps the best articulation was provided by Y Combinator co-founder Paul Graham in a seminal 2013 essay called “Do things that don’t scale”.

One of the most common types of advice we give at Y Combinator is to do things that don’t scale. A lot of would-be founders believe that startups either take off or don’t. You build something, make it available, and if you’ve made a better mousetrap, people beat a path to your door as promised. Or they don’t, in which case the market must not exist.

Actually startups take off because the founders make them take off. There may be a handful that just grew by themselves, but usually it takes some sort of push to get them going. A good metaphor would be the cranks that car engines had before they got electric starters. Once the engine was going, it would keep going, but there was a separate and laborious process to get it going.

Do things that don’t scale

Another wonderful anti-TAM essay, and the starting block of many great startups or entrepreneurs, is Kevin Kelly’s 2008 essay, “1000 True Fans”

Perhaps some of you are thinking this reminds you of the Japanese concept of Ikigai? Aligning your interests and skills with your profession? Specifically, this Venn diagram?

View in Browser

I hate to tell you this, but not only is this approach wrong, it’s not even Ikigai.

Like TAM, this Venn diagram is also a top-down way of looking at this. “If you can situate yourself at the intersection of these four circles, then life will be good.”

But that isn’t Ikigai.

Starting small

Ken mentions in his book the five pillars of ikigai: starting small, releasing yourself, harmony and sustainability, the joy of little things, and being in the here and now.

The first pillar is “starting small”, and Ken associates it with the concept of kodawari, an approach where craftspeople take extraordinary care of the fine details of their work — people can achieve their goals by taking small steps.

Japanese people do understand that there is no way to attain perfection, but they still appreciate the effort that one nevertheless puts in to always striving to be better. The important thing is the process — trying to attain their goal and learning from their attempts to do so.

[…]

The joy of the little things

The fourth pillar is “the joy of little things.”

“No matter where you are in the world, if you make a habit of having your favourite things sooner after you get up, for example, chocolate and coffee, dopamine will be released in your brain, reinforcing actions getting up prior to the receipt of the reward.”

Ken proposes starting the day with some ikigai and getting into the habit of using the little things to create pleasurable, rewarding experiences that facilitate contentment. With these embedded in one’s routine, it doesn’t matter if people get recognized for their performance; no matter what, they have still experienced moments of happiness.

Rock Star Neuroscientist, Ken Mogi’s 5 Pillars of Ikigai

It keeps coming back to you. To your interests. To what makes you tick. To what makes you, you.

Your interest is a very reliable lead indicator of your chances of success.

Therefore, instead of the classic innovation funnel that starts wide and narrows down to find focus over time, we prefer at The Ken to implement a reverse funnel.

One that starts very narrow, usually from just a colleague wanting to do something that excites them.

We encourage them to wear blinkers and ignore things that are in the future. We let them build things that may not look scalable, or perhaps don’t have an obvious business model.

Almost always, they expand their horizon and ambition as they go along. TAM be damned.

First Principles is just one example of that at The Ken, but we have many more like Visual Stories, Learning, Ka-Ching!, Daybreak, and so many yet to be born.

2. Is the golden era of (software) engineers over?

Praveen Gopal Krishnan, whose Saturday newsletter The Nutgraf is probably chapter 101 in The Ken’s anti-TAM product thinking book, is also my co-host on our weekly podcast, Two by Two.

This week, we spoke with Kailash Nadh, CTO of Zerodha, and Amod Malviya, co-founder of Udaan and former CTO of Flipkart, on the perfect storm facing software engineers.

Rohin: So everything that you took for granted during the past 15–20 years, that you could just blindly go do an engineering degree, blindly get a job, blindly get promoted, is no longer going to be true.

This incentive chain, what breaks it? Will it break? Will it change? Why will it change?

Amod: 100% it will break. See, every abstraction has a half-life, okay? So we are like, probably four half-life cycles into it.

3. First Principles Spotlight

After our 14 July edition, “Animals and choices”, I got this reply from K. Vijay, the founder of Meatright.in:

Your Sunday newsletter immediately resonated with me on 2 counts. When i used to stay in Chennai, my drive to office through the Porur traffic signal meant, everyday for 15 minutes i was witness to the brutality of chickens being handled without any care for its physical body and slaughtered without a faintest of attempt to give them a dignified death; and the slaughtering is in full view of the other still caged chickens who are awaiting their turn. Like you I turned vegetarian for some time but could not hold it long, yet I still yearned for meat from ethically reared and slaughtered animals.

The second count – yes this was not the way humans reared animals for meat throughout history; bar the last 70 years. It’s ironic that we have progressed the most in the last 70 years materialistically and even become more civilised as a society but have hardly given any heed to the well being of animals which nourish us. This somehow points to a collective moral hollowness of our society that I can’t exactly articulate (given my limited writing skills).

I wanted to make sure the meat I ate was from animals that had led a happy, healthy and natural life. It set me on an exploration path and in the process I learned a completely new industry existed away from the knowledge of most meat eaters. It’s called Factory farming of animals. It’s truly industrial scale cruelty on sentient beings.

I did not find any good options so I started meatright.in. Have I been able to tackle all the aspects of cruelty in the supply chain?  No. By my own standards a lot is yet to be achieved especially on the logistics front and on the mutton front. But I hope to get there in due time. On your scale of hundred I would say we are probably at 80 for chicken meat (not a scientifically derived number).

I’m constantly on the lookout for conscientious eaters.

Note: Broiler chickens are not available on our site. Broiler chickens cannot be humanely reared; they are a product of multiple rounds of selective breeding through artificial insemination. And they cannot be reared without antibiotics.

4. A gift list

Hello readers, and happy Sunday! It's Anushka here and I’m writing this little section on a Saturday morning—as I usually do—but this time, while in an autorickshaw with a friend. We’re on our way to have lunch and I haven’t planned my work schedule particularly well today. Hopefully, by the time we get there, I’ll be done. 

Thankfully, I know what I want to write to you about this week, because it’s something I’ve been thinking about for a while. The simple act of gifting books. 

In my day job, for five days of the week, I work (usually not in autos) as a producer on Two by Two, The Ken’s premium business podcast. You’ve probably been reading about it in this very newsletter for the last few weeks, and I hope we’ve been able to get you to listen to us. (If you haven’t, what are you waiting for? Check it out here.) 

We follow a weekly cycle for production—a largely repeatable loop with tasks and owners which helps us create episodes consistently. From ideation, guests, narratives, recordings to post production. One of the smaller, fun things we do is pick out a book to give to our guests at every recording, as a gift. It was a simple idea that Rohin suggested, and all our guests have always been pleasantly surprised by it. In fact, when we first started picking books, I actually came back to the list of book recommendations we’ve published in First Principles over the last year. 

But it makes you wonder. How do you really pick books to gift? Sometimes, you may know exactly what a person wants to read because they’ve been talking about it. But more often than not, you have to guess—if you like a book, does that mean they’ll like it too? Should you go for the popular bestsellers? Can you gift a book confidently without having read it yourself? 

We battle with these questions every week for Two by Two, but it’s always rewarding to find a great book. 

One of our repeat picks is a great little paperback called Ways of Seeing by John Berger—I’m sure some of you might have read it, or watched the splendid 1972 BBC documentary it’s based on. The book contains seven essays, of which three are entirely pictorial. It has an unusual font, unusual spacing, and has the distinct, strange vibe of not actually being a book. I think it’s been able to stay true to its original medium this way; it makes you feel like you’re reading a documentary. Most of our guests on Two by Two are veterans in business and tech. Ways of Seeing might seem like an unusual choice for this cohort, but our only parameter is that we want to find a book that makes people pause and think.

5. Abstraction ⇔ Ambiguity

Hello Ishan, it’s Jonathan here with another issue of First Principles Silent Sunday, your weekly treat of photos served up entirely by the First Principles community. 

I’m a sucker for abstracts. At least, I’ve become one these past few years. When I was about a decade or so younger, all the squiggly lines and the blurred subjects and the (to me) incomprehensible treatment of everyday things just came across as pretentious. 

“Sure, it’s pretty. But what’s the point?!” my art-critic voice would go in my head. 

I guess you’re just so darn certain about everything in your teens and your early twenties that something whose entire raison d’etre is to throw ambiguity in your face feels like an attack. So I punched back. 

“These modern-art chaps just draw a couple of lines and some funky dots to con rich people who don’t know what to spend their money on.”

That was when I was feeling wordy. Other times, it was “seizure-inducing”. Or just “meh”.

Well, my teenage self has been eating crow these past few years because all it took was some time out in the real world for my certainties to be shown up as immature and lazy. Very few things in life are certain, after all. 

By now, I’ve learned to embrace the ambiguities around us. I’ve even learned to love them, for all the myriad possibilities they hide behind their mystifying facades. 

I now adore abstract art for the same reason I hated it before—it makes me stop and ponder. 

Let me just quote from psychologist, writer, and photographer John Suler’s book on photography and the human psyche:

People who truly love abstract images will often tell you that figuring it out is not the objective. In fact, they may not even try or want to know. They take delight in its ambiguity. They immerse themselves into the purely perceptual experience of the colors, textures, and patterns. They like to immerse themselves into the visceral sensations, moods, and primeval ideas aroused by the image. They don’t “think” about the image per se, but rather use intuition to sense its meaning and impact. Does it create sensations of power, mystery, love, loneliness, grief, pain, joy? Is it the essence of sharp, hard, smooth, brittle, hot, cold. sweet, bitter, loud, or soft? It touches them and moves them on a purely intuitive level that includes what they taste, touch, smell and feel, as well as see. It’s not about a particular person, scene, or thing that is brittle, lonely, mysterious, or cold, but rather about the pure abstract idea and the resulting gut-level experience of those qualities.

Piet Mondrian, a famous non-representational artist, said that “Every true artist has been inspired more by the beauty of lines and color and the relationships between them than by the concrete subject of the picture.” Psychological research on people in altered states of consciousness, most notably psychedelic experiences, similarly reveals how they draw away from reality as we usually perceive it, and find themselves thrown into a joyful realization of the colors, patterns, and textures underlying reality. They get a glimpse into the pure essence of things. That’s what good abstract photography is about.

Photographic Psychology: Image and Psyche by John Suler

6. Sunday reads and links

I’ve got quite a few reads for you today, many of which I’ve been saving up for a few weeks.

The first set of reads have to do with one of most powerful human abilities, and how Ozempic and a rash of GLP-1/semaglutide drugs are set to make it exponentially more common and accessible.

I’m talking about self-control.

Self-Control as a Performance-Enhancing Drug

…self-control measured in childhood correlates with a range of important adult life outcomes. Long story short, self-control is an all-purpose good like IQ: It predicts health, wealth, and all things good.

The Nature and Nurture of Self-Control

…self-control is around 60% heritable – that is, genes explain roughly 60% of the differences between individuals in their levels of self-control.

But what is self-control? And how do those who have it, wield it?

The secret life of people with high self-control (it’s easier than you think)

“People with high levels of trait self-control are good at avoiding temptation — not resisting it.”

[…]

“To be sure, avoiding temptation is itself an act of self-regulation, indeed one requiring forethought, effective anticipation, and self-knowledge. — — In a sense, avoiding temptation is a meta-regulation strategy that enables the self-regulator to manage self-regulatory resources effectively. By avoiding temptations, one can save oneself the presumably greater expenditure of willpower that would be necessary to resist them, thereby putting oneself less often into a depleted and vulnerable state.”

The people who seem good at self-control are probably just good at anticipating potential self-control failure and doing things to prevent it. They’re acing meta-self-control.

And some other great reads.

The introverts are winning

In any case, in 2024 it is possible to eat delicious food you didn’t make yourself, watch movies that have recently come out in the cinema, buy all manner of clothes, tools and fripperies, do the food shopping, speak to friends and family and earn a wage – all without ever leaving the house. Why should we, then? What’s in it for us?

There are a number of ways to answer those questions, not all of which will appeal to everyone, but it is worth setting them out. Living a real, physical life outside the home is good because humans need friction. Convenience is alluring but it is dangerous, because getting used to it means forgetting that being alive isn’t meant to always be easy. We should run our errands in person and queue at the Post Office and eat in restaurants because it is good to remember that sometimes we have to wait around, or go to several shops because the first one didn’t have what we needed. Resilience is one of the most important traits a person can and should develop, and it works like a muscle. Glide effortlessly through life and, when something bad does happen, because it always will, you won’t know how to react.

It’s time to retire the term “user” (April 19, 2024) – Taylor Majewski

People have been called “users” for a long time; it’s a practical shorthand enforced by executives, founders, operators, engineers, and investors ad infinitum. Often, it is the right word to describe people who use software: a user is more than just a customer or a consumer. Sometimes a user isn’t even a person; corporate bots are known to run accounts on Instagram and other social media platforms, for example. But “users” is also unspecific enough to refer to just about everyone. It can accommodate almost any big idea or long-term vision. We use—and are used by—computers and platforms and companies. Though “user” seems to describe a relationship that is deeply transactional, many of the technological relationships in which a person would be considered a user are actually quite personal. That being the case, is “user” still relevant?

[…]

Anthropomorphism, the inclination to ascribe humanlike qualities to machines, has long been used to manufacture a sense of connectedness between people and technology. We—people—remained users. But if AI is now a thought partner, then what are we?

Well, at least for now,we’re not likely to get rid of “user.” But we could intentionally default to more precise terms, like “patients” in health care or “students” in educational tech or “readers” when we’re building new media companies. That would help us understand these relationships more accurately. In gaming, for instance, users are typically called “players,” a word that acknowledges their participation and even pleasure in their relationships with the technology. On an airplane, customers are often called “passengers” or “travelers,” evoking a spirit of hospitality as they’re barreled through the skies. If companies are more specific about the people—and, now, AI—they’re building for rather than casually abstracting everything into the idea of “users,” perhaps our relationship with this technology will feel less manufactured, and it will be easier to accept that we’re inevitably going to exist in tandem.

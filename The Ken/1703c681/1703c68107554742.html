It's hard to look at hospitals and think they aren't making money hand over fist. The expensive equipment, the high-paid doctors, the legions of support staff, and the eye-popping bills we've all been on the receiving end of—all make it seem like a certainty.
 
The reality, at least in modern India, isn't quite that rosy. Government policies, coupled with the dwindling base of cash-paying patients, have left hospitals with shrinking margins and cash flow problems. Hospitals are struggling. Many smaller ones are desperately seeking buyers.
 
In all of this, rural India is the worst affected. If hospitals are struggling to cut it in major cities, why would they bother expanding to rural India? Large chains like Narayana Health have even chosen to expand overseas rather than within the country.
 
The Union government hoped its flagship health insurance scheme—Ayushman Bharat—would help the situation. By boosting the healthcare spending power of 40% of the population, it would incentivise hospitals to expand.
 
That hasn't happened. 
 
So, in the latest Union Budget, the Indian government decided to intervene. It would make a significant contribution towards the development of private hospitals in India's poorest districts through viability gap funding (VGF).
 
On the face of it, this is great news. After all, a quarter of deaths in India happen due to a lack of medical attention. But is VGF really a silver bullet for rural India's healthcare woes? Hardly, indicate various stakeholders in today's story. In it, Ruhi explores the various reasons—from low spending power to a lack of specialists—that ensure hospitals in rural India remain unviable.

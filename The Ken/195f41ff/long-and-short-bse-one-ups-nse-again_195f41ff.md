Long and Short
Wed, 02 Apr 25
The story behind the numbers and motivations driving India's stock market..
Good Morning Ishan,
The contrast is stark, the irony deep.
Since September—when the crazy Indian stock-market bull run finally started feeling gravity—stock-exchange BSE’s scrip has shot up more than 50% on a bourse. That bourse is its only rival and 800-pound gorilla, the National Stock Exchange of India (NSE). Meanwhile, the stock of NSE, looking to list for the longest time, has fallen over 13% since late 2024 on unlisted platforms.
In the battle of the bourses, eternal challenger BSE is getting the better of near-monopoly and eternal listing-hopeful NSE.
You may have seen this coming. In November, I [wrote](https://the-ken.com/story/nses-monopoly-was-unshakeable-until-now/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter) about how NSE’s monopoly is on borrowed time as market regulator Sebi squeezed its star player—options trading. More specifically, the trader-favourite Bank Nifty weekly contract. Most of the derivatives trading in the country happens on the NSE, and the regulatory crackdown could wipe out 30% of the stock exchange’s volumes in this segment, with BSE gaining at its expense, the story had anticipated.
“The rejig gives BSE a chance at getting to a 50% market share in weekly-options-contract volumes,” a capital-market analyst told The Ken. Currently, the bourse’s share is about 10%. Even if BSE’s share goes up to 25–30%, that’ll be a big dent in a key business that has been driving NSE’s monopoly so far.”
— NSE’s monopoly is on borrowed times as Sebi squeezes its star player, [The Ken](https://the-ken.com/story/nses-monopoly-was-unshakeable-until-now/?searchTerm=nse)
The story also said that the NSE stock’s dream run in the unlisted market could come crashing. It had more than doubled over a year buoyed by big profits and renewed hopes of a long-pending IPO, but was oblivious to the big business risks ahead.
That seems to be playing out.
Options volumes have [crashed](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/nithin-kamath-81136242_the-markets-are-finally-correcting-given-activity-7301165452821487616-U_X9/?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAACtuX6AB-Ca3G_x0LuF7wjgALy6DNm52XdQ) by over 40% since November, with NSE bearing the brunt of it.
Over the past month, the momentum—away from NSE and in favour of BSE—got a boost. More so over the past week.
In February, NSE seemingly tried to undermine BSE’s derivatives volumes by moving its expiry from Thursday to Monday; just before the latter’s Tuesday expiry. This was to kick off on 4 April. In response, the BSE stock fell sharply.
But last week, Sebi’s [consultation](https://www.sebi.gov.in/reports-and-statistics/reports/mar-2025/consultation-paper-on-the-final-settlement-day-expiry-day-for-equity-derivatives_93087.html) paper nixed the move. Without naming NSE, the regulator pulled it up saying: “One of the exchanges has now proposed to change the final settlement day to Monday.”
Sebi wants expiries of all equity-derivatives contracts on an exchange to be uniformly limited to either Tuesdays or Thursdays. This prompted NSE to put a pause on its proposal, and like a seesaw on a playground, the BSE stock sprung right up.
“[Sebi’s proposal] may abate concerns around BSE’s potential loss of market share…” said research house Jefferies.
But this isn’t the first time that NSE tried to undermine BSE, without success.
In 2023, as demand for Sensex options contracts increased, NSE tried to undermine BSE by moving the expiry day of the Bank Nifty contract to Fridays—the same day as Sensex—only to be compelled by Sebi into a reversal, market insiders had told The Ken.
For years, Sebi sought to dilute NSE’s outsized dominance and it seems to have ring-fenced [BSE](https://the-ken.com/story/bse-was-almost-invisible-then-a-new-ceo-challenged-nses-monopoly/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter) yet again.
On its part, BSE pressed its advantage last Sunday by approving something that many investors love: a bonus shares issue. For every share held by its shareholders, the company is giving two bonus shares. The move will see its stock price adjust to a third, making it at least optically affordable for many more retail investors. It’s currently perched high at about Rs 5,450.
Meanwhile, NSE’s IPO jinx—since 2016—continues.
While the exchange issued bonus shares last year to make its shares seem affordable, and also recently eased its earlier-complicated share-transfer process, Sebi seems to have thrown a spanner in the works again. The regulator has reportedly [flagged](https://www.ndtvprofit.com/ipos/nse-ipo-delay-sebi-regulatory-issues-governance-concerns-exclusive) several issues including concerns related to the exchange’s internal processes and governance that could delay the IPO by another two years. Last week, Tuhin Kanta Pandey, the new Sebi chief, also [pointed](https://www.thehindubusinessline.com/money-and-banking/litigations-governance-issues-diversification-of-clearing-corporations-stall-nse-ipo-sebi-chief/article69390338.ece) to issues stalling the IPO.
NSE is said to have [sought](https://www.ndtvprofit.com/ipos/nse-seeks-regulatory-clarity-for-ipo-waiting-on-noc-from-sebi-sources#:~:text=The%20NSE%20had%20first%20sought,on%20the%20condition%20of%20anonymity.) a no-objection certificate again from the regulator, but it’s anyone’s guess when the IPO will see the light of day.
For now, BSE seems to have the upper hand. The stock has rallied on the back of a gain in the derivatives-trading market share. But that market itself has shrunk; and it may continue to, thanks to Sebi’s squeeze. That’s not good news for either NSE or BSE. Besides, the sharp rally in the BSE stock has taken its price-to-earnings to nearly 80X now—higher than before, and much ahead of NSE’s 48X. For all its travails, NSE is still far bigger and far more profitable than BSE.
If the valuation twain has to meet, the chances of BSE stepping down from here seem higher than of NSE climbing up.

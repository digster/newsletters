1. Lessons from Buffett

On Monday, 10 November, Warren Buffett wrote his final letter to the shareholders of Berkshire Hathaway. He is 95.

For a newsletter around First Principles thinking, it would be awfully remiss not to talk about the letter. But it also seemed borderline disrespectful to attempt to annotate or explain something that is so simple, powerful, and timeless.

The letter spoke to me of classic, old-fashioned values. Plainspeak. Friends and friendships. The opportunities from staying put. How serendipity requires time and how connections are revealed in retrospect. Of mistakes, humility, and humbleness. And loving what you do. Of children, even if they are beyond retirement age too. Of course, compounding. And working long hours from offices. Of aging and anti-ageism. Time intervals in decades. Making as few decisions as possible. FIRL, not FIRE. Wealth and envy. Perverse incentives. Nurturing and protecting your independence. Kindness. Pragmatic patriotism.

I will urge you to read the entire letter (it won’t take more than 5–10 minutes) before proceeding.

Warren Buffett’s final letter to the shareholders of Berkshire Hathaway Inc. (pdf)

Buffet started writing them in 1978, as a 48-year-old. He was around the same age then that I am today.

When we think of lives and careers these days, it’s easy to accept–even convince yourself–that by the time you will be 48, the best is behind you.

Buffett answers this towards the end of his letter.

One perhaps self-serving observation. I’m happy to say I feel better about the second half of my life than the first. My advice: Don’t beat yourself up over past mistakes–learn at least a little from them and move on. It is never too late to improve. Get the right heroes and copy them. You can start with Tom Murphy; he was the best.

Remember Alfred Nobel, later of Nobel Prize fame, who–reportedly–read his own obituary that was mistakenly printed when his brother died and a newspaper got mixed up. He was horrified at what he read and realized he should change his behavior.

Don’t count on a newsroom mix-up: Decide what you would like your obituary to say and live the life to deserve it.

How many 48-year-old Buffets might be getting started today? Imagine the scope of possibilities and opportunities if they think about what they want to do over the next 47 years.

Perhaps you’re not career-minded. What do you think might be the “peak” of emotional satisfaction for you? One of the world’s pre-eminent experts on this question is Professor Laura Carstensen at Stanford University.

We’ve done a major longitudinal study looking at this, and it looks like the 20s are the worst. Then you begin to see some reduction in negative emotion, and it’s really going down by your 40s and 50s. The 60s and 70s are the peak of life, emotionally speaking. It doesn’t get worse significantly as you get older than that, but that’s where it kind of levels off—so the major bang-for-the-buck change is between 40 and 60.

I’ll leave you with this WSJ article from 2018, which has been a favourite of mine for years: “What is the Perfect Age?”

If you had a pill that could stop biological aging in its tracks, when would you take it?” asks Dr. Olshansky, a professor at UIC School of Public Health in Chicago.

He asks his university students this question. Many think 30 is old, so they would take the pill in their 20s. He asked his father, then 95 years old. His father said 50 was the best year because the kids were grown and he was in good health. Dr. Olshanky, 63, says life is good for him now, but if he had to pick a perfect year, it would probably be 50, too, because that was before he started having little aches and pains.

2. Theme of the month: Mythology

Hello everyone, this is Tanim, and this week we are taking a glimpse into the world of mythology. 

Myths are rooted in our cultural tapestry. Greek myths, for instance, have been such a profound influence that we use their references in our everyday speech–phrases like “Achilles’ heel,” “Pandora’s box,” “Trojan horse,” or “playing with fire,” and the list goes on. In fact, this Thursday, we published a story that was titled, “Why Prosus boarded Ixigo’s Ship of Theseus.” (Side note: you should definitely watch the movie of the same name by Anand Gandhi.) 

As Joseph Campbell described in his interviews with Bill Moyers, “Myths are clues to the spiritual potentialities of the human life.” He emphasises that myths represent how humans “experience” life. But what happens when the spiritual lens through which our parents understood the world is fragmented for us? Campbell’s answer is to turn to reason:

…this is the ground of what the myth is to be. It’s already here: the eye of reason, not of my nationality; the eye of reason, not of my religious community; the eye of reason, not of my linguistic community. Do you see? And this would be the philosophy for the planet, not for this group, that group, or the other group.

Hard disagree. Experiencing life isn’t a purely “reasonable” thing, especially when we are so different. Can we truly appeal to reason for the Palestinian genocide to end? Can we appeal to reason to stop people from littering? In a post-truth society, reason cannot be the main character. So, how do we relate to each other? 

By engulfing ourselves in a hyperreal world, one caught in an infinite loop of self-reference–a phenomenon we clearly see in memes. This includes not just internet memes, but also the original psychological concept: a unit of practice or belief through which a society or culture evolves and that passes from one generation (or even one person) to the next. 

Memes are the new myths of this generation. As with mythology, their motto is “same characters, different contexts”. All Ramayanas have Sita and Ram, but how we understand their relationship is a reflection of our own experiences. It is true that we experience life through myths, but our experiences also shape them in turn. The fact that three hundred Ramayanas were created in a pre-internet society, where communication and adaptation happened at a slower pace, is a testament to this. Today, we see thousands of memes built from a single template, each one carrying a fragment of its creator’s experience, meant to be shared and understood by someone else feeling the same way.

The most fitting way to enjoy and pay tribute to mythology has been savoured by writers from Euripides and Sophocles to Vaishnavi Patel, among many others. Patel’s book, Kaikeyi, is a reimagination of its namesake’s narrative. Fluid writing meets fluid mythology in this rendition of the Ramayana. Subversive in nature, the plot frames Kaikeyi as a misunderstood woman who had to do what she did for the “greater good”. It’s a lesson on textbook feminism, as Patel draws references from various instances where she felt women suffered injustice. Her larger point is more or less a variation of a critique like this: 

…I knew there was no redemption for Ahalya—the gods would help Indra but never a woman who had slept with another man.

I love a good retelling, and this one was tethering on perfection until I noticed that Patel was subtly suggesting that religion and technology cannot co-exist. In making the black sheep grey, she ended up painting the rest of the sheep in stark black and white. In some ways, technology has always challenged mythology by trying to excavate its origin point, so I understand the sentiment. But hey, not every book needs to align with every one of my viewpoints for me to enjoy it. 

If you also enjoy a good reimagining, here are a few recommendations from our readers for this month’s theme.

Parvinder Singh recommends Lanka’s Princess by Kavita Kane. 

“Lanka’s Princess by Kavita Kane retells the Ramayana from Surpanakha’s perspective, showing her as a misunderstood woman rather than a villain. The book explores the complex, often strained relationships between Ravana and his siblings, revealing emotional depth and family dynamics that challenge traditional portrayals.”

Parashuram Joshi recommends Parva by S L Bhyrappa 

View in Browser

“It’s a Mahabharata story from the points of view of Kunti and Draupadi. Explores the depth of both characters and how each was a core to the Pandavas–Kunti in raising the kids and Draupadi in keeping them together. It also explores the relationship between Draupadi and Bhima, highlighting her vow not to braid her hair until the war was complete. When Bhima and Arjuna’s sons Ghatotkacha and Abhimanyu respectively died in the battle, Draupadi was there to console them and stand by them.”

And if you want to go beyond the borders, Rachel, our ever-enthusiastic audio journalist, recommends The Poppy War by R F Kuang. 

“The Poppy War is one of the most harrowing books you’ll ever read. It’s a brutal examination of the toll of war and the way violence follows generations. Kuang doesn’t shy away from any details, making it a point to paint the gore and viscera that follow imperial projects and refusing to whitewash history. While the book is set in a fantasy version of ancient China, the military conflict it centres on is based on the Second Sino-Japanese war.

The book follows Rin, a young war orphan who joins a military academy to escape an arranged marriage, shortly before her country breaks into war. Her journey illustrates the corrupting influence of power, as her initial intentions to improve her standing in a society that has impoverished her slowly erode her morals and beliefs.

She soon discovers she doesn’t just have access to political and military power. The book’ magic system revolves around an intricate mythos woven by Kuang and inspired by various religions and pantheons, including Buddhism, Daoism, and Chinese mythology–mainly the four cardinal gods who guard each corner of the world. It turns out, Rin and a few other characters are Shamans. Especially when they’re high on opium (the book’s name is also a reference to the Opium Wars), they can channel divine powers, which they soon use for military advantage against the invasion. Of course, this just further adds to Rin’s corruption; the Phoenix God she channels craves destruction, and throughout the story, Rin has to ensure its voice doesn’t take over her own.

It’s a difficult read, but what I love about The Poppy War is the clear attention to detail and how textured the world is. The political intrigue and action are all well-paced and keep you hooked. Kuang also has the talent to make you truly care for characters who are doing terrible things. Even as your heart sinks with every terrible decision they make, you can’t help but hope that by the last page, they might find some form of redemption.”

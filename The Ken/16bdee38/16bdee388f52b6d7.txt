Good Morning Ishan,

I bought my house a few years back. The hunt was not a pleasant experience. But it was nothing compared to the nightmare that followed—furnishing and decorating. Or ‘making it into a home’ as the marketing tagline goes. Matching laminates with furniture, getting carpenters and electricians to just show up, all while trying to hold down a full-time job. That's a job for superheroes or interior designers as we call them.

It comes as no surprise then that furniture giant IKEA's first investment in India was in interior design startup Livspace. The interior market in India is estimated to be worth $25-$30 billion. And that's just the top ten cities. Livspace and its competitors have not even been able to capture 1% of the market. A huge opportunity then. But the same challenges, only this time at scale.

While it is easy to standardise the process—lease a factory line, have a set catalogue, fix a supplier base, etc—the human element remains the X-factor. Managing carpenters, plumbers, freelance designers and contractors is still a pain point. But, in the business of aesthetics, even standardised products are a problem. Everyone wants a different look, something unique.

This is where the IKEA-Livspace link-up shows promise.

“IKEA has huge data on consumer buying behaviour, Livspace has more India-specific data that will help them customise their offerings. IKEA also has a strong sourcing ecosystem; there could be synergies where IKEA introduces some of its sourcing partners to Livspace,” says one banker involved in the deal.

But IKEA itself hasn't quite lived up to the hype since it entered India last August. So, will the IKEA-Livspace combine get a leg up in the unorganised interiors design market? Or will their combined problems find a new home to roost?

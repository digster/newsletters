Good Morning Ishan,
 
Three years ago, a rag-tag, self-funded group of seven friends and family beat out 312 other teams, including government- and corporate-backed ones, to bring to life the tricorder. Yes, the fictional Star Trek device to diagnose illnesses and analyse atmospheric conditions.
 
Remarkably, the real-world tricorder, called DxtER and designed to self-diagnose 34 medical conditions, was ideated around the family kitchen table. It's a true against-the-odds story peppered with some good old jugaad, or Indian for 'necessity is the mother of invention.'
 
Now, facing the coronavirus pandemic, the Indian medical fraternity has also turned to jugaad to mass-produce ventilators. It's the need of the hour, given an estimated requirement of 110,000 to 220,000 by mid-May versus the current availability of about 40,000, and with imports all but impossible.
 
So what's happening? 
 
Well, the Indian Institute of Technology-Kanpur has zeroed in on two companies to build a prototype ventilator in a start-to-finish effort of a remarkable 10-14 days. Another company is modifying its ventilator, currently for purifying the air in labs, to make it usable in hospitals. One doctor has hit upon 3D-printing parts that can split one ventilator between multiple patients. And all of these efforts have three targets in mind: speed to market, capacity for mass production, cost less than existing options.
 
There are obstacles of course - working remotely, supply issues after the national lockdown, funding, testing. But true to jugaad, the fraternity is working around them. Even nobler, after relying on other open-sourced material for designs, some are committed to making their innovations free too.

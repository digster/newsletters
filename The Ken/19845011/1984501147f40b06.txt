Today’s edition of The Nutgraf is about some of the themes I cover in the event itself. At the rate that AI is changing things, the shelf life of a perceptive point one can observe about AI has collapsed to a few weeks. 

With all of those out of the way, let’s talk about last week, because in the world of AI, well, let’s say it was a week where several things clicked… all together. AI has always been moving at a pace of its own, but the past week was a time when several disparate companies all seemingly moved together in lockstep towards one inevitable destination—creating code. 

It all began with Lovable. The company hit a million dollars in annual recurring revenue (ARR) in just the eighth month. This made Lovable the fastest growing startup in history, surpassing other AI companies like OpenAI, Cursor, and the rest.

Impressive. 

In a move which I’m sure was a complete coincidence, last week, Microsoft CEO Satya Nadella announced a new extension to Github called Github Spark—this enabled anyone who wrote code to extend it to create apps and websites (Github calls this ‘Sparks’, but come on).

That wasn’t all. Another company which also made a big announcement last week was Figma. It rolled out its product Figma Make to all users—which is essentially a way for anyone to use Figma to design and build things using prompts. Figma is used by designers, and well, now Figma wants Figma to be used by… everyone.

In the post announcing the move, the company said https://www.figma.com/blog/figma-make-general-availability/, “With Figma Make, designers can test more complex interactions, engineers can create high-fidelity design artifacts, and product managers can create functional mockups to validate ideas. Now designers, engineers, and product managers can all make high-fidelity artifacts to bring to the team for a more informed discussion.” 

Joy.

Lovable, Figma, and Github Spark are all essentially stumbling into each other’s turf, extending all their functionalities to all users. Lovable starts with a prompt and ends with code. Figma Make, lets anyone use prompts to design. Github Spark helps anyone create mini applications using prompts.

That’s not all. Another company that wants all its users to create code and software and websites is… well, an unexpected one. 

Every business has different needs. Different teams. Different processes. Different ways of working. But most software doesn’t care.

All-in-one suites often force you into rigid templates and fixed workflows, built around someone else’s idea of how your business should run.

So you start filling the gaps. You buy a patchwork of point solutions for every edge case, each one disconnected from the next. You duct-tape together spreadsheets and forms. You beg engineering or IT to build something custom, only to watch it break the moment your org changes.

Every option is a compromise.

That’s why we built App Studio.

It’s a place inside Rippling where anyone across the company, from ops leads to HRBPs to finance analysts, can build high-impact applications on top of a foundation that already understands your business. No more brittle workarounds. No more rigid systems. 

Introducing App Studio: The end of one-size-fits-all software, Rippling https://www.rippling.com/blog/introducing-app-studio

I mean, come on!

Rippling is a SaaS company for HR people to manage things like payroll, HR, IT, etc. And now they are making their users create… apps, which is essentially software.

We now live in an era with a new and updated version of Zawinski’s law, which was observed by James Zawinski in 1995. The original version, written 30 years ago, was, “Every program attempts to expand until it can read mail. Those programs which cannot expand are replaced by ones which can.” It turns out that the 2025 version of this is that every program attempts to expand until it starts with a chatbox and ends with an application or software. 

All of this is both exciting and terrifying. In a world where all software can do pretty much the same thing, i.e., they all start at the same place and end at the same place, is there really going to be specialised software to do anything? 

And nobody is safe. 

Not even the world’s fastest growing startup.

But the real question is… with what?

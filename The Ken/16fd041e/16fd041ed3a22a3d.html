Good Morning Ishan,
 
There are some records worth celebrating and some that don't make any sense in the larger scheme of things. But then, in India, paradoxes abound.
 
In November 2019, an organ donation drive in India broke the Guinness World Record for most pledges—54,626 to be exact—in a single day. India also enjoys the honour of the second-highest number of transplants in the world.
 
The paradox: 500,000 Indians die every year, waiting in vain for an organ donor. 
 
The solution is in the hands of, ironically enough, not the living, but the dead. The living account for the majority of organ donations. However, it's retrieving organs from the dead that makes more sense. 
 
For medical reasons—specific organs, such as the heart and lungs, can only come from the deceased; and for commercial ones—heart and lung transplants can fetch hospitals up to seven times what a kidney transplant can.
 
The paradox: Only a handful of hospitals can do heart and lung transplants at scale in a country that is called the kidney bazaar of the world.
 
If by some miracle a donor is found, an endless list of factors pretty much puts paid to a fast and seamless process. Such as matching blood groups, size and weight, or checking the patient waiting list. Of course, this is without considering those who use "contacts" to climb the waiting list. 
 
And oh, India's track record in transplants splits the country in half. But you'll have to read Ruhi's story to see which half comes out on top—the north or the south. 
 
Her story is not just on problems, but also what authorities are doing to make sure India's newly minted world record translates into more than just Guinness Book value.

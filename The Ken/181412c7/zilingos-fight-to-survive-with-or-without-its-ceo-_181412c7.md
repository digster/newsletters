A celebrated founder at 25, a pariah and an ousted CEO at 30.
The high-decibel dismissal of Ankiti Bose at Zilingo, one of the most hailed and well-funded startups in the Southeast Asian region, has shaken many in the industry. Trial by media is on and multiple sides are leaking stuff to whoever can publish them.
Bose herself has been tweeting out screengrabs of documents besides selectively speaking to the press about what she calls a “witch hunt”. With US$300+ million already invested in the fashion tech startup that was last valued at US$970 million, investors are worried about their money as well as the damaged reputation.
In an interview with Ka Kay, writer of today’s story, Bose said, “Less than 10% of what actually transpired is out in the public eye.”
In keeping with the one-side-speak-at-a-time nature of this unfolding saga, Bose tells The Ken: “I want my shareholders to talk to me and ask me questions, and I will answer them.”
Let me just say our story today is not about the palace intrigue or how stakeholders at Zilingo are exchanging blows. It’s about the business that the Zilingo founders built, or claimed to build, and if and how that can be salvaged as the company navigates troubled waters.
In an email—seen by The Ken—co-founder and CTO Dhruv Kapoor confirmed that there are inbound M&A interests and that he believes the business just needs ~US$8 million for a turnaround.
Bose, who is out raising money to pay back Zilingo’s creditors, also believes the company has a future in its SaaS and enterprise business verticals, both being high-margin and high-retention.
Why then does the Board, apparently dominated by the largest shareholder Sequoia Capital India, want to liquidate the business? How much of the so-called accounting malpractice or financial irregularities, which were ostensibly known to investors, are actually red flags for the business' survival? Can a fund manager turn it around?

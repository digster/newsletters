October is an exciting month for those of us at The Ken. It’s our anniversary month; and this year, we turned four. We have something interesting cooking for you this month and the entire team is going the extra mile to deliver. Watch out for our email tomorrow morning.

This week also saw another anniversary. This Friday was Mahatma Gandhi’s 151st birth anniversary, a yearly holiday marked by politicians spinning the charkha—the spinning wheel—and giving out hearty messages.

But the fact is that Gandhi has increasingly become a figure that people don’t know what to make of. So much so that both ends of the political spectrum dislike him for their own reasons.

But I’d rather not get into those. Instead, I’ll mention something that Gandhi was, something that probably both sides will agree on.

Gandhi was imperfect. He made a ton of mistakes, some in his youth, others much later in life. He wrote about them, he displayed them, and he was at ease with them.

We, too, have become increasingly accepting of imperfections around us.

If you’re in the middle of a Zoom meeting and your kid or your dog interrupts it—it’s okay.

If you’re a budding content creator, you can have a YouTube or TikTok following, minus all the jazz that big studios bring with them. It’s okay. It’s even appreciated.

Imperfections often drive and enable businesses, just as they do individuals. This week had stories of many such imperfections driving businesses and the myriad ways in which companies dealt with them.

On Thursday, Pranav wrote abou https://the-ken.com/story/the-credit-card-up-ipo-hungry-mobikwiks-sleeve/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newslettert Mobikwik. Once one of the most promising fintechs in the country, it was disrupted and disrupted again. By policy and competition. Yet, the company hung on and now has disruption plans of its own (hint: credit cards).

Wednesday’s story https://the-ken.com/story/has-y-combinator-failed-in-india/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter was on an organisation that many starry-eyed entrepreneurs look at with awe—Y Combinator. Sumanth wrote about why the startup accelerator, responsible for many a unicorn (and dreams), hasn’t yet made it big in India. Despite investing in over 50 startups in India, it doesn’t have a single Indian unicorn in its lineup. Its impact on the Indian startup ecosystem, however, isn’t something to be scoffed at.

Shruti, on Monday, wrote about the aftershocks https://the-ken.com/story/the-fmcg-aftershocks-in-a-reliance-retail-future-group-earthquake/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter that Reliance Retail’s acquisition of Future Group left on the Indian FMCG sector. Though FMCG companies enjoy immense clout, it may not be as easy as before to navigate the retail sector due to Reliance’s newly acquired muscle. The company’s grocery store count has shot up from 800 to 2,000, and its revenue from the sector was up 57%.

On Tuesday, I wrote about https://the-ken.com/story/affle-rides-on-conversions-to-beat-the-adtech-ipo-curse/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter a company valued by the public markets at a billion dollars that few of us have heard of, but most have seen in action. Adtech firm Affle is behind the ads on many of your apps for the most prominent of brands. Yet, it’s treading on shaky ground, with an aggressive sales strategy that’s bringing in the bucks as opposed to tech. It’s now trying to change that.

Getting your brand online is huge for commerce. Ask Amazon. On Friday, Bhumika delved deep https://the-ken.com/story/shopify-shops-for-e-commerces-business-in-india/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter into Shopify, which has the potential to disrupt the growth of the biggest e-commerce companies in India. The company sets up and helps small businesses run their own online store; opting for a marketplace may place a big dent in their wallets. It’s not easy, though, even though it can be potentially more rewarding.

If you’ve not read Gandhi’s autobiography, The Story of My Experiments with Truth, give it a shot if you get the chance. He has been quoted to say—“Freedom is not worth having if it does not include the freedom to make mistakes.”

His autobiography reflects his quote in the fullest.

That’s all from us this week. Have a great Sunday.

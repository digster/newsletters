Good Morning Ishan,
One of the selling points of driving for Uber is that you get to work on your own terms, following your own schedule. So when the company launched a new line of business called employee transportation services (ETS), the move seemed remarkably un-Uber—it’s essentially a shuttle for enterprises in Mumbai, Pune, and Chennai to bus their staff into the workplace, with fixed routes, schedules, and pricing.
Uber has gone from disrupting taxi services around the world to entering the low-margin and operationally complex business of bussing people to work. And it wants to take on operators that have spent close to two decades doing this.
As it turns out, the idea is to let Uber drivers flip between B2C on-demand rides and B2B corporate trips. Drivers can earn more in between ETS pickups and drop-offs, and Uber can keep its B2C pricing low.
That means the unglamorous affair of moving workers to workplaces could become a crucial segment for Uber.

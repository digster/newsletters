Finance teams associate it with the tallying of accounting books at the end of a year, with the objective of trying to identify and resolve inconsistencies. The goal is to find differences.

If reconciliation is the process of finding and resolving differences, what does it mean to reconcile yourself?
I’ll let you soak in that question for a while.
For me, it means reflecting on the year gone by and trying to find major differences between what I wanted, what I did, and what made me happy. Regardless of our best-laid plans, life always has something different in store for us each year. Some years, it’s slightly different. Other years, dramatically so.
Most of us—me included—don’t give ourselves enough time to truly document these “differences”. Even when we do, we see them as “dissatisfaction”, not differences.
If I were to use a simplified explanation, we have ambitions and goals (professional or personal) at the start of a year. And results (or the lack of them) at the end. We see the differences between what we wanted and what we got as either satisfaction or dissatisfaction.
But if we dig a little deeper and add in a bit of reflection, it changes to this.
If that looks like an example of double-loop learning, then it’s because we have [talked about the concept earlier](https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/why-arent-you-changing-your-mind/).
An even deeper examination of this should lead us (hopefully) to the conclusion that every year, we change too. We evolve. So, why shouldn’t our reflection on the year gone by guide our own conscious evolution?
Why would we not use our learnings to decide how we want to change ourselves? It would be a wasted opportunity not to.
And that is the purpose of reconciliation for me. If I attained some things that I set out to achieve, but realized they did not really make me happy, should I continue wanting more of it?
Conversely, if I did not achieve what I wanted to, but in the process, found happiness through something else, should I change my goals to reflect that?
Reflecting and reconciling these differences between what we wanted, what we got, and what made us happy sets us up for annual cycles of evolution and growth. And not merely satisfaction or dissatisfaction.
What if we’ve been seeing it backward all this while? That yearend reflections are a way to figure out how to get what we want better. Instead, what if they are a way for us to better figure out who we are and what we want to be?
Put simply, changing merely your actions to better achieve your goals is a single-loop learning process.
But changing your goals and your own self each year to better achieve happiness is a double-loop growth process that you can apply year after year.
It is undeniable that we change each year. Why shouldn’t we be in control of that?

Dr Alexander Smalley, the lead author of this paper, believes that sunrises and sunsets both have a marked effect on the human psyche. The awe that they invariably provoke, he says, is quite effective at reminding us that we (and by extension all our seemingly insurmountable problems) are quite small and insignificant compared to the vastness of the universe.
…spotting a particularly special light show in an environment you frequent daily brings a dose of novelty to what’s otherwise familiar, Dr. Smalley says; this comes with its own benefits, like the release of dopamine in your brain and a related spike in mood and motivation.
Gazing at the sky on a regular basis can also pluck you out of your own little world and reconnect you to the rhythms of the seasons, Dr. Smalley adds. Urban environments “are very good at insulating us” from those patterns, he explains, as they provide us with artificial lighting, heating, and cooling so we can go about life seemingly autonomously. But the sheer vastness of a whole-sky sunrise or sunset can quickly remind you of the larger natural system we’re all a part of, which can be deeply grounding.
The Surprising Health Benefits of Watching the Sunrise or Sunset, [Self](https://www.self.com/story/sunrise-sunset-health-benefits)

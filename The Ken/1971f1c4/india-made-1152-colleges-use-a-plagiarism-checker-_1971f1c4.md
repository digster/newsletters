Good Morning Ishan,
“It’s amazing how quickly mistrust has become the default these days, and rightly so,” remarked a colleague recently. He was referring to the trend of good writing—from unexpected sources and sometimes even from expected ones—being first viewed with skepticism. Thank you AI, for turning so many of us into doubting Thomases, suspicious Sunils, and apprehensive Amits. The world will never be the same again.
The skeptics have a point though. It’s become easy-as-pie to use AI tools like ChatGPT and its ilk to churn out great reports, assignments, and projects. And they are indeed being used across homes, schools, colleges, universities, workplaces, you name it. Even when they aren’t supposed to be used. So, out come the AI-detection tools to try and identify the unoriginals. It’s a cat-and-mouse, catch-me-if-you-can game. AI tools, those shape-shifters, keep evolving and stay a step ahead.
While it’s an equal-opportunity offender, this AI problem is now perhaps most acute in colleges and universities across the world—including in India. Atul’s story today has a great example of how a student, at one of the IIMs no less, bagged an A+ for his AI-assisted project.
While many centres of higher learning in India have for years used plagiarism checkers to sniff out copying, AI detection is a different animal altogether.
But one company is trying to crack the problem—and making headway. It’s also hitting roadblocks. It was never going to be an easy fight against the machine.
Drillbit—the protagonist of today’s story—has its work cut out. This plagiarism-checker in over a thousand colleges is now also their AI-detector, replacing global player Turnitin. But can it get the better of ChatGPT and its mutants? Is it even possible?

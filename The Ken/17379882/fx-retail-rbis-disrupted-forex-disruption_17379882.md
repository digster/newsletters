Life pro tip: When designing a disruption, make sure it isn't beholden to those you're trying to disrupt.
Obvious, I know. But it's exactly the trap the RBI, India's central bank, has fallen into.
Like so many stories that go awry, this too started with good intentions. Indians complained that the margins on buying and selling forex, especially those offered by banks, were extortionate. The RBI agreed, and last year, it came up with a platform called FX-Retail.
The platform brings customers and banks together, but unlike before, it does this with far more transparency. Banks offer, bid, and compete on currency prices; customers buy and sell currency. It isn't the take-it-or-leave-it system that otherwise dominates the space.
To give you a sense of how radical FX-Retail is, here's an example. Where the difference in the price at which a bank buys and sells a dollar can be as much as Rs 3.50, on FX-Retail, that difference is around Rs 0.06. The platform practically allows even the smallest users to transact at rates often reserved for companies making major forex transactions.
Despite this, while India's average daily forex volumes stand at around $33 billion, FX-Retail sees no more than a few million pass through it each day.
How does a proposition this good not take off? Well, because it still requires banks—both to buy and sell currency, but perhaps more importantly, to evangelise and even approve users for the platform. Given how crucial forex margins are to their own bottom lines, banks have been far from eager to push RBI's next big hope, writes Shreedhar.

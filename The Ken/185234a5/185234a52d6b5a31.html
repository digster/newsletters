Good Morning Ishan,
 
This week, I learned that only one word could describe Bengaluru’s real-estate market: insane.
 
Humour me. I've been looking for a new house for weeks, and it is all I think about while cooking, cleaning, driving, and even working! After work, on weekends, and during lunch breaks, I check prospective houses and haggle with brokers.
 
My roommates and I act like an army, combing through an excel sheet of contacts and negotiating inflated prices all day. (Yes, we made an excel file. Yes, I'll share it with you if you ask nicely.)
 
And then there is the “security deposit” one has to pay at the start of the tenancy; it can cost as much as a car! We've realised that saving and availing such large amounts of cash is incredibly difficult, especially for young professionals like us.
 
From where I am, I can clearly see the allure of easy credit, especially for younger customers. It likely explains why ‘buy now, pay later (BNPL)’ can be a huge hit among users in their twenties.
 
On Friday, however, my colleague Rounak looked at a very different and interesting alternative: save now, buy later (SNBL). His story explored three young SNBL fintechs—Multipl, Hubble, and Tortoise—the challenges they face and why it might not be an entirely fresh idea.
 
On Thursday, Shruti wrote about Taiwanese EV giant Gogoro's India roadmap and the various forces, including corporate lobbying, at play. The EV giant hopes to use its technological strength, advocacy efforts, and operational experience to electrify fleet vehicles in the country, where policymakers are yet to finalise battery-swapping standards.
 
On Wednesday, Seema Singh, our Editor, talked to Aniket Doegar, the 33-year-old founder of Haqdarshak, which helps larger corporates extend government schemes to their workers. Doegar has a counterthesis: given India’s federal structure, it needs only about 1,000 government schemes out of an estimated 20,000 such programmes. He adds that anything less than that will undermine the states’ role.
 
On Tuesday, Nadine explored the reasons behind Czech non-bank Home Credit’s exit from Indonesia and the Philippines. It was not a grand exit for the consumer-lending firm, which continues to hold on to India and Vietnam, but a path likely to be followed by peers.
 
On Monday, yours truly teamed up with Adhithi to tell you about the changing strategy of VC behemoth Tiger Global in India and why some startups are wary of taking its money despite its founder-friendly image.
 
That was it from The Ken this week. In the meantime, I intend to keep saving to keep up with my broker's mind-boggling deposit quotes.
 
With warm regards (and a general cry for good house leads),

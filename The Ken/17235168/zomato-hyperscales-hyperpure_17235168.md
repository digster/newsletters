"Fools rush in where angels fear to tread."
The startup scene in India has gone bonkers. Companies are pivoting, pirouetting and cartwheeling into new segments (or out of some) faster than acrobats in a circus. Even Alexander Pope would be hard-pressed to tell the fools from the angels.
Amidst these gymnastics, Zomato has chosen to double down on what it has been doing for a while now. No, not food delivery. Guess again.
Not even its restaurant subscription program, Gold. Try again. I'll give you a hint—groceries.
If you haven't already guessed, the answer is Hyperpure. The farm-to-fork supply business Zomato started in 2018 through an acquisition. I know, you're thinking "But everyone is delivering groceries to me nowadays."
Not Zomato.
The forks Hyperpure are targeting are not in your home but in restaurants, some 7,500 of them in Bengaluru and Delhi. It's tiny right now, about 3% of total revenue, but it does something Zomato's sprawling food delivery business doesn't—make money on every order.
It's also pandemic-proof, it would seem. Zomato says the business is back to pre-Covid levels already and one executive says they expect demand to jump 2-3X due to supply chain disruptions caused by the lockdowns. So naturally, it would expand. Hyperpure started in Mumbai last week, and the plan is to reach 12 cities in the next few months.
Restaurants love it not just for being a reliable service that costs less than a trip to the local shops. Using the service gets them a hygiene tag on Zomato's restaurant discovery platform. A label that Zomato claims gives restaurants a 20-25% increase in visibility and orders.
But it's not all harps and angels on clouds for Zomato. For one, there is a reason so many others have struggled with grocery delivery. Even Hyperpure's expansion plans last year didn't go as expected.

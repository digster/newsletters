Ed Set Go
Mon, 17 Feb 25
The one stop to understand the biggest shifts in education, written by the best edtech journalist in the country..
Good Morning Ishan,
My part of the world—I live in Washington DC now—has been rocked with headlines straight out of a dystopian novel. If these are the end times, they’re unfolding at a gratingly slow pace.
Even as Donald Trump’s plans threaten the education department of his own government, the new US President met with India’s Prime Minister amidst the chaos to talk about “enhanced collaboration” between the higher education institutions of both countries. The two leaders acknowledged, as per an NDTV [report](https://www.ndtv.com/education/india-us-to-boost-educational-collaborations-focus-on-offshore-campuses-student-mobility-7706821), that “more than 300,000 Indian students contribute over US$8 billion annually to the US economy, creating a significant number of direct and indirect jobs.”
There were more promises too: joint dual-degree programmes in both countries, twinning arrangements, and even the possibility of US universities creating more offshore campuses in India.
The timing of these discussions is ironic. Competing headlines suggest that things are getting worse for Indian students who’re in the US right now. Jobs upon graduation are harder to get (read [our edition](https://the-ken.com/edsetgo/the-h-1b-visa-debate-sparks-worries-for-indian-immigrants-not-so-much-for-study-abroad-edtechs/) on this topic), and now, a potential crackdown on the H-1B visa might hurt their chances even more. An offshore campus of a US university does make sense in this scheme of things.
But Indian students who’ve overstayed their study visas are now in a vulnerable situation. Some are working—illegally—at gas stations and grocery stores, hoping that they aren’t the next target of Trump’s aggressive deportation plans.
Students are faced with the [very real consequences](https://timesofindia.indiatimes.com/nri/us-canada-news/amid-deportations-indian-students-in-us-face-random-checks-by-officers-in-uniform/articleshow/117999093.cms) of overstaying their visa, coupled with the challenge of mounting debt back home.
“Last week, officers came in and began questioning the staff at the restaurant where I work six hours every day after college. They asked for my college ID. Fortunately, I was coming out of the restroom, so I told them I was just there to use the facility. My employer backed me,” said an Indian pursuing a masters in cybersecurity in Atlanta.
“The experience was so petrifying that I resigned the very next day,” the student added.”
[Amid deportations, Indian students in US face random checks by ‘officers in uniform’](https://timesofindia.indiatimes.com/nri/us-canada-news/amid-deportations-indian-students-in-us-face-random-checks-by-officers-in-uniform/articleshow/117999093.cms), Times of India
Such accounts are likely to continue unless there’s some reprieve in the crackdown on undocumented immigrants or those who’ve overstayed their visa. Study-abroad agents and companies, who’ve definitely taken notice, have been under pressure to find new courses and new countries to send Indian students to. It’s the US this year, it was Canada last year—geopolitical tensions have made things dicey.
What’s interesting to me about this whole push-and-pull situation are the changing expectations from Indian students. The set pathway of a degree—> OPT (the time period where you’re allowed to work and look for permanent employment)—> job—> green card or citizenship was so well laid out. Obviously, things aren’t as stable anymore.
Study-aboard businesses are now urging students to pick destinations “carefully”. They’re being asked to quit their part-time jobs which help pay their expenses, and understand the “realities” of the immigration system. But as borders tighten everywhere, these realities could hit these businesses harder than expected. How will Indian students stand out against aspirants from other countries? Who’s going to fight to keep foreign universities open and viable for our youth?
If you thought political leaders will pave the way for this, I have only to refer to the one-sided negotiations and tariff deals that the Indian delegation came away from the White House with this week. Basically, don’t hold your breath.

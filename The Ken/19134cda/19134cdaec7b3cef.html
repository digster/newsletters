For a long time, most people agreed that if you wanted to start a tech company, the “default” place to do it was Bengaluru. It had everything that young startups wanted—a vibrant city, access to tech talent, VCs, and even great weather. For over a decade, some of the most notable startups emerged from Bengaluru. Everyone believed this was an advantage, i.e., the ecosystem made these companies stronger, resilient, and more innovative.

But of late… we aren’t so sure about that. Even though most people in Bengaluru believe that they are doing exciting, innovative work, it’s the startups from another city that are really succeeding as businesses. And that’s Delhi.

“There is a lot of focus on the gold, not on the shovel, so you can’t actually build shovel businesses in Delhi.”

That’s Arnav Gupta, one of our podcast guests, explaining how Delhi companies are different from the Bengaluru ones. Arnav would know. He spent the first five years of his career working at engineering leadership roles at Delhi startups such as Zomato and Coding Blocks, and another three years in Bengaluru—at Scalar, and now as engineering head at Jiocinema. If anyone understands the nuanced differences between Delhi startups and Bengaluru startups, it’s Arnav. 

There’s evidence that Arnav is on to something. The list of tech startups that have gone public are mostly all Delhi startups—Paytm, Zomato, Delhivery, Mamaearth… or Infoedge, Makemytrip from an earlier era. Even anecdotally, there are enough people who believe that Bengaluru startups have gone soft, and have started believing that running a startup is more important than running successful businesses.

But one person doesn’t exactly see it the same way, and that’s Prashant Singh, the head of Product at Jar—a Bengaluru-based startup. Prashant has spent the bulk of his career in Delhi, and he believes this city-based startup narrative is somewhat… overblown. 

“I basically somewhere disagree with the core premise of this discussion. Essentially that companies are, startups are fundamentally probabilistic and diverse.”

Prashant’s point is that success is driven by several factors—and location is just one variable. 

Normally, we could all agree to disagree with each other. The question of whether Bengaluru startups are better or Delhi startups are better is usually just an angry discussion on X (formerly Twitter), but we decided to do an episode on this topic because we believe that this is getting closer to becoming a zero-sum game. 

Startups are in an age of scarcity—less funding going around, limited base of paying customers, fewer investors, and of course, limited talent. And right now, with Bengaluru companies like Swiggy, Ola Electric, Flipkart, and Inmobi set to go public over the next couple of years, they are also going to be competing for the attention and money from public-market investors.

And right now, it looks like Delhi is the one that’s winning.

So for 75 minutes, Arnav, Prashant, Rohin Dharmakumar (my co-host), and I discussed how Delhi pricked the Bengaluru bubble.

I recommend subscribing and listening to the episode in full. Here are some of the things we discuss:

Delhi startup employees have less optionality, and that’s their advantage 

Arnav: Delhi startups, I have seen there’s a lot less distraction. It’s hard to hire talent. But once the talent is in, they don’t leave quickly.

How Bengaluru startups simply don’t give up

Prashant: For example, look at the amount of time Bengaluru startups stay on one task. Instamart, or you look at like, Super, Swiggy Super, or something. Like, if I may, some other fintech companies are also doing innovation.

But in Delhi and in some other parts of the country also, if something doesn’t work, one or two iterations, and then you fold and start pivoting.

Does Bengaluru understand the difference between “needless intellectualisation” and “craft”?

Rohin: Needless intellectualisation. I think there’s a lot that happens in Bengaluru, where I think the focus on craft tends to cross over into needless delays. And the sense of urgency is lost.

Bengaluru startups and the “VP of engineering from Amazon” obsession

Arnav: When you’re starting to sort of scale and, you know, go from a 10-member engineering team to a 100-member team, you say, okay, I need an adult in the room who will set up an engineering team the proper way… and to do that, you go and hire somebody from Amazon.

Nobody gets fired for hiring a VP of engineering from Amazon. Just look around Bengaluru. Every startup has a VP of engineering from Amazon. Somebody who will bring in structure and all of that. I mean, that’s the stereotype, but there’s a lot of leadership, which is ex-big tech, right? There’s also culture imbibed as a result of that—they’re not the kind of people who are going to be the first to say, hey, bring your ass to the office tomorrow.

Delhi founders are not like that.

How Delhi startups hit the real world quickly

Arnav: So I think like a lot of those startups in Delhi were out last, they were swimming against the tide, but it also gave them the advantage that they hit the real world very quickly.

So they had to, they were outside the bubble already, right?

Praveen: So that’s a good way to put it.

Rohin: Yeah.

We also talk about other things, like how Swiggy, the Bengaluru startup, is an operations company and Zomato, the Delhi startup, is a food company. Plus other stuff like how engineering careers in Delhi change and become “better” due to a lack of optionality. And also about how big Bengaluru’s bubble is exactly, and how it affects companies like Ather, Mokobara, and others. 

It was a fun discussion. In fact, we continued long after the microphones were switched off. Here we are, in the studio at 100-feet road in Indiranagar (the heart of the Bengaluru startup scene), along with Anushka, our producer.

It’s safe to say 2025 was not a good year for Indian aviation.

In June, Air India’s Boeing 787 Dreamliner flying from Ahmedabad to London crashed seconds after takeoff. More recently, Indigo’s flight-duty-time-regulations fiasco resulted in over 4,500 cancelled flights and nearly a million stranded passengers.

For flyers, it’s been one crisis after another. For competitor Akasa Air, the timing may be as conducive as it is likely to get.

Founded in 2022 by a team of aviation veterans led by former Jet Airways CEO Vinay Dubey, Akasa is backed by investors such as the Jhunjhunwala family, Ranjan Pai’s Claypond Capital, and Premji Invest. Its ambitions, too, are sky high: the airline placed an order for 226 aircraft in its very first year, of which 33 are now operational. In contrast, Indigo started with a mere six aircraft in 2006, though it has since scaled to 434.

In fact, Dubey bookends every town hall with the same proclamation: “We are building an airline for our children and grandchildren and the generations to come.”

But underneath the grand sentiment, the airline has struggled with more mundane problems such as delays in aircraft delivery, failure to secure parking slots in airports, and more recently, the exit of co-founder Praveen Iyer.

In today’s story, Nuha spoke to Dubey to take stock of where the airline is at—and where it wants to go.

It’s an exciting time to be in Indian pharma. At least, that’s how some of the big names are making it seem. One to watch, in particular, is Torrent Pharmaceuticals, which recently completed the buyout of a 46.4% stake in JB Chemicals for nearly Rs 12,000 crore.

And the timing couldn’t be better. With pricing pressure rising for generic drugs and the Indian stock market on the lookout for a differentiated playbook, Torrent is now armed with a powerful tool—JB’s CDMO business. However, as Seema writes in today’s Long and Short, this advantage could be conditional.

Good Morning Ishan,

The “Not Invented Here” syndrome is one of the worst maladies that tend to affect organisations that develop new products or intellectual capital. It happens when these organisations start distrusting products or knowledge developed outside of its walls. It’s also called “Let's Reinvent The Wheel” syndrome, and left unchecked, over the long term, it leads to a progressive destruction of an organisation’s innovation capacity.

India’s premier space research organisation, Isro, is going through such a moment around satellite broadband.

As the world of satellite communication moves towards high-speed broadband beamed from space (OneWeb, a Virginia-based communications company in which Airtel and Hughes have invested, has begun beaming internet from space at 8Gbps), India has yet to decide how Ka-band frequencies are going to be used.

Instead, Isro is forcing Indian TV broadcasters (who use “Ku-band” transponders) to shift from foreign satellites to its own ones. Why? Because of the total 877 satellite channels today, at least 80% are on foreign satellites. And international prices have fallen 40-60% in the last two years.

Under the garb of “Make in India”, Isro is engaging in rent-seeking behaviour. Why?

Because it is one of the rare organisations globally that is a space research organisation, regulator and commercial player all rolled into one. One entrepreneur quoted in the story says he’s been waiting four years to hear back from India’s Department of Science for permission to launch two broadband satellites.

Thus the following quote in March from a senior bureaucrat in a public meeting, “Here is the paradox, we produce the cheapest satellite but the costliest bandwidth.”

Seema and Harveen’s story starts with the news about the unplanned delay in Isro’s largest satellite to date, GSAT-11, and then goes on to link it to Isro’s insecure and insular behaviour around satellite broadband.

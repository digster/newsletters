Simplicity sticks. Be it in design, theory, investment, life, management, or stories. Allow me to share a story.
 
A few years ago, after an hour-long chat in Lindau, Germany, with John C Mather, NASA’s only Nobel laureate so far, I asked him if the Prize had changed his life, and how. Mather, who got the Prize in 2006 for providing evidence in support of the Big Bang scenario of the origin of the Universe, said, “I get to drink better quality wine.” Seeing the look on my face, he expanded: “I now get invited to bigger meetings and parties.”
 
The simplicity of that answer stuck with me.
 
In our article today, contributing authors Saurabh Mukherjea and Anupam Gupta make a case for simplicity. Starting, of course, with the investment world—from Systematic Investments Plans or SIPs (where ‘S’ could very well stand for simplicity) in the Indian market to ETFs (exchange-traded fund) in the global market, simplicity is at work. And it works. Simple businesses can also make for great investments, they say.
 
From the world of investment into psychology, then onto the principles of simplicity for knowledge workers, the article today straddles many disciplines, resting finally on developing a set of simple rules to live our lives by. Rules which act as a framework for decisions, but not a rigid one. Rules which play a powerful role in forcing us to stay within our narrow circle of competence (and rely upon the expertise of others for matters outside that.)
 
I have to say, the authors are really a fan of thumb rules. For that matter, journalists are too. We also have thumb rules. Like, always ask that last question however clichéd it may be. Like, you often get the best soundbites when you have one foot out the door. Both proven true in Mather’s interview!
 
Read the piece (it's free), make your own thumb rules and have a nice weekend. I’ll see you next week.

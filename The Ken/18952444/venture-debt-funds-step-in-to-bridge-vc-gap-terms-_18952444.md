Good Morning Ishan,
Venture debt can seem as tedious as venture capital is thrilling.
I had to lead with this since it’s quite likely you thought the same when you read the subject line of this email.
Startups and debt don’t make great headlines together, unless of course they involve Byju’s and default.
That’s not without reason, though. In 2022, venture capital worth $26 billion flowed into the Indian startup ecosystem, while venture debt was less than $1 billion.
But VCs are no longer eager to write startups with questionable business models eye-popping cheques.
“Companies that raised money at valuations above $500 million in 2021 are looking at massive down rounds if they want to raise equity today,” the director of a venture-debt fund told my colleague Gaurav.
Startups, however, still need money, and here’s where venture-debt funds are looking to step up. For instance, Stride Ventures has gone beyond what passes for venture debt to offer supply-chain financing to startups.
But this is venture-debt firms’ moment to dictate terms too. Stride’s rival Blacksoil Capital has made sure one of its portfolio companies has enough money to last six months, compared to just three earlier, and capped the debt it can raise.
As Gaurav’s story shows today, venture debt is a necessary evil startups are coming to terms with: [https://the-ken.com/story/venture-debt-funds-step-in-to-bridge-vc-gap-terms-and-conditions-apply/](https://the-ken.com/story/venture-debt-funds-step-in-to-bridge-vc-gap-terms-and-conditions-apply/?utm_source=story_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=story_newsletter) (7-minute read)

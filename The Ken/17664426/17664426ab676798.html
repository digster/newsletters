"Investing should be more like watching paint dry or watching grass grow," the Nobel-winning American economist Paul A Samuelson is believed to have said. "If you want excitement, take $800 and go to Las Vegas."
 
But Robinhood, the American stock-trading app, has notched up an $11 billion valuation by thumbing its nose at Samuelson. 
 
The seven-year-old company has made it simple—even too simple—for amateur investors to trade in stocks and more complicated products like options. Robinhood has pulled this off by making its app fun, with "most popular" lists and digital confetti. Another hook Robinhood uses is free stocks during the trial period. 
 
A year and a half ago, an Indian stockbroking startup, Upstox, took a leaf out of the Robinhood playbook by handing out complimentary shares. Thanks to a $25-million investment by VC firm Tiger Global, Upstox also did away with account-opening charges and started offering referral bonuses. 
 
Upstox hit paydirt with these hacks, thanks to a newfound fascination with the stock market among retail investors during the lockdown. 
 
The company hit 1.5 million registered users in November, going past ICICI Securities to become India's second-largest stockbroker. Zerodha, with 2.7 million registered users, leads the pack. 
 
But Upstox is not content merely being a stock-trading platform. So it's now looking to sell mutual funds, digital gold, and fixed deposits. However, as Arundhati deftly examines in today's free story, Upstox is going to have a tough time making money off its new products while fending off competition in its mainstay.

Good Morning Ishan,
“Today, we are a K-shaped economy.
There are two different angles at which the economy is progressing.
There’s one set of customers, you know, who are upwardly mobile and sitting in the urban areas. And the other ones who are struggling, you know, for sustenance.”
That was Shivaprasad Krishnan, one of the two guests we had on Episode 10 of the Two by Two podcast. And we were talking about the state of India’s health insurance sector.
Shivaprasad currently runs Kricon Capital, an investment banking firm, but he was earlier part of the founding team at ICICI Lombard, one of India’s first private health insurers.
And what he says is really the truth about health insurance in India. The divide is clear. Yes, there are a whole load of people who are insured under government schemes ([close to 300 million](https://www.dailyexcelsior.com/under-the-ayushman-bharat-health-insurance-scheme-more-than-300-million-card-generated-through-beneficiary-nha/#google_vignette)) and group insurance (~200 million), but very few hold personal health insurance (just ~50 million).
At the other end are around [350 million](https://www.ncaer.org/wp-content/uploads/2024/02/WP-162-Covering-the-Missing-Middle-in-India_Feb-12-2024.pdf) people who remain uninsured.
“I would say at least for half the population… the government will be a primary participant in their healthcare journey.
And whether it comes through an Ayushman Bharat scheme, whether it comes to free treatment in the government hospital, they simply have no means to access healthcare in the private sector.
But for the other half, the top 10% of the country, they can take care of themselves.
You don’t need to worry about it.
They can buy policies, they have companies, they have wealth of their own.
They will always be fine, no matter what.
That is the 10% that is covered, and that is the market that is growing.”
That was Viren Shetty, the Executive Vice Chairman of one of India’s largest hospital groups, Narayana Health. Viren has also been spearheading Narayana Health’s foray into the health insurance segment, and he was the second guest to join my co-host Praveen Gopal Krishnan and I for this episode.
The problem with relying so heavily on group and corporate health insurance policies to do the job, though, is the fact they very often aren’t the best option for individuals.
Because the incentives here aren’t aligned with the interests of the customer, but rather, more skewed towards the benefit of the insurer.

Group health insurance is growing very fast because it is easier to acquire in bulk. But more importantly, India’s insurance regulator incentivises it by insisting firms reduce their commissions and expense ratios. Which is why insurers have focussed on acquiring more and more group insurance customers, even though their claims ratio is much higher.
And because group insurance has been made so cheap, health insurance policies for the individual customer are priced far higher than they should be in order to subsidise the group insurance business.
Group health (insurance) has been seen as a working capital product…
Group health is something that the organisation would want to cover their employees for, and so, insurance would also be bundled with this.
And if you’re actually a good property risk, then how do you get the benefit of good pricing?
That benefit of pricing would happen because of the bundling, which is how group health started to get underpriced.
That was the genesis of underpricing of group (health insurance).
There are other headaches too, most prominent of which are claims rejections by the insurer.
As Viren says, getting a claim settled can be an extremely frustrating experience for both hospitals and customers.
I’ve been running hospitals for close to two decades now and got to participate in a lot of the tomfoolery that exists in how insurers work with hospitals and doctors in admitting, denying, submitting claims.
And it is always the most frustrating experience for any hospital and for any consumer, no doubt, when we do all the work and get the patient to a point where they come nearly at death’s door, put an incredible amount of effort, save their lives, do the surgery, treat them, put all the medicines, get them off ICU care, get them off the ventilator, have them ready to walk home…
And all of that goes away in that last couple of hours when the bill is submitted to the insurer, and the insurer says, deny this, deny that, this cap, and so on.
And then, the patient starts yelling at us because we are there. We are the ones who are present.
And that led us to a journey to try and work backwards and see what is going on that is so wrong.
Here’s where Narayana Health is trying to do something different. Two months ago, it started piloting its own insurance business, and what it’s trying to do is build closer integration between healthcare services and insurance, something similar to how health insurance works in the US. And health insurance, unlike term insurance, is a product that is almost certain to be utilised by policy-holders, making it a much riskier product to sell.
Viren Shetty: Health insurance claims ratios are measured in the high 90s, sometimes in the 100%, right?
You will always pay out what you collect.
The only thing a health insurance company can do is to manage float with the money that you have, manage claims in the network that you provide, and manage risk… Which hospitals know how to do.
Shivaprasad Krishnan: Typically, for individual health insurance, the claims ratios are between 60–65% on an average across the entire industry.
But in group, I think these numbers are upwards of 90, 95, 105, 110% in terms of the claims ratios alone.
Health insurance is by no means an ignored product in India, but many of the pitfalls lie in how it’s sold to people, and how people view it.
Around the time of the pandemic, for instance, health insurance policy sales saw a significant uptick. But as soon as things went back to normal, most of the growth dissipated.
Viren Shetty: I think it was Covid that was an inflection point for the category fundamentally, which is when you saw a huge rise in premiums, especially for what we call as the top-up cover, which means you had a basic cover given to you by the organization.
[…]
And on account of anxiety, not knowing what it would cost to get hospitalised, people started buying these top up covers, right?
But sure enough, when the anxiety dropped in a year or two, the top ups fell off the cliff.
The renewals never happened.
It is also a hard product to innovate, right from getting the pricing right to how it is sold. And the high claims ratio doesn’t make things very easy either. So there are risks to what Narayana Health is trying to execute. But Viren says they are going about it in a fundamentally different way than how health insurance in India has operated so far.
We will deliver healthcare on demand, but make sure that we keep you out of the hospital because one, as a profit-seeking entity, it is our incentive to keep you out of the hospital, but it’s also fully aligned with what you as a person also want.
Nobody wants to be in the hospital.
You want health.
So that’s what we’re trying to do.
Viren is well aware of what the cost of such disruption can be.
We’re trying something different.
You know, if you’re trying to disrupt, it should look disruptive, it should look nonsensical, and it should be something that also does not make money. Because if it was making money, if it was such a great idea, somebody else would have done it by now.
And there is certainly space for disruption.
Even the regulator—the Insurance Regulatory and Development Authority of India—wants insurers to step in and pull their weight. Which is a welcome change, because in most cases, regulators are busy playing catch up.
The regulator has been very proactive.
The regulator is the one actually that’s pushing much more than us.
They were the ones who actually squeezed the timeline for us to submit our forms, to get all the paperwork.
They were just completely on the ball and said, you guys get your act together.
You need to submit this form because I can get your approval done in record time.
All good signs, but the push has to come from the insurers now.
The opportunity is there for the taking. The question is, who else is willing to take the risk?

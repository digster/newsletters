With each passing day, Trump’s capricious tariff wars—the latest being the 25% duty on auto imports into the US—make it clear as daylight that it is now every country for itself. That adds all the more urgency to India’s quest, over the past few years, to fix a big chink in its armour—its manufacturing base, especially in electronics.

The country has been trying hard to replicate its yesteryear success in IT services in electronics manufacturing. Generous sops have been rolled out to get the ball rolling. There have been notable wins, too, for instance, in the making of high-end phones and their components. But it’s just a drop in the ocean. There is still a long way to go, with the need and potential huge across a wide swathe of electronics.

Except, there is something crucial that is in short supply, something that could trip the ambition and hope. As Shristi, the author of today’s story writes, India faces a huge talent crunch in electronics manufacturing. At least 10 million of the 12 million potential hires over the next couple of years may lack the relevant skills to hit the ground running. It doesn’t help that there’s no practical training in colleges and universities that churn out this manpower.

So, what do helpless electronics manufacturers do? Self-help is the best help, a handful have decided, taking a leaf from the erstwhile playbook of IT giants like Infosys and TCS. Except, electronics is hardly the same as IT, and there are challenges galore. Not to mention an old pesky talent competitor. Yet, hope springs eternal. Can the gap be bridged? Can the baby steps lead India’s electronics dreams into the happily-ever-after?

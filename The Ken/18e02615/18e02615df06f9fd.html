The Spotlight by Gaurav Bagur
Staff Writer

Good Morning Ishan,

A couple of days ago, I watched a recently released film called Anatomy of a Fall. It’s a French drama that piqued my interest even before it was announced as one of the nominees for this year’s Academy Awards in the much-coveted Best Picture category.

The film deals with the mysterious death of a man, Samuel, at his chalet in the French Alps, where he lives with his family. His wife Sandra insists that Samuel committed suicide, while the authorities accuse her of murder. It falls upon their blind son Daniel—and the audience—to decide who to believe.

Anatomy of a Fall is a great portrayal of the intricate and messy dynamics of a family, but what really struck me is how well the film managed to replicate what the experience of reporting is like. The questions it poses to Daniel aren’t too far off what I ask myself when I’m working on a story.

How can you understand something that happened in your absence? How can you piece together a coherent, satisfying narrative out of a string of such events? And how sure can you be that your reconstruction is a fair, accurate representation of facts?

The other side of it is closure. Every story I write has a finite number of words—like every movie has a fixed runtime.

Real-life stories encompass a much more expansive universe of characters and span larger timelines. Unlike the media, they rarely come packaged in neat, self-contained parcels. And that leaves it to the storytellers to decide where to draw the line in how we construct our narratives.

It’s not surprising that a courtroom drama feels like reporting, I suppose. There’s a reason a number of my college peers went on to pursue law after studying journalism. I was one of those who considered that path, too—but here I am today, in one of Asia’s most exciting newsrooms.

And on that note, let’s revisit the engrossing stories that my talented colleagues put out over the past week.

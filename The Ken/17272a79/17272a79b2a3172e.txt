Flipkart (Walmart) is not a favourite

Abinaya

India’s Department for Promotion of Industry and Internal Trade (DPIIT) has rejected Walmart-owned Flipkart’s application to start its food retailing unit, Flipkart FarmerMart. The e-commerce regulator, which falls under the commerce ministry, cited regulatory issues.

It’s safe to say that Flipkart did not see this coming.

It was meant to be a routine bureaucratic check. After all, its rival Amazon got a licence to invest US$500 million in a separate food retail business in 2017. That year, online grocer Grofers too got the nod to invest US$40 million to retail food products in India. Currently, India grants 100% foreign direct investment (FDI) in food retail through the approval route to sell food produced locally.

Last week, almost a year after Flipkart FarmerMart filed its application, the DPIIT wrote to the Walmart-controlled company that it cannot be allowed into food retail-which was carved out as a separate category for FDI-as an entity that runs a marketplace, or has equity from it, and will not be allowed to sell product on its platforms.

Govt rejects https://timesofindia.indiatimes.com/business/india-business/govt-rejects-flipkarts-plan-to-enter-food-retail/articleshow/76126956.cms Flipkart’s Plan to Enter Food Retail, Times of India

There has been no change announced to the food retail policy. Of course, Flipkart’s application could have just had a clerical error.

But there could be another reason. Flipkart wanted to integrate the food retail unit into its e-commerce platform, which is a violation of Press Note https://dipp.gov.in/sites/default/files/pn2_2018.pdf (2), said industry experts The Ken spoke to. The note says that e-commerce marketplaces cannot hold inventory or have ownership over goods sold on their platforms. Amazon and Flipkart did a hasty rejig of their units to remain compliant in 2019. Flipkart will now re-apply for the food license.

Amazon’s food unit, Amazon Retail India was granted permission in 2019 to continue selling on its e-commerce platform after de-linking its warehouses and supply chains.

FDI in food retail will be out of the purview of curbs in the e-commerce FDI policy that came into effect on February 1, according to the clarification issued earlier by the Department for Promotion of Industry and Internal Trade (DPIIT).

Amazon Retail is out of revised FDI purview, can sell on Amazon India, Entrackr https://entrackr.com/2019/02/amazon-food-retail-business/, Feb 2019

It’s unclear, then, why Flipkart’s application was denied. But let’s consider for a moment that Flipkart might not receive the licence. That would mean the government is contemplating altering the special rules for food retail. That’s bad news for Amazon, who, in April, invested Rs 284 crore (US$37.6 million) into food retail.

Who doesn’t get affected? Reliance Retail’s JioMart, which has no FDI hoops to jump through as a domestic player. And DPIIT.

As of May this year, DPIIT is not just regulating the e-commerce space, it is also technically a participant. The Confederation of All India Traders (CAIT) launched https://www.moneycontrol.com/news/economy/policy/cait-to-launch-national-e-commerce-marketplace-5211171.html an e-commerce portal—BharatEMarket—in partnership with the DPIIT. The details of the partnership are not known, but CAIT said the DPIIT will help with the initial “hand-holding”.

So the DPIIT will now set rules that govern e-commerce companies and itself, regulating the very space it is now a competitor in. Never a dull day in Indian e-commerce.

Openwashing?

Rohin

While India’s Covid-19 graph refuses to flatten, downloads of the central government’s contact tracing app, Aarogya Setu, continue to rise. Much to the celebration of senior government officials.

Amitabh Kant is the head of Niti Aayog, a central government super think tank that’s bang in the middle of most of its biggest policy interventions.

Aarogya Setu has been viewed with suspicion by privacy-conscious citizens and security researchers. Because it collects a lot more data than experts say is required to trace contacts, and because its code was opaque and proprietary.

This is data that is very valuable to a bunch of Indian startups, VCs, and consulting companies. Here’s a slide from a presentation made by the Boston Consulting Group about Aarogya Setu. It’s from our 18 May story https://the-ken.com/story/the-elite-vc-founder-club-riding-aarogya-setu-to-telemed-domination/.

So, last week, Kant announced the open-sourcing https://indianexpress.com/article/technology/tech-news-technology/centre-shifts-stance-releases-source-code-for-aarogya-setu-app-6428741/ of Aarogya Setu’s code. Then, India’s open source community started going through https://twitter.com/jackerhack/status/1266971326918455298 the code. This Twitter thread https://twitter.com/jackerhack/status/1266971326918455298 by Kiran Jonnalagadda, the co-founder of HasGeek, is instructive.

And this https://twitter.com/jackerhack/status/1266971326918455298, which sounds suspiciously like “openwashing”.

Openwashing: n., having an appearance of open-source and open-licensing for marketing purposes, while continuing proprietary practices.

Audrey Watters https://twitter.com/audreywatters/status/184387170415558656

Incidentally, Mozilla Foundation, the parent organisation behind the open-source Firefox browser, used the phrase “openwashing” yesterday https://blog.mozilla.org/netpolicy/files/2020/05/India-NODE-Consultation-Mozilla-Response-31052020.pdf to describe India’s all-encompassing NODE (National Open Digital Ecosystems) framework that connects together government APIs, data privacy and consent mechanisms.

However, the current white paper also leaves much to be elucidated on both the need and manner of implementation of such ecosystems before a national strategy can be finalized. In addition to a distinct lack of clarity on how governance mechanisms of NODEs would operate within existing and upcoming regulatory frameworks, the paper also creates the potential for ‘open-washing’ of projects. The white paper leaves the definition of "open" vague and at the complete discretion of individual implementers. Consequently, implementers are not required to adhere to any minimum baseline of "open". This risks empowering private parties to develop closed ecosystems that are only open in appearance while being closed in practice.

Here's a link to the NODE policy https://www.medianama.com/wp-content/uploads/mygov_1582193114515532211.pdf, hosted by Medianama https://www.medianama.com/.

Netflix for Insurance

Varun Bhagat (Reader contributor)

What is the incentive for social distancing by staying home and not driving? One, we help flatten the curve (really?). Two, it leads to a dramatic drop in accidents (yes, really https://indianexpress.com/article/india/coronavirus-covid-19-crime-rate-road-accidents-suicides-6386519/). Meaning fewer accidents to cover for insurance firms.

However, this doesn’t reward customers who are still paying premiums during tough times. So here’s another incentive: major US car insurers are offering refunds https://in.reuters.com/article/health-coronavirus-insurance-auto-factbo/insurers-return-part-of-auto-premiums-as-coronavirus-cuts-driving-idINKBN22V1R1 to policyholders following a decline in driving.

American Family Insurance said it would return a total of US$200 million to auto insurance customers. Customers will receive US$50 per vehicle covered by their policies.

Some companies are also offering credit in the form of discounts on premium payments. Chubb—the world’s largest listed property and casualty insurance company, for instance, is giving credit on annual renewal premiums, reflecting a 35% cut for the months of April and May.

In India, Acko General Insurance offered a one-month https://www.thehindubusinessline.com/money-and-banking/covid-19-impact-acko-general-insurance-offers-one-month-free-auto-insurance/article31277194.ece free auto insurance extension to car and bike owners. The Insurance Regulatory and Development Authority of India (IRDAI) also extended the grace period for renewal on life insurance policies.

But you are still paying a premium when your vehicle is collecting dust. Seems unfair, right?

At this rather interesting time, Bharti AXA General Insurance is set to offer usage-based https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/wealth/insure/motor-insurance/bharti-axa-general-insurance-to-offer-pay-as-you-drive-motor-insurance-policy/articleshow/75443512.cms?from=mdr motor insurance policies (own damage only) for private car owners under the IRDAI’s Sandbox project. The company has tied up with insurance aggregator, PolicyBazaar.com, to distribute the product. Pay-as-you-drive will charge a premium based on the kilometres covered by the insured or the time you are on the road. The customer pre-declares vehicle usage for a period of one year.

Basically, drive less. Pay less. Save money.

Who knows, soon you might be buying your car insurance for a day, a week, a month, or even an hour. With most of us staying at home for the foreseeable future, might this be the beginning of on-demand insurance in India? Call it more like a subscription–like a Netflix or a Spotify for insurance.

The grim costs of lockdowns

Jum

While the debate about coronavirus lockdowns has largely focussed on the economic tradeoffs, other grim consequences are starting to emerge. Such as the increase in cases of child sexual abuse and domestic violence.

In the Philippines, which has been dubbed as a global hotspot https://finance.yahoo.com/news/study-philippines-global-hot-spot-074058711.html for sexual exploitation of children online, cases have tripled https://www.scmp.com/news/asia/southeast-asia/article/3086801/online-child-sex-abuse-cases-triple-philippines-lockdown during the lockdown.

This, as families have struggled to earn a living with widespread job losses https://www.rappler.com/nation/261529-dole-sees-5-million-jobs-lost-coronavirus-pandemic—estimated at around five million—from closures and flexible work arrangements since the country’s lockdown measures began. Experts found that traffickers are actually parents or close family members of the victims.

And with schools shut, children are stuck at home and spending more time on the internet, making them more prone to various kinds of abuse. Detection also becomes harder because children have little or no access to teachers or community members who may be able to spot signs of maltreatment.

In the US, police have noted a spike https://www.foxnews.com/us/women-and-children-at-higher-risk-of-violent-and-sexual-abuse-and-predators-amid-coronavirus-lockdown in domestic violence cases, while some hospitals have reported treating more children https://www.usatoday.com/story/news/nation/2020/05/13/hospitals-seeing-more-severe-child-abuse-injuries-during-coronavirus/3116395001/ for child abuse injuries.

Even when lockdown restrictions lift and abuses stop, those who’ve fallen victim have a long road ahead to recovery.

Filling newspapers with banknotes

Rohin

“The government should pay people to dig holes in the ground and then fill them up.”

This quote by the late economist John Meynard Keynes is often used to justify (or criticise) aggressive government spending during economic slowdowns. The full quote https://www.bradford-delong.com/2011/08/paul-krugman-reminds-us-of-the-context-of-keyness-bury-banknotes-in-the-ground-and-dig-them-up-discussion.html, with context, is even more interesting.

“If the Treasury were to fill old bottles with banknotes, bury them at suitable depths in disused coal mines which are then filled up to the surface with town rubbish, and leave it to private enterprise on well-tried principles of laissez-faire to dig the notes up again (the right to do so being obtained, of course, by tendering for leases of the note-bearing territory), there need be no more unemployment and, with the help of the repercussions, the real income of the community, and its capital wealth also, would probably become a good deal greater than it actually is. It would, indeed, be more sensible to build houses and the like; but if there are political and practical difficulties in the way of this, the above would be better than nothing.”

Disused coal mines might be hard to find, or dangerous to get into. Where else might the government place old bottles filled with banknotes, and leave it to private enterprise? Newspapers.

Here are large government advertisements in India’s leading English business newspaper, The Economic Times. While they’re ostensibly addressed to millions of India’s poor farmers, shopkeepers, hawkers, and fishermen, it’s hard to imagine many of them reading business newspapers.

New bottles with banknotes buried in plain sight.

The Taxman cometh… for your electricity bill

Arundhati

The Indian government has been trying to get more people to pay Income Tax for a long time. Out of the 1.3 billion in India, only about 15 million people pay income tax. This has been a sore point for the government, which is now staring at a massive hole in its pocket.

The government’s net tax revenue stood at Rs 21,412 crore (US$2.8 billion) in April, revealing a massive 70% decline from a year ago.

That played a role in widening https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/news/economy/indicators/fiscal-deficit-widens-to-4-6-of-gdp-in-2019-20/articleshow/76093931.cms India’s fiscal deficit. The gap between the government’s revenue and expenditure increased to 4.59% of India’s gross domestic product (GDP) from the previous fiscal, overshooting the government’s revised target of 3.8%.

So the government has to find creative ways to find more people to tax. And so far it had only been looking at people's income to impose a tax. But now the income tax department is also interested in your expenditure—electricity bills, foreign travel bills, and your current account balances. Business Standard reported https://www.business-standard.com/article/economy-policy/power-bill-over-rs-100-000-now-it-s-must-to-file-income-tax-return-120060100046_1.html:

People incurring large spending will not be able to escape paying taxes, with the government making filing mandatory in specific cases even if their income is below the taxable limit. With this, those spending over Rs 2 lakh on foreign travel, incurring more than Rs 1 lakh electricity bills, or depositing over Rs 1 crore in the current account will have to mandatorily file ITR.

This means the income tax department is going to be peering into your electricity bills. “All the government needs to do is ask people to attach the Aadhaar number (which is in turn linked to the PAN card) to the bills, and your electricity bills are easily trackable,” said a chartered accountant The Ken spoke to.

While the government has allowed people more time to file their income tax (the deadline was moved to 30 November from 31 July) as a relief measure, it is tightening the screws as well. And between relief and revenue, it’s apparent which side the government wants to lean more towards.

A swansong for Tiananmen?

Jon

Hong Kong is in the midst of an existential crisis as China prepares to assume greater control of the special administrative region. In a sign of what may come, this year’s vigil to mark the anniversary of the Tiananmen Square massacre has gone digital after the annual event was cancelled for the first time.

Covid-19 is the official reason for the ban, but there’s worry that a park-based vigil will never happen again. The event generates vivid media coverage that accentuates Hong Kong’s free speech and highlights repression in China.

Whether the commemoration will be allowed to go ahead in Hong Kong next year - when the new law targeting what Beijing considers to be terrorism and subversion in the territory will most likely be in force - is unclear.

Good Morning Ishan,
Recently, while watching Zach Cregger’s horror film Weapons at PVR Director’s Cut in Gurugram, something happened that really ticked me off. And I mean, apart from the movie itself, which—controversial opinion—left me severely lacking in answers. Who was that woman? What did she want? What did she do to those kids?
PVR charged me Rs 600 for a bowl of Maggi. This, when the price of the ticket itself was around Rs 400. And to make things worse, the Maggi was bone dry, with large chunks of uncooked capsicum thrown in. Ever since, I’ve made it a point to never order food there.
PVR has its reasons, I guess. Occupancy in theatres has fallen from 35% pre-pandemic to around 25% now. The rise of OTT and increasingly atrocious Bollywood films have hit cineplexes hard. It makes sense, then, that F&B sales have stepped up to bolster its top line.
But this fiscal, PVR has found a way to expand into new markets while reducing its skin in the game. Sounds contradictory? Well, just look at large hotels like Marriott, which have managed to expand into 143 countries while owning less than 2% of their hotels. PVR is now aping this hospitality playbook. Of the 45 screens it added this fiscal year, 18 were through this approach.
But movies aren’t hotel rooms. Can PVR successfully pull off a Marriott? Read Aakriti’s [story](https://the-ken.com/story/pvr-inox-wants-to-run-its-cinemas-like-hotels/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter) today to find out.

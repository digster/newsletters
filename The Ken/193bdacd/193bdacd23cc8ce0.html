Towards the end of Bennett Miller’s Moneyball, there is an exchange between Billy Beane, coach of baseball team Oakland Athletics, and John Henry, owner of the Boston Red Sox. At one point, Henry says:

I know you’ve taken it in the teeth out there, but the first guy through the wall—he always gets bloody. Always…

There’s a bit more to that dialogue, and I’ll come back to it in a bit, but it’s quite true, isn’t it? History has shown us that the first person to adopt a radical idea often suffers for it, and in some cases, even gets destroyed in the attempt to make it work. 

India’s software-as-a-service (SaaS) sector is at a similar inflection point. Most Indian SaaS companies have, for quite a long time, used pricing strategies that required customers to buy a more expensive plan in order to unlock a new feature. The goal has always been to push you into buying more to get more. 

But with AI entering the fray, solutions are going to get a lot more client-specific, and it’s going to become imperative for these same SaaS companies to start charging their clients based on outcomes. If they don’t—or even hesitate—they risk frugal, emerging AI upstarts snatching away what they’ve spent years building. 

Take the case of buy-now-pay-later giant Klarna, which decided to replace its SaaS providers Salesforce and Workday with internally built AI.

But these are overseas companies. What about Indian SaaS firms that have always been quite comfortable with the conventional SaaS playbook? 

That was precisely what hosts Praveen Gopal Krishnan and Rohin Dharmakumar sat down to discuss with our guests for the latest episode of the Two by Two podcast. Sumanth Raghavendra is the CEO and co-founder of Presentations.AI and one of the co-founders of The Ken. And Sidu Ponnappa is CEO and co-founder of Realfast and former managing director of Gojek India.

SaaS can be a lot more than just a business record

Traditionally, SaaS has largely remained a system of record-keeping—a way to organise, store, and process businesses’ customer data. 

But do traditional SaaS solutions provide clients with the ability to do anything more? 

Not quite. And that’s despite companies in India being more than comfortable throwing people at problems. There’s just too much data to process and synthesise. 

Sidu had an interesting observation around this dynamic, and how AI forces SaaS companies to change these assumptions.

Sidu: There is an interesting sort of side thing that has spun out of this trend, right? Which is arguing that, “Look, we should charge for outcomes. Forget about charging for software.”

And the argument (against this) is that, historically, we’ve gone into this with the mindset that SaaS, by definition, must be asset light on the balance sheet.  SaaS must, you know, have the classic SaaS margin profile.

But once you allow AI into the fray, it can get such tasks done much faster and more efficiently than humans ever can. Which means that the old business model needs to change—from just charging clients for access to service licences to promising real outcomes.

The rise of micro-markets

“SaaS had to be sold, AI will be bought.” 

That was Sumanth, and it’s a line that stuck with me. 

Here’s how Sumanth elaborated on it.

Sumanth: I think a silver lining for India is that the eventual winners are going to be in the vertical SaaS domains, right?

In vertical SaaS, you go long and deep into a single niche.

And you win only in that. 

You don’t go and build out these large horizontal products, like what people used to do in the days of yore with Freshworks or Zoho. Instead, you build a solution that caters to just, say, dentists in the Midwest.

So you build that entire moat there in terms of having data that caters only to them, right? You build domain expertise.

The casualties of AI

Sumanth: Companies who are around, let’s say generic workflow automation tools, and what we can think of as feature commodities. And also the companies that are just doing basic data processing kind of stuff. 

Those people are obviously going to be casualties.

And then, because the SaaS universe has expanded so much and there are a lot of companies that were just one-hit-wonder single-feature companies… those are also going to be in trouble.

And finally, there are a lot of Indian SaaS companies, unfortunately, which were just doing the arbitrage play.

Remember the dialogue from Moneyball that we left incomplete earlier. Well, here’s the rest:

…It’s the threat of not just the way of doing business, but in their minds it’s threatening the game. But really what it’s threatening is their livelihoods, it’s threatening their jobs, it’s threatening the way that they do things. And every time that happens, whether it’s the government or a way of doing business or whatever it is, the people are holding the reins, have their hands on the switch. They go batshit crazy. I mean, anybody who’s not building a team right and rebuilding it using your model, they’re dinosaurs.

In the case of India’s SaaS companies, when everyone’s solutions end up looking pretty much the same, how do you stand out from the crowd? After all, being a big gun won’t matter as much when AI levels the playing field.

Clearly, change is imminent. And the only question that remains is whether or not you’re getting onboard.

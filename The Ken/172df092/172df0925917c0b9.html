The National Centre for Disease Control, or NCDC, is India’s equivalent of the US CDC. The central organisation tasked with monitoring, tracing and controlling the spread of infectious diseases, it forms India's healthcare trifecta, alongside the Indian Council of Medical Research (ICMR) and the National Health Mission (the NHM manages healthcare delivery).
 
But in the midst of a pandemic, when it should be rising to the fore, NCDC is being pushed to the background. 
 
By states, which under India’s constitution, have the right to manage healthcare within their borders. 
 
By the government, which has been underfunding its flagship disease surveillance programme, leaving it understaffed and overstretched. 
 
By its gargantuan mandate, which forces it to track over 21 different diseases across a country of 1.3 billion people. 
 
By its peer, the ICMR, which quietly superseded it on India’s Covid-19 response.
 
As a result, India’s disease surveillance programme—which was set up in 2004, incidentally, to protect India from another potential threat of a pandemic, H5N1—is not getting the importance it should. Without robust disease surveillance and contact tracing, India’s ability to curb the spread of an epidemic in the future is severely limited.

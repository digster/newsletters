Incidentally, PGK just completed his fifth anniversary writing The Nutgraf newsletter on Saturdays for The Ken yesterday. I wouldn’t be exaggerating at all if I said that First Principles exists on Sundays because The Nutgraf exists on Saturdays.

Allow me to quote a few paragraphs from his anniversary edition newsletter.

Since that day, every Saturday morning, I’ve showed up in the inboxes of The Ken’s subscribers barring vacations and sick days. By my approximate calculations, I’ve written 230-odd editions, and nearly half a million words in all. Five years ago, if you’d told me that I’d still be here (admittedly with a much better logo), still writing this newsletter, I wouldn’t have believed you. Discipline and consistency have never been among my better qualities. I’m less ‘slow and steady wins the race’ and more ‘buy expensive shoes, fill the registration form, feel like I’ve done a lot of hard work, then tell myself I deserve a day off, and so decide to skip the actual race’ kind of a person.

There are other reasons why this is such an unlikely product. A lot of people don’t know this—The Nutgraf began as a side-hustle of mine at The Ken. Fewer people know that it’s still just a side-hustle. Every week, I spend roughly five hours on The Nutgraf, and the rest of my time is devoted to my actual job, i.e., running the business team of The Ken. However, these five hours come at the most inopportune time—right at the end of the work week. When everyone else logs off for the weekend, that’s when my work begins. For five years, I’ve not done anything on Friday nights, except research, write, and edit the next day’s edition of The Nutgraf. I’ve skipped invitations for dinners, birthdays, and movies. I’ve written on flights, on trains, in cabs, in hotel rooms in random cities, and most memorably, once at The Ken’s annual company offsite. Even a global outage couldn’t stop me from writing today. I’ve written when I didn’t feel like writing, when I wasn’t in the best place mentally or physically, and when I simply couldn’t think of anything to write about.

I’ve seen PGK slog through his week after week of writing The Nutgraf over the years. I’ve seen him relish it, struggle with it, get frustrated, and get recharged.

But most importantly, he stuck with it. And thus, with time, his momentum made everything seem “effortless”.

“Success requires first expending ten units of effort to produce one unit of results. Your momentum will then produce ten units of results with each unit of effort.”—Charles J. Givens

(I know financial advisor and author Charles Givens was sued for fraudulent promises and claims over the years, leaving his reputation in tatters. But I know First Principles readers are mature enough to appreciate the wisdom of a quote without getting burdened too much by who said it.)

Momentum is one of the most underrated and underappreciated concepts in life and work. Most of us innately “feel” and live it when we’re on great “streaks”, be it in the gym or at work. But not enough of us spend time trying to consciously craft our momentum.

Here’s a bit from Good to Great by Jim Collins. He uses the invaluable concept of a “flywheel” to explain the role momentum plays in our lives.

Picture a huge, heavy flywheel—a massive metal disk mounted horizontally on an axle, about 30 feet in diameter, 2 feet thick, and weighing about 5,000 pounds. Now imagine that your task is to get the flywheel rotating on the axle as fast and long as possible. Pushing with great effort, you get the flywheel to inch forward, moving almost imperceptibly at first. You keep pushing and, after two or three hours of persistent effort, you get the flywheel to complete one entire turn. You keep pushing, and the flywheel begins to move a bit faster, and with continued great effort, you move it around a second rotation. You keep pushing in a consistent direction. Three turns … four … five … six … the flywheel builds up speed … seven … eight … you keep pushing … nine … ten … it builds momentum … eleven … twelve … moving faster with each turn … twenty … thirty … fifty … a hundred.

Then, at some point—breakthrough! The momentum of the thing kicks in in your favor, hurling the flywheel forward, turn after turn … whoosh! … its own heavy weight working for you. You’re pushing no harder than during the first rotation, but the flywheel goes faster and faster. Each turn of the flywheel builds upon work done earlier, compounding your investment of effort. A thousand times faster, then ten thousand, then a hundred thousand. The huge heavy disk flies forward, with almost unstoppable momentum.

Now suppose someone came along and asked, “What was the one big push that caused this thing to go so fast?” You wouldn’t be able to answer; it’s just a nonsensical question. Was it the first push? The second? The fifth? The hundredth? No! It was all of them added together in an overall accumulation of effort applied in a consistent direction. Some pushes may have been bigger than others, but any single heave—no matter how large—reflects a small fraction of the entire cumulative effect upon the flywheel. … Here’s what’s important. We’ve allowed the way transitions look from the outside to drive our perception of what they must feel like to those going through them on the inside. From the outside, they look like dramatic, almost revolutionary breakthroughs. But from the inside, they feel completely different, more like an organic development process.

I don’t want to turn this edition into a tome, so here’s a really comprehensive post about cultivating and nurturing momentum in your lives. Do read it.

If momentum exists on a longer time scale, then perhaps on the other end of it lies “first impressions”, a concept whose importance too we greatly underestimate.

The best first step to building momentum is a great first impression. As human beings we’re wired to let down our guard when coming across people or things for the first time. We just don’t have enough “priors” to form a judgement. We are open to being pleasantly surprised. And once we are, it’s easier for us to stick with that opinion instead of reformulating it again.

Even machines (read: algorithms) value first impressions, since they’re built to hack our minds. This is why you see “freshness” as such an important concept in digital products, visible everywhere from home page feeds to recommendations.

If you miss your first impression window, it gets progressively harder to get subsequent chances. A second chance is much harder. A third, perhaps exponentially so.

Earlier this week, we launched Two by Two after many weeks of intense work. We hit #5 on Apple Podcasts across all categories on the very first day.

View in Browser

It would be more accurate to say we were pleasantly shocked, not surprised. Apple’s algorithms had gently nudged and pushed us up through the charts, past podcasts that generate way more daily downloads than us, because it prioritises “first impressions” and freshness.

Of course, staying there is virtually impossible, as the “decay” factor in the algorithm gradually allows us to “sink” into more realistic chart positions. But it’s up to us to use the advantage of our first impression to build something much more lasting—momentum.

So we were walking in Chicago admiring the amazing architecture and the skyscrapers. We walked past the Tribune Tower (which was home to the Chicago Tribune till 2018) and I just saw random tiles and pieces of stone attached to the outer wall of this building. It was very intriguing and it had a collection of fragments of buildings such as Taj Mahal, Great Pyramid, Great Wall of China, Hagia Sophia, Angkor Wat, Parthenon, Notre Dame, Petra—so on and so forth. At that time, we were in a rush to go someplace so we just walked by without admiring this fully.

At night, we again were walking past the Tribune Tower and we all took half an hour to see which more fragments were there and discovered that there are 149 stones embedded to the Tribune Tower. Even a piece of steel recovered from the World Trade Center has been added to the wall. 

The why is more interesting. Before the construction of the Tribune Tower, correspondents of the Tribune had brought back bars and rocks from multiple historically important sites at the behest of Colonel McCormick, the publisher of Tribune. Since then, a lot of fragments have been attached to the outer wall and facade.

That was definitely the best tale I’ve heard this week, especially because it involves journalists doing cool things.

4. On Pests, and other things

Good morning dear readers, this is Anushka with this week’s books section. While it may seem entirely unrelated, can you remember the last time you had pest control over at your house?

It’s a hassle. I especially loathe getting all the utensils and supplies out of the kitchen and then having to restock them. It’s a particularly humbling exercise, to see all my hoarded condiments and spices in various stages of decay. Anyway, while reading this short review for one of our recommendations today, I noticed the word pest within double quotes.

Rodents, cockroaches, even pigeons and monkeys… all are called pests by us humans. And it’s determined by a simple parameter: they’re breaking a law! They’re trespassing into our houses, munching on our food, intruding, vandalising. All illegal. For humans. And so, they’re punished for breaking these laws, made for humans, by humans. 

This is the premise of the book Animal Vegetable Criminal by Mary Roach—recommended today by Kirti Manian, a subscriber. I have to admit, the first reason that I quickly went and looked up this book wasn’t the premise itself. It was (and this is a bit of a tangent) the giddyness of finding a case of nominative determinism randomly—a phenomenon that theorises that people may be psychologically predisposed to pursue certain interests or careers that fit their names. Think of a firefighter called Les McBurney or a neuroscientist called Lord Brain or a meteorologist called Storm Field (all real examples, by the way). So then, to encounter an author called Mary Roach who’s written about the relationship between humans and “intrusive” animals made my day. 

But I found myself taken to more interesting places from there—in the same review, I found out about how different cultures deal with local wildlife menaces differently. Elephants in West Bengal, for one, who like getting a bit drunk on beer (and then injuring humans and property in nearby villages) are still respected and considered “gods” in these villages. I’ll leave the other examples out—and urge you to pick up this book.

5. Two by Two, “Storified”

At The Ken, we know how to turn stories into podcasts. My colleagues Snigdha and Rahel are pastmasters of it, with their brilliant daily podcast, Daybreak.

We also know how to turn podcasts into newsletters. You’re literally reading one, even though the “parent” podcast is no more.

But how do we turn a 60- to 70-minute freewheeling discussion podcast between four opinionated people into a story?

I tried.

Will Flipkart become Phonepe before Phonepe becomes Flipkart?

Walmart-owned Flipkart and Walmart-owned Phonepe are both headed by ambitious CEOs. Both companies want to IPO. Both have over 500 million users. And both are also increasingly competing on each other’s respective turfs

“Cousins” Flipkart and Phonepe are both massive, each boasting over 500 million registered users, implying roughly one in every two Indian adults.

Both are leaders in their respective categories, e-commerce and digital payments.

Both are led by ambitious and accomplished leaders. Kalyan Krishnamurthy, CEO of Flipkart, and Sameer Nigam, CEO of Phonepe.

Both are gearing up for their respective IPOs over the next few years.

They’re cousins because both are majority-owned by American retail giant Walmart.

So far so good.

Flipkart and Phonepe are slowly but surely launching new products that step into each other’s respective turfs.

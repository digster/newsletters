Over a year ago, a friend happened to mention that his doctor had advised him to have cold-pressed juices as part of his diet. So he was drinking two bottles of Raw Pressery juices every day. I hardly uttered a word when he told me this because I was quickly calculating how expensive this nutrient intake was.

At Rs 150 (~$2) for a tiny bottle, he was shelling out Rs 2,100 a week and Rs 9,000 a month.

I'm no cheapskate, but that's a helluva lot for liquefied fruit.

If only Raw Pressery had more devoted customers like my friend, it wouldn't be where it is now. Hobbled by a small addressable market and punishing distribution expenses, Raw was looking to course-correct. But just about then, Covid dealt the company a blow it wouldn't recover from. Its investors went back on a $3 million fund infusion agreed upon months earlier owing to differences over the deal terms. Raw lost the lifeline it was pinning its fading hopes on. The company, which gave hipsters a fad they could get behind, was then left with two choices: find a buyer ASAP or shut shop.

In a deal brokered by common investor Sequoia, the eight-year-old company was recently sold to dips and sauces maker Wingreens Farms for roughly Rs 100 crore ($13.7 million). That's a steep drop from the Rs 500 crore valuation Raw commanded just two years ago. Now, Wingreens has the unenviable task of proving Raw's critics wrong by walking a tightrope between trimming costs and scaling up.

Worth over $1.5 billion today, Fractal has raised close to $700 million in venture capital over its lifetime. But the path it took to get here is anything but boring. 

Growing up in a middle-class Indian family, Srikanth Velamakanni, Fractal’s co-founder and group CEO, remembers his father telling him there was no such thing as an “honest businessman”. The phrase was an oxymoron. 

“So when I grew up, I told myself that while I’d go and get a world class education, I would always work for a high quality company. I would never start a business. It was very clear to me,” says Srikanth as we sit down for the latest episode of First Principles. 

And yet, in 1999, just a year out of his MBA, he was telling his close friend that he was going to quit his very first job and start a business before the turn of the millennium. 

“I didn't know where that came from. It was as if a large language model was popping those words out automatically. And then I said to myself, okay, did I just say that? Really? Do I really mean it?”

If it’s possible to reduce to one word the changes that prompted Srikanth to make that u-turn, it’s this: Infosys. 

Through his engineering and then MBA degrees, Srikanth saw the gradual but undoubtedly professional and ethical rise of Infosys. If six professional co-founders could build an organisation like Infosys out of nothing, why couldn’t he? 

So, in 2000, Srikanth and five of his friends quit their jobs, scraped and pooled in Rs 2 lakh (~US$2,400) each, and started Fractal. 

But that was merely a “necessary but not sufficient” condition to succeed. The missing key ingredient was capital. 

“Having grown up in families where there wasn't that much wealth to go around, it was inconceivable that you could start and build a business. I always thought you needed capital to create wealth,” he says.

Therefore, one of the ideas that bound all the co-founders was their desire to raise venture capital for the new company.

That was in 2000. 23 years ago. 

Srikanth says it took Fractal 13 years to raise any meaningful venture capital. 

The stress got to the founders. There were differences and disputes. Which put paid to funding rounds that were at the term sheet stages. 

Those that were left slogged on.
I learned during this time that there is a power in building a profitable business from the ground up. It is slower, but you learn the fundamentals of the business much better when you know that you have to make ends meet without raising money.

Then, in 2009, a year shy of Fractal’s tenth anniversary (only 2% of companies that are founded get to celebrate this milestone), a venture capitalist who also happened to be a senior of Srikanth’s from his engineering college dropped the ultimate bomb, after spending an entire day reviewing Fractal’s business and operations. 

“We really like your business, but we don't like you. We can't fund Fractal because you're so important to the business. We have to have faith in your ability to scale this business, and we don't,” the investor told him. 

It takes a lot to build companies for the long term. The school of hard knocks is unforgiving on young companies and first time founders. The odds are measured in terms of survival first, and not success. 

Fractal and Srikanth were part of the 2% that survived the first 10 years. Listen to the latest episode of First Principles to understand how the soon-to-be 25-year-old company plans to beat the odds and still be around 50 years from now.

In a typically wide-ranging discussion recorded inside Fractal’s Mumbai office overlooking the sprawling Aarey forest, I asked Srikanth about a lot of topics. Like: 

The sudden and rapid rise of AI, and its implications

“For the first time, at least in my life or my career, I've seen AI reach a point where it feels like artificial general intelligence. The idea that machines are smarter than human beings in every respect seems around the corner. It could be as little as three, four years.”

[…]

“Everything powerful can be used for bad purposes. Question is, can we stop? It seems impossible. The pace of scientific progress is unidirectional, and it is unceasing.”

The number of co-founders “required” for starting a business

“Three-people co-founding teams have the problem that two people can gang up and the third person feels isolated. Four-people teams can get into a deadlock.”

Investing like a venture capitalist

“We were about to do a fund raise from Khazanah in 2016. So I approached the new investors saying, look, we could make a 25% EBITDA margin in this company. That should be very easy. But I do not want to make that margin right now.

I want to spend half of that money in growing our current business to accelerate growth. And the remaining half, which is 12.5% of our revenue, I want to use that for building Fractal startups (which are now called alpha companies in Fractal parlance). We wanted to incubate them.”

The evolving roles of a CEO

“I think I'm the cultural leader of Fractal. The most important value is to know that there is a soul of Fractal and I must represent that. And that we will not do something stupid because if Srikanth is in charge, nothing stupid will happen.”

Important metrics for growth

“We also look at one more metric, which is revenue growth percentage plus profitability percentage. We add the two numbers and we call it the efficiency score. There's a rule of 40 in the world, which is like if you have 20% revenue growth and 20% profitability, you're at 40. So we look at that number and we want to be at least a minimum of 50 or 60 ideally. And some years we have done even better than that.”

On becoming a better dad via ‘feedforward’

“I believe in Marshall Goldsmith and his style of coaching. Marshall Goldsmith is one of the greatest executive coaches in the world. He says, ask everyone, all your stakeholders, how can I get better?

So I went to my daughter and asked her this question a couple of years back, saying, hey, how can I be a better dad? And she actually gave me some very good feedback. I was surprised that at eight years of age, she had things that she wanted me to get better at. She said, when you're at home, please do not use any phones. Spend more time with me, and so on.”

Multi-sensorial reading hacks

“I usually have the Kindle version and the audio version, or I use Kindle and use text to speech to read as well as listen at the same time. I read everywhere. If I'm commuting, I'm reading. If I'm in the car for 10 minutes, I'm reading. If I'm in the plane, I'm reading."

Good Morning Ishan,
As I type this, Delhi—and North India as a whole—is officially in the middle of a heat wave. Temperatures at a five-year high, Met department on red alert, the works. About 45 degrees Celsius in the middle of the day, compared to the early 30s and late 20s for my colleagues in Bombay and Bangalore.
It’s amazing what an impact even small changes in temperature have on us. 20 degrees Celsius? Lovely. 40 degrees? Feels like death—even though it’s just 7% hotter. The future does not bode well (I leave the math up to you).
Yet even as we boil, we toil to bring you stories that—we hope—are a breath of fresh air.
To kick off the week, we had Olina, Arundhati and Rohin banding together to break down a loans crisis fermenting at Byju’s. Parents unable to cancel bail on the loans, leaving lenders high and dry, while Byju’s chugs on.

With each of its brands a different company, sometimes even competing with each other, it has a total share of over 30%, beating Samsung and breathing down Xiaomi’s neck.
Then we had Seema and Suraksha writing on Portea—India’s biggest home healthcare company, and a case study in why “move fast and break things” doesn’t work in healthcare. It raised, and burned, tens of millions of dollars over the past few years, and launched its services across the country while others trod cautiously. But today, [brand Portea finds itself taking a beating](http://link.the-ken.com/wf/click?upn=HQSq6eeRV1xAVaGuFOahuNCZVVXPeP-2FJpSqxhvIUDrkHxsiViHfiTyDlvakuipYvRGiSLRMLDq-2FriSgmeuRdvbsgi1UnSj-2FeoPS-2Ffv-2FGNyqcLChzVHDNXYfLY3anY9uoPyckOFaQAlW8nJFicbdbu2FR4qhd7ePfr12pc511KoDRQ0jqkl03jGz2phOtlzB2ZOrgzNmDODmNAjBZI3rhE3gz-2Fm9xys1JOxUy0KIP8DA-3D_S4jLOsNx-2Fv6Jev2nvow8b7P9pBCWGd8KNZfb200gkZLQNlXQHio0noZzXtsWoloZmPWeyZsh7a4QCV9ufwCUKi00jlBT0uEdvwYDMuVuqcPfsSv3L1JehE6jmPgPsTQ123wGA9Hel0wmnSU1-2B-2B5F66Arub0T0-2Bv1RECe-2BheoQGU5bonmTfSdjz1LR84abzwgcWOeTSjnzw14dpxW2lH22KheHMQMERGK53seSLFM-2BoshrcrfPPW1ElcoEofLRAo5HXLwhOKK6LbDx2ZGw-2F4AwMDiQA0I2ohOBUbJ72afNQlb030wTy-2F5i7qaOI1lN-2BisQuPapa-2Fb6IJ9OC9nwgjmsaKdVmvlF2bDxgEDpwqgO8WrnCj82g5gmM-2Fzi-2BBwv4ko6vWt-2FH4vafN4lQ3PcwGNSOznyM77kN9HbbAJ9gmwi4c-3D), with one of its largest hospital partners thinking of backing out. How did it get so bad?
Returning to Chinese tech companies, on Thursday, our contributor Nilesh Christopher had an interesting tale to tell—how UC Browser, which held dominated the Indian browser field as recently as 2016, was crushed by Google Chrome. It’s a battle between two giants, Alibaba and Google, that has [shaped how Indians access the web](http://link.the-ken.com/wf/click?upn=HQSq6eeRV1xAVaGuFOahuNCZVVXPeP-2FJpSqxhvIUDrmNfhbMHM7T1xgirkADiYlkHDp1sAUNzRgBNNL4wUNavE63gWSWAosiaDDtbJ-2FEkdhCBZd-2FdqNh0ar-2Fx4w5pI2IAWopgfdV-2FOXswJ0upTyTgCfmYdI1Bp6cd42-2BEGOXs8cfHhAd15hkj70mHvegUnc-2F_S4jLOsNx-2Fv6Jev2nvow8b7P9pBCWGd8KNZfb200gkZLQNlXQHio0noZzXtsWoloZmPWeyZsh7a4QCV9ufwCUKi00jlBT0uEdvwYDMuVuqcPfsSv3L1JehE6jmPgPsTQ123wGA9Hel0wmnSU1-2B-2B5F66Arub0T0-2Bv1RECe-2BheoQGU5bonmTfSdjz1LR84abzwgcWOeTSjnzw14dpxW2lH22Bk21tQJ4cZLrllTNK5-2BDc6RfqH3GYw-2BVeYtQAMNgoE8D8etPSUVvKZVDo5hCFvxZqvdi3Fn287MBIE8fQ-2BgxoRJCNenLC9Vyta-2FlVM11Pd-2BMrnSiebPvnB1i38WSvA91F6S2IgGBcJ7-2Fc4WUeqDsEn1s0aqQS0kn5BICCiKSoqkJ69phcTs-2BDvGmp9Bhfhu-2Fx8i8wRicwiXkabCVFbWd5k-3D). And could dictate whose ecosystem wins in one of the fastest growing smartphone and internet markets.
On Friday, Roshni took us into the complex shifts in the world of… almonds? Over the last seven years, Indian has been inundated with California almonds. India’s cultural affinity for the nut, she writes, makes it a prime target for the well-funded Almond Board of California. Which used a mix of savvy marketing and cheaper goods to [push out Iranian, Afghani and even the local Kashmiri varieties](http://link.the-ken.com/wf/click?upn=HQSq6eeRV1xAVaGuFOahuNCZVVXPeP-2FJpSqxhvIUDrnOq8eT7SK1H4OcbT-2FbavNeRcAh2T03ypEf4JmZMQ7JbrUYoMrDfu3F0RzXLi38zAix9NR4vJUo2JQez-2F01tQZdyZ8rNyTCyV7vDQPldw-2ByuS1YuIehw5wpTbF3Pb7pdV4OoQu4N7ZN224-2F9H8A57Ff_S4jLOsNx-2Fv6Jev2nvow8b7P9pBCWGd8KNZfb200gkZLQNlXQHio0noZzXtsWoloZmPWeyZsh7a4QCV9ufwCUKi00jlBT0uEdvwYDMuVuqcPfsSv3L1JehE6jmPgPsTQ123wGA9Hel0wmnSU1-2B-2B5F66Arub0T0-2Bv1RECe-2BheoQGU5bonmTfSdjz1LR84abzwgcWOeTSjnzw14dpxW2lH22JFqkDvbp8I6AIbsQkcTY9MpQvWFfQrzrDAA6wF0IdOl2KWAe5MiwnvCcw33Of-2FTjxId0LLWr8DBwLieUEIOXmgiLsnNSw15IqsvT605-2FZ48URjK3SLgk7XJKr-2FcRSAKtPHttE5XxHiBF2HvenLU-2FjjK0zJHGj4stgYr4UwJPjduoW3bSq3sM-2B-2FIk86afJFbyVPH7NAUcsNCQsCyhvRLO3I-3D).
To wrap up the week, we had Ruhi’s thoughtful piece on the state of mental therapy companies in India. Startups selling remote therapy had swept in, hoping to take the stigma off of approaching mental health professionals—but [they quickly learnt that it wasn’t so easy](http://link.the-ken.com/wf/click?upn=HQSq6eeRV1xAVaGuFOahuNCZVVXPeP-2FJpSqxhvIUDrn0oW8D1OOBmeqb4Nq9rd1cBydVb-2ByYSIx9eSuupUKBMQIYIsU9lkpoe3TyjJBS4uV-2BIqeUi2V0BsnP1dutm1LvScmRSY-2FcXr4aMwt0w5-2FzGkOmo5VlAhEm2WhRtFpK5w-2Fp14B32xKB69EzMcIpAMFA1Wc7jE6VYulYVYn5oOftQA-3D-3D_S4jLOsNx-2Fv6Jev2nvow8b7P9pBCWGd8KNZfb200gkZLQNlXQHio0noZzXtsWoloZmPWeyZsh7a4QCV9ufwCUKi00jlBT0uEdvwYDMuVuqcPfsSv3L1JehE6jmPgPsTQ123wGA9Hel0wmnSU1-2B-2B5F66Arub0T0-2Bv1RECe-2BheoQGU5bonmTfSdjz1LR84abzwgcWOeTSjnzw14dpxW2lH22KjgoePQgB0CX-2FA5MlVT4-2FYsGJqbRG2g-2FOV-2BWZgubCvop5Flm5k4YdSQn9VZWQ7s5VVW00y7jVfZo0qZQ0HhJHmirNAPY9GtgkoViOnsvwlHZxXt5VjZ8oHaKhbeWPgzTpAJRp-2FghGwDH7IICblLzfzrrVOpqWnE6EYtwRL8wvuCQESFigwTSo0vc57lON4IX0O2OJHQnLhKiMqdwf-2BuWqQ-3D). The silver lining is that apps that offer self-help tools are gaining traction, and can help supplement in-person care.

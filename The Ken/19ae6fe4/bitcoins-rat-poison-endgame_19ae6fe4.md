More than seven years ago, Warren Buffett called Bitcoin (BTC) “rat poison,” a financial mirage, he warned, defined by wild and unpredictable price swings.
Recent events appear to prove his point. After reaching an all-time high of over $126,000 in early October, Bitcoin’s price plummeted 36% to around $80,600 by late November. It has since recovered somewhat to about $92,800 in early December, but the volatility remains constant. What’s especially notable is how little it took to spark such a drop: an average of just $3 billion in daily trading—0.15% of Bitcoin’s total issued supply of 19.95 million tokens—was enough to shake investor confidence over a single month.
This kind of plunge is nothing new. Over the past decade and a half, Bitcoin has experienced a number of major crashes, losing between 70% and 99% of its value in 2011, 2013, 2018, and 2021. To put it simply, the Bitcoin edifice has always stood and continues to stand on shaky ground.
So, will the “rat poison” ever expire? Will Bitcoin finally stabilise? For now, its history suggests we shouldn’t hold our breath.
The numbers, however, tell only half the story. Bitcoin’s significance is linked by its fanatical adherents to:
- Its appreciation from a humble $0.003 in March 2010 to its current price.
- Its permanence, supported by its widespread purchase—it is estimated that some 500 million people hold Bitcoin directly and through exchange-traded funds, or through institutions.
- Its perceived invulnerability, on account of its finite nature
For all that, it still falls short as a means of transaction settlement, sustainable investment, and real source of value.
First, despite the vision articulated in 2008 by the pseudonymously named Satoshi Nakamoto in a white paper titled Bitcoin: A Peer-to-Peer Electronic Cash System, Bitcoin never really gained widespread acceptance as a medium of exchange. While famously used on 22 May, 2010 to buy two pizzas for 10,000 BTC in Florida, its role has shifted. Since that time, on account of regulatory proscription in the US and being able to enable incognito transactions, BTCs have been linked more to illicit activity than everyday commerce. As recently as October 2025, for instance, the US government seized $14 billion in Bitcoin from Cambodia’s Prince Group over allegations of a massive crypto scam—just one example in a long history of arrests and confiscations.
Ultimately, Bitcoin is not a legitimate currency because no government or entity stands behind it. Even in El Salvador, where it was briefly adopted as a legal tender, Bitcoin functioned more as a digital token pegged to the US dollar. This experiment highlighted its impracticality: prices could swing widely between ordering a meal and paying the bill, making routine transactions fraught with uncertainty. Eventually, facing an acute balance of payments problem, El Salvador agreed to scale back BTCs in December 2024, disqualifying them for tax payments, under pressure from the International Monetary Fund (IMF), in lieu of a [$1.4 billion](https://www.imf.org/en/news/articles/2024/12/18/pr-24485-el-salvador-imf-reaches-staff-level-agreement-on-an-eff-arrangement) bailout package.
Moreover, Bitcoin’s reliability as a long-term investment remains highly questionable. From the very beginning, the ecosystem has been plagued by crises of trust. The first major Bitcoin exchange Mt. Gox collapsed in 2014 after hackers stole $500 million, just a year after Bitcoin’s market cap first surpassed $1 billion. Many over-leveraged firms followed suit, with Sam Bankman-Fried’s FTX being the most notorious example in recent years to highlight Bitcoin’s risks as an investment.
Citing threats to financial stability, energy security, and economic sovereignty, 24 countries—including China, which once hosted the world’s largest crypto industry—have banned Bitcoin trading. Others, like India, imposed burdensome tariffs, subjecting Bitcoin to a 30% capital gains tax and a 1% transaction levy in addition to surcharges and GST levied by exchanges, keeping it in a state of regulatory uncertainty.
The core problem, however, is foundational. As a speculative asset, Bitcoin isn’t valued for generating cash flow like a company or paying interest like a bond. Instead, investors rely on technical charts and market sentiment, hoping to time a profitable exit. This makes its price widely reactive to unrelated external events, as seen in the recent crash. That sell-off was attributed not to Bitcoin’s own fuzzy fundamentals, but to stock market volatility from fears of an AI bubble, shifting expectation for Federal Reserve rate cuts, and political headlines like Trump’s renewed call for a trade war with China.
Even the recent entrance of major institutions like Morgan Stanley, JP Morgan Chase and others via spot Bitcoin funds hasn’t brought stability. These investors treat Bitcoin like any other high-risk asset; when prices tumbled in November, wiping out $1 trillion in value, many sold to cover losses, demonstrating little inherent faith in Bitcoin itself.
This leads to Bitcoin’s existential flaw: a lack of underlying utility or economic value. Many of Bitcoin’s assertions are risible. These include:
- Aspiring to be digital gold, even though unlike a physical commodity, Bitcoin is intangible code with no industrial use.
- Ascribing economic value to solving a complex mathematical puzzle at enormous cost—called proof of work—to validate transactions and mine new coins by way of reward.
- Being a hedge against fiat currency by acting as a check on monetary policy through limited availability, even though this would undermine the sovereign currency (US dollars) from which its value derives.
In reality, Bitcoin faces a conundrum much like that of Spanish silver in the 1500s.
Within 50 years of conquering Mexico in 1521, Spain had flooded Europe with so much silver that its value plummeted, threatening to undermine the entire colonial enterprise. The endeavour was saved only when the Ming Dynasty, switching to silver coinage when the public lost faith in paper currency, created an insatiable new source of Chinese demand.
History may be repeating itself with Bitcoin.
Since returning to the White House with strong support from the crypto community, Donald Trump has promised to legitimise digital tokens. His administration has issued an executive order to create a Strategic Bitcoin Reserve, mainly obtained from civil and criminal forfeiture—on which the government may currently be carrying unrealised losses—and to allow alternative assets like Bitcoin into 401(k) pension plans, ostensibly as an inflation hedge and a tool to reduce national debt.
As a result, institutional interest has surged. Trump’s own wealth has grown through crypto investments. Financial giants like Blackrock now own over 4% of all Bitcoin, and companies like Microstrategy, the largest corporate holder of BTC, acquired so much Bitcoin that their stock price was correlated directly to Bitcoin’s value for most of 2025. When Bitcoin crashed recently, Microstrategy’s share price fell 60% from its peak, illustrating this risky interdependence.
For a local context, we [wrote](https://the-ken.com/story/how-jetking-lets-indians-bet-on-bitcoin-without-paying-crypto-tax/) about Jetking in August which is holding Bitcoin on its balance sheet to give Indian investors exposure without facing the country’s steep 30% crypto tax. But it’s in murky waters now as the BSE denied its application to list shares from a preferential allotment.

So, what are these powerful investors like Blackrock and others hoping for?
Certainly not for an irreversible crash. Instead, they are wagering that the US government will eventually add Bitcoin to its reserves—driving up the price so they can sell their holdings at a massive profit.
Though, this can result in a perverse outcome: if a misguided administration actually tries to “monetise” Bitcoin to pay down debt, a sudden government sell-off could trigger a catastrophic price collapse. In response, the government might feel forced to halt sales, and even buy back Bitcoin to stabilise the market. At that point, Bitcoin would become “too big to fail”, leaving American taxpayers to underwrite the losses of a speculative bubble.
But, hey, at least the rat poison will have lost its potency. Institutions and pension funds will breathe a sigh of relief, and the public may learn to disregard Bitcoin as just another Ponzi scheme, even as their attention is directed towards another shiny object.

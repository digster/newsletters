Here’s a question: why is India’s Jio Platforms looking to list on an overseas stock market, maybe in the United States? Could it have something to do with China? Jio Platforms: Made in India, listed abroad?​

Savio

Days after India cleared the deck for homegrown companies to list on overseas stock markets without having a domestic listing, media reports say Jio Platforms is considering such a move. The preferred destination of Jio Platforms, the digital and telecommunications unit of Reliance Industries (RIL), could be the United States. More specifically, the tech-friendly Nasdaq stock exchange.

Which also makes sense given that the US is the headquarters of the five firms that have picked up a 17.12% stake in Jio Platforms in the past few weeks for a total consideration https://www.business-standard.com/article/companies/reliance-looks-at-nasdaq-listing-for-jio-platforms-ipo-likely-by-2021-120052700048_1.html of Rs 78,562 crore ($10.3 billion), valuing the company at Rs 4,91,000 crore ($73 billion).

Source: Business Standard

A US listing is widely perceived to give companies cheaper cost of capital, a larger shareholder base, greater liquidity and enhanced prestige. It also helps foreign investors avoid currency risks.

Investing in Indian equities comes with not only the asset class, but also carries a currency risk. A depreciating currency tends to impact dollar returns for foreign portfolio investors. With this move, a foreign listing would cut the foreign currency risk for this class of investors.

FM Nirmala Sitharaman clears the decks for Indian companies to directly list overseas, Financial Express https://www.financialexpress.com/industry/fm-nirmala-sitharaman-clears-the-decks-for-indian-companies-to-directly-list-overseas/1962061/

An overseas listing will give the private equity investors a better exit https://www.livemint.com/companies/news/mukesh-ambani-may-consider-overseas-ipo-for-jio-platforms-11590502364713.html, one banker told Mint. PEs like KKR, Silver Lake, Vista Equity, and General Atlantic?

The American dream

The American Depository Receipts (ADRs) of a host of big Indian companies—like IT firm Infosys and HDFC Bank—already trade in the US markets. However, they are eclipsed by Chinese heavyweights like Alibaba, Tencent, Baidu and JD.com. The escalating US-China tensions could soon change that.

The US Senate passed a bill that could prevent companies from listing in the country unless they follow its audit and regulatory rules. The bill applies to all foreign companies, but it is targeted https://www.reuters.com/article/us-usa-china-stocks/senate-passes-bill-that-could-block-chinese-firms-from-u-s-securities-exchanges-idUSKBN22W2Y2 at Chinese firms. No wonder companies like JD.com are moving closer to home https://www.reuters.com/article/us-china-listings-hongkong/netease-and-jd-com-set-dates-for-5-billion-hong-kong-listings-idUSKBN22Y1UU—to Hong Kong, which is tweaking rules to make them feel more welcome https://www.cnbc.com/2020/05/19/china-techs-alibaba-xiaomi-to-benefit-as-hong-kong-changes-hang-seng-rules.html.

That’s where a tech cold war may help. Wall Street investors have been able to profit from the explosion of e-commerce in China, even though the likes of Facebook and Amazon.com Inc. are largely shut out of the People’s Republic. If that access gets curbed by geopolitics, then Ambani’s story becomes more compelling. He can offer the vision of a vast retail network that has Facebook’s popular WhatsApp messaging system processing orders and payments for neighborhood shops connected digitally to a billion-plus buyers. That could be a big draw. A U.S. home is within Ambani’s reach, especially if Chinese firms are forced to vacate.

A Tech Billionaire May Find Use in a Cold War, Bloomberg https://www.bloomberg.com/opinion/articles/2020-05-27/ambani-s-jio-can-profit-from-u-s-china-tech-war?srnd=premium-asia

Then, there’s also the small matter of Jio’s 5G push https://the-ken.com/story/reliance-jios-5g-push-to-be-indias-answer-to-huawei/ to be India’s answer to Huawei. What’s that they say? “The enemy of my enemy …”

TikTok has a reinforcing feedback loop problem in India

Rohin

ByteDance is among the most disruptive technology companies in the past decade. At the apex of its rapidly expanding product portfolio lies TikTok. One of the best analyses of this powerful company, which emerged out of nowhere to find entirely new product, market and growth opportunities, is this Substack post https://turner.substack.com/p/the-rise-of-tiktok-and-understanding by Turner Novak, a VC with Gelt Venture Capital.

ByteDance is arguably the most serious competitor to Google, ever. It reaches consumers in the two largest middle classes in the world, China and the US, at a scale larger than any other digital advertising company. Like its Chinese counterparts, its seen success with monetization models beyond just advertising, which US-based competitors have struggled with to-date.

There’s a third market too with a massive middle class where TikTok rules—India. The country accounts for nearly a third https://www.moneycontrol.com/news/technology/covid-19-impact-tiktok-crosses-2-billion-downloads-on-ios-android-records-best-quarter-for-any-app-5207081.html of its 600 million-plus downloads. 

But that popularity has also painted a target on its back from enemies large and small, who want it banned or controlled.

Indian YouTubers https://www.businessinsider.in/india/news/thanks-to-carry-minati-indians-want-to-ban-tiktok-again-for-the-fourth-time-in-a-month/articleshow/75760470.cms want it banned. They’ve been behind a coordinated campaign to downrate the TikTok app on Google’s Play Store, after a popular YouTuber got into the video version of fisticuffs with a famous TikTok star. TikTok’s Android app rating fell to as low as 1.2/5, with Google taking the drastic step of mass-deleting https://in.news.yahoo.com/tiktoks-rating-again-google-deletes-074932801.html?guccounter=1 5 million reviews to correct things (TikTok’s rating went up from 1.2 to 1.6).

An affiliate of the RSS https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/news/politics-and-nation/rss-arm-sees-an-enemy-in-china-wants-hot-seller-tik-tok-out-of-indian-phones/articleshow/68056957.cms, a powerful Hindu nationalist organisation that is closely aligned with the ruling party at the centre, wanted it banned. Its official publication lent its voice to the issue with a scary article https://www.organiser.org/Encyc/2020/5/19/TikTok-allows-videos-glorifying-rape-love-jihad-acid-attacks-and-other-criminal-content.html.

Animal rights activists https://theprint.in/india/working-for-china-maneka-gandhi-slams-tiktok-wants-strict-against-animal-abuse-videos/427434/, including sitting Members of Parliament, are angry at TikTok for turning a blind eye towards videos that include animal abuse.

Women’s rights organisations https://www.hindustantimes.com/india-news/tiktok-faces-criticism-as-ncw-leads-calls-for-ban-over-hate-content/story-teLpPZtz5MpdpfFt1rv0kO.html want it banned because, every now and then, videos condoning or glorifying acts of violence on women pop up.

Unknown actors are using it to spread misinformation and hatred against Muslims https://www.indiatoday.in/india/story/surge-in-tiktok-videos-aimed-at-misleading-indian-muslims-over-coronavirus-precautions-1662930-2020-04-03.

TikTok is getting sucked into a strengthening vortex spiral of negative coverage. Its Chinese ownership complicates things further, with many campaigns asking for Indians to uninstall it out of respect for Prime Minister Narendra Modi’s “Vocal for Local https://www.google.com/search?q=vocal+for+local+tiktok&hl=en&tbm=vid&source=lnms&sa=X&ved=0ahUKEwinn8Pvr9HpAhUDC-wKHb8TAfwQ_AUIDigB&biw=1824&bih=930&dpr=1.58” campaign.

A startling sign that this may be working comes from the inexplicably rapid rise https://www.moneycontrol.com/news/technology/indias-tiktok-rival-app-mitron-clocks-five-million-downloads-within-one-month-of-launch-5314561.html of a clone in less than a month. Even as TikTok downloads fell 34% and 28% https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/small-biz/startups/newsbuzz/covid-impact-tiktok-stares-at-a-gloomy-india-picture/articleshow/75989697.cms in April and May, respectively.

Why does TikTok unnerve so many? Because it unlocked social media for a much larger and poorer section of the population and let them become stars at it. From Turner Novak’s post referenced earlier:

Extremely short videos, algorithmic discovery on the FYP, the muted importance of a follower graph, and constant traffic into the app via heavy marketing, cross-platform posting, promotion by influencers, and (eventually) habit allowed TikTok to deliberately control the allocation of social capital across the network to its most talented creators, both new or old, as new users poured into the app. Alex Zhu, Musical.ly founder and now Head of Product at TikTok, likens the process to creating a new country and giving a greenfield of opportunities to a new class of creators.

This new class of creators is upsetting many power structures, as this article https://theprint.in/opinion/pov/tiktok-vs-youtube-is-the-new-class-war-on-internet-it-all-began-with-a-roast/423346/ by Unnati Sharma in The Print highlights. 

YouTubers who roast TikTok content creators often call its videos cringeworthy. They mock their appearances and use homophobic slurs for TikTokers who often cross-dress. CarryMinati in his recent video used words like ‘Meetha’ and ‘Pari’, which are often used to refer to the people from transgender community. That’s not all. Some also refer TikTokers as ‘Shudras of the internet’ with the popular perception being that they are mostly lower-class, low-caste individuals in low profile jobs who make videos for ‘time-pass’.

This only reflects the insecurities of YouTubers who are now perturbed by the sharp rise in popularity and fame of TikTok content and its creators from small towns and rural areas.

The Chinese video-making app is immensely popular, especially among the teenagers, and has provided a platform to many for expressing their creativity. From a rural housemanager to a brick-maker to a street vendor, anyone can become viral and famous overnight. These influencers from small towns and villages, with their newfound voice on TikTok, are finally in the category of content creators and telling their stories.

In fact, the TikTok vs YouTube war is looking like a microcosm of the larger political story raging across India–between raw aspiration and entrenched entitlement.

TikTok vs YouTube is the new class war on internet. It all began with a roast, The Print

Negative and reinforcing feedback loops, once at a critical mass, can be hard to come out of.

Glove mania, at what cost? 

Kay

Malaysian glove manufacturers are having their heyday, thanks to Covid-19. They’re expected to deliver about 225 billion gloves this year, and that is about 65% of the total world requirement of 330 billion. 

Top Glove, the world’s largest supplier of nitrile and latex medical gloves, is expecting to command 30-35% global market share this year, as overseas orders surge. Smaller glove manufacturers have also been topping the charts in local stock exchange Bursa Malaysia—four out of 10 top gainers on 27 May were glove makers.

(Source: Bursa Malaysia)

Good time to be in the glove-making business, right? Yes and no. 

The #1 challenge for the industry, labour shortage, continues to haunt glove makers. As of 28 March, Top Glove said it needs about 10% extra workers https://www.reuters.com/article/us-health-coronavirus-malaysia-top-glove/worlds-largest-glove-maker-sees-shortage-as-coronavirus-fight-spikes-idUSKBN21G04Z to keep up with surging productions. Top Glove and another local glove manufacturer, WRP Asia Pacific, have been accused of mistreating https://www.theguardian.com/global-development/2018/dec/09/nhs-rubber-gloves-made-in-malaysian-factories-accused-of-forced-labour its migrant labourers, who hail mainly from Nepal and Bangladesh. Both companies have denied the allegations. But as pressure mounts https://twitter.com/usembassykl/status/1243344412135972866 on glove-makers, who is to say that they will not turn to forced labour to meet rising demands? 

To make matters worse, the Malaysian government has seized https://www.straitstimes.com/asia/se-asia/coronavirus-breaks-out-at-detention-centres-in-malaysia-health-ministry-warns-against thousands of undocumented migrants since the beginning of May in order to curb Covid-19. This led to a surge in new cases among the foreigners held at cramped immigration depots, causing further labour shortage in various industries. Who’s really in the wrong, though? Migrant workers? Or labour agents and employers that withhold the workers’ identifications? 

The snowballing problem of unsanitary habits

Olina

Some in the Covid-19 frontlines—like sanitation workers—don’t really have the option of minimising their exposure to the virus.

And their job is getting way more hazardous by the day; they’re now supposed to collect and dispose of biomedical waste from containment zones, or Covid-quarantined families. The new guidelines by the Indian Council of Medical Research (ICMR), asking patients with milder symptoms to recuperate at home, makes their job even tougher. “Mild” isn’t mutually exclusive from “infectious”.

Authorities have figured out a routine for their workers, complete with personal protective gear like hazmat suits, masks, demarcated pick-up vehicles, and special “yellow” bags to collect the waste in. It’s a complex process, bound to get more complicated as more mild and asymptomatic cases are asked to stay home or within hot spots. 

Getting rid of Covid waste isn’t an insurmountable problem yet. But it has the potential to overwhelm quickly. Take the Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB) guidelines for one, which are pretty clear about what kind of biomedical waste should go where:

(Source: Citizen Matters)

This is a full layout for a healthcare facility. But if more patients were to get home treatment, isn’t it possible that they’d generate various types of biomedical waste—possibly even syringes and intravenous tubes? The risk for sanitation workers, and their responsibility to keep this newly-hazardous bio-waste from infecting the uninfected, grows.

The municipal authorities have managed to keep workers safe so far. But the snowball effect has already started. There is, reportedly, inefficient monitoring on the ground, a lack of upgraded incinerators to burn the waste, and possibly the most evil, banal problem: mixing waste like discarded masks and gloves with solid waste.

Guess old habits are generating newer, potentially fatal problems for society:

In Pune, face masks dumped by users in household garbage were being collected by rag pickers, The Indian Express reported https://indianexpress.com/article/cities/pune/coronavirus-no-system-in-place-for-disposal-of-used-face-masks-6327063/ on March 23, 2020. “There is no mechanism for collection and disposal of masks and medical waste generated by more than 2,000 people under home-quarantine across the district for having a travel history or showing COVID-19 symptoms,” the report said.

In Thane, a man was caught putting over 100,000 https://mumbaimirror.indiatimes.com/mumbai/crime/1lakh-masks-dumped-in-bhiwandi/articleshow/74543028.cms used face masks out to dry so that he could resell them in the market, the Times of India reported https://timesofindia.indiatimes.com/videos/city/mumbai/mumbai-man-arrested-for-dumping-used-face-masks-in-open-space/videoshow/74590346.cms on March 12, 2020. 

A heap of medical waste was found lying in the open in Delhi’s Sharan Vihar area, The Indian Express reported https://indianexpress.com/article/cities/delhi/kalindi-kunj-refugee-camp-medical-waste-coronavirus-hunger-6341211/ on April 1, 2020. Discarded face masks, tunics, gowns, caps and syringes were found in the dump.

Sanitation Workers At Risk From Discarded Medical Waste Related To COVID-19, IndiaSpend https://www.indiaspend.com/sanitation-workers-at-risk-from-discarded-medical-waste-related-to-covid-19/

Paying for space junk

Savio

Not only on land, but garbage is a problem in space as well. And there’s one unique solution.

From dead satellites to bits of rockets, the amount of debris orbiting the planet is already so great that space agencies often have to alter the course of satellites to avoid collisions.

With the world increasingly reliant on orbital infrastructure to maintain communications links and steer new generations of autonomous vehicles, scientists warn that the danger posed by debris is growing exponentially.

The best way to deal with it would be to charge satellite operators an annual orbital-use fee for every satellite launched, according to an economics analysis by researchers at the University of Colorado at Boulder.

Could a space congestion charge clear up junk-ridden skies?, Thomson Reuters Foundation https://news.trust.org/item/20200525181746-5n81u/

Investors turn towards ESG funds

Shreedhar

One school of thought for a post-Covid world (or a world surviving alongside Covid-19, rather) is that all efforts should be made to get things back to normal. 

Another school of thought is that a post-Covid world should not repeat the same mistakes that made the pandemic worse to endure.

Where we end up is anybody’s guess. But some investors seem to have made up their mind.

Sustainable investing – the integration of environmental, social and governance (ESG) factors into analysis and decision making - has seen a remarkable rise over the past couple of years. Starting from modest levels 15 years ago, it is now estimated at over US$ 30 trillion. So far, the outbreak of the Covid-19 virus has not stopped the growth of ESG investing, which has seen a steady increase in inflows and better-than-average returns since the start of the pandemic.

Covid-19 Is Accelerating ESG Investing And Corporate Sustainability Practices, Forbes https://www.forbes.com/sites/georgkell/2020/05/19/covid-19-is-accelerating-esg-investing-and-corporate-sustainability-practices/#77b8591626bb

ESG funds have attracted investments https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/markets/stocks/news/sustainable-funds-in-india-attract-500-million-during-covid-19-selloff/articleshow/75784924.cms in India too. 

There’s another way to think about it. ESG funds invest in companies that invest on multiple fronts, including environmental and social. Companies which do that are also more likely to be those that can afford to do it and are keeping a long-term view in mind. 

Maybe, just maybe, ESG companies are just better companies. And investors prefer companies that are better over those that aren’t.

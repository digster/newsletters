Today’s theme is power. Regulators want to flatten the unseemly gains made by OTTs with a new rule book. WhatsApp, with its reach over millions, knows full-well it can pull the plug on dependent business models. The Indian government is playing a game of 50 questions with banned Chinese app makers. And TikTok’s power is that in its absence, it has never been more present.
For the last time this week, let’s dive in.

Praying at the altar of an absent god
Olina
I have an old tale that lines up perfectly with contemporary events. But you will have to allow me a detour. Bear with me.
In a pokey old college lecture, I’d once chanced upon the theory of the “absent centre”. It’s a sort of simplified derivation from Algerian-French philosopher Jacques Derrida’s theory of [presence vs absence](https://lucian.uchicago.edu/blogs/mediatheory/keywords/absence-presence/).
Absent centre, in religious terms, is the absence of God. The argument though, is that the most staunch atheists turn the absence of God into its own theism. God is present even in absence—always at the heart of the denial.
I only venture into philosophy to draw a rather surprising modern-day parallel.
TikTok is Indian tech’s absent centre.
Last week’s headlines have been full of announcements by would-be TikToks. Each one—whether InMobi’s Roposo, the Modi-inspired Mitron or homegrown ShareChat’s Moj—seems to have hit a personal best.
|
“Moj is a short-video platform that emulates the features of TikTok, which was amongst the apps banned by the government. Sharechat itself is a competitor to Helo, another social media platform that was owned by TikTok’s parent company, Bytedance.”
|
ShareChat’s TikTok clone, Moj, gets 10 million downloads within 6 days of launch, [Mint](https://www.livemint.com/news/india/sharechat-s-tiktok-clone-moj-gets-10-million-downloads-within-6-days-of-launch-11594130368003.html)
|
|
|
|
Instagram, too, is “reeling” in TikTok’s influencers.
|
“While still in testing phase, Instagram Reels is expected to become a solid competitor to Indian TikTok rivals such as Chingari, Mitron, Moj, and Roposo. Several TikTok creators have already moved to Instagram, asking their followers to catch up with their latest content on the app.”
|
Instagram Taps TikTok Creators in India as It Begins Testing Reels [Gadgets 360](https://gadgets.ndtv.com/apps/news/instagram-reels-india-testing-tiktok-youtube-creators-new-content-2259068)
|
|
|
India’s “digital strike” on Chinese apps has exacerbated the absent centre. We’ve banished TikTok the deity, but we can’t stop referencing it or even building clones in its image. And the vertical content feed isn’t the only clue. The new apps are also converting followers en masse.
It’s clear. God's not dead. We all still pray at the altar of an absent TikTok. Even the anti-Chinese atheists. Great, now there are two absent centres.
|
Coming soon to an app near you—“50 Questions”
Rohin
The Indian government has apparently [sent ](https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/tech/software/govt-sends-questions-to-59-chinese-apps-seeks-beneficial-owner-details/articleshow/76859517.cms)[50 questions](https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/tech/software/govt-sends-questions-to-59-chinese-apps-seeks-beneficial-owner-details/articleshow/76859517.cms) to the publishers of the 59 apps that it summarily banned last week for engaging in activities that were “prejudicial to sovereignty and integrity of India, defence of India, security of state and public order.”
|
“We are sending out a list of questions to the Chinese companies,” a senior government official told ET. The questions are around the financial structure of these companies, who is the beneficial owner, the country of incorporation of the company, identifying the parent company, board of directors and the location of their data centres, the official added.
|
|
|
|
On the surface, it appears like a rather simple step. The Indian government asking some harmless questions which no legitimate app owner should have an issue with, right?
But scratch away a layer, and you realise the government has arbitrarily and opaquely chosen these 59 apps based on criteria that can neither be [adhered to nor challenged](https://internetfreedom.in/59-apps-blocked-our-statement-and-initial-action/). There are other apps owned or operated by Chinese entities that have been spared, for reasons unknown.
Which means that if your app (or favourite app) is banned next, you’ll have no warning or recourse. The privacy-conscious search alternative to Google, DuckDuckGo, [is a case in point](https://internetfreedom.in/blocking-of-duckduckgo-needs-to-be-reversed-we-wrote-to-the-government-whattheduck/).

In the spotlight

India’s ‘other Warren Buffett’ won’t stop selling while everyone’s still buying 

Pulak Prasad-run Nalanda Capital considers itself a “permanent owner” of its portfolio companies. And so, the marquee investor’s numerous recent exits may be a canary in the mine

Singapore-based Nalanda Capital, with an enviable reputation in the Indian stock market, has been selling partial stakes in many of its portfolio companies. The Pulak Prasad-led fund house with a Rs 50,000-crore portfolio has a philosophy of never selling on valuation. It seems to be making an exception now. Nalanda Capital draws lessons from evolutionary biology in its investment approach: “avoid big risks, buy quality at a fair price, and don’t be lazy, be very lazy”. It has been anything but lazy in its “sell” decisions in recent times—potentially flashing warning signs in a runaway bull market that has seen valuations soar. [Read the full story]

A Peak XV-b​​acked school declares war on IIT coaching—by hiring 200 IITians

Orchids, a K-12 school chain, is not alone in losing students to test-prep centres after tenth grade. But it has an unusual fix

An indifferent rote-based learning in schools gave rise to India’s tuition republic—a Rs 58,000-crore after-school coaching industry. Coaching centres have flaunted ‘star’ faculty and a conceptual learning approach to do a one-better in traditional schools. But Orchids The International School has had enough. In 2 years, it has hired 200 IITians. The star teachers are not just bringing hands-on learning to classes but are tasked with helping retain students after class 10. For a school struggling to carve its niche and arrest student attrition in higher classes, this could be a make-or-break experiment. And IITians are just the beginning of the school’s ambitious un-coaching plans. [Read the full story]

Big Business

Bajaj Finserv wanted to make waves in healthcare. So, it bought a wobbly ship

While it aims to build a unique ecosystem with insurance intermediary Vidal Health, larger rival Medi Assist has upped the ante by making its own acquisition

Started in 2020, Bajaj Finserv Health wanted to provide a complete healthcare package to consumers. However, its vision changed and it acquired the second-largest third-party administrator (TPA), Vidal Health, earlier this year. Now, BFH wants to leverage Vidal’s TPA strengths and client base to sell its own offerings. The idea is to service more corporates. However, the biggest TPA player stands in its way—and so do regulatory and sectoral concerns. [Read the full story]

Ola Electric wants to take on Hero’s Splendor. But e-bikes are not e-scooters

The Bhavish Aggarwal-led EV maker is entering a market where demand is tanking and companies are struggling 

On India’s 78th Independence Day, just six days after Ola Electric went public, it launched three of its electric motorbikes. With a catchy price tag and promising performance, Ola Electric has set its eyes on India’s largest two-wheeler segment. While Ola Electric enjoys a sales uptick in Tier-2 cities, its latest electric two-wheeler may alienate some potential customers. Marked by low demand and companies closing down, India’s e-motorbike will put Ola Electric’s optimism to test. [Read the full story]

Beyond India

Sea Ltd’s stock soars on Shopee’s growth. But Tiktok and Temu loom large

Increased competition in Southeast Asia’s e-commerce space has resulted in companies fighting in each other’s turfs. And customers now get to choose between intent-driven and impulse-driven buys

Shopee is still the leading e-commerce platform in Southeast Asia but newcomers like Tiktok Shop and Temu are wooing customers in the region. Users in the region appear to be increasingly open to making impulse purchases on Tiktok Shop and Temu. E-commerce platforms are emulating each other—Shopee and Lazada are adding live-streaming and short-video features that resemble those of Tiktok Shop, for instance. Meanwhile, Shopee’s parent company, Sea Ltd, is finding ways to improve logistics efficiency and develop ad products that are more profitable. [Read the full story]

Bengaluru’s EV battery-making hopes are draining fast—without ever being fully charged

Foxconn CEO’s India visit sends states scrambling for manufacturing dominance, but Karnataka’s battery ambitions are stuck in low gear, battling a sluggish start and mounting hurdles

Bengaluru was on its way to being a hotspot for Li-ion cell investments, with Nsure’s Rs 1,000 crore and Exide’s Rs 6,000 crore bets making headlines. The demand for lithium-ion cells in India is also set to skyrocket, potentially surpassing 100GWh by 2035. Yet, progress is stumbling over bureaucratic red tape and sluggish incentives. To top it off, a dramatic plunge in global lithium-ion cell prices is just adding more fuel to the fire. [Read the full story]

Dear Ishan, hello again. Wow, I did not realise pathos and sadness would resonate among so many of you.

I got 74 song submissions for the Pathos playlist, our highest ever! What a sappy lot we are, ha ha ha!

We have the Pathos playlist for you to wallow in this Sunday, of course. It’s at the end.

Today is also the first time I’m writing to you from an iPad. Ugh.

Gen Zs might be native to Tiktok and Gen Alphas might be native to ChatGPT. I am native to laptops and full keyboards.

I accidentally left my laptop behind after yesterday morning’s event on rethinking product making in the age of AI.

Ah, never mind, I thought to myself. This is exactly why I had bought the iPad’s “Magic” keyboard case a while back. I plugged the iPad into it and tried to write into the Word app (yes, I still write in Word) for iPad. It was so bad. I kept hitting the wrong keys. The screen was too small. I felt cramped, unable to express myself.

Don’t give up, Rohin, I consoled myself. We live in the Golden Age of USB-C. I plugged the iPad to my external monitor (yes, I use an external monitor at office and home), and boom, the iPad created a second display for me. Word could now live on a large screen I was used to.

Rohin, aren’t you glad you bought the Logitech Wave keyboard and mouse, which support up to three different devices? Sure I was.

So, I paired my external keyboard and mouse to the iPad.

Now, I was typing on a proper keyboard, using a proper mouse, and viewing my words on a proper display.

And yet, it doesn’t quite feel like you’re doing the exact same thing, just with a Macbook plugged into the monitor instead of the iPad.

The cursor arrows are different. The “speed” at which letters appear or the cursor moves is slow. I briefly considered dictating my thoughts via Claude or Gemini, but I could not imagine saying my unformed thoughts aloud. They usually only flow from my brain to the screen as I type, character by character, via the keyboard. (Much like an LLM!)

Old habits die hard. Or perhaps, it’s hard to teach an old dog new tricks.

Whatever the analogy, I cannot for the life of me “create” (I hate the word “creator”) things on an iPad. So, full respect to those of you who can.

1. Yes? Or no?

Modern life and careers are complex. We’re always encountering problems or opportunities that require us to make subjective decisions. We are also encouraged to be collaborative. Finally, we’re told not to make binary “yes or no” calls, but instead to explore the nuances that reside within the two.

The result can often lead us towards a form of thinking and decision making that shies away from hard questions, and hard answers. As these “bubble up”, they eventually reach decision makers who don’t have the option to keep things in limbo or purgatory. Let someone go, or hire someone? Shut down a feature, or keep it on? Invest more into a hypothesis, or call it a failure and move on?

Being constrained to only “yes” or “no” decisions has a good side-effect though—sharper clarity on the decision.

Thus, I tell a lot of my colleagues who have complex asks from me, with respect to things they’re doing, to reduce them to simple decisions. “Give me something I can say yes or no to,” I tell them.

That forces them to restructure their own thinking. In the process, many are able to get to their own “yes” or “no”, without needing me.

My other preferred method is to debate their ask through a binary decision tree where every node must either be a “yes” or a “no”.

This allows us to retrace a number of prior decisions or assumptions in the process. A new pricing isn’t working? A simple “yes” or “no” decision would force us to, perhaps, kill it.

But if we traverse “up” the decision tree and go back in time to when it was launched, we can ask a more fundamental question: do the current plans work as well as we want them to? Yes or no?

Clearly the answer must have been a “no” for a new plan to have been launched. Is that still true? That might lead us to another decision.

This retracing of all the assumptions that led up to the immediate decision often ends up helping people see the problem better. By “forcing” every node in the decision tree to be a “yes” or “no” teams reach places where only subjectivity lies ahead. That’s where their creativity and expertise come in.

If you want a slightly alternate take on “yes” and “no”, here’s an article from Harvard Business Review that I agree with.

The inflow is daunting. And now more than ever, your professional success and personal well-being depend on how you manage it. You can’t say yes to everyone and everything and do all of it well. When you take on too many or the wrong things, you waste time, energy, and money and distract yourself from what’s really important. Still, no one wants to anger or disappoint colleagues or other contacts—or, worse, turn down key career and life opportunities.

You must therefore learn when and how to say both no and yes. A considered no protects you. The right yes allows you to serve others, make a difference, collaborate successfully, and increase your influence. You want to gain a reputation for saying no at the right times for the right reasons and make every single yes really count.

How do you do it? Through decades of research into what makes people the most highly valued, indispensable employees at hundreds of organizations, I have uncovered a framework that I believe works. It has three parts: assess the ask, deliver a well-reasoned no, and give a yes that sets you up for success.

Learn When to Say No—by Bruce Tulgan, Harvard Business Review

Before we leave this topic, I will say that it’s important to also question the hard nos. Were we being inflexible? Conversely, a lot of the easy yeses beg the question, were they just being polite?

2. Community responses (on Pathos)

Amar Chandramohan

I feel the modern world has structures in place (both implicit and explicit) that reward qualities like stolidness, detachment, power, efficiency, etc., and in a dog-eat-dog society, the utilitarian effects of those latter traits only get accentuated.

I may be stating the obvious here, but against this backdrop, most people would- I suspect- see little incentive in indulging in pathos, as it can often be unproductive and crippling, particularly if progress is what they’re after. But, in my view, for someone to feel pathos, I think, is affirmation, even if it is tenuous, of the existence of the human soul, in what is increasingly becoming a soulless society.

Speaking of pathos and music, I wanted to introduce you to an indie/alternative rock band (taking the liberty of assuming that you haven’t heard of them), I’ve been following for the last two decades (may not be fitting to label them as ‘indie’ anymore, on account of their collaboration with Taylor Swift, and a Grammy win in 2018).

I’d like to think that this band doesn’t really produce material that will gratify instantly; perhaps it takes a few listens to get them. However, Matt Berninger’s lyrics (and his baritone voice), the musicianship of the Dessner brothers (particularly on the guitars), and Bryan Devendorf’s drumming, help produce a very unique concoction of brooding music.

Meanwhile, I’ve dropped a couple of their songs for the FP Pathos playlist, but here’s a piece on them, which I found interesting, calling them the “Sad Dads”. Ha!

The Sad Dads of the National | The New Yorker

Also, since you write so much about stray dogs and dog parenting, I wanted to recommend this writer Ted Kerasote, and his book- Merle’s Door, which sheds some fascinating insights into the dog-human connection.

Sandeep Gautam

Putting Into Words

Tears can’t envelop it, the feeling is sublime,
It drowns your heart, without reason or rhyme.
There is a sense of loss, as if you’re less than before,
The past makes a comeback, bringing memories to the fore.

From the moment that was vanquished, to the moment that is bleak,
A pathos engulfs you, you just can’t contain the leak.
Everything shrinks, your path hits roadblocks,
A darkness spreads, that you typically kept in locks. 

It’s an effort to choose a track, that will lift your mood,
You can’t write, or even read, just quietly lament and brood.
The presence of others mocks you, makes you create instant walls,
You’re OK for it to fester, but to nip it you don’t have the balls.

If I don’t put into words daily, by the day end I feel a sense of loss,
Sadness creeps in when I cede control, and make poetry my boss.

******  

Ashok Saravanan

What fascinates me more about pathos is literature’s obsession with it, particularly the epics, which are largely accounts of pathos, IMO. I gravitate towards the Mahabharata very often and seek comfort in being with the plight, grief, and sadness of certain characters.

There is a favorite novel of mine by MT Vasudevan Nair, Rendamoozham, which tells the entire Mahabharata from the viewpoint of Bheema. It’s basically about how he is always denied importance/credit where due and given a second place by everyone – Mother, brothers, guru, and also Draupathi. After I finished the book, I realised that even I had given the poor fellow a second place. 

There is another one by PK Balakrishnan, Ini Njan Urangatte, which tells Karna’s story from the viewpoint of Draupathi. It is a concentrated dose of pathos. 🙂 I have been reading another novel(1st of 27-part series) based on the epic called Venmurasu. Peak pathos it is! Talks about the characters and incidents of the Mahabharata in depths you wouldn’t have read/heard before. Though intense, I am absolutely enjoying this. 

I mainly wanted to write about my close encounter with pathos last week in Thekkady(where I went for a work vacation). I went to a Kathakali show called Naragaasura Vatham. It was a small play about Nakrathundi, Naragaasura’s sister. The story goes like this.. Nakrathundi is a Rakshashi. She sees Indira’s son, Jeyanthan, and falls in love. She morphs into a beautiful woman, Lalitha, and confesses her love. He ignores her, denies her, and insults her, saying she looks disgusting. She feels abandoned and gradually turns back into her Rakshashi form. Jeyanthan sees that, is taken aback and cuts her nose and breasts. 

I don’t understand Kathakali, but since I knew the story, I was able to feel the love, joy, disappointment, and the pain of Lalitha/Nakrathundi. The question of why does she deserve this was running in my head for the next couple of days. It reminded me of what Bheeshma did to Amba. He didn’t physically hurt her but the damage isn’t different from what Jeyanthan caused.

I don’t know why this sadness/pathos in literature/art fascinates me. So, it is tough for me to answer your question of how I feel about it. I thought that writing this long email would help me verbalize it. Unfortunately, it didn’t. I shall write again whenever I get clarity on it. 🙂

The fear isn’t that we build an evil AI. It’s that we build an indifferent one. A superintelligence optimizing the wrong thing, with full commitment and zero empathy.

We’re not worried it will hate us.

We’re worried it won’t notice us.

But we don’t need to imagine an unaligned superintelligence. We’ve had one running for centuries. It’s called the market.

It takes inputs (resources, labor, capital), runs them through optimization loops (competition, pricing, growth pressure), and outputs structured behavior—at global scale. It shapes every government, every institution, every life.

It adapts. It evolves. It corrects. It predicts. It optimizes. And it doesn’t sleep.

If that’s not AI, what is?

https://www.nytimes.com/2025/07/05/upshot/boys-men-mentors.html

The importance of role models for girls seems irrefutable. Efforts since the 1990s to provide female role models have had great success opening doors for girls and young women, who now outpace boys in education, outnumber men in law and medical schools and excel in male-dominated fields like tech and politics.

At the same time, boys have many fewer male role models in their daily lives. While men still fill most positions of power across American society, the people who interact with children are largely women. Occupations like pediatrics have switched to mostly being done by women, while those that were always female-dominated, like teaching, have become more so.

At a crucial time in their lives, boys are increasingly cared for by women, especially the many boys whose fathers aren’t a regular presence. This lack of male role models, say researchers, parents, young men and those who work with them, is contributing to their struggles in school and employment — and the overall feeling that they’re adrift.

What Happens When Most of the Adults in Boys’ Lives Are Women—by Claire Cain Miller, The New York Times

https://rogermartin.medium.com/strategy-artificial-intelligence-entry-level-hires-4da6cab808f0

Within a few years, a dramatic reduction in entry level hiring will compound dramatically alter the age and tenure profile of companies. They will become older and longer tenured. As the earlier examples illustrate, you can never know for certain how a single meaningful change will alter any CAS. Maybe having an older, longer tenured workforce will improve company culture. But it is at least equally possible that the reduction of younger, newer blood in the mix from the current level will damage culture in ways that are hard to forecast. And with any CAS, forecasting the nature/direction of the change (or lack thereof) is a mug’s game.

This is a fundamental strategy choice for business leaders. The opposite — not cutting entry level hiring — is not stupid on its face. And it is an important choice that will have long term consequences. But for many leaders, AI is such an exciting (and well-hyped) new tool that they feel compelled to use it maximally. And their target is what they see as an unnecessarily high numbers of junior workers, whose jobs they believe can be performed cheaper and maybe even better by AI.

Strategy, Artificial Intelligence & Entry Level Hires—by Roger Martin

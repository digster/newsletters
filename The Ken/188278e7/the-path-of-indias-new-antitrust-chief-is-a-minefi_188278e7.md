Good Morning Ishan,
The Competition Commission of India (CCI) has a vision: to be “fair, competitive, innovative and enhance consumer welfare while promoting economic growth.”
That would seem like a buffet of objectives to be achieved for a watchdog that ensures India doesn’t become an economy described by American economist Irving Fisher as devoid of any competition. Surprising then, that the government let CCI be a headless chicken from October last year when its full-time chairperson Ashok Gupta retired.
With global behemoths such as Google, Amazon, and Walmart and homegrown ones such as Reliance, Adani, and other conglomerates always looking to net the next small promising fish—to grow even bigger and capture some sectors of a growing economy—the CCI’s role became even more strategic.
So, what happened when the government decided to cool its heels over appointing someone to the top post of CCI? In today’s story, The Ken decodes the logjam that ensued, chronicles the bureaucrats who became disoriented, and details high-profile deals that got stuck.
What were these deals? Why did the government take an armchair approach to an exigency? Why did it take so long to appoint an Indian Administrative Service (IAS) officer with considerable experience in the Ministry of Finance as the top gun?
As a member of CCI said, “It’s unprecedented in the history of the 13-year-old Competition Commission of India.”
While the rest of the media reports on her appointment, Vanita tiptoes the minefield-littered path for Ravneet Kaur, the next chairperson of CCI. Ravneet is a 1988-batch IAS officer from the Punjab cadre.

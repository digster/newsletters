Good Morning Ishan,
 
The protagonists of our story today are two pioneering startups.
 
One, founded in 2008, blazed a trail with its doctor discovery and teleconsultation platform. It was last valued at $330 million in 2020.
 
The other, a decade younger, wanted to become patients' primary choice for minor surgeries, even if it didn't run a single hospital. It was worth $250 million in 2020.
 
Now, if I were to tell you that one of these companies is buying the other, which would you say is the acquirer? The former? After all, it has been around longer and had a higher valuation last year. Fair enough.
 
But you're wrong.
 
Pristyn Care, the young elective surgery care provider, is the one looking to gobble up Practo, the 13-year-old healthtech company. Practo struggled to capitalise on its initial momentum and is yet to turn a profit. And it's now worth half of what it was worth five years ago.
 
Pristyn, on the other hand, is on a tear. In the past year, it has scaled from 600-800 surgeries a month to 4,000-5,000, or so it claims. Half of its 1,800-2,000 employees are business development managers with unrelenting targets. And Pristyn is now looking to raise $100 million at a $1.4 billion valuation—a nearly 6X jump in 12 months.
 
So, why is Pristyn interested in Practo? Enter the deus ex machina of this tale—Sequoia India, the venture capital firm that has invested in both. "An in-patient company buying an out-patient company is not a straightforward healthcare merger," Maitri and Seema write in today's excellent piece. "It's an investor-engineered deal."

Reliance Jio may not like satcom, but it’s coming. Calls from space, sea, or tunnel—India’s telcos are dialling them all 
 By Seema Singh https://the-ken.com/writers/seema/

Good Afternoon Ishan,

You are reading First Principles—a Sunday newsletter on mental models and leadership 

If you’ve noticed your feed is full of satellite, spectrum, and Elon Musk news over the last 48 hours, then let me just say there’s more to the ongoing India Mobile Congress (IMC) than the fighting over spectrum for beaming broadband from space.

When I asked the CEO of one of India’s largest telcos what was different about this edition of the annual tech event, he said the B2B offerings from a range of companies “are real” now. And as I went from booth to booth sampling these products and services, I could see firsthand what he meant by “real”.

But it was also true that some serious product companies lamented that it was only when you “know somebody who knows somebody…” could you get even a tiny pie of telecoms funds. So ease of doing business hasn’t reached all, but everyone agreed that communications minister Jyotiraditya Scindia’s intent is clear. The Indian telecoms industry must evolve as a “vertically integrated” sector with design and manufacturing under its belt.

You’ll read some of these stories later in The Ken, but for today let me say my piece on the noisy satcom debate.

For a long time now, Reliance Jio has been arguing against satellite communication. It objected to any such spectrum allocation through the administrative route; it wanted all interested parties to buy spectrum in an auction just like telcos buy spectrum for their cellular (or mobility) services.

It’s surprising India took so long to make Jio and others (Vi?) understand that the mid-band satellite spectrum is designated as ‘shared spectrum’ by the International Telecommunications Union. India cannot auction it. It can price it according to a method or a formula. The minister made it clear on Tuesday, and therefore this response from Elon Musk.

Since its initial opposition a few years ago, Jio has stitched a joint venture with global satellite services provider SES to offer broadband services over India. But as it happens, SES does not have low earth orbit (LEO) satellites; it has medium earth orbit (MEO) and geostationary (GEO) satellites in its constellations. Only Bharti Group-owned Oneweb and Musk’s Starlink have LEO satellites that are live, functional, and beaming services. 

So on optics, this is strike one for Jio, which is the largest operator in India and will soon go for a public listing. We all know the stock market loves any story that makes the hero look invincible. Who doesn’t remember that when Vi struggled, Bharti Airtel’s share price rose because the markets saw the Jio-Airtel duopoly becoming stronger? 

Strike two concerns Starlink and Amazon’s Kuiper, which will reach orbit in two years (it has booked all its launches), coming closer to users. So private terrestrial mobile-service providers like Jio, Airtel, and Vodafone-Idea (Vi) know that satellite communication will eventually come calling at their customers’ doorsteps. State-owned BSNL is putting its own plan in place.

It’s just a matter of time until regular phones come with the required chip and the accompanying application. (Like recent iPhones, Google’s latest Android phones support satellite messaging, an SOS feature when you don’t have cellular connectivity.)

Incidentally, the Indian government gave provisional licenses to Oneweb and Jio-SES earlier this month to test their satcom services. An executive from Oneweb India said its satellite services “live in every nook and corner of the country”, and that they are just waiting for the spectrum approval.

Satcom is coming, whether Jio likes it or not.

At the IMC, the telcos’ booths reflected their focus. Airtel prominently displayed all Oneweb-branded hardware from its ground control partners, including a Jeep-mounted terminal, for satcom. Jio, on the other hand, was high on “AI-enabled services”, demoing even a set-top box that it’s going to launch in January. As we wrote earlier https://the-ken.com/story/your-5g-phone-is-bleeding-airtel-and-jio-dry-is-3g-history-repeating-itself/?utm_source=weekly_email&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=archives, Airfiber is a big 5G bet for Jio and it’ll do everything to make customers go for its fixed wireless services. 

But at the sprawling exhibition, it was Vi which made the most noise. Literally. Live bands played and coordinated with members in remote studios and a hostess extolled the features of 5G and real-time syncing. (I enjoyed the music as did many others but a senior government official in the booth behind it was tearing his hair apart because the deafening noise didn’t allow them to entertain their visitors.)

When I asked a Vi executive why was the telco being gimmicky—they had placed at least six full-size mannequins in mining attire and were running an augmented and virtual reality show named ‘Dwarka Darshan’—she told me, “We are just very happy to be here.” 

Not exactly the answer I was looking for, but it was good to see Vi in action.

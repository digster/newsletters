The Indian government spent over Rs 4,500 crore on the first phase of UDAN and earmarked another Rs 1,000 for the second. The scheme is still struggling to take off
The government’s UDAN scheme, which promised affordable and easy travel to tier-2 and -3 cities, is struggling with delays, cancellations, and a lack of operational routes. Despite a financial safety net from the government for the first three years, airline operators are finding it difficult to survive. Plus, India’s fleet of 750 aircraft is half of the 1,500 needed to meet the government’s connectivity goals. Failures have shown the government that the scheme should have followed a different model from the start. Despite its high costs, thousands of Indians are opting for digital therapeutics to manage their migraines better and monitor heart health
As many as 217 million people in India suffer from migraines, and nearly a third of the deaths due to non-communical diseases in India relates to cardiac issues. Pharma companies have realised that rolling out treatment for chronic conditions is more useful for people than for acute ailments like pain and infections. So, bets like Nerivio, a migraine-management device, and Lyfe, a cardiac-care-management app from companies like Dr Reddy’s and Lupin, respectively. While drug-free products means long-term care for patients sans the side effects of pills, for companies, it opens the doors to bundled products, robust patient data, and a chance to be pioneers in global healthcare. [Wooden Street’s furniture play that Pepperfry and Urban Ladder missed](https://the-ken.com/story/wooden-streets-furniture-play-that-pepperfry-and-urban-ladder-missed/?utm_source=weekly_email&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=weekly_recap_card)
Limited customisation, in-house manufacturing, and spacious stores are helping this US$177 million firm stay profitable in a difficult sector
In December 2024, Wooden Street raised Rs 350 crore from Premji Invest, in one of the largest investments in the furniture segment in recent years. Wooden Street has been profitable for about four years now, at a time when the rest of the industry is still experimenting with scalability and suffering losses. Its success is attributed to the “Goldilocks zone” strategy, offering limited customisation of colour and fabric while standardising internal components like dimension. An in-house manufacturing facility and a dream of making it big using bigger factories have let Wooden Street maintain control on inventory, its quality, and market trends.

It’s been a glorious week in Bengaluru, alternating between grey skies, dark clouds, and copious rains on one day to blue skies, billowy clouds, and bright sunshine the next. It’s been a hectic and rewarding week for me. After last Sunday’s supermega Fitness Quest, I was exhausted. But the thing to charge me up happened the very next day over less than 45 minutes.

1. The Recharge

7.30 a.m, Monday, 3 June: “The Door”

I’m tired but also strangely exhilarated as I unstrap my Luna sandals outside our door. I flex my toes to feel around for any signs of metatarsal stress, but there seems to be none.

The newspapers have been delivered. “???/543” reads the NDTV ad on the front page of The Hindu. Election results are just a day away.

View in Browser

Gabru is waiting inside, as always. He’s seen me coming back from his vantage point in the balcony and has run back to wait at the door. “It’s time to go out for my morning walk, human!” he barks and jumps at me as I enter.

7.25 a.m: “Cool Down”

I’m walking back home from the lake. The brown “alpha” dog with one white paw isn’t at his usual spot outside the supermarket. He’s the leader of a pack of three and gets triggered every time I walk past him with Gabru on our morning walk. He studiously also refuses to take the dog cookies I offer him as a peace offering too.

A few days ago, I’d made a mental calculation of the number of street packs in the roughly two-kilometre stretch from here to Sarjapur Road. There were 11.

If you’re a runner, then you notice and recognise your neighbourhood street packs very quickly.

I step over a few broken Jamun branches with raw and half-ripe berries still on them. It’s the work of children from the nearby government school, bless them. I saw a bunch of them plucking the berries and shaking branches on Sunday. There are few sights more joyous than watching kids trying to get at fruits still attached to tree branches.

7.20 a.m: “I’m out”

We’re nearing the end of the running trail around the lake, and I tell Senjam I’m done.

“Let’s do another round,” he says.

“Don’t want to. I normally run around 3.5 km, and today I’m probably close to eight. I’m out.”

“Chalo, bye,” he says.

I veer off towards the gate and he continues running. I stop my running timer. It’s only 7.14 km, not 8.

Hmm, I misjudged that.

7.08 a.m: “Third Round”

We start running the third round in the same direction. I’m not used to running and talking, but I guess I’ll have to learn.

“I used to love shoes like the ones you’re wearing,” I run and say to Senjam. He’s wearing fluorescent green running shoes and the same coloured t-shirt. “But then I switched to barefoot shoes a few years ago.”

I’m running in the ultra-minimal “Huarache” style Luna sandals made by “Barefoot Ted” from the book “Born to Run”. I’ve owned them for about two years now and used to wear them to office too.

“I get it,” Senjam replies. “We all have our moments that pushed us to change some habits.”

Yes, I nod. I realise the third round isn’t as bad as I feared it would be.

“For me it was a book that changed my life. Do you have one?” he asks.

Sort of, I say. I loved “Born to Run”. It put me on the path to “barefoot” running and walking.

“The book for me was this book called “Healing Back Pain” by this Professor whose name I forget. It changed my thinking and life,” says Senjam.

(Ex post facto: I googled up the book and author after coming back. There are some incredible reviews around. I’ve ordered the book.)

We get to running-talking about our respective fitness routines. I try to run two–three times a week and work out with weights a similar number of days. “I’ve realised my risk of injury goes up if I do more than three days of weight training,” I tell Senjam.

“I do an AM and PM routine. I run in the mornings and work out in the evenings,” he replies.

“Wow!” I say, “I’m envious.” Because I just cannot muster the energy and motivation to work out in the evenings, I tell him (I’m a morning person). Besides, I’ve realised I need clear days of rest between my workout days. “But good for you!” I say. 

Senjam tells me he works out barefoot too. Along with normal strength training he mixes in callisthenics using gymnastic rings and rope climbing. “I’ve installed a rope at my home in Guwahati,” he says.

When did he move to Guwahati, I ask him. Because I remembered he used to stay in a community down the same road from me.

“After the second lockdown. We left as soon as it was possible to,” he says. “That’s been the biggest contributor to my health. My stress levels have come down so much. The other factor that has helped is sleep.”

We’re roughly halfway through the round, which is slightly over two kilometres.

“Yes, hard agree on sleep,” I reply. “It took me many years, but I think I’ve solved for better sleep for a few years now.”

“But as a founder I’m sure stress isn’t that easy to solve for, right?” says Senjam. He’s right. It is harder.

We’re running past the section of the lake that’s covered with lotuses. I see the young migrant woman again, still sitting by the sloping shore.

She’s hugging her son tightly—who’s stopped crying now—and is looking into the distance, stoically. I strangely feel both her sadness and her comfort for having her son with her at this moment.

The sadness of a parent when they’ve even momentarily lost hope is painful for a child. The child knows it, even though it can’t explain it. The woman’s son has stopped crying and is quietly hugging her. He wants to console his mother even though he probably doesn’t know or understand why. It broke my heart.

We pass them and our conversation moves on to First Principles.

“I love your First Principles podcasts. Listening to enough of them is a great lesson to young people who might want to start up,” says Senjam.

“Thanks,” I reply, half-smiling and half trying not to lose breath.

“Though they’re a bit too long. It takes me two–three sessions to finish an episode. So, I usually reserve them for flights,” he says.

We’re doing something about that, I tell him. Wait for it.

There should be a better way to find such ideas and advice for those of us interested in fitness and health, Senjam continues.

“Why don’t you start something like that? Become a founder!” I tell him, laughing.

Me? Ha ha, no way. I value my own health!” he replies, also laughing.

We pass a painted stork wading through some half-sunken logs near the edge of the lake.

7.07 a.m: “About turn”

“I loved all the ideas and thoughts in your Fitness special. I wish more younger people, including our kids, would get interested in fitness earlier on,” says Senjam.

“I guess we all come to it at our pace and time. Young people don’t need to worry about it as long as they have their awesome metabolism working for them,” I tell him.

We both laugh.

“But I feel that it is still an unsolved problem. Why isn’t there a startup or tech that makes it easier to keep fit?” he asks.

“Because it’s not a technology problem, but one of human motivation,” I tell him.

We’re at the end of the trail, near the gate. I tell him I’m done with two rounds and am going to stop.

“Let’s run another round, come on!” says Senjam.

I check my energy levels and instinctively look at my feet. Both feel okay.

I guess I’m running a third round. 

6.56 a.m: “About turn”

There’s a young woman sitting on her haunches by the side of the lake. Judging from her well-worn saree, she’s most likely a migrant construction worker from one of the numerous houses being built in the nearby neighbourhood (“layout”). Her roughly two-year old son is in her lap and he’s crying, saying something to her to get her attention. But she’s holding him and looking out into the distance, across the lotuses and past the waters and beyond the tall apartments.

And even though I can’t see her eyes, I can sense she probably has tears. The child is sad and crying because he senses that his mom is too.

I want to ask her if she’s okay, but cannot summon up the courage. The barriers are too high. What will she think of this strange man talking to her? What will I even ask her? What might others on the trail think?

I continue running, now with the added weight of sadness.

A few minutes later I run past Senjam as we cross paths. We’ve seen each other from a distance and wave as we pass.

“I loved your Fitness edition,” he shouts as we crossed.

I smile and say thanks with a thumbs up.

Roughly six minutes later we’re crossing each other again, towards the end of my final round.

We wave and smile again, but this time Senjam decides to turn around and run with me.

“I read your Fitness piece man. Loved it!” he says, as we’re both running.

6.44 a.m: “First Round”

I run through the partly open gate to the Kasavanahalli lake trail and turn left to begin my first round. For as long as I can remember, I’ve always run clockwise. It’s just been a default, subconscious thing.

But today, just like always, there are people running in the opposite direction too.

It’s a wonderful day to be running. The recent rains have done a good job of filling water into the lake. Someone is sitting by the edge of the lake and meditating while looking across the water at the sun.

I run at an easy pace roughly between 9-10 km per hour. “80s Hits Essentials” is playing in my ears (don’t judge me).

Halfway through the trail, I recognise another runner coming from the opposite side. Senjam Raj Sekhar is someone I’ve known for about a decade and a half now. He was the former head of corporate communications at Bharti Airtel back when I was a journalist at Forbes India. Senjam then went on to helm the same role at Flipkart while I was starting The Ken. Where was he now again, I wondered.

(Ex post facto: Raj leads corporate communications for the MPL Group).

“Hi Senjam!” I wave and smile at him as we pass each other. Sometimes runners are in their “zone” and not really paying attention to other people. I think Senjam was in that zone and got a bit startled, though he smiles back reflexively. I shout and mouth back, “Rohin, from The Ken” as we cross and keep on running.

6.40 a.m: “Porsches and Lunas”

I’ve finished my brief stretching routine for the ankles, legs, and hips. I hate stretching and have taken to it reluctantly after finding stress-related injuries becoming more common as I’ve gotten older.

Gabru is watching me from a distance. He knows this isn’t his morning walk time, so he’s content to just keep an eye on me. “I’ll wait for my human to come back from wherever it is he comes back from, all sweaty and tired. That’s when I’ll attack him for my walk,” he’s thinking.

Should I go running on the road, I wonder. It’s been raining the last night, so surely there will be puddles on the sides of the road in many places. Because the art of building roads that slope and drains that work was perhaps lost with Mohenjo-Daro and Harappa.

Did I want to dodge crazy early-morning drivers while dodging the puddles?

I’m sitting on the shoe rack, wondering whether to wear my Vibrams. The “ugly”, “Gorilla-style” shoes (“a reliable signal someone is either a virgin or married a long time,” commented a friend) have been my regular road running shoes for a few years now.

I could go for a run around the nearby Kasavanahalli lake, but then my Vibrams would get muddy and require cleaning.

Or I could wear my Luna sandals. Except that I overdid the lake trail with Luna sandals last month and ended up with a mild inflammation of my left foot’s metatarsals.

Vibrams and road, or Lunas and lake trail?

There’s a folded piece of newspaper lying next to me which I don’t notice, at least not in a conscious way.

I pick the Lunas.

3. Coffee to save the day?

Hello, everyone, it’s Hari here. One thing that’s central to most conversations before they start is setting the scene. You say, “Coffee?” and you’re in a coffee shop. I’ve even started using the line “Coffee?” with my colleagues, even though I don’t love coffee all that much. And before you go, yeah, that’s what people usually do when they want to talk. I’d just like to preface that in Kerala where I’m from, we drink tea, like, six times a day. So forgive me if I find saying “Coffee?” a bit odd. 

But coming back to conversations over coffee, let me raise the stakes for the conversation and set the scene. This one involves a million-dollar deal, a coffee trader-turned-entrepreneur, the software he made that makes trading coffee beans easier, a potential client, a mess-up that has put the deal at risk of falling apart, and the entrepreneur flying all the way from Bengaluru to Singapore to save that deal. And the scene is set, of course, in a coffee shop.

But does he bag that deal?

Well, you’ll find the answer to that in Rohin’s conversation with Manav Garg, founder and former CEO of Eka Software, now co-founder of TogetherFund, in the latest episode of First Principles. Oh, and this is not only the coffee shop story that Manav narrates in First Principles. In the first part of his conversation with Rohin, he told a great story about flying all the way to Heathrow Airport to convince a potential hire in a coffee shop at the airport.

P.S. As for me, I have lost all love for tea too after moving to Bengaluru.

4. Cropped

Hello everyone, welcome back to another issue of Silent Sunday photographs! It’s Jonathan here. Did you miss us these past two weeks? I fell ill on the week of 26 May, and then we had our special Fitness Quest edition last week, so Silent Sunday has been on a bit of a hiatus since mid-May. But of course, that doesn’t mean the photo contributions ever really stopped—things just… slowed down a bit. 

This also means I have a huge collection of pictures for you to browse, admire, and soak up today, culled from an even bigger set of contributions. 

Before we get to them, though, a quick detour. In the very first Silent Sunday edition I wrote, I started with a quote from photography pioneer Henri Cartier-Bresson. I admire him, as do the millions who’ve come across his pictures. But I also have to admit that he was kind of a snob about something that I think is one of the most useful skills a photographer can cultivate. 

See, Henri didn’t like people cropping photographs. He believed real photographers composed in the viewfinder (“Get it right in the camera!”). To click away willy-nilly and then try to create art from the results didn’t really sit right with him, and he was pretty vocal about it. 

But… one of his most famous pictures was almost certainly cropped. Henri’s defence was that he had no choice. 

Me? I believe a good crop can elevate even the most carefully thought-out photos. Maybe someone photo-bombed the edge of what was otherwise your best frame. Or maybe a different composition looks better when you revisit a picture after a good night’s sleep. Don’t hesitate, crop. 

Photography is art, and you can’t shame an artist for wanting to make the best of their work. 

That little rant over—(I brought it up because I ran into a friend who told me how everything in photography nowadays was “inauthentic” because of heavy post-production. I understand where he’s coming from, but calling people out for cropping is a bit excessive, I think)—it’s time for our top pictures this week. 5. Fiction week

Happy Sunday, readers, this is Anushka and I have some really interesting book recommendations from the community this week. One thing’s different, though, and perhaps you can tell from the titles. 

This week, we have a fiction edition.

As this week closes, we have over 70 recommendations for the First Principles’ Book Section(!). And I haven’t done the numbers, but I can safely say that a majority of them are non-fiction books. The community is full of non-fic veterans, which is why when the fiction submissions roll in, I’m always eager to read the reviews—to see what stands out to the readers (and what the readers think we will like about the book). 

With non-fiction books, the takeaways are clear, concise, and extremely useful. But with fiction?

“Prepare to laugh, ponder the meaning of life, and maybe even learn the ultimate question—if you can figure out what it is.”

“This is a novel that’ll get you started with reading or restart after a slump. Give it a try”

“The free verse form of writing makes it easy on the eyes but heavy on the heart.”

All of which brings us to today’s excellent list—let’s get on with it.

Ed Set Go
Mon, 25 Nov 24
The one stop to understand the biggest shifts in education, written by the best edtech journalist in the country..
Good Morning Ishan,
After William C Morton, the part-time investor, hedge-fund manager, and one-time Youtube interviewee, there’s another character that’s popped up in the neverending tale of legal woes starring Byju’s. His name is William Hailer.
If you remember, we’d [written](https://the-ken.com/edsetgo/the-curious-case-of-william-c-morton-and-the-international-hunt-for-us533-million/) about Morton and his firm Camshaft Capital earlier. Camshaft had, allegedly, held US$533 million for Byju’s till the money was moved to an offshore firm in the UK. The group of lenders who’d lent Byju’s US$1.2 billion for its US operations alleged that this money was being “hidden” from them.
There’s now a new twist in this tale. It seems that at least part of those funds, about US$11 million, were sent to Hailer’s Washington DC-based political consultancy firm Rose Lake. In a statement made to the bankruptcy court in Wilmington, Delaware, Hailer, the chief executive of Rose Lake, alleged that Byju Raveendran was “using” him to buy back the US companies he’s lost control over, such as Epic! Creations.
Hailer claimed that Raveendran had sent him a ticket to Dubai to discourage him from testifying. And that he had [promised](https://americanbazaaronline.com/2024/11/22/byjus-founder-told-businessmen-to-flee-us-to-avoid-testifying-93732/) him the same quality of life that Raveendran’s family now enjoys in Dubai, despite the severely diminished edtech business of Byju’s.
Hailer also claimed that Raveendran had promised him a full-time position with a salary of at least $500,000 per year, along with an equity stake in the company. “I was also told by Raveendran that he would move me and my family to Dubai, arrange housing and schools and ensure we have the same quality of life that he has,” Hailer said.
While the first William in this story wasn’t able to tell the court where the US$533 million had gone, the second William (what are the chances?) has hinted at a firm called OCI in the United Kingdom which may be holding this elusive half-a-million with them.
Between the two Williams, will we finally get to see the money Byju’s lenders have desperately searched for?
Raveendran, in his signature black t-shirt, used to command the attention of any room he entered. This was back when Byju’s was the ultimate name to contend with in edtech. He always had an entourage of media consultants and employees around him.
That picture is in stark contrast to what Hailer detailed in his recent deposition—which is an image of someone who’s trying to leave things behind, even as he tries to set the record straight. Hailer said Raveendran didn’t really act like someone who “ran a legitimate business”.
“Most times, Byju carried three phones. He used personal email instead of work email for communications. He would isolate people into individual meetings or only include for certain periods of time,” Hailer said.
And then there’s this worrying—if slightly funny—detail.
“On one occasion, while Byju was showing me how AI worked, a ChatGPT screen showed a previous search for how to defend against corporate fraud accusations,” he alleged.
Hailer also alleged that Raveendran wanted to control Epic again, a company Byju’s acquired in the US which is currently controlled by a group of its lenders. Additionally, Raveendran allegedly told Hailer that he would give up more of his shares in the test-prep giant Aakash and use those funds to start a new business.
What Raveeendran can actually manage to do after this disclosure is unclear. There’s a good chance he might be held liable in the Delaware court for trying to sway a witness.
The real question in my mind is: what happens to Epic? The company was an institution in American schools and with parents. It had solid brand recall, a spread-out distribution system across public schools, and a top-notch subscription-based business. How much of that survived Byju’s?
In a separate lawsuit [filed ](https://www.business-standard.com/companies/news/byju-s-affiliate-drained-cash-from-us-units-violating-bankruptcy-rules-124100900091_1.html)in October, a trustee appointed by the court to oversee Byju’s US assets alleged that money had been “siphoned off” from these entities to a company called Whitehat Education Technology, which is owned by—you guessed it—Raveendran. The trustee said a Stripe account was used to drain almost US$700,000 from the US companies to the Stripe account of Whitehat Education. The trustee has asked the court to prevent any money being transferred out of Whitehat now.
I bring all this up only to highlight that the American companies bought by Byju’s are likely out of funds themselves, and more importantly, talent and resources.
And the lenders, even if they get their US$533 million back along with the rest of the term loan, will have to put time, effort, and resources into shape these companies into saleable assets.

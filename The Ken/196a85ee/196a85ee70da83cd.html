Few companies are as good as Oyo Hotels at not going public.

It first filed for its initial public offering in 2021. Then again in 2023. It was about to give the share sale another shot when its largest shareholder had other ideas, Bloomberg reported last week.

SoftBank has balked at the proposed timing and pressed Oyo to hold off on an initial public offering until its earnings are stronger, according to people familiar with the matter, who asked not to be identified as the information is private.

Oyo will now apparently seek to list as early as March 2026 at a valuation of more than $7 billion, a sharp drop from the $12 billion it hoped for in 2021.

But forget these numbers for a moment. If there’s one thing tech companies have learnt, much to their chagrin, it’s that public-market investors are much less credulous than venture capitalists. Sure, retail and institutional shareholders may be in thrall to whatever yarn the founders spin, but they have no trouble snapping out of it just as quickly.

Bhavish Aggarwal knows this better than anyone else.

Shares of Ola Electric, the scooter maker he heads, doubled within a week of listing in August. But Ola has since lost its leadership in electric two-wheelers and faced flak over the mismatch between its own sales numbers and the government’s vehicle-registration data.

The result: Ola’s shares are now trading at one-third of their August peak.

Small wonder, then, Masayoshi Son is not chuffed about Oyo’s flotation now.

But the problem is not when to take it public, but what exactly will be taken public.

In 2019, Bloomberg named Ritesh Agarwal*, founder and chief executive of Oyo, as one of 50 people who “changed the game” in business, politics, and entertainment. The list also featured Ankiti Bose, who was fired as CEO of fashion e-tailer Zilingo for alleged financial irregularities three years later. Just saying.

The interview with Agarwal that accompanied the piece was predictably fawning, calling him an “amazingly ambitious hotelier”. But what was equally striking was how it described Oyo:

Like most hotel chains, OYO doesn’t own its properties. Instead it provides capital and training to hotel owners who rebrand to OYO’s specs, along with proprietary data-mining technology that helps properties maximize revenue.

That’s a great way to quickly conflate Oyo with giants of the hospitality industry such as Marriott, Hilton, and Hyatt, which own 1–2% of their hotels. Sure, the key to their global expansion is lending their brand name to properties owned and often also run by someone else in exchange for a franchisee fee. The interview even said Oyo was already the world’s second-largest hotel operator by rooms and would soon surpass Marriott to take the top spot.

Sure, why not?

But Oyo is anything but Marriott or Hilton. Those chains dictate everything from the location of the hotel to its design to pricing to the standard operating procedures for everything—guest service, hotel maintenance, et al. It’s their brand that’s at stake, after all.

Oyo, on the other hand, gives small hotels software for inventory management and digital check-ins, among other things. In its 2021 IPO document, Oyo claimed to assess hotels every month for “compliance with our standard operating procedures”.

But it’s nothing like the stringent arrangements large hotel brands have with owner-operators. Which is why the number of Oyo hotels, which expanded from under 13,000 to 18,100 in FY24, shouldn’t inspire awe.

Oyo’s attempts at shoring up its bottom line are evident. It swung to profits in FY24, even though it came at the cost of not adding to its revenue. In the two years to FY24, Oyo’s parent’s revenue rose a mere 13% to Rs 5,540 crore. The Tata Group’s Indian Hotels Company, on the other hand, more than doubled its revenue in the same period to almost Rs 6,800 crore.

What’s interesting, though, is that it also made a helluva lot of money in the process. Indian Hotels’ profits in FY24 were more than 4X of what it reported pre-Covid.

I wonder what Agarwal’s pitch will be when Oyo goes public. Talking up its hotel network won’t fly. Investors have already tasted blood in the 1,000% returns they have got in five years from Indian Hotels, an all-things-to-all-guests company that runs the gamut from Taj to Ginger hotels.

Those looking to bet on an efficiently run, few-frills hotel operator gravitate towards Lemon Tree Hotels. Even a company with a mere 11 hotels in three cities, Blackstone-backed Ventive Hospitality, has found favour among investors with its industry-leading revenue per available room. ITC Hotels and EIH, which runs the Oberoi and Trident chains, have their fans too.

Whatever their differences, what’s common to all these companies is how simple their business models are. None of them will be a headscratcher to anyone even remotely familiar with hospitality. Then comes Oyo with its grand claims about “empowering” thousands of small hotels with its “two-sided technology platform”.

There’s also the little matter of optics. In 2019, Agarwal borrowed $2.2 billion from lenders including Mizuho and Nomura, with a guarantee from Son, to increase his ownership in Oyo. Reports suggest that the repayment timeline of the loan, which was restructured in 2022, is tied to Oyo’s listing.

While the transaction has little to do with Oyo’s business, the idea of its CEO repaying a loan backed by the CEO of its largest shareholder is sure to turn away those investors on the fence.

*Dayzero Holdings, in which Oyo founder and CEO Ritesh Agarwal is a director, is an investor in The Ken.

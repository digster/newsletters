Trade Tricks
Tue, 18 Mar 25
A weekly newsletter about the biggest changes in commerce—focusing on shifts that matter to you..
Good Morning Ishan,
I am fasting this month for Ramadan. And among the many things you are apparently not supposed to do before sunset is brush your teeth. There are people who’ll vehemently argue that you can, but I don’t trust myself to not swallow the toothpaste; I’ve been told many have the same fear.
Not brushing a second time, or even once, during the day isn’t an uncommon occurrence in India, whether you partake in Ramadan or not. According to toothpaste major Colgate-Palmolive’s FY24 earnings call, “only 20% of urban households in India brush twice a day, while 55% of rural households don’t brush daily.”
In some ways, this is a reflection of how basic oral care is seen by a large portion of the Indian market. You don’t do much about it. And when you do, you don’t really give it much thought. “Oral care is something that’s boring. It’s a fairly low-involvement category,” says Jatan Bawa, the co-founder of Perfora, an oral-care brand that sells a range of teeth-whitening products, toothpastes, and electric toothbrushes.
But there is a growing segment of consumers that do give it some thought, and they’re moving from basic oral care to beauty—picking up serums, strips, and pens to keep teeth as white as possible.
This is the wave Perfora wants to ride.
In fact, Bawa and Tushar Khurana, the brand’s other founder, claim that Perfora was the first to launch a purple teeth-whitening serum in India in August 2023. The product uses “colour corrective technology” to address yellow teeth stains, leveraging the purple colour to counteract and cancel out yellow hues, along with other teeth-whitening ingredients such as bromelain and papain enzymes.
The space, however, is attracting increasing attention from segment heavyweights.
Colgate, the market leader in India’s oral care segment, launched its own purple teeth-whitening toothpaste in March 2024.
Now, teeth-whitening as a primary feature isn’t new to the Indian toothpaste scene. After all, Colgate had launched its Visible White range as early as 2013 (along with a teeth-whitening pen), followed a year later by rival Close Up’s Diamond Attraction. But the segment has acquired more of a shine these past few years.
Perfora started by selling electric toothbrushes, followed by a mouthwash and a water flosser, but by February 2022, it had introduced its first whitening product—a teeth-whitening powder that it claimed resulted in whiter teeth in seven days.
A month later, Colgate launched a new product in its existing range called Visible White O2, which promised whiter teeth even faster—in just three days. The same year, in June and November respectively, Perfora launched a whitening pen and a whitening toothpaste.
Even Hindustan Unilever’s Closeup has added to its teeth-whitening range with a new toothpaste called White Now.
It’s apparent that both segment leaders and new entrants see teeth-whitening as a key opportunity.
Let me quote Colgate-Palmolive’s managing director Prabha Narasimhan from an earnings call in December:
The second pillar of our premiumisation [strategy](https://www.bseindia.com/xml-data/corpfiling/AttachHis/d1a58da2-73a9-4692-b01a-f6c93d14b4f4.pdf) is Whitening. I have said this before, but just to repeat the data point, whitening penetration in this country is very low single digits, actually some 2%. This segment is 25% in other markets, like the US, for example. This presents a tremendous opportunity for growth to marry oral health and beauty. We are seeing strong double digit growth in this portfolio.
…what makes this category particularly interesting and exciting, because it is a beauty category, is the opportunity for innovation.
In the same call, Narasimhan said that the new purple toothpaste has seen a “tremendous early response” with “lots of excitement”.
Khurana thinks the market leaders are watching Perfora’s moves closely, but wants to push the envelope to where larger competitors might take longer to follow. “The idea is to go further wherever those guys are reluctant, because it takes a bit of conviction for them.”
Perfora, backed by the venture-capital arm of the RP-Sanjiv Goenka Group, grew its revenue 2.8X to Rs 42.2 crore in FY24 from Rs 15 crore in FY23. And it expects sales of close to Rs 80 crore in FY25, according to Bawa.
Teeth whitening is about 40-45% of this business.
Preeyam Tolia, an FMCG analyst with Axis Securities, thinks Perfora could be the Mamaearth of oral care: “Mamaearth saw a niche and launched products with traditional ingredients like onion. Perfora has done the same so that later on, they can expand to the entire oral-care segment.”
But while teeth whitening is one of the fastest growing categories in the oral care segment, smaller brands do face some big challenges, like distribution. “You need a lot of activation and sampling, because switching a customer from one brand to the other is a tough task,” said Tolia.
For Perfora to want to paint the town purple on Colgate’s watch is equal parts bold and improbable.

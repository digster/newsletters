I’m sure you’ll have a lot to say about my colleague Aakriti’s story, too. It’s about a fascinating company. One that was often dismissed as a flash in the pan in its early days, and for two reasons: it was founded by two 19-year-olds, and they believed there was a huge market in delivering groceries in 10 minutes, which seemed laughable then.

But now, quick-commerce is on a tear and Zepto just hoovered up US$665 million, 2024’s largest fundraise in India, and got valued at US$3.6 billion. It’s reportedly in talks to raise more, which will give it a valuation of US$5 billion.

Months ago, even before there were any signs of a fundraise, any analyst or fund manager I talked to about the rise of Zomato-owned Blinkit would wax eloquent about Zepto’s “execution chops”.

Zepto was considerably smaller than Blinkit and was even behind Swiggy Instamart. But the attention Zepto got was clearly disproportionate to its size. If you want to know how well Zepto has piggybacked on Blinkit’s rise, you have to read Aakriti’s story.

Most caller apps on Android phones have the option to mark a caller as “spam” besides blocking them. Perhaps Google could make these dialer apps—including its default dialer—share these spammer numbers to a common database.

While it may at first look like a TC clone, there are a few advantages: (a) All the user needs to do is to mark a number as spam, no 3P app to be downloaded, (b) Google is subject to far more scrutiny than TC and has much more at stake to comply with Indian law.

Also, as a thumb rule, *never* trust a government-provided app or service. They are exempted from the government’s own laws, such as DPDPA, so you have no protection or recourse. And experience suggests that government-run firms are the most vulnerable to attack and the least likely to do anything about it. BSNL just got hacked twice in six months.

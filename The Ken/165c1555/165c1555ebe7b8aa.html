Good Morning Ishan,
 
It hit me hard yesterday that next year, it’ll have been 20 years to the year that I entered B-school for my MBA. 
 
Two decades. 
 
During those two years spent earning our MBA, some of us had a phrase to describe alumni who’d graduated 10, 15, 20 years ago: “super seniors”
 
It hit me yesterday, while at the verdant ISB campus in Hyderabad to deliver a talk as part of their annual leadership summit, that I was now a super senior myself (not from the ISB though). It was a sobering realisation.
 
While super seniors may be sobering, super apps are exciting. Which is what today’s story is about.
 
Bangalore-headquartered Cure.fit is building the “super app” that will connect everything related to healthcare and fitness for you. From gym workouts to yoga sessions to healthy meals to health checks and disease management. All from the same app.
 
Meh. Yet another app that wants to do everything, you say. This is where it gets interesting. 
 
Because Cure.fit is going full vertical (or, as some of its critics say, full postal), Apple-like, in offering these services itself.
 
It runs dozens of its own gyms. And yoga studios. Its kitchens turn out thousands of meals each day, to be delivered to customers across Bangalore and Delhi. It streams live workout sessions, Peloton-like. And offers guided meditation sessions, Headspace-like. And oh, it is setting up its own healthcare clinics. 
 
It looks like Cure.fit is competing with dozens of players across multiple sectors. And yet, its founders say they can’t think of one direct competitor.
 
VCs have handed them over $170 million in venture funding for this gambit.
 
Over the past many weeks, Gayathri has gone deep inside Cure.fit’s spider-like web of distinct business lines, all converging into an ambitious vision to become an Apple-like ecosystem around healthcare and fitness. 
 
It’s either a very bold and audacious plan. Or an impossible and foolhardy one. But not both.

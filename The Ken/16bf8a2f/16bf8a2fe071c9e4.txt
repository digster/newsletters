In 2014, the Indian government mandated that all companies above a certain threshold (Rs 5 crore in profits or Rs 500 crore in net worth or Rs 1000 crore in revenue) compulsorily spend 2% of their average 3-year net profit on corporate social responsibility, or CSR.

You will believe what happened next.

Tens of thousands of companies, large and small, now had to spend 2% of their profit each year. On the other side, thousands of non-profit and social organisations now had a ready source of funding.

What popped up between the two were companies like Fiinovation, the subject of today’s story by Suraksha.

Fiinovation is a creature of the CSR mandate. It exists to act as a go-between for companies looking to give out CSR funds, and those wanting CSR funds to actually make a difference. For playing this role, it asks for a cool 10% of the amount it raises for a non-profit. 4% up front.

The emergence of organisations like Fiinovation is a given once you realise that companies are now spending anywhere from Rs 10,000-12,000 crore each year on mandated CSR. And in the 4 years that the law has come into being, experts estimate a total of Rs 50,000 crore has already been spent. 

That’s. A. Lot. Of. Money.

Fiinovation’s slick offices, frequently feted CEO and transparent business model snared thousands of non-profits and social organisations, reports Suraksha. Many of whom ended up paying it 3-4% in advance of receiving the promised CSR funds, which never materialised. They’re now running from pillar to post, sending emails, filing court cases and banding together.

The law of unintended consequences? Capitalism working as intended? A case for the government to stay away from mandating CSR? All of the above?

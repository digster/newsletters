Good Morning Ishan,
 
The New Year is an increasingly distant memory for most of us as the end of January approaches, but, for more than a billion people, another festive period is here—the Lunar New Year, also known as Chinese New Year.
 
This year’s celebration is chaotic for millions—with the Chinese city of Wuhan in lockdown to prevent the spread of coronavirus—but CNY ties into this week’s email, which is about starting afresh.
 
Thousands of OYO employees are embarking on a new journey in 2020 as a result of waves of layoffs within the unicorn hotel startup’s ranks. In our Monday story, Abinaya and Savio went into detail on these layoffs. 
 
You may have read news of the layoffs in the press, but our story chronicled just how OYO is going about shedding staffing costs—including first-hand accounts from terminated employees—closing divisions and generally restructuring its loss-making business.
 
On Tuesday, we shifted gears to another mega tech company, WhatsApp—the Facebook-owned messaging service. It’s used by a staggering 400 million people in India each month. Arundhati looked at WhatsApp’s newest focus for India: conversational commerce. 
 
The strategy takes a leaf from WeChat—the super app that dominates China’s internet usage. Facebook’s goal is for WhatsApp to go beyond being the chat app of choice, and become a medium for buying goods and services using chat as the connector between businesses and consumers. There’s lots of competition, and Facebook needs this to work as it has struggled to turn WhatsApp’s popularity into revenue in India.
 
This past week, news broke of a new home for Uber Eats India as Zomato agreed to buy the business. On Wednesday, Sumanth dissected the deal—the same way I dug into many samosas during our recent office ‘onsite’—to explain what motivated each side. Essentially, fierce competition in India meant Uber Eats spent big just to retain market share, which weighed on its global business. Zomato, meanwhile, is on a charm offensive to woo investors. It believes adding the Uber Eats business—and Uber to its cap table—will help raise more capital to battle its fierce foe Swiggy.
 
Organ donors are the definition of those hoping for a new start, and India is—on paper—a place where many get their wish. The country is second in the world behind the US when it comes to the number of organ transplants conducted, but there’s a problem. Organ donation has become a taboo subject. India has some of the lowest rates of deceased donations, and not enough transplants are retrieved from brain dead patients. The government and hospitals are working to change that and save lives. Ruhi’s story on Thursday looked into why that’s a difficult challenge for all concerned. It was the free story for the week, so do share widely.
 
Finally, on Friday, we closed the week with the story of online beauty marketplace Purplle, which is enjoying its time in the sun after finally entering the mindspace of many people, including investors. But it wasn’t always that way, Purplle focused on the fundamentals and cut its cash burn in order to grow independent of needing to raise money. The inevitable irony is that not needing investors for its business attracted investors to its business. It ended 2019 with a $30 million round that included Goldman Sachs, with a further $8 million arriving this month. In her story, Vandana looks at the future for Purplle, including the challenge of battling rivals that include Amazon.

“Gong Hei Fatt Choi” to those who celebrate Chinese New Year, and a happy Republic Day to all our Indian readers!

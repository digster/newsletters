Good Morning Ishan,
India’s higher-education system hasn’t woken up to the Dornbusch Law, but it’s already in action.
Paraphrasing the German economist, Rudi Dornbusch, crises take longer to arrive than you can possibly imagine, but when they do come, they happen faster than you can possibly imagine.
The gig-ification of the Indian university-teaching system, which started with UGC’s 10% cap on temp teachers five years ago, is complete and official with the cap removed this year.
India’s public universities, put in ever-tightening financial fetters by the central government, are increasingly employing teachers on short-term contracts rather than full-time. Delhi University, for example, has hired just under 500 permanent teachers since 2015, data from the education ministry shows, but an army of contract lecturers. Nearly 5,000 by one count.
Across 56 central universities, 25% of the nearly 19,000 teaching positions are vacant. State universities are no better. At Mysore University in Karnataka, for instance, over three-quarters of the teachers are on short contracts.
Ironically, these “gig” teachers aren’t even covered by India’s new labour codes which mandate better pay, benefits, and security for gig workers, though they appear to fit the definition.
A crisis of learning, scholarship, and institution building is unfolding all at once.

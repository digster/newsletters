Why The Whole Truth Foods isn’t the hero of its own story. The company is weaving a tale that puts the consumer centre-stage 
 By Neha Mehrotra https://the-ken.com/writers/nehathe-ken-com/

Good Evening Ishan,

You are reading our subscriber-only newsletter Inciting Incident—a weekly commentary on brand narratives and storytelling 

I know you’re used to ending the week with Ruhi in your inbox, but just this one time, I’ll be taking her place. I’m Neha Mehrotra and I’m a newbie here at The Ken, still settling in. So I’ve spent my first week getting the hang of my job as Desk Editor and having conversations with other colleagues who work out of Delhi—you know, just those initial chats where small talk swerves to land on the most random things.

I was in one such conversation with Ruhi this week when I found myself unwittingly talking about a brand I’d just encountered, and its multiverse of Instagram reels, Linkedin posts, and Youtube videos that I’d been sucked into. I’m a window shopper at heart, and will probably never buy their product. But their approach definitely intrigued me. 

The Whole Truth Foods (TWT) is a D2C health and wellness brand that sells protein powders, health bars, and muesli. And their latest innovation is the new FBI Programme—which is just the brand’s usual cutesy way of saying Food and Beverage Investigator Programme. Essentially, a two-hour workshop for kids meant to teach them how to read “back of pack” food labels and ingredient lists. Maybe your kids (and you) might finally find out what monosodium glutamate, or hydrogenated oil, or artificial flavouring is. 

Anyway, I don’t bring up TWT to spread the word about the programme. Instead, what I was truly intrigued by was how seamlessly it fits in with TWT’s declared mission: to “catch the jhoot (lies)” and “tell the whole truth”. Here’s a brief excerpt from a Linkedin post https://www.linkedin.com/posts/bansalsamarth_our-kids-are-being-fooled-just-like-i-was-activity-7297839210223284225-towl/ by their head of content, Samarth Bansal, introducing the programme:

Our kids are being fooled, just like I was. At age 27, I finally saw through it. Those “wholesome” cookies? Sugar bombs. That “whole grain” breakfast cereal? Mostly refined flour. That “no added sugar” juice? Packed with concentrates. For decades, I was the perfect consumer—clueless about what really went into my food.

Our kids deserve better. In every supermarket aisle, on every screen, food marketing targets them relentlessly—using cartoon characters, bright colors, and misleading claims.

This was NOT okay for the 6-year old Samarth. This is NOT okay for 6-year-olds today. This must change.

In one swoop, the brand is cementing an idea they’ve spent years—and countless videos on Youtube and Instagram—building. And it’s a very simple one. 

First, there is a villain, i.e., Big Food companies, which target your kids with shiny packaging and misleading claims. 

TWT’s founder Shashank Mehta admits to being a victim of Big Food himself, laying bare his journey from being a 110kg 19-year-old to spending the next decade losing, gaining, and again losing 25kgs. “As if I was stuck in a loop. Doomed to being a WHO obesity statistic every three years,” he says in the brand’s weekly newsletter Truth Be Told. To drive home this point, he even signs off every post with “Fat Kid, Fit Adult, Fully Human”. 

So, yeah, there’s your villain. 

Usually, and regular readers of this newsletter would know this well by now, this is where the brand would step in and put itself in the place of the hero. But that’s not what TWT does. 

Instead, it’s positioning itself as your friend and confidante, someone who has your and your kids’ best interests at heart. 

This leaves the position of hero to be taken up by *drumroll* you. The consumer. 

In fact, “empowering you to think for yourself” is a refrain that doesn’t just run through their new FBI programme. It’s present in all their content (like pieces deconstructing red dye and why it’s still in our food; deep dives into the dirty secrets of marketing with ad man Karthik Srinivasan; etc). 

This might, or might not, lead to you buying their product. But whatever you decide, there’s a good chance you end up trusting them. 

No wonder their ‘hype factor’ is so high. Hype Factor is a metric that helps quantify the hype around a company, and you can work it out by dividing Capital Raised by Annual Recurring Revenue. The Ken has written about this https://the-ken.com/story/mamaearths-ipo-marks-the-end-of-the-d2c-gold-rush/ earlier. 

Most companies have a hype factor between 1 and 2; TWT’s is a whopping 9.8. 

Add to this some radical transparency and it looks like you’ve got yourself a winning strategy. From inviting consumers to tour their kitchens to making lab reports for their products public, the brand insists on screaming it has nothing to hide. If an anonymous Reddit user trashes their whey protein for being heavy, Mehta makes an Instagram post addressing the claim. If a well-meaning consumer points out an error in their ingredient list, they are quick to acknowledge the mistake and fix it. 

All this is not me saying go and buy their products. But I do find it very interesting how they’ve carved out a place for themselves in the big, bad world of packaged food. All by placing themselves by the side of the consumer. 

For now, that’s a wrap for this edition. Thank you so much for reading, and here’s wishing you a very Happy Holi!

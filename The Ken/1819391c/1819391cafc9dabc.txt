Six years after Indian telecom’s (forced) natural selection and the survival of the ‘fittest’ telcos, another evolutionary pressure is on hand. From the telcos’ own collaborators.

In an unprecedented but not unexpected move, Cisco, the networking giant, has applied for a Unified License (UL) for access services in India. It’s a licence only telcos owned so far; they use it to provide wireline and wireless services.

Cisco’s video conferencing service Webex has already obtained a unified licence for Virtual Network Operator (VNO). While VNOs can be used for many services, Cisco’s is for international long-distance calling and national long-distance calling, among others.

The pandemic sent the demand for its video conference services soaring. In his exclusive report today, Pratap says that Cisco has been lobbying for extensive changes in the VNO licence policy with the help of its consulting partner, EY. Especially for changes that would allow the merging of the two networks—Internet Protocol and PSTN (circuit-switched telephone network).

Once it gets the licence for access services, Cisco-Webex could allow Indian users to dial into the video conferencing service via a traditional wireline (landline) or wireless phone and not be dependent on internet connectivity.

This licence can veritably put Cisco in direct conflict with telcos, which bring in a good chunk of Cisco's revenues.

Telcos, on the other hand, are not happy about this possible encroachment of their turf. They argue it poses a security threat and could potentially be misused. They are lobbying via their association COAI to restrict such private networks to machine-to-machine (or automation) communications.

The Ken has learnt that government officials believe in bringing on more players into this market. Those like Zoom are closely watching Cisco’s move; others like Amazon and Google have made their desire to provide network-as-a-service amply clear.

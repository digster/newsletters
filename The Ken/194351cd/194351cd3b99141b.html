Dear Ishan, good morning. It’s been a while since I’ve struggled to write an edition of First Principles Sundays. Saturday was that day for me.

I’d been playing around with ideas, reading older notes to myself, speed browsing books, and even looking out the window at the blue skies and warm January sun for hours. And yet, nothing. At least, nothing good enough to be worthy of being “the first edition of 2025”.

After a few hours “wasted” (by now, you know it was anything but), I came to the realization that I was stuck because I did not want to enter 2025—the third year for this newsletter—merely carrying over what we did the last year. I never want to be in a state with this newsletter where you can guess or predict what it will be about. Because being able to guess what someone is about to say (in an article, a book, a movie, etc.) is a lead indicator for what often follows as a lag indicator, boredom.

I decided to look closely at the evolution of FP Sundays in search of answers. Because of all the things we do at The Ken, FP Sundays is easily the one that’s most organic and dynamic. Except for its start, virtually everything has been a to-and-fro between us.

So, how do we keep FP Sundays unpredictable and interesting in 2025?

Before we get to that, and the other things my fellow writers have for you this week, here are a few photos of the nearby lake that Gabru and I went walking around today morning. Thanks to copious rains last year, this is the fullest I’ve seen the lake in years, for a January. The banks were still brimming. Ducks, pelicans, and cormorants were everywhere. And fish jumped between little pink lotuses, making that thud-whoop-splash sound that’s so characteristic if you’ve grown up around lakes or backwaters.

1. Evolution

We started off in May 2023 as a companion newsletter to the First Principles podcast, largely riffing off or extending from conversations with my guests. Leadership was the key theme.

By July 2024, we were a standalone newsletter, as the FP podcast itself came to an end. But our Sunday newsletter had self-evolved to add community-submitted photos, book recommendations, music playlists, and answers to “Quests”. And instead of leadership, self-reflection, discovery, careers, and growth became our new themes.

In 2025, what will we become? What should we become? To answer this, consider the following arcs of our evolution over the last 18 months. Why did #Books and #Photos become regular weekly sections, with people contributing each week, but #Habits, #Art, #Community comments, and #Surveys did not?

I’m a big fan of laying the blame at our own feet. I don’t think we worked hard enough to sustain #Habits, #Surveys, and #Community comments as sections. #Art I can understand—it can be daunting to expose your work to such a diverse and accomplished set of people.

This year I’d like to actively seek more talented members of the First Principles community to contribute and be part of our 2025 story. Perhaps they’ll anchor new sections, or perhaps they’ll contribute occasional features.

In 2025, what new features would you like First Principles Sundays to evolve into or offer?

2. What next in 2025? Vol. 2—Recommendations from The Ken

Hello, dear Ishan, Happy New Year! It’s Hari here.

I hope you got to celebrate exactly the kind of New Year that you wanted. I, for one, continued with my tradition—a quiet New Year’s Eve. No going out and meeting friends or celebrating with family. Just a quiet night as I sat down to watch a movie I haven’t seen before (this time, it was David Gordon Green’s George Washington) and read 40–50 pages of my book of choice before dozing off for the night. 

Over the past few weeks, I’ve been asking our dedicated community of readers for books they’d recommend we pick up in 2025, and we ended up with some very interesting recommendations. So I thought perhaps I should ask my colleagues here at The Ken for their recommendations too.

4. Two by Two: Are we past peak Amazon India?

Hi Ishan, it’s Hari again.

What do you guys think, are we really past peak Amazon India? And also, if Amazon is playing catch-up in e-commerce, are there other verticals it can use to its advantage?

Those were the questions Two by Two hosts Rohin and Praveen Gopal Krishnan posed to our two highly insightful guests on the podcast this week. First, we have Srikanth Rajagopalan, CEO of Perfios Account Aggregation Services and a former ‘Amazonian’. And Professor Vishal Karungulam, who teaches a broad range of subjects at the Indian School of Business (ISB), including software product management, digital innovation, and disruptive technologies. The professor has also previously worked in big tech firms such as Microsoft and Google.

The latest episode is a wide-ranging, engrossing conversation that I, for one, am glad I caught. So if you haven’t had a chance to listen to the episode yet, you can do so on The Ken app or Apple Podcasts* via a separate monthly subscription.

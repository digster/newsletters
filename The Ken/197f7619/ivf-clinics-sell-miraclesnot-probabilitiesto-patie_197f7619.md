Good Morning Ishan,
What’s the probability you’d notice an IVF clinic within a 2–3 km radius of your workplace or home?
Very high. I’m afraid I can’t put a percentage to it because nobody has the total number of clinics in a city or a state. But they are popping up everywhere with great frequency.
In contrast, the probability in IVF treatment can be more easily calculated. One in every 12 retrieved eggs may result in an implantation-ready embryo. Then you add things like age, sperm quality, and uterine health into the mix, and you arrive at a ballpark for every couple.
Yet that number is rarely communicated. What is instead sold these days is—miracle.
Nearly four in every 10 reproductive-age adults in India have cited financial limitations as a barrier to having children, according to a recent worldwide fertility crisis report by the UN Population Fund, a global agency working on reproductive and maternal health. But when IVF chains can have margins of 50–60%, money will flow into this business. And the sales-first approach of clinics has ensured that the mindset on IVF has shifted from being a need to a want.
Sudeshna visited at least 10 clinics and has answers to why insurers are running away from IVF, but investors are running right at it, with open arms.

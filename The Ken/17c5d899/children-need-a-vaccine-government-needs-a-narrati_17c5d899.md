Good Morning Ishan,
I love new beginnings. Birthdays, new years, even the first sentences of stories.
This Monday, we celebrated The Ken’s birthday (and mine, too) by kicking off a brand-new array of weekly newsletters. My extremely talented colleagues have already launched five of these this week and if you haven’t checked [them](https://the-ken.com/newsletters/) out yet, I highly recommend you do.
It is now Friday and to add to this week of new beginnings, here comes our sixth new newsletter—Inciting Incident—knocking on your inbox.
Beginnings, all writers will tell you, are the hardest part of storytelling. We struggle with the research and stare at our screens trying to get our heads and hearts to sync, until the perfect words for the perfect introduction flow through us. It needs to have the potential to pull in the reader, to carry them through a nail-biting middle to a satisfying resolution.
A compelling ‘inciting incident’, most storytellers agree, is the safest bet that a story will remain resilient till the end. It is the epicentre from which both the back story and the future emanate.
Harry receives his invite to Hogwarts. Neo escapes the matrix. Anna Karenina meets Vronsky at the St Petersburg train station.
Inciting incidents are planned and executed in the real world all the time too. They look like funding rounds, product launches, and marketing campaigns. Some are the beginnings of compelling and authentic stories that break new ground, and others are oversold narratives that burst like a bubble.
This newsletter, as its name promises, will pick such inciting incidents from the week that went before and break down the story underneath. You will find it in your inbox at 7 am India time, every Friday.
Here is this week’s fare.

Children need a vaccine, government needs a narrative
The first Covid-19 vaccine for children is here. And the government may begin vaccinating Indian children over 12 years of age with Zydus Cadila’s ZyCov-D shot as early as next week.
This week though, it focussed on controlling the price of what isn’t just the first vaccine for children approved in India, but also the most expensive Covid-19 vaccine yet. By controlling the price, it has begun to control a powerful position in the narrative that determines who gets credit for vaccinating India’s children.
Ahmedabad-based pharma major Zydus Cadila had pegged the price for three doses of ZyCov-D at Rs 1,900 (US$25) when it was approved for emergency use in August. And the company had stuck to this price up until this week.
As India's first Covid vaccine for children, ZyCov-D was expected to make a splash. Zydus thought it had enough going for it. The new shot is needle-less, the first DNA-based vaccine, and the only one approved in India for children as schools start to reopen.
But the central government, currently in negotiations with the company, has reportedly brought down the per dose price to [under](https://www.indiatoday.in/coronavirus-outbreak/vaccine-updates/story/zydus-cadila-covid-vaccine-dose-negotiations-centre-1860479-2021-10-04) Rs 300 (US$4). Or maybe even [less than](https://theprint.in/health/modi-govt-drives-hard-bargain-zycov-d-vaccine-could-cost-rs-275-dose-or-even-less/746347/) Rs 275 (US$3.7).
A senior government official told The Ken that the central government has also asked the company to “keep a low profile”. Zydus Cadila has no choice but to accept. Because after [receiving flak](https://the-ken.com/bfo/inside-indias-25-of-covid-19-vaccines-free-market-experiment/) on differential pricing during the second wave of Covid-19 in May and June, the government had been forced to [make](https://www.reuters.com/business/healthcare-pharmaceuticals/indias-modi-pm-announces-free-covid-19-vaccines-all-adults-2021-06-07/) the vaccines free by 21 June. And now, the government is the only buyer.
Covid-19 cases have also come down significantly since June and there is no private market for the vaccine anymore. A few privileged Indian parents keen on getting their children vaccinated have taken [extreme](https://www.indiatoday.in/coronavirus-outbreak/story/indian-parents-move-bombay-hc-to-send-minor-daughter-to-usa-for-vaccination-1809827-2021-06-02) measures such as [flying](https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/nri/study/indian-parents-are-sending-their-children-to-american-universities-for-a-shot-at-getting-covid-19-vaccine/articleshow/82025657.cms?from=mdr) their children to the US.
But in India, the demand for a children’s vaccine has fallen. “There is no pressure anymore from parent groups or anyone to vaccinate children soon,” the senior official said.
Thus, the story is now no longer about pent up demand, or how soon the shot becomes available, but about keeping the vaccine free, or as affordable as possible, under the government programme. Which is why Zydus Cadila’s lost the price negotiations with the government.
There is still a need for the vaccine, but not enough demand to change the status quo policy of free vaccines. This week, Zydus failed in convincing the government, its only buyer, that the three-dose course of its vaccine was worth Rs 1,900 (US$25).
ZyCov-D’s reported price still has some premium over Covaxin and Covishield, which the government buys at Rs 157.50 (US$2) and Rs 225.75 (US$3) per dose respectively. But it is nowhere close to the price Zydus began the negotiations with.
There is another reason why Zydus lost out. It does not have enough supply at the moment. The vaccine-maker can provide [nearly](https://www.indiatoday.in/coronavirus-outbreak/vaccine-updates/story/zydus-cadila-1-crore-doses-vaccine-by-october-1843707-2021-08-21) 10 million doses this month and is targeting about 100 million doses annually. The 12- to 17-year-old child cohort in India, meanwhile, [needs](https://www.indiatoday.in/coronavirus-outbreak/story/india-needs-26-crore-covid-vaccine-doses-to-inoculate-children-in-12-17-age-group-dr-vk-paul-1811275-2021-06-05) 260 million doses.
|
|
|
The core of the narrative determining who gets credit for vaccinating India’s children is now centred on targeting children at risk due to comorbidities first. And treating all children—privileged or not—with equity by providing vaccines for free under the government programme.
It has become a long game now. The first step in this story is making a plan to avoid criticism in the future, and the central government’s [plan](https://timesofindia.indiatimes.com/city/pune/task-force-draws-clues-from-second-wave-to-prioritize-vax-for-adolescents/articleshow/86819991.cms) comes out next week.

Meesho's new round begins the war for talent
On 30 September, Indian social commerce company Meesho raised US$570 million at a valuation of US$4.9 billion, more than doubling in value in just six months. This is still only a [fraction](https://the-ken.com/story/piecing-together-softbanks-us300m-social-commerce-bet-on-meesho/) of what Indian e-commerce giants like Flipkart are worth. But now, Meesho has the funding to chase its ambitions to grow its business and market share, and compete at a more equal footing with its larger rivals.
But the valuation story is also about something else. It’s a way to attract talent.
Over the last couple of years, Meesho’s co-founders have repeatedly argued that hiring and retaining talent is as crucial for them as growing transactions on their platform. In fact, Meesho [plans](https://www.business-standard.com/article/companies/social-commerce-startup-meesho-raises-570-mn-valuation-doubles-to-4-9-bn-121093000509_1.html) to use this funding round to grow its technology and product talent 2.5X.
“We will continue to invest in talent to build best-in-class tech and product solutions, while focussing on growth and furthering our efforts in scaling our online grocery business, Farmiso,” Vidit Aatrey, founder and chief executive officer, Meesho, said in a [statement](https://yourstory.com/2021/09/funding-meesho-570-million-series-f-fidelity-b-capital-49-billion-valuation/amp).
The company might have [struggled](https://inc42.com/startups/meesho-revamps-indias-unorganised-retail-by-empowering-resellers/) in the past to acquire talent, when all it had was a vision. But the recent validation from investors has strengthened its brand in the talent market.
Meesho has also backed this up with several softer moves over the last few months. Last month, it [announced](https://hr.economictimes.indiatimes.com/news/trends/meesho-declares-10-day-work-break-as-part-of-its-reset-recharge-policy/85864375) a 10-day work break as part of its Reset & Recharge policy for all employees and a [30-week](https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/news/company/corporate-trends/new-parents-at-meesho-to-get-30-weeks-of-gender-neutral-leave/articleshow/86446156.cms) gender-neutral leave policy for all new parents, with no differentiation between natural childbirth, adoption, or surrogacy. It had also announced a four-day work week in May.
It isn’t stinting on the financial perks either. In February 2020, about 200 of the company’s 800 employees held stock options, and out of the 60 who were eligible for Meesho’s ESOP scheme, 20 [exercised](https://www.livemint.com/companies/start-ups/meesho-enables-esop-buyback-for-20-employees-11581588179935.html) their options for a collective Rs 7 crore (US$1 million). It had also rolled out another ESOP buyback programme worth [about](https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/markets/stocks/news/meesho-undertakes-esop-buyback-worth-5-mn/articleshow/79146697.cms) US$5 million in November of the same year.
|
|
|
In a [podcast](https://yourstory.com/2021/10/secret-sauce-category-defining-startup-social-commerce-meesho-vidit-aatrey/amp) this week, Aatrey also went on to argue that it was the compelling story of how Meesho helps small entrepreneurs that has convinced talent to join and stay.
In the past, he said, he would give potential employees the contact numbers of vendors on the platform and ask them to have a conversation. It was a way to make them aware of the impact that Meesho was having on small business owners. And despite the recent upswing in its fortunes, it is a practice that he says he would like to continue. In Aatrey’s experience, getting potential employees to listen to the stories of real businesses is still a strong tool to hire talent, high valuations notwithstanding.

Musk's broadband vision for India is built on hope
Sanjay Bhargava, who had worked with Elon Musk as part of the team that founded electronic payment firm PayPal, [joined](https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/industry/telecom/telecom-news/sanjay-bhargava-to-head-elon-musks-starlink-satellite-broadband-venture-in-india/articleshow/86612212.cms?from=mdr) the billionaire entrepreneur’s satellite broadband venture in India on 1 October.
Over the last week, several news [reports](https://m.economictimes.com/industry/telecom/telecom-news/jeff-bezos-elon-musk-could-soon-take-on-mukesh-ambani-and-sunil-mittal-in-the-space-turf/articleshow/86672757.cms) have said that Musk and Jeff Bezos have consulted the government and want to offer broadband in India. Even though they haven’t yet applied for a licence, Bhargava has announced in a LinkedIn [post](https://www.linkedin.com/in/sanjaybhargava/detail/recent-activity/) that pre-orders from India have crossed 5,000.
“Our stretch target is to have 200,000 terminals active in India in December 2022. Actual numbers may be much lower than that or even zero if we do not get government approval but it is very unlikely that we will exceed 200,000,” he added. Starlink has started charging a deposit of US$99 (around Rs 7,350) per user.
In September, The Ken had [reported](https://the-ken.com/story/bharti-tata-take-shortcut-to-space-as-reliance-jio-looks-on/) that the Broadband India Forum, an association of technology players like Google, Hughes, and Facebook among others, and the Cellular Operators Association of India are up in arms against SpaceX. While Starlink is taking consumer bookings at a monthly fee of US$100, industry experts say that the costs are a lot higher. A space executive from Delhi quoted in the story had said: “Musk is trying to pump that kind of subsidy [US$2,500] which is not a sustainable model, though the hope is, with tremendous volumes, it’d come down to US$500.”
Whether Musk sticks around to play a long-term role in India’s broadband story will depend on whether these hopes come true, especially since he plans to take on India’s telecom czar Sunil Bharti Mittal and the Tatas.

Body positive ad campaign comes to India
Last week, Indian intimate wear and loungewear brand Clovia [launched](https://www.livemint.com/industry/advertising/clovia-launches-ad-campaign-featuring-its-leadership-team-11633336915946.html) a new advertising campaign called ‘Nothing But Real’. It shows the company’s leadership team sporting their collections instead of the usual line-up of hired models. It was a major step for Clovia and in keeping with the global trend of promoting body positivity.
A milestone in this rising story came in June, when American lingerie, clothing, and beauty retailer Victoria’s Secret, known for its high-visibility marketing, did a complete turnaround from the past by rolling out a new [campaign](https://www.cosmopolitan.com/style-beauty/fashion/a36753559/victorias-secret-launches-vs-collective-celebrity-ambassadors/) that seeks to redefine the very idea of what “sexy” is.
|
|
|
The New York Times has deeply [analysed](https://www.nytimes.com/2021/06/16/business/victorias-secret-collective-megan-rapinoe.html) what this new kind of branding would mean to the customers:
|
They will be spearheading what may be the most extreme and unabashed attempt at a brand turnaround in recent memory: an effort to redefine the version of “sexy” that Victoria’s Secret represents (and sells) to the masses. For decades, Victoria’s Secret’s scantily clad supermodels with Jessica Rabbit curves epitomized a certain widely accepted stereotype of femininity. Now, with that kind of imagery out of step with the broader culture and Victoria’s Secret facing increased competition and internal turmoil, the company wants to become, its chief executive said, a leading global “advocate” for female empowerment.

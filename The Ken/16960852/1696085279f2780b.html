“In God we trust, all others we monitor.”—that’s often quoted as the unofficial motto of the US National Security Agency, perhaps the epitome of government snooping. (The line is actually used by a rather less interesting agency under the US Air Force called AFTAC, which monitors nuclear treaties, but hey, it’s the thought that counts.)
 
It does lose a bit of its punch, though, in a time when just about everyone is monitoring or tracking everything we do. From the Facebooks, Googles and Amazons to a whole host of faceless marketing, advertising and tech companies around the world.
 
Today’s story is about one of those companies. A seven-year-old Indian startup called SilverPush, founded by a man named Hitesh Chawla.
 
After dabbling with “smart push notifications”, the company came into its own five to six years ago when Chawla and Co. hit upon a novel method of tracking what ads people were watching. Not just online, but on television—through ultrasonic audio beacons hidden in the TV signal, inaudible to the human ear but which could be picked up by apps on your smartphone that used SilverPush’s tech.
 
It’s an advertiser’s dream. And business went well, funding rounds were raised, and nobody outside the tech/startup world really paid attention to SilverPush. Till it caught the eye of a US-based non-profit that flagged it to the authorities, and all hell broke loose for Chawla. The US FTC issued a warning to app developers, the technology press ran with it, investors turned cagey, his co-founder quit and revenues more than halved.
 
It was time, Chawla decided, to get the hell out of the tracking business. (“SilverPush Quits Creeping World Out”, one headline read.)
 
Fast forward to today. Just about two weeks ago, SilverPush raised $5 million. Revenues, it claims, more than doubled in 2018. It has more than a hundred clients, including names like Unilever, Nissan, Nestle and Sony Pictures. Global expansion is going well. All on the back of some interesting (and less creepy) new tech it has patented in the past couple of years.

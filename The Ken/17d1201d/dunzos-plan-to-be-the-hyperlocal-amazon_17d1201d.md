My first experience with Dunzo was at a friend's home in Bengaluru, maybe five years ago.
As we sat down to lunch, he said we should have potato chips from a specific shop a couple of kilometres away. I said we could go and get them. His response: "Are you mad? Let me just Dunzo it."
I was aware of Dunzo, but I was dumbfounded that you could get a gofer of sorts to pick up a single packet of chips that cost half a dollar. (For context, I live in Mumbai, and Dunzo launched here in 2019.)
But Dunzo has since come a long way from being known as a concierge service. Around 85% of its business now comes from grocery and food delivery. And it's on the back of grocery that Dunzo plans to expand its footprint–from eight cities to as many as 25.
“Well-funded startups like Swiggy and Grofers took seven years to build what we'll create in the next 18 months," says Kabeer Biswas, co-founder and CEO of Dunzo.
Dunzo believes there are 500,000 products suited to being delivered in 30 minutes. But the economics of expanding beyond the most essential 2,000-2,500 stock-keeping units (SKUs) is tricky, especially when Dunzo is burning $2.4 million a month.
Complicating matters further is the fact that everyone from e-grocery market leader BigBasket to upstart Zepto is promising instant delivery. But Dunzo could have a giant in its corner in this bruising fight. Billionaire Mukesh Ambani's Reliance Industries is reportedly leading a $200-250 million funding round in Dunzo at an $800 million valuation.

Can Rapido become Ola before Ola becomes Rapido?

Something caught my eye yesterday. 

And yes, it’s about my favourite company.

US-based asset management company Vanguard has marked down Ola’s valuation to $1.25 billion, according to an SEC filing. This represents more than 80% drop from its peak valuation of $7.3 billion in 2021.

In February last year, Vanguard had for the first time valued the Bhavish Aggarwal-led company below the $2-billion mark — at $1.88 billion. However, it later revalued the company at around $2 billion in November.

The latest development comes as Ola continues to lose ground in the ride-hailing space. Rapido has emerged as the market leader, rapidly expanding market leadership in bike taxis to its auto rickshaw and cabs business, followed by Uber, with Ola now relegated to the third spot.

Vanguard slashes Ola’s valuation to $1.25 billion, down 80% from peak, Financial Express

If you’ve been reading me for a while, you’ll know by now that I usually tell subscribers not to read too much into startup valuations. Unicorn clubs are not too different from unicorns themselves—they are fictional creatures. In fact, nobody knows this better than Bhavish Aggarwal, co-founder and CEO of Ola, who practically created a unicorn out of thin air. Startup valuations are just imaginary animals. 

Anyway, today, I’m going to break that rule for the only good reason to do it, i.e., to tell a great story.  

While Vanguard was marking Ola’s valuation down periodically, another ride-hailing startup’s valuation has been moving in the opposite direction. In September last year, Rapido raised $200 million from Westbridge Capital, which valued the company at $1.1 billion. 

So to summarise—at least one of Ola’s investors believes that Ola is worth $1.25 billion, and at least one of Rapido’s investors believes that Rapido is worth $1.1 billion. 

Even if you ignore their specific valuations, the trend is uncontestable. Ola’s been falling, Rapido’s climbing, and now, their paths cross in a moment of reversal.

How did this happen?

Well, depending on how you think about it, two things come to mind.

One theory is that Rapido simply “out-executed” Ola. When we were discussing Rapido in The Ken’s internal Slack, Seetharaman G (deputy editor and tamil meme expert) wrote, “Regardless of how successful Rapido is, I just find the businesses it’s in—ride-hailing and food delivery—so monumentally boring now. They are like steel and cement. What can Rapido do now that’s unique?”

After taking suitable offence on behalf of my family (both my father and my father-in-law spent their entire careers in cement and steel respectively), I had to admit that Seetharaman was probably right. Maybe the answer is boring, i.e., Rapido simply out-executed Ola. 

There’s some evidence for this. Rapido’s revenues have been climbing every year, while Ola’s declined last year. Rapido has been growing, expanding its products and experiences. Ola seems to be playing defence. Paradoxically, Rapido’s execution can be explained by two dual, and often competing forces—focus and opportunism.

One of the defining traits about Ola is its ability to spin businesses up quickly depending on the ‘vibe’. When food delivery becomes popular, it creates a food-delivery business.

When quick-commerce is in fashion, it starts doing 10-minute delivery. Tomorrow, if bootcut jeans make a comeback, I fully expect Ola to jump on the bandwagon. None of these businesses usually go anywhere, but Ola is there, always waiting to leap into the water to swim with the current.

Chances are that most people would classify Ola as the “opportunistic” company. 

All of these people are wrong. 

Rapido’s opportunism is much more measured, but it’s also deadlier, and more effective.

Last week, when I wrote about how ONDC is losing, I wrote that restaurants are walking back from ONDC because they no longer believe it can “save” them from Zomato and Swiggy. 

Guess who neatly stepped in to rescue them.

Ride-hailing unicorn Rapido is in advanced discussions with the National Restaurant Association of India (NRAI) to onboard restaurants for its foray into the food delivery space. NRAI, which represents over 500,000 restaurants across the country, has long been advocating for a viable “third-player” in a market currently dominated by Swiggy and Zomato, together accounting for a 95% share.

Rapido in advance talks with NRAI for food delivery foray, Financial Express

I don’t think anyone, not even Ola, could credibly tell you how many times the company has attempted food delivery. The earliest news article I could find that talks about it dates back to 2015. It made another attempt in 2017 after acquiring Foodpanda, another food-delivery startup. Then it tried again in 2019 with something called Ola Cafe. Now it’s back with another avatar on ONDC with something called Ola Dash, which is 10-minute food delivery.

Meanwhile, all this time, Rapido watched and waited. It waited and waited. 

And now, it has struck, targeting the right people, with the right offering, at the perfect time.

The opposite of opportunism that diversifies your business is focus, which forces you to stay in your lane. And well, for years, Rapido’s founders have stuck to what they were doing, while Bhavish Aggarwal discovered side-hustles like electric cars, AI, semiconductors, and mentoring Elon Musk.

I suppose knowing when to be opportunistic and expand, and when to focus and do nothing else is the simplest description of effective execution. 

Another theory is that Rapido just came up with a better business model. In the past, I’ve written how companies like Meesho and Rapido disrupted incumbents like Amazon, Flipkart, Uber, and Ola by innovating not just on product but also on pricing. Both companies opted for a “zero-commission” model, which fundamentally altered the dynamics of how sellers operate on these marketplaces. In fact, Rapido’s zero-commission system even forced Uber to change its business model. 

Rapido started off at the bottom of the market, aggregating two-wheeler cabs. Uber and Ola tried to fight it off, but then relinquished that segment thanks to a combination of regulatory uncertainty and lower margins. Then Rapido went into autos, pushing Uber and Ola up-market. Today, Rapido operates cabs, and Uber has no choice but to go even higher to create products and services like Uber Black. As longtime readers may recall, this is classic disruption theory. 

Yesterday, Rapido disrupted Uber and Ola. Now, it’s trying to disrupt Swiggy and Zomato. 

Who’s next? 

Both of these reasons explain why Rapido is doing better than Ola, but it doesn’t explain Rapido’s and Ola’s valuations. Sometimes, a company’s performance is correlated with its valuation, but sometimes, it isn’t. 

Maybe the real reason is that Rapido didn’t have to make the market. It came late, and from an investor’s standpoint, that makes Rapido much more attractive than an Ola or an Uber. Essentially, the theory is that companies that have to go first to create a market are riskier bets for an investor than companies that have to go later to take advantage of it. Seen purely as a risk-reward equation, I imagine there are many more investors willing to bet on a new, shiny startup that can take market share away from an incumbent than they will be on it to create a completely new market. Look at Zepto as an example. Even though Swiggy started Instamart years earlier, there are enough investors ready to bet on “shorting” Swiggy’s chances of holding on to the market, and for Zepto to take a chunk of it away. Companies that come in late also have the advantage of cleaner, sparser captables, which makes it a much easier and attractive decision for investors. 

Maybe it’s not just Rapido that’s more focused and opportunistic. 

Even Rapido’s investors are more focused and opportunistic. 

And maybe at the end of the day, the ultimate reason why Rapido can become Ola before Ola becomes Rapido comes down to just that.

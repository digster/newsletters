Good Morning Ishan,

“I’ve been told I’m single handedly helping India to make better data regulations and to fine companies if they lose user data like GDPR. Didn’t expect this outcome when we hosted this site.”

Would you want to hazard a guess who said these famous words, which are quoted in today’s story?

Edward Snowden? Margrethe Vestager? Nandan Nilekani? Mark Zuckerberg? 

All wrong. The correct answer is ninja_storm.

Who’s ninja_storm, you ask? It’s the hacker who claimed to have hacked into the systems of one of India’s largest digital payment platforms, MobiKwik*. And allegedly downloaded KYC (“Know Your Customer”) and usage data of over 100 million users.

ninja_storm claims to have accessed this information on 24 February, and then offered to sell it for 1.5 BTC (approximately $90,000).

But a few days ago, the hacker claimed to have deleted all of the hacked data. The site where the stolen data was earlier hosted now reads, “All Mobikwik data is deleted on our servers. All users safe!”

To which Sumanth and guest author Anand Venkatanarayanan, the authors of today’s story ask, quite reasonably, “Is there any guarantee that the blackhat hacker, a bad actor by definition, hasn’t retained a copy of the data for himself or has sold it to other buyers? After all, if he has indeed downloaded MobiKwik’s entire data, such rich details about 100 million users would be worth well in excess of the $90,000 ransom amount demanded.”

Indeed. The hacker’s page has a response to this, too.

"You’ll believe our claims when you don’t see any statistical raise (sic.) in misuse of Indian KYC data linked to Mobikwik accounts in next one year or so." 

If only it were that simple. Read today’s story to understand the immense scale of the alleged breach, its potential ramifications, and what changes it might lead to in the way late stage Indian tech platforms think about security and privacy.

Because, if nothing is certain in our internet lives except taxes and data breaches, it’s time we spend some time to understand the latter, especially the sheer scale of KYC data that must be sitting with dozens, possibly hundreds of FinTech apps and startups.

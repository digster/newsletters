Commenting on the stock market, legendary investor Warren Buffet once said it was wise for investors to be “fearful when others are greedy, and greedy when others are fearful”.
Unfortunately, that’s playing out in homecare in India. Patients have become fearful as many homecare providers are getting greedy; and greedy they are because everyone wants assured care during the pandemic.
In May, we
[wrote](https://the-ken.com/story/the-elite-vc-founder-club-riding-aarogya-setu-to-telemed-domination/) about the mega telemed hustle; we said it was a “demonetisation moment”. And what a moment it has turned out to be—for established hospital chains, overnight homecare providers, and everyone in between. Even for vanilla health call centres. In Karnataka, Piramal Swasthya quoted a monthly budget of ~Rs 3 crore ($405,000) for running Covid call centre services. It was a shocker of a proposal.
Patients will have to brace up. Private hospital chains now offer homecare packages ranging from Rs 4,900 ($70) to Rs 2.5 lakh ($3,400). It’s virtually the wild west.
Even though telemedicine guidelines were amended in March, the government gave its nod for remote care monitoring only in mid-May. Today, whether it be state-sponsored homecare services or those offered by private hospitals, telemedicine is the common link. Even the most inexpensive packages offered by hospital chains have teleconsultations with doctors and remote monitoring by nurses baked into them.
But all of that is inadequate with cases surging in some parts of the country. In Delhi, one in two Covid-positive patients are not even receiving the first phone call from the state, officials manning Delhi’s triaging system confirmed to The Ken. And it’s the terrifying uncertainty that is prompting patients to turn to private hospitals.
Union health secretary Rajesh Bhushan told Maitri, the writer of today’s story, that the Centre is observing “best practices” employed by the states in homecare. But given that almost every state is a country unto itself with wide variations in service, the current ‘wait and watch’ strategy will only allow for more patient fleecing and distress.

Our ACs are groaning under the summer heat. Where is the tech to save them, and us? Current air conditioner models are ill-designed to deal with rising global temperatures

Good Morning Ishan,

For the most part of March, April, and May, my morning workplace ritual was to go around increasing the air-conditioner temperature from 16°C to 24°C. And every time I picked up the AC remote muttering “it’s so cold” under my breath, I’d hear someone—the early birds at The Ken office in Bengaluru—mutter sotto voce, “it was stuffy” or “too hot”. 

This should tell you two things:

1) That the AC we use in the office is old. A newer model won’t go below 18°C because globally, ACs are being preset to higher temperatures to limit rising energy usage for cooling. India, which became the first country to draw up a cooling action plan, “advises” OEMs to preset ACs to 24°C.

2) That sometimes I pull rank and cause discomfort to my colleagues. But if you’ve been reading Green Margins editions on air conditioning, you surely understand my itch. 

The world has just seen its 12 hottest months on record, on the trot. While parts of southern India have cooled thanks to the monsoon, northern and western India is still burning. I hear of ACs sputtering, or not being effective in this heat, or just straight out catching fire https://timesofindia.indiatimes.com/city/ghaziabad/why-acs-are-catching-fire-during-a-heatwave/articleshow/110777885.cms.

Are our ACs even designed for such high ambient temperatures? 

I checked my Daikin ACs, bought in late 2022, and they are “designed” for 36°C. A colleague bought one last month and the “max rated temperature” for that LG model is 52°C. That’s better than 36°C for sure, especially when the ambient daytime temperature is consistently crossing 40°C in many parts of India and even 50°C in some places, but it’s still not good enough. 

We need a step change in ACs, and a massive one at that.

Cooling is hot, but also up for disruption

As you can see from the chart above, India’s space-cooling demand is on a tear. OEMs say it’s tier-2 and -3 towns that are driving the growth; I’d written about this earlier https://the-ken.com/greenmargins/why-cooling-is-going-to-be-hotly-debated-in-dubai-this-month/: 

“Once air conditioning is introduced, it quickly spreads from transportation hubs, commercial enterprises, and ultimately to the home. This is the case in India, where we expect demand for air conditioning to rise at a 12-15% CAGR through 2030.”

AC manufacturers are launching new models and adding fancy features, but for nearly 70–80% of the market, there’s very little product differentiation. That’s because the core AC technology remains the same. The last big invention in the AC technology came around the 2000s, when Daikin introduced VRF ACs in India, a few years after launching them in the global market. 

For quick context, here’s a very simplified version of how ACs work (you can skip over it if you don’t want to get too deep into the weeds):

1. There’s a refrigerant, which leaves the room section of the AC having absorbed the heat from the air and hence cooling the room. 

2. It heads to the compressor, which as its name suggests, compresses it into a denser and hence hotter state. 

3. This hot gas is fed through the condenser, where it transfers its heat to the outside air and becomes cool again.

4. The now cooled refrigerant then heads back to the room section and the evaporator, where it absorbs the heat and cools the room. 

5. And the cycle—imaginatively called the refrigeration cycle https://www.buildingengines.com/blog/knowledge-refrigeration-cycle/#:~:text=A%20refrigeration%20cycle%20has%20four,by%20the%20principles%20of%20thermodynamics.—keeps going as long as the AC is on.

VRF models—VRF stands for variable refrigerant flow—are essentially smart ACs where the compressor modulates the load and the cooling in the room. 

It also came with a new refrigerant gas, R410, an eco-friendly material that didn’t pose any danger to the ozone layer. (It was R410 that led to the phasing out https://blog.msasafety.com/r-22-refrigerant-phase-out/ of older refrigerants like R22 which were resulting in emissions that were depleting the ozone layer.)

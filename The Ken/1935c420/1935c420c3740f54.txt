NSE’s monopoly is on borrowed time as Sebi squeezes its star player https://the-ken.com/story/nses-monopoly-was-unshakeable-until-now/?utm_source=weekly_email&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=weekly_recap_card

The markets regulator’s new rules will wipe out 30% of the stock exchange’s volumes in options trading, its largest business

The National Stock Exchange shut down options trading in the trader-favourite Bank Nifty weekly, following Sebi’s mandate aimed at curbing equity derivatives. The NSE, which accounts for about 90% of the equity-options trade, stands to lose at least 30% trading volumes; yet, its unlisted stock has seen a surge. Market participants anticipate the stock to reflect the impact of F&O-trading squeeze, at least when the NSE’s long-jinxed IPO hits the ground. Meanwhile, the regulator’s move may prove to be a blessing in disguise for its smaller rival BSE, which may end up with a larger slice of the pie. Mamaearth sold investors on its FMCG dreams. Consumers had other plans https://the-ken.com/story/mamaearth-sold-investors-on-its-fmcg-dreams-consumers-had-other-plans/?utm_source=weekly_email&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=weekly_recap_card

Honasa Consumer’s switch from super-stockists to direct distributors left it with dead stock, mounting losses, and shaken investor confidence

In November, Mamearth’s parent reported its first loss since its public listing one year ago. The culprit: a one-time hit from a shift in the company’s distribution strategy—dumping super-stockists in favour of an in-house sales team. The fallout: an inventory write-down of around Rs 70 crore, but some distributors allege the damage is even worse, with Rs 300 crore worth of stock lying unsold and unclaimed. The result: the stock hit an all-time low, wiping out Rs 3,500 crore from its market cap. Tata and ITC are harvesting what small organic-food brands cultivated for years https://the-ken.com/story/tata-and-itc-are-harvesting-what-small-organic-food-brands-cultivated-for-years/?utm_source=weekly_email&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=weekly_recap_card

Conscious consumers are gravitating toward organic food. But, with a mix of small to mid-sized brands and FMCG majors, the playing field is far from level

There’s no shortage of companies offering organic food products in India; at least 10 brands, including startups and FMCG majors offer cold-pressed oil on quick-commerce platforms. While these small companies have put in the ground work in educating people about organic food and building a farmer network, larger companies are reaping the benefits. That’s because the demand for organic food has seen a sharp uptick—from 1.5 million households to 5–6 million in under four years. Yet, the space is seeing slower adoption rates, thanks to regulatory pain points. [Read the full story https://the-ken.com/story/tata-and-itc-are-harvesting-what-small-organic-food-brands-cultivated-for-years/?utm_source=weekly_email&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=weekly_recap_card]

Parents and fintechs love school-fee loans. So why are schools not all in? https://the-ken.com/story/parents-and-fintechs-love-school-fee-loans-so-why-are-schools-not-all-in/?utm_source=weekly_email&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=weekly_recap_card

At no extra costs and with freebies like ice-creams and movie tickets, school-fee loans should be easiest to sell. Yet, half a dozen fintechs are learning the hard way—cracking the profitability equation isn’t an easy one here

Until a few years ago, taking loans for school fees was unheard of. Now the fintechs are racing to win in this virgin market. While zero-cost EMIs are a game-changer for parents burdened with heavy expenses, for schools, it means a positive cash flow and less collection headache. Yet fintechs are finding it hard to find a footing in the fragmented space, especially when loans are for convenience not sales for parents and schools. From turning into a payments platform to turning to a flat-fee model, they’re trying new tricks to capture the nearly 400,000 private-school market.

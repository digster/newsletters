Ed Set Go
Mon, 21 Apr 25
The one stop to understand the biggest shifts in education, written by the best edtech journalist in the country..
Good Morning Ishan,
It’s not a great time to be, or plan to be, an international student. Geopolitics right now is a tinderbox, with some of the world’s biggest players locked in an intense game of chess—or chicken, as the case may be.
In the US, international students—of whom Indians make up a significant bulk—are facing unimaginable hostility. Their legal status is tenuous, and there’s little explanation for why almost 400 Indian students have had their visas revoked. Some are fighting back on legal grounds, but for most, the cost of a lawyer—in addition to the tuition money they’re losing—is going to be prohibitive.
There are no clear reasons for why the US is brandishing this sledgehammer, but universities have promised that students won’t lose any instructional time—they can finish their term online and still earn their degrees. This move carries a strong whiff of pandemic-era measures, when thousands of students had to be plugged into international classrooms. At least, the level of uncertainty is the same. It also reminds me of a much smaller exodus of Indian students from Ukraine when war broke out in the region.
Online degrees, though, often don’t carry as much water as on-campus ones. There’s been more success in upskilling, where several edtechs such as Upgrad have tried their hand at hybrid degrees. A hybrid degree gets a student some facetime with teachers on a foreign campus, and combines that with a year or two of online classes from their homes in India. That movement, though, doesn’t come anywhere close to the wave of students who continue to plan and pursue international degrees in person.
The Union Ministry of Education has been sending feelers to foreign universities to set up shop in India. There’s even a designated location for this purpose—GIFT City in Gandhinagar, Gujarat.
But is it a realistic alternative for students who want an international education without the uncertainty of unstable geopolitics and immigration rules? So far, two Australian universities—Deakin and Wollongong—seem to have set up international campuses there. The University of Southampton from the UK is expected to launch its Gurugram campus this August. The foreign universities in question, and the Indian government, are relying on students to make a simple [cost-benefit analysis](https://www.theguardian.com/education/2025/feb/07/uk-universities-open-campuses-india-financial-woes-at-home) on choosing to stay in India versus going abroad:
Indian students wanting to take a BSc in business management at Southampton’s home campus would pay £24,000 in annual tuition fees, plus hefty visa, travel and accommodation costs.
But from August, the university’s new branch at Gurgaon’s International Tech Park, a special economic zone, will teach the same course for 1.3m rupees a year, about £12,000.
Foreign universities, though, have a high bar to meet if they’re planning to set up in India. The first stumbling block is that there can’t be any discrepancies between the quality of the courses offered at their Indian campuses and those back at home, even if they’re among the top-ranked globally. It’s one of those compliance measures that’s going to be hard to measure, especially since the universities in GIFT City can be set up outside of the prescribed norms of the University Grants Commission.
The second is finding world-class talent that’s willing to move to the tech-park environs of Delhi and Gujarat, since these foreign university campuses are only allowed to take 10% of their courses online. Even if university professors wanted to offer world-class courses to Indian students, they wouldn’t be able to without actually moving to India.
The third, and maybe the knottiest problem to solve, is the job market. The lure of Optional Practical Training (OPT) in the US is that students get to stay on, work, and ultimately gain permanent resident status. Generations of tech workers have used this route when there was demand for high-skilled workers in foreign countries.
Now, even with an international degree in hand, will Indian students find the jobs that justify their investments?
Maybe with an international degree, Indian students can still enter an international job market. The question, however, is whether these degrees are accorded the same recognition, and command the same salaries, as a traditional international degree. There’s also the question of future visa caps on international workers.
Indians with degrees and qualifications need to be competitive global products that can move seamlessly between job markets. But institutions, on both sides of the ocean, are closing more doors than opening them.

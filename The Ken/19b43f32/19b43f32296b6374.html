Zachariah George is an Indian-born investor running one of Africa’s most active early-stage venture firms. From Cape Town, his fund, Launch Africa Ventures, has invested in more than 160 startups across 25 countries in just a few years. That scale alone makes him hard to ignore. What makes him more interesting is what he doesn’t believe in.

George doesn’t think Africa can afford the kind of patience that defined India’s startup boom. Too many countries. Too few buyers. Too little margin for long, expensive bets that only work if a unicorn eventually shows up. So he does something venture capitalists are not supposed to do: he sells early, sometimes quickly, and often for returns that look unremarkable by VC standards.

The logic is uncomfortable. In a market where exits are rare, proving that liquidity exists at all may matter more than swinging for billion-dollar outcomes. But that logic raises its own questions. If everyone exits early, who builds scale? And if returns start to resemble public markets, minus the liquidity, what exactly is venture capital still offering?

Today’s story by contributor Sriram sits with those questions rather than smoothing them over. It follows George’s journey from Lehman Brothers in New York to an unexpected VC career in Africa. As funding into African startups falls for a third straight year and he struggles to raise a larger second fund, Sriram examines his results, and also the incentives and tradeoffs beneath them: management fees, lifestyle arbitrage, investor unease, and a past firing from a major African bank that doesn’t show up in pitch meetings.

This is not a celebration of a contrarian VC. It’s a closer look at what happens when a familiar playbook meets a market that refuses to cooperate, and what gets compromised along the way. Read the full story here.

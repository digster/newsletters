I’m sure you’ve got it by now. To brachiate means to move about by hanging and swinging. Primates are largely unique in their ability to brachiate, aided by freely rotating wrists, short fingernails, smaller thumbs, and most importantly, flexible shoulder joints.

Of course, we humans diverged from our primate ancestors by choosing to travel on the ground using bipedalism instead of through the trees using brachiation. But, our shoulder joints, spine, and body structure still haven’t fully made the evolutionary shifts. Worse, we’ve transitioned from walking around to sitting around in just a few hundred years.

When is the last time you recall swinging from monkey bars? Or hanging from a pull-up bar without gloves, tape, or assistance? Most modern people lose this skill as they do not use it regularly, but it can be vital to having healthy and functional shoulders. The shoulder joint is a ball and socket joint, similar to the hips, which allow for glide and rotation. If our arms generally hang to our sides, why are our shoulder girdles designed to rotate? Were we designed to hang overhead? The shoulder girdle: Think about this joint of the body. Unlike the spine, hips, knees, and ankles, the shoulders are not loaded while standing upright against gravity. With that said, why do so many modern-day humans suffer from pain caused by cervical spine disc bulges, herniations, stenosis and spondylolisthesis? If we aren’t routinely walking on our hands (I don’t know about you, but I’m not), how do issues like shoulder impingements, rotator cuff, AC joint injuries, frozen shoulders, and carpal tunnel syndromes come to be? And even further, why are humans physically deteriorating as we advance technologically? Perhaps the issue is not only the movement patterns we are overusing but also about the positions and postures we were built to do but no longer utilize.

Why We Took a Break From Brachiating & Function of the Shoulder, Boundless Body

Kids are brilliant at brachiating. You see them swinging gleefully from jungle gym bars in parks. Us adults though, by and large, suck at it. 

I’ve been learning to brachiate for the last week, and I can tell you, it’s been incredible for my recurrent rotator cuff, neck, and shoulder pain. I’m up to three reps of 60 seconds each from a doorway pullup bar. In fact, my fitness mantra for 2024 is, “Bro, do you even brachiate?” 😬

My 2023 fitness mantra was, “Bro, do you even barefoot?”

1. First Principles Quests

For the last few weeks, Jonathan, Anushka, and I (and starting last week, Hari, our new intern!) have been discussing how to tap into the incredible depth and diversity of insights and experiences we have among the FP Community. That is, folks like you.

First Principles started as a way for me to talk about the interesting conversations I had with my podcast guests. It very quickly evolved to (still) me also sharing my favourite mental models, learnings, habits, and nature photos.

Then, we started getting many of you to send us your book recommendations, photographs, and habits.

I feel we’re ready to make a big leap together.

Given the richness of interests, experiences, and insights that exist in the FP community, I think we should harness it proactively. Thus, “Quests”.

“Quests” will be a planned edition of First Principles every 4-6 weeks, where we collectively pick a theme that we’re interested in, and try to generate the most interesting of questions, approaches, learnings, habits, and recommendations. Together, we will embark on these Quests every now and then, taking a First Principles lens to some of the most important topics in life.

The things we think about on, you know, Sundays.

Fitness. Ambition. Parenting. Starting up. Coaching. Pets. Reinvention. Art. Friendships. Solitude.

As you’ve probably guessed, we’re starting with Fitness. It’s an open-ended Quest. I am not defining the meaning of fitness, or its boundaries. I understand it means something different to each of us. I understand even that that meaning changes as we grow older. For instance, fitness for me in my 20s was very different from what it was in my 30s or my 40s.

So, I ask you, how should we approach our first Quest, Fitness?

  • What do you want to learn from this Quest?

  • What kind of community responses (or members) do you want to hear?

  • What should this Quest not be about? What should we avoid?

  • How would you even define this Quest in one or two sentences, if you had to?

  • What are some of the other topics you feel the FP Community should collectively think about?

Mail your ideas and thoughts to us at fp@the-ken.com.

While I’m not yet fully clear what our Quests will turn out to be, I am 100% sure what they will not be. I will set a very high bar to ensure we’re not merely collecting conventional wisdom, AI-generated nonsense, self-promotional insights, or untested and dangerous advice.

Great Quests should have insightful, personal, experienced, non-judgemental, and often counterintuitive learnings that make us question the way we’d been thinking about topics.

Brachiation is one of those things for me. As was the idea of “barefoot” walking and running, which a former colleague set me off on the path to by lending me a copy of Christopher McDougall’s brilliant book, “Born To Run”. One of these editions, perhaps even in the Fitness Quest, we can dive deeper into that.

2. #FPPodcast 🎙

One of the questions that we like to ask almost every guest on the First Principles podcast is this one: what drives you?

It’s a simple question, right? But I often look forward to this one because the answer may be something straightforward, but it is often deeply personal to the founder or leader. And it’s always answered quickly, readily, like a refrain—solidifying for me that it’s as important to repeat your purpose, as it is to have one.

And this week, it was Viren Shetty, the vice-chairman of Narayana Health, who told us what this purpose was, for him.

Rohin Dharmakumar

What is that passion that’s driving (you)—is it that vision that you talked about of building something, which does not exist and can be seen as a mode, in the rest of the world? 

Viren Shetty

Yeah.

Because you’re going through this day after day in the normal corporate mode. I mean, that would absolutely make me want to kill myself towards the end of it because it can bog you down. 

It can be this thing that would be either overwhelming or just more of the same, like I mentioned about the Groundhog Day of one fire after another. 

Like the job… there has to be some passion behind it. It has to be challenging. It has to be interesting. 

And so it’s like I’m playing this game on hard mode, right? Now, hard mode, meaning go after things we don’t know that we can do. 

We don’t know that we can run insurance, we don’t know that we can do software, we don’t know that we can even successfully implement, you know, the OpenAI bots into our discharge protocol, but try it. 

And so if I can, if we can make it work, it’s interesting. It’s challenging. It’s something the world has not seen before. That is what makes me passionate about doing that. If it’s just more of the same. If it is… then I would definitely succumb to all sorts of ennui that a lot of people end up feeling as mid-career professionals.

5. #FPLinks 🔖

One of the loopiest but funnest reads I’ve come across in a while:

How Elites Cause Crime By Acing Marshmallow Tests

“Which brings us to my favorite essayist, hotelconcierge, and his wonderful thesis that in attempting to select our modern elites purely through meritocratic academic competition, we inadvertently also select heavily for the psychology profile that drives a teenager to study 8 hours a day throughout childhood: “The Desire To Pass Tests” (TDTPT).

[…]

And his key observation is that this Desire — the raw need to “please authority figures” — is heavily selected for in our modern elites.

Once you understand that additional study improves test scores and outcomes, everything else slots into place. Between two smart-enough kids, which one gets into college?? The one who pushed themselves harder to read dull textbooks despite hormones, social media, videogames, and marijuana all vying for attention.

[…]

The “maladaptive perfectionist” of course — whose gut churned with anxiety and self-loathing when they received that 95% and fell far short of their expectations.

The more insane someone’s expectations about their own performance, the more they push themselves. The ones who overachieve the most are those who devise the most effective ways to manipulate their own psychology into jumping through ever more inane hoops, no matter the test that’s put in front of them.

His follow-on point is that these people are all deeply unhappy.

Thus: at scale, on the societal level, selecting for academic performance also selects for unhappiness.”

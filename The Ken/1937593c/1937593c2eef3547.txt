Marketing is eating itself from the inside. It’s a mad rush to sell, and in many ways, that’s what marketing has become all about today

What do you think makes for a great advertisement? Something that just sticks in your mind for a long time? 

Before you argue that it’s quite subjective, let me put forward at least one thing a great ad does: it starts a conversation. In the mind of a customer who is already in love with the product, it elevates a brand’s appeal and fosters a sense of belonging. In the mind of someone who’ll never use it anyway, it sticks. 

They talk about it. They understand what it stands for. They might never use it, but they remember.

And good marketers can do a wonderful job getting a brand to this place. Great marketing is a slow, intentional exercise, founded on insight and intuition in equal parts. And it takes years, if not decades, to build a brand that cuts across generations, leaves a lasting impression, and—ultimately—sells.

But today, the way the world of marketing has expanded, it’s almost impossible for someone in the industry to understand all the moving parts that are at play. Most marketers’ day jobs now consist of staring at dashboards for all the different campaigns they run, and not much beyond that.   

Sure, they have all the data in the world, but are they encouraged to trust their instincts and intuition to do something out of the box? 

And what about the brands? The internet is replete with online ad campaigns from D2C businesses, all fighting for your attention. This might help them move some inventory, but is it really doing anything to build a brand that people actually remember? 

The answer, more often than not, is no. 

Professor Y L R Moorthi teaches marketing, brand management, and marketing strategy, and has decades of experience in the space. And he put it quite simply:

“Suppose this brand is not available for the next three months, are you okay? 

These are some of the things that tell you what brand salience actually is. If you’re not tracking stuff like that, I think none of these things actually matter over a period of time.”

And that’s how marketing is starting to come undone, and why the things that once made it an exciting job have largely disappeared from the picture. 

The professor was one of our guests for this week’s episode https://the-ken.com/podcasts/two-by-two/marketing-is-eating-itself-from-the-inside/ of Two by Two. Joining him were Deepali Naair, group CMO at CK Birla Group and a practitioner of marketing for decades, and co-hosts Rohin Dharmakumar and Praveen Gopal Krishnan. 

Rohin and Praveen went into this discussion with two key questions—why is marketing dying? And how do we bring it back?

And while both guests were in agreement with our two hosts many times, they also didn’t hold back when they disagreed.

Bring back the marketing ‘anthropologist’

One of the most important roles the old-school marketer played was functioning as a conduit—bringing stories about customers to companies, and taking stories about companies to customers. This even allowed them to influence product decisions sometimes. 

But power like this doesn’t come easy. It goes beyond just doing surveys and tracking engagement. It comes from actually talking to the customer, understanding how they interact with your product, and most importantly, what they actually care about.

Prof. Moorthi: The anthropologist and the ethnographer are missing… the fellows who get that insight.

The other day, I was watching this guy, Chaitanya Ramalingegowda from Wakefit, which apparently is a Rs 1,000-crore (US$118 million) brand. And he was saying, “We are an online brand, but we pick up the phone every time and ask the customer, ‘Do you like this? Do you like that?’ I can do it on a chat box, but I don’t want to.”

And if marketers do stuff like this, and a whole lot more, then perhaps they get a seat at the decision-making table. 

As Deepali noted with an example from her time at consumer-goods company Marico, expanding roles such as Chief Business Officer and Chief Growth Officer show the influence that marketers can exert if they make a tangible difference with their effort. 

Deepali: A lot of the work that they do is the discipline of marketing. But it demonstrates itself differently inside organizations.

The KPIs (Key Performance Indicators) are sales, the KPIs are cost per lead, cost per sale, and things like that. Now, there are two ways of looking at that metamorphosis.

Somebody like me was extremely happy about it, because it actually gave me a seat on the table.

Because, you know, when I grew a business—and this is a real number I’m talking about—from contributing 7% to the company’s top line to 15%, I had a seat at the table.

I could actually ask, “What changes are you making to the product and pricing?”

Before that, as a marketer, I could only recommend, I could only suggest, I could only, you know, implore.

The never-ending jigsaw puzzle that is building a brand

A great brand never ceases to develop and evolve as times change. This is necessary because further growth requires you to expand your target demographic.

Prof. Moorthi: Let me give you an example that I heard from Cadbury a long time ago. They wanted to move from kids to adults, because almost all the kids were already eating it.

…and one line (they came up with) probably changed it all.

Adults want to eat chocolate, but do not want to be seen eating chocolate. Get them out of that, and they’ll start eating. Well, we’re all eating merrily now. So that’s really the point.

The market went up at least 2X or 2.5X after adults started consuming chocolate.

So you need this one line.

(And) you can collect any amount of data without arriving at that one insight.

The “line” that the professor is talking about is the chocolate-maker’s Asli Swad Zindagi Ka https://indianexpress.com/article/entertainment/opinion-entertainment/indian-ad-age-how-cadbury-adulted-chocolate-6038214/ (the real taste of life) campaign, which turned things around for Cadbury in the mid-90s. 

But brands are also like jigsaw puzzles that can never be completed; however close you come, there are always going to be more pieces that you can fit around the edges.

The commodity choice

What many of the new-age D2C brands have done—by flooding the market and doubling down on deep discounting—may have brought them some revenue. But because they haven’t really focused on building any real emotional connection with their customers, they are now viewed largely as commodities. 

And the consequences are starting to show, as this excerpt from my colleague Aakriti’s story last week makes clear:

But scaling in FMCG isn’t just about getting on the shelves—it’s about staying there. Marico’s brands, like Parachute and Saffola, have spent decades building trust and customer loyalty. Mamaearth, with its 200,000 retail outlets, isn’t there yet. Meanwhile, HUL, which directly supplies 2.5 million outlets, moves mountains of products because customers trust the brand.

For all its marketing muscle and IPO theatrics, Mamaearth misjudged the leap from a plucky D2C startup to a typical FMCG company.

Mamaearth sold investors on its FMCG dreams. Consumers had other plans, The Ken https://the-ken.com/story/mamaearth-sold-investors-on-its-fmcg-dreams-consumers-had-other-plans/

But there are still glimmers of hope with some startups; not everyone has chosen to run the rat race. Some are building their brand with patience. 

Given how fast consumer behaviour is changing, there’s the question of whether these outliers can hold true for long. But at the least, we can still hope for some good old marketing to pop up on our feeds every now and then.

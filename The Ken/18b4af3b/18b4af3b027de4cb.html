How would you describe Swiggy? As a food-delivery company or as an e-grocer, right?

But ask anyone familiar with these businesses, and they’ll say Swiggy—recently valued at under $8 billion by one of its investors—is a top-notch logistics company that happens to deliver food and groceries (besides medicines, documents, and laundry).

Once you’ve figured out how to take something from point A to point B, it doesn’t really matter what you’re taking, an executive working with a rival of Swiggy’s once told me.

There are a few variables here—from the product being delivered to the restaurant or warehouse it’s picked up from to the time it is delivered in.

But the most important one is the person carrying the product to the customer. Swiggy has over 350,000 of those working across the country. For these delivery partners, until the pandemic, the choice was binary: deliver food for Swiggy or find work with some other company.

But with three delivery services to choose from now—food, Instamart (grocery), and Genie (courier)—these partners’ decision is not an easy one. Especially when the way Swiggy pays them is not tailored to each of these services.

For instance, a customer can order food from a restaurant that is 15km away. But their groceries will usually be delivered from a warehouse not more than 4km from their house. Swiggy’s delivery partners have been complaining that their remunerations don’t reflect these differences.

Some of them recently went on strike in Mumbai before Swiggy yielded to their demands. This is not the first such instance for Swiggy in recent times.

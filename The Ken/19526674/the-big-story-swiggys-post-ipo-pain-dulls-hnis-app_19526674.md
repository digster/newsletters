Good Morning Ishan,
When Swiggy debuted on the stock market in November, my social-media feeds and friend circles were abuzz with stories of ‘wealth creation’. Suddenly, everyone had a friend, cousin, or neighbour who was about to strike it rich from cashing in the shares they were granted during a previous stint at the foodtech.
The ultra-rich had already heard the same story well before Swiggy listed. In fact, they’d been queuing up for a piece of the pie since the company’s pre-IPO secondary round months prior to listing. And yet, with Swiggy’s share price down 50% from its peak, they’ve now been left in the lurch, writes Suprita in today’s story.
The pre-IPO round is a fresh phenomenon. For one thing, there were just around 250,000 high-net-worth individuals in India five years ago. That number is now 850,000 and is expected to nearly double by 2027.
Plus, barely any high-growth, venture capital-backed tech startups had gone public back then. Now? There have been 12 of them in the last two years alone.
Incidentally, five of those are now trading below their listing price. It’s a bubble built on bandwagoning jet-setters, who bought in because of narratives about the next big thing, rather than fundamental indicators of a company’s financial health.
But when it pops, as is inevitable, do they learn their lesson?
Not really, going by Suprita’s excellent reporting. Just click the [link](https://the-ken.com/story/swiggys-post-ipo-pain-dulls-hnis-appetite-for-pre-listing-bets/) and read on to understand the pitfalls of the FOMO-fuelled pre-IPO frenzy.
Let’s say you’re Blinkit, with a billion dollars in your pocket and a yen for disrupting the Indian quick-commerce market. What do you do? Would you rather invest it all in making five-minute deliveries the normal? Or push for a more sustainable mix of delivery offerings—including a four-hour one?

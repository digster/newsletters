Tue, 23 Sep 25
Stories about the future of work and how we stay relevant through it all.
Good Morning Ishan,
You’ve heard us say this on 90,000 Hours before: the traditional 40-year career path is fading. In its place are shorter stints, and career journeys that require plenty of self-improvement and reinvention.
The Ken ran a [career longevity survey](https://the-ken.com/story/why-more-40-somethings-are-becoming-unwilling-retirees/) last year, and more than 80% of respondents between the ages of 40 and 60 said their job prospects had started to shrink drastically after working for two decades. Then, in April this year, we made career disruption the [focus of our first live event](https://the-ken.com/event/bfo/building-unique-career-lattices/), where our guests spoke about the demise of the conventional career and how to plan ahead in times of uncertainty.
Few people embody that shift as clearly as Kirthiga Reddy. When I met her this June, she [joked](https://the-ken.com/podcasts/90000-hours/meta-to-softbank-to-verix-kirthiga-reddy-on-navigating-six-careers-in-one-lifetime/) that her career looked “more like a jungle gym than a ladder”.
“I feel like I’ve lived through at least six careers in one lifetime,” she told me.
| Kirthiga (R) with Rahel (L) during the recording session |
I had originally reached out to her to try and find out how the nature of careers was changing, and how successful people had found their own way through those shifts.
One look at Kirthiga’s Linkedin profile told me that she had reinvented herself over and over again. She seems almost wired for disruption, with a sixth sense for spotting what’s next, or a knack for being in the right place at the right time. She was Facebook’s first hire in India, where she led the company as its first managing director for India and South Asia. Later, she became the first woman investing partner at Softbank. And now, she’s in the founder’s seat as CEO of Verix, a blockchain-powered credentialing platform.
In the [latest episode of 90,000 Hours](https://the-ken.com/podcasts/90000-hours/meta-to-softbank-to-verix-kirthiga-reddy-on-navigating-six-careers-in-one-lifetime/), Kirthiga joins me for the show’s first full-length interview. It’s an hour-long conversation on risk, reinvention, and culture through the lens of her career.
Here are three key takeaways.
Kirthiga’s first order of business after she became Facebook’s managing director was to hire a team. But this was in 2010. Back then, the challenge wasn’t just finding talent. It was also convincing them that Facebook was a “real company”.
“For our early hires, I would often have to do closing calls with their parents,” Kirthiga told me. “They would say their child had offers from banks and consulting companies. Why should they join a site for making friends?” She had to explain the vision behind Facebook to an audience that she just hadn’t anticipated.
When it came to the kind of people she wanted on the team, Kirthiga says there were three qualities she looked for—strategic thinking, ability to execute, and cultural fit.
Those three attributes remain her anchors even today as she builds Verix.
But now, there’s a critical addition.
Kirthiga: The big question is: are you best-in-class in your use of AI? I don’t just mean whether you are using it, but whether you are best-in-class. Each one of us, in our own function, has to strive for that. It’s a milestone we set for ourselves when hiring.
Rahel: Okay, but how would I know that I am best-in-class? How do you test for that?
Kirthiga: Any step forward matters. Even if it’s just 10, 15, or 30 minutes a day. Lean into exploring some aspect of AI. Think about the places where you get most bogged down during the day, and then look for the resources that can help you address them. Small increments compound. Yes, you can go take a course and you should, if that’s an option. But it’s just as important to focus on small, incremental changes every single day.
Start with a baseline: how are you using AI today? Then look around. There’s such a big community out there, so many people sharing best practices. Of course, there’s a lot of noise too, so the challenge is cutting through that, finding the right voices to benchmark against, and steadily moving forward.
It reminded me of what Harshil Mathur, CEO of Razorpay, [told me](https://the-ken.com/podcasts/90000-hours/at-work-its-ai-or-bust-whats-your-move/) in July when I asked him how he defines a top performer at the fintech firm.
“It’s hard to say,” he admitted, “but the truth is, a mediocre performer with AI is now better than a top performer without AI. Of course, when our top performers start using AI, they’re still ahead. But AI creates such a massive delta that you can’t fight it with hard work alone.”
I caught the Las Vegas-based Kirthiga in the middle of a whirlwind six-city tour of India, which she told me had become a monthly thing. That’s the price, I suppose, of managing a team spread across seven countries.
With colleagues scattered across time zones and geographies, who may have very different approaches to working, it becomes crucial to create a sense of shared culture.
Kirthiga: I remember my HR partner saying, “This is a very hierarchical culture. It’s impossible to build a ‘be open’ culture where people feel comfortable giving constructive feedback.”
So I worked with my L&D partner to put a specific focus on training people in constructive criticism. We even ran fast feedback sessions. It was sort of like speed dating, where you would share one thing someone was doing well and two things they could do better, just to build that muscle. And I’ll say this: given the amount of constructive criticism I used to get, we did a very good job of building a ‘be open’ culture.
American author and academic Erin Meyer wrote about this in a [Harvard Business Review](https://hbr.org/2024/07/build-a-corporate-culture-that-works) article last year:
“One of the biggest mistakes companies make when articulating their desired organisational culture is to focus on abstract absolute positives (integrity, respect, trust, and so on)… When you articulate your culture using absolute positives, it makes a statement, but it’s unlikely to drive the day-to-day decision-making of your workforce. The trick to making a desired culture come alive is to debate and articulate it using dilemmas.”
Build a Corporate Culture That Works, [Harvard Business Review](https://hbr.org/2024/07/build-a-corporate-culture-that-works)
When I try to think of companies in India that genuinely debate and live out their stated values, though, only a handful come to mind. For most, it remains more slogan than practice.
When I asked Kirthiga if she had any second thoughts about her choices, she told me, simply, that she isn’t the kind to look back with regret.
Graduating from Stanford Business School, she first went from being a director of engineering at Silicon Graphics, which she described as the “Nvidia of its time”, to an individual contributor at a mobile software startup, taking a 40% pay cut and a less fancy job title.
It felt risky, even reckless. But she wanted to be in the right industry at the right time. Soon enough, she was promoted to director of product management. The startup was acquired by Motorola.
Her lesson: optimise for learning, not titles.
A few years later, Kirthiga took a different kind of risk. She co-led an all-women management team for a special purpose acquisition company (SPAC). This type of shell corporation enables private companies to go public through a merger, instead of taking the conventional path to an IPO.
Just as she took on this responsibility in 2022, the SPAC market saw a downturn, prompting most managers to wind down their funds and leaving many investors with nothing. But her team didn’t do that. Out of a sense of responsibility, they chose to keep going. A downturn they thought would last for just four months stretched to more than three years.
When you look back at it, every chapter in Kirthiga’s story is a reminder that careers rarely follow straight lines. They zig. They zag. They stall, and then accelerate in unexpected ways.

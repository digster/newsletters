Good morning dear Ishan, I have long been an unapologetic believer in careers—“the series of jobs that a person has in a particular area of work, usually involving more responsibility as time passes.” (Oxford Dictionary https://www.oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com/definition/english/career_1)

I’ve enjoyed my career, even though there were large parts of it that were nondescript and boring. Over the years I’ve managed to hew closer to things that give me joy, serendipitously and consciously. I’ve quit jobs to write books (twice, both failures), to become a journalist, and to turn an entrepreneur (twice, failed first).

Yet, I’ve always had the implicit cushion of knowing that a career was a given. That at best, I’d been knocked down a few notches on the career ladder, but I could try and climb my way up again.

I am not sure that is true today. Having a professional career that lasts roughly 40 years—from your early twenties to your early sixties—is not a given. Having a reliable career ladder you can ascend over the years, learning, growing, and earning, is not a given. Entry-level jobs are being targeted with AI tools. Mid-level jobs are getting squeezed between flattening corporate hierarchies and waning belief in the need for managers. Finally, senior jobs are at risk from the double biases of ageism and “expensive” salaries.

Salesforce has already said it will hire no engineers in 2025 https://www.salesforceben.com/salesforce-will-hire-no-more-software-engineers-in-2025-says-marc-benioff/ because it’s got a 30% productivity boost from AI solutions.  They’re not alone. 41% of global employers https://gizmodo.com/41-of-employers-worldwide-say-theyll-reduce-staff-by-2030-due-to-ai-2000548131 estimate they will reduce their staff by 2030 thanks to AI.

What does a 20-something who enters the workforce today believe about their career? What can they “take for granted”? What are the rungs on their career ladder they can step on?

It’s all up in the air.

Which is why we’re kicking off the first Quest for 2025—the reimagining of careers. I am also hoping to turn this Quest into our very first live event in Bengaluru. If you’d like to attend it, please let us know in the second part of the survey below.

1. Lost in the pages

Hello Ishan, It’s Hari here. When was the last time you read something that captivated your imagination so much that you just had to keep turning to the next page? Something that made you forget that you had tasks to finish and places to be?

It’s been a while for me. My best guess right now is that it must have been sometime during the pandemic, when I had nothing better to do and was wrapping up George R R Martin’s A Song of Ice and Fire series (as much of it that is out there, anyway). Then, there was Harsh Realities https://www.goodreads.com/en/book/show/58056243-harsh-realities, a compilation of history and insights from the man who built consumer goods giant Marico into what it is today. I’m certain I threw away everything else when I was reading this because we were researching and prepping for an episode of First Principles with Harsh Mariwala https://the-ken.com/podcasts/first-principles/from-parachute-to-saffola-maricos-harsh-mariwala-on-building-and-branding-indias-biggest-consumer-products/, the man the book is about.

I’m looking for something to get lost in again, especially because I want to break out of the current rut I’m stuck in. I’ve picked up so many books (the count is six right now) that I’m finding it hard to finish even one of them. 

I think I might have found one answer, at least, in our first book recommendation for this week. Apart from that, we also have three wonderful nonfiction recommendations, one of which is from our former FP books editor, Anushka Mukherjee.

2. Light and movement

Hello Ishan, this is Jonathan here. Welcome to another issue of Silent Sunday. 

Regular contributors would know the drill, but if you are one of our new subscribers, this is where we publish and appreciate the wonderful pictures clicked by all the talented photographers in the First Principles community. If you, too, would like to have your photographs published, you can use this form https://theken.typeform.com/FPSilentSundays to send them to us.

We publish only the best contributions we receive, and one way you can increase your chances of being featured is by making sure they meet some basic standards. As I wrote in one of our editions last year:

Nearly three-quarters of the pictures we’ve chosen not to carry in First Principles, for instance, have been stymied by two of these checkpoints: 

1. Too much or too little exposure: The picture was either so dark or saturated with so much light that we couldn’t see much, even the subject

2. Low resolution: The picture was so highly pixelated that it was difficult to make out the subject of the picture

I understand that much of this depends on the capabilities of the device you are working with, and these days, that’s often a mobile phone that may or may not have a good camera. However, you can avoid at least some of these problems by clicking pictures when there is good but not overpowering light (such as mid-morning and evening) and avoiding very low-light situations unless you know your camera can deliver.

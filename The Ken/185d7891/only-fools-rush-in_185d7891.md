We all carry with us certain phrases spoken by our parents or grandparents, whose echoes linger long after they were first shared. One such phrase in Hindi—jaldi ka kaam shaitaan ka—has stayed with me.
Often translated as ‘haste makes waste’, it speaks of the dangers of acting hastily, which all too often results in wasted time, effort, and resources. But a deeper, more ominous meaning can be read in the literal translation of the phrase: the devil often goes about its work quickly. The phrase tells us that those with nefarious intentions often act swiftly so as not to be detected.
As I reflect on the subjects of the stories The Ken published this week, the adage comes to mind. Many of the companies we reported on seem to be in a wild rush, driven by various (not necessarily nefarious) motives.
On Monday, our editor, Seema Singh, wrote about why India—and Serum Institute—is in a hurry to launch an adult TB vaccine. Prime Minister Narendra Modi's target of eliminating TB by 2025 has sent the health ministry scrambling. Serum is asking for an emergency-use nod for its rBCG vaccine, even though there is no data available to support the efficacy of rBCG in reducing new infections, protecting against TB, or decreasing transmission. The
[story](https://the-ken.com/story/why-india-and-serum-institute-is-in-a-hurry-for-an-adult-tb-vaccine/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter) also tells us about the cancellation of a tender to procure TrueNat—a point-of-care molecular-diagnostic technology—even as Serum's investee, Mylab Discovery Solutions, is making a move to enter the TB-diagnostics market.
Rounak, on Tuesday,
[reported](https://the-ken.com/story/for-startups-working-the-7b-debt-collection-market-out-its-still-a-leg-day/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter) on the world of debt collection, on which banks spend more than $7 billion a year. This murky space is made murkier by the lenders' anxiety to recover loans through any means possible. The Reserve Bank of India's regulations to curb such practices and the pandemic-induced lockdowns have aided the emergence of "collection-tech" startups trying to provide an alternative to the door-to-door approach.
Our Wednesday
[story](https://the-ken.com/story/us1-2b-good-glamms-acquisition-spree-is-a-tale-of-user-growth-and-retention-challenge/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter) was about The Good Glamm Group, a $1.2 billion conglomerate that has also been in a rush. Over the last two years, it has acquired a dozen beauty, media, and content brands. As Aayush writes, while it has managed to attract new users for its acquirees, the retention of these users will be a key challenge for the brands' future growth within the group.
Meanwhile, Lentra, a four-year-old startup that provides software services to banks and non-banking financial companies, is setting its sights on Southeast Asia. Rahul
[explains](https://the-ken.com/story/indias-new-financial-data-sharing-system-has-us400m-fintech-lentra-turning-towards-southeast-asia/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter) that this is not only about interest in new geographies and the opportunities they offer, but also due to a threat posed by a regulatory shift in the way India handles financial data.

[story](https://the-ken.com/story/hcl-backed-guvis-growth-story-is-a-silver-lining-in-the-edtech-gloom/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter) for
The Ken. She writes that though 2022 was all doom and gloom for the edtech sector, GUVI and Vidyakul attracted investors with courses in Indian languages. While India's policy environment has been working in their favour, they still have to contend with slim margins.
So this is what we were up to this week. I highly recommend that you read at least some of these stories, but do take your time and don't be in a rush.

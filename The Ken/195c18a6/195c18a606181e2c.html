Good morning, Ishan. Hello again from sunny, hot, and gridlocked Bengaluru. After the relatively pleasant traffic last week on account of the extended Holi break, this week has been terrible. 

But I’ve learned to make my peace with it by accepting that quality of life in any Indian city is about picking and optimising between three factors—weather, people, and infrastructure. But you can only pick two at any one time!

Speaking of weather, remember I told you about how many of the pink Tabebuia trees near our office had started flowering too early, in February itself? Well, they’ve shed all their flowers and are now doing a second round of flowering!

Back at home, here’s Gabru one morning this week, watching out for an impending cat invasion of our backyard.

View in Browser

View in Browser

Over the years, he’s learned to associate the cacophonic screeching of squirrels from the neighbouring Peepul tree as a warning of a prowling predator, which almost always is a cat (but a few times a year, it could be monkeys visiting us from the adjoining forested army camp).

And speaking of dogs, remember Bribo? Well, the little champ is doing okay, all things considered. He’s developed this limp-shuffle style of walking and running, which is simultaneously heart-breaking and cute. I cannot explain it.

We’ve developed this daily evening feeding routine where he rolls over and gets belly rubs and pets from me for the first few minutes, all the while making those whining and whimpering sounds that I only recently understood as excitement or pleasure. Then, I check his rear paw (the one that was broken) for any signs of rawness. Occasionally, I spray it with a spray-on aerosol bandage (Aluspray, if you’re curious) while his whimpers change tone from pleasure to mild irritation or pain.

That done, it’s food time. He polishes off the food in a jiffy, curled up tail wagging while he’s eating. Then, I try my best to get him to drink some water. It’s the most frustrating thing!

Despite it being summer, most days, the little bugger just refuses to drink water that I pour for him. I’ve tried various tricks, from pouring a few drops down his throat to get him started to pouring a long spout of water in front of him to make it look appealing. Unfortunately, my success rate in getting him to drink is only around 40% of the time. 

Food and water done, it’s time for one final round of pre-bedtime petting. Often, he’ll hold on to me with one paw as I pet him, eyes locked into mine (I’m telling you, this is why we humans are powerless against dogs!). If I stop too early, he won’t let me go.

Walking back home after this is one of the most content moments of my day. And if it’s one of those days where he’s had enough water to drink too, I just feel so much happier.

Do I do it for Bribo or do I do it for myself? Does it matter?

My next project with him is to try and give him a proper bath (guessing he’s never had one in his life). If you have any tips on how to do that with a trusting streetie, please email me. I’d love to know.

2. Detours

Hello Ishan, it’s Hari here with this week’s books section. 

One of our community recommendations this week came as a bit of a surprise to me, in the sense that I was taken aback we hadn’t already featured it in our lists. From subscriber Maya, Ed Catmull’s Creativity Inc is exactly the kind of book we are always on the lookout for at First Principles. 

Maya writes: “It’s a must-read for anyone who dabbles in team management, especially where a lot of creativity and idea generation is involved. It’s more about how to create an environment where creativity can thrive.”

View in Browser

The book also took me down a bit of a detour, because Ed Catmull is one of the co-founders of Pixar Animation Studios, which was eventually acquired by the Walt Disney Company. And whenever I think of Disney these days, my mind circles back to a research and development unit in the company that has played a key role in shaping its sprawling theme parks, resorts, and cruises.

The people who work in this department are called “imagineers” and I recently learned about them while looking up a book called Walt Disney Imagineering.

View in Browser

I haven’t managed to lay my hands on a copy yet, but from the little I managed to dig out, the book is apparently filled with beautiful ideas and concepts for theme parks and rides that I’ve always wanted to experience. Judging by the reviews, it’s probably more of a fun-to-look-at-than-read book, but why can’t some books be just that? 

Lastly, we also have a sci-fi listing from subscriber Pranjal, who recommends Ursula K Le Guin’s The Left Hand of Darkness.

“After many years, I made it a point to read a few sci-fi books this year, especially those with a philosophical tilt. For this week, I chose the book, and from it, what I gained is patience and an open mind, which may sometimes open doors which we probably may not even know exist. It is hard to show and act on virtue. At extremes, biological self-limits and cultural conditioning have power over us, though they are inconspicuous and forceful, and we make choices we don’t explicitly think we would. Fear is natural and present. The force can condition the fear to hate, or with deftness, the fear can be subsumed into love.”

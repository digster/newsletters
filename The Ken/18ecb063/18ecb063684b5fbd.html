Despite regulatory plaudits and sustained profitability, the country’s renewables poster child, ACEN, risks losing a pivotal stock market edge amid a clean energy slump

Good Morning Ishan,

Eid Mubarak! We don’t have a story from India today on account of the festival, but we’ve got you covered with a feature from the Philippines (and a survey!).

Given the pace of sociological change, technological disruption, and the emergence of AI, careers are not what they used to be. Can people bank on having 40-50 years of work life anymore? We’d like to know what you think. Take our survey here.

“Climate risk is investment risk,” Larry Fink, CEO of Blackrock, the world’s largest asset manager, wrote to corporate leaders in 2020. This was a time when everything sustainable was hot. 

So it was no surprise when, the following year, the Philippines’ largest renewables company was included in the country’s benchmark index of 30 stocks.

But that was a different era for ACEN, whose shares are down 70% from their peak. This is not a reflection of its performance, though. ACEN, part of diversified conglomerate Ayala, increased its installed capacity by a fifth to 4.8 gigawatts over the last 15 months. And its profits are up, too.

“We do believe we have a very good story to tell. It’s just a pity that the market doesn’t seem to recognise that,” ACEN’s chief financial officer and chief strategy officer, Jonathan Back, told The Ken.

Maybe ACEN has to get better at telling stories because, as Katrina argues, it’s at risk of losing its blue-chip-stock status.

Renewables, EVs––the climate-tech slump is everywhere

ACEN is not alone among clean-energy companies in having to deal with unenthused investors. Renew Energy, which is India’s second-largest renewables company, has been languishing on the Nasdaq.

You can find such examples throughout the world as mutual funds and other moneybags have once again fallen in love with coal and oil.

They are shunning the other big part of climate tech, too: electric vehicles. Tesla is now worth US$550 billion––more than halving since 2021. The combined market value of six smaller EV makers, including Rivian and Lucid Motors, is now a mere US$65 billion, compared with US$400 billion in 2021, according to The Economist.

Tepid demand for battery-powered cars is one thing, but the European Union believes China is making life hard for EV companies from elsewhere with its handsome subsidies. In September 2023, the EU Commission launched a probe to look into this.

Earlier this week, China’s commerce minister slammed the investigation and said China’s advances in EVs were thanks to “continuous technological innovation”, Bloomberg reported.

For good measure, he added: “Trade protectionism under the guise of ‘de-risking’ runs counter to the objectives of promoting green transformation. The EU should not champion green development while simultaneously wielding the ‘big stick’ of trade protectionism.”

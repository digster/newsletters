At 20-something, Gaurav Munjal wanted to build a YouTube for education. At 30, he is building a Netflix for education.
Munjal’s five-year-old Unacademy has been on a tear like many in the edtech segment. The startup hit $100 million revenue rate, raised $150 million from SoftBank in September, got valued at $1.45 billion, and has been building stuff like crazy.
Building comes easy to Munjal. It shows in how he has built a marketplace of education content, attracting educators, and enabling them to build their own content. It’s this product-led thinking that has helped Unacademy scale much quicker and faster than others without burning tons of VC money.
And with Netflix as its north star, Unacademy has legitimately focussed on product and user experience—all classes are available to the user as long as she is a subscriber. But to arrive at this model, Munjal did something which few founders would have the courage to do. It was the mother of all pivots—tear down a successful, diversified model for a bundled, single slab model.
“To be honest, it was a scary time for us. We had to go tell our investors that we are pivoting and there will be no revenue,” Munjal tells Sumanth in today’s The Interview. People loved it and voted with their wallets. Munjal got bolder from thereon. Now, he’s readying to take the edtech battle to the fiercest ground, K-12, and face the biggest competitor, Byju’s.
There’s a certain tenacity in Munjal. Five years ago, he and his co-founders met more than 60 angel investors over 14 days, carpet bombing every person they knew. And boy, how he clinched commitments!
In this freewheeling interview, Sumanth has done what he does best—break things down to the smallest digestible factor. Munjal has been a sport, and you’ll come away knowing so much about, well, edtech and entrepreneurship.

Let me introduce you right away to the fascinating acronym that doesn’t just threaten India’s most successful edtech, Byju’s, but many of its fintech lenders too: First Loss Default Guarantee, or FLDG.
 
To understand FLDG, we must understand how Byju’s (and many of its Indian peers) became so successful at driving sales in a country where many of its customers are struggling to make ends meet.
 
Loans.
 
That’s right. An expensive two-year edtech course costing, say, Rs 50,000 ($640), is sold to a customer who obviously cannot afford to purchase it outright by facilitating a loan from a third-party lender. Customers repay a small amount of the loan each month, say, Rs 2,000 ($26), while their kids continue to use Byju’s courses.
 
So far so good.
 
Except, households that are barely making ends meet will often be unable to repay their loans. Even those taken for their kid’s futures.
 
Normally, lenders will treat such loans as very risky and tighten approval criteria or raise interest rates. Unless, of course, the business guarantees to repay some or all of the loan in case its customers default. That’s called the first loss default guarantee, or FLDG.
 
Think of FLDGs as largely invisible strings running through the lending ecosystem. They make riskier loans appear safer. They mostly don’t show up to banking regulators. They encourage lenders to load up more risks on their balance sheets.
 
It’s exactly for these reasons India’s central bank, the RBI, wants to clamp down on FLDGs. If that happens, a *lot* of edtech loans might start to unravel, because they’re backed by FLDGs. That’s risk #1 for Byju’s.
 
But that’s not even part of today’s explosive story by Jaspreet, Olina, and Arundhati. Instead, it’s the fact that Deloitte, the longtime auditor of Byju’s, has not yet signed off on its financial statements for the year ended March 2021.
 
That’s right. It’s been 15 months now. Deloitte’s delay in signing off has triggered panic amongst the edtech’s investors and rival edtech leaders.
 
When The Ken sent Deloitte a list of questions for today’s story, the company declined to respond. But multiple sources told The Ken that at the root of Deloitte’s reticence lies in the edtech’s increasingly complex revenue recognition practices.
 
For instance, that sale of the Rs 50,000-course I mentioned earlier. How should it be recognised as revenue? Most of it upfront? Monthly, over the two-year duration of its usage? A mix of both?
 
This is a critical moment for Byju’s and Indian edtech. If Deloitte doesn’t sign off on Byju’s financials, it’s going to severely impact the company’s plans to go public. And that will set off the dominoes for an entire sector that’s banking on that grand “exit” to provide the fuel and hope for everyone else.

There is a rise in discreet reference checks, and recruiters are leaning on them increasingly often

Good Evening Ishan,

It could happen that next Tuesday afternoon, while you sip a coffee at your desk, you receive a text—“Did you work closely with so-and-so at your last job? If so, there’s a founder who would like to talk to you, in the strictest confidence.”  

 

You take the call, and the founder tells you that they’re interviewing your former colleague for a critical role at their startup. So could you please help them feel more confident in their decision by answering a few specific questions? 

 

You really liked working with the candidate, so you say yes, and the questions start flowing: how do they respond to criticism? How do they deal with deadlines? How do they deal with motherhood? Are there any specific anecdotes you can share?

You answer honestly and with great fondness for your friend; the enthusiasm in your voice is unmistakable. A few weeks later, you see that your former colleague has been hired! You feel happy for your friend. You feel good about yourself. The world is a beautiful place. 

 

But what if it all turned out differently? 

 

What if your answers ended up influencing the startup to reject the candidate? What if you altered the course of her entire career, and for the worse? 

 

Maybe the best way is to just avoid such calls; say you’re busy or on holiday or something. But that means you miss the chance to engage meaningfully with an interesting founder and build your own social capital. And the founder will infer your evasiveness as a signal anyway—he’s heard it before, he knows what this means.

Maybe you get on the call, but are vague with the answers—you didn’t know the candidate that well, it was a long time ago, you don’t remember… But the founder is paying attention to everything you say and do. And all of your actions are open to interpretation—signals that could make or break a person’s career. 

 

So what options do you really have? What should you do when you get that text?

I don’t have answers. But I will say that such predicaments are only likely to become more frequent. If you work, or have worked, in the startup ecosystem, it’s only a matter of time before a CXO, founder, or recruiter calls you about a former colleague. 


Reference checks are now getting deeper and more discreet because hiring has changed, because of the arbitrage of social capital at play, and because these networks are only getting stronger. 

That’s what this week’s episode of Cost to Company is about: why discreet reference checks are becoming more frequent than ever in the hiring process.

Good Morning Ishan,
 
A glance at the cover of Seth Godin’s All Marketers Are Liars—where the last two words are scratched out and overlaid with “Tell Stories”—would explain why my profession has the ruinous reputation it does. After all, the book basically explains how people can be made to believe that ‘wine tastes better in a $20 glass than a $1 glass’, or that expensive shoes, seen on celebrities, can make them fitter than some no-names.
 
If I told you I got this from reading the entire book, I’d be lying. That’d probably be for the best, though—if the book is to be believed.
 
My problem with Godin’s argument, in this instance, is the equation of telling stories with lying. In all other instances, it’s that his 200-page books tend to rephrase the same five points over and over, until my watery eyes pine for the few hundred rupees I’ve spent on them.
 
This time, I knew to directly check the four-minute summary for a comprehensive overview—there were no lies involved. On the other hand, I’ve fallen for and bought some of Godin’s books in the past—please write to me if you’d like them—but I’m never buying another one.
 
Lies don’t work, especially in the long run. Today, with public communication at people’s fingertips, they’re even harder to go undiscovered. My life, I realised, would be made much easier by telling stories that don’t need lying.
 
The hard part, then, is delivering on the promise.
 
Earlier this week, I wrote about why a birds’-eye view of India and Southeast Asia is essential to understanding how Asia, as a region, is building the future. This story by Nadine Freischlad and Sharath Ravishankar was the perfect example. A visual depiction of how Indonesia’s education system coped with a lost year of schooling, its insights into the intersection of edtech and policy—for a country with a school-going population only smaller than China and India—are relevant for the entire region.
 
Our next story was about the curious, unwritten lies sold to Prabhat Dairy’s minority shareholders. After agreeing to a $234 million sale to French dairy major Lactalis in January 2019, public shareholders are yet to see a single rupee from the sale. Suspicions of fraud and regulatory interventions make for a fascinating tale of corporate intrigue in this story.
 
On Wednesday, Arundhati followed up her revelatory story from January about the proliferation of fraudulent Chinese loan apps in India. In this one, she writes about just how they do it: by cobbling together an app, not unlike the way I make my sorry omelettes, but using ‘tech ingredients’ bought from an unregulated market.
 
On Thursday, we wrote about Indian audio darling boAt, which has the fifth-highest market share in the global wearables market. It got there through a clever combination of marketing and pricing, but from hereon in, its path to success may be paved differently, especially if it intends to pursue its IPO dreams.
 
We closed the week with a free story by Maitri and Pratap about the government’s telemedicine platform eSanjeevani. In just under a year, it has clocked in over three million consultations, out-performing well-funded, private players like Practo and DocPrime. 
 
In this week’s The Nutgraf, Praveen explained the regulatory climate of e-commerce in India, in light of Reliance’s appeal about foreign players circumventing the spirit of the law. Lessons from Roman history, anyone?
 
Our daily newsletter, Beyond the First Order had a lot of exciting pieces this week as well. Jon, whose kids love the gaming company Roblox, explored what could be the next growth lever for the company; Olina said that while edtech companies were given a booster shot due to the pandemic, their employees may not be too happy; Nithin delved into why ITC, the cigarette-hotel-FMCG conglomerate, has split the investment community into two sides. He also explained what the finance industry's greed had to do with the collapse of Greensill Capital. To cap off the week, Arundhati wrote that Netflix could learn from Microsoft about piracy. The best part about telling stories that don’t require lying is that the difficult part—of delivering on those promises—falls to other people. They’re all you need to believe me when I say the future is at SEA.

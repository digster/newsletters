Good Morning Ishan,

A glance at the cover of Seth Godin’s All Marketers Are Liars—where the last two words are scratched out and overlaid with “Tell Stories”—would explain why my profession has the ruinous reputation it does. After all, the book basically explains how people can be made to believe that ‘wine tastes better in a $20 glass than a $1 glass’, or that expensive shoes, seen on celebrities, can make them fitter than some no-names.

If I told you I got this from reading the entire book, I’d be lying. That’d probably be for the best, though—if the book is to be believed.

My problem with Godin’s argument, in this instance, is the equation of telling stories with lying. In all other instances, it’s that his 200-page books tend to rephrase the same five points over and over, until my watery eyes pine for the few hundred rupees I’ve spent on them.

This time, I knew to directly check the four-minute summary for a comprehensive overview—there were no lies involved. On the other hand, I’ve fallen for and bought some of Godin’s books in the past—please write to me if you’d like them—but I’m never buying another one.

Lies don’t work, especially in the long run. Today, with public communication at people’s fingertips, they’re even harder to go undiscovered. My life, I realised, would be made much easier by telling stories that don’t need lying.

The hard part, then, is delivering on the promise.

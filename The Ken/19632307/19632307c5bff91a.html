Cars have been described as computers on wheels for years, and that’s truer today than it ever was. It isn’t uncommon for one to roll off an assembly line with as many as 3,000 semiconductors within its body. Those chips make many advanced features possible, such as automatic parking, AR head-up display, driving assistance, and obstacle detection.

Anyone who’s in the market for a brand-new electric vehicle expects their car to do all of that and more. So, when Mahindra started showcasing what two of their newest electric SUVs could do, pre-orders started rolling in.

In mid-February, when Mahindra officially opened bookings for its XEV 9e and BE 6 electric SUVs, buyers had already written cheques worth Rs 8,500 crore. Deliveries were supposed to start a month later.

But most buyers still haven’t received their vehicles, and delivery dates keep getting pushed back over and over again; all the while, Mahindra’s dealerships have asked its customers to pay for their vehicles in full.

Those dealers say it’s Mahindra’s fault, and Mahindra points its finger at the dealers. The real reason appears to be issues related to the vehicles’ software.

For today’s story, Suprita spoke with 12 people who have paid for the Mahindra electric SUVs in question. Nine still haven’t received their vehicles despite promised deliveries, and the other three have encountered many software issues. The hiccups show us how easy it is for an automaker to overpromise while trying to stay competitive. Read Suprita’s story to find out more.

Beyond the First Order: Building Unique Career Lattices

The 40-year career that professionals took for granted as they entered their first job has been crumbling for many years. But today, even a 10-year career arc is getting hard to predict and plan for. The default “up-or-out” career ladder is gradually disappearing as roles get eliminated, hierarchies get flattened, work gets automated with AI. 

How do professionals, young or old, plan their careers in such an environment? What are the new “constants” they can make their plans around? How do they balance professional growth, stability, and career satisfaction?

Could viewing and planning careers as three-dimensional lattices be a better way?

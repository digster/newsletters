If you've lived in India, odds are you've used a Godrej Consumer Products Ltd (GCPL) brand at some point. Perhaps it was the mosquito repellent Good Knight, or a bar of soap from Cinthol, or maybe you had your hair styled at BBlunt, the chain of salons the company has invested in.
 
If you've lived outside India, chances are you've probably come across a GCPL brand, possibly even used one, but maybe you just didn't realise it.
The company's portfolio abroad is just as, if not more, sprawling. Babycare products in Indonesia under the brand Mitu, hair extensions in Africa through the brand Darling, hand sanitisers in the US through Purest Hygiene, and cosmetics through its Latin American brand Roby. It's a vast, even if often invisible, empire.
 
That empire, though, has seen better days. Over the last five years, its growth has slowed to a crawl, especially when compared with the five years prior. Over the past year, both revenue and profitability actually declined.
 
The almost 20-year-old company, the crown jewel of the 123-year-old Godrej Group, is in desperate need of a turnaround. Now, after 11 years and three professional managing directors, the family that owns GCPL is taking the wheel again. In July, Nisaba Godrej, the daughter of group chairman Adi Godrej, stepped in as managing director.
 
Taking the reins in a pandemic is an unenviable job, but there are winds blowing in her favour. For instance, the Covid-19 pandemic has turned the health and hygiene category—something GCPL has an outsized dependence on—into a literal matter of life and death. Capitalising on this will be important, but, as Seetharaman writes in today's piece, it is hardly a silver bullet.

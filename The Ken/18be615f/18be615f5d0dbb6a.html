I. Regret Minimisation

Wednesdays are “Deep Work” WFH days at The Ken. People are allowed to reject all meetings and calls so they can spend quality, undisturbed time on things they consider important. Without the pressure of the morning office commute, those are the days I allow myself a bit of time to linger around post a morning workout, reading the day’s newspapers a bit more slowly. 

 

Which is why I’m caught by surprise when my watch alerts me to a Zoom call with Srikanth Velamakanni, the co-founder and CEO of Fractal Analytics, which is due in five minutes. I do not have to change from my workout clothes. 

 
On the other end of the line, Srikanth is dressed in a crisp white shirt and already on the call from a standing desk at his sprawling office in Mumbai, with sweeping and beautiful views of the Aarey forest. 
 

I feel under-dressed, and hence (even though it makes no sense), under-prepared. Perhaps it’s because I’ve known Srikanth to always be the opposite—well-prepared.

 

Thankfully, he is also very polite (almost to a fault) and disarming, so my awkwardness quickly disappears as we start talking mental models while drinking our respective coffees. 

 

I ask Srikanth if he has a favourite First Principle he relies upon most often when faced with major decisions. He does. “My favourite mental model is that of regret minimisation. Because just asking this question, what will you regret more, just really helps to clarify. What will you do when you look back one year out? Two years out? 10 years out? What will you regret more?”

 

Amazon founder Jeff Bezos has sworn by this concept too. 

 

But on the face of it, “regret” is a curious emotion to base our decision making upon. Because it implies things went wrong or mistakes were made. They’re the opposite of what we associate leadership and decision making with. 

 

I ask Srikanth why regret is key to decision making, even if it is minimising a hypothetical and future one. Why bring emotions into decision making?  

 

His answer isn’t what you’d expect from the CEO of an organisation built around analytics, AI, and mathematical models. 

 

“I think all decision making is emotional. And rationality is only a slave to emotion. So, if your emotion says I should go there, then your rationality is basically a very intelligent slave, which says, okay, now that you have decided this, let me give you a very beautiful argument for why that makes sense. That's how the human brain really works. One of our principles or philosophies is that 99.9% of all decisions are emotional. And rationality is a way to explain what emotion is already telling you,” Srikanth says.

 

But wait, isn’t that the opposite of what we’re taught growing up? Don’t trust your emotions, instead go for rationality.

 

I ask Srikanth how he would advise someone to effectively use emotions in decision making.

 

“The first thing I would say is that when emotions are controlling you, or when you're not able to control your emotions, then it's not the right time to make decisions. Because emotions can lead you to the worst decisions possible when you are not in control. That is different in my opinion from saying that all decisions are emotional. Emotions are giving us a very important signal, which we must understand. But it's not the same as saying that you will let the signal run amok,” he explains. 

 

I steer our interesting conversation back to Srikanth’s favourite mental model of regret minimisation. Have there been recent decisions he’s made that were guided by the principle?

 

“We have been in the throes of a large acquisition. So when we were thinking about this very large acquisition, we were also thinking about what we would regret more. Not buying this company? Yeah, certainly, we would regret it, in the sense that it was a huge opportunity and together we could be much larger and better. But then, there are chances that we will, you know, screw up the decision, and therefore bring the whole house down. And that could be catastrophic. So, looking at that decision, we said, okay, let's take a pause. We are not yet big and strong enough to actually absorb this acquisition. I think this has been the case for at least two other acquisitions that we have come close to,” answers Srikanth.

II. Books
Of Boys and Men by Richard V Reeves—from Rohin Dharmakumar

As the father to a (now teenage) boy, you might say I have a “double vested interest” in reading and learning about boys. But it’s not easy to talk about the needs and voices of boys in a world where we all default to sort of a zero-sum gender equality prism. If we’re talking about boys, it must be coming at the cost of not talking enough about girls. Or worse, why would we want to pay special attention to the gender that has everything going for them? Down with male apologists, right?

 

Wrong.  

 

And Richard Reeves’ masterful book, “Of Boys and Men: Why the Modern Male Is Struggling, Why It Matters, and What to Do About It”, explains why. 

 

Though referencing realities largely in the US, Reeves is able to lay out how boys and men are being ignored even as we go through tremendous social and economic upheavals ranging across schools, homes, and workplaces. In schools, boys are not just doing worse with each passing year, they continue to fall behind girls (who, in contrast, do better each year). At work, they face the double whammy of losing jobs to automation, migration, or globalisation, and are unable to adjust to newer careers. And finally, in homes, as dads, they struggle to find their modern identity and relevance in the face of long-held gender norms falling by the wayside. 

 

It isn’t Reeves’ point that we need to somehow set the clock back for boys and men. Or that they need special treatment of any kind. 

 

Instead, he says that we cannot afford to ignore the changing realities of half the world’s population. And that it's very much possible to help boys and men become better in schools, at work, and in homes, without girls and women having to give up any of their hard-won rights. 

 

That Reeves is an economist and researcher at the Brookings Institution is clear from the copious amounts of endnotes and references in the book. But the amazing part is that none of that makes the book dreary or its points less impactful. Truly a must-read, regardless of your gender. 

 

Perhaps the best expression of gender equity that doesn’t ignore boys and men I have seen is from Rohini Nilekani Philanthropies and their “Laayak” field of work.
“In India, the masculine identity is inextricably tied to traditional male breadwinner models and the ability to espouse power through social and economic roles in society.
 
As global shifts in the nature of work take root in India as well, we worry that these trends will exacerbate gender-based conflict, leaving men more frustrated and women with worse choices. Accelerating meaningful engagement with men and boys is a critical pathway to gender equality and equity.
 
There’s a need to create spaces for young men and boys to speak, to share and to grow. A critical pathway to gender equality and equity is to invest in programs that accelerate meaningful engagements with men and boys.”

One From Many by Dee Hock—from Nipun Jasuja
A book I absolutely adore is One From Many by Dee Hock, the founder of Visa. It’s meditative and it’s a thing of beauty. Dee Hock never intended Visa to become a duopoly, which makes the book that much more revealing.
 
I work in the fintech industry and so much is said about Visa and Mastercard, yet so little is understood about these companies. On the plus side, when people talk about Visa or Mastercard, it's primarily in reference to the elusive concept of network effects—something that so many unicorns and fintech darlings would do anything to have. On the not-so-plus side, you see constant digs at the high interchange that these duopolists charge and all the negative that brings to the merchants. Both the plus-es and the minus-es don't capture the essence and the miracle of the wonderful innovation that Dee Hock pioneered. He thought of value exchange as effectively an exchange of 1s and 0s. Through a lot of grit and tenacity, he ended up creating a global system of value exchange. Just think about it—50+ years ago, he created a payments system which worked pretty much everywhere across the world. It's mind-blowing. To add to that, if Dee Hock had his way, Visa/MasterCard wouldn't be an oligopoly today, and there would be many, many more card networks. Dee Hock passed away last year, but his legacy will linger on for generations to come, even as UPI and other equivalent systems along the world begin to eat into the core business of card networks. This book gave me an appreciation of Visa, and the innovation it unleashed, and that is the main reason I love it so much. 
 
I also love the way it's written. The book is part memoir and part philosophy. There are roughly sequential chapters chronicling the birth of the global network that ultimately became Visa. And interspersed between these chapters are Dee Hock's inner musings, structured as conversations between himself and his "old monkey mind". It gives you a sneak peek inside a beautiful and restless brain. I am not the kind of person who writes in the margins of a book very often, but this is one of my most underlined and notes-laden book. From his thoughts on leadership, to management, to structures of an organisation, to how information and money flows, everything is quotable!
 
It introduced me to this notion of a chaordic organisation. Chaordic is a word that Dee Hock popularized and it's a combination of chaos + order. He expresses his wonderment and puzzle throughout the book on why organisations (be it political, social, corporate, etc.) are largely command and control in nature, and so bad at functioning properly. And in the book, through talking about Visa and the musings of his monkey mind, he delves into a better way of organising and running companies. It's a treat to be cherished.

Uncommon Service by Frances Frei & Anne Morriss—from Rohin Dharmakumar

I read this book after Srikanth Iyer, the founder and CEO of Home Lane, told me in episode 14 of the First Principles podcast that it changed the way he built his company. I will say I enjoyed it, because it busts some commonly held myths about customer service. Namely, that you should try and satisfy customers all the time. 

 

Instead, the book’s core idea is that you must explicitly choose to be average (or even bad) at some things customers want, in order to be great at others. That doesn’t come easily. I’ll leave you with a few snippets from the book.
"Our message begins simply enough: you can’t be good at everything. In services, trying to do it all brilliantly will lead almost inevitably to mediocrity. Excellence requires sacrifice. To deliver great service on the dimensions that your customers value most, you must underperform on dimensions they value less. This means you must have the stomach to do some things badly.”
 
[…]
 
“People working within the limits of the physical product space tend to have an easier time with the concept of tradeoffs. For example, a widespread rule in the construction business states that a customer can expect two of the following three things: speed, quality, and price. You can get good quality built quickly, but it will cost you. You can get something built fast and cheap, but the quality will suffer. Or you can get good quality for a competitive price, but the wait will be painful. This is also known as the impossible triangle. It’s considered irrational to ask for all three.
 
Our goal is to bring this same kind of intuition to service managers. Service managers often behave as if the laws of physics don’t apply to them. Without blushing, they make it their goal to be the lowest-cost, highest-quality, fastest-moving service providers. Many managers also believe that they’re inspiring the troops by demanding it all. In our experience, the opposite often occurs. Cynicism can build quickly among frontline employees who are confronting the trade-offs every day, while their managers continue to deny reality.”

IV. Habits

From Karthik Shankar:
A character trait I try to cultivate is to take something up seriously only if I can do it at least four times a week.
 
For instance, I am learning to sing. I realised that I have struggled in the past to stick to two classes a week and sustain my interest. Instead, along with the two interactive sessions, I try to join as a listener online and follow along. What this does is that it reduces gaps between classes and also ensures continuity.
 
Similarly, at work, I try to ensure that I do the most important activity, school visits in my case, at least four times a week. Now, this gives me enough of a buffer to not feel overburdened and enough space for growth. Therefore, if I meet my target, I keep the fifth day to do a fun session at a school, usually with content I have developed.
Since the core of our work in education is character-building, the underlying framework we use is Thoughts -> Action -> Habit -> Character.
 
When I start doing something, I ask myself if I can do it at least four times a week. If yes, I take it up. Else, I don't pursue it. I swim in the pool in the apartment complex. Sometimes, it takes effort to overcome the inertia to go through the steps until I am in the pool, especially on a busy day or when I haven't slept well. So, I ask myself how many times I have been to the pool and then use that as a trigger to overcome laziness.
And I'm sending this email also because I've decided to write at least four times a week—in whatever form... since I have been invited to contribute a book chapter, but am struggling to get into the flow!

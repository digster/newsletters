“If money grew on trees, farmers would be justly rewarded.” This was the resounding thought while I worked on a story on the agricultural finance situation in the Philippines back in January.

When I read Isa’s story today on the recent government move to “help” farmers, the resounding notion returned.

It revolves around the Philippines’ recent agricultural-debt forgiveness and how it has been heralded as a beacon of hope for struggling farmers.

Isa argues that the agricultural-loan condonation mirrors medicine’s use of palliatives to address symptoms rather than root causes. The Philippine government has passed the New Agrarian Emancipation Act, a remedy that promises to forgive a staggering US$1 billion in agricultural debt.

Many farmers welcome the relief, but the story reveals a disheartening truth: the seemingly generous debt forgiveness may not be as farmer-friendly as it initially appears. A banking system that seldom lends to farmers, precarious land distribution, and a flawed trading system that shaves profits away from growers—all remain ignored. Banks don’t benefit from this arrangement either, say experts.

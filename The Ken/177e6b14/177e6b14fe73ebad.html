Good Morning Ishan,
 
Yuval Noah Harari’s book Sapiens: A Brief History of Humankind is an interesting piece of literature. It does exactly what its name suggests—it gives us a brief overview of how we came about and how we went on to conquer the world.
 
The book is at its most fascinating, not when it talks about the industrial revolution or globalisation, but way earlier. Like way earlier, around 30,000 years earlier. Because, Harari says, that’s when a switch flipped.
 
Homo sapiens, bang in the middle of the food chain till then, the species that travelled and hunted in packs, whose lives could be upended by a rogue tiger at any moment, began to change the way they communicated. Their language evolved from “There’s a tiger” to “There’s a tiger that attacks every week at sundown, here’s how to evade it.”
 
We began telling stories.
 
What I love about journalism is that we are at the forefront of it all. We make a living out of telling stories. Journalism, after all, is the first draft of history. And this week, we have some fascinating stories about companies that are trying to shape the history of humans as well as technology.
 
On Monday, Olina shined a light on the divergent roads that two mobility companies with similar ambitions are taking in the post-pandemic world. For Bounce, it is all about the asset—the scooter, while Rapido is building an asset-lite mobility platform.
 
Arundhati, on Tuesday, wrote about the fascinating world of online rummy. Around 55 million Indians spent $5.5 billion playing the game online this year. And rummy companies, with their 10-12% commission rates, are seeing windfall after windfall, especially after the pandemic pushed many with time on their hands into playing the game. The addictive nature of the game is costing more than just money for some—their very lives—but rules about online rummy are still in the grey area.
 
Another place that’s seen a similar influx of money and people is the stock market. On Wednesday, Anand wrote about Happiest Minds, a mid-tier IT company that’s currently a stock market darling. He argues that the company’s stock price is inflated due to incorrect comparisons with bigger companies, competitive pressure, and that it doesn’t take into account future rise in costs. The thing about bubbles is that it is easy to spot one. More difficult is answering the question of when they will burst.
 
On Thursday, Seetharaman wrote about the ever-increasing Indian presence of 21st-century empire Amazon. After its battle with Flipkart in e-commerce, Amazon India has spread its tentacles into many spheres of people’s lives. Its presence can be felt on your screens, hardware, your local brick and mortar stores, and a giant portion of the internet. And this expansion is being shaped by seemingly everyone from well-funded startups to Reliance Industries to independent merchants to regulators.
 
On Friday, Bhumika narrated a superb story about MX Player and how it beat the likes of Hotstar and Netflix in the matter of grabbing eyeballs. Explicit content aside, MX Player strategically targeted the young crowd from tier-2, and -3 cities and towns searching for edgy, local fare they wouldn’t get anywhere else. It's our free story of the week, so do share it widely!
 
Praveen closed out the week’s fare with a follow-up to a graphic story we did back in October, titled ‘India doesn’t need better media’. So far, Indian media had actively demanded, encouraged, and sought rules like the brand new IT Rules announced this week—as long as those rules were directed at its competitors. And now that the monster has turned on its cheerleaders, the cheerleaders are… publishing editorials, writes Praveen in this absorbing edition of The Nutgraf.
 
That’s all from us this week. Take care.

Indian startups have much to celebrate even in a year like this.
After all, the ecosystem is spitting out unicorns as they raise round after round of capital. Even on the exit front, the well-funded ones are lining up for an IPO. But, for the everyday startup that is looking to exit via acquisition, it is a road paved with spikes.
The everyday startup in question here is Indus OS, a marketplace for regional language apps that come bundled on smartphones like Samsung and Micromax. It claims to have nearly 100 million users and 400,000 India-centric developers. The recent ban on Chinese apps and Google’s self-own in the form of a 30% Play Store commission meant that Indus OS’ popularity soared. The company claims to have grown 10X in the last 18 months.
Walmart-owned payments app PhonePe’s offer to buy it at a valuation of US$60 million—big money for a company that already raised a total of US$25 million—would have been the icing on the proverbial cake. The deal would have checked all the boxes for everyone involved.
It would have left the Indus OS founders richer by US$12 million. For the US$5 billion PhonePe, the narrative of having an in-house app marketplace targeting regional language audiences would have allowed it to raise more funding at a higher valuation. Never mind that actual business gains in terms of engagement and revenue through transactions and advertising may not exactly justify the US$60 million price tag. As for investors like Affle, the deal would return almost 2X its original investment in under a year.
But Affle contested this deal and the matter lies in the Singapore court with suits and counter suits underway.

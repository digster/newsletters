"Any sufficiently advanced technology is indistinguishable from magic" is easily the best known of the three adages (usually called "laws") postulated by one of the greatest science-fiction writers, the late Arthur C. Clarke.
And if there's one technology that seems purpose-built to fit Clarke's third law in these times, it's quantum computing. The underlying concepts of quantum computing are so different from even the most advanced forms of "classical" computing known to us, that it can appear like gobbledygook or magic even to sharp readers.
Let's consider, for instance, the notion of secure communication. (I say "notion" because it could turn out to be just that sometime soon.) Classical computing achieves this by using random bits of digits, called keys, to encrypt the digital streams of data that sent and received by both parties.
By exchanging the keys with only each other, the two parties ensure that anyone else who chances upon their encrypted conversation will have to spend years or decades crunching through possible keys to decrypt the underlying data.
Then comes along Google's quantum computer, which last September claimed "quantum supremacy" by saying, hey, I can calculate in 200 seconds what would take even the most powerful of today's computers 10,000 years. And just like that, the notion of secure communication starts to fade, because encryption can be brute-forced by quantum computers.
That hasn't happened yet, though.
What has happened, however, is that there's a newer form of secure communication now, one that uses quantum computing. The communication is encrypted and sent or received classically—as 1s and 0s bundled up as a stream of optical pulses with one pulse carrying millions of photons—but the keys are generated in real time and sent in the quantum state. This is called Quantum Key Distribution, or QKD.
Which means you send a single photon or ion. The laws of quantum physics allow the photons or the particles to be in a state of superposition; meaning, they represent 1s and 0s in multiple combinations at the same time. They are called qubits. Not only is this superposition state extremely fragile and hard to maintain, the photon itself, while travelling, gets absorbed by the fibre-optic cable. So, for longer distances, repeaters are required in the network to amplify the signal.
"It's like two people are thinking about the same thing at the same time," says the CEO of QNu Labs. This is an ambitious startup that uses quantum computing to secure digital communications. From an 80 km QKD network for India's defence needs to the underpinning for a stealth secure video communications platform, QNu's story is fascinatingly told by our editor, Seema.

Who can’t relate to this superficial gripe: doing the dishes is work, real work. After all, months of lockdowns have confronted us with the enormity of the task. In India though, the realisation has challenged long-held gender role beliefs.
On the African continent, another long-held assumption is slowly eroding: that Chinese automakers have the upper hand. In Delhi, hospital beds will ask for your ID, while in Malaysia, scammers are out for the medical glove market.

“Bartan Dho Liye?”
Rohin
Just a few weeks into India’s national lockdown—one of the world’s strictest—the household chore memes started circulating. First, the harmless ones.
|
“Bartan dho liye?” in Hindi means “Have you washed the utensils?” Most people who forwarded this meme with schadenfreude-ridden glee did not realise or mind the implied sexism and privilege embedded in it. That prior to the lockdown, the utensils were being washed by either a partner or domestic help. In both cases, probably a woman.
It was no joke. Because India is the global outlier when it comes to gender disparity at home—specifically household chores, aka “unpaid work”. At close to six hours, Indian women spend [nearly 7X more time doing unpaid work](https://link.the-ken.com/ss/c/ltnUOFpyR3HixIc27rLthQQVlGlQ9dabtjbX9E6219LsrEci5boAvSfBLOzA9NbpWtMCZMpowdtK1fjdXTm7xZTaiujW4ar3u9XzCTUixwQ/32h/d480is29TyCVzt22NMpbPA/h0/0uR4g8aQnMSU8CNyGo_iD4rXdf0i83cZGZeZwWzIMWY) as compared to men, who average 52 minutes a day. I chanced by the statistic via this [superb tweet thread](https://link.the-ken.com/ss/c/aX9wIf2EaINWwJhuQmbIakCi3QKiquSeh3Od0cZriLMsnCN9zwkf8WvpODoXgaL0PiUWgKDgBKhwfGx7z_p6Gg/32h/d480is29TyCVzt22NMpbPA/h1/eT7GPKjaVNOO3Zr1UWi5zTuvZTmEYzwFGVbDVyxvY64) by Navneet Chahal, a management consultant, over the weekend.
|
Why is India such an outlier? The reasons are numerous and systemic.
|
|
|
The Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD) measures multiple parameters to calculate a composite score, called Social Institutions and Gender Index (SIGI) to measure discrimination against women in social institutions. Its four key dimensions are discrimination in the family; restricted physical integrity; restricted access to productive and financial resources; and restricted civil liberties.
To good measure, the OECD measures this across 180 countries.
As you might have guessed already, India fares pretty poorly, falling below countries like Tajikistan and Burkina Faso.
But, remember “Bartan dho liye?”
Covid-19 and India’s strict lockdown broke down deeply held beliefs about gender roles at work and home. Of course, men could spend a few hours helping their partners or spouses out each day. Of course, women could work remotely with no discernable loss of productivity, allowing them to better balance work and home.
Covid-19 could prove a gender reset for India. It has a once-in-a-lifetime opportunity to change the status quo. Chahal makes the following points in her tweet thread.
- We are finding that remote work is as productive. We are able to balance child care and professional commitments.
- Freelancing could become common esp as companies search for flexible cost structures in the face of volatility. All of this can really help women get into the workforce. If they wanna step up/down on professional pace - project based assignments could allow that.
- A talented female dev could work remotely for 6-8 hrs a day while supporting the household. She could do this even if the disproportionate burden of child care falls on her. Women are resilient that way :)
- Women need opportunities. Companies need to give them those.
- At home, men do need to step up. BJP (Bartan Jhadu Pocha) is low complexity, you can do it. And your boss is doing it too, so you won't lose your prestige there.
- More importantly, men need to share the cognitive load of running the household. And not wait for instructions.
- Finally, women need to push through during this key phase. Size of our ambition will partly determine where we get, where India can reach, what the world can be.
- Nothing stirs a movement like a few role models. And we have plenty already - Sindhu, Mary, Indra Nooyi.
|
Whose bed is it anyway?
Seetharaman
Arvind Kejriwal, the chief minister of India’s capital Delhi, on Sunday stirred up a hornets’ nest with his decision to reserve all beds in hospitals run by his government for residents of the national capital.
To tackle Covid-19 in the face of a potential shortage of beds, the hospitals operated by the state government would only be used to treat locals, he said. So would private hospitals, except the ones doing specialised surgeries, including in oncology and neurology, and also transplants. Delhi has the second-highest number of active Covid-19 cases among Indian states, after Maharashtra. Kejriwal is having a Covid test on Tuesday after exhibiting some symptoms since Sunday.
Criticism of Kejriwal’s decision came in thick and fast, including from a former finance minister of India.
|
And from a journalist, who posed a very logical counterquestion.
|
|
|
But, according to Kejriwal, the Union government-run hospitals in the city would still be open to those from other parts of the country.
Delhi’s Lieutenant-Governor would have none of it and [overruled](https://link.the-ken.com/ss/c/SghZ5ZJE7WzFN-FRJFrM0EPidToNmnoMk1uOEPqMmCywDSjaHlVPsfwEZkYJfasUEjYs91SvfgdR_xtHt_4vG9daESbjYBkT63WlaDJB-c2xWrxU-e5rOVhWuVmRrHcQxnqnflBBI3uz3IJPW5KeNRiBGG5aT74jjODBPSsyyIxE0Lp8_bDat3srgd46kwn2Xm2zZQR-FcAF9FKRSynhaA/32h/d480is29TyCVzt22NMpbPA/h2/PMJBBsEsTknGHSzswIK9MFGqVdxI5YAMwb3P6-juZBQ) Kejriwal’s decision on Monday evening.
Delhi is part of the National Capital Region (NCR), which has parts of three other states. Which means someone from a city bordering Delhi, like Gurugram or Noida, may not be able to go to a hospital run by the Delhi government. There were also [questions](https://link.the-ken.com/ss/c/jhJyCfo75UetkDlrpsRJzhkJPAiS6_lzh7iWzByoDg3B2QbuMjYzC78fNiZdSxgcIcZLfxSYtiKjyqjs2PdFSSoLFbbR-hbzlqAk3QwP-VGGkVm8rUtkOAGrvsh-k5qHELLguraiu4b-NcUksq8bXk8b10SzvjLddm846ld0OEg/32h/d480is29TyCVzt22NMpbPA/h3/MUXjBkd5VgRonJwb0kcY54FyXoixzoUJhnVz5pqhvW0) about the eligibility of those living in Delhi lacking proof of residence.
This flies in the face of Indian citizens’ right to live and work anywhere within the country. So did past [attempts](https://link.the-ken.com/ss/c/SghZ5ZJE7WzFN-FRJFrM0JrKv1N62eF5y-N8fTo3KyVqb21cmXA7ompZTCMEUStXjvdzZpyh65qqhaYtc2YZpaub7wjkGHOjDaegRVc5_p2_t0X0BL610OAX8Y5Qj6INSbDTXiSW8TCkgPk7U70mAQ/32h/d480is29TyCVzt22NMpbPA/h4/hm4o9UaO8_J3VSqNkyICypKkqdyvtq0rR4EonX0Rr8s) by states to reserve the majority of jobs in industrial units for locals.
The pandemic has made internal borders more prominent. While efforts by states in America to discourage travellers from other states has sparked a debate on the move’s [constitutionality](https://link.the-ken.com/ss/c/SghZ5ZJE7WzFN-FRJFrM0EGdi5Nmk2GcE8KGIck4gf_0M2jQ8Qr7dE5BG8F4UcUisM04JLckpzpXgi1Csin8bOQ2QlOaij_YYi5Ldij_QIBjUXJSG1Q8Jbq6ewhELk5fsnKfX6_qL-5_4SJdF1COttiVX_KxB5Dcb5etcS2pEyg-71Im4TSF4wOpMggvcOfR/32h/d480is29TyCVzt22NMpbPA/h5/9PHHo18pP1-6rwIWv10KbHlcJTIN-A4IPhC45f67U6o), that has not been the case in India. Despite the lockdown being a lot less stringent now, states are still playing it safe by [restricting access](https://link.the-ken.com/ss/c/SghZ5ZJE7WzFN-FRJFrM0AhzSl5_79YkBNwiyMVOaiD3FnkMsI-rFB1hb94puI_D90udLxl9Gd3GKX4LxWgTVHrvYKDn4sZqXgYpDhtq3BDemDTzub7MJYO3g7G4YvRaf0YZExU-DFTJirkpTImNb8sJTBctCNdcDWAQzhgtCTqF1Mzt_gWP9eGSaTVNHDZ6/32h/d480is29TyCVzt22NMpbPA/h6/_6nV90vqfPw_Qm6KZ19iMPIddW-poyLmcNJwsRWJsPc) from their neighbours.
States which do not have too many active cases could argue that it’s in their interest to keep borders closed till the situation is brought under control elsewhere. But to discriminate against “outsiders” at a time like this? So much for ‘love thy neighbour’.

Malaysia’s gig economy enters the mainstream
Kay
The pandemic has officially thrust gig workers into the ‘essentials’ category as they form the backbone of all delivery services. The Malaysian government is now looking to [form a framework](https://link.the-ken.com/ss/c/SghZ5ZJE7WzFN-FRJFrM0PGs_nMp9ESFI4DhNGZLh0Xp14T22t_WKaHar5ddLRT_cE7sQ8zlSz4KoKOfTHyIYYp-iiog9j2ehoA6P28YLx05ja_46JGDF_n5YTrtqxbtJieajKjhELWp1iXlxgPWax5KBkEOd1G4AzQAdbJSz9zvJG0xtj9vClAn26KalHsV/32h/d480is29TyCVzt22NMpbPA/h7/csEUPEoT1c0YYaJjh4tlB2YfquDBW82-uSmLOPnA5Y4) to safeguard their welfare.
One of the measures is to provide a matching grant to incentivise them to continue with voluntary contributions to their pension scheme. Currently, self-employed workers can make voluntary contributions to their pension fund accounts and have this topped up with a government incentive of 15% or up to RM250 (US$58.60) annually from 2018 to 2022.
The new framework for gig workers sets aside RM50 million (US$11.7 million) to support this particular scheme.
But it’s most likely a stop-gap measure. Are gig workers even making enough to set some savings aside for retirement?
Plus, once the money is deposited into their pension scheme, they will not be able to withdraw the capital until they reach the age of 55. Unskilled gig workers would likely prefer to have some liquidity in hand instead of locking the money up for the next 20 or even 30 years, especially now. This brings about another question: will delivery platforms ever be in the position to pay gig workers more?
Long term, a set of [formal regulations](https://link.the-ken.com/ss/c/SghZ5ZJE7WzFN-FRJFrM0F_tcq-TI9-2KXIxGwUBHxYVd1mGo53ovmVeVOQA_u9iQwk4COqnWf6ao3Noa-MKmxlhE_z6MIfqXbDiESuSPRyK5eSZFGJRiPTtZUOwP_og/32h/d480is29TyCVzt22NMpbPA/h8/GEj_VDXQ8sXFpyHHqPCRJw7SrW46a9VCIpPwTcCmO6s) are needed to take the sting out of the gig economy, especially as more Malaysians are set to join. The country’s unemployment rate jumped to 3.9% this March and 2 million Malaysians may have [lost their jobs](https://link.the-ken.com/ss/c/SghZ5ZJE7WzFN-FRJFrM0PGs_nMp9ESFI4DhNGZLh0Xp14T22t_WKaHar5ddLRT_nPC4E7vv-kQU84dAeLO5FLrkh36mZxlSGUTbXzJUAueZYr-oix_nsRMbZ2p4MCCDL64e6NNJczurFMw3HVZElJa5vlhQ6VYwQTytdFHyCi6mdXC1uabrvXVu9o_q3Tgm/32h/d480is29TyCVzt22NMpbPA/h9/JesINwb301c6hP0kj51iwz8AUm3b7D1hm_4Afyj4Pq4) due to the pandemic.

When in trouble, grasp at straws
Shreedhar
The Indian government is in a predicament. After implementing what was one of the harshest lockdowns in the world, the number of Covid-positive cases has been steadily rising.
It’s perhaps desperation then, that the government keeps releasing optimistic figures for the future of Covid-19 in the country.
Late last week, health experts from the ministry of health claimed that their analysis showed that the epidemic will come to a close by mid-September.
|
The Covid-19 pandemic may be over in India around mid-September, claim two public health experts from the health ministry who used a mathematical model-based analysis to draw this projection.
The analysis shows that when the number of infected becomes equal to those removed from circulation by recovery and death, the coefficient will reach the 100 per cent threshold and the epidemic will be "extinguished".
|
Covid-19 pandemic may be over in India around mid-September, claims mathematical model-based analysis, [The Times of India](https://link.the-ken.com/ss/c/aX9wIf2EaINWwJhuQmbIamwePdUW86Wq16YaD2jYVZgTYxbzme_DJMqpmOPLD41e85ZChfWBuot-2_tuPf_voNUp-zoEEoV7Px0mPXZky8B1y-taYMo1Y33-aUcL8nwCEKdxvs9EEH43PpXKixhgraja_ykFsfR3JizW3jl9enSzMmP0BorFszNngyRa4NmEifxufKtg2pFT_wD7HKDIqhHz4tHrq_E6FQpwmKehku8/32h/d480is29TyCVzt22NMpbPA/h10/4PFLGvMDPXlQMolrAwArSvFfIibICHM9ycL0luiXZRI)
|
|
|
Soon enough, issues were found and highlighted.
Giridara Gopal Parameswaran, an epidemiologist, [pointed out](https://link.the-ken.com/ss/c/aX9wIf2EaINWwJhuQmbIasT0uq_gQFEhLr7rDEX-D7AInOFgqEmWnrUuysNUsojdtocpGahX1wyESlArL6q7Cg/32h/d480is29TyCVzt22NMpbPA/h11/f00d0RxDbWZ9SHu0AZHYkEYqouWwhzNflq6tEHHQQKo) that the model that was used for the study was for malaria, one which doesn’t apply to an infectious disease such as Covid-19.
|
The Wire [puts](https://link.the-ken.com/ss/c/z3SXHVbiSGsrYGuBZuOfszKKPxKYcFHt_kgQOIj0Ewza3mjntlksemeUhdcVAq7gENeH-oAfLvXDCgYLM-ATLLPfpRIM9IGsZrBxqXBkq1iM8cDDZDnwG8ZvzLuLo1bugUAXqEiNp53Wl2IsdeAraP61INSOEl2m7BcxN_PCHaY/32h/d480is29TyCVzt22NMpbPA/h12/_HUwSuUqe7xnuwORkXv0BRXucpYMVyKcBazRxqFISts) the study’s issues together. Among the most egregious problems: The paper’s editors are also the authors, and the journal it appeared in has a history of taking payments in return for publishing.
But this isn’t the first time a government body has made optimistic claims about the future.
In late April, government think tank NITI Aayog was [confident](https://link.the-ken.com/ss/c/SghZ5ZJE7WzFN-FRJFrM0NTr0xgbXvBFjOQToij0rL6XAHCk0BVgopBiDPmvJ5te5rFHij1GlA2DOrVrcmp41WEo1wObPLe9z2dea7CKd_gXAlnPOebfwHcl6y8FVQ8FcrpPjT2SkfaU6vzEtO-eEw/32h/d480is29TyCVzt22NMpbPA/h13/Kva62A64AROSble1Bs4O3zqKZoPfcPxZc9LI0gJqRe0) on the basis of its own study that there would be no new Covid-19 cases beyond 16 May. Earlier in the month, it had also tried to [overplay](https://link.the-ken.com/ss/c/SghZ5ZJE7WzFN-FRJFrM0AtLil0Hm-L96L81tl6K7ZOjgUb5zLlYVV7wYT6FUcZL2Lrod0dah2iyz1iXYOnTYgvmtu3o5jbbiz0xhDk-eFUALwfjgx81viegNmGinRQF4lbNnsXUwlLiWonE7jDt2iVH-NVOEeF8pPwCw2oxKsE/32h/d480is29TyCVzt22NMpbPA/h14/NrUbyxibVXNjnh-XedBVQkNHsEMtMc66QBMvi3XmA5k) the effect of the lockdown by releasing data to the press without mentioning a source.

Medical product scams on the rise
Jum
The pandemic has caused the shortage of medical products: masks, testing kits, rubber gloves—you name it. Now, opportunists are working hard to take advantage of buyers who are desperate to get a hold of supplies.
The most recent scams were observed in Malaysia, whose rubber glove manufacturers’ association has reported about [fake agents](https://link.the-ken.com/ss/c/SghZ5ZJE7WzFN-FRJFrM0K4XL--e1waRVPu78v-VKT9cbSEtyGvSCwusefaf_8wFp-4cxBcsOwSMUeLU1BeDAJm1DiHP4nayn-FrGs0W56DXH6QTWzpJaiH6LNckUd8FOjOgaohqUQxxC1Rsuo2uw37yG2gVHRi3Nft63M7QNRA/32h/d480is29TyCVzt22NMpbPA/h15/s3Q6f-E-bONxQf4x7y_GJZ6f-k9NI11O3UJ_1fUVoPg) claiming to represent its member companies in order to strike deals. Bogus company letters show potential customers were being quoted ridiculous prices with a promise of short delivery time.
The association made it clear: the glove shortage is expected to carry over into next year, and the industry’s supply has been fully booked. So buyers should be wary of parties claiming to have quick stocks.
The world’s top glove producer, Malaysia supplies two-thirds of medical glove consumption globally, which is estimated to jump 11% to 330 billion pieces this year. But a surge in demand across nations—due to [panic buying and stockpiling](https://link.the-ken.com/ss/c/SghZ5ZJE7WzFN-FRJFrM0CrquqWt8lARA5w77dhTuqNf7FOu2G4ImGjCBt6coP8u88GkvvOgXhQlFFKfkUQB5kdsJg8BIcIR40xsZ1ZZkgaR7fQiWNA2BYMXiigEhRxgP9pY_p5tgUWVx9Cj60G-c7bIVXNw8fkIZ1A6PwCRbtsAJsGWMkvDqFT-1UF5FI23/32h/d480is29TyCVzt22NMpbPA/h16/wixUll-GjwdyRcK0ncFE-cYVV6ih3SdlufYtlpAdQi0)—has outstripped Malaysia’s production capacity. It didn’t help that the country imposed lockdown measures in March, limiting staffing in glove makers’ factories to 50% of capacity at the time. This resulted in fulfillment of orders running as late as four months.
Everywhere else, [scammers](https://link.the-ken.com/ss/c/SghZ5ZJE7WzFN-FRJFrM0Hn04ZOEJSu8PhZa-5zFIVIKhJ-nFXX8FQQbvqIC8IvvSn6nnhEuC4QUpxIjwTD_Ni9AMfSpq-1CR7tl9dwPDAbhMrPpTGprgb1-PXXmdOq4OZjIQ3cX78fhGZ99CaydL0xP9FMXBMsrN11WnBUf_-M/32h/d480is29TyCVzt22NMpbPA/h17/Z5Gc2Zo0AeC0C3BgxmLIN-iYTQ3aTqz3hAIN4_K6YQ8) selling fake protective equipment and testing kits have mushroomed. Counterfeit products are most likely not fit for purpose and therefore only endanger the life of whoever’s using them. A phony testing kit, for example, could lead someone to believe they’re negative for the virus when they’ve been infected.
Indeed, crises don’t just bring opportunities, but also opportunists.

In the spotlight

Flipkart and Bigbasket start their own q-commerce fight—away from Blinkit and Zepto

They are making up for lost time by turning to a category in which they have an edge over the incumbents: electronics

The push to deliver as many products as possible in 15 minutes has taken hold of a core e-commerce category: electronics. Walmart-backed Flipkart and Tata-owned Croma, who built the market for electronics, are using their networks to dispatch items faster, even setting sights on delivering a TV. A lot of factors such as credit-financing options, large dark stores, and huge inventory work in their favour which quick-commerce platforms like Blinkit may face a roadblock with. With the market still in nascent stages, they’re trying to do to electronics what Blinkit, Zepto, and Swiggy Instamart did to groceries.

Boat, Noise unleashed cheap smartwatches on India. Rivals hurt them with dirt-cheap ones

India’s smartwatch market has crashed, and its leaders are now racing to fix what their own price wars broke

Smartwatch brands Boat and Noise lost market share to new entrants like Fire-boltt, trying to undercut each other on price, causing consumer fatigue. At about 9 million units, smartwatch sales in the country are down by nearly half from a year ago, owing to a lack of innovation in the sector. It looks difficult to regain lost ground in smartwatches with established brands like Titan doubling its share in the past six months. Boat hopes to get back on track by doubling down on its core audio products, while Noise has stopped playing the volume game. [Read the full story]

Longform features

Dear EV subsidy planners, the data has a few questions

After a year of flip-flops and sales spikes-dips, it’s clear that passenger cars and jeeps will continue to pollute the most, and subsidies have ‘medium-term’ benefits, not just short-term sales gains

The government’s EV registration data shows a steady uptick in most vehicles, but EV penetration has declined in 2024. If this year was bad for subsidy planning, it was worse for some OEMs, especially when Bajaj offered a Rs 1 EV exchange offer. India might end its EV subsidies by FY26, but there’s a case for medium-term benefits that policymakers must look at. A major economy like India has never directly subsidised passenger cars and jeeps, but they will pollute the most by 2030. [Read the full story]

How facial-recognition firms turn public deals into private gold

The divide between public and private contracts is forcing India’s surveillance startups to walk a fine line

Government surveillance projects have been ramping up, even in the final months of the Rs 1.64 lakh crore Smart Cities Mission, which boosted many startups’ profiles. Indian startups are capitalising on this demand to secure high-visibility government contracts. But government projects are unpredictable, often shelved due to budget cuts or personnel changes, and larger contracts are difficult for smaller startups to win. As a result, private clients become crucial for profitability, but both private and government contracts are important for long-term sustainability. [Read the full story]

Indian taxpayers cut the largest cheque for e-bus makers. Private operators ditch the ride

As the government gets its bus electrification dreams in gear, e-bus makers like EKA Mobility win big with public-private partnerships—but the public and private operators feel left out

In its latest Rs 10,900 crore EV subsidy, the government set aside the lion’s share for e-buses. EKA Mobility, which started out as a vendor and now is an OEM boasts of an orderbook of 1,500 e-buses worth Rs 3,000 crore. Unlike the Indian bus landscape where private operators are in a majority, all e-buses run on a public-private model. Even as the government pushes for public transport electrification, private bus operators are not hitching the ride. [Read the full story]

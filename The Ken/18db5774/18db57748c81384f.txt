Good Morning Ishan,

Zomato reported its quarterly earnings last week. 

Everything was along expected lines. But the real surprise was the performance of Blinkit, Zomato’s quick-commerce business.

It wasn’t just growing. It was growing remarkably well, and much faster than Zomato’s food business.

Zomato's quick ecommerce wing Blinkit said its gross order value (GOV) grew 28 per cent QoQ and 103 per cent YoY largely due to the robust uptick in demand due to the multiple festivals and occasions in the quarter. The company noted its revenue from ads outpaced the order value and registered an increase of 220 per cent YoY during the December quarter.

Zomato Q3 FY24 results: Blinkit posts second consecutive positive quarter, revenue doubles on-year, Business Today https://www.businesstoday.in/latest/corporate/story/zomato-q3-fy24-results-blinkit-posts-second-consecutive-positive-quarter-revenue-doubles-on-year-416727-2024-02-08

More bizarrely, it wasn’t burning more money to grow faster.

Blinkit reported a revenue of Rs 644 crore in Q3 FY24 as against Rs 301 crore in the year-ago quarter and Rs 505 crore in Q2 FY24. The quick commerce vertical’s adjusted EBITDA loss further improved to Rs 89 crore in the quarter ended December 2023 from Rs 227 crore in Q3 FY23 and Rs 125 crore in Q2 FY24.

It’s not just Blinkit. Instamart, Swiggy’s quick commerce business is also growing much faster https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/tech/startups/swiggys-fy23-revenue-grows-45-to-rs-8265-crore-net-loss-expands-15/articleshow/107267730.cms?from=mdr than its other businesses. Zepto’s revenue grew by 14X last year https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/tech/startups/zepto-sees-14x-surge-in-fy23-revenue-while-losses-triple/articleshow/104732751.cms?from=mdr. In a year where demand has stagnated in other categories, quick commerce stands out as an outlier. For some reason, more and more people find the idea of getting diet coke and chips in less than 20 minutes alluring. 

Also, it’s not what happened last year. Senior executives at both Zomato https://www.businesstoday.in/entrepreneurship/start-up/story/can-blinkit-become-bigger-than-parent-zomato-heres-what-zomato-cfo-akshant-goyal-believes-is-possible-408752-2023-12-08 and Swiggy https://www.moneycontrol.com/news/technology/davos-2024-instamart-can-be-larger-than-food-delivery-says-swiggy-boss-sriharsha-majety-12057421.html have gone on record to say that their quick-commerce vertical could eventually become bigger than their food delivery businesses. Clearly, everyone is bullish about the future of this business.

As long-time readers of this newsletter know, I’ve been somewhat sceptical about quick commerce’s long-term prospects, while simultaneously being amused and interested in the structural changes that led to the emergence of companies like Blinkit, Instamart, and Zepto. Initially, I broke down the business model by explaining the core insight that grocery startups had suddenly figured out https://the-ken.com/the-nutgraf/why-10-minute-grocery-deliveries-are-finally-here/—reduce selection instead of increasing them. I’ve also written about the fact that 10-minute grocery companies have limited avenues for differentiation, except by brand https://the-ken.com/the-nutgraf/two-stories-about-brand-association/, and so all of them tripped over themselves by making ridiculous claims about their delivery times to create the association in your head. 

All of this was fun and amusing until the economic slowdown began. I wrote how Zepto was in a vulnerable position https://the-ken.com/the-nutgraf/zepto-is-looking-for-a-chair/, and as 2022 ended, I predicted that 10-minute grocery would be in a “hold” year all through 2023—which didn’t really happen https://the-ken.com/the-nutgraf/2023-predictions-revisited/. If anything, quick commerce grew rapidly last year, while most other categories flatlined. 

For some reason, the surprising resilience of 10-minute groceries is a uniquely Indian phenomenon. Remember, quick commerce emerged from Western markets and was widely seen as one of those high-risk, indulgent business bets by VCs during an era when money was freely available. And while Indian quick-commerce companies soared last year, the story was very different in other markets.

As July drew to a close, Getir announced plans to withdraw from three more markets. The Turkish rapid grocery delivery startup pulled out of Spain, Portugal, and Italy, in a move that reduced its European footprint to just four countries.

It’s the latest chapter in a string of difficult encounters for the company and for the q-commerce or rapid grocery delivery market more generally in Europe. The space is undergoing a tough transition as VC money dries up and cost-of-living challenges abound for consumers.

As for why Getir decided to slim down its operations, a spokesperson said that the “withdrawal from these three markets will allow it to focus its financial resources on existing markets where the opportunities for operational profitability and sustainable growth are stronger.”

Re-focus has been a common theme in the industry.

Why Europe’s grocery delivery space is in a state of flux, The Next Web https://thenextweb.com/news/europes-grocery-delivery-space-state-flux

For the record, it’s quite possible that Indian quick-commerce companies are operating on a lag as compared to their Western counterparts, and soon, they’ll find themselves in a similar position. The viability of the business model is still an open question, even if their growth rate last year is not. 

Despite this, I tried to figure out the reasons for this resilience. There are the obvious ones. The cost of delivery labour is less expensive, and demand is disproportionately driven by India’s California’s users https://the-ken.com/the-nutgraf/why-india-wont-see-a-100-billion-internet-company-anytime-soon/—a small, tech-savvy, elite set of consumers who punch way above their weight and drive growth for most internet companies. 

But I found a few more, and I think all combined, it explains why 10-minute groceries are in a somewhat unique position in India.

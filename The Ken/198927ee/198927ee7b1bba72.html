Good morning Ishan. Remember how I wrote about triangulation a few weeks ago? Well, this week the universe sent me a wink back in return.

A few days earlier, I was in Mumbai to interview Anand Jain, the co-founder of Clevertap, for the next episode of the First Principles podcast (it airs on Monday).

We were talking about one critical tension that founders of 10- to 15-year-old organisations inevitably encounter: how to balance promoting from within versus hiring from the outside, both of which come with their own advantages and challenges.

“But what we’ve learned, every time we failed, is (that) we did not hand hold the person. Hand holding is making sure that you’re working with the person to get them into the fold and culture of that function. See, (promoting) homegrown talent into a new role could mean that they’re very good at, say, engineering, but they may not know anything about customer success or running marketing. And you have to now help sandpaper them into this new role,” said Anand.

Then, later during the week, one of my colleagues used the same sandpaper analogy to explain what The Ken had done to her career over time. She got the reference from observing how professional painters first sandpapered the walls of her home before painting them.

Finally, she reminded me of the Steve Jobs “rock tumbler” anecdote, which I’d used in an earlier edition of this very newsletter.

Meta triangulation! Thank you for that. Let me share my gratitude by recommending a wonderful (and cheap) “deep-work aid” that I bought recently.

1. Rubber Ducking

I think I’ve told you earlier that I don’t have a way to visualise you. You’re you, of course, but because there are so many of you across ages, genders, professions, time zones, and nationalities, there is no meaningful way to picture you. You’re everybody, anybody, and nobody.

Or, perhaps, are you a giant rubber duck?

In one of the nerdiest internet jokes ever, Stack Overflow, a website for programmers, introduced a rubber duck for an April Fools’ Day joke. The duck’s role was to help visitors solve computer programming problems. It would listen to people’s questions, and after hearing them out, the duck would start “typing,” only to produce a mere “quack” after the wait.

As any software developer can tell you, many problems can, in fact, be solved by simply explaining the situation to someone – even if that someone knows nothing about the field, is not particularly smart, and is not interested in offering any guidance whatsoever. And yes, even if that someone is just a rubber duck. The solution doesn’t come from the outside – it is found in the process of explaining the problem in natural language.

Stuck? Ask a Rubber Duck: Solve problems by explaining it to someone—or something, Psychology Today

I am only being half facetious. Writing these Sunday emails have for me, over the years, become a reflective and deeply contemplative exercise in clarifying my own thoughts aloud. Each week that I write to you, I learn something more about myself in the process too.

Reading things aloud leads to something called the “production effect”, causing us to remember the things we “produced” better (because we can now hear it back through our own ears too).

In the end, having produced a piece of information makes it memorable. By our view, a produced item becomes distinctive against the background of items that were not produced, and that distinctiveness can later be used as diagnostic regarding whether a piece of information has been experienced. The boost imparted by production is quite consistent across different types of encoding, across different study and test procedures, and now across different types of material.

Much as I’m guilty of “over speaking” in meetings at The Ken, my favourite types of meetings are when we’re brainstorming a new product, the likes of which we’ve not done before. Just the act of speaking my thoughts out loud has often helped me evolve and sharpen my views during a single meeting, sometimes in as less as 10–15 minutes. This is why the cliché about CEOs using “in-person brainstorming and meetings” as one of the key reasons to drag their colleagues back to office is so true. There is no replacement for a quality brainstorming discussion done in person.

I’d argue that writing too creates a similar “production effect”.

Developing Strategic Thinking: Writing forces leaders to prioritize key messages, structure their thoughts logically, and simplify complex ideas. These skills mirror the mental discipline required for strategic decision-making.

Building Reflective Practice: Writing acts as a reflective exercise, helping leaders process challenges, reframe obstacles, and articulate goals. Research has shown that reflective writing improves emotional intelligence and decision-making.

Why Leaders Should Add Writing To Their Leadership Toolkit In 2025—Scott Hutheson, Forbes

“Like a muscle, your mind needs reflection to reenergise and grow stronger,” say professors James R. Bailey and Scheherazade Rehman in this Harvard Business Review article on reflection (which is what writing can often unlock).

Research shows the habit of reflection can separate extraordinary professionals from mediocre ones. We would go so far as to argue that it’s the foundation that all other soft skills grow from.

The practice itself is all about learning, looking back on the day (without bias or regret) to contemplate your behavior and its consequences. It requires sitting with yourself, taking an honest moment to think about what transpired, what worked, what didn’t, what can be done, and what can’t. Reflection requires courage. It’s thoughtful and deliberate. Being at the “top of your game” only comes when you extract from your past how to engage the future.

Don’t Underestimate the Power of Self-Reflection—by James R. Bailey and Scheherazade Rehman, Harvard Business Review

2. So long, farewell

Hello Ishan, it’s Hari here. The title of this section is a direct rip-off of one of my favourite songs ever from The Sound of Music. It’s a delightful number with lyrics that bring me great comfort at this time—especially given that this is the last time I will be penning this section; one of my wonderful colleagues will be taking over in the coming weeks. 

As I sat down to write this, much too late for our newsletters editor Jonathan’s liking (I’m terribly sorry for all the previous times too 🥲), I didn’t really know what I wanted to recommend to you this week. 

An hour or so spent zoning out in front of my desk, however, did eventually lead me to something I’ve always felt is a great Sunday read.

View in Browser

I mean, how do you say no to Gulzar? And this delightful book takes us through some of his best work—mostly written for Bollywood, but with a poetic quality that is hard to ignore.

I’ve held on to it for a while, and read through it every now and then.

I know this might not be quite the book you expected for your Sunday read. But trust me, some of my best weekends have been spent soaking in these pages as the corresponding songs play in the background. It’s nostalgia, but even more than that, as I’ve come to expect from Gulzar, it’s reflection.

As for our reader recommendations today, I’ve picked Ramya Kancharla’s suggestion because what she had to say about the book really touched me. So much so that I’ve bought a copy for myself. 

It’s Beautyland by Marie-Helene Bertino.

View in Browser

In Ramya’s words: 

“Beautyland follows Adina, who is sent by aliens to observe and report on Earth via a fax machine. I picked this up thinking it would be a fun sci-fi novel, but it is actually about life on Earth. It’s quite funny but has a thin thread of sadness running through the book. This is a beautifully written and beautifully observed novel about belonging, about being an outsider looking on, about life and longing. It is without a doubt one of the best things I have read this year!”

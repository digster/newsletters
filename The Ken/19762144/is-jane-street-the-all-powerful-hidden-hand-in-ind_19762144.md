Good Morning Ishan,
On 9 April, while global markets convulsed with fear, the Nifty 50 did something bizarre. It did absolutely nothing. No movement, no volatility.
Then came 15 April. Despite a jittery week, the Sensex stayed flat. Even on 24 April, two days after the terror attack in Kashmir, the Nifty refused to react. No selloff, no panic.
To the average retail trader, it looked like the markets had found Zen. But to seasoned options desks, it smelled fishy. That kind of stillness isn’t natural. It’s engineered.
Back in January, The Ken [broke a story](https://the-ken.com/story/the-mystery-fund-playing-god-and-wreaking-havoc-on-the-stock-market/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter) about a “mystery fund” manipulating expiry days—sometimes through silence, sometimes through finagled chaos. At the time, (almost) nobody knew who this was. But the signs were there—giant positions, timed index moves, and a strategy that made implied volatility the new puppetmaster of the Nifty.
Now, we may have a name: Jane Street.
The ultra-secretive US quant firm known for its stealth and success—so averse to attention its employees don’t wear company hoodies outside the office—is reportedly under Sebi investigation. The regulator suspects Jane Street and its entities in Singapore and India were quietly skewing the index to suit their giant options positions. Month after month, expiry after expiry.
A now-settled US court case also revealed just how “immensely valuable” Jane’s Indian options strategy was. In 2023, it made $1 billion trading Indian options. Last year, closer to $3 billion, say some traders. Sure, Sebi did propose rules to limit this kind of manipulation.
Now, guess what? The manipulation’s back—with both tiptoe and sledgehammer.

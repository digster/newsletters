Good Morning Ishan,
Halfway through a year of landmark IPOs, a new equilibrium has emerged between founders and private investors. The former is paying to re-enter as a promoter group; the latter is loosening its bargaining grip.
The Bluestone Jewellery public issue closed last week. Whether IPO will pop this week remains to be seen, but its founder paid a premium, above the IPO price band, to buy additional shares and beef up the promoter shareholding.
Of course, what the Lenskart founder did a few weeks ago still takes the cake for the warmest founder-investor handshake just before a public listing.
In 2021, when some of the most promising startups went public, they leaned on the then-Sebi move to sideline the “promoter” concept. It was acceptable to all as a sign of the fragmented, venture-backed ownership.
But between 2024 and 2025, the regulator has come out with rules nudging founders back to the drawing board to classify them as the promoter group. It’s even making the promoter tag more attractive by allowing founders to revisit their decisions later. Tax lawyers say draft IPO papers with identifiable promoters now get quicker approvals, another quiet nudge in the same direction.
All this because the regulator’s concern runs deeper. In her [story today](https://the-ken.com/story/lenskart-to-bluestone-why-startup-founders-headed-for-ipos-suddenly-love-being-promoters/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter), Arundhati examines what this means for the culture and captable of companies on the cusp of a public life.

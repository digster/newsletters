Have you ever heard of 'negativity bias'?
It is the idea that things of an unpleasant nature, like bad news on the telly, for example, tend to stay with us more than something positive. Turns out, it's a survival trait. The part of the human brain that regulates and processes emotions, the amygdala—or the lizard brain as it is known as—is wired to seek out negative information so it can combat threats.
This would have been useful 6,000 years ago. In the modern world? Not so much.
And unfortunately for us, the last five months or so have been filled with nothing but bad news. Coronavirus. Bushfires in Australia. Riots in Delhi. A cyclone in West Bengal. A standoff between India and China. Locusts! A Google search beginning with "Everything that..." autocompletes to "...went wrong in 2020."
The amount of negative information in front of us is staggering and overwhelming. Coronavirus Explained, the latest iteration of Netflix's limited series called Explained, has some bleak numbers. Nearly 50% of the doctors and nurses in China reported depression between January and February 2020. Nearly as many reported anxiety.

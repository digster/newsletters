I’m sure you’ve heard this one before: “Mutual funds are subject to market risks. Please read the offer document carefully before investing.” In fact, you’ve probably heard this disclaimer so many times that the words no longer register—they just go in one ear and out the other.

But if you were Smallcase, you might just wish someone had given you the same warning about your own product.

The ten-year-old startup promised users that they could browse through bundles of stocks tied together by an investment theme and buy them directly. But its USP—the rush of picking your own bets—isn’t so hot now that the market is moody. Retail investors just want safe, solid returns. And who’s giving them that? Good old mutual funds.

That’s not the only reason Smallcase is losing ground. You don’t need tens of thousands to invest in a mutual fund—just Rs 500 will suffice for an SIP. And by the way, those bundles are nothing to write home about in terms of returns. That’s before factoring in the heftier taxes.

Smallcase didn’t just underestimate how difficult its mission would be. It also made its own job harder with missteps like trying to wring more money out of the analysts and advisors who design its bundles. But what’s a startup to do when it’s posting losses to the tune of Rs 174 crore?

Faced with a classic disruptor’s dilemma, Smallcase’s only option is to become less exciting in order to survive, writes Archishma in today’s story.

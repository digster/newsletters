Good Morning Ishan,
If someone buys a car in Indonesia, there’s a good chance that it’s being sold by Astra International. The company is Southeast Asia’s largest independent automotive group. In particular, it has been the biggest car dealer in Indonesia for years, consistently selling over half of the cars sold in the country.
You would think that position would’ve prepared Astra for the transition to battery-powered cars, but that isn’t the case.
Carmakers like BYD and Wuling Motors from China, as well as South Korea’s Hyundai, are beating it to the draw. These overseas brands are now the go-to choices for car buyers who are switching from gasoline to the grid. Meanwhile, Astra and its partners—Toyota, Daihatsu, Isuzu, Peugeot, and BMW—are losing out.
The situation for Astra is made worse by the Indonesian government’s goal to put more electric cars on the roads. Astra, of course, is aware that it’s falling behind. It’s trying to keep up with shifting consumer preferences, but business ties—the same ones that propelled it—are now keeping it from growing in new directions.
In today’s story from Indonesia, Prima explains why Astra is being left out of the country’s rapidly growing electric car segment.

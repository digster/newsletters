Good Morning Ishan,
There’s acrimony between India’s hospitals and the General Insurance Council. The point of contention is Cashless Everywhere, a new health-insurance initiative that will let patients receive treatment at any hospital across the country without forking over cash, even if the institution is not within their coverage network. The goal is to get to 100% cashless claims from just about 63% now.
On the surface, that sounds great for patients. But hospitals say insurers are forcing them into a severely disadvantageous position. The conflict is bitter, and it’ll be the people seeking medical care who bear the brunt, as Anand writes. Don’t miss his [coverage](https://the-ken.com/story/health-insurers-cashless-everywhere-is-going-nowhere/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter) today.
And now, elsewhere in Asia…
I’m on a long-overdue visit to Bangkok to reacquaint myself with the city and meet a few of The Ken’s readers. (If you’re in town and feel like introducing yourself, [please drop me a note](mailto:brady@the-ken.com).)
In the past six days, I’ve seen just two electric cars in the wild. That’s far fewer than expected since [78%](https://www.nationthailand.com/business/automobile/40033576) of all EVs driven in ASEAN during Q1 2023 were on Thailand’s roads. Chalk up my experience as an outlier, but recognise that there is real resistance and hesitation among Thai drivers considering the switch.
Some of the reasons are run-of-the-mill, like a shortage of charging stations and range anxiety. Others are more specific to this location, like the short warranty period that most EV brands offer in Thailand, as three drivers told me.
Vehicular electrification is often associated with blue skies and breathable air, and now there is data to tell us just how big that impact can be. Research released by the American Lung Association on Wednesday suggests a full transition to electric vehicles in the United States would [eliminate](https://www.axios.com/2024/02/21/electric-cars-children-health) millions of asthma attacks in children each year and that over 500 cases of infant mortality simply wouldn’t happen.
It’s fair to expect the impact to be even greater in the densest parts of Asia, where the air is often still smoggy. After all, [99%](https://www.wri.org/insights/air-pollution-southeast-asia-cities-jakarta-el-nino) of Southeast Asian households are in areas where pollution breaches the World Health Organization’s standards for safe air. Consider the load it places on local healthcare systems—and how your choice of a personal vehicle might have life-changing ramifications on the people around you.

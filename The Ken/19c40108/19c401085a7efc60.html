One fine morning this January, the boarding gates at Raipur airport were cramped and crowded. Passengers in long queues fidgeted with Aadhaar cards and boarding passes. On my way to catch a Delhi-bound flight, I walked past them, hands free. I smiled at the camera, blinked once, and the gates opened.

This is Digiyatra. Built under the Ministry of Civil Aviation, the facial-recognition system promises a contactless, paperless airport experience. Travellers create an account, pre-verify their identity using Aadhaar, upload their boarding pass, and then move through green-lit gates by simply showing their face. Convenience is automated.

It is also catching on fast. Roughly three in 10 Indian air travellers already hand over their facial data for that convenience, according to government data, and the ministry now wants to push that number to 8/10 within three years. The Ken had reported on airports relentlessly pushing travellers to download the app in 2023 when it first launched.

India’s enthusiasm stands out because elsewhere, the mood has shifted.

Elsewhere in the world, biometric data is being discouraged for airport entry. In November 2025, the Spanish Data Protection Agency (AEPD) shut down the country’s facial-recognition boarding gates and imposed a 10 million euros ($11.8 million) penalty on Aena, Spain’s largest airport operator. The reason was inadequate “data-protection impact assessment”. The problem was not just that facial recognition was intrusive, but that Aena could not demonstrate where the biometric data flowed, who accessed it, and for how long it was retained. Stemming from the EU’s General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), passed in 2018, convenience alone, the regulation holds, cannot justify privacy risk. A similar sentiment was echoed in Italy’s recent suspension of facial-recognition boarding at Milan airport.

India has chosen a different path. The Digital Personal Data Protection (DPDP) Act, passed in 2023, finally gave the country a comprehensive data-privacy framework. The rules followed in 2025, and now the timeline for compliance by companies has been fast-tracked from 18 months to 12. But unlike Europe’s GDPR, it leaves a crucial concept deliberately vague: what exactly counts as legitimate use.

Instead, one of DPDP’s hardest questions for enterprises is: where does personal data actually reside? In technical terms, what does “data discovery” look like?

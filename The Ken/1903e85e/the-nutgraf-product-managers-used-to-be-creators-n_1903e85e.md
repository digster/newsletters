Good Morning Ishan,
Yesterday, Deepinder Goyal, CEO of Zomato, announced a “cool new update”.
My reaction when I saw this was, “That’s quite cool.” And then I forgot about it.
However, on Linkedin and Twitter (now X), the reaction from the product and tech community was quite different. A few people, just like me, thought it was cool. But the vast majority of people simply couldn’t let go of it. The overwhelming response to this launch was either:
a) I don’t understand why you are doing this. What are you solving?
b) Here’s my theory about the real reason why you are doing this… and how you need to do something else more ‘useful’.
I love this example because this is a perfect illustration of something I’ve [alluded to earlier](https://the-ken.com/the-nutgraf/how-vcs-killed-the-meaning-of-work/)—the purpose of building products in India’s tech companies has changed. Most people refuse to believe that companies can build the occasional feature that’s fun, interesting, and cool for those reasons alone. Instead, they believe that everything that’s created must be done exclusively to solve some customer problem, or move some metric, or have some measurable impact that can be tracked on some random dashboard.
And if it doesn’t do either of these things, then it’s a sign that the company is doing something wrong, or is grossly inefficient.
There are many reasons why this destruction of ‘cool’ has happened, but if there’s one group of people who bear the most amount of responsibility for this, it’s Product Managers.
One of the ways to tell the difference between the older and newer generation of Product Managers, particularly in India, is how they respond to product features like this. If you’re a PM, or someone who has recently become a PM in the past, say, five years, I imagine you’re more obsessed with why Zomato did this. You probably use words like ‘hypothesis’, ‘use-case’, ‘gamification’, or you’re going to obsess over how to attribute and measure growth and adoption for a feature like this. You’re mentally picturing the PRD document that elaborates the masterplan behind this feature, and you’re wondering what the Mixpanel dashboard inside Zomato that’s tracking this would look like. Mentally, you’ve filed this away as a potential interview question already on that Notion document.
However, some of us—admittedly a much smaller group of PMs—see this completely differently. The first reaction of Product Managers from an earlier generation, like mine, isn’t “why did Zomato’s Product Manager do this and what are the success metrics?”, but instead, “I wonder how this Product Manager at Zomato got this done?”. And by how, I don’t mean the nuts and bolts like databases, UI frameworks, or anything like that. By how, I mean, “How did this Product Manager navigate this feature past all the people, processes, and nonsense inside the company that’s setup to actively stop them from doing this?”
The reason why we’re thinking about the how is because some of us find the why or the so what of Product Management less interesting. Sure, the vast majority of features are built to move metrics, but occasionally… just once in a while, we know that Product Management is also about creating things that are, in Deepinder Goyal’s words, a “cool update”. I’m not privy to Zomato’s internal workings, but if I had to bet, I’d say that Zomato probably already had this feature built out, maybe at some internal hackathon, or by some bored developer. Someone must have shown it to Deepinder Goyal or a senior leader and they’d have found it cool, and decided to add it to their next release just for the fun and whimsy of it. I doubt this was part of a carefully constructed plan. I highly doubt anyone in Zomato is tracking adoption or cares about the ‘impact’ of how this feature is doing. They felt good that they’d done something fun, told the world it was something they did for fun, and went home. End of story.
For an entire generation of Product Managers, the idea that product features can be built like this is simply inconceivable.

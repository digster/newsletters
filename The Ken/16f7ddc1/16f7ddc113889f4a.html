In September 2016, the International Union for Conservation of Nature (IUCN) upgraded the status of Giant Pandas worldwide from “endangered” to “vulnerable.”

It was a rare victory for nature.

The tech industry can often mimic real life. In Southeast Asia, one particular breed of panda is enjoying its own resurgence.

Foodpanda, the on-demand food delivery business started by Rocket Internet but now owned by Germany’s Delivery Hero, is plotting a comeback in Asia.

The company retreated from numerous markets, including Indonesia—the world’s fourth-largest country based on population—in 2016 on account of the strength of ride-hailing companies Grab and Gojek. Armed with vast fleets of motorbike taxi drivers, they were far better suited to the wild plains of Southeast Asia.

Foodpanda did retain a presence in some markets. But it decided to double down in 2019, the year when food delivery went from an occasional treat for consumers to a daily habit for millions.

With lucrative growth on the table, Foodpanda recovered its appetite for Southeast Asia. And Delivery Hero—its parent—is prepared to fund its splurge to fund its hunger.

Delivery Hero remains deeply unprofitable—to the tune of a $468 million EBITDA loss in Q3 2019. However, it put hands in pockets to fork out a tasty $4 billion to acquire Woowa Brothers, the company that operates Korea’s top food delivery service, Baedal Minjok, and is *suspend belief* profitable.

Delivery Hero’s share price surged more than 40% on news of the Woowa deal. There’s a belief that Southeast Asia’s food delivery market, tipped to pass $20 billion by 2025, growing 50X in a decade, is ripe for Foodpanda to battle with Gojek and Grab.

But, three’s a crowd, as the saying goes, especially when the party is a food party.

Delivery Hero’s plan is still at the appetiser stage, and only time will tell how the story ends. Can this vulnerable animal boost its chances of survival further?

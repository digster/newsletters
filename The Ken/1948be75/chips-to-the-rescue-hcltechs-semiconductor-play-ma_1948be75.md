Good Morning Ishan,
Last week marked the 25th anniversary of HCLTech’s listing. It could have been a milestone for celebration, but it was marked by markets giving it a drubbing. Analysts were let down too.
The Indian IT is navigating a period of uncertainty, with global economic factors weighing on demand. What everyone is missing, though, is the muscle that HCLTech has built over 26 years—its semiconductor business, which forms the pillar of its engineering services that accounts for 16% of its topline.
It’s the only Indian IT company to have a relationship with all the leading foundries in the world, including Taiwan’s TSMC, and is now doubling down on expanding this business. The elusive GenAI gains are also somewhat converging here.
In the December quarter, a Japanese semiconductor giant inked a deal with HCLTech to develop a system-on-chip (SoC) for autonomous vehicles. Curiously, the agreement appeared in HCLTech’s earnings presentation under the umbrella of “AI and GenAI deals”, write Shristi and Abhirami in their story today.
As the semiconductor self-sufficiency takes centre stage—not the least because Donald Trump is back—Indian IT firms are acquiring fabless companies. HCLTech started the trend five years ago, and others have followed suit. The latest being Infosys which acquired Insemi in January last year.
Gradually, then suddenly. That’s how HCLTech’s silicon roots appear to be its next big thing. Let’s get to [the story](https://the-ken.com/story/chips-to-the-rescue-hcltechs-semiconductor-play-may-outshine-its-it-woes/).
Reliance Industries’ 2021 clean energy push was a blockbuster—US$10 billion on the table, backed by major partnerships and acquisitions. But by 2024, the hype had started to fade, with sustainability as an investment theme losing steam. Still, Reliance seemed committed to the course.

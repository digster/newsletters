The US$700 million-worth startup wanted to reshape the food supply chain. But it increasingly looks like an FMCG company

Delivered to Ishan, Subscriber to The Ken.

Wed, 24 Apr 2024

Deputy Editor

Good Morning Ishan,

“Farm to fork” is quite hackneyed by now. But how about “fork to farm”? The latter is how Waycool Foods, a nearly decade-old company, managed to get to US$700 million in valuation.

The Chennai-based company made its name helping retailers and restaurants source fresh produce, dairy products, and grains from over 50,000 farmers across the country. By its own admission, it wanted to flip the “food value chain from supply-led to demand-driven”.

But almost a third of its US$190 million top line in the year ended March 2024 came from its own FMCG products like rice, pulses, and ready-to-cook products. That business has grown 10X since the pandemic.

What was once a luxury has now become a necessity. Waycool, which had to shut down warehouses and drastically reduce its headcount, believes FMCG “could be its largest growth driver”.

It’s not just Waycool, even peers like Peak XV-backed Dehaat and Walmart-backed Ninjacart are looking beyond their mainstay.

If agritech is supposed to be a multibillion-dollar opportunity, why are the largest companies in the space so eager to diversify? Let’s get to Narayanan’s story to find out.

Once you’re done with that, you can head to Katrina’s timely chat with a top official at a regulator that is one of a kind in a region where it’s hard to be so.

Monetary authorities around the world are still trying to figure out whether and how to issue digital-only banking licences. On the other hand, five countries in Southeast Asia already have very clear rules around this.

Katrina sat down with Lyn Javier, the assistant governor for policy and specialised supervision sub-sector at BSP, the Philippines’ central bank, to understand why the country has been a lot more welcoming of foreign digital-bank applicants than Malaysia and Thailand. And what is making BSP reconsider its earlier decision not to issue any more digital-bank licences than the six it gave out in 2020.

Masalas’ moment of reckoning

A few days ago, Hong Kong banned some products of Indian spice brands MDH and Everest, and Singapore ordered a recall—both because these products were found to have a cancer-causing pesticide. Now, India’s food-safety authority will reportedly do its own tests.

It’s quite probable that, like with most other things, consumers will forget this sooner than later. But even a momentary setback, particularly the regulatory fallout, will make FMCG majors rethink their newfound love for masala brands.

In 2020, ITC acquired Sunrise for US$260 million, and two years later, Dabur bought 51% of Badshah for US$70 million. Last year, Tata Consumer and Wipro Consumer Care were rumoured to be in talks to gobble up Aachi. There’s no other product category within FMCG in which a bunch of deep-pocketed suitors have pursued a handful of brands in recent times.

As I wrote last year, “While it may not be easy selling a masala brand popular in the eastern region to consumers in the south when they have their own local favourites, companies like ITC can at least put it on the shelves of tens of thousands of stores across the country right away. And suddenly, it’s no longer a regional brand.”

But nothing makes you immune to a regulatory curveball.

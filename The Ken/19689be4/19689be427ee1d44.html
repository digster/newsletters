In the last couple of months, I’ve taken to supplementing my dinners of dal and rice with a certain brand’s high-protein chips. I’m no fitness freak—it’s more about the crunch and the flavours. But hey, if the packaging says it has 10 grams of protein, it’s one step up from junk, right?

As it turns out, I’m not the only one who thinks this way. If anything, plenty of others take it even further.

Most Indian traditional foods are low on protein and nearly two-thirds of households are deficient in it. So when it starts sneaking into chips and peanut butter? Fair enough.

But you know what else comes laden with protein now? Parathas, kulfis, and even water. Does anyone else feel like it’s starting to get just a little bit ridiculous?

More than that, some of those muscle-fuel munchies may not be all that good for you. Just take a look at the ingredients list.

So, just how much of the high-protein food movement is about a real gap in the market versus brands selling a buzzword at a premium? Pranathi dives into this question in her story today that’s rich with data, science, and insightful graphics. Don’t miss it, especially if you’re a gym rat.

Who will be the next Blusmart?

That’s the question hosts Praveen Gopal Krishnan and Rohin Dharmakumar discuss with Vikas Bardia, co-founder and CEO of Shoffr, and Arpit Agarwal, investment partner at Blume Ventures, in the latest episode of Two by Two.

Blusmart used to represent success, scale, and customer love—a reputation secured as an insurgent against incumbents like Uber and Ola. At its height, Blusmart had around 8,000 cabs in Delhi and Bengaluru—hardly a small number. But now, things are tumbling down.

Tune in to today’s episode to understand what happens next, and who—if anyone—can become the next Blusmart.

Eighty-thousand complaints a month is bad for a business of any size. But if your product is hardware, it’s probably the worst nightmare you could dream of. 

For Ola Electric, this is reality. 

Pressure has been mounting on all fronts, whether that’s irate customers showing up at its service centres or the onslaught of grievances flooding social media.

It also hasn’t helped that its mercurial founder and CEO Bhavesh Aggarwal got into a social-media spat with a standup comic who is quick with his comebacks. All that’s done is put a brighter spotlight on the enormous number of customer complaints Ola Electric is having to deal with. 

For a public company, which Ola Electric has been for the past four months, it’s hard to understate the importance of public perception—especially if you’re a commodity or service people directly interact with on a daily basis. The best practice generally is: acknowledge you are having trouble, promise you will fix it, show that you are fixing it, and then hope that things turn around before too many quarters go by. 

Well, not many of those things have happened. 

There’s been a lot of noise about how “Ola Electric’s stock will fall”. But volatility is part and parcel of the stock markets, and all the markets really tell you is which direction the tides are flowing. 

Which is why my co-host Rohin Dharmakumar and I thought this would be a great time to scratch beneath the surface and look at the big picture in our latest Two by Two podcast. Just what is happening at Ola Electric? What are the options before it? And how is India’s broader electric two-wheeler space shaping up? 

For our guests, we got two people who could really help us make sense of what’s going on: Jinesh Gandhi, Research Director at Ambit, who has more than 20 years of experience tracking multiple sectors, and Narayan Sundararaman, an accomplished leader with over 28 years of experience in marketing strategy. Narayan has worked at Cadbury, Star TV, and is the ex-CMO of Bajaj Auto.

Just one snag. Both of them were based in different cities. But we made it happen. And what we got out of it was a great discussion that’s worth a listen.

The early-mover advantage?

Here’s an interesting thing to chew on, Ola Electric wasn’t actually the first large-scale EV manufacturer from India. In fact, they were late to the race. Hero Electric, Ather Energy, TVS Motor Company, and Bajaj Auto (which is now making massive headway with Chetak EV) all launched e-scooters a long time before Ola Electric.

What Ola Electric did do well was get the word out about its product and make EV scooters mainstream. So they became an integral part of the conversation.

Jinesh: The first mover in electric two-wheelers was Hero Electric, which is now under 1% market share from being half of the market, say, five, six years back, whereas Ola, in the current set of OEMs, was the second-last OEM to launch electric two-wheelers.

It’s more of an early-mover advantage. And I would say that advantage comes with the risk of ensuring that you do not goof up on some of the critical aspects.

Because while this is a tech-oriented product, ultimately, it is a utility product for the customers. It is not an app or software which you can change very rapidly.

So for a utility-based product, first mover or early mover advantage definitely helps. It counts a lot if you’re able to get everything right.

The point in case being Hero Splendor or Honda Activa—people go by word of mouth on those products.

A relationship that starts after you’re done selling

As we can see with what’s happening at Ola, getting ahead of the curve early isn’t enough on its own. You need to keep working on the experience. No level of vertical integration and connected experiences is going to be the answer if your overall customer experience is bad. 

After all, internal combustion vehicles worked just fine, so the idea is to offer something better. This is not to suggest that ICE manufacturers have a stellar track record at after-sales service, but they mostly ensure that customers are heard and their issues are resolved as quickly as possible.

Narayan had a few things to say about how legacy companies have attained scale and sustained it, and why they pour so much money into building out both the acquisition and distribution sides in parallel.

Narayan: It depends from category to category, industry to industry, but whether it is tech or not tech, I think a first-mover advantage is definitely something to, it’s great to have in your pocket.

However, building a business over the long term requires you to do two things.

One is, you need to acquire customers.

So a first-mover advantage certainly helps in acquisition of customers, because at any given point in time, there are always early adopters—people who will be at the cutting edge of adoption of something new.

But I think, equally, you also need to show capability to retain those acquired customers, as well as to signal to the market that you have that power to keep things going. If there’s a problem with a customer, you sort it out really quickly.

If it’s an app, there’s not much at stake. But if you’re buying what I call an asset—and these are all asset categories, two wheelers, four wheelers, air conditioners—it means that people have parted with a substantial sum of money, it’s a big thing for them.

In these cases, I think it’s not enough to just be damn good at acquisition.

I think your long-term survival and doing well depends on your ability to maintain those relationships, sustain them, service them.

It’s actually two aspects, there is acquisition and retention.

Rohin, for his part, drew an analogy with the triathlon. You might be a great sprinter, but then, you have to swim in the open sea, and then bicycle. It’s not a linear race. How you sustain your pace when the obstacles keep shifting becomes the real question.

Ola Electric’s lasting competitive advantage?

Something that most companies are eventually forced to do, either when they hit a user base ceiling or competition seriously heats up, is expand their product portfolio and diversify offerings. But the automotive industry requires a lot of capex, and that’s one thing Ola doesn’t have a lot of. With changing market sentiment, even raising capital through private investors can be a bit of a hassle.

Narayan: What I would look for first is product portfolio.

This is a fast-evolving market, and (whether) you call them use cases, or functionality, whatever it is, there is more to be explored.

One thing is clear—everyone who is in need of buying vehicles is thinking about going electric.

And the large belly of the market, which is what everybody is eyeing, is the commuter customer. Be they riding scooters or 100–125cc motorcycles, that commuter customer is the big fish. 

Now, there are various use cases that exist, so (you need) a product portfolio that covers a lot of it—whether it’s different kinds of range… essentially different catering to different audiences.

So, for example, one of the advantages of the scooter is that everybody in the family can drive one, whereas a motorcycle is solely for men. So that finds a certain resonance in some parts of the country more than others, depending on, you know, the extent of female participation in the workforce, for example, or education levels.

So I would look for a product portfolio, which means R&D capability, which means, you know, what’s in the pipeline…

Ola Electric still maintains its number one position in terms of sales, largely on the back of the discounts it offered during the festive season. But competitors are catching up, and fast. So taking a bet on what they’ve already promised might be the way to go.

There is, of course, a lot else that we ended up discussing during the episode—including a few other ways Ola could consider diversifying its business, interesting business models they’d considered, and much more.

Good Morning Ishan,

When people find out I’m originally from Bangalore, they often ask me why I choose to live in a city like Delhi. And while I have a lot of love for my adopted home, the excuses have started to sound a little thin of late. 

How does one explain voluntarily living in 48°C weather? Or the subsequent 60% humidity of a bafflingly non-committal monsoon. Or its follow up—smog. And then, just when the air is fresh enough for one to leave home again, the final season—allergies.

Today, in the midst of a short jaunt back to Bangalore, I’m able to look past the city's traffic snarls. I can find beauty in its infrastructural inability to deal with even the slightest drizzle. Heck, I’ve even developed a kinship with the auto rickshaw drivers who demand one-and-half times the actual fare no matter what the destination. Everything is beautiful when you aren’t suffering one of the many torturous avatars of the Delhi summer.

But as much as I’m glad to be back in Bangalore, I’ve been forced to contend with a depressingly unavoidable truth—change is the only constant. The root of this realisation was something seemingly innocuous—a sandwich from a neighbourhood food joint. One that hadn’t failed me over 14 years, but one I could no longer recognise. 

Looking around me, it was evident. It wasn’t just this cardboard-flavoured sandwich—my hometown had moved on while I was away. And even the old faithfuls that survived the cull of Mother Time had lost a bit of themselves along the way.   Wither and die, survive and stagnate, or evolve and thrive. These seem to be the three options on the table for most things, and this holds true for the subjects we covered this week.

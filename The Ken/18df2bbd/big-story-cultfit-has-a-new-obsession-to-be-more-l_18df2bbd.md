Good Morning Ishan,
The nine lives of Cure.fit—rather, Cult.fit—is one way to sum up our Big Story today.
Set up as the omni-healthcare provider—with fitness, nutrition, mental health, and medical diagnostics services—Cure.fit soon realised its different units looked better on the initial business plan.
One by one, the businesses were heavily scaled down or spun off, except for the gym service. The company shed its name Cure.fit; it became Cult.fit and switched its mission statement from “make health easy” to “active lifestyle for everyone”.
Transition has been a constant theme at the company founded by Mukesh Bansal, well-known as the fashion portal Myntra’s co-founder. Cult.fit is doing well in its offline gym business, and it raised a fresh round of $10.2 million earlier this week, which valued the seven-year-old company at nearly $1.5 billion.
With an eye on public listing, Cult is onto its next transition—to become a Decathlon through Cult.sport. It’s even started opening outlets—two of the three in Bengaluru are located close to Decathlon’s sprawling stores.
Is its Decathlon dream a distraction, its destination, or yet another transition? Nathan has a meticulously reported [story](https://the-ken.com/story/cult-fit-has-a-new-obsession-to-be-more-like-decathlon/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter).
India put faces to its crewed space flight and named the four astronauts on Tuesday. The low-earth-orbit flight next year is as much a technology (and ambition) demonstration as it is a precursor to Isro’s plan to send astronauts to the moon by 2040.
In August, India pulled off a controlled landing on the lunar surface, only the fourth nation to do so.
The second space race is truly on. I’m tempted to refresh my memory on how the first moon race—a nail-biting competition between the Soviet Union and the United States—captured people’s imagination and inspired a whole generation to study science and tech. Economist Mariana Mazuccato has documented the spillover benefits of that programme. Inspired by it, she wrote a second book, Mission Economy, which argues how the public sector can use such missions to rally resources.
But Isro hasn’t had much luck attracting mainstream young talent. The agency runs its own space science and technology institute. And though it proactively works with 300+ small and large companies, there’s no private space ‘industry’ as such.
To that end, last week, India approved 100% foreign direct investment in the space sector.
Fly me to the moon, Frank Sinatra!

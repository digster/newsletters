For many people in developing coastal countries, the ocean holds promise.
A young person can commit to a life at sea, pick up specialised skills, and steadily rise through the ranks. For some, this is a path to eventually command their own crew.
Filipinos who commit to the training before they work on ships know what’s ahead of them—a demanding environment and weeks or months away from home. But there’s also the promise of a steady income. To get there, would-be seafarers take on a load of courses and soak up all the knowledge needed to keep a ship (and perhaps its payload) in tip-top shape.
But the way Filipinos have been trained to join ship crews has left these cadets short-changed. There’s a lot to unpack, however, the main takeaway is that training schools in the Philippines have been cutting back on what their courses cover.
This leads to two consequences: graduates aren’t equipped with the same skill set as their counterparts from overseas, and seafarers need to fork over additional tuition for extra classes to attain the appropriate level of professional training.
A more serious effect is that Filipino seafarers may be banned from European ports and waters. For a country that supplies 700,000 of the world’s 1.9 million seafarers, that’s a significant problem.

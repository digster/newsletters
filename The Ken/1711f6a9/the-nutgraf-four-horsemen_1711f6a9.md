In Christian theology, the harbinger of doom is symbolised by Four Horsemen, representing pestilence, war, famine, and death.
To be clear, I don’t think we are at the end of the world (even if it feels like it while reading the news), but I did think of the Four Horsemen when I was writing this newsletter.
A lot happened after India announced a complete lockdown across the nation earlier this week. Different states interpreted the measure differently. Confusion arose. Which also led to violence. Now, India is a big country, and historically, it isn’t really known for strong, efficient administration, so some of this was inevitable.
The broad view is that the decision to impose a complete lockdown across the nation—one of the most expansive measures taken by any country—is a good idea. India has taken swift, decisive, sweeping, and even radical measures in the past. An announcement is made. Everyone listens. The majority obey.
The question is...what next?
The answer: supply chains.
Supply chains are complicated things. Fundamentally, it’s a system of processes, people, and organisations involved in the distribution of a commodity, a product or a service. To make supply chains work, things need to go from one place to another in an efficient way.
India finds itself in a position where it has to manage not one or two or three but four supply chains. Arguably more than and at a scale that surpasses every nation in the world, barring China. Never before in history has India been forced to do this, not even in wartime.
In fact, one could reasonably argue, it has traditionally struggled to get even one right.
Now it has to solve four.
In a few weeks.
In this edition, I’ll try to explain what this means.
The supply chain of money
There are two ways to control money. Through fiscal policy and monetary policy. Fiscal policy is done by the government through spending. Monetary policy is done by the central bank through interest rates. At times of crisis, both are usually invoked.
Here’s what India did.
- From a fiscal standpoint, it announced a Rs 1,70,000 crore (~$22 Billion) fiscal package, mostly aimed at low-income households and the poor. It did this through a combination of direct cash transfers, food security measures, and fuel subsidies.
- From a monetary perspective, the Reserve Bank of India (RBI) unveiled a series of measures to boost liquidity by changing interest rates. As a result, it expects to inject close to Rs 3,74,000 crore into the banking system.
I’ll say this. These aren’t entirely bad ideas. No, the problem isn’t the size of the fiscal or monetary measures.
It’s in the transmission.
Take the fiscal stimulus. What the fiscal measures have done is focus on the most vulnerable sections of India’s society, ensuring they don’t starve. And to do this, it has used its existing schemes.

While large companies may be encouraged to protect the incomes of their workers, they have little influence over their downstream supply chains. In order to support informal sector workers, a limited-term basic income scheme must be introduced.
Putting cash in the hands of people not only spurs demand for goods and services but has a significant effect in buoying public sentiments. The finance minister’s proposals in this area are relatively small, bordering on the insignificant—there is no real increase in the budgets allocated for either pensions for senior citizens and widows, and the announcements made for SHGs (self help groups).
RBI’s monetary policy is another example of this. I’m not going to go into the details of repo rate and reverse repo rate, but essentially what the RBI has done is make it unattractive for banks to park money with the RBI.
The assumption here is that if the banks can’t keep the money with the RBI, they will have no choice but to lend.
This puts the ball in the bank’s court. It’s on them to transmit these gains to the end-user. All this while bank depositors are withdrawing money at an [unprecedented rate](https://link.the-ken.com/ls/click?upn=sy6ExvMQ-2BqIqW2Qsf-2FVG5m7bczKA1MiN-2BipzBwhvsCpOZUoCMMYjMAHDTeQr40X-2FW-2F2TaI3G5pxp4Z85q0ZGGMEXxsrR7cY0EiGvqVs0ttvJohsb-2BlCvZpbF71EEm5pfqPTfGQy5iI4APBbMnEREAuUgDdsfEX36WNTqQ-2BrTCN1Grco2ZqhtuNP0UyPWpnO-2BYNb7ucDLqh8t87wPsWc3QA-3D-3Dfelo_dmDs81p1OM5gBGYvHfsMFvgp9PcMB6iZkwowmLx-2FVhwlHXbL5m6csiptGuneRwktW27c6ZwIBsjV67BZcfkOlPppVavmb9GVjXF4V-2FTqNpWXDA3K11MTwd0uZ4XCMeruoTzGaTEH9vW8nbwgYz-2Fr7gdOnxngEerAXvMwljd-2FUrfbmDIaS6H937xI2kc3swVMMydb34wn5PAvwwVjhc70rAeRUwUjo1fs7XPmXi2xP5cVByxT5lptR-2FSLzxX5tGnhS6J28UhTD3Xx70BCH3afVkyNizAlIQtmoTwZciBeq2UswTD5QxRcPjWimDgj8mj-2FORuUGg3iYNW2pv5OsOHgbL2O7gxJhwerTvY0lYpmpFn8yv-2B296rkdtrLEbRgnwln2IdabVrutaR-2F8wrx8ghBMOxmPuVFs0nHAAg59l-2BQ3B61jk02olhGcq0zwOLVPD-2Fi).

The supply chain of food
I’ll start with the good news.
India will have its highest stockpiles of foodgrain reserves this year. By all accounts, there’s enough to feed India’s poor for at least another 18 months. Through a combination of a great harvest last year and some decent planning, reserves are not a problem. There’s enough food.
Getting the food to those who need it is another matter altogether.
And Covid-19 is going to make that complicated.
As an example, take Karnataka, one of India’s largest states.
On Monday, when the lockdown was in place, Karnataka’s State government announced that Indira Canteens—which are located across the state, would provide food for free to daily wage workers and poor people.
A day after he announced free food for the urban poor at Indira Canteens during the COVID19 outbreak, Chief Minister BS Yediyurappa made a u-turn. Pointing out that crowding increases the risk of transmission, the CM on Tuesday ordered a lockdown of the canteens, without providing an alternative for the poor.
“We saw crowding at Indira Canteens after announcement of free food. At times of an epidemic, we cannot allow it. It’s impossible to keep it open. We will have to make changes in our announcement. Till March 31, people will have to bear with it,” the CM said when asked about what those who cannot afford to buy food are to do.
It’s one thing to want to provide food.
It’s another to actually do it.
This isn’t an easy problem to solve. Not at Indira Canteens. Not at ration shops across the country.
The supply chain of people
Both India and China share a common facet that makes Covid-19 much more dire and deadly.
Both countries have a high share of migrant workers. Unlike the US, which is a bit more local, China and India have a vast share of people who live in rural areas and move to cities to find work.
How many?
As many as 470 million in India.
That’s one of the biggest reasons Covid-19 spread so rapidly out of Wuhan.
And it’s also a big problem for India.
A new humanitarian crisis may be unfolding in the country. Tens of thousands of desperate people, mainly migrant labourers reduced to joblessness by the lockdown and with little or no money or food, have been streaming out of India’s major cities for the past few days, trying to walk hundreds of kilometres to reach their homes in small towns and villages.
These tides of men, women and children, driven by the uncompromising arithmetic of survival and fear of contracting the coronavirus, pose a new challenge to the government’s all-out attempts to contain the spread of the disease.
Transporting people across the country has never been as important as it is today in India.
But how does one do it without risking transmission of the virus?

A chance entry into the business, a decade of slog, a near-death experience, a national monopoly, an enviable seat next to the Chinese global monopolistic play, and now, a sudden turn in the local market that could eat into its turf.

It's the stuff of business legends.

India's only solar glass manufacturer, Borosil Renewables, has a 40% domestic market share—the rest is imports—and is the largest non-Chinese producer of solar glass in a world where the Chinese command 97% of the market.

And, it has seen a 350% rally in its share price in the past year despite the threats to its market share.

Solar glass is a specially coated, patterned glass that lets only a minimal amount of heat through it. It's a tough market to operate in owing to technical challenges, low returns, and cheaper imports. Even global glass majors such as St Gobain had to shut down their solar plants. But Borosil held on.

Now when the government is laying the red carpet for local manufacturers—thanks to its gigantic renewable energy requirement this decade—and big guns like Reliance Industries and the Adani Group are entering solar manufacturing, Borosil once again needs to hold on.

In fact, the company has a history of fighting for its market. It held on to its business despite the stiff competition from cheaper Chinese and Malaysian imports, lobbying for the imposition of import tariffs.

"My great grandfather had applied for a licence to make paper but got a licence to make glass. He had no clue about how to make glass. He was a broker, but he said, 'why not?'", CEO Shreevar Kheruka, and the fourth generation of the family to run the business, tells Shruti Sonal, our newest writer.

"Competition has come late, but it will make us stronger. Without Rafael Nadal and Novak Djokovic, I don't think Federer would have been as good."

Good Morning Ishan,
Ever doomscrolled at 2 a.m., staring at headlines about mass layoffs and economic downturns? Or been algorithmically fed a steady diet of other forms of fear and rage? Congratulations! You’ve seen AI doing exactly what it was designed to do: optimise your anxiety for maximum engagement.
Jo Aggarwal wants to use AI to do the opposite of that.
Especially because mental health care in itself is broken. Everywhere. Too few therapists, too little insurance coverage, and waitlists that stretch for months. Tech hasn’t fixed it either. The internet is littered with the ghosts of mental-health startups, and there’s still no Uber or Airbnb for therapy.
But Aggarwal thinks Wysa might be the closest thing to a breakthrough.
As the founder of one of the world’s most clinically validated AI mental-health apps (it found a mention on Bill Gates’ blog a few months ago), Aggarwal has spent years figuring out what works. And now, with her acquisition of April Health in the US, she might have cracked the business model, too.
When Wysa started in 2016, most AI chatbots were little better than IVR systems, but Aggarwal soon realised something: people open up to AI three times faster than to humans. The challenge? No VC wanted to fund AI that helped people—they wanted apps that kept users hooked. So Wysa took a different path: clinical research, FDA approvals, and embedding itself inside healthcare systems.
Now, she’s going even further.
In this sharp, candid conversation with Seema, Aggarwal explains:
- Why mental health is now the #1 reason for unemployability—and how Wysa is turning self-care into a workplace skill
- How 60% of US mental-health referrals go unfulfilled—and why AI-powered care managers could fix that
- Why Gen Z’s biggest challenge isn’t AI taking their jobs—it’s mental resilience in a world where traditional career paths are disappearing
- The economics of mental health in India
The problem isn’t just access—it’s design. The mental-health system is a puzzle because it’s not built to handle scale or affordability. Wysa is betting that AI, blended with human care, could be the missing piece.

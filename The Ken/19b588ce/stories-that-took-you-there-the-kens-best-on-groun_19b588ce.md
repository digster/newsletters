Good Morning Ishan,
One of the tenets that all writers try to abide by at The Ken is to show the reader, not tell.
Even on-the-nose business stories strive to rely on facts and reportage to make a point. This year, we pushed the “show” boundary within our lean team. As a career journalist, I’d say that’s also the best way to remain relevant and vital.
Mryunmayee hit the road in December to understand what it takes to build a tech city at a time when southern states are jostling with each other to take the top spot. I asked her about her experience.
The first thing she did before planning a trip to Tirupati was scope out its Starbucks/Third Wave Coffee scene.
Coming from a Bengaluru perspective, it can be very hard to imagine a tech hub without the network-y cafes, she says. “Mysore has them. Hubballi has several Cafe Coffee Day outlets. Where else would you go to work on pitch decks and schmooze with founders?”
And yet. The Starbucks on Tiruchanoor road, just 20 minutes from the Balaji temple, felt to her more aspirational than a networking hub. There were couples on dates. Families with kids excited about mocha frappucinos overloaded with caramel. Not a single laptop in sight.
That, more than the residential buildings converted to tech offices, showed her how this was a very different kind of tech city. The cafes, the one bar, the Zudio—they weren’t built to pull in techies from the big cities.
They showed up because local people had better jobs and could spend more on nice things. Which, arguably, is the entire point of expanding tech beyond metros, she says.
When analysts and government officials speak of building tech cities, they talk about transforming them. They promise gleaming towers, apartments with a pool, maybe even a nightlife. “And yet when I asked people in Hubballi, in Tirupati, even in Mysuru what they would want from a tech city, they simply said, ‘a job, and good money’,” she says.
“As I went from Mysuru to Tirupati, travelling on surprisingly smooth tier-2 roads, I could see that maybe transformation might be the wrong benchmark altogether,” she says.

Early in December, India finally removed the shackles that held back the textile sector and laid out key reforms, including GST tweaks that’d fix tax distortions. With this move, the country also lobbed the ball into the garment makers’ court, to upgrade their mills, designs, and fabrics.
When Valli Vikram made a few trips to Tiruppur, Tamil Nadu, in late October, she was in for a shock.
“I carried the usual stereotype about its factories with me: dimly lit sheds, endless looms and machines, workers bent under stifling heat, efficiency wrung out at the cost of exhaustion. The kind of factory that produces clothes cheaply by making people invisible,” she said.
What she found instead looked uncannily like a college campus.
At Best Corporation, KPR Mills, and Perlei Textiles, factories were organised around sprawling lush green courtyards and wide corridors. There were auditoriums where workers rehearsed music and dance on weekends. Vast cafeterias buzzed at lunch. A quiet study room where employees were encouraged to prepare for graduate school exams. She also found several small cafés selling burgers and specialty snacks within the factory. There was even a sports ground where throwball tournaments ran monthly.
“Tiruppur is often described as a textile hub because of its machines, its scale, and export numbers. But walking through these factories, it became clear to me that its real infrastructure is human,” rued Valli.
One of the key reasons why the cluster endures. You can read her story to understand how garment makers are taking up the tariff challenge and the stakes therein.

Let me now take you to Mohali in Punjab.
The city with a 1.5 million population turned 50 on 1 November. Fast emerging as a seat of education, it’s also long viewed as a poor cousin of Chandigarh, the planned city. But Mohali now sprawls over 1,190 sq km, an area 10X the union territory.
As I grew restless in the cab in early October, spending more than an hour for an 8.5km drive from Plaksha University to the airport, the driver shared my vexation: “They (civic authorities) are putting up buildings wherever they can, but they are not building roads.”
Thrumming to a new beat, Mohali forms the perfect petri dish for a technology university that wants to train its students to be socially aware problem solvers. Inside the university, I found evidence of that approach—a systems-level curriculum for engineers so they don’t just go out and fling themselves on to the job they are hired for.

Not far from Mohali, in Noida and adjoining areas, Uttar Pradesh is cementing its spot in India’s manufacturing-hubs list. From nearly nothing about a decade ago, the state now contributes more than half the smartphones India manufactures, thanks to makers such as Samsung, Vivo, and Dixon having set up their bases there.
After a quick tour of the area, Priyal Mahtta wrote that UP’s triumph masks an underlying tension: all its industrial growth is concentrated in one belt—Noida, Greater Noida, and the Yamuna Expressway corridor—which makes up barely 2% of its expanse.
Since her story was published in early December, the UP government has announced three greenfield hubs for electronics manufacturing, in Pilibhit, Barabanki, and Gorakhpur. We hope to get you a story on the new hubs sometime in 2026, but for now, you can read Priyal’s story here.

Up north in Kashmir, specifically in the state’s saffron belt spanning Pulwama and Budgam districts, farmers are lamenting the declining production of one of the world’s most expensive spices.
Contributing writers Mehroob Mushtaq and Numan Bhat visited many fields that once took pride in their purple crop. For most agricultural crops, much of the value-addition happens outside the fields—during packaging, branding, and retailing. And that’s precisely where saffron is losing out.
[Kashmir’s saffron is priceless. For some, it’s increasingly worthless](https://the-ken.com/story/kashmirs-saffron-is-priceless-for-some-its-increasingly-worthless/?searchTerm=kashmir)
Back in south India, we scratched our itch to understand how Tata Electronics, a reliable vendor for Apple, was turning Trump’s tariff tyranny into an opportunity.
Its vast factory in Karnataka’s Narasapura Industrial Area is a strict no-go for journalists, but Srishti Achar hung around the factory to understand why the company was scaling like its life depended on it.
This excerpt from the story is telling:
Nearly 200 workers leave each day, lured away by better pay, lighter shifts, or simply the grind of factory life. But then, “at least 300 workers are also being hired for the factory every day,” a recruiter near the facility said, gesturing to a room with a few benches where women huddle over crumpled forms. The Narasapura hiring ecosystem is a small economy unto itself, with around 15 recruiters each pulling in about 20 workers a day.

We’ll close out this list with a trek to the 72-acre campus in Perundurai, a two-hour drive northeast of Coimbatore, that’s home to the household brand, at least in South India, Milky Mist.
When Seetharaman G drove down to meet the chief executive K Rathnam, little did he know that this would also be the year when the company would get the regulator’s nod for a Rs 2,000 crore IPO.
“I was in my hometown in the last week of December, and a story I had pursued for months began to come together when Rathnam, who is quite media-shy, agreed to meet me at the company’s headquarters and sole factory. Here is a company whose products I’m used to ordering on Blinkit and Bigbasket 1,200km away, in Mumbai, and I got to see the scale of its operations,” he said.
“Coincidentally, Milky Mist took shape a couple of hours from where I spent the first two decades of my life, but I was as intrigued by its rise as anyone,” he says.
In the largest milk-producing market, here’s a brand that aspires to be recognised nationally not for milk—it doesn’t sell milk—but for its value-added products. There are other peculiarities of Milky Mist. Read the story and find out.

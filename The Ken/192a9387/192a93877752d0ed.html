The Big Story

How rich Indians game the NRI quota in medical-college admissions

What was meant for aspiring doctors living abroad is now a gateway for academically weak locals willing to pay 5X the fee

At 5X the cost, NRI seats have been sold for years to rich, low-scoring aspirants. In some states, even their friends and family. Auctioned to the highest bidder in private medical colleges, the 15% seats reserved for the NRIs are no longer as desired with a plethora of options—including studying abroad. As vacancies pile up and seats are converted to merit seats, some states like Punjab tried to expand the definition, drawing the ire of even the Supreme Court. From admission agents promising to get NRI sponsors and sweet deals from colleges to blocking of seats by candidates, the NMC is trying hard to reign in the fraud in medical admissions. Tata Zudio makes a no-brainer entry into beauty products. Here’s why ‘anyone’ can 

Mamaearth, Mcaffeine, and Zudio have something in common: their manufacturers and a strong focus on marketing 

Tata Zudio is the newest entrant in the beauty and personal-care space joining other expanding D2C brands. The entry barrier to starting a skincare brand has lowered, with many relying on contract manufacturers for ready-made formulation. For many D2C players, bumping up sales means piling up products at distributors and showing big numbers to investors. But launching these brands is one thing, sustaining them is a whole other game. Deep dives

RBI is competing with its regulated entities—and killing competition

Account aggregators, fraud-protection startups, and invoice authenticators have been long-time lenders’ partners. But the RBI is disrupting the rhythm

The RBI’s Innovation Hub started with the philosophy of identifying gaps in the financial-services sector and creating relevant platforms. But with the launch of its first two products, it has caused serious unrest among fintechs and technology-service providers. The one concern all stakeholders are converging on is why the regulator is competing with market participants and killing competition. But even as players feel intimidated, the entity’s actions may just be for the good of the ecosystem. [Read the full story]

******

The Make in India paradox: how Kaynes Tech hit a $4.3B valuation by hiring fewer workers

The initiative is aimed at manufacturing locally and creating millions of jobs. But companies like Kaynes, an electronics maker, are happy doing one without the other

Electronics-manufacturing services sector is one of the key beneficiaries of the “Make in India” push, harbouring investments worth Rs 10,000 crores in four years. Domestic companies in the sector have been cashing in on the momentum, scaling their manufacturing capacities rapidly. While that opens several job opportunities, companies like Kaynes Technology are plugging it with automation and lateral hires. Sure it helps reduce the employee cost, but what happens to the promise of creating millions of jobs? [Read the full story]

This $800M Indian SaaS firm is winning over Microsoft and Cisco against global rival Walkme

Digital-adoption platforms are set for exponential growth soon and Whatfix is doing whatever it can to outdo its rivals

The digital-adoption-platform space is expected to balloon up to US$3.7 billion in size by 2032—growing at over 18% compound annual rate. India-based Whatfix raised US$125 million from the likes of Warburg Pincus recently, while its biggest competitor and market leader WalkMe was acquired by SAP for US$1.5 billion. Whatfix is reportedly poaching high-profile clients from WalkMe to get a bigger share of the DAP market. While WalkMe’s acquisition makes it easier for Whatfix to get more clients, it faces challenges in the form of smaller companies trying to copy the products. [Read the full story]

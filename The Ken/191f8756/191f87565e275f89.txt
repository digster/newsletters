Good Morning Ishan,

Greetings from a chilly fall morning in Washington D.C. Every week, I steal moments away from the busy work-week to check in with Indian edtech. And while the sector’s been unusually quiet, and funding hard to come by, there are always reliable characters who never disappoint. They’re holding the edtech fort down in India. 

One person born with main-character energy is Alakh Pandey. Since its inception, Physics Wallah (PW) has grown exponentially, and with it, Pandey’s reputation as a consummate teacher, mentor, and influencer. A large part of PW’s appeal comes from Pandey’s own salt-of-the-earth personal narrative: how can every child, regardless of opportunity, background, or economic conditions, make it to engineering or medical school? 

What this public image deftly hides, though, is the burgeoning business model that’s chugging along in the background. 

Once, a few years ago, Pandey spent an interview with The Ken lobbing all questions about growth and funding to Prateek Maheshwari, his co-founder. The dynamic seemed clear—Pandey would create the massive vision, Maheshwari would execute.

So when PW expanded internationally to the United Arab Emirates (UAE) last March, acquiring a local test-prep start-up called Knowledge Planet, one could have well asked: does this fit into Pandey’s vision to uplift the common Indian student? Or is this Maheswari’s master stroke?

The Byju’s-coded playbook

The UAE is a popular destination for Indian edtechs. Byju’s moved into the larger Middle East years ago, trying to offer its premium products to school students in cities like Dubai, Sharjah, and Abu Dhabi. The Indian diaspora there is sizeable and has more disposable income to spare for their child’s education than people in India. Byju Raveendran and his family have even, reportedly, shifted their base to Dubai.

But PW’s USP isn’t premium. Instead, the company has grown by offering its users free or heavily subsidised access to content. In its 2.0 avatar, PW offers paid products such as in-person coaching, but even these are aimed at students who couldn’t afford to pay for coaching earlier. So, a premium course for students in the UAE doesn’t really fit into the plan.

Unless, of course, you look at the maths.

It’s a great mini-pipeline. Come for after-school tuition, stay for the IIT coaching. In fact, the website lists courses under the “Foundation” (class 9 and 10) and “Pre-foundation” stages (class 8), and also offers combinations such as CBSE+JEE+NEET or just plain old CBSE+JEE.

Priced between 4,000 and 6,500 AED (US$1,090–1,770), these yearly courses could fetch PW in the ballpark of Rs 1 lakh each student. PW Gulf already reports having 7,000 students on its roster, so that’s an approximate annual earning of Rs 70 crore (US$8.3 million). This is a conservative estimate, too. 

PW’s whole pitch to UAE’s Indian students is that they should also get the same opportunities to prepare for entrance exams, just like their Indian counterparts. And through Knowledge Planet, PW has plans to expand https://gulfnews.com/technology/companies/edtech-physics-wallah-eyes-possibilities-beyond-indian-curricula-in-uae-1.1726141165266 its reach significantly.

The company is working with schools on awareness initiatives such as the Gulf Champions League, a scholarship test designed for CBSE students. It is also introducing School Integrated Programs (SIPs), allowing schools to partner with the company and offer competitive coaching to students within their campuses. Future plans include expanding the choices by introducing programs in coding, AI, and upskilling, aiming to cater to students following British, American, and national curricula.

The business motive dovetails with the public message

Pandey has found a way to reconcile his India-first message with this global expansion—he’s appealing to Indians everywhere to come back. In an address he gave to students in the US this April, Pandey urged https://www.moneycontrol.com/news/trends/physics-wallah-speaks-at-harvard-stanford-university-asks-indian-students-to-return-to-country-see-post-12709720.html Indian students to return and help make India a better country.

“Jai Hind. I was invited as a speaker at Harvard University, Stanford University and University of California. There, I motivated Indian students to come back to the country.

“Yes, our country has a lot of flaws but no country is perfect. The youth of the nation should work towards making it better.”

And for students who do take up Pandey’s appeal, there’s the brand-new startup school that PW has launched for budding entrepreneurs. 

Whatever the motivation may be, patriotism or hard-nosed business ambition, it seems all roads lead back to PW.

Over the last year, the Indian government has had a puzzle to solve—Covid-19. Ever since the virus first sank its claws into the country in March 2020, the government has amassed a truckload of puzzle pieces—in this case, Covid-19 data—that it must put together.  Data on vaccinations, infections, reinfections, breakthrough infections, etc. The synthesis of all this could help India avoid a third wave as deadly as the second one we're just getting past.

On 24 May, the government carved out a nine-member committee to do just this. The task, however, is an unenviable one. The committee must figure out how to bring together mountains of data that are currently scattered across government departments and ministries.

The disjointed and often incomplete data collection and sharing efforts have already spelt disaster for the country. For instance, experts trying to model India's second wave predicted a peak of 150,000 cases a day. They were off by 250,000 cases. One of the experts involved told The Ken that this inaccuracy was because they were only given access to outdated sero-survey data.

In other cases, the bureaucratic maze that scientists must wander to obtain information from the Indian Council of Medical Research has also hindered their work. With the data locked away, scientists don't even know what to ask for. “It’s like a restaurant asking you what you want to eat before letting you know whether or not it’s on the menu,” said one scientist. 

The whole situation reads like a tragicomedy.

Neck deep in an ocean of scattered information, can India finally get its act together? Maitri delves into this in today's story.

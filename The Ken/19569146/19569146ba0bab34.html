I’m a UPI app, and I think cash and ATMs are overdoing it. Neither are in as bad a shape as popular narratives would have you believe
By Rounak Kumar Gunjan
7 min read
View in browser

Good Morning Ishan,

You are reading our subscriber-only newsletter Ka-Ching!—a weekly commentary on fintech and personal finance

Don’t you get bored with Rounak’s weekly dispatches? The same old know-it-all, insufferable tone? Despite that, I would never discourage him from writing to you. Not because I like him. I don’t. I just quietly enjoy it when smart subscribers corner him in the comments. Haha!

You see, I’m quite familiar with sad faces. My entry to the big stage marked the end of a famous Indian hack—toffees for change, or confectionery as currency. Now those little sweets, with hopeful eyes, remain stacked inside boxes, to come out only when someone actually wants to buy them.

I’ve also been told I’m responsible for wallets suffering loneliness, currency notes feeling ignored, mobile phones being overworked, and personal expenditure budgets going for a toss.

If you haven’t guessed it already, I’m the dashing frontman to one of India’s most significant innovations of the past decade. I’m a UPI app on Rounak’s phone. And I’m interoperable, so it’s not important whether I’m Phonepe, Paytm*, Gpay, or any of the others. 

What matters is that this is my maiden attempt at writing. I’m tired of just saying “…your payment is successful…” I have more to say. Rounak is busy subscribing to the dumbphone movement—the latest antidote to smartphones—and is taking some time off screens, so I’ve hijacked his phone. 

Hopefully, I’ll get enough time to complete my defence.

The aggrieved

For many, I’m the ultimate villain. Just listen to the accusations flung about whenever a group of Indian currency notes assemble. All they talk about are their shrinking use cases and how UPI is the culprit.

I mean, last year, in this very newsletter, a Rs 500 note wrote a sob story about never seeing the light of day for years.

For instance, I’ve been inside Rounak’s wallet for more than three months now. But he finds it easier to unlock his phone, select his payments app, unlock the app, scan the QR code, and key in a third passcode, than just whip me out. Damn you, UPI!

Seriously? 

I can’t help it if I’m more efficient.

And it’s not just currency notes, try passing by an ATM at night, when it’s quiet outside. You can hear them complaining to the air conditioners. 

“Remember the time when there were two of you installed in my room to take care of me? When there were queues outside and our guards weren’t bored at work? When people took detours just to use our services? Now, they’re killing us because they think we’re just an unnecessary expense on the balance sheet.”

Honestly, the old cash dispenser may have a point here. So, I asked Father Bank—you folks call him the RBI—for some data. Apparently, between January 2024 and January 2025, India’s total ATM count went down by nearly 3,000 to 2,16,706. This includes, both onsite and offsite ATMs.

But hang on! Don’t start sympathising with ATMs already. You need to scan the data more closely to understand the real picture. (And scanning is a piece of cake for me!)

My redemption

What if I told you that India actually has ATMs that are thriving? You’d probably say I’m low on network because I just quoted a data point that proves otherwise, right?

Well, look closer.

Father Bank’s data provides a breakup of ATMs—onsite and offsite. ATM machines set up inside the premises of a bank branch are onsite; those set up on a standalone basis in other locations are offsite.

Overall ATM numbers are down. But as you can see, the number of onsite ATMs have only grown in the last five years. It’s the offsite ones that are being shut.

View in Browser

I recently heard Rounak talking to a banker, who explained to him why banks are choosing onsite ATMs over offsite ones:

“Banks across the board are expanding their branch network. They want more people to visit branches, more often. It helps them sell loans, deposits, and other services. And an integrated onsite ATM only adds to the number of such touchpoints. A standalone ATM offers nothing but cash [and a couple of related services]. So the stagnation of overall ATM numbers does not tell the full story.” According to a government release, the total number of bank branches has gone up from 1,17,990 in March 2014 to 1,60,501 in September 2024. 

See? Do you find UPI anywhere in that explanation? 

And I’m not done yet. Because I’m also going to debunk the claim that cash is somehow existentially threatened because of me.

Stop whining, cash

I read the newsletter edition where the Rs 500 note said it was supposedly gasping for air. Well, let’s look at the numbers, shall we? 

As per RBI, currency in circulation had risen 2.5% year-on-year as of February. In fact, between 14 February 2015 to 14 February 2025, this has been the growth in India’s currency circulation:

View in Browser

Despite UPI’s introduction in 2016 and the cash slump during demonetisation, circulation has consistently grown in the last ten years. 

Now, some may argue that currency in circulation is too broad a trend to conclude that I’m not cornering cash. But hang on, I’ve got more proof. 

I also scanned through reports from CMS Info Systems, one of India’s largest cash management companies, and a logistics and technology solutions provider to banks and financial institutions. 

In one of its reports for the financial year 2024, it says, “on a monthly basis, the average ATM cash withdrawals in FY24 were higher compared to FY23, with withdrawals in 10 out of 12 months exceeding the FY23 monthly average.” The monthly average in FY23 was Rs 1.35 crore. In FY24, the most recent year for which I found data, it rose to Rs 1.43 crore.

But the real question is, are Indians spending it or just hoarding it for emergencies?

An RBI paper released in July last year says that while “digital payments are growing rapidly, demand for cash still remains generally high.” And while UPI has eaten into market share as a preferred mode of payment, it’s nowhere near the major threat it’s portrayed to be—after all, according to the paper, 60% of all consumer expenditure was still cash based as of FY24. And the MSME industry has almost 80% cash usage, across demographies. You don’t see me whining because of it. 

All I’m trying to say is, why demonise me when we can co-exist? I hope this edition reaches reasonable ATMs and currency notes, and we can call a truce, if not a peace. 

For now, Rounak’s no-screen time is over, and it’s time for me to leave. But do try to reach me in the comments, or write to kaching@the-ken.com with your thoughts and suggestions.

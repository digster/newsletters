Good Morning Ishan,
Would you buy clothes the way you buy groceries on Blinkit, Swiggy Instamart or Zepto—expecting doorstep delivery in minutes, or a few hours at most? A few startups are betting that you would. Never mind that many still see “fashion quick commerce” as an oxymoron. Clothes, like coffee, are meant to be tried, tested, and savoured leisurely, not rushed, goes this line of thinking.
That has not stopped players such as Slikk, Knot, Newme Zip and Myntra’s M-Now from trying to make speed useful in fashion. The climb has been steep. Customers order multiple sizes or styles and return what does not work. Convenience is high, but so are the costs. Riders complete fewer deliveries than traditional quick commerce allows. Scale is key but elusive. Fundamental building blocks seem shaky in the try-and-buy fashion at your doorstep pitch. At least one early entrant has already shut shop.
Then a much larger, more seasoned player steps in. One for whom costly, venture-funded experimentation is not an option. Since November, Decathlon, the French sportswear giant with a decade-long presence in India, has been piloting two-hour deliveries across several cities. It first tested the waters through Blinkit. This time, it is doing so through its own channels.
Is this a game-changer? What is Decathlon really testing, and what is it actually selling? Is this a blueprint for others to follow, or will the contradictions of fast fashion at the doorstep catch up even with a disciplined retailer?

Dear Ishan, good morning to 2026! First Principles has now entered its fourth (calendar) year, thanks to you.

One of the recurring questions I get from non-journalists (sometimes even journalists, too) is a variation of “Are you sure you’ll have enough things to write about? Won’t you run out of unique topics or subjects to write upon?”

We got this when we started The Ken in 2016, when friends and subscribers asked us, “How will you find enough interesting stories to produce a new one every day?”

We got it when we started topical newsletters, for instance, on the second- and third-order effects of the pandemic. “After all, how many things are there to write about from a pandemic point-of-view?”

We got it when we started podcasts. “Are there enough interesting people for episodes? Are there enough interesting topics to create 2×2 maps for?”

It’s a fair question. I’d be lying if I said my colleagues or I conclusively knew the answer to those questions was a yes. Instead, my answer was always a mix of “I think so” and “We’ll figure it out as we go along.”

Which brings me to the First Principles newsletter. If you ask me today, “Will you have enough topics to write about each week for the next four years?” my answers would still be, “I think so,” and “We’ll figure it out as we go along.”

Back to 2026. We’ve been talking for enough time for you to (perhaps? I hope?) know that I don’t have any “new year’s resolutions”. Instead, there are things I am looking forward to spending more time on in 2026. Most of them come with no deadlines or milestones.

For instance, I want to become more conscious, more deliberate, more “leaned in” with music this year. In 2025, I restarted listening to music on a hi-fi stereo after nearly a decade and a half of putting up with lossy but convenient music. I might even take the leap back in time to vinyl!

On the fitness front, I’m planning to add callisthenics to my workout plan. My 2025 goal was to get stronger (which I did) while avoiding getting injured (which I did not). The sum total of my learning from the last year has been, “Tendons, tendons, tendons!”

On the technology front, I’m starting the year by switching to a new browser, Vivaldi, after decades of trusting Firefox. The corrosive influence of AI is why I’m switching.

On the personal ergonomics front, I trained myself to use a mouse with my left hand (I’m naturally right-handed) and cured myself of both wrist and shoulder pain. I’m now back to right-handed mouse usage for the next three to six months, after which I intend to flip again.

Back to January. It is a tricky month, perhaps the trickiest even. Fundamentally, we know nothing has changed from “December 31” to “January 1”. And yet, so much has.

The weather in Bengaluru already feels warmer. The trees feel sepia-tinted as their leaves start yellowing, preparing to shed themselves. The roads feel dustier and drier.

In workplaces, including at The Ken, we have transitioned from merry, holiday vibes to nervous, cautious plans.

All because of the Gregorian calendar? It’s funny.

It’s a trap I’ve walked into for most of my working life. I know I should be doing a better job of ideating, reflecting, and planning in December so that I can start January fully prepped.

But December being December, I end up enjoying it more than using it. Which means I start January on the defensive. It’s like you sleepwalk from the end of one year into the beginning of the next, and when you open your eyes, you’re already a week into it!

“Oh damn! Just 3 more weeks left for January. And I’m already late on the things I was supposed to do. Not to mention the ideas and plans about the rest of the year! Damn. Damn. Damn!”

I call this the January chasm. For years, I’d muddle through the early half of January, guilty for not having done a better planning job in December, and then muddle through the second half of January trying to catch up, and compress both planning and doing into a few weeks. By February, I was so thoroughly unhappy with the compromises I’d made that I’d lost the excitement of a new year and new plans.

There are few feelings I hate more than beginning things with an anxious and defensive mindset instead of a hopeful and cheerful one.

So, this year I decided to use January to plan better for the rest of the year. I reset my personal 2026 “clock” to start ticking from February 1.

How do you manage to cross the January chasm?

1. The January Chasm

The December-January chasm is trickier in more ways than one. Because when we’re in resolution-making mode (in December or early January), we tend to think long-term. We are more rational and less hurried. We think of ideal states. We dream of possibilities.

But once we’re past the first few weeks and in the thick of action, we are short-termers. In our minds, we let go of the abstract and of the possibilities to deal with the daily here-and-now.

New Year’s Resolutions are decisions that focus on the “big picture” of our lives. When we make these resolutions, we are in an abstract mindset. Decisions made in this mindset almost always favor long-term benefit (e.g. losing weight) or short-term cost (e.g. not eating the chocolate cake).

In our day-to-day lives, however, our decisions tend to veer toward the immediate. When we make daily decisions we are in a concrete mindset. Decisions made in this mindset often favor short-term benefit (e.g. this tastes great) over long-term cost (e.g. this will make me fat).

Turning Resolutions into Reality by Marshall Goldsmith (HBR, 2008)

The problem, at least for me, is the mismatch between the relatively open-ended, ambitious and hopeful futures I want to create for myself versus the ticking clocks, deadlines and calendars that I have no control over.

Take, for instance, your desire to learn a new skill or find a new job. The moment you attach deadlines or milestones to these, you invariably kick off the process of disappointing yourself and losing motivation.

Every week or every month you “lose” makes you more defensive and self-critical. You switch to playing catch-up. You rejig the deadlines. You shift the goalposts. You question your original assumptions.

In short, you start spending more mental bandwidth thinking about the meta-aspects of what you want instead of doing it. The joy starts disappearing. It starts to feel like a chore.

At some point, a few years ago, I think, I stepped back and saw how ridiculous this pattern was. Deadlines and calendars were actively dampening my most ambitious and hopeful dreams. Would it really be so bad if I did things a few months or even a few years too late?

One of the best articulations of this quandary I’ve read is by Abbie J. Shipp, a professor at Texas Christian University. She argued that the conflict is really between objective time and subjective time.

Whereas objective time focuses on the clock and calendar as a measure of time external to individuals, subjective time brings in the internal, personal experience of time. In this way, subjective time reflects how people perceive, interpret, and mentally travel through time, using memories and forecasts to make sense of the present.

…

Deadlines are another example of a subjective interpretation of objective time. Many deadlines we perceive as “real” are anything but. Instead, deadlines are socially constructed dates to plan one’s work and synchronize with others. I found that completing a task by a certain date or responding to a request within a certain timeframe was often motivated by arbitrary deadlines. Like the classic metaphor of which balls you can drop and which ones will bounce back, I began to move or even eliminate some deadlines without losing my commitment to others, creating an immediate boost to my level of engagement.

The key takeaway is that time is not as objective as we might believe. Time is primarily a subjective interpretation, making time management incomplete unless we also highlight the subjective constructions of time we live and create.

My Fixation on Time Management Almost Broke Me by Abbie J. Shipp

Read her full article.

2. Reading list 2026

Hello Ishan, this is Tanim, and welcome back to the First Principles books section. 

New year, new possibilities, and a whole new year of reading ahead. Here’s the reading list for 2026, if you are up for it. 

I am looking forward to all the books you are going to read and recommend—and of course, all the books I’ll be reading and sharing with you. 

View in Browser

For the first theme, I have decided to read Creative Confidence by Tom Kelley and David Kelley. To be honest, if I was part of a reading Book Club, I might have skipped this one. But hear me out. I consciously selected it to push myself toward a book that might help me professionally or personally. And since the new year always feels like a fresh start, I thought now would be the perfect time. 

And believe me, it took some scouring to find this one. I was even tempted to use AI at one point, but I resisted. Instead, I searched on Reddit, Medium, Goodreads, Storygraph, and almost every other site that looked interesting. 

I could have easily picked a book on writing, something I know I enjoy, but I wanted to explore a bit more before giving up. 

That’s when I randomly came across Creative Confidence on Google while travelling on the metro. I am not entirely sure what pulled me in. Maybe it was my subconscious love for alliteration. But it led me to the concept of design thinking, which apparently the book is about. 

I want to leave a little mystery for myself, and so I will read it without googling what design thinking is and then read the definition after. I have a feeling it might eventually help me think about writing, too, but at least that’s not the main focus of the book. 

Hope you have a fun and productive reading year! 

But before you go, if you have something to share that you read over the holidays, we would love to feature it in our First Principles community recommendations.

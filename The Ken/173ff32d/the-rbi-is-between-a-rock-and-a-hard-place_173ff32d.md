Shaktikanta Das, the governor of the Reserve Bank of India (RBI), may have just ruffled a few feathers in the central government in the past couple of weeks.
On 6 August, at the monetary policy meeting, the RBI decided to hold interest rates at 4% and not cut them further. And last week, the RBI transferred (just) Rs 57,128 crore (US$7.6 billion) as dividends to the government.
Here is the catch-22 for the RBI.
Interest rate cuts and transfers of money from the RBI are important to the government. Why?
1. The Indian economy (gross domestic product) is expected to face its first contraction in four decades. Lower interest rates are expected to spur companies to borrow money, spend more, and stimulate economic growth.
But there is another reason why interest rates matter to the government.
As a part of their mandatory liquidity requirements, banks invest in government bonds, which can quickly be converted into cash. But with risk appetite being low, they’re not lending money. Instead, they’ve invested the excess in government bonds. And interest rate movements affect the valuation of these government securities.
When interest rates go down, bond prices go up, and vice versa. So, when interest rates are cut, the value of the government bonds held by public sector banks (PSBs) goes up. The PSBs make profits, which at least optically improves their capital base. This means that the government can get away with a lower recapitalisation amount to prop up the struggling banks. And, instead, focus on more productive uses of money during these testing times.
This is exactly what former deputy governor of RBI, Viral Acharya explains in his [new book](https://www.livemint.com/industry/banking/the-repo-rate-cut-cycles-help-psbs-shore-up-capital-11596985509931.html), Quest for Restoring Financial Stability in India.
2. As for why more dividend or transfer from reserves is important, the government is facing a shortfall in revenue. Its divestments of stake from state-run companies could fall behind schedule and it has had to increase its borrowing to Rs 12,00,000 crore (US$160.5 billion) from the initial estimate of Rs 7,80,000 crore (US$104.3 billion) for the year. This means that India’s debt to GDP ratio, which is at 72% at present, is [likely to rise to 87%](https://www.deccanherald.com/business/dh-deciphers-what-is-public-debt-why-is-the-government-borrowing-more-866088.html) by the end of financial year 2021.
So, one way is to draw out more cash from the central bank and not worry about borrowing externally and maintain the credit rating in global markets.
So why isn’t the RBI cutting rates or transferring more money to the government?
1. Since February 2019, the RBI has cut interest rates by a cumulative 2.25% to 4%. But with India's retail inflation at 6.93% in July, it means that real interest rates are negative. So, depositors’ money in the bank, after inflation and taxes, is losing value.
This theoretically means that if depositors don’t see value in bank deposits, they might spend more instead; but more importantly for the RBI, inflation is overshooting the upper band of their inflation target of 6%.
As per the RBI Act, the monetary policy committee is deemed to have failed if retail inflation remains outside the mandated 2-6% range for three consecutive quarters. And while lower rates will be needed to improve sentiment and growth, the RBI doesn’t want to fail at inflation targeting and then have to explain why it failed.

Good Morning Ishan,
 
When the lockdown was first imposed in March last year, I reluctantly began yoga classes via Zoom. I’d never enjoyed it much, compared to kickboxing or dance fitness classes at the gym; yoga was too slow for my taste.
 
But circumstances demanded that I choose. Lazing around all day in front of the laptop or learning something new that did not need much? Over the next few weeks, much to my surprise, I ended up befriending the yoga mat.
 
It was hard in the beginning. According to my yoga teacher, I was so concerned about the outcome of my workout (a good handstand photo for my Instagram page), that what I was doing was, in fact, not yoga. “It’s all about the process,” she would tell me repeatedly.
 
Over time, her esoteric jargon—“trust the floor”, “accept where you are in your journey”, “surrender to the flow”—began to sound less and less weird. The process, as she had promised, slowly, almost imperceptibly gave way to outcomes. No, I’ve still not managed a photogenic handstand.
 
Reading last week’s stories at The Ken brought me back to the same question I had first asked myself around this time last year. Process or outcome?
 
Why is Bharti Airtel trying to unlock value from its digital business, similar to Jio Platforms? The short answer: because it’s in a rat race trying to emulate its younger but larger competitor that’s backed by India’s largest conglomerate and a small army of the world’s most powerful tech and PE firms. On Monday, Pratap wrote the long answer explaining that this daunting proposition stems from a desperate need to win.
 
On Thursday, Yunindita wrote about Indonesia’s beauty industry looking increasingly Korean. The local players are vying for more share in a market that’s poised to hit US$7 billion in a few years by refashioning the K-beauty playbook for Indonesian skin. Another example of a business trying to copy the winning formula.
 
On Tuesday, Sumanth and our contributor, Anand, wrote about the Mobikwik data breach, arguing that the harder Mobikwik tried to control the outcome, the more it lost control. Despite assertions on social media that hackers had access to MobiKwik's servers for more than one month, it firmly denied any sort of hack. The smoke was the result of a fire that is only going to grow in the post-pandemic world.
 
Seetharaman, on Wednesday, explored the story of funds that invest in companies evaluated on environmental, social, and developmental grounds and rated accordingly by agencies. However, what sustainable investing looks like in places like Europe and the US is not what it looks like in India.
 
On Friday, I wrote a story of surrender. Before the pandemic, India had a double burden of diseases—infectious and lifestyle. Now it has a triple burden: those two and the pandemic. The government of India and hospitals have surrendered to stopping the virus while patients with lifestyle diseases continue to suffer. This was also our free story of the week, so do share it widely.  
 
Praveen closed the week with The Nutgraf, writing that it’s time for India to accept failure when it comes to the vaccine situation. By treating the  Serum Institute of India with a mixture of disdain and ownership, India has ensured that nobody wins. Neither the government nor SII. Definitely not India’s citizens.
 
This week in our daily newsletter, Beyond the First Order, Seetharaman wondered about India's love for Truecaller; Nithin questioned the blow up of Archegos Capital and how Goldman Sachs always seems to get out before sh** hits the fan; he also wrote about China's experiment with its central bank digital currency, something which India could be keeping an eye on.
 
I was optimistic about 2021 last month. I planned a vacation and renewed my gym membership. But I am, once again, gearing up for the yoga mat. Trying, once again, to commit to the process and forgo attachments to the outcome. Accept that control is an illusion. Hope for the best.
 
What about you? What do you think about more—slow chaotic process or shiny, bright, instagrammable outcome? Did last year change your opinion? Let me know.
 
Sending you patience and strength for the next few months.
 
Take care,

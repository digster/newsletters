Good Morning Ishan,
 
South India in the 90s had a real-life boogeyman—a notorious dacoit named Veerappan, who sported a moustache that would give Hercule Poirot a complex. His infamy grew to almost mythical proportions when he orchestrated the kidnapping of one of Karnataka's most famous actors, Rajkumar. 
 
The kidnapping paralysed the state. There were riots. Buses were burned. Stones were thrown. But, more importantly for me, still in middle school with a general aversion to homework, schools were shut.
 
It took 108 days for the release of Rajkumar, and for long stretches of that, schools remained closed. I would be lying if I said I wasn't rooting for Veerappan at the time.
 
Today, schools have been forced to close their doors again. This time, it is not just Karnataka but the entire country. The villain, too, is far scarier—a deadly virus, spreading steadily and silently. While India is only under lockdown till the middle of April, this could continue indefinitely for as long as the novel coronavirus continues to wreak havoc.
 
Rather than hit pause on the academic year, though, schools are boldly going where only edtech companies dared tread—online.
 
It's a fascinating example of ingenuity in times of crisis. Especially in the Indian education sector, which is notoriously reluctant to change. Classes are being held on video conferencing apps, parents are updated via WhatsApp, teachers punching in for work on cloud-based spreadsheets, assignments done through Google Sheets. All in a matter of weeks.
 
But school is more than just lessons and homework. Can the physical school experience really move online? That's what Olina looks to answer in today's piece. We're sharing this with our readers in Southeast Asia because as more countries move to implement lockdowns, this may soon be the norm across the board.

For a long time, product and marketing were two separate functions at tech companies. Product would make decisions about what the product would be, how it would work, what features it would have, and what they’d build next. Marketing would then take this product and figure out how to make it popular, whom to sell it to, and how to find new customers. Neither of them could succeed on their own without the other, so product and marketing had to work together. This often resulted in difficult conversations, because Product cares about building features that make the product experience better, while Marketing cares about finding new users to experience those features. Two teams with two different goals.

After several years of this, somebody (probably a Product Manager after a particularly annoying meeting with marketing) asked the question—maybe Product could make features that acquire new users? That way, growth would come through the normal course of users using the core product itself. So instead of asking Facebook’s marketing team to acquire users, the product team could add features that let existing users send Facebook invitations to non-users. And they could add this right in the middle of their regular flow, like when users were uploading a group photo and tagging their friends who weren’t on Facebook. 

Essentially, that’s what Product-led Growth (PLG) is all about. 

There are three rules for Product-led Growth:

Rule 1: Growth is created by the product itself.

Rule 2: The core product must be the engine of growth.

Rule 3: The value the product signals is identical to the value the product increases by, in exchange for growth.

I’ll come back to PLG in a bit, but here’s what happened last week.

Zomato has renamed its ‘pure veg’ mode to ‘veg only’ and dropped the plan to have a separate colour-coded fleet for exclusively delivering vegetarian food, following an online backlash citing concerns around community and caste-based discrimination.

Several users took to social media to criticise Zomato's decision to use the term ‘pure veg’ and have delivery personnel dress up in green instead of the regular red uniforms to ferry vegetarian food.

On Wednesday, Zomato rolled back green uniforms which the pure-veg food-delivery fleet was earlier supposed to wear.

“Delivery partners onboarded on Zomato will never be discriminated against on the basis of any criteria,” the company said while pointing out that customers cannot select delivery partners based on the drivers' dietary preferences.

There are two ways to look at this. 

The first is through the lens of society. There are the implications of vegetarians using ‘pure-veg’ to describe themselves. Many people believe this is an honest description of their dietary preferences and nothing else. Several others believe that this is actually a form of religious preference and that words like purity have historical and often discriminatory roots in caste. These are important, relevant conversations, and worth having. In and of itself, these are also good enough and sufficient reasons for Zomato to have decided not to proceed with the idea.

The other way is to set aside the caste and religious connotations for a moment, and to have a separate conversation about Zomato’s strategy itself. Did Zomato’s initiative even make sense from a business and product standpoint? One argument I’ve often heard is a variant of, “well, Zomato is simply trying to build a product for a customer segment that fulfils an existing market demand. What’s wrong with that?”. 

Well, nothing—except that they got this wrong too.

Zomato has always been a unique, somewhat iconoclastic brand. I wrote about this in much more detail a few years ago https://the-ken.com/the-nutgraf/two-stories-about-brand-association/. It’s a brand that’s advertised itself on porn websites, created edgy billboards on highways, pushed the boundaries in TV advertising, and also taken strong, unambiguous stands against religious discrimination. Zomato always bends and tests existing conventions. Sometimes that makes them stand out and the rest of us admire them for doing things brands don’t usually do. Sometimes this places them in difficult positions that they need to apologise for, or retract, and the rest of us understand where the boundaries lie and which lines cannot be crossed. 

Zomato gives us both of these. One comes with the other. 

Last week, they did the same thing with Product-led Growth. 

Essentially, Zomato attempted a somewhat audacious form of Product-led Growth, and it did it by breaking existing rules of how it’s usually done, and discovering newer, complicated problems. This is one of the reasons why it failed. As a consequence, it has learnt the most important lesson about PLG—companies simply cannot think of their users like they do right now. If you’re in Product Management, or in Growth Marketing, today’s edition is especially for you.

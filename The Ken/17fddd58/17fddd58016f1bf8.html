Good Morning Ishan,
 
Back in 2002, when footballer David Beckham was voted “the biggest metrosexual in Britain”, all he had was a light five o’clock shadow on his face. (A metrosexual is one who enjoys the supposedly “feminine” pursuits of grooming.) 
 
Over the last five or so years, the male-identifying half of the world has spurned the stubble for a ‘lumbersexual’ look—a rugged, burly look that includes plaid shirts for some reason, but is mainly characterised by a full beard.  
 
Trends and consumer products are generally chicken-and-egg situations—no one knows which came first but all of a sudden there’s a market and a whole lineup of products to cater to the market. In the case of beards, where one would have been hard-pressed to find styling wax in supermarket aisles 15 years ago, there are entire direct-to-consumer (D2C) companies dedicated to men’s facial hair requirements. Beard oil. Charcoal shaving cream. Beard trimmers with so many settings that one of them could probably make the trimmer dance if you asked it nicely.
 
Beards are just one example. There’s now a slew of D2C companies catering to customers’ every whim no matter how niche they get. They’re agile, have a finger on the pulse of India’s 20-somethings—nearly half of the country’s 1.3 billion population, by the way—and can develop and launch a product in just under three months. D2C products account for just 3% of India’s total fast-moving consumer goods (FMCG) revenue currently, but that’s slated to rise to 8-10% in the next few years. 
 
While all of this has been going on, India’s big FMCG companies such as Marico and Emami haven’t exactly been twiddling their thumbs. But the size and complexity of their operations mean they can’t work like the young crowd. 
 
Instead, they’re turning to investments. Just this month alone, Emami acquired a 19% stake in D2C nutrition brand TruNativ for an undisclosed sum. And the venture funding arm of Wipro Consumer Care, along with two other investors, backed cosmetics brand The Ayurveda Company.
 
What happens, however, when the old clashes with the new? The two sets of companies are basically chalk and cheese, to put it mildly. Do the upstarts need the money and backing of the behemoths more than the behemoths need the flexibility and popularity of the young ‘uns?

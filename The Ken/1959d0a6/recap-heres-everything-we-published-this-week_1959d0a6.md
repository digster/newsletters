[How Blackstone became the real winner in IGI’s global dominance in lab-grown gems](https://the-ken.com/story/how-blackstone-became-the-real-winner-in-igis-global-dominance-in-lab-grown-gems/?utm_source=weekly_email&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=weekly_recap_card)
Blackstone-backed IGI hedged their bets on the lab-grown diamond market and it paid off. But the golden run may not last forever
IGI India is on a roll with a stellar IPO and solid profits, riding the wave of lab-grown diamonds and controlling 65% of its certification market. Hitching a ride on this deal is Blackstone, having made back most of its investments by selling 23.5% of its shares and offloading IGI’s European subsidiaries onto India. With a streamlined supply chain, IGI India owns the brand, while Blackstone cashes in on the dividends. However, the rise of lower-priced lab-grown diamonds, with no resale value could soon make certifications, at least as we know them, irrelevant. [Hari Menon has to save Bigbasket from itself](https://the-ken.com/story/hari-menon-has-to-save-bigbasket-from-itself/?utm_source=weekly_email&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=weekly_recap_card)
The Tata group e-grocer’s CEO is finally chasing Blinkit and Zepto’s quick-delivery playbook—but in a race where speed is everything, showing up late is hard to undo
After years of playing will-they, won’t-they, Bigbasket finally jumped into quick commerce, realising slotted delivery wasn’t enough. Once the leader in online grocery, Bigbasket now trails far behind Blinkit, Zepto, and Instamart in the quick-commerce race. Bigbasket sticks to Tata’s rigid principles, refusing to cut corners—even when it means falling behind faster-moving rivals. With Tata reportedly pushing for speed, Bigbasket faces the tough task of reacquiring lost customers—without burning cash like its competitors. [India lost the solar race to China. Ohmium thinks green hydrogen will be different](https://the-ken.com/story/india-lost-the-solar-race-to-china-ohmium-thinks-green-hydrogen-will-be-different/?utm_source=weekly_email&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=weekly_recap_card)
The six-year-old American firm saw an opportunity when the US batted for clean energy. But when the tables turned, Ohmium turned to India’s $2 billion PLI and Europe’s clean-tech policies
Green hydrogen is the fuel that India needs to outpace Chinese competition in the renewable-energy space. The country is even generous with a subsidy, encouraging companies to not only produce the fuel for local consumption but also export, especially when the US is turning away from clean energy. Ohmium, which makes green hydrogen through PEM electrolysis, unlike Chinese firms that depend on alkaline solutions, believes its technology lends it an advantage in this race. But green hydrogen is still pricey in a market dependent on fossil fuels. So, lower installation costs and better efficiency are what Ohmium is betting on.

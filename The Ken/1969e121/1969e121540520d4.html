What did investors learn from edtech’s boom-and-bust cycle? The soundbytes seem dated
By Olina Banerji
6 min read
View in browser

Good Morning Ishan,

You are reading our subscriber-only newsletter Ed Set Go—a weekly commentary on Indian edtech and education

It’s been a while since we talked about the hard-nosed world of edtech investing. But nothing much has been going on. I can count the recent updates on one hand: Physics Wallah (PW) is allegedly very close to an IPO; Eruditus is profitable and scored an investment last year; GSV, an American investor, held a series of events in India to mirror its annual do in San Diego; AI is the next big bet; Byju’s has sworn off investors after its founder somehow held them responsible for the company’s downfall.

I’m sure you can tell, like me, that these aren’t stellar updates, despite headlines like these sprouting late last year:

View in Browser

Here’s what the numbers, sourced from the business intelligence website Traxcn, tell us:

1. Total investment in India’s edtech sector fell from a now-unbelievable high of $5.58 billion in 2021 to $24 million in 2025 (till date).

2. There are just over 18,000 edtech companies in India, of which, about 1,300 were funded; 314 companies have raised a Series A round or higher, and 191 have made it to a Series C round.

3. The number of acquisitions also went down from 38 in 2021 to just four in 2024, with 2025 set to potentially show the same trends.

The outlook isn’t great. But this is exactly where investor spin/confidence can work its charm. After a period of public chastisement over the companies they funded—without any real scrutiny—VC sentiments towards edtech companies have started to sweeten again.

There’s one big fish in the pond that everyone has their hooks in. Physics Wallah. 

Much like their common interest in Byju’s—investors like Lightspeed and GSV are backing PW in the hopes that it will have better luck and better management of its sprawling edtech business.

The other, and arguably saner, bet is on Eruditus, which has chugged through a difficult market to emerge as a top player. If PW ever combusts like Byju’s did, there’s a fallback for these firms, just like the two-horse race between Byju’s and Unacademy a few years ago. But PW and Eruditus operate in different consumer markets, which, hopefully, should give investors some cover.

In light of these new beginnings, I wanted to see whether the thesis for investing in India’s edtech sector has changed from five years ago. Are they still touting numbers as the only game in town, or has someone taken a different tack?

Well, if you were looking for inspiration, look elsewhere I’d say.

Dev Khare, partner at Lightspeed India, went from sharing this picture on this Twitter feed in 2018…

View in Browser

…to tactfully dodging questions about Byju’s in an interview with CNBC. 

When the interviewer asks him about investor sentiments after the Byju’s debacle, Khare is quick to shift focus to Lightspeed’s investment in PW—“India’s biggest edtech company right now.”

Khare also points out that his outlook on the Indian market remains strong because of the size of the test-prep and school-edtech markets. “That endures, whether it is one company or the other that comes through. I don’t think there’s been too much impact because of them [Byju’s]. We are very sure the market will endure.” Khare refused any specific comments about Byju’s comeback.

Michael Moe, a founder and CEO at GSV, was in India in early February and put AI into the mix when answering questions about his firm’s interest in India.

“We are very bullish on the Indian market. We think it’s the most important edtech market in the world,” he states.

[…]

“AI is like air. It’s invisible, it’s ubiquitous, and you need it to live,” he notes. “What we see is not just the opportunity to create a more personalised learning experience, but one that is enhanced because it can provide what I need, when I need it, on a real-time basis. I call it just-in-time learning, as opposed to just-in-case learning,” Moe elaborates. “What it does is allow anybody, anywhere, to have access to the greatest learning experience that they could imagine,” he adds. According to the GSV chief, the firm intends to play a very prevalent role in this to back entrepreneurs. “It’s hard to imagine us being interested in a company that wasn’t using AI somehow.”

Moe also said that GSV backs founders, rather than just companies. Founders who are “authentic” and driven by a need to “make meaningful impact”. PW slots neatly into this description of an ideal investment, so it’s possible the thesis is slightly influenced by the biggest bet. To Moe’s credit though, this is a more involved take than “woah, look at the size of the market”.

Finally, let’s talk about Prosus—a once-keen backer of Byju’s that had to write off its entire investment in the firm and is now part of an effort to litigate alleged fraud committed by the latter. In an interview, general counsel David Tudor told Outlook Business that India’s investment opportunity remains “unblemished”, provided companies can get their governance right. There are still large pools of “untapped investment potential” in the country, he said.

“You look at the massive numbers of connections that you have. And then the number of people who are using smartphones. That is a huge untapped opportunity. There’s a massive gap there. That for me was a sort of eye-opening insight that we maybe are underestimating the scale of the opportunity. If anything, it (could lead us to) bring more capital in India than we were,” said Tudor.

This is a thesis based on the belief that more technology will lead to larger business opportunities. It doesn’t take into account, though, how this opportunity is distributed between income, gender, and geographically diverse groups. It’s a bold move for Prosus when it comes to Indian edtech. As we’ve seen, just technology can’t solve the problem of access, and hence, the problem of paying for a service.

It matters which thesis comes to the fore because it’s likely that one dominant thesis will pull the market in a specific direction. But it’s also possible that the companies that strike gold early—and show results like PW—could help morph the thesis into whatever they need it to be. I get the feeling that, ultimately, we may see little diversity in opinions as investments dovetail toward a few companies. 

What’s your thesis? Or better, what’s your anti-thesis?

Good Morning Ishan,

“It’s a crazy idea for a digital payments company,” said the Indian government official.

Wow, really?

“It’s a revolutionary solution with the potential to transform cash in India,” said the international executive.

Okay.

“We have asked (them) to stop this now as we seek the regulator’s permission,” said the official associated with the National Payments Commission of India, the body that runs retail payments in the country.

It’s that disruptive?

Three very different reactions to a feature that’s still on the fringes of India’s payments sectors 10 years after being launched—“cash at point of sale.” A feature that allowed any Indian armed with a debit card to visit a shop, swipe it, and get back the same amount as cold, hard cash.

But after India banned over 85% of its physical currency notes in 2016, cash became an endangered species. And fed by the compost from the destroyed cash, hundreds of fintech startups of all sorts mushroomed. 

The most famous of which is PhonePe, owned by Flipkart (and now Walmart).

But why on earth is PhonePe, the exemplar of a pure digital payments company, touting the ability of customers to walk up to stores, transfer cash digitally to merchants, and be paid back the same amount in cash?

The merchant makes no commission. PhonePe makes no commission. There’s seemingly no business model around this curious to-and-fro transaction.

And yet, digital payments companies are on edge. Why?

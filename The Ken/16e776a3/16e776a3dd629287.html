Good Morning Ishan,
 
If you read our weekly newsletter The Nutgraf yesterday (and you should), you might also be of the opinion that we live in uncertain times. I decided to deal with it in a well-adjusted way by reading Paul Torday's Salmon Fishing in the Yemen, where British fisheries scientist Alfred Jones is asked to create a river for salmon fishing. It is a story that is as preposterous as it sounds. 
 
The last time I wrote about The Truman Show, I had many emails telling me that I had ruined the ending (it was released in 1998!), so I will steer clear of that mistake. It's a lovely book, but a novel about the trials of introducing freshwater fish in the desert is filled with uncertainty and impossible dreams. That got me thinking of this week's stories, and how many of them deal with uncertainty, so here is a quick recap while I evaluate my escapist tendencies. 
 
On Monday, I wrote about the massive changes American retailer Walmart is making at Flipkart, its largest acquisition. It wants to turn Flipkart from a cash-guzzling startup to a profitable e-commerce player. The question is, does Flipkart have what it takes to get there?
 
On Wednesday, Olina took a hard look at the three doors the Indian government has to get through to fulfil its gigafactory ambitions by 2024. A lack of raw material, equipment and tech means that India's future as one of Asia's most powerful economies is uncertain. The story rattled a few doors of its own and is our free read for the week. Do share widely. 
 
Sumanth wrote about credit card payment platform CRED and founder Kunal Shah on Thursday. For the Freecharge founder, raising money for CRED was the easy part, Sumanth writes. Shah and CRED must now figure out how to use the funds to deliver investors a unicorn exit. 
 
On Tuesday, contributors Mark Kahn and Shruti Srivastava, along with Sharath, used data to show increasing funding for India's agriculture sector. The rising focus on agritech by generalist VCs and impact venture funds has been one of the biggest changes in the last five years. 
 
We ended the week with contributor Nilesh Christopher's take on Chinese live-streaming company YY Inc's rapidly growing presence in India. ByteDance's app TikTok has been the reigning app in India so far, but that might not be forever, he writes. 
 
That's all for this week. Have a good weekend!

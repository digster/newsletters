Good Morning Ishan,

When the subject of this story first came up at work, I was instantly reminded of something my mother had once said—"It was painful".

Of course, IUDs or intrauterine devices today—the subject in question—are not as bad as my mother's Copper T back in the late 80s. Mum got an infection thanks to the device, one that ended with a massive ovarian cyst. She eventually had to get an ovary removed.

Interestingly, she almost considered a hysterectomy—having her entire uterus removed. She didn't. And two years later, I was born.

Close shave.

When it comes to pregnancy, painful menstruation, and the options women have, nothing is perfect. And yet, the contemporary hormonal IUD, which releases micro-doses of the hormone levonorgestrel or LNG, is a viable option. A viable option most Indian women don't go for, instead opting for hysterectomies. So much so that India's rate of hysterectomies is four times that of developed nations.

The problem is that IUDs exist in the market but are largely unavailable to the poor. And this is despite the fact that these devices are on India's National List of Essential Medicines and the World Health Organization's essential medicines list.

Since 2015, there have been at least five IUD LNG options made available in the market: Rs 2,424—for HLL Lifecare's Emily—and Rs 3,500—for Bayer's Mirena, Eloira by Pregna International Ltd, Emirelle by DKT India, and Fiona by Meril Life Sciences. Why then are they not reaching Indian women?

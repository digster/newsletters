The phrase “disruption”—short for disruptive innovation—was coined by the late Clayton M. Christensen in 1995. The idea became so popular and overused that he wrote a second piece in 2015 to clarify it again*. Do read it. 

“Disruption” describes a process whereby a smaller company with fewer resources is able to successfully challenge established incumbent businesses. Specifically, as incumbents focus on improving their products and services for their most demanding (and usually most profitable) customers, they exceed the needs of some segments and ignore the needs of others. Entrants that prove disruptive begin by successfully targeting those overlooked segments, gaining a foothold by delivering more-suitable functionality—frequently at a lower price. 

A smaller company….with fewer resources…is able to challenge established incumbents…by delivering more-suitable functionality…at a lower price. 

I don’t know if Arumugam Govindswamy, the 53-year-old founder of MBit Wireless, sees the world like that. But it is how a telecom professional from Seema Singh’s story today sees the market for wireless chipsets that go into billions of smartphones and intelligent devices. A market dominated global by two companies—US-based Qualcomm and Taiwan's MediaTek. 

“Companies like Qualcomm work at very high margins—60% or thereabouts. And Wall Street loves such companies—high margins beget high market capitalisation. Why would it be interested in selling chipsets for low-end phones?”

That’s exactly the market MBit wants to go after. It's why it has been filing patents, building chipsets, and signing up customers for a period that is unheard of—10 years.

Today, MBit has two commercially ready chips that can enable 4G low-end smartphones and 5G IoT. With its design base in India, it has tested its chips in four regions—India, US, Japan, and Korea. In the field, says the company, its 4G solution is on par with the Qualcomm solution and has demonstrated similar performance. 

The company has shipped one million chips, but Govindswamy says this is just a teaser. “When we hit 50 million, likely in two years from now, is when we will come up for air and talk about our company more freely.” 

The world of wireless chipsets is brutal. It’s where even giants like Intel, NXP, Texas Instruments, etc., have failed. What chance does MBit have? Is it bringing a knife to a gunfight? Or does it know something others don’t?

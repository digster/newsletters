A couple of weekends back, when I was at home, I ran out of coffee powder. So I used one of those fancy, on-demand apps to get it delivered home. The delivery partner who was assigned the order called me a few minutes later. He didn't understand the app, he apologetically explained in chaste Hindi. But if I told him what I wanted, and gave him directions to my home, he would figure out how to get it to me, he promised. I did. Twenty minutes later, he was at my door. Still apologetic. A short conversation later, he told me that he was a lawyer, who had passed the bar council exam in Uttar Pradesh, in North India. He was doing this until he learned Kannada, the local language, so that he could eventually practice law in Bangalore's district court.
A lawyer delivering coffee. It must be difficult, I mused.
Not really, he said. He loved it. Good money. An opportunity to understand the city. Learn the language. It was great.
Crazy right?
But that's what is happening. College students. FedEx delivery agents. Dropouts. All powering the on-demand gig economy in India for companies like Swiggy, Zomato, FoodPanda and Dunzo. There are nearly 185,000 delivery agents in these companies. And growing fast. None of them is complaining though. Salaries have spiked for delivery agents - today most of them earn as much as graduates do in entry-level 'white collar' jobs. And it continues to rise.
For me, today's story, by Salman, is about a classic conundrum - one that we have seen before. It's about the future of billion-dollar companies dependent on the backs of delivery riders to fulfil tasks that privileged folks like you and me ask for. Now. Instantly. All this while the folks doing the delivery mostly neither understand nor are loyal to the firm whose logos are imprinted on the jackets they wear. All while VCs fund the mania. How sustainable is this? And for how long? Why are these companies battling for delivery personnel? At what price? How could it all end?

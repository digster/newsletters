Good Morning Ishan,
India likes to call itself “the world’s pharmacy”—a massive generics powerhouse, supplying cheap medicines to developing countries that would otherwise go without. It’s a success story… until it isn’t. Because while Indian generics help millions abroad, they’ve also been linked to tragic deaths in Asia and Africa in the past few years.
Back home, though, it’s a different story.
The very same generics that were once the awkward stepchild of India’s Rs 2-lakh-crore pharmaceutical industry are suddenly the darlings of investors. Not because of any noble social mission, but because they make shockingly good business sense. Truemeds, Zeelab Pharmacy, Zeno Health, Davaindia—they are not here to disrupt healthcare with clever tech or last-mile logistics. Their entire playbook is to sell cheap, unbranded pills at massive discounts, sometimes up to 90% off branded alternatives.
Why does this make sense? Because the supply chain is a leaky pipeline. A Rs 12 pain gel somehow costs Rs 150 by the time it hits your local pharmacy, thanks to layers of distributors, medical reps, and middlemen. Cut the nonsense, pass the savings, and suddenly margins look a lot healthier.
Of course, with quality standards still patchy and enforcement sporadic, trusting a platform is increasingly the only way consumers can navigate this mess.
Sudeshna’s [story](https://the-ken.com/story/jan-aushadhi-unleashed-the-generics-genie-no-one-wants-it-back-in-the-bottle-now/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter) today looks at how generics went from a public-welfare side hustle to a private-equity play. And why the race is less about altruism and more about capturing the next big arbitrage.
On another note, yesterday, hedge fund Jane Street managed to score a point in its ongoing battle with market regulator Sebi. With Rs 4,800 crore (and possibly much, much more) on the hook, not to mention the risk of huge reputational fallout, this is a fight neither party can really afford to lose.

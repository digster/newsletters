Help.
 
As India remains in partial paralysis, thanks to the Covid-19 lockdown that's now on day 45, it's what most businesses in the country need. 
 
Help, however, has not been forthcoming. The government rolled out a $22 billion stimulus package aimed at India's poorest. But it hasn't been as generous when it comes to businesses.
 
Help was meant to arrive, though. Well, sort of.
 
On 21 May, an ambitious online lending marketplace named Sahay was to be unveiled. Sahay, incidentally, is Hindi for help.
 
The platform was seen as a game-changer. It would enable hundreds of thousands of India's micro, small, and medium enterprises (MSMEs)—who typically struggle for loans—to access credit in under five minutes. At a time when businesses are struggling, it would have been a godsend.
 
Sahay, however, is now in limbo. The grand unveiling on 21 May stands scrapped. In a climate where cash flows are uncertain, the platform—based on flow-based lending—isn't seen as viable.
 
Sahay was meant to be a three-peat for iSpirt, the architect behind UPI and Aadhaar, arguably India's two biggest mass-scale tech interventions. But the think tank hasn't yet succeeded in getting Sahay off the ground. The project is riddled with the sort of conflicts and controversies UPI saw. Ownership and power without accountability. Conflicts of interest galore. Rules that are created on the fly.
 
Indeed, in response to various questions sent by The Ken, iSpirt repeatedly directed us to a document ostensibly drawn up to keep the think tank on the straight and narrow. A document that was published the same day iSpirt sent its responses.
 
"They are involved in policy making and people associated with iSpirt are also likely market participants. So you end up becoming a rule-maker, jury, and judge. This is not healthy for a market economy," said one industry executive The Ken spoke with.

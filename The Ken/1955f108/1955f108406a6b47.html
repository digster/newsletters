Several of my friends have had babies over the past four years. My go-to gift is a set of books meant to be read to the kiddos until they can read the stories themselves. My favourite is Punky Aloha, a tale of adventure about a little Hawaiian girl finding courage as she ventures out to buy a stick of butter so she can bake banana bread with her grandmother.

It only takes a couple years to see the difference between kids who are read to every day since they’re born and those who aren’t. Presumably, this will pay off even more years later, when they start school.

I mention this because it reminds me of today’s story. You’ve probably realised that the pandemic—and being away from the routine of a classroom—has weighed down students’ learning levels across all school years.

A couple figures tell us just how detrimental that is in India: only 35.5% of private school Class 3 students could read a Class 2 textbook in 2024, a noticeable drop from 40.6% in 2018.

There are anomalies in the number of students that have gone back to school too. Take Blossoms School in north Bengaluru as an example: it had more than 1,500 students in 2020, before remote learning became the norm. Now, around 660 students walk onto its premises every day, and the school’s administrators expect that figure to fall further.

That’s part of a broader trend of student enrolment going down. Data compiled by the Indian government says there are now at least 10 million fewer students in India’s schools compared to 2018. Other research indicates learning levels in private schools, which account for nearly one-third of India’s educational institutions, have not recovered to pre-2020 levels.

The Karnataka Associated Managements of English Medium Schools, a network of more than 4,000 schools, estimates that 75% of its member institutions have fewer students than they did five years ago.

Atul’s story today dives into the details. Read it to find out how India’s foundational education is changing.

Nothing like a little mystery on a Sunday morning. That, good coffee, family or friends, and some personal reading time. And now that you and I have that covered, let me lay out the facts of today’s story.
The man has taken on Facebook. Seems like it was his first taste of blood. He was the lawyer fighting against Facebook, and he won. In 2012.
He then took Uber to court.
He is currently fighting WhatsApp in the Supreme Court of India. WhatsApp, as you know, is owned by Facebook.
Of course, tech companies aren’t his only adversaries. In 2018, he filed a public interest litigation in the Supreme Court asking it to use double-sided prints for legal documents and petitions, saying it would save millions of litres of water and thousands of trees. Specifically, 2,000 trees and 24,000 tankers of water.
He runs an NGO called Centre for Accountability and Systemic Change (CASC).
Last year again, through CASC, he wrote to the Prime Minister of India asking the government not to give free medical care to bureaucrats who smoke and drink. His logic: A healthy bureaucracy will ensure availing fewer medical facilities, thereby reducing the expenditure.
He has taken on the Government of India. In a legal notice, he has demanded that political parties be told to set up internal committees to deal with sexual harassment (a rule that applies to all organisations in India)
When my colleague Pranav spoke with him, he said: “Ultimately, what are we doing? I can say in one word that this is a process of self-actualisation.”

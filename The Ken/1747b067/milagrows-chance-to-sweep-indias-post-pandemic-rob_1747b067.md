"The little beeping guy, no longer a stranger but a member of the household, has a personality. You root it on in its quest," said a
New York Times [review](https://www.nytimes.com/2002/12/26/technology/test-drive-rooting-for-roomba-can-it-catch-the-dust-bunnies.html) of the first iRobot Roomba, a pioneering floor-cleaning robot, back in 2002. "But while it is more fun than vacuuming yourself, it is not as effective—certainly not in comparison with a conventional upright."
Indians would tend to agree with the last sentence fully. After all, we've been spoilt rotten by cheap domestic labour. But with the lockdown in place, those of us who had relied on domestic workers were back to sweeping the floor and doing the dishes by ourselves. We were finally open to embracing something we'd pooh-poohed only weeks earlier.
And Gurugram-based Milagrow HumanTech was waiting with a range of floor-cleaning robots, priced between $95 and $1,600. Between April 2019 and March 2020, the 13-year-old company sold 600 of those robots, a number it surpassed in just the following four months.
But the company was in somewhat uncharted waters. Pre-Covid, only 30% of its floor-cleaning robots were bought by individuals. But with offices and restaurants shut during the lockdown, that number surged to 90%. As a result, Milagrow could no longer rely on its direct sales channel. It had to get its online act together to compete with rivals such as including iRobot and Ecovacs.
That's hardly the only problem Milagrow had to tackle. Two-thirds of its robots' components, by value, are imported from China, and the recent India-China fracas has disrupted Milagrow's supply chains, driving up costs.

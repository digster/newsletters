Good Morning Ishan,

Over lunch with colleagues recently, someone passed me a Hershey's SoFit. On the face of it, the soya-almond drink seemed to tick all the boxes—protein, vitamins, dietary fibre. A closer inspection, though, revealed nearly 20 grams of sugars per serving. 

This isn't unique nor should it be surprising. And India is finally about to do something about it. The Food Safety and Standards Authority of India (FSSAI) plans to introduce Health Star Ratings (HSR) on the front of packaged foods that will tell you just how healthy a particular snack is. Ratings go from one to five stars—the healthier the food, the more stars it gets.

On the surface of things, this seems like a good move. But is it? 

Health star ratings have proven ineffective, even harmful, in countries such as Australia. This is because manufacturers could still manage good ratings on unhealthy foods simply by adding a few healthy ingredients. And by not going beyond the star ratings on display, customers also made poorer nutritional choices. According to one expert, 73% of ultra-processed foods in Australia had a rating of 2.5 stars or more.

FSSAI didn't decide on star ratings out of the blue. It made its decision on the basis of a pan-India survey conducted by Indian Institute of Management-Ahmedabad. A closer look at the survey, however, explains why doctors, consumer groups, and even FSSAI's own experts are sceptical and believe that warning labels that clearly state the problems with any given snack would have been a better option. It's somewhat telling that food and beverage companies, which have fought warning labels in other countries, seem supportive of FSSAI's star ratings system.

With FSSAI looking to move quickly after years of logjam, the HSR system could be made into a law within a year. Maitri's story today highlights the issues with this decision  and explains why star ratings often obscure more than they reveal.

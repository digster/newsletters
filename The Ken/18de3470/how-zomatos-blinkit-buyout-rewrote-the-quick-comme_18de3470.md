Good Morning Ishan,
The wheels of fortune have been turning for Blinkit, and how! From gasping for funds and facing a near-death experience a couple of years back, the quick-commerce platform today is not just alive but also kicking. The knight in shining armour in this rescue act—food-delivery major Zomato—scooped up Blinkit for a neat Rs 4,447 crore (US$570 million) in the middle of 2022.
There is a nice rhyming ring to the whole episode, I think: Deepinder saves Albinder. Friends in need, friends indeed—the Zomato CEO and the Blinkit CEO have been known to be old buddies. Albinder even worked at Zomato before he co-founded Grofers, the earlier avatar of Blinkit. Many market pundits were sceptical about what Zomato would get from Blinkit’s get-groceries-before-you-can-blink proposition to customers.
Let’s just say they were off the mark. Since the acquisition, Blinkit’s zooming gross merchandise value has seen it pip nearest rivals Swiggy’s Instamart and Zepto to pole position; its losses have shrunk; and wait—Zomato executives now expect quick-commerce to outpace the mainstay food-delivery business within 3–5 years.
Now, if you thought acquisitions by deep-pocketed friends were enough to salvage a struggling business, you would be off the mark, too. Sure, that helped. But much more went into the turnaround—from ruthless prioritisation to leveraging core competencies by both acquirer and acquired. Nice, but will the “what-got-you-here-won’t-get-you-there” syndrome afflict Blinkit now? [Aakriti digs deep to get you the deets in this story today. Don’t miss it.](https://the-ken.com/story/how-zomatos-blinkit-buyout-rewrote-the-quick-commerce-story/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter)
I can’t help but wonder at the double-edged nature of this sword. Blinkit seems to have worked, at least so far, for Zomato. But, the corporate world is littered with examples of expensive acquisitions proving to be the undoing of the acquirer.
Exhibit A here would be the erstwhile poster boy of Indian edtech—Byju’s. Some years back, anything that walked by its corridor would be acquired, it was said, only half in jest. From coaching centre Aakash to coding platform Whitehat Jr, Byju’s paid top-dollar for its many buys. Then, the cycle turned, as it always does, and many of these buys became banes. Byju’s investors are now baying for the founder’s blood, and he’s stretching every muscle to stave them off.
Acquisition attempts provide mirth, too. Ajay Singh, chairman of Spicejet, wants to buy the bankrupt airline Go First. Never mind that beleaguered Spicejet itself has been truant, settling dues of many, including failed banker Credit Suisse. The Supreme Court of India took stern note of the irony last week.
To buy or not to buy, that is the question, as the Bard might have put it.

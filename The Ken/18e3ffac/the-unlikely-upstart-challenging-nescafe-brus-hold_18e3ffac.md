Coming to which, this perfect Friday read by Aakriti has two things to root for: an underdog taking on giants, and coffee, the elixir of life. Ok, I go overboard with the latter, but you get the drift.
Coffee, for many millions like me, isn’t just a drink, it’s an emotion. In December, I was in Chikkamagaluru in Karnataka, the place where coffee was first introduced in the country by Baba Budan, the Sufi saint who smuggled seven seeds from West Asia in the 16th century.
The seeds sown then have grown leaps and bounds to become a US$1-billion market in India today. South India, in particular, loves its cup of joe.
Apt then that Continental Coffee, based in the South Indian state of Andhra Pradesh, has emerged over the past three decades as one of the world’s largest contract manufacturers of the beverage. The listed company has largely been in the shadows so far—content with scores of domestic and international giants sticking their brand names on its produce and hawking it as their own.
Not anymore. The mostly business-to-business player now seeks its place in the business-to-consumer sun. It has become a business imperative. And in this pursuit, it’s not shying away from challenging the near-duopoly of global giants, Unilever and Nestlé, in the instant coffee market in India.
Continental Coffee has tasted initial success, but can it really move the needle in a market where brand loyalties run deep and even regional names are no pushovers? Wake up and smell the coffee
That’s what India’s stock market regulator Sebi has been telling investors about small-cap and mid-cap stocks for several weeks now. It has been concerned about the breathless run, non-stop fund gush, and froth building in these segments of the market.
The results of the stress tests that Sebi directed mutual funds to undertake will start coming in from today, 15 March. But even before these were in, a jittery market hammered down small- and mid-cap indices earlier this week.
Sebi may have its heart in the right place—protection of the retail investor. But are its words and actions resulting in panic selling? Is the regulator overstepping and distorting the market, not allowing it to find its own equilibrium? Should it just let the market do its thing and let retail investors learn their lessons? Would that make both investors and the market more deep and mature?
Lessons learnt the hard way are seldom forgotten.

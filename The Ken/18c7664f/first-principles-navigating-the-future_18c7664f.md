Good morning Ishan. I was meeting a friend over drinks recently and we got round to talking about our kids (this isn’t a parenting-themed edition, don’t worry). We agreed that kids today were almost universally better than us, back when we were their age. Except for one thing.
“They have terrible spatial awareness!” he said. I agreed.
Was it perhaps that we (fathers) grew up in the pre-Google Maps era, where you had to learn to navigate without GPS or real-time directions? Or was it because we were forced to (pointlessly) memorise countries and capitals at school, and be forced to point them out too?
I don’t know.
What I do know is that we’re increasingly becoming less spatially aware in our lives, which is making it harder for us to navigate it too.
Because we are all explorers and travellers. On any given day we’re navigating neighbourhoods and roads, yes, but also products, relationships, careers, and even time.
This is a Sunday newsletter (even if many of you read this on a Monday), so I’m allowed to lead with reflection.
Welcome to edition 24 of First Principles. This Sunday we’re going to talk about navigation, maps, and compasses.

1. Navigating with compasses and maps 🧭🗺️
|
|
I was talking to you about our increasing lack of spatial awareness earlier. On the roads, it's thanks to GPS and Google Maps (or OSMAnd, Apple Maps etc.). Why bother wasting brain cycles memorising landmarks, roads, and directions if we can count on technology to assist us 24x7, right?
But what if spatial awareness isn’t just about physical space? What if it’s about the obstacles and destinations in a career? Or in a product’s strategy?
I think we’ll all agree that we live in a world where order and stability seem to be replaced by chaos and unpredictability each day. I argue that it interferes with our ability to plan for the future. We need a mental map of what we think the future might look like, so that we can plan on getting there in the way we want.
Without one, [we struggle](https://www.businessinsider.com/remote-work-fueling-gen-z-job-anxiety-stress-mental-health-2023-12).
|
"Anxiety is driven by uncertainty," Ellen Hendriksen, a clinical psychologist and the author of "How to Be Yourself: Quiet Your Inner Critic and Rise Above Social Anxiety," said. Because they grew up in the digital age with nearly unlimited amounts of information at their fingertips, Hendriksen said, Gen Z has the least experience with uncertainty. "When you need to know where to go, you can pull up Google Maps," she said. "If you are going to a new restaurant, you can look at the menu ahead of time. There's a lot of certainty in this world now which didn't exist before."
|
|
|
But it can be hard to stay focused on your career when you don’t know if GenAI is going to eat up your role. It can be hard to think about your product if you don’t know how much runway your company has, to invest for the future.
Which is where leadership matters. It is the job of a leader to provide colleagues with a mental map of the future.
Imagine early explorers setting out to discover the world. They had a map of what they thought the world might have looked like, which they then set out to discover.
In business parlance, they had a working hypothesis, which they tried to validate.
Whether it be about careers, products, or even organisations, it is the job of the leader to create compelling maps of what the future might look like. Where is the competition? Where are the opportunities? What are the dangers? Which are the allies?
While maps are great to get a sense of what the world ahead of you might look like, navigating it requires a compass.
|
“People easily become lost and confused when their focus is diverted from the path immediately in front of them, and they can often miss the signs telling them to turn left or right. One of the most important and fundamental choices of modern leaders is to decide whether they will lead by map or by compass.
Command-and-control-style management is about maps. It is about strict adherence to a chosen course, with no deviations allowed.
It assumes someone at the top already knows the best path. It is about discipline, control and compliance. The problem is it creates a dependency mentality because management is about having the answers.
Transformational leadership is about a compass. It is about a process of discovery, which assumes we will learn as we go and get stronger as we gain experiences, collect insights and make decisions along the way. It assumes there is more than one right path, and no one person is smart enough to know which one is best. It is about personal freedom and collective accountability. It serves to create an independence mentality because leadership is about asking the right questions.”
|
|
|
A lot of the literature I’ve read on this sets up maps and compasses as alternatives. Instead, I think both are required.
One recent leader I met who prides himself on helping his colleagues navigate with a compass is Ritesh Agarwal*, the founder and CEO of OYO Rooms. Here’s an excerpt from my conversation with him about why he takes great joy in being called a “Chief Clarity Officer”.
|
“I never worked for a full-time job, (because OYO) was my first full time job to some extent. But before that I had only done some odd jobs, like selling SIM cards. When I was starting to meet other professionals for co-founding the company or joining the company in the early days, one of the things I used to always ask is, “Why are you willing to leave your company?” And more often than not, they used to say, “Clarity”. Meaning “I don't have clarity of what am I supposed to do” or “Where I'm going with my journey in the company", “Do I have growth (potential)”, or “Whether the company itself has clarity about what they're trying to pursue?”
I've seen this question quite often.
So, my perspective became that the company that I would build would try as much as possible to ensure that clarity would not be a problem. For the colleagues, for the hotel owners, or the customers I work with. So that's sort of how it started. What does it mean in real life? I want to take a moment and explain it. It means I'm constantly on the lookout for places where my colleagues across layers might be confused. It may be, “Should I sign up for this hotel or not?”, “Should I launch this feature or not?”, “Should I make a certain kind of investment decision or not?”
In each one of these areas, a lot of times in companies they are left to the chance that somebody else has to do it. Because then there will be new roles, new organisations, new teams being set up, and new promotions announced. So, my perspective is that not having clarity breeds an immense amount of dissatisfaction. And because decision making slows down, people don't get results, newer roles get formed. So, whenever I have an opportunity, I try to speak to our younger team members as much as my senior team, perhaps maybe more. When they are campus recruits, I try to take on two who will report to me. So, I can work directly with the freshers. Because (it increases) the amount of learnings that I get across the organisation about where there is confusion. I feel like I (can then) come back and say there is clarity.
So, I call myself chief clarity officer, and sometimes also chief solution officer. I expect people to bring the most complex problems and the most complex confusions to me so I can hopefully solve them. I feel that's the role the founder and CEO should pursue.”
|

The first habit comes from Hari Shenoy, an artist, rapper, and songwriter who writes his way out every day.
“I write four lines of verse everyday. I did this for two years and last week, I released a rap album.”
|
“I am a new-ish convert to hip-hop. I admire how artists in this genre create something incredible from nothing. Rhythm and poetry when combined to tell good stories makes for a magical combination. Long story short—I experienced health challenges, reevaluated my purpose in life, and knew I had to write. For me, there was no better way to make each day count. I decided to write verse and make a rap album. What was the worst that could happen? I began writing four lines of verse every day. I did this in 2022 and in 2023. I still do. Making haste slowly ("Festina lente", like Augustus Caesar said) led to words for songs coming together, followed by music, recording, mixing, mastering, album cover art and much to my own surprise, I released my first album "Man of Substance" under the nom de plume "Thought Brownie" last week.
Sustaining the daily streak of writing four lines helped me have kinder conversations with myself. Until I picked up this habit and kept at it, I used to think that inspiration will strike and then, I would write. Instead it worked the other way round. I showed up to write and words showed up. Now I have empirical proof and lived experience that have established the power of showing up, staying present and persevering. It makes me feel like I can apply what I have learnt to chase other ambitious goals with curiosity, patience and equanimity.”
|

The second habit recommendation is from Rishi Tiwari: “CIO with the world's largest plant-based food company (Upfield) who loves being curious, doing long drives, and staying healthy.”
“Understanding one's food”
|
|
“I have always been quite conscious of having an active lifestyle (running, exercising, avoiding junk food, etc). However, it is only this year that I really started focusing on what I am eating and when! This has truly brought out the need to know one's food. Food which causes insulin spike, grains with low GI (glycemic index), understanding the split of carbs and proteins per food (amongst other components), etc. So the habit I am referencing is getting the knowledge of the food you are about to partake in your muscle memory. Of course, this is easier for those who get an intellectual kick out of knowing such stuff, but just being acutely aware is itself a big start to becoming healthier. Accompanied with self-discipline, one will surprised at how many time you start making the right food decision when faced with a dilemma!”
|
|
|
And what has that led to?
|
|
“I had reduced my running dramatically over the last 12 months (>2,000 km/year to ~1,000 km/year) to conserve my knees for old age and had switched to greater strength training. I was apprehensive if I would bulk up and gain weight. However, to the contrary, I have increased my muscle mass while actually becoming leaner (without becoming thin), just by shifting to more conscious food habits and introducing elements like tofu into my food routine. I have dropped 3-4 kg in just one quarter without losing muscle mass. The side benefit—the mind is at ease when I see my pair of jeans hang loose on my frame even as I continue to gain strength. :)”
|

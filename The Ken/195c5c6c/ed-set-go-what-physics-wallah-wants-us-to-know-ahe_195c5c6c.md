Ed Set Go
Mon, 24 Mar 25
The one stop to understand the biggest shifts in education, written by the best edtech journalist in the country..
Good Morning Ishan,
It’s been a long wait to get here—an India-grown edtech, aimed at Indians, has finally taken the plunge into the public markets. Physics Wallah (PW) filed draft papers last week for a Rs 4,600 crore IPO through the confidential route, according to reports.
Founded by Alakh Pandey, a Youtube star tutor-turned-entrepreneur, PW quickly rose to fame when it upset the established course fee structure put in place by test-prep edtechs that preceded it. PW gave away its content—quirky, performative videos for students writing competitive exams—almost for free, creating a land rush of users who couldn’t afford to pay Rs 20,000–30,000 for courses from Byju’s, Unacademy, or Vedantu.
PW democratised online education in a way that edtechs didn’t think was possible before. Before PW’s foray, a company called Doubtnut had tried to solve the problem of scaled-up edtech at low cost by largely focusing on automatic doubt-solving. PW took that idea a step further and turned it on its head. Pandey gave away for free what costs edtechs the most amount of money—quality videos—and crashed the cost of acquisition.
PW’s rise to fame, from a Youtube channel to an edtech empire, is well documented. It’s even interesting that PW took very little time to open up physical coaching centers, something that other edtechs waded into only when pushed to, after the pandemic took away the artificial gains of all students being online all the time.
PW has always crafted a careful image about what type of company it wants to be. It has a controlled media narrative, sullied only by the occasional Youtube war between loyalists and defectors. But now that it is finally going public, what does it want potential public investors in its business to know?
It’s risky to build a brand solely around the mythology of one person. We saw what it did to Byju’s, when all decisions flowed from one person at the top. Byju Raveendran could do no wrong, and the company has pretty much stuck to that belief, despite tons of evidence to the contrary.
Physics Wallah is different. While Pandey’s image is woven into the fabric of the business, he has been quick to take advice. From Prateek Maheshwari, the business-side co-founder, and from his investors, who seem to have played some role in PW’s choice of areas to expand into. Pandey is the superstar, but he isn’t a ruler with a tight-knit court of yes-men. At least publicly.
The flip-side to this kind of brandbuilding is that PW will always need his personal branding to sell products. Even their suite of AI products is called “Alakh AI”, as though without the name, the product doesn’t have the credibility to answer questions correctly. And this isn’t a new problem. From the very first round that the edtech raised, for US$100 million, Pandey’s brand has been PW’s biggest strength and its biggest frailty.
And nothing seems to have changed in that department, despite PW having launched a slew of new businesses in areas that Pandey himself likely has little personal expertise in, like career tech or coaching for government jobs.
Will PW always be one set of rumors away from ruination? What if Pandey decided to walk away and start a new venture? Can the company survive the “wallah”?
For now, though, it’s pretty clear the company is depending on the man to take it into an IPO. And Pandey isn’t in a hurry to cash in.
There’s no doubt about the type of product that PW wishes to be. They are code-crackers who help you ace exams at different points in your educational journey. Things are hyper-focused on the exam effort, not a slow-paced, holistic immersion into a subject.
PW makes no bones about this either, because it knows its audience. It knows students are tuning in for quick solutions, revisions, and someone to tell them that—as long as they remember a specific set of things—they’re going to be fine. But PW also goes a step further, and I suspect this is linked to Pandey’s own core personality as a tutor. Ever so often, you’ll stumble across a video where Pandey or a tutor are consoling their legion of invisible students who don’t score well or those tired by the grueling struggle of appearing for a competitive exam. Dust yourself off and try again, PW tells them.
Despite its central exam prep model, PW has tried to show investors that it can expand into different kinds of businesses. It’s just going to bring the same ethos to every category. The one business stream that doesn’t seem innate to its core model is its foray into upskilling, internships, and careers. It’s clear from the sparse information on these subsites that PW has added these business lines as a way to present a complete solution to potential investors—from school to college to careers. Pandey can’t, and won’t, drive this part of the business. But then again, this track isn’t the main pitch—that’s still test-prep.
How much PW steers into careers and the like will be a measure of how much its investors influence its mission. With an IPO in the works, PW might just focus on getting better at what it already does well. With the window dressing, if the careers add-on is truly that, eventually branching off into its own parallel business.
We have to go back a bit for this reference. Pandey made a public appeal to the courts back in 2024, when the National Testing Agency bungled its response to reports of mismanagement with the medical college entrance exam NEET. Pandey petitioned the Supreme Court to probe the matter and reverse decisions giving grace marks to some students while leaving the rest without any recourse.
Pandey’s open protest against the NTA was different from the subtle lobbying that other edtechs have done with the government. He signaled that PW was willing to put its significant reputational heft behind the petition to fight for its students. Much like a political leader would do.
But PW also has a business-savvy part. It has adopted all the nice nuances of AI learning like the rest of its peers, and relatively quickly. It’s trying to tell its investors that it’s an agile business, but with a heart.
PW’s IPO success rides on a lot of other factors, not just the ones we discussed above. In fact, much of it will depend on the sustainability of its offline centers, which have become its bread-and-butter.
But right now, how the company projects itself is important too—quick-footed like a VC business, while also building a traditional offline empire. And oh, with the promise of more entertainment along the way.

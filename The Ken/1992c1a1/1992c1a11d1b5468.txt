From Reliance’s Campa to Coca-Cola, why glass bottles are making a comeback. Also, Tata Trent soft launches a Zudio sibling

Last month, my family took a day trip to Barsana, a small city in western Uttar Pradesh famous for its temples. We are a family of five, and while three of us (the majority) were uninterested in doing much temple-hopping, our parents held a veto. 

Anyway, as it happens on road trips, we stopped at a local dhaba to savour the obligatory dhaba-style dal and tandoori rotis. At the entrance, we noticed a life-sized banner for Campa, the aerated cola brand Reliance Industries acquired and revived three years ago. As a consumer beat reporter, I was not too surprised, but the rest of the lot were pretty excited to try a drink they had loved back in the day.

Divided by destination, united by Campa Cola.

Funnily enough, they even served us Campa in glass bottles, just like old times. 

That did surprise me, though. Weren’t we done with those glass bottles? I thought most companies had largely shifted to plastic, or PET, bottles. After all, glass bottles are cumbersome, both for the consumer and the beverage maker. You can’t carry them off, they are tricky to re-use, and they are expensive for the bottler.

Was this some marketing ploy to play on older customers’ nostalgia? 

I did some digging, and it turns out, it’s more a move born out of necessity. 

Beginning 1 April, 2025, the government has mandated beverage makers—including the likes of Pepsi, Coca-Cola, Campa, and Parle—to use e-PET (recycled PET) bottles for at least 30% of their yearly production. In fact, the government plans to increase this threshold by 10 percentage points each year, until by 2029, companies are using recycled plastic for 60% of their volume. 

The problem is, there doesn’t seem to be enough recycled plastic to go around.

From an environmental point of view, glass is certainly a good choice. One glass bottle can be reused about three to four times, says a consumer analyst with a brokerage firm, who wished to remain unnamed because they are not authorised to speak to the media.

But for companies, using this environment-friendly packaging comes at a price. Packaging makes up as much as 15–20% of the total cost of goods sold (COGS) in the segment. And using recycled plastic bottles is at least 30% more expensive than regular ones. As for glass bottles, the cost is even higher, the analyst with over a decade of experience told The Ken.

For now, though, they don’t seem to have much of a choice. 

With the new regulations kicking in, Ganesh Ecosphere, a publicly listed recycler, mentioned in its latest analyst call that the cost of plastic scrap (i.e., the plastic they recycle) has gone up due to high demand. Their raw material cost swelled to 70% of the revenue in the quarter ended June, up from 64% the previous quarter. And beverage companies have deferred purchasing much of the recycled plastic they will need to meet the new regulations. 

“The (offtake is) nowhere close to the 30% requirement, as per the mandate,” the company told https://www.bseindia.com/xml-data/corpfiling/AttachHis/4cb5ce4d-6ba0-4a03-afbf-dadfff106408.pdf analysts last month.

Of course, it’s not like companies weren’t recycling earlier. In fact, for Varun Beverages, the largest bottler of Pepsi in India, packaging used for 88% of overall volumes https://www.bseindia.com/xml-data/corpfiling/AttachHis/%5C65486ad5-1835-4414-9038-51543138d964.pdf in 2024, some 181,887 metric tons, was recycled. But what these companies were doing is called open-loop recycling, where the plastic produced is broken down into small pieces, and then, in many cases, winds up being used as yarn in textiles, or in other industries.

But now, the government wants beverage companies to adhere to closed-loop recycling, which means recycling companies need to follow a circular product life cycle, reprocessing materials to make new products of the same type. So, a recycled plastic PET bottle for cola needs to come from a similar plastic bottle. 

Right now, Varun Beverage uses only 1.5% recycled plastic in its total material (by value).

While the government’s plan is great for the environment, the recycling ecosystem in India just doesn’t have the capacity yet to meet the demand. India has only a handful of recycling plants for plastic waste that can supply the needs of beverage makers, and together they can meet just about 15% https://www.cnbctv18.com/business/fmcg-sector-seeks-relaxation-in-plastic-reuse-recycling-norms-cites-capacity-crunch-19573397.htm of the total demand for food-grade recycled plastic. Companies can import the rest, but that is both pricey and not exactly environmentally friendly. 

Another major bottleneck has been the FSSAI (Food Safety and Standards Authority of India), which has been slow to grant licenses to recyclers. It was only in the last two months that about 10+ new licenses were handed out while the regulations kicked in from 1 April. This means a large part of the financial year is already past us, noted Janavi Papriwal, an investor at Circulate Capital, a private equity fund focused on waste management and circular solutions. 

Beverage makers are scrambling; with not enough capacity, they aren’t sure how to meet the mandate. But they are waiting to see how the regulations and penalties play out, Papriwal added.

Which explains the glass bottles. 

But Papriwal also believes this isn’t really a long-term solution for brands. There is already talk that the government could bring two dozen more commodities under EPR regulation, including glass. And while glass bottles can be sanitised and reused, they are in fact harder than PET to recycle at scale, according to her.

Beverage companies have decided they’ll do for now, though. Until they figure out a way to meet the recycled-plastic mandate. 

Zudio gets a sibling

In other news, Tata group’s Trent has made a new bet. 

Enough case studies have been written about Zudio, the fast-fashion juggernaut in Trent’s stable. It has outperformed its rivals in sales per square feet https://the-ken.com/tradetricks/why-trent-outclasses-hm-and-uniqlo/, growing rapidly during periods of even slow consumer demand. In the last five years, Zudio’s success has resulted in 660% returns for Trent’s investors.

So why the quiet launch of Burnt Toast last month?

An answer may lie in Trent’s desire to deliver an Indian version of a global fast-fashion brand, with sensibilities similar to an H&M or Zara. (Zudio, on the other hand, is usually categorised as a value retailer.)

Incidentally, Trent seems to have gleaned some lessons from Zara in this, through a partnership with the Spanish conglomerate that started in 2009.

As we wrote https://the-ken.com/story/zara-taught-zudio-how-to-outdo-zara/ earlier this year:

“When Zara came knocking at its doors in 2009–10, Tatas’ retail arm Trent had already been selling western apparel in the mass-premium (Rs 2,000 and above) category. A partnership with the billionaire Amancio Ortego-owned brand meant a larger consumer bracket. Particularly, the more affluent type that Trent’s Westside couldn’t attract….

Now, the Tatas are considering upgrading Westside to a premium-apparel outlet, while planning to launch a new segment targeting mid-premium users, according to two industry executives who did not want to be named. Essentially, the new segment would cater to Westside’s current target consumers. And the upgraded Westside would go on to compete against the likes of Zara and H&M.”

There are both similarities and differences between Burnt Toast and Zudio. Both purportedly target a similar consumer group: young people aged 15–25. However, Zudio’s target audience has become more diffused over time; Stores now include kids’ sections and are frequented by shoppers over 30. With Burnt Toast, though, Tata wants to strictly focus on Gen Z and Gen Alpha—what an ICICI Securities report calls the “fast-forward 15–25-year-olds.” For context, about half of our population is below 25.

This is likely why Tata has chosen cities like Bengaluru, Pune, Mumbai, and Surat to soft launch Burnt Toast; all of them have leading colleges and a high concentration of college students and first-time job holders. 

Also, products at Zudio start from Rs 199, while at Burnt Toast, they start at Rs 499.

But why launch a new concept when Trent already has the recognisable Zara brand under its fold? 

Well, for one, Indian youth are not strongly attached to any particular brand, and there is opportunity for new brands to carve out a market—as shown by the growth of brands like Newme https://the-ken.com/tradetricks/shein-is-back-where-does-that-leave-its-clones/. Second, Tata may have learned what it needed from its partnership with Zara and decided it was time it floated its own bet. 

But Burnt Toast has a long way to go. According to ICICI Securities, there were hardly any walk-ins when its analysts visited the Bengaluru store, though that might be largely due to how quiet the launch has been.

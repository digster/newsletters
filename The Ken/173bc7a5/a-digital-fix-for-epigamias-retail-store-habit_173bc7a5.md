A few years ago, I introduced my mother to milk in Tetra Paks. I told her she could buy a whole bunch of them at once as they would be good for six months. "Six months! What do they put in it? Is it even milk?" she said with a look of disgust. She was used to the milkman coming to her doorstep twice a day.
And then I bought her packaged yoghurt and told her it would last a couple of weeks unopened. "That's still too long," she said.
But companies selling yoghurt wouldn't mind a longer shelf life. They need a distribution operation that works like clockwork. They can't afford to have their products close to the expiry date right before the customer picks them up.
That's the problem 12-year-old Drums Food, which owns the Epigamia brand of Greek yoghurt, encountered during the lockdown. With supplies to stores disrupted and footfalls receding, Drums thought e-tailers would be its bridge to customers. But deliveries in some cases were so delayed that they rendered the yoghurt pretty much useless.
So Drums, which makes 100,000 units of Epigamia a day, launched its own online store in May. It followed in the footsteps of cereal brand Soulfull and dips and sauces brand Wingreens Farms. Drums, with nearly $50 million in funding to date, including from French food major Danone, promises to deliver within 72 hours in major cities like New Delhi, Mumbai, and Bengaluru. But last-mile logistics is hardly its forte and much larger brands have struggled with it.
Equally tricky is Drums' plan to test subscriptions, which the likes of juice brand Raw Pressery offer. Convincing Indians to pay upfront for something that is not essential is hardly an easy proposition.

Good Morning Ishan,

It's not news that India is facing a vaccine shortage. While India was administering almost 25 million vaccines in the first week of April, this week, in May, we're barely managing 7 million doses. May is also the month India started rolling vaccines out to its 18-plus cohort.
 
The government's solution to this shortage has been to engage PSUs, or public sector undertakings, in making Covaxin, Bharat Biotech's vaccine. Bharat Biotech says it can make 1 billion doses by December.
 
That's great! Or is it?
 
Let's look at the government's three PSU champions to get a better sense:
 
  1. Haffkine Biopharma in Mumbai—which is expected to dole out Covaxin doses in six months—would need a year to start production.
  2. BIBCOL in Bulandshahar, which has no experience with viruses whatsoever.
  3. And Indian Immunologicals Ltd in Hyderabad, which has successfully managed to repurpose its rabies vaccine manufacturing facility to make Covaxin.
     
One out of three isn't a good strike rate. Another PSU, HLL Biotech in Chengalpattu, which has started to invite tenders from pharma and biotech companies, is yet to hear back from any. A biotech CEO who evaluated the facility said the price quoted—Rs 750 crore ($103 million)—was outrageous since the site has no equipment, and a similar investment would be required to get it up and running.
 
But India is apparently well on its way to producing 150 million doses per month starting August till December. Or so India's Covid Taskforce seems to suggest in a spreadsheet shared with the media on 13 May.

And even as India's Covid Taskforce tries to put a positive spin on things with some feel-good (even if far-fetched numbers), independent academics are being excluded from vaccine studies and drawing up a vaccine roadmap. “Look at every other country which has moved quickly on vaccines, it is the academics who are doing the trials,” says Gagandeep Kang, one of India's foremost virologists, in today's story.
 
India must also be open to more technologies, better kinds of vaccines; even ones that can be upgraded. After all, there are new variants cropping up faster than you can finish saying variant. “Subunit vaccines will ultimately deliver what we want—safety, long term safety, good efficacy, and lower cost,” says K V Balasubramaniam, former Managing Director of Indian Immunologicals and a life sciences consultant, as quoted in today's story.

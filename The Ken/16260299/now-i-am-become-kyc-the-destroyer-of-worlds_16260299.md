Good Morning Ishan,
No. Robert Oppenheimer, the “father of the atomic bomb”, didn’t say that. Instead, he said “Now I am become death, the destroyer of worlds,” after witnessing the explosion of the world’s first nuclear bomb. (The quote is drawn from The Bhagavad Gita, though Oppenheimer’s translator [mistakenly gave him the word “death”](http://www.wired.co.uk/article/manhattan-project-robert-oppenheimer) for “world-destroying time”.)
In present-day India though, there is a particular mushroom cloud that’s spreading through multiple industries—KYC, or Know Your Customer.
During the last one year, it has disrupted sector after sector in its wake. Telecom got upended with the entry of Reliance Jio which signed up 100 million subscribers in less than six months powered by “eKYC”—a form of electronic verification using Aadhaar, India’s unique ID project. Kotak Mahindra Bank signed up 12 million customers for a new type of banking account, “811”, using just an app.
On the flip side, digital wallets have seen a precipitous drop in transactions during the last month, as high as 40-50%, over pending KYC of customers. Airtel, India’s largest mobile operator, was fined by the reserve bank and temporarily banned from doing eKYC because it misused the KYC procedure to surreptitiously open bank accounts for people who had no idea what was going on.
From insurers to fintech startups to credit card companies, there is only one word (well, three) on everyone’s minds—K. Y. C. Judging by the way every other company seems to be spamming, haranguing and pleading with their customers and prospects for KYC, it is clear that everyone seems to consider this an existential threat.
How did this happen? Why did KYC, a fairly global concept to ensure businesses know their customers, morph into a force of nature? Shashidhar tried to understand this by unravelling the various strands that seem to lead up to the perfect storm that is KYC.

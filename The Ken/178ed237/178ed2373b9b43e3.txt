In October 2013, Facebook spent $120 million to buy an Israeli company called Onavo. It wasn’t a social media company. It offered free utility apps that let users keep track of which of their apps used the most bandwidth and helped reduce their data usage by throttling apps running in the background.

Why would Facebook be interested in such an app?

Because it let Facebook secretly monitor all the other apps on a user’s smartphone. How often they were used, how much data they transferred, the profiles of the users, and even what content users liked.

Onavo enabled Facebook to spot potential rivals, like Snapchat, and ready its own rival offerings (for example, Instagram Stories) while rivals were none the wiser.

Something similar is happening in India’s pharmaceuticals space.

A consortium of India’s largest pharma manufacturers came together this month to buy two extremely boring-sounding companies—AIOCD Pharmasofttech AWACS Pvt Ltd and Pharmarack Technologies.

The two companies are barely known outside of pharma circles, but they perform a vital function. Like Onavo.

Together, they track drug sales across many of India’s largest cities and across nearly 16,000 distributors and 375,000 retailers. The two have representative data on which medicines are sold where and when in India. Data that will now be controlled by the pharma makers.

But why do pharma makers need to buy companies that track sales of their products, you ask? Surely, they know it!

No. Because drug makers sell their products to large distributors and the subsequent movement of their drugs is invisible to them for most parts.

“We currently have little sense, for instance, in Maharashtra, about what drugs people demand and consume every month beyond Mumbai, Pune, Nagpur and other large cities,” said a senior executive with one of the pharma majors part of the consortium.

In today’s fascinating—and free—story, Ruhi and Maitri team up to explain the conflict between pharma makers, distributors and retailers (one’s profit is the other’s margin) and the role that data can play. All the while a newer category of giants like Reliance and Amazon is waiting in the wings with their own private label generics.

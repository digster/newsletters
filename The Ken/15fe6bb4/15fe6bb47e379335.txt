'Press a button, get a cab' was Uber. 'Press a button, get something done' is Dunzo.

The Bengaluru-based startup enjoys a fanatical cult following, thanks to its almost magical service quality and virtually unlimited repertoire of tricks.

We've used it to get standing desks made, frame Goan tiles, order distilled water to top up a car's leaking coolant, send documents, buy Halloween masks, and order coffee and burgers. Dunzo co-founder Kabeer Biswas reckons he might've delivered more iPhones during the first 6 hours of its release in Bengaluru than Amazon and Flipkart combined.

At its core, Dunzo is a platform that connects a 1000-strong (approx) group of bike-riding delivery partners to a 100,000-strong customer base who use them to get things done.

Now, it wants to expand beyond Bengaluru to other cities, starting with Pune. And it wants to soup up its tech platform using world-class mapping and AI talent. But that will cost money. Serious money. $40-50 million kind of money.

It's a tough ask, in a tough market. Even though the ambition and the potential market is fairly audacious.

Will Dunzo manage to make the leap from a Bengaluru-favourite to a pan-India delivery layer for offline retail? Read Arundhati's story to find out.

Good Morning Ishan,

At an industry gathering recently, a senior pharmaceutical executive said something startling: “Let’s put statins in water tanks.”  His solution to the rising cardiovascular disease burden.

“Whaaat? You forget the epidemic of aches and pains that these drugs have already unleashed?” fumed a doctor. (How you thank the Newtonian law at play in such cases: Every PhD has an equal and opposite PhD.)  

The debate that day was inconclusive. But I am glad we have a story today which discusses exactly that, in the context of a new movement in the country to aggressively lower the statin therapy threshold. To bring down the (bad) cholesterol level to 50 mg per decilitre of blood. The Europeans have the most stringent threshold, of 70 mg/dL.

In a country where 2.1 million people died of cardiovascular disease in 2015, according to new data published in July, preventing 10 heart attacks out of 100 is a noble goal for statins. The catch though is, those who need it the most aren’t getting it and those who may not need it, will have it thrust upon them.

The Lipid Association of India, formed by cardiologists from corporate hospitals, is championing a risk score of 5%. It’s 10% in the UK, 7.5% in the US. Not to miss is the irony that the existing risk calculators are developed by respective countries for their population, not for India.

There’s more. A new drug got approved in India in July. Costing upwards of Rs 10K-15K/dose, it can greatly bring down the cholesterol level. 

Behind this wave, to a great extent, is an Indian-American researcher, who admits he is paid by pharma companies in the US to travel to India and educate cardiologists about the need for aggressive treatment.

Wonder why companies don’t fund development of an Indian risk calculator, to begin with. Anyhow, there’s a lot in today’s story to chew on. It’s also free.

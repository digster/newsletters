Reliance Jio blinks

Rohin

It can be hard to not be intimidated by Reliance Jio’s sheer size and momentum. Already India’s number 1 mobile operator, it raised over US$20 billion in the last few months from the global who’s who of private equity and technology. Jio wants a piece of the entire technology “stack”, right from silicon to apps and services, as Praveen explained lucidly over the weekend in The Nutgraf https://the-ken.com/the-nutgraf/jio-is-in-the-endgame-now/.

Why then, is it scared of its smaller rivals Vodafone-Idea and Bharti Airtel offering faster speeds and better services to their most valuable customers?

Last week India’s telecom regulator Trai put a hold on plans https://www.livemint.com/industry/telecom/trai-blocks-airtel-vodafone-idea-s-premium-plans-on-violation-of-service-norms-11594570583526.html announced by Jio’s rivals to offer better and faster services to their most valuable post-paid customers. Trai’s hold followed a complaint made to it by Jio https://www.thehindubusinessline.com/info-tech/trais-action-on-vodafone-idea-airtel-followed-a-letter-from-reliance-jio/article32077568.ece, in which it claimed that providing better services to one set of customers would “undoubtedly deteriorate the service quality available to another to another set of customers.”

A few days later though, a telecom tribunal set aside the hold https://www.hindustantimes.com/business-news/tdsat-grants-interim-relief-to-vodafone-idea/story-DdMHg1f3XTwouC5BU6ZyiJ.html and allowed Vodafone-Idea to continue with its offerings while the case was heard.

“According to the TDSAT order, Vodafone Idea alleged that Trai’s decision was “only to help the cause of Reliance Jio” and that the Mukesh Ambani-led company was not a necessary or even a proper party to the case. However, the tribunal has allowed Jio to be a party. Jio had pleaded that it will be directly impacted by the result of the case.”

Why would Jio be so concerned with the experience of a rival’s customer? Further, why would it say that it would be “directly impacted” by how its rivals treated their most valued customers?

Because premium customers are Jio’s Achilles’ Heel. Accounting for just 4-5% of subscribers, these customers contribute nearly 25% of revenues https://m.economictimes.com/industry/telecom/telecom-news/postpaid-the-new-battleground-for-indias-top-telcos/articleshow/70946286.cms.

Jio raced from non-existence to market leadership in a few years on the base of disruptive pricing. But three things happened as a result.

First, its all-4G network slowed down as millions of data-hungry but lower-value users sucked up bandwidth. Speed tests by almost all private players show Jio as having slower speeds now. Jio even sued Ookla, the company behind Speedtest.net, for declaring Airtel https://www.speedtest.net/awards/india/ as India’s fastest mobile network. It lost https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/tech/internet/court-dismisses-jio-plea-against-airtel-ookla/articleshow/59945361.cms.

The only silver lining is speed data gathered by regulator Trai https://myspeed.trai.gov.in/, which has consistently shown Jio as the fastest network for years.

Source: Tutela https://www.tutela.com/blog/india-state-of-mobile-networks-2020-0

Source: OpenSignal https://www.opensignal.com/reports/2020/04/india/mobile-network-experience

Second, one consequence of Jio attaining market leadership was that its market share crossed 30% https://www.indiatoday.in/technology/news/story/reliance-jio-now-biggest-telecom-operator-by-subscriber-base-1637617-2020-01-17. Per regulations, it is now considered a “significant market player” and is not allowed to undercut rivals on price.

Third, more customers are shifting their preferences from cheaper prices to better network experience.

This puts Jio in a double bind. If its rivals offer a better experience to their most profitable customers, the users it will likely poach will be lower value customers who will only weigh down its networks even more. 

Worse, it must guard against its most profitable customers churning out to its rivals for a better experience. 

This is exactly why Bharti Airtel in late 2018 culled 49 million of its own subscribers and decided to focus on going premium. We wrote a 2-part series on this last April. This is from part 2 https://the-ken.com/story/airtels-fightback-plan.

“Our strategy is to move [our] pricing up, get quality customers, and let the lower quality customers go to Jio, choking their networks further,” says the Bharti Airtel executive.

This means chasing the roughly 450 million subscribers (out of 1 billion) who it says contribute between 80-85% of the industry revenues.

And this is from part 1 https://the-ken.com/story/368337-2-airtel-is-changing-the-way-it-fights-jio/.

And in spite of its best efforts, Jio has not been able to make a sizable dent to Airtel’s post-paid subscriber base, estimated at between 50-55 million out of its overall subscriber base of nearly 290 million. An average post-paid subscriber contributes an ARPU that’s nearly 3.5-4 times a pre-paid one’s.

The bug in the insolvency food chain

Arundhati

When a company declares that it is insolvent, and its debts need resolving, there is an established order for who gets to pick on the assets first.

Source: Seeking Alpha

Creditors are second from the top having lent the bankrupt company money. In fact, that's what makes India's Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code, 2016 (IBC) special—when a borrower defaults with her repayments, the creditors gain control over debtor’s assets and take the decisions to resolve insolvency.

Because of this, as credit rating agency Crisil says https://www.crisil.com/en/home/newsroom/press-releases/2019/05/in-three-years-of-ibc-more-hits-than-misses.html: "There is a sense of urgency and seriousness among defaulting borrowers because losing their asset is very much a possibility if the resolution process fails.”

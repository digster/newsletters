Good Morning Ishan,

Today’s story will resonate with many of you. Because we’ve all been conned or rushed (to help our relationship managers) into buying insurance policies which we regret as soon as the ink has dried on the cheque.

You may even be one of those—nearly a fifth of the policyholders at the top five insurers in India—who did not pay their policy premiums in the second year.

A few days ago, the insurance regular backtracked on a big reform that would’ve helped this class of policyholders—who drop off and surrender their policies even at a huge loss. The IRDAI had wanted to sharply increase the surrender value that life insurers pay their customers.

But since the proposed changes pertained to non-linked or traditional policies, a category that comprises nearly the entire portfolio of India’s largest insurer, Life Insurance Corporation, it’s believed the regulator capitulated.

Anand and Narayanan have been digging into the surrenders, dropouts, customer complaints, and agent commission data to arrive at some valuable insights. You cannot miss this story.

Handsome is what handsome does

For four years, fast-moving consumer goods players Hindustan Unilever (HUL) and Emami have been fighting a trademark battle over the use of the word “handsome” in their fairness creams for men.

Emami accused HUL of rebranding its “Fair and Lovely” line of products for men to “Glow and Handsome” as a trademark infringement of its “Fair and Handsome” mark, the first fairness cream to be launched in India for men in 2005. This week, the court granted an interim injunction to Emami, restraining HUL from using the mark “Glow and Handsome”.

This case has left me wondering: why are legacy brands stuck with the use of words like “handsome” and “lovely” in their products when the world has moved on? Consumer preferences, their funnel of research for buying products, their response to brand promotions, etc. have all changed. And I’d wager, the change is significant.

Incidentally, The Ken ran a fascinating story this week on the shifting skincare preference in the Indian market and how legacy brands are lagging in being honest about the chemicals used in their products. The two names that stand out for me—for their choice of brand words—are The Ordinary and Minimalist, the former being an American brand that inspired two Jaipur-based Indian entrepreneurs to start the latter.

Instead of duking it out in the court, can the two parties reinvent their trademarks?

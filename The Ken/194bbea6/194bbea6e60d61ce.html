Transparency is good. So why does everyone still keep secrets? Trust thrives on transparency, but secrecy is human nature
By Ruhi Kandhari
5 min read

What if the best way to win trust at work is to address your problem?

This Tuesday, the head of analytics at an Indian bank confessed he had been getting something crucial wrong his entire career. He had assumed that hiding a piece of unpleasant information or a negative-emotion-evoking data point is better than revealing it. But, as he later realised, the opposite is true. 

In fact, highlighting an issue, addressing it, and even owning it makes for more effective (and even efficient) communication.

But this is not how people typically operate. In a sales pitch, for instance, the instinct is to downplay the problem to make the solution sound effortless. But it turns out that giving an elaborate account of the problem makes the pitch more compelling. The audience trusts you more when you acknowledge the hard part. The elephant in the room, once seen and named, stops being a looming threat and alchemizes into a beautiful plot device.

This analytics professional made the confession at The Ken’s leadership workshop while discussing “literary conflict” as defined in the storytelling vocabulary. It’s the tension that drives a story from its inciting incident to its climax. He was surprised to find that the presence of conflict had the power to make emails, all-hands meetings, pitch decks etc, more engaging. It helped the audience feel trusting towards the presenter.

But even though vulnerability and transparency are proven ingredients in a trustworthy organisation, asking professionals to let go of the ingrained need to hide information is not a simple shift.

Of course, it’s a paradox.

Why do secrets survive even in transparent organisations?

One of the most famous American authors and speakers on marketing, Seth Godin, wrote a poem called Hiding in his blog in 2016:

Your customers are hiding.

Your prospects are hiding.

You are hiding.

All of us are.

Hiding from change. Hiding from responsibility. Hiding from the prospect of feeling foolish. 

We hide by avoiding things that will change us. We hide by asking for reassurance. We hide by letting someone else speak up and lead.

We live in fear of feelings.

We’re lucky enough that the things we used to fear don’t happen so often any more, so now we fear feelings.

We will rationalize in extraordinary ways to avoid coming out of hiding.

When in doubt, look in the hiding places.

Olly, olly, oxen free.

In any society, everyone hides something. Some of the common reasons are a feeling of shame, fear of others’ reactions, being seen or judged as an imposter, or any reason that evokes a sense of being unsafe in an environment. And if Godin is right, it is this fear of feelings that may result in rationalisation. Some may look in the hiding places, but according to the latest research, others may not.

In a piece titled “Office Confidential: Keeping Secrets at Work Can Be a Lonely Job”, Nir Halevy, a professor of organisational behaviour at Stanford Graduate School of Business, argues that organisational secret-keeping is becoming more prevalent.

Along with Michael Slepianopen in new window of Columbia University and Eric Anicichopen in new window of the University of Southern California, Halevy proposes that organizational secret-keeping can increase stress and isolation while boosting a sense of meaning and importance on the job. “It’s fascinating that the same phenomenon can have opposite effects,” he says. “Keeping organizational secrets simultaneously hurts and helps employee well-being — in different ways.” This study is the first large-scale look at how workers are affected by organizational secrets in real-world settings.

The two polar-opposite effects that the researchers state are stress, isolation, and worry, and feelings of privilege and status because information is seen as a source of power. The secret could make someone feel lonely and valued at the same time. This study, thus, concludes that there is a reason why even when transparency in the workplace is considered progressive—and is directly linked to increased trust from all the stakeholders—a behaviour of hiding information and protecting secrets, too, survives. 

On one hand, research shows that nearly 87% of job seekers look for transparency in a future workplace, organisations with a high degree of transparency report profit margins that are 21% higher than average, and employees are more engaged.

Transparency builds trust, and trust is the cornerstone of accountability, results and creativity. Leaders at all levels better understand how and why decisions are made, and it fosters open dialogue. When people trust and believe in leadership and understand what is expected, they are more committed to their work. They know how their job fits into the company’s mission and how their work impacts its success

The Case For Transparency In The Workplace, And Its Impact On Organizational Performance, Forbes

On the other hand, Deloitte has called transparency a paradox. The consulting major acknowledges that an increasing focus on trust and transparency will have the greatest impact on an organisation’s success over the next few years. In fact, companies deemed “trustworthy” tend to outperform their competitors by up to four times by market value. But there is more to trust than just transparency.

Deloitte’s research suggests that trust needs mutual vulnerability that can develop only when people feel empathy and psychological safety. Overall, trust is an outcome of high competence and positive intent, underpinned by capability, reliability, humanity, and transparency. Just introducing transparency has downsides, especially, because it can invite a lot of resistance from employees.

Social scientists have identified various behaviors people undertake in response to demands for transparency, typically to protect themselves or manipulate a situation in their favor. These include dishonesty, hiding, cheating, posturing, productivity theater, window-dressing, and impression management—for example, using a mouse-moving machine to trick productivity-tracking software.

So, as leaders and organisations realise the value of transparency in organisational behaviour and in conversation, they need to first acknowledge the psychological need to hide and create a system that builds trust by balancing privacy and transparency.

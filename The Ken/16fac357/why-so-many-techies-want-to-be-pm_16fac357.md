Product Manager, that is.
Engineers are rebranding themselves, from project to product managers even though the two roles are like chalk and cheese, and the career leap, often a near-death punt. “People say anything to get in [to the PM role],” says a senior manager at a travel app company.
Conversely, as the digital tidal wave sweeps industry after industry, companies want to hire more and more PMs. The rise of appification has turned everything—from grocery shopping to dog-walking—into a product. Even box pushing retailers are hiring PMs. A role that traditionally existed as a filler between many functions now has a cool label.
On Naukri.com—India’s number one job search portal—listings for “product managers” jumped 14% between May 2018 and January 2020. Exactly double the growth of overall job listings on the website.
But here’s the thing. Several PMs that writers of today’s story spoke with, across several companies, say it’s ultimately about the X-factor. The role, as well as the person.
Part visionary, part executive. In smaller companies, a PM thinks and strategises like a founder; in large companies, she fights for ownership. No one particularly reports to the PM, but the PM is ultimately responsible. With a good mix of left- and right-brain thinking, a PM specialises in being a generalist. (It has a nice ring.)
The exact confluence of experience and skills, say PM themselves, is hard to replicate. Yet, online institutes like Udemy, UpGrad and Pragmatic Leaders have jumped on the opportunity to, well, train or churn out PMs. Courses cost anywhere from $2100 to $850, for a few months to a few weeks.
But can anyone really teach product management? Equally important, can recruiters sniff out the difference between project and product managers? Because, says a PM from Swiggy, the latter earn almost 60-70% more than the former.

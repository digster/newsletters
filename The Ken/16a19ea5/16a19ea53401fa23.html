Good Morning Ishan,

How good are you at giving second chances? What is your yardstick when it comes to judging whether someone or something is worthy of another chance?

Would you, for instance, go back to a tailor who messed up an outfit?

Re-hire an employee who quit for whatever reason? 

Put your trust back in the hands of a hairstylist who made you look like 90s reggae-pop singer Diana King instead of Lady Diana?

Or re-elect a government that didn’t keep its promises in its first term?

The number of chances one gets is, of course, directly proportional to the available options as much as what is at stake. But I find that sometimes, second chances are also doled out very easily. Like when you just succumb to a decision because you don't want to go through the effort of finding an alternative. I hate to admit that many times for me, it is just that. And when you do that, it almost always comes back to bite you again, usually in disastrous ways.

We saw a lot of second chances being taken in our stories, too, this week:

Rohin and Vandana had a classic case on Friday. While many may have written Airtel off in the face of Jio's rise and rise, Airtel believes it has a second chance at success. It's shedding metrics that the industry has traditionally valued—subscribers, minutes of usage (MoU) and data GBs/per day. The only things that will matter going forward, it believes, are revenues and profits. Speaking of second, this was the second part of a two-part story.

Ola believes electric vehicles are worth another chance. On Thursday, Pradip wrote about how even after a failed experiment in 2017 when Ola spent $8 million on an EV pilot, the company is taking a second chance on them. What changed? Well, you have to read the story for that.

For parents looking to supplement their children’s education, Byju’s, India’s largest edtech company, is all about giving students a chance to learn better. In our Wednesday story, Olina put Byju’s content to the test. She found that its pedagogy and learning outcomes show key design flaws. 

An IPO for a startup like Delhivery, India’s largest third-party logistics services provider, is a chance at making it big. A new lease of life. But an IPO makes for better optics more than better opportunities, argue Sumanth and Ashish in Monday's story. Enter second chance: the SoftBank Vision Fund. Even though it came offering $413 million, taking the money was no easy decision. 

But not all second chances are for the right reason, as Seema‘s scoop on Tuesday showed. She wrote about how India’s Department of Space created a new commercial space arm called Newspace India. Possibly due to the billion-dollar liabilities that Antrix, the Indian Space Research Organisation's commercial arm, currently faces. This is also a free story, so do read and share.

And finally, as the nation evaluates whether the incumbent BJP-led government should be given a second chance, Ruhi and Suraksha take a comprehensive look at the government’s healthcare policies, especially after it promised health for all in 2014. This one's a free story as well, do share it widely.

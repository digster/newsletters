Every so often, one of us at The Ken receives an email from Rohin, our CEO, asking us to transfer a few thousand dollars to an overseas bank account or to send him our bank account details for "urgent" reasons.
No, this isn't a call for you to start a GoFundMe campaign to help Rohin tide over these dark times. It's a reminder of just how widespread phishing attacks—where bad actors use fraudulent messages to trick people into revealing sensitive information—have become.
Over the years, phishing attacks have become highly sophisticated, involving not just believable email IDs but often the creation of realistic-looking websites and other such gimmicks to prove their authenticity. Hackers study social profiles of targets and tailor their attacks to seem as plausible as possible.
Today, the technique is a valued tool of every state-sponsored hacker looking to compromise a rival nation's secrets. And India is often on the receiving end of these attacks.
In February, one Pakistan-linked phishing attack allegedly compromised over a hundred armed forces personnel—both serving and retired. Using malware sent through the compromised email account of a high-ranking army officer, it succeeded in stealing a myriad of information, including documents regarding troop movements.
Another similar attack saw malware sent to over 1,000 government officials, even compromising computers that had information on Prime Minister Narendra Modi and National Security Advisor Ajit Doval.
And even as India battles wave after wave of these attacks, the basic steps to fend off phishing attacks aren't being followed. The Ministry of Electronics and Information Technology (MeitY) issued instructions that all official communications be done through an email network provided by the National Informatics Centre. Still, government officials continue to favour private providers like Gmail or even messaging services like WhatsApp.
A push for two-factor authentication was also largely ignored. Meanwhile, the teams meant to protect against cyberattacks are often underfunded and understaffed.

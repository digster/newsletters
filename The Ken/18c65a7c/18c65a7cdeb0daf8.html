Today’s story took me back to years ago when I first learned what digital banks are, how they are supposed to work, and how they are unique in terms of clientele.

Eventually, as a journalist, I covered various launches of the early forms of digital banking in the Philippines. At these events, the air was very celebratory and proud, with a specific tinge of unbridled optimism in what the banks were about to embark on.

A significant reason behind these lenders’ optimistic outlook was their aim to serve the country’s underserved areas—specifically, targeting the unbanked population and micro, small, and medium enterprises (MSMEs).

Interestingly, digital banks in neighbouring Indonesia have been echoing a similar narrative. In their early days, they all positioned themselves as the saviours of the MSMEs typically marginalised by traditional banks.

However, as is often the case with tales of heroes, rescuing those in need isn’t as straightforward as putting on a cape and swooping in at the right moment.

Today, Prima’s report delves into how Indonesian digital banks’ loans are declining in the MSME segment—the very market they intended to serve.

Data reveals that as of September, the country’s top three digital banks by assets—Seabank, Bank Jago, and Bank Neo Commerce—had outstanding loans of 7.9 trillion rupiah (US$506 million) in the MSME segment. It is a 22% decrease from the figure recorded in December 2022.

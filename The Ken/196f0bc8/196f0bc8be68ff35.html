Today’s story begins in Karnataka’s Narasapura Industrial Area, specifically one of Tata Electronics’ factories. Its workforce has swelled from 13,000 to nearly 20,000 in the past eight months because of the addition of a new facility. Those workers—mostly women—assemble iPhones. Three hundred new recruits are added to Tata Electronics’ lines each day. Two hundred quit.

Taxing turnover aside, Tata Electronics has also taken on Rs 9,080 crore worth of debt, all to service Apple’s desire to make more iPhones in India as it shifts part of its operations out of China.

Even though this is a low-margin business, the stakes are clear: Apple sources around 40 million iPhones from India at the moment. That figure is set to rise to 70–80 million by 2026. Tata Electronics currently makes around 30% of those phones, with Foxconn picking up the rest.

Tata Electronics is one of the few enterprises willing to bleed cash to secure its position as a major Apple supplier. It’s been doing this for three years, and has its sights on playing a larger role in Apple’s network of suppliers.

Shristi and Sumit took a closer look at Tata Electronics’ strategy to become a permanent presence in the global electronics value chain. Profitability is still a long way off for the company’s iPhone production, but it’s playing the long game. Check out today’s story for the details.

Once the envy of the retail world, Vedant Fashions is now struggling to break out of its growth rut. The Manyavar-owner’s stock, within three years of its listing, has fallen below the issue price. Its target consumers aren’t exclusive to the brand anymore. In this week’s Long and Short, Seetharaman writes about how Vedant Fashions lost its shine.

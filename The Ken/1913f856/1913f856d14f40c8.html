In the spotlight

Allen Career Institute sours on Kota, the city that made it a test-prep behemoth 

With students wanting to move out of Kota’s pressure-cooker model, the test-prep giant finds itself being one, at least for its 4,000-strong staff

Coaching giant Allen Career Institute has never seen its enrolments drop in Kota as much as in 2024—by over 30%. With Kota’s reputation hit by frequent student suicides and the nearby town Sikar attracting more coaching institutes, Allen’s stronghold seems to be hit. That translated into reduced pay for staff. The company also hired people in other cities, in a possible move to shift its base out of Kota. Now, it’s banking on the expansion and its bets in the digital space to balance the scales, even as the industry says it’s eating its own business.

A tiny discount fuels backlash inside Rs 75,000 cr IRCTC 

The Centre’s UPI-payments push is hurting IRCTC. But the chairman’s hands are tied over an unofficial agreement with the government

IRCTC officials want the Centre to retract a government mandate imposed in 2019. It’s denting e-ticketing—IRCTC’s highest-margin product and the second-highest contributor to revenue. The displeasure has moved beyond water-cooler conversations and reached IRCTC’s boardroom. But IRCTC’s chairman is unlikely to take the matter to the ministries. Thanks to a secret barter from 2019. Two by Two

Delhi pricked the Bengaluru bubble 

What makes Delhi startups resilient, and what happens to Bengaluru? 

For a long time, most people agreed that if you wanted to start a tech company, the “default” place to do it was Bengaluru. It had everything that young startups wanted—a vibrant city, access to tech talent, VCs, and even great weather. For over a decade, some of the most notable startups emerged from Bengaluru. Everyone believed this was an advantage, i.e., the ecosystem made these companies stronger, resilient, and more innovative.But of late… we aren’t so sure about that. Even though most people in Bengaluru believe that they are doing exciting, innovative work, it’s the startups from another city that are really succeeding as businesses. And that’s Delhi.

What happened when a PE giant challenged Jockey’s innerwear supremacy in India 

Under the watchful eye of Advent International, Modenik is floundering: missing targets, stumbling through integration, and shuffling leadership

In 2021, US-based private-equity giant Advent consolidated inner wear brands Dixcy and Enamor to challenge market leader Jockey. Despite enlisting former executive chairman Sunil Sethi to oversee the integration, it hasn’t panned out as hoped. Now, the PE firm is looking for an exit route while steering the brands towards a better business model. With a new chairman at the helm of Modenik, Advent’s task is cut out for them. Do Kunal Bahl and Rohit Bansal really have the Midas touch? 

The Snapdeal duo—thanks to exits from Mamaearth, Urban Company, and Ola Cabs—are often considered first-cheque wizards in India. But they don’t exactly break the mould; sometimes, it’s just a dash of luck

Snapdeal founders Kunal Bahl and Rohit Bansal often grab headlines for making 100–200X returns on their early-stage investments. In July, they exited Urban Company with Rs 100 crore. In August, Unicommerce, their portfolio company, is going public. But if you scratch the surface, the story behind their success isn’t all glittery. Much of the hype stems from their “spray and pray” investment strategy. [Read the full story]

Malaysia’s coffee giant Zus wants to outcool Starbucks in the Philippines 

After outpacing Starbucks in Malaysia, Zus Coffee wants to expand from 12 to 150 outlets in the Philippines by the end of 2024  

The Philippines consumes 420 million kg of coffee beans each year, making it the world’s fifth-largest coffee consumer. While coffee consumption remains high, it has evolved over time, especially among the younger demographics. International coffee chains are flocking to the Philippines, hoping to corner this new segment with unique offerings. One such is Zus Coffee from Malaysia, that has beaten the American giant Starbucks at its own game. [Read the full story]

IVF treatment can break the bank. So how are states offering it for “free”? 

India’s fertility rate has dropped below the replacement level. To bandage the issue, states are promising “free” IVF, but experts warn the devil’s in the details

With the World Health Organization (WHO) declaring infertility a public health emergency, there’s a major need for better and affordable care. In India, private fertility clinics charging big bucks have been fueling the IVF boom. Now, some states are talking about offering “free” IVF at government hospitals. But IVF is expensive and complicated, and most government hospitals just don’t have the setup to handle it—except for a few exceptions. [Read the full story]

What a story we have for you today. Before we get to it though, a quick refresher on one of the most discussed scientific paradoxes of all time: Schrödinger's cat.
 
Postulated by the late physicist Erwin Schrödinger in 1935, the thought experiment consists of a cat locked in a steel box along with a flask of poison in a glass jar, a tiny radioactive source, and a Geiger counter that can trigger the breaking of the flask if it detects radioactivity. 
 
Due to the wonderful nature of quantum mechanics, which is beyond both the scope of this email and this writer, the cat, after a while, is understood to be simultaneously alive and dead in a state known as quantum superposition. 
 
Something similar exists when it comes to the hacks or breaches of large and well-funded companies in India and Southeast Asia that end up in leaks of large amounts of valuable customer data.
 
Here’s where it gets interesting.
 
From the time hackers manage to ferret out customer data from the still unaware companies, to the time that the world comes to know about it from the media (or from a disclosure by the affected company itself), the breach exists in what can only be described as quantum superposition. 
 
It has simultaneously happened and not happened.
 
Meaning, if the hacker who has the data never releases it on the Internet or the dark web, who’s to say the hack ever happened?
 
This is where it gets even more interesting. And where Pratap comes in with his wonderful story of a 2-year-old cyber threat intelligence firm called Cyble, which claims to be part of the famed YCombinator’s Winter 2021 cohort.
 
It spotted the enormous and monetary arbitrage opportunity that lay in the Schrödinger's hacks of large and well-funded companies. Companies like Bigbasket, the Indian e-grocery company worth over $2 billion; RedDoorz, a Southeast Asian budget hotel chain that runs over 1,800 properties across Indonesia, Singapore, the Philippines, and Vietnam; Dunzo, a wickedly popular hyperlocal delivery platform headquartered out of Bengaluru. And even Indian PM Narendra Modi’s Twitter handle.
 
Cyble reaches out to these companies even before they’re aware of their breaches and offers an irresistible deal: for an expensive subscription to its annual threat intelligence services and some money to pay unknown hackers on the dark web, it can buy back all the breached data even before it is leaked.
 
The breach would not have happened at all.

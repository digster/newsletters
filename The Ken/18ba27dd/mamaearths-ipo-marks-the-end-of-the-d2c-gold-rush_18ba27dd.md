Good Morning Ishan,
In the startup universe, the definition of what a company does is sometimes stretched to fit a neat little label.
A business that buys fresh produce from farmers and sells it to supermarkets becomes an agritech bet when it’s merely glorified trading.
A startup that lets you buy medicines and book tests online is a healthtech when it’s a lot closer to e-commerce.
Direct to consumer, or D2C, is another such misleading label.
Even in the heady days of the pandemic, D2C brands were never quite D2C. Boat, the wearables brand, got over 85% of its revenue in FY21 from marketplaces like Amazon and Flipkart. Boat’s own website brought in just 2%.
“D2C” and “online-first” were being used interchangeably. But even that has come under attack lately as people have returned to in-store shopping, pitting D2C brands against FMCG giants in an arena where the latter has an unfair advantage.
Mamaearth, the personal-care and cosmetics startup set to list in the next few days, now gets one-third of its revenue from offline stores. Two years ago, the figure was less than a fifth.
I’d wager that’s partly why the demand for its shares was almost eight times its supply.
Even as D2C companies and investors celebrate the Mamaearth IPO as a watershed moment, Aakriti, through her sharp analysis, makes a case for why it’s anything but.

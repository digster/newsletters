Good Morning Ishan,
 
If wishes were horses, two think tanks, India’s Niti Aayog and America’s RMI, would paint Indian energy and transportation green today, rather than by 2030 or by 2040.
 
Since 2015, when Niti Aayog came into being, over 70% of Niti’s research reports on electric vehicles and related subjects have been co-authored by one particular organisation: RMI. It’s the new name for Rocky Mountain Institute, a 40-year-old non-profit that is known for pushing adoption of “market-based solutions”.
 
While it’s apparent that the influence of consultancies and think tanks on Indian policymaking has significantly increased in the last decade or so, RMI’s swift rise in India is a different story. (We wrote about Boston Consulting Group’s ascendance last year.)
 
RMI’s timing has been perfect. After it entered China in 2014, it was looking for an equally big market to find a footing in. Niti Aayog, meanwhile, was looking to establish itself. It deliberately wanted to set up a new ecosystem of think tanks because the earlier established ones like The Energy Research Institute (TERI) or Centre for Policy Research had perceived or real linkages with the previous political regime.
 
Even before its official entry in 2019, RMI had begun making inroads via workshops and advisory work. Today, as Shruti deftly reports, RMI is not only playing a bigger role in advocating certain policies, but also in their implementation, and management. It is pushing for certain technologies and financing models, and increasingly acting as the link between the government and corporations.
 
For an organisation that provides pro bono work to governments, it has an enviable list of donors. From the billionaires like Jeff Bezos and Warren Buffet to philanthropies like Rockefeller Foundation. Even Wall Street mavens such as Goldman Sachs, BCG, Wells Fargo, JP Morgan are standing behind it.
 
In the turbulent sea of climate goals and commitments, RMI perhaps acts like a guided submarine. For instance, it authored a three-part series on the need for advanced chemistry cell storage for energy right after the government announced a production-linked incentive scheme for it.
 
Following its stated roadmap—Think-Do-Scale—RMI is well on its “do” trajectory in India. Shruti has a fine story today. You mustn’t miss it—it’s also a free story: https://the-ken.com/story/us-think-tank-rmis-swift-rise-to-become-niti-aayogs-go-to-cleantech-advisor/ (10-minute read)

Fighting a pandemic is a zero-sum game. 

When the West African country Sierra Leone was battling Ebola in 2014, it witnessed a 39% decline in children receiving malaria treatment and a 23% drop in institutional births, according to Unicef. 

With most of the medical resources directed at the disease in question, everything else took a back seat. And the same has happened with non-communicable diseases (NCDs) in India over the past year. 

With India scrambling to curb the spread of the coronavirus, NCDs like diabetes, cardiovascular diseases, and cancer got short shrift. 

Take, for instance, those dealing with kidney failure, which is often a result of diabetes and hypertension. In February 2020, the month before India announced its first lockdown, India had 700 kidney transplants. A year later, there were just half as many. 

And now, with hospitals filling up again thanks to the second wave of Covid, the gradual improvement in the diagnosis and treatment of NCDs could well be reversed. This is cause for alarm as NCDs claim 5.9 million lives every year—that's 60% of all deaths in India. 

But, as Ruhi's sobering piece highlights, if at all there is a silver lining in this otherwise gloomy scenario, it's for health insurers, for whom 2020 was a record-breaking year.

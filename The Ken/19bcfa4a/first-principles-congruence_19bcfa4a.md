In the middle of watching Ricky Gervais’ latest Netflix special, Mortality, I was struck by one of the least controversial and most normal things he said.
You’re probably not going to believe me when I say that I immediately made a note of it. Because it was a very profound point.
What is our “favourite thing”? How many of us truly know it?
We’re taught in school to focus on studies and exams, because that determines our likelihood of getting into a good college. We spend our college years thinking about and optimising for the subjects and projects that will increase our likelihood of getting a good job. We spend much of our careers doing things that increase the likelihood of getting a promotion or salary hike.
We change ourselves to chase the outcomes we think are valuable. Along the way, we get used to that being the default. We tell ourselves that, one day, we’ll do what we really want. Our favourite activity. But we don’t realise that we’ve gradually lost the ability to even know what we want.
Coincidentally, (by now you must have realised that coincidence and serendipity is a recurring theme in our lives and this newsletter!) I came across this wonderful quote from the late American singer and comedian, Margaret Young: “Often people attempt to live their lives backwards, they try to have more things or more money in order to do more of what they want so that they will be happier. The way it actually works is the reverse. You must first be who you really are, then do what you need to do in order to have what you want.”
We’ve covered this concept, of course, in our edition on [“Dreams of Retirement”](https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/dreams-of-retirement/).
The subtext with much of the FIRE (Financial Independence, Retire Early) movement is that most jobs or work are crap. So, you suck it and get it done, so you can find joy later.
But there’s an opportunity cost to waiting. You lose the ability to discover your “favourite”. You learn to suppress it to get that other thing, which is external and keeps changing.
Instead, every opportunity should help us figure out what we want, who we are. We’ve talked about[ “Cognitive Dissonances”](https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/listen-to-your-cognitive-dissonances/) and [“Changing Your Mind”](https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/why-arent-you-changing-your-mind/) in past editions, both of which are based on exploring those feelings of discomfort when our selves and our lives are somewhat out of sync.
But what is it that we should be listening to? Or watching out for?
The things that intrinsically motivate us and lead to “autotelic experiences”. This is from Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi’s Flow, a book we discussed in the edition on [“Treasuring Your Frustration”](https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/treasure-your-frustration/).
The key element of an optimal experience is that it is an end in itself. Even if initially undertaken for other reasons, the activity that consumes us becomes intrinsically rewarding. Surgeons speak of their work: “It is so enjoyable that I would do it even if I didn’t have to.” Sailors say: “I am spending a lot of money and time on this boat, but it is worth it—nothing quite compares with the feeling I get when I am out sailing.
The term “autotelic” derives from two Greek words, auto meaning self, and telos meaning goal. It refers to a self-contained activity, one that is done not with the expectation of some future benefit, but simply because the doing itself is the reward. Playing the stock market in order to make money is not an autotelic experience; but playing it in order to prove one’s skill at foretelling future trends is—even though the outcome in terms of dollars and cents is exactly the same. Teaching children in order to turn them into good citizens is not autotelic, whereas teaching them because one enjoys interacting with children is. What transpires in the two situations is ostensibly identical; what differs is that when the experience is autotelic, the person is paying attention to the activity for its own sake; when it is not, the attention is focused on its consequences.

Richard Logan proposes an answer based on the writings of many survivors, including those of Viktor Frankl and Bruno Bettelheim, who have reflected on the sources of strength under extreme adversity. He concludes that the most important trait of survivors is a “nonself-conscious individualism,” or a strongly directed purpose that is not self-seeking. People who have that quality are bent on doing their best in all circumstances, yet they are not concerned primarily with advancing their own interests. Because they are intrinsically motivated in their actions, they are not easily disturbed by external threats. With enough psychic energy free to observe and analyze their surroundings objectively, they have a better chance of discovering in them new opportunities for action. If we were to consider one trait a key element of the autotelic personality, this might be it. Narcissistic individuals, who are mainly concerned with protecting their self, fall apart when the external conditions turn threatening. The ensuing panic prevents them from doing what they must do; their attention turns inward in an effort to restore order in consciousness, and not enough remains to negotiate outside reality.
I love the idea of nonself-conscious individualism. You learn to recognise and do more of the things that give you joy, not because they are only a means to external rewards.
Does that move us into a state of “coherence”? I’ll leave you with this post a friend forwarded to me a few days ago.
Congruent people compel us because they have little to prove; they have converged on an inner authority. Thus, when you encounter them, you don’t feel like you’re being enlisted in their ongoing arguments with themselves. You’re not recruited to shore up their self-image, or resolve their dilemmas. You’re liberated to be as you are—talking to them feels like entering open space. Their love isn’t grabby and manipulative, and they can say hard truths from a place of simple observation. They can deeply understand you without needing to suck up your essence, or merge with it. Being listened to in this way, by a person capable of it, is psychoactive; you hear yourself anew.
[The rare people who are solid](https://sashachapin.substack.com/p/the-rare-people-who-are-solid) by Sasha Chapin

Hello Ishan, Tanim here.

So yes, I already know what Rohin has written above, and today, I would like to connect that thread to our community book recommendations. Coincidentally, many of you have been reading books that fit the larger theme of trying to harmonise your life.
Sumedha Khoche recommends [ A Marriage at Sea](https://www.penguinrandomhouse.ca/books/771662/a-marriage-at-sea-by-sophie-elmhirst/9780593854280) by Sophie Elmherst.
“A couple defying convention in the 70’s setting off on a voyage from the UK to NZ. The man is asocial but adventurous. The girl is courageous and does not know how to swim. Their boat sinks after a dying whale breaks the hull. oh and they don’t have a radio because they wanted to do things ‘old-school’- be guided by the same tools that the ancient explorers were. Sophie Elmherst’s is a journalist and it shows in her attention to detail and her deft storytelling. The prose is not flamboyant or dramatic but she makes you feel as though you are on that raft, rolling with each wave, getting drenched in the storms and wondering how you will make it. It’s an insightful and tender account that covers many themes so skillfully- all in 250 pages, through the retelling of this extraordinary experience of an ordinary couple who survived 118 days adrift…a tale of endurance and survival but also marriage and love in such an “outlier” situation.This has been one of the most compelling and relatable reads of the year for me, because at its heart the book is about relationships..with our partner, with ourselves, with the ‘system’ that we develop to live our lives, who are we on an everyday basis and who do we become in extreme situations. Found myself re-reading and smiling at the truisms that pepper the book, like this one ‘What else is a marriage, really, if not being stuck on a small raft with someone and trying to survive?’”
But what happens when you find yourself at odds with the life you had envisioned? Gauri Mhaslekar’s recent recommendation, [The Wedding People](https://us.macmillan.com/books/9781250899576/theweddingpeople/) by Alison Espach offers a poignant and witty exploration of just that. Gauri wrote in:
“Surprisingly funny and unexpected, the book is about a woman who sets out to die and (call it a cliché or not) figures out what it means to truly live after 40 years of teaching herself not to.
‘So don’t even bother telling me you have plans, because I know you were planning on being dead right now,’ said the bride to the protagonist.”
And for a return to the fundamental questions of what makes us human, the aptly titled, [Foundation](https://gnomepress.com/16-foundation/) by Issac Asimov, recommended by Anubhav Dingra, is a good fit.
“With so much happening in the world right now, this sci-fi series takes you far ahead into the future and offers an escape to a world where humans have colonised the entire galaxy. But despite that, the psychological makeup of us humans will always remain the same which makes for interesting drama within the characters. The series has a total of 7 books but the original three are the highlight for me.”
But how many of us have the privilege of living a congruent life on our own terms when society sees us differently?
On that note, Kuldip Joshi and Harminder Sahni offer their recommendations: [Recitatif](https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/678624/recitatif-by-toni-morrison/) by Toni Morrison and [Ambedkar: A Life](https://www.alephbookcompany.com/book/ambedkar-a-life/) by Shashi Tharoor, respectively.
Here’s what they have to say about these books (again, respectively):
“It’s a story of two American girls who meet at an orphanage. We know that one is white and the other one is black but the author intentionally keeps the races of these girls ambiguous throughout the story. It’s a very interesting thought experiment and that too in Toni Morrison’s lyrical writing.”

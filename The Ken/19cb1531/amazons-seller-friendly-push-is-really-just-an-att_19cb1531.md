Good Morning Ishan,
On Monday, Amazon India announced that it was bringing seller commission fees to zero on products priced below Rs 1,000 on its platform. The company claimed it was a seller-focused platform and that its success is tied to the success of its sellers. A company [blogpost](https://www.aboutamazon.in/news/small-business/amazon-seller-fee-reduction-zero-referral-march-2026) also explained the economics behind the move:
Starting March 16, we are expanding zero referral fees to over 12.5 crore products, covering items priced under ₹1,000 across 1,800+ categories. We are also reducing Easy Ship fees by more than 20% for products under ₹300. These changes can save sellers up to 70% in total selling fees.
…
A 10x+ expansion of zero referral fees — from 1.2 crore products under ₹300 to over 12.5 crore priced under ₹1,000. Here is what that looks like for sellers:
- A fashion jewellery necklace priced at ₹999, sold through Easy Ship — a seller saves ₹224 per unit. That is a 69% reduction in total fees.
- Earphones priced at ₹798, sold through Fulfilled by Amazon — a seller saves ₹139 per unit. A 56% reduction.
- A T-shirt priced at ₹299, sold through Easy Ship — a seller saves ₹15 per unit. A 21% reduction.
But what was left unsaid was how the move is the global e-commerce giant’s desperate attempt to take back market share, not just in terms of customers but also its sellers.
In a press release, Amit Nanda, director of Amazon India’s selling partner services, said: “This move is designed to make selling on Amazon.in more lucrative and simpler, particularly for small businesses and entrepreneurs in tier-2 and tier-3 cities.”
By specifically targeting sellers in tier-2 and tier-3 cities, the company is essentially trying to gain back what it has been losing to other e-commerce companies like Meesho.
Nanda added in the press release that the move will also help sellers shift from a “business that is surviving” to “one that is scaling”.
For a lot of sellers, Amazon is the easiest platform to onboard. But once they are on it, they face the challenge of “high commissions”, which typically range between 5–15% of the product’s MRP, depending on the category, according to Renu Bisht of Commercify360, a consultancy firm that helps brands scale on platforms like Amazon.
This makes sellers want to look elsewhere. Other platforms like Meesho and Flipkart charge lower commission fees, or sometimes even zero, as one apparel seller told The Ken.
While Flipkart reduced its commissions to zero last [November](https://www.livemint.com/companies/news/zero-commission-on-items-below-rs-1-000-flipkart-announces-new-structure-for-eligible-sellers-what-you-need-to-know-11763118853877.html), Meesho has adopted this from the very beginning to successfully broaden its seller base. It now stands at about 8,46,000 sellers. This strategy, in fact, was one of the key differentiators among the e-commerce big names, which allowed Meesho not only to break into the Amazon-Flipkart duopoly but even beat the latter to an IPO.
But whatever its intentions, Amazon’s move is a welcome step as sellers can now maintain a uniform price for their products across platforms.
This, however, is not a completely new move for Amazon India.
Last year, the company did the same for products that were priced below Rs 300. And while that price point is considered e-commerce’s “sweet spot”, products below Rs 1,000 are even sweeter, because collectively, they account for 55–60% of total SKUs listed on the e-commerce platforms, according to Bisht.
Last year, my colleague, Nuha Bubere, wrote about Amazon’s change in focus:
There’s another agenda: to go head up against the likes of Meesho. To that end, the company also did away with the referral fee—the commission that sellers pay to Amazon for each product sold—on over 12 million products, across 135+ categories, priced below Rs 300.
Sellers on Amazon Bazaar, the site for cheap fashion and home products, continue to enjoy zero referral fees on all products under Rs 600, the company spokesperson said. “We’ve also lowered our weight handling fees—now Rs 18 less for shipments up to 1kg and Rs 15 less for heavier items. These changes are designed to help businesses grow with Amazon Bazaar.”

With the new move, the policy now affects over 125 million products listed on the site.
And there’s a reason for it. Meesho caters to a larger market. Of the Rs 83 trillion retail market, tier-1 cities account for 28% of sales, while the rest 72% lie in tier-2+ and rural markets, per estimates from Redseer Research, cited in Meesho’s [IPO papers](https://www.hdfcsec.com/hsl.docs/Meesho%20Ltd%20IPO%20Note-202512021622101142058.pdf).
Amazon, which has so far been stronger in metros, is trying to elbow its way into smaller cities, just like Meesho.
Focusing on the mass consumer who is not brand-obsessed has helped Meesho deliver. In the first nine months of FY26, the company reported a 37% year-on-year rise in net merchandise value to Rs 30,189 crore. And the number of annual transacting customers rose 31% year-on-year to 251 million users. The company [claims](https://investor.meesho.com/investor-web/_next/docs/quarterly-results/Q3_9m_Dec_31_2025_shareholder-letter.pdf) that it is the largest platform by “annual transacting users as well as orders” in India. It also reported a contribution margin of 3.3% in the same period, which is a rare feat.
As both Amazon and Flipkart are privately-held companies, their data is not publicly disclosed.
Now that Amazon has caught up to Meesho with a move branded as “seller-friendly”, the real challenge of making the platform “customer-friendly” remains.
Meesho has cracked how a value-seeking customer behaves by reverse-engineering its algorithm, an analyst who tracks the company told The Ken.
Amazon and Flipkart rely on keyword search to show products to their typical customers who type in a product name or brand. Meesho, on the other hand, forgoes this Western model to cater to window shoppers. Its customer base, it found, likes to browse, and if they happen to come across a good deal, they purchase the product. So Meesho made its search more discovery-led by throwing recommendations to customers simply browsing their apps.
In addition, Meesho is also running a pilot program for a limited set of customers, where products can be purchased on a month-long credit, just like your “kirana bhaiya (grocery shopkeeper)”, the analyst, who works for a Mumbai-based brokerage, added.
So Meesho is treading a delicate middle path, attempting at once to be both seller-first and buyer-first.
The company also makes money through its logistics services platform, Valmo, and through advertisement fees it charges its sellers. Its advertising revenue currently is at about 3% of its net merchandise value, and the company expects it to increase by 5–6% in the next few years, per a Kotak Institutional Equities report.
The expectation with the zero-commission strategy is that once sellers start making money, they will pay for advertisements and logistics as well.
Meesho’s stock is down 9% since its listing on the bourses in December, while the BSE Internet Economy Index is down 11.5% over the same period.
Amazon, which is already doubling down on profitability, has made it clear that it won’t let go of the large e-commerce market just yet. As an e-commerce expert put it, its next goalpost is “grabbing the land back”.

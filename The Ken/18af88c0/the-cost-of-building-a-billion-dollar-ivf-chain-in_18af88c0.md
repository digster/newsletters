Good Morning Ishan,
In my formative years, I knew only two sets of twins—one in my family and the other in my class.
But now, in my apartment complex, there are at least four pairs. Every time I see any of them, I can’t help but wonder if they are the result of in-vitro fertilisation (IVF).
They may not be, but it’s easy to make the association. Not just because twin pregnancies are more common in IVF but also because IVF comes up in conversations so often these days.
When I read Arundhati and Seema’s meticulously reported Big Story today, I could see that the growing popularity of IVF in India is more than just anecdotal.
And nothing reflects that popularity better than the rise of Indira IVF, a chain of fertility clinics in which Baring Private Equity Asia bought 60% for $650 million in July. Indira IVF is now worth $1.1 billion—the second-most-valuable IVF chain in the world.
What’s even more fascinating about Indira IVF is not that its market share is equal to that of all its rivals combined or that it has managed to do this in just 12 years.
Instead, it’s how the chain has transformed the business of IVF in India. Earlier, gynaecologists would refer infertile couples to IVF clinics. Then came Indira IVF, with its disruptive plan to go directly to such couples. It now runs over 115 centres across 20 cities.
But IVF is not like a spa treatment, so how did Indira IVF pull this off?

CO-FOUNDER AND EDITOR

Good Morning Ishan,

Technology, design, customer experience.

Pricing, sales, distribution.

Those are the defining attributes of two fan manufacturers, both market leaders in their own right—Atomberg and Crompton.

By launching energy-efficient brushless direct current (BLDC) fans in India nearly seven years ago, Atomberg caught the breeze. It also caught the tailwind last year from a government mandate on star ratings for fans.

Today, the company has half the market share in BLDC fans which comprise 20% of all fans sold in India. For a VC-funded hardware company, a rarity in India, this is as good a story as it can get. But then the VC playbook also lays down growth at breakneck speed.

Incidentally, Atomberg’s fan business is growing at nearly 2.5X the industry. But that’s not enough. It must get into newer products and break the mould, at least of design and technology. Or as one of the founders says, “solve a particular consumer problem”.

The nine-year-old startup has chosen a category where market leaders are nondescript, consumer loyalty is fleeting, and margins are higher than fans—electrical appliances.

How well-placed is Atomberg to remake the appliances market? Nuha brings you the story.

BCG comes to BSNL, again

Boston Consulting Group (BCG) is back at BSNL. To revive the state-owned telco from its near-death situation and at a fee of Rs 132 crore (US$16 million).

Nearly 15 years ago, BSNL hired BCG to create a “four-pronged revival” plan because even though it was the second largest telco by subscriber base, it was surviving only on interests from its cash reserves.

Today, BSNL is the fourth telecommunications company by subscriber base with less than 8% share in the wireless market. The Financial Express reported that BCG will “devise strategies and also help implement them”.

It’s not very public what happened to the first BCG plan because if it had worked, BSNL wouldn’t be at the precipice that it is at. I also learnt while writing this BCG story that its then India lead, Neeraj Aggarwal, took much pride in the BSNL contract.

Apparently, that vintage of BCG leaders wanted to use their global infra creds to win more such projects in India. The first opportunity came with the Delhi airport and then with the port revitalisation under transport minister Nitin Gadkari. Ports had hardly made money in India and with BCG, things started to turn around.

But this is 2024; BSNL’s subscriber base is eroding every month. And the government has slowly but surely been hollowed out.

So, while the new BCG-BSNL deal talks about the desired outcomes, as taxpayers, we should be asking where is the enablement piece in all this? In trying to turn around BSNL, will BCG create anything for the government so that the next time such a turnaround is required, the government doesn’t need a BCG? Or a McKinsey for that matter.

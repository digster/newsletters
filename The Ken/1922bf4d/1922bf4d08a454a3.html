What's next for Indian fintechs? The fintech–bank relationship is getting yet another makeover
By Arundhati Ramanathan
8 min read
View in browser

Good Morning Ishan,

Indian fintech has grown from being hot to just… sad. The excitement from the Global Fintech Fest, held just a month ago, has already worn off and weariness has once again set in. And before you say ‘You… Peee…’ let me stop you right there, because financial services are much more than just payments. 

So if you are a brave founder looking to set up a fintech, which way should you go now?

This week’s news about two neobanks will help you find some of those answers. 

First, there was Open, which partners with banks and lenders to give businesses a fully digital current account along with other services related to credit, payroll, etc. It was India’s 100th unicorn, back when unicorns were still a thing. And this week, we got to see its financials for the year ended March 2024, which showed just how tough the road has become for neobanks. Open’s revenue fell 17% to Rs 25 crore (US$3 million) and it continued to make annual losses of over Rs 100 crore (US$12 million). 

Then there was Jupiter, another neobank, which Techcrunch reported is looking to buy a small 5–9.9% stake in SBM Bank India. This is more of a partnership of equals, where both are trying to strike a safe landing after a turbulent flight. Jupiter offers its customers a slick user experience for banking products like savings accounts or fixed deposits offered by its banking partners Federal Bank and CSB Bank (formerly Catholic Syrian Bank).

But as The Ken wrote earlier, it has run into a wall there. So it is trying to use lending as its ticket to growth.

To grow lending, Jupiter needs more users. But drawing users for a banking experience by itself is also not that simple.

Jupiter’s non-bank ambitions hinge on its shaky neobank origins, The Ken

As for SBM Bank, it has been struggling to get back to business as usual after the Reserve Bank of India (RBI) came down hard on it last year. It was always a bank meant to be a partner bank to fintechs, never one to issue deposits and operate like a traditional bank. Now, it is likely looking for partners it can count on. 

So a partnership of any kind between SBM and Jupiter should help the two go some distance. 

But this is news of yet another prominent fintech joining the ranks of Bharatpe, Navi, Slice—all of which either attempted to get a banking licence or at least a stake in a bank. And that is an important chapter in the life of Indian fintechs.

Here is a brief history of fintech in India so far: 

2016–2019: Fintechs want to disrupt banks and get VCs to fund them. Banks see red. 

2019–2020: Fintechs want to partner with banks because they realise they can’t operate without them.

2020–2022: Fintechs want to be banks, because banks as partners were too restrictive.

2022–present: RBI won’t allow fintechs to be banks, so fintechs are settling for buying stakes in them. 

Buying stakes in banks may seem useful on the face of it, because these fintechs will have a seat at the table, and launching products the way they want gets easier. But a minority stake is actually not that beneficial, said a fintech executive who works at a large fintech.

“These are small banks with small balance sheets, so you would still need other large banks to partner with.”

In fact, it could even end up hurting the fintechs more than helping them, the executive added. Large banks could turn sceptical about working with fintechs that have a stake in another bank, because now they see you as competition instead of a partner. 

So in a way, this could set the clock back to 2016, when major banks saw fintechs as a threat. 

Incidentally, the only fintechs that are still seeing VC interest are the likes of M2P, which works with both banks and fintechs and helps them with software integration. This week, M2P raised US$100 million in a series D round. And then, there are the banks themselves. Lightspeed and Sorin Investments put in US$11.5 million in three-year-old Shivalik Small Finance Bank in May. We wrote about this deal here. 

So, if the buying stakes in banks doesn’t go as envisioned, what’s the next chapter for Indian fintech going to look like? Should they just close the book and move on to, say, quick commerce?

What else caught our eye

 

US Department of Justice sues Visa for violating antitrust law

The United States Department of Justice (DOJ), which oversees federal law enforcement in the country, is suing payment card services giant Visa. The Justice Department believes Visa is not compliant with its antitrust laws because the company has been imposing a list of ‘exclusionary agreements’ that would severely penalise merchants who use the services of its competitors. The DOJ is also alleging that Visa made payments to companies, including Apple, to limit their innovation in the payment services space.

Payments platform Mobikwik finally gets to have an IPO

Mobikwik Systems has received Sebi’s approval for an IPO to raise Rs 700 crore (US$84 million). The company plans to invest the funds towards growth in its core business, payment devices, data infrastructure, AI, and more. While Mobikwik had previously received Sebi’s approval for a Rs 1,900 crore (US$228 million) IPO in 2021, it had subsequently halted the process citing a downturn in the global equities market.

UPI now mandatory for applying to public issues of debt securities 

On Tuesday, market regulator Sebi directed individual investors who are applying to pick up debt securities for under Rs 5 lakh (US$6,000) to only use UPI to block the funds. According to its circular, this is to streamline the application process for public issues of debt securities, non-convertible redeemable preference shares, municipal debt securities, and securitised debt instruments with that of the public issue of equity shares.

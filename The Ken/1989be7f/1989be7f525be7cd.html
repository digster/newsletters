Welcome to another quick Trade Tricks roundup. 

India’s traditional (and still vast) network of fast-moving consumer goods distributors really do not like quick commerce. 

I mean, look at all the efforts they’ve mounted so far to get someone, anyone, to pull the reins on the segment—much of which we’ve covered here in this newsletter. 

In just over the past year, the All India Consumer Products Distributors Federation (AICPDF), which claims to represent nearly 400,000 FMCG distributors across the country, has written to the finance ministry warning about the rapid “fund accumulation” and “deep discounting” by quick-commerce platforms; complained to the Competition Commission of India (CCI) about “unfair practices” put in place by these companies; written to the food regulator alleging the sale of expired products; and even shot off an open letter to FMCG manufacturers urging them to halt supplies to platforms “that are undermining traditional trade”, and then threatened to launch a “non-cooperation” movement against manufacturers that didn’t respond to said letter. 

Their disquiet is understandable, especially given how quickly the segment is growing and its rather unprecedented impact on the small neighbourhood mom-and-pop stores who form the mainstay of offline distributors’ customers. We’ve written about this.  

…this is not the first time that kiranas are up against the brick wall. In the early 2000s, supermarkets such as Spencer’s and Big Bazaar challenged kiranaswith their proposition of more variety at lower prices. Then, in the following decade, e-commerce firms and their promising home deliveries for products that were also better priced tried to change the game.

However, neither dented the business of neighbourhood kiranas since the two couldn’t breach the latter’s moat, i.e. convenience. Quick-commerce firms, though, are targeting exactly that.

In fact, they go one step ahead with their doorstep delivery. They are changing not just the behaviour of the buyers but also the rules of the fast-moving consumer goods (FMCG) game.

Zepto and Blinkit threaten kiranas in ways that Bigbazaar and Amazon never could, The Ken

Well, it seems the AICPDF isn’t anywhere close to giving up. 

Because in an article on Sunday, Business Standard reported that the organisation has now followed up with additional details on its initial allegations to the CCI, as requested by the antitrust watchdog. Among other things, AICPDF is claiming that quick-commerce platforms:

“​​…had contracts with suppliers, and such deals often included prioritising stock allocations to their dark stores and reducing supplies to traditional trade”

“…used location-based pricing, by which consumers would get different prices for the same product, depending on where they lived.”

“…offered discounts of 35–50%, pricing products below what distributors offer.”

The CCI has already asked quick-commerce platforms to address these allegations, and while it hasn’t done so yet, it does have the authority to begin an investigation if it deems it necessary after evaluating comments from all the parties involved. 

That said, I wouldn’t be surprised if AICPDF opens yet another front in its running fight against quick commerce before the CCI manages to come to any decision. 

Tariffs and realignments

In other developments, President Donald Trump’s announcement of new higher tariffs on Indian imports into the United States is starting to turn large gears at India’s FMCG manufacturers. 

Trump has said that Indian exports to America will now be levied a tariff of 50%, citing India’s purchases of oil and gas from Russia. And that the new rate will come into effect on 27 August, making it the US’ most-heavily taxed trade partner in Asia. Unless, of course, India manages to strike some sort of a deal before the deadline. 

But it seems the country’s FMCG manufacturers are already starting to think about options. 

In an article last week, The Economic Times reported that major FMCG players such as Amul and ITC are starting to consider shifting their production facilities for the US market out of India and into more favourable locations—a.k.a, locations that do not attract the same kind of tariffs that Indian goods will—or even the US itself.  

While dairy brand Amul has been producing and selling milk in the US, making it more cost-effective, the company is now planning to extend it to cheese, paneer, and butter instead of importing from India, said Amul’s Jayen Mehta. He said dairy products already attract a high 60-70% tariff in the US, and the additional duty announced by US President Donald Trump will make Indian exports to US unviable from the price point of view.

An industry executive said ITC may consider exporting biscuits, frozen food and shrimps, and ready-to-eat products from Dubai. A final decision will be taken after a few months to see whether the US government continues the high tariff regime on India. ITC currently packages atta in Dubai for exports to the US since India has an export ban on wheat and wheat products, the executive said.

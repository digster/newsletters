Good Morning Ishan,
India is gearing up for a blockbuster initial public offering (IPO) unlike any it has seen before.
Hyundai Motor India Ltd (HMIL), the country’s second-largest carmaker after Maruti Suzuki, filed its draft red herring prospectus with the market regulator this month. This IPO is set to make its South Korean parent, the auto giant Hyundai Motor Company (HMC), very rich.
HMC plans to sell up to 17.5% of its 100% stake in HMIL, and it could make between Rs 21,000 crore and Rs 25,000 crore (US$2.5–3 billion) from the sale by year’s end.
However, my colleague, Anand, found that a few things don’t add up after poring over the IPO prospectus.
For one, the company’s expected price-to-earnings ratio—a key valuation metric—lags far behind peers such as Maruti Suzuki and Mahindra & Mahindra (M&M).
That may sound like the company is doing a good thing for Indian retail investors.
But it’s thanks to some canny moves that HMC made ahead of the IPO to milk Hyundai India, much like how the promoters of India’s largest aviation company IndiGo Airlines did a few years back at the time of its listing.
Don’t miss Anand’s astute [analysis](https://the-ken.com/story/hyundai-indias-ipo-pays-way-more-to-its-korean-parent-than-meets-the-eye/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter+) of what is expected to be India’s largest IPO.
Apple shuttered its buy now pay later product, yesterday, in just a year of its launch. It let people in the US buy things up to US$1000 and pay for them in four installments within 6 weeks.
Apple Pay Later, as it was called, was offered by Apple Financing LLC, a subsidiary of Apple Inc, and was responsible for credit assessment and lending.
It charged no late fees, collected no interest, and didn’t even report defaulters to the credit bureau for a missed payment. Sounds a lot like the cash-rich Indian fintechs of 2022.
No wonder it shut down. Apple, however, wound down on its own and didn’t wait for a regulator.
However, this is not the end of Apple’s financial services dreams. It is now going to play distributor and will work with other lenders and banks globally to offer flexible payment modes to users via credit card, debit card, and partnerships with lenders like Affirm.
If I were the head of India’s retail payments body, I’d be on the phone with Tim Cook—pitching him to include UPI via Rupay credit cards as one of the payment options in Apple Pay. And maybe also urge Cook to use the US$67 billion cash it has in hand to finance some cashback, while at it.

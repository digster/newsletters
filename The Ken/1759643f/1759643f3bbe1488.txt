In 1993, Sunil Vachani borrowed Rs40 lakh ($54,000 now) from his father to start a company. The new company was called Dixon and started as a contract manufacturer of televisions for GoldStar (you know it as LG now), largely for export to Europe.

Before starting Dixon, Vachani had spent time at his dad’s factory that made televisions under the brand Weston. Originally launched in the 1980s, Weston couldn’t keep up with competition from newer television brands like Onida and BPL.

It would take Dixon over a decade to evolve from making cathode ray colour televisions to LCD TVs, and then LED TVs.

Today Dixon makes televisions for the likes of Samsung and Xiaomi. It also makes washing machines. And phones—both feature phones and smartphones. It is India’s largest contract manufacturer.

But that’s not why Dixon’s stock is up 150% this year, even as India’s benchmark index is down 2.4%.

The reason is that Dixon looks poised to bag a significant portion of smartphone manufacturing moving from China to India. Including a lot incentivised by the Indian government.

How much? An ambitious $140 billion over the next five years.

If Dixon plays its cards right, it sees an opportunity to become to India what Foxconn was to China. The Taiwan-headquartered contract manufacturer rode the iPhone wave as Apple’s partner and grew to $178 billion in revenue. 

There’s just one problem. Foxconn is already manufacturing from India. As are peers Wistron and Pegatron (which has announced its decision to set up manufacturing). 

But Dixon has history in India. It has customers. It has insights. It has ecosystems.

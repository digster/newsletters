Good Morning Ishan,
 
At the top of the rarefied, secretive, and high-stakes world of strategic consulting sit two global giants—McKinsey and BCG. They tower over everyone else in size, aura, and ambition.

Between the two, McKinsey—“the firm”—is the older and larger one, globally as well as in India. It crossed $10 billion in global revenue in 2017. BCG, in contrast, is younger and hungrier. Last year, it earned $8.5 billion globally.

Hiring either of these firms isn’t for everyone though. Because they’re really expensive. And I do mean, really. It’s widely believed that there are perhaps 50-odd companies in India that can afford to hire McKinsey or BCG without breaking the bank. Just a “basic" project team comprising a leader and 2-3 consultants can cost $250,000 a month. Of course, basic is rarely enough, especially if the stakes are your company’s entire future.

In part one of an extensively reported story months in the making, Seema, our editor, starts by dropping a bombshell. In a call with over 80 of its alumni, BCG’s India head Alpesh Shah claimed to have beat McKinsey in India last year, on both overall India revenues and the number of partners. McKinsey vociferously denies this.

Starting from this dramatic upset, Seema traces back the arc of BCG’s two-decade journey to unseat arch-rival McKinsey in India. To do so, it employed an arsenal of weapons that McKinsey either ignored or lacked. From using their initial work with public sector banks to slide into financial services and then Fintech; to embedding themselves deeply into the unprofitable but powerful government sector; to taking canny bets on Aadhaar, India Stack, UPI, and Niti Aayog.

It’s a rare look inside a world where secrecy, prestige and connections rule roost and where “becoming the story” is abhorred: https://the-ken.com/story/how-bcg-pipped-mckinsey-to-1-in-india/ (12-minute read)

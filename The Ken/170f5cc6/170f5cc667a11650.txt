Good Morning Ishan,

While it may seem easy from the outside, good interviews are incredibly difficult for journalists to pull off.

Why so?

You see most folks who are interviewed fall under one (or more) of the following categories:

The substitute PR agent: The one who dishes out "quotable quotes" originally intended for grandiose public relations statements to paint the company with mythical candy-floss colours

The egoist: The one who loves the sound of his own voice and believes that an interview is an ideal opportunity to massage his own ego

The wannabe capitalist philosopher: The one who has read (and liked) all of Naval Ravikant's tweets and repackages those thoughts as his own original insights

The shrinking violet: The one who will clam up when posed with the first uncomfortable question... or better still, walk out of the interview.

The masochist: The one who agrees to feature on Arnab's TV shows just to be humiliated and shouted down

Good interviews, on the other hand, offer us a unique and unparalleled ringside view into an entrepreneur's mind; surgically dissecting the worldview that helps create and shape great companies. These interviews tease out hitherto-hidden facets, both ambitions and fears, that drive entrepreneurs towards great success as well as big failures. 

Today's story by Rohin is a great example of one such good interview where he dives deep into the mind of Sriharsha Majety, the CEO and co-founder of Swiggy. 

Majety and his team have successfully parlayed Swiggy into a verb that most of urban India uses for ordering food to be delivered to their homes—how did they get here and what lies in the future, especially in these uncertain times? A time when folks will avoid going out to eat and most restaurants are likely to be shut. Is this an existential threat to Swiggy, or is it an unprecedented demonetisation-type moment?

Ed Set Go
Mon, 28 Oct 24
Your weekly newsletter on the most critical pivots in edtech and education in India.
Good Morning Ishan,
The United Kingdom is [short](https://schoolsweek.co.uk/how-england-is-locked-in-global-tussle-for-teachers/) on school teachers. So is the US. In the UAE, international schools have become big recruiters for qualified teachers who can teach science, mathematics, and technology. Guess who has rushed in to fill the gaps? Indians.
Well, not just Indians. Filipinos, Ghanians, and Jamaican teachers, among others, are in high demand in many western countries, which are struggling to renew their depleted teacher corps as people who quit during the pandemic haven’t really come back to teaching.
In the US, this problem is [particularly acute](https://www.cnn.com/2019/10/06/us/international-teachers-us-shortage/index.html)—schools have struggled to staff positions for consecutive years, and are more heavily dependent on either substitute teachers, or teachers with alternative certifications that usually take less time—sometimes as little as a few months—to complete. The need for qualified teachers is at an all-time high.
Just like blue-collar labour in the 60s and 70s, and techies in the 90s and 2000s, it looks like teachers could become India’s newest export. And where there’s a demand and supply problem, an edtech solution, and investors, are never too far away.
This October, a company called Suraasa [raised](https://entrackr.com/2024/10/suraasa-raises-6-mn-in-new-round-from-reach-capital-ets-and-others-2/) a modest US$6 million from a clutch of investors led by Reach Capital. Suraasa is an online teacher-prep platform that offers “masterclasses” to improve teaching skills, an international certification recognised by countries such as the UK, and most importantly, has facilitated a pathway for Indian teachers to teach in the UAE, and now increasingly in the US. Some of Suraasa’s early backers include Cred’s Kunal Shah; Vamsi Krishna, one of Vedantu’s co-founders; and Manan Khurma from Cuemath.
Suraasa’s platform is trying to do what companies like Scaler did for techies and would-be product managers—upskill teachers to help them earn more, keep up with the latest trends in their industry, and move on to higher positions.
Except, teaching—online or otherwise—isn’t easy to fit into a few training sessions. It’s a combination of theoretical and practical skills, wrapped up within a human being, who, by default, must have immense determination and staying power. No one who learns how to teach can accurately predict how complicated and tough a classroom of constantly distracted students can get.
Upskilling, though, can’t be Suraasa’s only game. Even if it aptly met the local needs of Indian schools or edtechs, online teacher upskilling isn’t going to be as lucrative as, say, online test-prep.
But if you add international schools, dollar salaries, and work visas into the mix, that becomes a whole other ball game.
Edtech models have already tapped into the teacher talent pool that’s often readily available in India. With varying success, edtechs such as Brightchamps have used Indian teachers to cater to an international student audience. In its heyday, Whitehat Jr also hired scores of Indian teachers to take coding classes for kids from different parts of the world. The salary arbitrage worked to its advantage, and parents in countries like the US, UK, or the UAE could get qualified help for their children without breaking the bank.
Sending teachers physically to other countries is a much more involved process. It means making sure a teacher’s qualifications are legit, that they have the skills to teach in a different context, and finally, that they meet all the necessary visa requirements. At scale, that’s no easy task.
Suraasa claims to have trained 1,000-plus teachers since its inception in 2018, with an aim to get to 5 million. What’s interesting is that Suraasa’s platform isn’t just meant for Indians—it claims that it makes teachers from other countries job-ready too. This opens up an international market for training, sort of like edtech company [Eruditus](https://emeritus.org/newsroom/eruditus-secures-150-million-series-f-funding-led-by-tpgs-the-rise-fund/)’ play to upskill professionals from several countries.
Suraasa hosts “masterclasses” in things like classroom engagement, social emotional learning, and using AI for assessment, all the right buzzwords in teaching right now. If you will, that’s the top shelf of its offering. Right below the veneer, though, is what I think is the real engine driving its model: getting teachers get out of the country and into a higher-paying job in a foreign classroom.
| Source: Suraasa’s website |
The first clue is that it has a whole section dedicated to “speaking in English like a foreign teacher”. Teachers from the Philippines and India do face difficulties when addressing an American classroom, so it makes sense that Suraasa offers this training.
Suraasa’s best known for something called the Professional Certificate in Teaching and Learning, or PgCTL, an online teaching certification that’s accepted in the UK, Ontario in Canada, and Australia. A paid programme, PgCTL seems to be the entry point for teachers to start availing the job opportunities Suraasa says it has “carefully curated” for them. On the demand side, Suraasa appears to have tied up with schools, or job portals, to get trained teachers into jobs. And these job opportunities can come with different perks, according to its press release—from free airfare to rent-free accommodations.
Like all companies solving for a demand and supply gap in labour markets, Suraasa’s going to be responsible for quality. It will have to keep updating the teaching qualifications constantly—and more so as AI starts to impact the learning process–and it will have to make sure that its international placements rise consistently. In countries like the US, where each state has its own, often subject-wise teaching requirements, Suraasa will have to help teachers navigate the regulatory complexities.
At 1,000 teachers, maybe this is manageable. At 5 million, they’re going to need several, country-specific teams to manage this.
Scaling qualified teachers isn’t easy. It’s the exact problem that nations have dealt with over generations. Now, in the developed world, teacher salaries and benefits don’t compensate for the burnout and mental stress that has steadily increased since the pandemic. Putting foreign teachers into the same situation may ease the immediate gaps, but it’s likely that new imports are going to need more support, just to help them fit in.
Accents are just the start.

Good Morning Ishan,
 
I won't lie to you. It's not been an easy week. 
 
The last time I was this hungry was 2012. Long before I'd even heard the words "vegan" or "keto", or rolled my eyes (hard) at people who'd joined these "diet cults", I had unwittingly turned both. My boss challenged me to drop the five big whites—rice, sugar, wheat, flour and milk—for a week, and document my experience in seven witty diary entries, to be turned into an article for the magazine we worked at. All week, I walked around in a hypoglycaemic rage, much of which was directed at this ex-boss. I knew then, quite conclusively, that I loved carbs more than I loved any other human being.
 
The experiment ended with my piece. I swore off trying to be healthy. But time and age don't leave you alone. And their most visible, vicious stamps are on your body. So I'm trying again, without the ironic eye-roll, to give up on carbs and sugar for good. I've joined the ever-increasing fringe cults of annoying keto-ists. 
 
Whoever said health and happiness go together was a cold-blooded liar. But I seek out spots of bright refuge in otherwise dull, salad-filled days. Even as my colleagues in the newsroom break down current events, stocks that have fallen, companies that are about to, Twitter rants and office gossip, little do they know I'm not listening at all. Instead, I'm dreaming of a perfect pastry box. With fluffy meringues, triple-layered trifles, dry tea-cakes full of raisin-y goodness and sinful slabs of deep, dark chocolate ganaches. 
 
So when I re-visited our stories for the week, I realised they were the perfect confectionary mix.
 
Plum in the middle of the week was Arundhati's three-layered, coffee-drenched tiramisu. It delivered a strong punch, in taste and content, to the Google-Amazon-Facebook trio, who, through alleged antitrust practices, predatory pricing models and serious privacy breaches, have angered regulatory watchdogs and competition bodies across three continents. But no one is more irked than Dina Srinivasan, Margarethe Vestager and Lina Khan—three women across the world who have each built the global debate on regulating these tech giants. Will Indian authorities follow suit? A long, thoughtful (and free) read, best served with a side of biscotti. (A few days after the piece, news broke that our very own Competition Commission is investigating Google.)
 
On Thursday, Harveen uncorked the secrets on India's largest craft beer enterprise, Bira. It reminded me of lemon tarts, which often surprise you with their powerful tanginess. Their bold, out-there-ness. Launched in 2015, Bira's novelty as a "craft" brand quickly made it a household name. Soon enough, its heady success opened the doors for upstart competitors like Simba and White Owl, and over 40 more brands. Their relatively quick climb in market share and taste preferences has Bira worried, as management exits and a potential fund crunch have put a spoke in its growth story. A must-read for all beer lover and lemon tart enthusiasts.
 
Monday was my own story, about how the Indian electric vehicle sector plans to charge up its ambitious growth plans. There were so many colourful characters in this one—CEOs of EV companies, manufacturers old and young, freshly minted start-ups with aggressive funding runways. I immediately thought of the tiny meringue sandwiches they stack upfront in pastry shops. As more B2B businesses adopt EVs in their fleet, to deliver people and packages alike, companies like SUN Mobility are setting up battery-swapping stations to service this need. On paper e-commerce fleets and battery-swapping are a match made in EV heaven. But without a common battery standard—much like round and fluffy is the standard for meringues—it's practically impossible for third-party energy providers like SUN to scale up. In a fractured EV market, who's going to make the first move?
 
On Tuesday, Ruhi wrote a cracker of a story on how SRL Diagnostics—India's biggest diagnostics chain—has struggled for 10 years to find a loving home. It's a deeply reported, well-crafted story about one of Indian pharma's most familiar faces. But just because it's familiar doesn't mean it's delicious... I mean, good. Her story took me straight back to the many disappointing encounters I've had with black forest—India's favourite birthday-cake flavour. Ubiquitous in nature, they hardly ever live up to the hype. Much like black forest, SRL is a victim of its own reputation. Tainted by association with Ranbaxy, SRL hasn't been able to raise money from the market like its peers. Nor is the IHH group, Fortis' newest buyer, interested in taking this diagnostics chain into its fold. Will SRL, the pharma sector's black forest, ever find a taker? 
 
Friday, Seema and I whipped up a soufflé of a story. And a kindly researcher, from the University of Kentucky—Gregory Erhardt—set the ball rolling. Or rather, got the mixer going (I don't really know how soufflés are made). Erhardt and his colleagues proved, conclusively, that ride-hailing companies like Uber and Lyft have significantly worsened congestion in San Francisco since their launch. What's more, ride-hailing has caused already congested areas to get much worse, often leading to gridlocks that may even double travel times. We analysed the new study through an Indian lens and found that the situation in our cities is most likely as bad, if not worse. With an almost 90% increase in taxi registrations in some urban centres, will Indian cities fail to act in time? Do read and make your own conclusions. Soufflés aren't everyone's thing.
 
The final treat in our box of written goods was contributor Tanvi Joshi's piece on India's very own opioid crises. It's the perfect end to the week—a chocolate eclair with a generous filling of whipped cream. Tanvi teases out, one by one, the abundant reasons why even mild opioids like morphine aren't administered by Indian doctors to ailing cancer patients. Arbitrary regulations on opioids aside, pain-management as a science itself isn't really well known or even practised by cancer doctors. Fixing the broken system of regulations, opioid distribution channels and doctor education, on the judicious use of opioid drugs, will take time. Something that cancer patients rarely have. It's also a free story, so do share widely.
 
I hope you enjoy sampling this mix of stories. I, for one, have already had my fill. And while you spend your Sunday looking up new brunch places with OTT menus, do spare a thought for my sugar-hungry soul. Keto-ed up or not, there's no harm in living vicariously.
 
See you next week folks, with a brand new box of chocolates.
 
Sincerely starving,

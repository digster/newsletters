Good morning,
In a new, post-Covid world order, inclusivity should be an organising principle. Pandemic-battered cities can make housing affordable for returning migrant workers. Skyscrapers can be eschewed for “greener” buildings. Even household items, like mops and wipes, need to rebrand for a more gender-equal household.
Regulators, too, should be more inclusive. After all, payment companies aren’t the only fintechs.
For the last time this week, let’s dive in.
|
The payments overkill
Arundhati
We get it. India beats any other country with its digital payments savvy. But it's really time to move on from payments. The country's central bank, the Reserve Bank of India, has set up yet another panel to study the “use of Quick Response (QR) codes to process contactless transactions in light of the ongoing Covid-19 situation”.
The panel is in the final stage of submitting its report, says The Economic Times.
|
The committee – comprising academicians, scientists, and industry executives – has been asked to figure out the economics of QR deployment at merchant locations, the benefits of having an interoperable and open-loop system as well as recommend guidelines for standardising deployments.
|
|
|
These are all problems well addressed. The benefit of an open and interoperable payment system—through which you can send money from a payments app to someone who doesn't have the same app—has already been established with the plain success of the Unified Payments Interface (UPI). As for the economics, any banker will tell you that QR code deployment is cheaper than other payment systems. Does it really need academicians and scientists to weigh in on it again?
It was just last year that RBI constituted a panel led by payments crackerjack Nandan Nilekani to review the status of digital payments, which also looked into QR codes.
|
|
|
Even the regulatory sandbox, which is supposed to be a rules-free environment for companies to experiment with, has been opened only for payments companies. There are many fintech initiatives that can use the kind of attention payments get. Account aggregators are still in limbo. Neobanks are operating in the blind. What is the status of peer-to-peer lending in India? Even within payments, issues of fraud and success rates of the new payment systems are still unaddressed. A panel to look into any of this, now, would be very useful for the industry.
It is time to move on from sharpening an already sharp payments axe.
|
|
Housing for all
Olina
Over 30 million migrants made their way home—many on foot—against the relief of a once-in-a-lifetime pandemic. It exposed massive fissures in the economic safety net for those that migrate to cities in search of better-earning opportunities.
So when it comes to building self-reliance, and re-positioning itself as a manufacturer for the world—à la China—India first needs to build for the returning migrant worker first. Andy Mukherjee, in a column for Bloomberg, says the first focus area should be affordable housing:
|
“Giving rural migrants an incentive to return is crucial to restoring economic activity to pre-Covid levels. But there’s an opportunity here to do much more. For India to industrialize, rethinking the housing situation will be as important as freeing the urban poor from large medical bills and helping them build retirement savings. If the country of 1.3 billion people wants to be a factory to the world — the next China — it must start by giving workers low-cost living quarters.”
|
Homes Can Shelter India’s China Dream After Covid, [Bloomberg](https://www.bloomberg.com/opinion/articles/2020-07-11/india-s-china-dream-depends-on-housing-migrant-labor-after-covid?sref=d6JWZbVx)
|
|
|
|
Mukherjee’s argument is that instead of rueing the loss from unsold premium housing stock, cities could instead leverage tax breaks and cheap government land to create a new asset class. If workers can be given decent housing in cities, India has a chance to create manufacturing hubs, full of individuals who can consume goods made for them.

From paying vendors to setting up a factory or offices, most companies cannot do without debt. And whenever they need to borrow, they turn to "arrangers", who could be small-time chartered accounts or dedicated desks at large banks.
But three-year-old Vivriti Capital wants to shake up this time-tested practice.
Through CredAvenue, its online debt marketplace, Vivriti makes 500 lenders—including State Bank of India and HDFC Bank, India's largest state-owned and private bank, respectively—available to a clientele of 1,200 companies. The platform is not just for loans; it also enables corporate bond issues and supply chain finance, among other kinds of debt.
CredAvenue's pitch is that it standardises credit evaluations and offers templated debt structures that lenders can choose from. As a result, lenders and borrowers are saved a lot of the hassles that they otherwise can't avoid. A process that would otherwise take as long as four months could be wrapped up within a few weeks through the platform.
And the pitch seems to be working. Since inception, CredAvenue has facilitated $8.8 billion of debt, which it hopes to take to $13.6 billion by March 2022.
But CredAvenue is not without its shortcomings, argues Arundhati in her rigorous story. With Vivriti itself being a lender on CredAvenue, there are concerns over conflict of interest. And once a lender and a company strike up a relationship on the platform, they could very well take future transactions offline. Old habits, after all, die hard.

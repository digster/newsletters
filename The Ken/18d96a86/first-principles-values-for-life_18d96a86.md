There were 277 of you who took the community survey from our 14 January edition ([“The Third Value”](https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/the-third-value/)). In case you weren’t one of them, or have forgotten the survey altogether, here’s a refresher.
- We asked you what your top three values for life would be
- Then we asked you what in your opinion the top values for children should be, growing up
- To help qualify responses, we asked if your top three values for yourself were different from the ones you suggested for children.
- Lastly, we checked if you were a parent
I love surveys and I cannot lie. Not so much the close-ended ones with drop downs, but the open-ended ones where you have no idea what answers you’re going to get. Designing them is a pain though, and summarising them even harder.
Across the 277 of you who took the survey, we got nearly 190 values. But we filtered out over 60% of those because just one of you had mentioned it, for example, “Magnanimity”, Rhythm”, “Shamelessness”, or “Prosperity”.
Thankfully, my teammates Jonathan, Anushka, and Hari helped painstakingly crunch all the raw data into patterns I could process. We were left with nearly 70 values that at least two of you agreed upon. Of them, I made a chart that shows the top values that at least 10 of you agreed upon. The size of each box corresponds to the number of people who mentioned it.
Here’s another one I made which is much more subjective, fair warning. I took all values that had at least two of you agreeing upon them, then tried to cluster them on a 2×2 matrix (you know I can’t resist them, [“Creating and embracing dilemmas”](https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/creating-and-embracing-dilemmas/)). Mind you, this is a very subjective and reductive interpretation of these complex values into binary classifications. You might arrive at a very different way to see the same values, and that’s okay.
On the x-axis I plotted values I felt ranged from individual or internal (values that drive you and don’t have much to do with others) to collective or external (values that make us behave towards others in a particular way). On the y-axis I plotted values that (to me) were emotions and feelings versus those that were either conscious thoughts or things that led to actions. Here’s how I saw the results:
What do you see? I saw a significant clustering in the individual x action quadrant, probably because of the professional bias we have as a group.
Things got more interesting as I tried to plot how people thought about values for themselves versus for children. A majority of you felt there are no differences in values between you, or between what you thought ought to be the values for children.
Lastly, there are the top 10 values—split between values we desire in ourselves versus those that we desire in children. I’ve used arrows to highlight value places that have a marked rank difference. For instance, resilience seems to be much more important for children (as viewed by us adults) than it is for ourselves. Conversely, empathy seems more important as an adult value than a child one. Could it be perhaps because we see children as already empathetic? Or that we ourselves are not so? There are also a few values that appear uniquely on each list. We want kids to be fearless, but are we?
I hope these results offer some nice Sunday food for thought for you. I would love to discuss the implications of these, but perhaps we should reserve that for an in-person meetup, whenever we manage to make that happen? 🙂
Hi everybody, this is Anushka, here in my day-time role as a podcast producer. Every week, I try to persuade you to listen to the most recent episode of the First Principles podcast because I truly do think that if you like reading the First Principles newsletter, then you’re going to enjoy our podcast. This week, I was wondering if this truly works—would I interrupt my newsletter-reading time on a Sunday morning to listen to a long-ish podcast?
Perhaps not.
So, let me switch it up a little bit this time. I’m going to tell you why I really liked our recent episode.
For this week’s episode, Rohin spoke with Vaibhav Gupta, the CEO and co-founder of Udaan. I’m sure you’ve heard of the company—it’s a B2B trading platform and in 2018, it became the fastest startup to rise to unicorn status (this mantle has since been passed to Krutrim). While researching the startup before the conversation, I remember being convinced that there was a crucial strategy that Udaan had cracked to get here. I was excited for Vaibhav to break it down.
But strategy is not what they have cracked. A company like Apple, Vaibhav says, has cracked strategy. But Udaan…
“We are trying to build a Walmart.
Or an equivalent, similar business. This business is all about details. It’s all about daily execution. It’s all about nuances.
The rice which gets bought in Bangalore is different from the rice which gets bought in Mysore. And if you don’t make the nuances right on a daily basis, you could be an unprofitable business versus a profitable business. So our business requires that.
And I think, to me, as we work through our business, our customers and our vendors and try to keep getting the playbook right – we’re realising that the number one thing is hard work.
If you have people who enjoy working hard, then 80% of your job is done.”
It’s the execution that Vaibhav and Udaan have doubled down on. Details. Repeatable tactics. And playbooks.
Vaibhav is direct, sharp, and answers questions with deliberate detail and examples. Usually, I listen to conversational-format podcasts like this one during my morning commute—I can drift in and out, pay attention to my route, entertain fleeting thoughts on my to-do list, etc. But this episode made me pay a little more attention to the stories, numbers, and tactics being discussed. So I think I’d rather park it for evening walks, exercise, or chores.
So, if listening to this episode now isn’t possible, click on this link and save it on your favourite podcast platform? Come back to it in the evening, on your Sunday walk.

“I’ll be more enthusiastic about encouraging thinking outside the box when there’s evidence of any thinking going on inside it.”—Terry Pratchett
Hi Ishan, welcome to another issue of Silent Sunday. This is Jonathan and that’s a quote from my favourite author by a long mile—an admonishment I’ve always held close to heart and one that has served me well in life, both personal and professional.
After all, haring off to explore new continents when you’ve never been to the next town is, for starters, perhaps a bit too adventurous?
What the always quotable and always hilarious Terry Pratchett does not talk about here, though, is the inevitable consequence of becoming a seasoned veteran of your box of choice.
Boxes, especially the ones you choose for yourself, get comfortable. And breaking out, even when you’ve outgrown them, is hard.
I mean, just ask Frog, who chose this box when he was a wee kitten of just eight weeks and has since refused to move on for 28… months. (And yes, Frog’s my cat; don’t @ me).
The other thing about boxes? They very often become monotonous, if not boring even—a realisation that struck home when I was sorting through our Silent Sunday submissions this week. Most were, as usual, beautiful landscapes from far and wide; some lovely shots of historical monuments too. I had some of my own candidates lined up, all falling squarely within those familiar boundaries (surprise, surprise).
But then, there were two that jumped at me. They were different, fresh—at least to my eyes. Shots breaching the lines that we seem to have started drawing for ourselves.

Hi, it’s Anushka again. In a few weeks, I will complete a year at The Ken. Do you, too, use milestones like these to think carefully about just how you felt this time last year? I remember I was nervous, excited, jittery. To be specific, I was spending two weeks at a writers’ workshop in Pondicherry, unable to shrug away thoughts about what my new life would look like. I came back to Bengaluru, refreshed.
Papery pink Tabebuia trees dotted the road to our office on my first day. It was a new beginning; this city’s version of spring. Maybe you think it’s cliche, but I love coincidences like these!
We’re all in the ‘new beginnings’ mood here on the First Principles team, spending time thinking about fresh (re)starts. I was thinking about a book I read last year: [The Odd Woman and The City](https://www.goodreads.com/en/book/show/22929524)—a memoir from Vivian Gornick, an American journalist and essayist. It’s a disjointed, indulgent book, I found. But Gornick persuades you to exercise a little patience, and leans into a very personal story about finding your bearings in a new city. It taught me a bunch. It’s the perfect ‘spring’ book, if you ask me. So, is there one for you? A book that braced you for a restart? For a new way of thinking?

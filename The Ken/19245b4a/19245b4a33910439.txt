Swiggy is even further behind Zomato than you thought. And in the business that matters the most 
 By Seetharaman G https://the-ken.com/writers/seetharamang/

3 min read

Good Morning Ishan,

After Swiggy confidentially filed for its US$1.25 billion initial public offering (IPO) in April, I argued https://the-ken.com/story/swiggy-is-at-the-mercy-of-zomato-for-its-ipo/ that the fate of the issue was tied inextricably to Zomato’s standout track record over the past year. 

[Swiggy] has squandered its dominant position in both food delivery, where its market share is down to around 45% from over 60% in 2018, and quick commerce, in which it controls just 32% compared with 52% two years ago.

Swiggy’s investment bankers have an unenviable task at hand. There are two reasons to invest in Swiggy, according to the senior mutual-fund executive quoted earlier.

“One is business-model confidence. So if Zomato is worth, say, Rs 100 (US$1.2), and Swiggy is valued at Rs 80 (US$1), and I think Swiggy is going to be the eventual winner, I’m fine investing at Rs 80,” they said. The other reason is a deal too good to pass up. “Swiggy is worth Rs 80, but I’m getting it for Rs 50 (US$0.6), so I put money in it.”

Right now, investors are bound to lean towards the latter, given the clear edge Zomato has over Swiggy. So it’s not a question of whether Swiggy should be discounted relative to Zomato, but by how much.

Now, Swiggy’s IPO document https://www.sebi.gov.in/filings/public-issues/sep-2024/swiggy-limited-updated-drhp-i_87047.html is there for everyone to pore over, and the company is looking even worse in comparison with Zomato than most people would have anticipated. 

To be fair to Swiggy, it doesn’t seem to have ceded space to Zomato in food delivery since 2022, and continues to control around 45% of the market, at least as of March. And they both have a similar average order value (AOV). But that’s as good as things get for Swiggy. 

For every monthly transacting user on Swiggy, there are 1.5X on Zomato. As of March 2023, Zomato had 20% more restaurants listed on its platform. A year later, the gap had widened to 25%. 

Most crucially, Zomato’s food-delivery business reported an adjusted Ebitda—a measure of operating performance—of over Rs 900 crore (US$107 million) in the year ended March 2024, a far cry from Swiggy’s Rs 47 crore (US$5.7 million) loss. The latter may have pared its losses sharply over the past couple of years, but it’s hard to overlook the fact that Zomato followed Swiggy into this business and not the other way around. 

Food delivery is not the vertical that potential Swiggy investors will salivate over, though, as I wrote https://the-ken.com/story/swiggy-is-at-the-mercy-of-zomato-for-its-ipo/ in May. 

For any flotation, a lot hinges on the timing. Had Swiggy chosen to list a year or so ago, only food delivery is what it would have been sized up on. Quick commerce was little more than a distraction. In fact, Zomato investors had punished the company for its decision to buy the quick-commerce firm Blinkit in July 2022.

In this scenario, Swiggy could have possibly counted on a duopoly premium in food delivery, never mind its declining market share. After all, the chances of a third player shaking things up seemed slim.

But now, quick commerce is where all the action is, and Zomato has flung a Blinkit-shaped Molotov cocktail at its competition.

Blinkit doesn’t enjoy a big lead over Swiggy Instamart in dark-store count. But the value of products sold on Blinkit in the year ended March—at roughly Rs 12,500 crore (US$150 million)—was 1.5X Instamart’s. What’s even starker is that Blinkit’s revenue from operations was 2.4X larger. 

This could mean a combination of things: Blinkit sells more products (such as electronics and apparel) that come with relatively high margins; it negotiates better terms with manufacturers, mostly of fast-moving consumer goods; and it charges brands more for advertising on the platform. If Blinkit continues to outgrow its competition, its ability to bargain with brand owners will only become stronger, further widening the gap with the likes of Instamart. 

Make no mistake, Swiggy is anything but a lightweight. It still controls a little under half of the food-delivery industry and a third of quick commerce. But valuations for listed companies are highly relative, more so than in private markets. So Swiggy’s pitch to investors has to be that they are getting a better deal than they would with Zomato.

Good Morning Ishan,
 
Oyo, the star of Softbank’s eye in the Indian startup space, wants to conquer the world. And why not, if you have $800 million in venture funding to spend in order to do so, with another $200 million on the way. And the promise of more to come.
 
There’s just a small problem. The “world” it must confront before it can conquer is nothing like its home market India. The hospitality landscape is vastly different around the world. China, the US, Europe, Russia, South America, Africa…none of them are cakewalks. The reasons range from power, homegrown players, fragmentation, regulations, and markets that are still too nascent. 
 
But Southeast Asia is another story. In fact, it’s the El Dorado for tech startups these days. A population of 600 million, of which over half are under the age of 30, and nearly half live in urban areas. 350 million Internet users, $30 billion in online travel, and $14 billion in online hotel bookings.
 
"Simply put, Southeast Asia is more promising than India,” says Ashish in his detailed story about Oyo’s SE Asia ambitions.
 
Which is why Oyo is committing to spending $200 million in the region over the next 5 years. By trying to build the McDonald’s of hospitality, but in reverse. IMHO.
 
McDonald’s became the world’s largest restaurant chain by creating an innovative franchise model that allowed it to control quality and generate predictable cash-flows from each restaurant. Between 80-90% of its restaurants are franchised. The company owns the land on which most of its restaurants stand, which it leases back to franchisees. Franchisees also pay it for setting up a new restaurant and licensing its brand.
 
Oyo is attempting to do this, but in (unicorn-style) reverse. It approaches existing hotels, often individual ones, and convinces them to hand over a large part of their room inventory. Which Oyo then promises to sell via its own site and those of other online travel players. To sweeten the deal, it will often not just guarantee a minimum payment for the rooms it handles, but also pay some of it up front as a sign of good, well-funded unicorn faith. The hotel owners are still responsible for the daily running and upkeep of their property.
 
In return, it keeps 20% of the room’s sale price as its commission.
 
But this model is fraught with many risks. Which is what Ashish’s story is about. Also the opportunities. And the competitors.

Good morning Ishan, how much music do you listen to? Me, I listened to ~4,800 minutes in January. That’s over three full days, if I did nothing but that.

There are many things I hate about streaming music services (the default quality, the focus on continuous engagement, the replacement of artists and albums with moods and playlists, etc.), but you cannot deny that their convenience has allowed me to listen to much more music than I might ever have otherwise.

Music for me is somehow more immersive than other art forms, like, say, movies or books. It is the art form and medium that most often transports me across time and space.

For instance, earlier this week, I decided to re-listen to Angelo Badamenti’s magical soundtrack for the most unclassifiable of TV shows, David Lynch’s Twin Peaks. Perhaps I was nostalgic for timeless TV shows as I drew to the final episodes of the final season of The Wire. The end of a great series always leaves you with a temporary void for a few days. That void is easier to fill these days thanks to Wikipedia, Youtube, and the (pre-AI) wonderful internet.

After finishing Twin Peaks, I remember scouring the internet to read explanations, critiques, analyses, and even a four-hour video essay!

Oh, I forgot. We were discussing music.

The Twin Peaks soundtrack is wonderful on its own, but boy, if you’re a fan of the series, it is a place you can go to.

It is whimsical, sad, and intriguing. But much more than those, it is dreamy, atmospheric, evocative, and haunting. Just like the series!

Likely the most influential and beloved television soundtrack of all time, Angelo Badalamenti’s dark jazz mood piece for Twin Peaks moves with a distinctly slow, sinister, sensual elegance. It was the second collaboration between the composer and director David Lynch, following 1986’s landmark Blue Velvet. At an initial meeting, Lynch, who didn’t yet have a script, described the feel of the show: a girl alone in the woods while the sycamore trees sway in the wind. With Lynch in his ear demanding it be slower, Badalamenti improvised the haunting, iconic “Laura Palmer’s Theme”.

The rest of the Twin Peaks score moves in similar shadows, stalking between noir moods and romantic swoon, cool jazz and warm dream pop. It’s a musical universe befitting Twin Peaks, Washington itself: slow-motion tempos, velvety synths, gently brushed drums, vibraphones, twangy guitar, simple melodies, dissonance and a liberal coating of reverb.

– Apple Music

As I finished the soundtrack, the algorithm in my head shuffled things and recommended another atmospheric, sad, and haunting soundtrack, also from an auteur director partnering with a talented composer—the 1980 Malayalam movie Manjil Virinja Pookkal (“The Flowers That Blossomed In The Snow”).

I don’t even have a very clear memory about the entire movie, because I must have watched it in the 1980s as a kid, but boy, is it another movie-music combination that is atmospheric (much of it was shot in Kodaikanal) and brooding! Over three decades later, I still get images of the mist, the hills, and the brooding every time I hear the songs from the movie.

When asked about the track Mizhiyoram, Jerry recalls Fazil describing the scene: ‘The heroine is cleaning the garden, plucking flowers, dusting off spider webs, and tidying up the room. As she goes about her chores, she hums a tune softly….’

“That was the beginning of the song,” says Jerry. “The instrumentation was kept very minimal, just a touch of tabla, one guitar, a small rhythm section, and a flute. That was enough. After all, when a young woman hums to herself at home, there’s no need for the grandeur of a symphony orchestra.” He was told later that the same song would reappear in the other side of the story, sung by the protagonist. “So I expanded the original tune, arranged it for a 40-piece orchestra, and personally gave the music score to each section. Yesudas sang it beautifully, and the picturisation was also done very well. That’s the story behind the two versions of the song,” he adds. 

‘Songs of Manjil Virinja Pookkal were created spontaneously,’ says Jerry Amaldev as the film completes 45 years, The New Indian Express

1. Bad outcomes aren’t the same as bad decisions

Our minds process an incredible amount of information through our experiences every minute of every hour of every day of our lives. Most of it, it ignores and discards. A very small proportion of it, it plays close attention to (“focus”). But a non-trivial portion of it is processed in the background, seen but not necessarily with focus or attention; filed, but not necessarily classified. They sit in our subconscious over the years, forming a web of experiences and observations that we’ve had, but lacking a very sharp conclusion or learning. We don’t even know this is happening.

Then, one day, something happens that magically “clicks” into a gap that we didn’t even know existed. And in the process, connecting many memories and experiences into a meaningful pattern. We suddenly know consciously what we could only feel subconsciously.

One of those things happened to me earlier this week. And all it took was one word—“Resulting”.

“Take a moment to imagine your best decision in the last year. Now take a moment to imagine your worst decision.

I’m willing to bet that your best decision preceded a good result and the worst decision preceded a bad result.”

…

“I have yet to come across someone who doesn’t identify their best and worst results rather than their best and worst decisions.” 

Thinking in Bets, by Annie Duke

When we conflate the quality of our decision-making with the quality of the outcomes of those same decisions, we engage in what poker players call “resulting”. Putting the results cart before the decisions horse.

It is unbelievable how many times I’ve made that same mistake over my career. Ask me about my best decisions, and I’ll hand-pick the ones that led to successes. Ask me about my worst decisions, and I’ll pick the ones that led to failures.

Take a moment to reflect on that. If I asked you about your best decisions, how many of them would be ones that led to failed outcomes? (If you did have some, congratulations, you are an evolved decision-maker. I’d love to hear your examples, if you’d care to share them with me. Write to me.)

“In a consulting meeting with a group of CEOs and business owners, one member of the group identified firing the president of his company as his worst decision. He explained, “Since we fired him, the search for a replacement has been awful. We’ve had two different people on the job. Sales are falling. The company’s not doing well. We haven’t had anybody come in who actually turns out to be as good as he was.”

“That sounds like a disastrous result, but I was curious to probe into why the CEO thought the decision to fire his president was so bad (other than that it didn’t work out).

He explained the decision process and the basis of the conclusion to fire the president. “We looked at our direct competitors and comparable companies, and concluded we weren’t performing up to their level. We thought we could perform and grow at that level and that it was probably a leadership issue.

I asked whether the process included working with the president to understand his skill gaps and what he could be doing better. The company had, indeed, worked with him to identify his skill gaps. The CEO hired an executive coach to work with him on improving his leadership skills, the chief weakness identified.”

“In addition, after executive coaching failed to produce improved performance, the company considered splitting the president’s responsibilities, having him focus on his strengths and moving other responsibilities to another executive. They rejected that idea, concluding that the president’s morale would suffer, employees would likely perceive it as a vote of no confidence, and it would put extra financial pressure on the company to split a position they believed one person could fill.”

“Finally, the CEO provided some background about the company’s experience making high-level outside hires and its understanding of the available talent. It sounded like the CEO had a reasonable basis for believing they would find someone better.

I asked the assembled group, “Who thinks this was a bad decision?” Not surprisingly, everybody agreed the company had gone through a thoughtful process and made a decision that was reasonable given what they knew at the time.

It sounded like a bad result, not a bad decision. The imperfect relationship between results and decision quality devastated the CEO and adversely affected subsequent decisions regarding the company. The CEO had identified the decision as a mistake solely because it didn’t work out. He obviously felt a lot of anguish and regret because of the decision. He stated very clearly that he thought he should have known that the decision to fire the president would turn out badly. His decision-making behavior going forward reflected the belief that he made a mistake. He was not only resulting but also succumbing to its companion, hindsight bias. Hindsight bias is the tendency, after an outcome is known, to see the outcome as having been inevitable. When we say, “I should have known that would happen,” or, “I should have seen it coming,” we are succumbing to hindsight bias.

Those beliefs develop from an overly tight connection between outcomes and decisions. That is typical of how we evaluate our past decisions.” 

Thinking in Bets, by Annie Duke

You see my point about how sometimes a single word can unlock a vein of insight stretching back years? Do read Annie Duke’s book.

But I wanted to talk about a song that is close to my heart:

Ottamuri Vakkumayi from Romancham (2023), sung by Pradeep Kumar, with music and production from Sushin Shyam, and lyrics by Vinayak Sasikumar.

I distinctly remember listening to this the first time in theatres. Till then, the movie was a blast: perfect comedic timing, likeable characters, interesting plot with enough intrigue and mystery to keep you hooked. But the moment Sushin’s keys started playing alongside his smooth humming, I could feel the energy of the entire theatre shift.

Then, a minute into the song, Pradeep Kumar and Vinayak Sasikumar pull your heart in and crush it with “Paaraake nin nere viral choondum neram ochayillaa sakshi njan…”, which translates to “As the whole world blames you, like a silent observer, I watch everything.”

To give you context, when not being India’s best active music director (a hill I will die on), Sushin Shyam has an alter-ego, as the keys and synth of The Down Troddence, a Rolling Stone award-winning heavy metal band from Kerala (who I am proudly also friends with).

And yet, you could not have guessed that if you heard this gentle, beautiful, heartfelt track, with some tasteful use of synth and quartet. 

Recommended mode of listening: Your best pair of headphones, closed eyes on bed at night, when contemplating what this life is all about, or if you get a side seat on a cold night ride.

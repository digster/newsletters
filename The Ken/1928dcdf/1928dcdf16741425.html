What does Trent know about lab-grown diamonds that Titan doesn’t? The two Tata Group companies have chosen diametrically opposite paths for their jewellery business
By Nathan Narde
5 min read
View in browser

Good Morning Ishan,

Last week, India’s salt-to-steel conglomerate Tata Group entered the lab-grown-diamond business. And despite its strong presence in India’s jewellery market (and a US$20 million investment in American lab-grown gems seller Great Heights), it wasn’t through 30-year-old Tanishq. 

Instead, it will be Trent, Tata’s budget-fashion retailer, that will spearhead the group’s push into the space. 

Artificial gems haven’t yet caught on in India, but there are takers. Senco Gold and Diamonds, another jewellery retailer, announced just a couple of weeks ago that its new brand Sennes would sell, among other things, lab-grown stones. And it’s not the only one; sales of lab-made stones in the country are estimated to be growing 15–20% annually. 

Trent will sell its own lab-grown diamond jewellery at five Westside stores, to start with at least, and a 1-carat solitaire engagement ring will cost around Rs 24,000–29,000 (US$285–345). At this price, the underlying cost of a 1-carat gem would be just around Rs 13,000–17,000 (US$155–200), according to a recent Kotak Equities report. 

That’s a pretty tidy advantage over natural diamonds.

…on an average, Pome’s pricing could be at a 30% discount to Rs 15,000–20,000 (US$180–240) SKUs of natural diamond-studded jewellery, and an 80–85% discount to high value Rs 5,00,000+ (US$6,000) natural diamond-studded jewellery.

Kotak Equities

The brokerage even wonders if the new business is another Zudio in the making. 

Be that as it may, if Trent can do this, why not Tanishq? 

Lab-grown diamond prices have seen quite a significant fall over these past two years, now costing only a fifth of what they did in 2016, as per a Financial Times report. But so have natural diamond prices, which have registered a 25–30% drop from their 2022 peak.

But not only has Tanishq eschewed lab-grown fare, it has doubled down on natural stones. In August, it signed a long-term partnership with De Beers, the world’s largest natural diamond producer, to “connect more Indian consumers with the rarity and preciousness of natural diamonds”. 

C K Venkatraman, managing director of Tata-run Titan Company, Tanishq’s parent, has his reasons though. During an earnings call in August, he said diamonds signal affluence in India much like a “big house” or “big car” do. And the nouveau riche don’t want to show off something that is still widely believed to be a poor substitute. 

Bottom line: it’s not worth Titan’s while to fret about the potential threat from artificial stones. This is a no-brainer, because the market for them in India is still trifling and Titan has a lot riding on natural diamonds. Studded jewellery contributes one-third to Titan’s jewellery sales and more than half to its operating profits. It isn’t hard to see why Tanishq runs circles around rivals on profitability. 

Trent, by contrast, is a formidable retailer largely because it has kept value shoppers happy over the years. First through apparel chain Westside and then through Zudio, a cheaper cousin, and Star, a supermarket chain on the mend. Zudio got its start at Star outlets just the way lab-made stones have now been launched within the confines of Westside. 

Trent’s wager is perhaps that its customers are more open to these gems than those of Tanishq—they see lab-grown diamonds as a fashion accessory rather than a piece of jewellery. This is possibly why the analysts at Kotak believe Trent’s newest business could disrupt Caratlane and Mia, Tanishq’s mid-market brands. 

What’s more, as far as Trent’s experiments go, this isn’t one to break the bank. And considering how spectacularly its last bet—Zudio—has turned out, investors would be happy to cheer Trent on.

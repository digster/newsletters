“If someone’s putting so much money behind it, there’s got to be something behind it. But we are not getting it…We didn’t get it for days. We tried everything that might work, tried all possible models. Will they deliver using cycles, deliver walking, get kirana stores to deliver? We discussed all the pros and cons. At the end of 3-4 days, we said: ‘stay put’.”
That’s Hari Menon, co-founder and CEO of BigBasket, reminiscing 2015 when Tiger Global, Sequoia and SoftBank cut generous cheques, totalling $165 million in one year, for the rival e-grocer Grofers. In the four years since, hyperlocal grocery has become the must-capture space for all retailers—Amazon, Flipkart-Walmart, Reliance, Dunzo, Swiggy.
The battle of the margins rages unabated.
Menon and team have kept their gaze on one number: 23%. Anything less in gross margins, you can’t make this business work. Order by order, Menon is getting there. Benchmarking fill rates, delivery, and variable costs to global standards.
“In the next 4-6 months, we will cover fixed costs and become contribution-margin-positive. It’s a big thing for us. We had told ourselves it would take 6-8 years, and I think we hit that number,” says Menon.
Execution is often where strategies go to die. So say management books. A founding team of 50-somethings, who started selling stuff online in 1999, fumbled, exited and regrouped to start BigBasket in 2011, their story of slaying one execution dragon after another is a riveting tale in Indian e-commerce where billions of dollars went down the bottomless pit.
In The Interview today, Rohin deftly gets Menon to spill the beans on BigBasket’s on-time milestones. As India’s largest e-grocer, with 50% market share and operations across 26 cities, BigBasket expects to touch Rs 5,300 crore ($747 million) by the end of March. Soon the company will be making money at a regional level, at an order level and at a transaction level. What’s next?

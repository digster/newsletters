5G is slowing in India. Airtel is speeding up spending. The multi-billion-dollar deals with Nokia and Ericsson are about one thing: keeping premium customers happy

Try this: run a 5G speed test on your phone today and another one in six months. Chances are, the results will disappoint.

Yesterday, for instance, my November 2023 screenshot already looked a bit outdated—and not in a good way.

Left: November 2023; Right: November 2024

View in Browser

It’s not just a grumpy me problem. It’s an India problem. The country’s 5G was pitched as a bullet train but is moving more like a groaning, overcrowded local.

Average speeds took a 20% slide in Q2 2024 compared to Q1 2023, according to analytics firm Opensignal. Meanwhile, India’s global ranking on Ookla’s Speedtest Index has nosedived to 26th from 12th in the five months to September.

A free fall like that can’t go unnoticed, least of all by Bharti Airtel.

For India’s telcos—especially the second-largest one—this isn’t just a bad look; it’s a potential existential crisis. A company that spent years marketing itself as the premium choice in Indian telecom can’t afford to be associated with slowing internet speeds. So, after years of half-measures to patch together cellular generations, Airtel isn’t worried—it’s just spending. A lot.

Last week, it signed a new “multi-billion-dollar” deal with Nokia—with which it has a decades-long partnership—following a similar one with  Ericsson earlier in October. The goal is to push its 4G and 5G infra into the present day—and, ideally, the future.

Here’s the thing: Airtel has spent a lot of time convincing India that it’s worth paying more for. Faster speeds, superior reliability, and better service were the carrots it dangled to justify higher tariffs in a market where Jio has long conditioned consumers to expect data for practically free. And perhaps surprisingly, this conscious choice is paying off in many ways.

Just look at the September quarter. Airtel’s postpaid user base—the “premium” folks who also expect high-speed 5G, glitch-free streaming—grew 15% year-over-year. The average revenue per user (Arpu) climbed to Rs 233 (US$2.80), far higher than Jio’s Rs 195. The July tariff hike helped, too, pushing Airtel’s Q2 Arpu up 11%, while Jio managed a modest 7%.

And the strategy isn’t just padding Airtel’s bottom line; it’s also narrowing the gap in revenue market share. Airtel now commands 40.5% to Jio’s 43.5%, Motilal Oswal said, analysing telcos’ earnings data. A year ago, this was nearly 39% and 44% respectively. In fact, since 2019, Airtel has steadily chipped away at Jio’s lead, which, while still dominant, is showing signs of levelling off.

So, for the first time in the post-Jio years, Airtel isn’t just surviving—it’s thriving. Analysts at JM Financial predict its operating cash flow will hit Rs 67,411 crore in FY25, up more than 20% from FY24. That’s great news, but here’s the thing: the cash-flow growth story is riding on a precarious foundation.

That’s because the days of artificially low tariffs are ending. With Jio’s free lunch no longer on the table, customers are starting to feel the heat.

“…If you take the two axis and plot the rate per GB and ARPU on two different axes and put all the countries of the world on that graph you will find India at the extreme left on both much lower than Sub Saharan Africa, much lower than Bangladesh, much lower than Indonesia and so on and so forth, so the opportunity to take up tariffs over a period of time is high.”

CEO Gopal Vittal at Airtel’s September earnings call

But higher tariffs are a tough sell in a market conditioned by rock-bottom prices. And if the network speeds don’t pick up, that’s a recipe for customer restlessness.

“In our view, telecom companies (telcos) will likely lose some subscribers after the tariff hike because the higher costs will push some consumers to reduce the number of SIMs they hold,” said S&P Global in a recent report.

The tariff hikes have also led to SIM consolidation and subscription cancellations. The overall number of mobile phone connections in India decreased by 10.1 million in September. It had shrunk by 5.77 million and 9.22 million in the preceding two months. Trai data also revealed that 13.32 million subscribers submitted requests for Mobile Number Portability in September, up from 14.6 million in August and 13.68 million in July.

And restless customers are an investor’s nightmare—Airtel knows it.

Which brings me back to those multi-billion-dollar deals. Sure, they sound impressive in press releases, but the reality is every big telco is doing this. Jio, Airtel, Vodafone Idea—they’re all upgrading infra. (In fact, all these telcos have struck great deals.)

The real story, though, isn’t the deals themselves—it’s why Airtel doesn’t have a choice. This is about keeping its premium users happy.

The premium throne is as secure as the network it sits on

Airtel didn’t exactly dive into 5G—it dipped a toe in and called it a cannonball. Rather than splurging equally on infrastructure and spectrum, it hedged its bets.

Now, it’s catching up.

And yet, Airtel still delivers the “best 5G experience,” according to Opensignal. How? Airtel’s subscriber base, while smaller than Jio’s, is more curated—premium users and fewer freeloaders. Jio, with its standalone (SA) 5G architecture that’s technically superior, suffers from its size: Jio has 75 million more subscribers than Airtel, many of them low-revenue, dragging down average speeds. It’s a classic tortoise-versus-hare dynamic, except the tortoise has higher Arpu.

View in Browser

Airtel, with 375 million users, hasn’t been chasing the subscriber-numbers game for a while. Its deliberate pivot in the search for profitability has led to higher Arpu and a comfortable financial cushion.

Even S&P Global is optimistic, noting that the risk of costly spectrum purchases in the near future has eased. Airtel, for now, can afford to exhale. And exhaling it is with the big deals.

In our view, the risk of expensive spectrum purchases by Bharti Airtel in the next 18–24 months has dissipated. Telcos in India are unlikely to pour significantly more resources into 5G until more returns can be reaped. This could happen in case of large, monetisable enterprise use cases for 5G, beyond the current use cases of fixed-wireless access and faster mobile connections.

But comfort is relative. Airtel’s premium positioning hinges on delivering premium experiences—and slowing 5G speeds could unravel that narrative fast.

View in Browser

But why is India’s 5G rollout, which had all the fanfare of a rocket launch, flying more like a paper aeroplane? Answer: congestion. The country’s population density—473 people per square km versus Brazil’s 26—puts an almost absurd strain on telecom infrastructure. Add in patchy fibre backhaul, especially in rural areas, and Airtel’s reliance on non-standalone (NSA) 5G architecture, and you have a perfect storm for bottlenecks.

Besides, Jio and Airtel continued to cut down on network capital expenditure for a while.

India ranks high globally for 5G availability, with users spending over 50% of their time on these networks. But availability without quality is just a slow-loading illusion.

While spectrum is a crucial factor influencing users’ 5G experience, there are other contributing factors, such as population density and backhaul infrastructure. India is the second most populous country in the world, with a population density, as per the latest World Bank data of 473 inhabitants per square km, compared to 26 in Brazil—18.5X higher. The limited availability of fibre backhaul in India, particularly in rural areas, leads to bottlenecks, slowing down 5G speeds. Mobile backhaul, though common in rural regions, lacks the capacity to support high-speed 5G effectively.

5G evolution in India and Brazil – a tale of two strategies, Opensignal

Jio, to its credit, is flaunting standalone 5G architecture, promising its users a better experience. Airtel, in contrast, is still making do with NSA 5G, a clever but temporary hack that leans on existing 4G infrastructure. That works, too, unless you’re one of the unlucky few with incompatible devices.

To Airtel’s credit, it’s planning to flip the switch to standalone 5G in the next 3–4 years, which should help bridge the gap and bring its networks up to speed, literally.

For now, Airtel’s brand rests on being the “premium” choice. But loyalty has limits, and slowing speeds test patience. If the lag continues, even Airtel’s most loyal customers might start exploring cheaper options—or worse, downgrade their plans.

Enter Nokia

Here’s where Airtel’s big move with Nokia and Ericsson starts to make sense—though, for now, specifics on the Ericsson deal are under wraps. But the headline here is simple: Airtel is finally investing where it counts.

Nokia’s role is key, as the Finnish giant will upgrade the telco’s network with next-gen 5G and refreshed 4G gear.

At the heart of this upgrade is Nokia’s 5G Airscale portfolio, which includes hardware like base stations and baseband units. These are the nuts and bolts of wireless communication—one is responsible for sending signals, the other for processing them—forming the backbone of Airtel’s 5G ambitions.

Together, they promise faster data speeds and lower latency. It’s the kind of tech talk that would make any telecom exec feel warm and fuzzy inside.

Nokia is also bringing its Massive MIMO radios to the table. These are antennas designed to use multiple data streams simultaneously, effectively squeezing more out of Airtel’s bandwidth, especially in the dense urban areas where telecom traffic is most congested. Central to this setup is the ReefShark system-on-chip (SoC). It integrates essential functions into one sleek, energy-efficient processor—making it not just faster, but smarter, saving both space and power.

But this upgrade isn’t just about the latest shiny tech. Nokia’s multiband radios will make Airtel’s existing 4G infrastructure 5G-ready. So, in essence, Airtel’s getting a turbo-charged network, while Nokia—along with Ericsson—gets hefty paycheques at a time when India’s telecom slowdown is hitting them hard.

Jio, for its part, surely isn’t waiting around for its rival to catch its breath. With Jio Platforms gearing up for a blockbuster IPO, Airtel—which entered the US$100-billion market cap club earlier this year—can’t afford to blink.

For now, it’s become clear that Airtel’s premium users are both its strength and its Achilles’ heel. The question is, can Airtel keep them happy long enough? Because in the knifefight in a phone booth that is Indian telecom, slowing down is not an option.

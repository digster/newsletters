Trade Tricks
Tue, 07 Jan 25
A weekly newsletter about the biggest changes in commerce—focusing on shifts that matter to you..
Good Morning Ishan,
On 6 January 2022, Dunzo sent as strong a signal as any that it wasn’t messing around with the still-untested business of delivering groceries in minutes.
It announced that it had raised US$240 million from a clutch of investors. Of those, just Reliance Retail Ventures Ltd—part of Mukesh Ambani-owned conglomerate Reliance Industries—pumped in US$200 million in exchange for a quarter of Dunzo’s shares.
Dunzo’s fundraise was 4X that of upstart rival Zepto’s more recent funding round, which happened two months ago. When Dunzo first made its announcement, Grofers, an e-grocer with a chequered history, had just joined the instant-delivery race by rebranding itself as Blinkit. Bigbasket, despite the financial might of the Tata Group behind it, was at least a year away from thinking it had to take quick commerce seriously. Swiggy Instamart, an early mover in the space, believed delivery in 30 minutes, and not 10 minutes, was the sweet spot.
In other words, at this point, the fight was Dunzo’s to lose.
And lose it did. Spectacularly so.
Even as Blinkit and Zepto powered ahead and had investors eating out of their hands in no time, Dunzo ran out of money, leaving irate [employees](https://www.deccanherald.com/business/companies/dunzo-lays-off-75-of-employees-restricts-workforce-to-50-report-3174608) and [vendors](https://inc42.com/buzz/nclt-pulls-up-cash-strapped-dunzo-for-failing-to-reach-settlement-with-creditors/) in its wake.
Last week, reports emerged that Kabeer Biswas, the last and most prominent of Dunzo’s four founders, has [quit](https://www.moneycontrol.com/news/business/startup/end-of-an-era-dunzo-co-founder-kabeer-biswas-to-quit-12902324.html) the company, and that Reliance Retail has [written off](https://inc42.com/buzz/reliance-retail-dunzo-write-off-kabeer-biswas/) its investment in Dunzo.
Why Dunzo failed has been [well](https://the-ken.com/story/dunzo-fans-made-it-a-verb-then-it-became-just-another-delivery-firm/) [documented](https://inc42.com/features/how-dunzo-lost-the-it-factor-quick-commerce-hyperlocal/), so I’m more interested in the other big question: why couldn’t Reliance Retail save Dunzo?
Not only is Reliance Retail India’s largest retailer, it has enough cash (almost Rs 11,000 crore as of March 2024) to put large venture-capital funds to shame. So, couldn’t it have thrown Dunzo a lifeline by forking out another half a billion dollars and giving the company a second chance in the hottest business in India’s internet economy?
Without a doubt, yes.
But it’s not hard to see why it didn’t.
It’s a truism that no business interests Reliance unless it can be a market leader, and the sooner the better. It didn’t take the company too long to put enough distance between itself and its older rivals in grocery, electronics, and fashion.
Reliance thought it could replicate this in e-commerce. And its rivals feared so, too, when it began selling groceries in 200 cities and towns through Jiomart in 2020. Reliance’s Amazon-like ambitions became clear when it began buying up startups in different categories of the online ecosystem. But the ones it acquired were unremarkable at best: furniture e-tailer Urban Ladder, e-pharmacy Netmeds, and online-listings platform Just Dial.
I [argued](https://the-ken.com/tradetricks/why-reliance-retail-loves-to-buy-losers/) why it chose this strategy in one of the first editions of this newsletter:
The reason Reliance doesn’t want to pay a premium for someone else’s market share is because it doesn’t have to. All it’s doing by gobbling up unremarkable companies is saving some precious time in figuring out the dynamics of a new business. Even with Reliance’s track record, it’s not easy to get a handle on everything from e-pharmacy to fast food in a jiffy.
Hence the acquisition route.
Once the deal is done, Reliance can supercharge the business, as it’s hoping to do by integrating Netmeds with JioMart.
I was wrong about the “supercharging” bit. No one is going to say Reliance has made Urban Ladder or Netmeds the company to watch out for in their respective businesses. But the problem wasn’t really in what Reliance added to Jiomart, it was in what was the very centrepiece of the venture.
Reliance’s counterintuitive plan of using the same neighbourhood shopkeepers who fretted about the potential impact of e-commerce to help it deliver groceries didn’t play out as expected. Reliance couldn’t be sure how many units of a particular product a store had at any point, nor could it count on the workers at the store to deliver it within the promised time.
Plus, these stores already had their hands full catering to their existing customers. Jiomart brought little to the table they could be excited about, so the big disruption Jiomart was supposed to unleash in e-commerce never happened.
Reliance was at least smart enough to sense early on that there was potential in instant delivery. But quick commerce was then a tiny sliver of the e-grocery market. And Reliance is not interested in slivers. It eyes big, juicy pies.
So Reliance wrote Dunzo a cheque and ran an unsuccessful pilot in Mumbai to ship groceries within 90 minutes. This was a time when most Jiomart deliveries would take a few hours or even a day. But even as Jiomart and Dunzo struggled to figure out how to instantly deliver household essentials, Blinkit, Zepto, and Instamart added cosmetics, accessories, and gifts for different festivals.
Such was their growth that Dunzo began to recede further into the background. In FY24, Blinkit sold products worth Rs 12,500 crore. In the first half of FY25 alone, Blinkit’s sales were over Rs 11,000 crore. Meanwhile, Dunzo struggled to keep the lights on and its headcount shrank to a mere [50](https://www.livemint.com/companies/people/dunzo-layoffs-e-commerce-delivery-fundraising-troubles-11725090942756.html).
Catching up to Blinkit and Zepto would take a lot more than a few hundreds of millions of dollars. Which is why Flipkart took its time with its own quick-commerce bet, and when it did launch Flipkart Minutes, it decided to differentiate itself by focusing on [electronics](https://the-ken.com/story/not-blinkit-not-instamart-not-zepto-why-the-next-big-quick-commerce-fight-is-different/), a segment it already dominates in e-commerce.
Dunzo had no such luxury. Nor did Reliance have any inherent edge in Jiomart that it could build on. Hence its decision not to throw good money after bad.
For now, Reliance can only gaze longingly at quick commerce’s red-hot run and echo Marlon Brando’s lament from On the Waterfront:
“I coulda been a contender.”

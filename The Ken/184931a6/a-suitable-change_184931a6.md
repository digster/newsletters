Last Saturday, I finished reading Vikram Seth’s A Suitable Boy. Published in 1993, the 1,535-pager is a story about a college student named Lata, who has to make some life-altering choices. But this mammoth of a piece quickly escalates into an account of a newly independent, post-partition India, descriptively narrating everything cultural, religious, social, and economic from that time.
Now, if you have resided in the country, especially in a small town, you will realise how true the book is to reality. Reading it, I would quickly, and frequently, be transported to my childhood days when I used to visit my grandmother during the long-awaited summer break.
What struck me the most about this fictional drama was how, ironically, India still feels the same—little has changed over the 75 years since independence.
But not all changes can be averted; they are even more imminent to suit the disruptive times—something our stories at The Ken happened to discuss this week.
On Monday, Arundhati
[examined](https://the-ken.com/story/galaxys-45-m-fund-is-caught-between-altruism-and-returns/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter) an angel-investing format taking shape in India. Renowned angel investors Naval Ravikant, Jeff Fagnan, and a few others have pooled together $45 million to create a fund called Galaxy. By way of this fund, a chosen few founders will be given a sum of $1 million each, which they, in turn, have to invest in any seed- or early-stage startup of their liking.
On Tuesday, Gaurav
[analysed](https://the-ken.com/a-year-after-ipos-internet-companies-feel-the-pain-of-creating-value-for-public-investors/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter) the performance of internet-economy companies— Zomato, Paytm, PB Fintech, and Nykaa—a year after their market debut. In this report card, he detailed how the tides have turned for these firms, with investors looking to exit as the lock-in period nears its end.
And sometimes, change is not a choice but a necessity. On those lines, on Wednesday, Rounak
[reported](https://the-ken.com/story/a-120-dispute-a-womans-death-and-an-rbi-ban-haunt-mahindra-finance/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter) how a death in Jharkhand and a consequent ban by the Reserve Bank of India is pushing Mahindra Finance—one of India's largest non-banks—to overhaul the way it used to reinforce collections and achieve repossession targets.
On Thursday, Maitri
[analysed](https://the-ken.com/story/why-big-pharmas-100k-strong-sales-force-is-turning-against-it/?r_edition=2&utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter) the reasons that have jolted the once well-established world of medical representatives. Reeling under the threat of job loss, these sales employees have moved the Supreme Court asking for a change—the implementation of the Uniform Code of Pharmaceutical Marketing Practices (UCPMP).
Vanita closed the week on Friday with her
[story](https://the-ken.com/story/indian-startups-grapple-with-adapting-and-abandoning-goal-setting-tool-okr/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter) on the objectives and key results (OKR) goal-setting system. The tool that shot up to success in Silicon Valley is finding many takers in the Indian SaaS industry, albeit as a performance measure. Startups need to find the right way to use it, or it’s bound to fail.

The reasoning here is straightforward once you take a moment to consider Tapiawala’s words. There’s a real need: in 2021, the International Monetary Fund [said](https://www.imf.org/en/Blogs/Articles/2021/12/08/metals-demand-from-energy-transition-may-top-current-global-supply) that “current copper, lithium, and platinum supplies also are inadequate to satisfy future needs, with a 30% to 40% gap versus demand.” And more recently, in July 2023, the Energy Transitions Commission, an international think tank focused on economic growth and climate-change mitigation, pointed out that there could be major [supply gaps](https://www.reuters.com/business/energy/energy-transition-faces-metals-gap-unless-investment-rises-report-finds-2023-07-19/) for important resources such as lithium, nickel, graphite, and other minerals in the next decade, unless there are more investments to drive higher outputs from mines.
This is all to say that resource extraction needs to become far more efficient before renewable energy projects can go online, electric vehicle transition plans can succeed, and net-zero emission goals can be met. That means intelligence on the exact locations of those important resources is more crucial than ever.
The ‘weakest’ force doing a big job
The tech that Atomionics uses could be the game-changer that makes resource detection and subsequent extraction more precise—and cheaper. Tapiawala and his team are building quantum sensors to make that possible:
|
Atomionics’ quantum sensor, Gravio, does this by exploiting a key principle in quantum physics: atoms can behave like both waves and particles.
The process begins with shooting lasers at atoms, which then emit photons. When this kinetic energy is released, the atoms slow down and become nearly stationary, making them cold. In this situation, the atoms begin to behave more like waves than particles, and disturbances in these waves can be measured. Specifically, gravity, which is a reflection of mass densities, can be measured precisely.
|
|
This is the same science that is used to detect black holes and gravitational waves, but applied to a commercial context linking up with green energy goals. It’s the result of practising the startup ethos in cold atom physics research and development. The team at Atomionics managed to fit a lab into a unit the size of two mini-fridges after iterating at a faster pace than is possible in, say, a research institute. This involves not only physicists and research scientists, but also folks with expertise in electronics engineering, mechanical engineering, software engineering, AI, and geophysics, all translating a piece of technology into an application or product.
When The Ken spoke to Tapiawala in early August, Gravio was deployed in the Australian Outback, with Arizona being next on the list. Both operations were for resource-exploration companies.
Laying down a map
To help explain the impact that Atomionics could have on the mining sector, Tapiawala mentioned a trip he took to Mongolia in July. He said the country is underexplored in terms of the resources that could be extracted, particularly those that are critical to the energy sector. Anglo-Australian mining giant Rio Tinto recently bought a mine there—but only after performing 119 drills that found no useful minerals. The 120th finally hit the motherlode.
|
If Atomionics’ methodology is implemented at a wide scale, it will be vastly different from the way open fields are explored right now.
Currently, the process of collecting data about useful resources is manual: a geologist is dispatched to the location of interest, and they may log a data point every 100 metres, repeating over a span of thousands of kilometres. “One pixel on that map is the size of a football field,” as Tapiawala put it.
Under the present paradigm, drilling to figure out the exact location of resource deposits involves trial and error. But Atomionics’ system, Gravio, can be loaded onto an SUV and driven around to create a map with a spatial resolution that is magnitudes higher. A pixel on this map would be shrunk down to the size of “a couple pizza boxes.”
|
|
Gravio’s commercialisation depends on detecting resources such as copper, cobalt, lithium, nickel, and zinc—now critical minerals in nearly every jurisdiction. These resources are part of the vehicle battery sector, a US$298 billion market. The sensor’s immediate utility happens to relate to the big questions of how nations will hit their goals to reduce carbon emissions and give renewable energy more prominence.
But Atomionics’ quantum sensor system isn’t just about pulling resources up from the ground. It’s a way to understand our physical world better, to provide sight beyond the surface.

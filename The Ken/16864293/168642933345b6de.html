I tell you all this because a deskie does none of these things. And yet, here I am, writing you emails, sometimes, week after week. So, quite certainly, there must be something not too obvious but important that enables me to? I'd like to think so.
 
A lot of our stories, indeed, are about finding that which is not immediately apparent, about looking beyond what is presented, what shines. Without further ado, I say let's dive into the stories we carried over the past week. Let's see if you can spot where we looked beyond the obvious.
 
On Monday, we had Saif Iqbal write his second contribution piece for us - this one on e-commerce laundering in India. A new set of govt. regulations seemingly disallows much of e-commerce from Flipkart and Amazon, but, it turns out, it’s “business as usual”. How? Read his piece.
 
Vandana, on Tuesday, looked into what keeps Airbnb from acing the hospitality game in India. Six years and thousands of homestays later, Airbnb continues to be a distant challenger in the country, she writes. How are they going to fix it? Are they?
 
If you have some favourite shows on Netflix, Hotstar in the like that have been facing the threat of censorship, Harveen and Vandana have news for you. Nine video streaming companies and a self-regulatory code are trying to address online content concerns in India. Maybe OTT will be censorship free? The story is free; share it.
 
On Thursday, Arundhati wrote about Google playing bully. The Google Play Store is turning off the tap on apps asking for SMS and call log permissions. While that plunges apps into a data drought, Google’s own products may gain a competitive edge.
 
Friday had Salman and Sidhartha team up to tell you about Drivezy's ICO...or non-ICO. "Could Blockchain be the solution to ride-sharing in developing countries? Drivezy thought it might," they write.
 
Also, if you missed Gayathri's free story on Sunday about IUDs in India, you might want to go back and give it a read. She wrote about Mirena, a contraceptive that has existed for three decades that could curb the rate of hysterectomies in India (and the newer and cheaper Emily), which is over four times the rate in developed countries. But there’s a problem—the lack of access.
 
That's all for this week. Hope you enjoy the stories. And do tell me if you find something that's not too obvious.

Good morning Ishan, welcome back to Sundays, and lying back and reading. Our day of the week! And the hero of my week has been the sun.

This last week in Bengaluru has been what I call the liminal sun week. You see the sun has gotten warmer, but is still in that phase where you can enjoy its rays on your face in the morning and its warmth on your skin through the day. Just about.

I suspect that by the end of next week, it will have moved past comforting to just tolerable.

And then the week after? Hot.

Yeah. Bengaluru speed runs from winter to summer as far as weather goes. The roadside trees and the skies might still signal spring, but not the sun.

Indiranagar, where The Ken has its offices and where I spend most of the week, is wonderful right now. Many trees are shedding their leaves while others are growing new ones. The sunlight dapples through all of them. It’s perfect for walking about and meeting friends and colleagues at the numerous cafes in the area.

My favourite spring tree, the Pongamia, is present on most roads. Its ridiculously bright and iridescent new leaves virtually radiate spring happiness. Apple Photos’ AI identifies it as all sorts of trees—Japanese Maple, Japanese Pagoda, Kentucky Yellowwood, Chinese Chastetree—except what it really is.

1. “Tours of duty”

Each week, my newsletters often “take me” to newer places and discoveries. This week, I ended up writing a job description for a product designer at The Ken once I was done with the mise en place https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mise_en_place for the actual edition. I’ll explain as we go along.

A recurring theme in this newsletter and in our conversations has been the gradual-then-sudden disruption of professional careers.

This isn’t a one-sided problem. For every professional, young or old, who wonders what the appropriate way to think about their own careers is, there is also an employer, startup or established, which is wondering about how best to think about the careers they offer to employees.

Therefore, solutions must take both sides into account—employee and employer. One approach that seems to have stood both the test of time (it was proposed over a decade ago when AI wasn’t the disruptive and threatening force it is presented as today) and first principles thinking (it still makes common sense) is “Tour of Duty”.

Based on the army concept wherein soldiers are sent on time-limited missions, Reid Hoffman, author, venture investor, and the co-founder of Linkedin, extended it to white-collar careers too.

“In an alliance at work, the tour of duty represents an ethical commitment by employer and employee to a specific mission that’s expected to last a finite amount of time. An ideal mission is mutually beneficial: the company gains new products, customers, profits, etc. and the employee gains new skills, experiences, connections, and so on.

The finite term of the tour of duty – initial tours at adaptive companies tend to be 2-5 years long — provides crisp focus and a mutually agreeable time frame for discussing the future of the relationship. It gives a valued employee concrete and compelling reasons to “stick it out” and finish a tour. Most importantly, a realistic tour of duty lets both sides be honest about their goals and time horizons, which is a necessity for trust.”

Tours of Duty: How to Organize Modern Employment https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/20141023153633-1213-tours-of-duty-how-to-organize-modern-employment/

If you’re the visual type, here’s a cheeky online presentation https://www.slideshare.net/slideshow/the-alliance-a-visual-summary/36981114 of the same concept, also from Hoffman. And because Hoffman is, well, American, there is of course a book too https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/20763746-the-alliance. And consulting offers https://www.theallianceframework.com/implementing-at-your-company/ to adapt the approach in your organisation.

I like the concept because it explicitly confronts and codifies what has been reality for a while now: that the length of the employee-employer relationship has been falling and will most likely continue to do so.

Lifelong employment gave way to decadal employment which gave way to years-long employment. But what if we embraced it? What if we could look at, say, a decade as a series of two- to three-year stints, with the same company even?

“Mutual investment was implicit in the old lifetime employment compact, to be sure. Because both sides expected the relationship to be permanent, both sides were willing to invest in it. Companies provided training, advancement, and an unspoken guarantee of employment, while employees provided loyalty and a moderation of wage demands. The new compact acknowledges the probable impermanence of the relationship yet seeks to build trust and investment anyway. Instead of entering into strict bonds of loyalty, both sides seek the mutual benefits of alliance.

As allies, employer and employee try to add value to each other. The employer says, “If you make us more valuable, we’ll make you more valuable.” The employee says, “If you help me grow and flourish, I’ll help the company grow and flourish.” Employees invest in the company’s adaptability; the company invests in employees’ employability. As former Bain CEO Tom Tierney used to tell recruits and consultants, “We are going to make you more marketable.””

Tours of Duty: The New Employer-Employee Compact by Reid Hoffman, Ben Casnocha and Chris Yeh, Harvard Business Review https://hbr.org/2013/06/tours-of-duty-the-new-employer-employee-compact

A special variant of tour of duty-style jobs are “terminal jobs”—jobs that have sharply defined time periods, at the end of which employees move up or out. For example, a two-year entry level leadership programme or a two-year product manager programme.

Counterintuitively, those who pass through such programmes are happier in the process. I am quoting from the same HBR article.

The Wharton School polls its students about their satisfaction with their pre–business school jobs. It has found that students who came to it from “terminal jobs”—two-year analyst programs, for example—are more positive about their work experience than their peers are. Terminal jobs are generic versions of tours of duty; personalized tours would probably produce even more positive feelings.

The Ken launched its first “terminal programme”—the Journalism Leadership Programme, or JLP—in 2023 https://the-ken.com/blog/the-kens-journalism-leadership-programme/.

You will join The Ken as a full-time employee in Bengaluru as part of this programme. You will be onboarded as a Staff Writer, and over the course of two years, you will work across The Ken’s newsroom to gain the requisite exposure and knowledge to emerge as a credible voice in your respective beats. This will set you on the path to becoming editorial leaders equipped to navigate the complexities of a rapidly evolving media landscape.

We launched the second edition in 2024. And the third version goes live later this year.

We found and hired multiple talented journalists through this programme, so the Wharton poll tracks for us too.

Alas, when I tried to replicate the same on our Business side https://the-ken.com/blog/the-kens-young-business-leadership-programme-yblp/, we failed to successfully hire even a single person after dozens of interviews. I chalked it down to our inability to paint a picture of a great “terminal programme”.

This brings me to today. As I started writing this edition about “tours of duty” jobs, I wondered why I wasn’t structuring the careers and roles of my colleagues at The Ken to offer them better defined career value addition.

“You can’t do this over a weekend, Rohin!” said the voice inside my head. I guess it’s right.

But why can’t I write job descriptions for new roles we will hire for in the shape of a “tour of duty”?

I could. And I did.

Product Designer @ The Ken (a “tour of duty”-style job description)

This is a critical role that bridges all three of The Ken’s foundational pillars—journalism, products, and subscriptions. The product designer will operate across the entire breadth of The Ken’s offerings in order to create new subscriber products/experiences as well as strengthen existing ones.

Detailed job description https://the-ken.com/jobs/product-designer/

2. Everyone’s turning pages

Hello dear Ishan, it’s Hari here. I’m writing this week’s books section from back home in Kerala, where I’ve been for about a week. It is as I remembered for the most part—summer is here, early as usual; lots of dust, thanks to the always ongoing roadworks; and because it’s wedding and pooram https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pooram season, lots of celebration all around. 

But I did notice a small change in my home, one I was very glad to see. There were books lying all around. Someone in the house was reading (or maybe all of them). What book? Honestly, it doesn’t matter to me. They’re reading, and that’s all I care about. 

Ours has never been a household that spent too much time reading. I know I only picked up a book because there wasn’t a phone I could mess around with for most of my childhood. But for everyone else, life got in the way and there was never much time to flip pages. 

I’m glad that appears to have changed. 

As for the progress I’m making with our community reading list, I’m still enjoying my copy of The Paris Review Interviews. And I’ve spotted four more books from our long community list that I’ve decided I will tackle next.

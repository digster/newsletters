First off, thank you for reading and writing back on the story ‘40 and outdated https://the-ken.com/story/40-and-outdated/’.?utm_campaign=The%20Ken&utm_source=Email&utm_medium=Newsletter As many of you pointed out, the irony is certainly not lost that from an individual point of view, 60 is the new 40!

But it turns out, we touched only the tip of the iceberg. One reader, identifying with the story, called in to say two weeks ago that SAP India dismantled its 500-strong quality assurance team of its HR product SuccessFactors. While the younger engineers were told to reskill, the older managers were asked to find jobs through the internal jobs portal.

Well, that, in nutshell, is what is playing out at most companies. In products and services alike, the industry has not rewarded people for being hands-on with tech. Managing people and working on tech became mutually exclusive. And when they saw the tsunami of change, even though the problem was colossal, the approach remained casual. Most believed the magic-bullet of reskilling would save them.

But what would you call reskilling when it doesn’t meet the employer’s or the employee’s need? That rolling in the muck is not the best way to get clean.

Deeper, structural changes are needed, at personal and organisational levels. It’s not 20s vs 40s vs 60s even though Mark Zuckerberg says “young people are just smarter”. Not disputing that, but the evidence is repeatedly emerging, in entrepreneurship and sci-tech research, that major contributions tend to come from those in their 30s and 40s, as opposed to those in 20s.

In Part 2 of her story today, Arundhati talks about what is making this industry shift painful and stark. There are no easy answers but let’s get talking about it.

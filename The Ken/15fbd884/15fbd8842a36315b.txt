You know I like good stories. So let me start there. In journalism and good storytelling, there’s a hugely desired but almost-never-to-be-found paragraph called the nut graph. It is essentially a paragraph that explains the news value of the story. In a way, it answers the questions, why is the story important, and why you, the reader, should spend your valuable time reading it. I’ve seen Editors use nut graph as nutgraf or nut graf. I’ve liked those Editors. 

Here’s the nut graph from today’s story by my colleague Seema. 

“A Meerut-company that pushed contraception out of closets and on to loud television ads, Mankind Pharma is now entering new therapies and big cities, where selling drugs requires the seduction of not just the patients but also the doctors. Specifically, the country’s top 5-10% doctors. The stage is set for Mankind 2.0. Crucially, at a time when Indian pharma is in a period of interregnum—between certainties of rapid growth and market conditions and uncertainties of the old ways of doing things no longer working very well. The average revenue growth of top five pharma companies was around 30% between FY13-FY15. It’s down to 10.5% for FY16 and FY17.” 

The nut graph is apt. And right after, Seema builds the scene: 

“At 4.30 in the evening, the reception is teeming with people. The industrial Okhla Phase III in Delhi is no place for glamorous corporate offices. But then, Mankind prefers to show off glamour on screens. The heavy rush of people reminds you that this is a company with the most aggressive field force in the industry—12,000 and counting…"

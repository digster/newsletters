The Big Story

“Where is Kunal Shah?” ask most Cred employees

The founder-CEO’s insistence on having a workforce that is largely self-reliant is now impeding the fintech’s growth

Cred CEO Kunal Shah’s philosophy of hiring former founders, serial entrepreneurs, and self-motivated individuals has worked till now. The method allowed Shah to focus on generating ideas for the company without having to worry about its execution. Cred’s talent-dense pool, on other hand, has been used to a fast-paced environment that read disrupt.

But things have changed. Cred’s older employees are leaving. And for the first time, the company needs Kunal to step in for some motivation [Read the full story here]

Would you send your child to a ‘microschool’ with just 50 students?

Wealthy parents ditched Indian exam boards for international ones, but now they want more: un-classrooms

As learning came home, pandemic enabled parents to peek inside classrooms. Several were disenchanted with what they saw. The last decade has seen several trends emerging in school education – from international curriculums gaining favour over nationalised ones to the home-schooling movements.

The latest in the trend are micro-schools. The un-classrooms offer customised hands-on learning, challenging every concept – from curriculums to assessments. At the heart of this movement is a school founder, Lina Ashar, known for setting up India’s biggest school brands at scale. Yet her next big bet is on the tiny classrooms, which she believes is where the future of learning lies [Read the full story here]

“I own three cars and two bikes”: the wealth effect of India’s stock-market boom

A survey by The Ken reveals that many individuals, driven by newfound wealth and a surge of confidence, are in no mood to turn their back on equities

Despite major shocks, such as the post-election meltdown, India’s bull market shows remarkable resilience, with “buying the dip” now a popular strategy.The upward trajectory has created a new, affluent class of investors who are significantly boosting their spending on luxury items and experiences.

About 20% of investors have experienced the “wealth effect”, spending more due to increased investment values, which is contributing to a premiumisation wave. Besides, there is a notable shift from traditional FDs to equities and mutual funds, reflecting a broader trend towards aggressive investment strategies [Read the full story here]

Peers of Foxconn and Samsung, check the weather before moving to Vietnam 

Vietnam wants to nourish high-tech manufacturers looking outside China, but its power grid can handle only so much load

Vietnam has been seeing a series of power outages in the past year and a half, owing to the weather and its own grid’s incapacity. Manufacturers like Samsung, Foxconn, and Amkor who’ve set up facilities in the country have incurred heavy losses owing to power cuts.

While the country has abundant renewable energy sources, it’s not in a position to divert that power to the factories despite approving a purchase plan. For Vietnam to continue attracting foreign investments, it’s necessary for it to upgrade existing power grids and also utilise renewable energy [Read the full story here]

Havmor family’s new ice-cream brand to consumers: stop counting calories 

With premium and low-cal options booming, Havmor’s founding family, after selling the legacy brand for a cool US$120 million, is now taking on today’s ‘healthy’ ice-cream makers—with a new spin on their classic edge

The Indian ice-cream market, valued at US$3.4 billion, is projected to be worth over US$5 billion in FY25. With the market booming, VCs are swooping in, eyeing companies catering to calorie-conscious eaters.

Amid this crowd, enters Ankit Chona, stepping out of his Havmor legacy for the second act. Bucking the trend, Chona is ditching the health-conscious route to treat ice-cream as a guilty pleasure [Read the full story here]

Why Nisha Uberoi’s move from Trilegal to JSA could shake up the business of competition law

Entered with a team of five, departed with 27. This high-profile partner exit shows how cutthroat the competition is among law firms

Nisha Kaur Uberoi, a renowned competition lawyer with a strong clientele, is considered a dominant force within antitrust circles. In her seven-year stint at Trilegal, one of India’s top legal firms, she built its competition practice from the scratch and was one of the firm’s top earners.

Last week, she quit and joined a competitor with her 27-member team, clients, and support staff. While it’s common in the industry for parents to move with their teams, Uberoi’s exit may pinch Trilegal [Read the full story here]

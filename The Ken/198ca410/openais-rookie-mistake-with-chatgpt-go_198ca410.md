A weekly newsletter about how finance is getting supercharged by tech in India, and how you can make money work for you.
Good Morning Ishan,
OpenAI may have top-tier talent packed into every square feet of its office, but all that super intelligence has been no match for India’s payment realities.
As even the smallest of Indian businesses will tell you, you can’t set up reliable payments by banking on just one payment processor or bank. Because if that falters, your sales can come to a standstill.
But it appears that OpenAI didn’t get that memo.
On Tuesday, the AI major made a big splash announcing ChatGPT Go for India, a subscription offering its most popular features—such as extended access to GPT-5 and better image-generation options—at a special price of just Rs 399 per month. Then it went an extra step and declared users could [even pay for it](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/kevinweil_we-just-launched-chatgpt-go-in-india-activity-7363412122870956033-mHOu/?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAAAAYqnoBkNTKwzrl29sr5b1QlRYuaHPBArI) via the Unified Payments Interface (UPI).
Clearly, it’s got a good handle on India’s pulse.
But for processing these monthly subscriptions through UPI, OpenAI partnered with the world’s mightiest fintech—Stripe. And Stripe in turn partnered with Yes Bank.
One payment aggregator, one bank. What’s the worst that could happen, right?
Well, for one thing, the entire channel could down, which is what happened with ChatGPT Go’s UPI payments within 24 hours of its launch. By the evening of the first day, only card payments powered by Stripe were available to subscribers—the company put out a note at 8:47 pm IST saying it has temporarily disabled UPI payments and that it expects the issue to be resolved within 12–24 hours.
In a country that relies on UPI for over 80% of its digital retail payments, this must have really hurt OpenAI’s grand launch plans.
A payments industry executive I spoke to says the issue was with Yes Bank’s UPI switch, and that the scale of transactions setting up autopay mandates must have made things more challenging.
The issue seems to be bigger, though, because problems persisted even on the next day, with UPI available only to a few users. This time, OpenAI updated its note to say that it was facing trouble with UPI integration.
Frankly, OpenAI should have expected hiccups when it launched with a payment-aggregator partner for whom India is not a priority. Stripe has been operating on an invite-only basis in the country since 2024, meaning businesses still need to apply to the fintech to accept payments through its platform. And this is after trying its hand in India for seven years.
You can listen to this Two by Two episode if you want to dive deeper into why Stripe hasn’t been able to crack the Indian market: [Why Stripe could not become the Stripe of India](https://the-ken.com/podcasts/two-by-two/why-couldnt-stripe-become-the-stripe-of-india/).
In fact, OpenAI’s team was reportedly talking to other Indian payment aggregators before ChatGPT Go’s launch, but it still decided to go solo with Stripe, because globally, its system is built for easy integration with the US-based fintech’s platform. Such a choice would have worked in most markets, but India is exceptional—in the sense that you need multiple payment providers to have a reliable online payments channel going.
The payments executive I quoted earlier, though, says OpenAI’s payment infrastructure does not allow it to integrate different payment providers right now. The AI firm will need to build a framework to enable multiple payment providers in different geographies, for which it will have to change its payments architecture. That’s something that will need to be executed centrally, the executive adds.
The best people in OpenAI are probably not obsessing over payments, but focussing on the next upgrade to their LLM. But this episode is a reminder that even the most cutting-edge company has to contend with gritty, local, payment realities if they want to make a sale in India.
To be fair to OpenAI, many international companies like Airbnb have tripped up on this. It usually takes multiple tries and failures to appreciate that they need to enlist local payment partners who are more attuned to the idiosyncrasies of Indian banking and payment services.

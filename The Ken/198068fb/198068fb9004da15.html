Hospital bills in India are starting to really sting. Not just patients, but India’s health insurers too.

A huge chunk of Indians still live without health insurance, just one medical emergency away from bankruptcy, but even for those insured, things are getting shaky. 

Over the past several months, insurers have repeatedly claimed that hospital pricing is getting opaque, and that patients are being overcharged if they are covered by health insurance. In an interview with Moneycontrol earlier this month, Ramaswamy Narayanan, CMD of the General Insurance Corporation of India (GIC), didn’t mince words and blamed the practice for rising health insurance premiums:

“Today, when a patient walks into a hospital, the first question asked is whether they have an insurance policy. If the answer is yes, the hospital’s attitude, and pricing, often changes dramatically,” he explained.

Hospitals routinely charge insured patients significantly more than cash patients, sometimes inflating costs threefold, triggering higher premiums, and this practice, while normalised, amounts to a systematic form of fraud, he said.

[…]

“Eventually, policyholders are penalised through higher annual premiums. While the IRDAI has set limits on annual premium hikes, the underlying problem remains unaddressed,” he said.

According to insurers, India’s healthcare inflation is currently around 12–14% year-on-year. If you are a patient, depending on where you are getting treated and what type of hospital (or even doctor) is doing the treating, things can change wildly—from pricing right down to quality of care. Getting treated at a private hospital, for instance, can cost you 6X more than treatment at a public-sector one. And with claim settlements rising as coverage expands, insurance companies say they are taking a serious hit. 

The answer to all these problems, insurers now believe, is a dedicated regulator for the healthcare sector—one that will keep unfair practices by hospitals under check and bring pricing under control. 

In that same interview, Narayanan revealed that both the GIC and the Insurance Regulatory and Development Authority (IRDAI) are in talks with the union government to roll out just such a regulator. Industry observers, of course, chuckle at the irony—of an IRDAI now functioning without a chairman for four months and a GIC struggling with low insurance penetration rates lobbying for a healthcare regulator. 

But the government is starting to move, at least to curb overpricing. 

On Friday, news broke that the National Health Claims Exchange (NHCX), the portal that handles all health insurance claims, will be moved from from the health ministry to the finance ministry, and henceforth come under IRDAI regulation. Officials believe that this will streamline monitoring and limit dodgy practices by hospitals. 

That hardly scratches the surface, though. Getting a tighter grip means having a proper leash in the first place—a.k.a., a dedicated healthcare regulator. 

But just what should a truly effective watchdog really look like?

Track outcomes, not just pricing

Establishing a potent healthcare regulator in India’s current landscape is easier said than done. 

First, health is a state matter in India, which means some states—such as Kerala—have better healthcare systems and outcomes than others. Unifying different state-based monitoring and oversight systems under a national regulator, with uniform rules, may invite quite a bit of friction. 

Not to mention, a central regulator would also need to work constantly with different state governments to have any muscle on the ground. 

Second, there is already a fractured ecosystem of existing agencies like the Central Drugs Standard Control Organisation and the National Accreditation Board for Hospitals & Healthcare Providers which provide some regulation for healthcare, albeit in a highly fragmented way. All of these would also need to be brought under the proposed regulator’s umbrella.

The bigger issue, though, is that hospitals have been quite successful so far at getting around policies aimed at curbing bills. For instance, in February 2017, when India’s drug-pricing authority tried to cap the price of stents, many hospitals changed the pricing of their packages to accommodate the manufacturers’ rates—a workaround where the consumer ultimately lost.

Thus, for any form of healthcare regulation to be truly effective, it must also be closely tied to consumer feedback, and tracking and publishing the outcomes that hospitals deliver.

Results that other consumers can see and make informed choices around. 

After all, if patients started flocking to hospitals that showed the best outcomes, errant hospitals would have no choice but to start toeing the line with far more alacrity.

Making consumers a part of the regulatory apparatus is already a thing in other sectors, and is being put to good effect. Sebi, for instance, has set up a platform called SCORES that allows market investors to report issues and get them redressed. Thousands of investor complaints are now solved every month, with average resolution times falling significantly. Telecom regulator Trai, too, involves consumers by letting them rate the quality of data and call services, and then uses such feedback to act on operators with poor records. 

Also, introducing outcome-tracking for healthcare won’t just make it easier for consumers and the regulator. It will benefit hospitals too, who would be able to self-regulate problem areas more proactively. 

After all, hospitals also need to get their due from a regulator, not just patients. Currently, all hospitals in a particular city range are treated the same way by insurers, and get basically the same rates and “packages”. But ideally, hospitals should be differentiated based on their infrastructure and capabilities; big private hospitals, for instance, can afford to do the same surgeries for a lower price than small clinics. A hospital ranking system proposed by the NABH is already attempting to solve some of these issues. 

It’s been a quarter-century since India introduced 100% foreign direct investment in the hospital sector. But the murky regulatory environment has made many foreign investors hesitant. Choosing an outcome-centric approach for regulation instead of obsessing on prices alone could help break those shackles and make Indian healthcare far more attractive.

Of course, tracking feedback and outcomes at such scale will require a significant—and probably costly—upgrade to data infrastructure, and medical records systems. 

But it could be well worth the expense. 

After all, is there a more effective watchdog than the consumer?

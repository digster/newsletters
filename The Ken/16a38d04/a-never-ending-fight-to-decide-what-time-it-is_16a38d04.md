Let me tell you a story about time.
A friend and his biker buddies (gearheads the lot of them, leather jackets and all) once rode from Ahmedabad in western India to Bhutan—about 2,600 kilometres. They hit Phuntsholing, a border city that acts as the gateway to the mountain country, around 1:00 in the afternoon one day. To go any further, they needed a permit—but first, lunch.
Two and a half hours later, appetites thoroughly sated, they decided to roll out. One asks the cashier at the restaurant, the RTO office is running, right? Nope, it’s shut for the day.
What? They hurriedly checked their watches. It was only 3:30. The website said it would be open till 4:00.
“It’s 3:30 in India. In Bhutan, it’s already 4:00.”
Oh sh*t.
The setting of national time zones is a delicate and oft confusing business. The general rule is to have one zone every 15° of longitude—i.e., in one-hour blocks ahead of or behind UTC (Coordinated Universal Time).
India is one of the outliers—it has a half-hour zone, UTC+5:30. And it’s been a bone of contention for decades. The country's wide enough that it spans two regular time zones. Sometime after Independence, it was decided that one would do, roughly down the middle. Every Indian on the same clock.
But in reality, it ends up a burden for those at the periphery. Particularly those in Northeast India, a region mostly cut off from the rest of India by neighbouring Bangladesh (which is half an hour ahead of India). Indian Standard Time, or IST, they say, is skewed towards the west—the sun rises at about 6:30 in my biker friend's hometown, but around 4.30 in a northeastern state like Assam.
In today’s story, my colleague Roshni takes us behind the scenes to the scientists who have been fighting for ages to change IST. Eighty-three-year-old DP Sengupta, for one, has been arguing since the late 1970s that shifting India half an hour forward, to UTC+6:00, will help everybody.
About 10 years ago, he and his research partner Dilip Ahuja published a seminal study showing that it would save thousands of crores in electricity costs, and even bring balance between east and west. On the other side are bureaucrats, who largely can’t be bothered to go through the trouble of resetting the national clock. And then another set of scientists, who say no, moving the meridian east won’t help, what India really needs is two time zones.

Good morning, Ishan. What a week, huh?

I don’t think my senses have managed to step back from fight-or-flight mode one bit. It’s surreal to watch America speed-run through a combo of hara-kiri (on itself) and kamikaze (on everyone else) each day.

We’re headed back to an era of global instability, trade wars, and geopolitical conflict. What makes me most sad is the prospect of the poorer and weaker (countries, economies, people) paying the price because the powerful and rich see everything as a zero-sum game. I can’t imagine what the next few decades will look like when people read history books in the 2040s and 2050s.

Ironically, a different sort of cacophony and chaos has helped soothe my senses through the week—the cacophony of spring!

The peepal tree adjoining our backyard is laden with red figs. Starting even before dawn, much of the day is filled with the cries of ecstatic birds of all kinds, tweeting, chirping, cooing, and whistling as they eat the ripe fruit. I still haven’t seen the hornbill pair from last year, but I did see a Golden Oriole.

Once the birds fly off, the bees come in. The water apple tree in our backyard is in full bloom, laden with thousands of blossoms, each one comprised of dozens of thread-like stamens. I’m thankful to see a healthy population of both large and small bees.

1. “Winning versus being on the winning side”

One of the reasons why I write this newsletter, and I hope why many of you read it, is to better reflect on what makes us do things. The vast majority of the tens of thousands of decisions we make in the course of our lives are made on autopilot and by relying on our subconscious mind. That’s how it should be, because we don’t want to overwhelm our brain with decisions about minor matters.

We like to “save” our decision-making abilities for important choices. For instance, where should we invest our money, how should we plan our careers, how do we want to stay healthy, etc.

If only.

In most of those spheres, we again succumb to subconscious decision-making. Or worse, we get tricked into emotional responses driven by our reptilian brains. One particularly devious trick is when we are forced to subsume our own self-interests in exchange for being part of the winning side.

Our mammalian brains still attach a lot of value to being part of tribes, especially the “winning” tribe. This is why we’re susceptible to FOMO—the Fear of Missing Out.

Our survival as an individual within a tribe, and thus our survival as a species, once hinged on our being aware of threats both to ourselves and to the larger group. To be “in the know” when we roamed around in small groups was critical to survival. To not be aware of a new food source, for example, meant you literally missed out on something that could mean the difference between life and death. When humans began to create more stable farming communities, being in the know involved paying attention, being in the right places at the right times to get resources and information, and engaging in the gossip of the day as it filtered through the community.

Do you remember the classic crypto FOMO taunt that was also a really clever marketing message? “Have fun staying poor”.

“You’re most likely to encounter the meme as a response to someone that has just sold bitcoin, or has said he or she would never consider buying bitcoin. In this sense, “have fun staying poor” could be uttered as a genuine call to reconsider those decisions.

As Coin Center’s Neeraj Agrawal explained over email, it’s meant to convey “that failure to open one’s mind will make you miss opportunities.”

Key to the bitcoin mind is the idea that bitcoin can only go up, while the U.S. dollar (and all other fiat currencies) are destined to collapse. “Have fun staying poor” is shorthand for that idea.”

While crypto may or may not be back, the current FOMO most professionals have to deal with is around AI. It runs something like, “Don’t get left behind”.

The underlying message from all AI companies and investors is that we all must jump aboard the AI bandwagon, or risk getting left behind.

Now, you may love or hate crypto or AI, that’s fine. But any time you are being emotionally tricked into making major decisions based on the fear of “not being part of the winning side of the future”, you should be wary.

Is merely being part of the winning side what you want? How are you going to perform better with your investments or your career simply because you did what many others were doing? What is your differentiation or uniqueness within that? What will make you distinctive? Here’s Jeff Bezos’ last letter to Amazon shareholders, from 2020. Some of the lines have aged like milk.

Differentiation is Survival and the Universe Wants You to be Typical

In what ways does the world pull at you in an attempt to make you normal? How much work does it take to maintain your distinctiveness? To keep alive the thing or things that make you special?

I know a happily married couple who have a running joke in their relationship. Not infrequently, the husband looks at the wife with faux distress and says to her, “Can’t you just be normal?” They both smile and laugh, and of course the deep truth is that her distinctiveness is something he loves about her. But, at the same time, it’s also true that things would often be easier – take less energy – if we were a little more normal.

This phenomenon happens at all scale levels. Democracies are not normal. Tyranny is the historical norm. If we stopped doing all of the continuous hard work that is needed to maintain our distinctiveness in that regard, we would quickly come into equilibrium with tyranny.

We all know that distinctiveness – originality – is valuable. We are all taught to “be yourself.” What I’m really asking you to do is to embrace and be realistic about how much energy it takes to maintain that distinctiveness. The world wants you to be typical – in a thousand ways, it pulls at you. Don’t let it happen.

You have to pay a price for your distinctiveness, and it’s worth it. The fairy tale version of “be yourself” is that all the pain stops as soon as you allow your distinctiveness to shine. That version is misleading. Being yourself is worth it, but don’t expect it to be easy or free. You’ll have to put energy into it continuously.

The world will always try to make Amazon more typical – to bring us into equilibrium with our environment. It will take continuous effort, but we can and must be better than that.

Leaving aside the accidentally prescient bits about “democracies not being normal” and “tyranny being the historical norm”, I love the sections about your distinctiveness coming at a price.

It’s our uniqueness that helps us stand out and win in the long run, whether we’re Amazon or Jasprit Bumrah.

“World number one fast bowler Jasprit Bumrah said something fascinating at a conference where he and I recently spoke in Bangalore. He said his unique action, which is one of his superpowers, is the result of him being “lucky enough to not have a coach when I was younger”.

As a result, he was able to discover and refine his unique action for himself. While he didn’t have a formal coach, he watched and mimicked other great fast bowlers in the process of finding his action.”

Many of the mega trends we’re facing today force us to choose between staying distinct and picking a side. Between winning versus joining a winning side. Our brains are tricked into reacting from a place of fear and loss avoidance. We’re stopped from thinking critically or determining our own unique POV or strategy. 

We like to be on the winning side. It’s less work. It’s less cognitive dissonance. 

Finding and nurturing our distinctiveness is harder. It comes at a price. But so does winning.

2. A story well told

Hello Ishan, it’s Hari here. I have a question for you: do you listen to audiobooks?

I already feel like I have committed a crime by asking that question to readers, and I’m sorry. But I myself am not particularly fanatical about it, though I do read more books than I listen to. I’ve listened to at least a dozen audiobooks over the last two years, usually books I’ve already read, and I don’t think audiobooks are really for passive listening. Something you can usually get away with when it comes to podcasts. 

I mean, I know lots of people do it, but I can’t imagine struggling on a hike and listening to a book like The Autobiography of Malcolm X, written by Alex Haley and Malcolm X and read by Laurence Fishburne. It just doesn’t strike me as a particularly good way to go about things. 

The biggest dealbreaker for me with audiobooks, though, is how they’re performed. Most of the time, I find that audiobooks are read by someone with a nice voice in a flat tone. I believe the thinking is that this is still a book, and so it’s important to avoid the voice artist’s perspectives (and consequently, the way they choose to stress certain words) from influencing the listener too much. Essentially, you should be allowed to come to your own interpretations. 

But that’s where I disagree. By its very nature, being read to influences how you experience the book. You’re not exercising control; you’re surrendering it. To the performer. So why make it boring? 

This, of course, does not apply to books read by the authors themselves. Wherein lies my undying love for Stephen Fry. He’s a fabulous writer, and I haven’t read a single word from his books. I’ve exclusively consumed his audiobook versions. The same goes for a lot of Sherlock Holmes, much of which was again read by Mr. Fry. 

And late last night, I started listening to Mythos, Fry’s refreshing retelling of Greek mythology, for the third time. And I found more joy than I would ever get reading it.

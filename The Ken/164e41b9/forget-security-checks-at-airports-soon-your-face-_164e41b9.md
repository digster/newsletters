I know the exact moment the word came to my mind.
It started with thinking about the long serpentine queues outside airports in India, baggage in one hand, ticket in another, delicately balancing a purse or a wallet in either, on the edge, the piece of paper flapping, hoping to reach the person soon, whose job it is to check if you are you, and you have a flight to catch anytime now, and so before the person says anything, before you are even there almost falling on another person, standing just too close, the queue taking forever, you draw out your PAN card, Aadhaar card, whatever card, a delicate balancing act considering your belongings are all over you, the cart too heavy or rusted to push, but you get there, you get the nod, you walk in; you maybe are just one of the many millions who say thank you, because many are just grateful to have gotten through, cautiously optimistic about the challenge that lies next. Procuring the actual ticket. Baggage check-in. Baggage drop off. Security check. Boarding gate ticket check. If you’ve done a self check-in, and you are flying Indigo Airlines in India, you hear, “ID please”. And then you reach the actual aircraft, and there’s another check which has a tearing sound, when paper meets the fingers of a human who rips right through it.
Sheep. The word is sheep.
We do as we are told. I guess that’s just the way we are. And nowhere is this behaviour more exemplified than at an airport, where we will do anything in the name of security.
Which brings me to the subject of today’s story. Is biometric technology the future of travel? By Arlene Chang.
Lufthansa, British Airways, Delta Airlines, Qantas, Jet Blue, KLM and Air Asia are some of the airlines that have embraced biometric technologies (like lounge entry, biometric boarding) in its processes. Among the airports, Los Angeles Airport, Sydney Airport, Heathrow Airport, Gatwick Airport, Changi Airport, Orlando International Airport, Frankfurt Airport and Schiphol Airport have tested and/or currently use biometric technology for passenger screening, identity verification and border control. Closer home, AirAsia launched its Fast Airport Clearance Experience System (Faces) in February this year. Hyderabad’s Rajiv Gandhi International Airport (RGIA) has said that Aadhaar looks promising. Bengaluru is reported to have been looking for a biometric screening system.
It surely will herald the kind of efficiency that airports could do with.
But not everyone is sure if biometrics are good. There’s a privacy tradeoff. Plus, are biometrics even reliable?

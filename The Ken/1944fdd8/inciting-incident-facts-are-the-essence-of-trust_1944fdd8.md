Inciting Incident
Fri, 10 Jan 25
Inciting Incident is our weekly newsletter about the most powerful tool of our age—stories. Stories told by businesses, leaders, governments..
Good Morning Ishan,
This week, Meta founder Mark Zuckerberg [announced](https://www.theguardian.com/technology/2025/jan/07/meta-facebook-instagram-threads-mark-zuckerberg-remove-fact-checkers-recommend-political-content) that the company will get rid of its fact-checkers in the US. Zuckerberg’s reasoning was that they had eroded more trust than they had created.
Meta will get rid of factcheckers, “dramatically reduce the amount of censorship” and recommend more political content on its platforms, including Facebook, Instagram and Threads, founder Mark Zuckerberg has announced… Zuckerberg said Meta’s “factcheckers have just been too politically biased”.
Every news publication globally shared their take on this announcement, which is hardly surprising given just how much influence Meta wields. The company operates two of the world’s largest social media sites—Facebook and Instagram—with a combined user base of over 3 billion users globally, or over one-third of the human population.
In addition, the scale of its fact-checking venture is truly massive—having spent over US$100m on nearly [80 organisations](https://www.livemint.com/companies/news/whats-community-notes-how-does-it-work-meta-removing-fact-check-all-you-need-to-know-explained-mark-zuckerberg-x-tech-11736318403286.html) and [partners](https://www.theguardian.com/technology/2025/jan/08/meta-layoffs-factchecking-partners) since 2016, all of which needed to be certified by the International Fact-Checking Network (IFCN).
And of course, last but not least, there is the political aspect of the move, with several commentators arguing that the measure was driven by partisan politics. The BBC, for instance, [argued](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/cjwlwlqpwx7o) that the decision is an attempt by Zuckerberg to get closer to the new Trump administration in the US, notwithstanding the risks it holds for Meta’s relationship with advertisers.
There are a plethora of perspectives and consequences to be analysed, and intentions to question. But in this edition of Inciting Incident, I just want to focus on trust, and its role in narrative science. In the past, I have written about how narratives can be rooted in trust ([June 2024](https://the-ken.com/incitingincident/inciting-incident-no-spin-can-save-a-brand-from-reality/) and [November 2021)](https://the-ken.com/incitingincident/policybazaar-needs-agents-of-trust/), and the many tools a storyteller can use to cultivate it.
But as effective as any of them are, all fail in the absence of one foundational ingredient—facts.
In an [open letter](https://www.poynter.org/ifcn/2025/an-open-letter-to-mark-zuckerberg-from-the-worlds-fact-checkers-nine-years-later/) following Meta’s announcement, the IFCN noted that Zuckerberg’s decision was questioning the very process that Meta had developed and the beliefs it has professed in the past. It also underlined the adverse impact of unchecked misinformation and offered evidence claiming that its certified fact-checkers are neither biased nor responsible for censorship.
Meta required all fact-checking partners to meet strict nonpartisanship standards through verification by the International Fact-Checking Network. This meant no affiliations with political parties or candidates, no policy advocacy, and an unwavering commitment to objectivity and transparency. Each news organization undergoes rigorous annual verification, including independent assessment and peer review. Far from questioning these standards, Meta has consistently praised their rigor and effectiveness. Just a year ago, Meta extended the program to Threads.
Your comments suggest fact-checkers were responsible for censorship, even though Meta never gave fact-checkers the ability or the authority to remove content or accounts. People online have often blamed and harassed fact-checkers for Meta’s actions. Your recent comments will no doubt fuel those perceptions. But the reality is that Meta staff decided on how content found to be false by fact-checkers should be downranked or labeled. Several fact-checkers over the years have suggested to Meta how it could improve this labeling to be less intrusive and avoid even the appearance of censorship, but Meta never acted on those suggestions. Additionally, Meta exempted politicians and political candidates from fact-checking as a precautionary measure, even when they spread known falsehoods. Fact-checkers, meanwhile, said that politicians should be fact-checked like anyone else.
The letter is detailed and elaborate, and you should take the time to read it, because not only does it open up questions on Meta’s motivations, but also articulates how important fact-checking is in building trust.
The loss of trust is already evident. Yesterday, for instance, Techcrunch [reported](https://techcrunch.com/2025/01/09/google-searches-for-deleting-facebook-instagram-explode-after-meta-ends-fact-checking/) that Google searches for how to cancel and delete Facebook, Instagram, and Threads accounts have seen an explosive increase in the US following Zuckerberg’s announcement. A large number of users are clearly ready to leave Meta’s platforms.
Meta’s wish to follow in the footsteps of X and use community notes to battle misinformation is also being questioned. In fact, IFCN’s letter offers the example of X and its Community Notes as an example of how social media sites lose trust in the absence of rigorous factchecking.
As noted by a former Twitter employee in an [account](https://www.independent.co.uk/voices/twitter-fake-news-tiktok-fact-checker-misinformation-b2559791.html) published by The Independent.
Since then, the EU has found that X is the social media platform with the highest disinformation rate. As Miah Hammond-Errey, the director of the Emerging Technology Program at the United States Studies Centre at the University of Sydney said last year: “Few recent actions have done more to make a social media platform safe for disinformation, extremism, and authoritarian regime propaganda than the changes to Twitter since its purchase by Elon Musk.”
[…]
Social networks are trying to combat this, to a greater and (in Musk’s case) lesser extent, and we need to help them – features like X’s Community Notes are a start, though it’s painfully little in comparison to what the platform used to have.
In fact, Elon Musk, the most followed user on X and the platform’s owner, has been accused of being [responsible](https://www.nbcnews.com/tech/misinformation/musk-election-misinformation-x-officials-twitter-voting-rcna176938) for sharing conspiracy theories and misinformation on the US elections, none of which were checked by the community notes features.
[Surveys now suggest](https://www.ibtimes.co.uk/survey-shows-americans-trust-amazon-tiktok-more-elon-musks-x-us-government-1727113) that the American population have the least amount of trust in X as a social media and digital platform. [Many organisations globally have also](https://www.civilsociety.co.uk/news/social-justice-charities-considering-leaving-x-due-to-misinformation-survey-finds.html) either left or considered leaving X due to the prevalence of misinformation on the platform.
When Elon Musk acquired Twitter in 2022, he laid off swaths of workers tasked with moderating the platform and embraced an experimental approach: asking users to fact-check one another.
Musk has touted the crowdsourcing program, called Community Notes, as “the best source of truth on the internet.” But the majority of accurate fact checks proposed by users on political posts are never shown to the public, according to research from the nonprofit Center for Countering Digital Hate (CCDH) and a separate data analysis by The Washington Post — suggesting that the feature is failing to provide a meaningful check on misinformation.
Elon Musk says X users fight falsehoods. The falsehoods are winning, [The Washington Post](https://www.washingtonpost.com/technology/2024/10/30/elon-musk-x-fact-check-community-notes-misinformation/)
It is the enduring tragedy of history that it repeats itself. And Zuckerberg and Meta are firmly on the path to becoming one more example for why in the absence of fact and truth and transparency, and a robust system to preserve them, there can be no trust.

Good Morning Ishan,
You may have seen the offers plastered on Facebook or Linkedin: MBBS seats are up for sale. Applicants just need to claim they are non-resident Indians.
Many of the young men and women who take up these offers, however, are born and bred in India. Generally, their NEET scores aren’t great, but other conditions give them a boost. They tend to be from well-to-do families, with “blood relatives” residing overseas.
That’s all they need to take advantage of a technicality and land a spot in their preferred medical college. Education consultants help connect these applicants with institutions—for a cost that’s based on their target schools and how low their NEET scores are. The service even includes an opportunity to meet the dean so they can close the deal in person.
This arrangement benefits private medical universities, too—in financial terms, anyway. Individuals with NRI status pay up to 5X for their tuition compared to domestic students. A senior official with the Indian Medical Association described this as a “major money spinner” for these schools and their owners, which are mostly politicians and education societies.
Competition is intense. In total, 15% of seats in private medical colleges and deemed universities across the country are reserved for NRI students, and many of these slots go to the highest bidder.
More than 1,500 students have already converted their status from “Indian” to “NRI” during this year’s admission rounds.

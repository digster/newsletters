Why has all the excitement and disruption gone out of startups? Startups are neither fun, nor are they able to build disruptive products and experiences. Are the two connected? Who is responsible? And how do we break this cycle?

By Praveen Gopal Krishnan
13 min read

Good Morning Ishan,

In an earlier edition of The Ken’s weekend newsletter, The Nutgraf, I wrote:

For over a decade, startups used to be the place where people went to do exciting, groundbreaking, challenging work that gave them a high—mostly because it gave them meaning to solve fun problems in creative, unconventional ways. I remember what it was like when startups suddenly became glamorous. I’d gone into it earlier when it wasn’t that “sexy” or cool, but very soon, I started getting calls from friends and B-school batchmates who were doing banking, consulting, and finance at large companies and looking for something fun and interesting to do.

All that has changed now. For months, all the stories I hear have a familiar tone to them. I’ve met several senior executives who have quit their jobs and are on “a career break”. Many more are in the process of quitting and are negotiating sabbaticals. I’ve heard stories about founders and co-founders of some of India’s biggest startups who confess in private, closed circles that they no longer feel much joy or satisfaction from what they’re doing—but they see no option but to continue running on the treadmill. Folks who are much younger in their career, often in their 20s, are throwing all their energies, creativity, and focus not in their work, but in retiring from work. Not to get a safety net to pursue work that they love, but simply to retire. Most of these people compensate for this lack of purpose by endlessly consuming content on podcasts, Youtube, and Instagram that exclusively promises a positive, optimistic future—from India becoming an economic powerhouse, or Artificial Intelligence (AI) changing the world, or stories of founders, or simply financial independence.

There’s an entire generation of people inside startups who are weary, tired, and see no purpose. These are smart, talented, ambitious people, who were once excited and found meaning in work. Now, they are either rudderless, or simply coping by consuming fantasies predicting the future, or about the past.

How VCs killed the meaning of work, The Nutgraf

This was a fairly provocative edition. I got tons of emails from executives, managers, and leaders agreeing with me. I also got emails insisting that I’d missed the point entirely about working in startups. 

Generally speaking, most people believed in one of two things:

Some put forward the idea that the reason why startups were no longer able to disrupt or be exciting is because of external reasons, i.e., disruption has become harder because of changes in the market, capital, and funding. Simply put—the environment is the culprit. 

Others blamed startups themselves—their position was that startups had structurally changed, and that they were no longer capable of creating disruptive products. Founders and executives do not have the right incentives, and this was responsible for killing excitement and disruption at work. 

Two forces at play—one external and one internal.

So we decided to pose the same question to two people who understand both of these factors really well. This kicked-off a fascinating, free-flowing discussion on who was responsible for killing excitement and disruption in startups, and how we will emerge from this. 

The first person we brought in was Ashish Sinha. Ashish is an experienced product manager and also the founder of Nextbigwhat, one of India’s oldest digital publications chronicling stories about products, technology, and startups from India. He used to work as a Product Manager at Yahoo in the 2000s. As a result, he’s witnessed all kinds of boom and bust cycles with startups in India.

When asked if the excitement in working at startups has diminished, his first words were unequivocal.

“I believe, yes.” 

The other person we decided to talk to is Professor Sourav Mukherji, who teaches Organisational Behaviour at IIM Bangalore. He’s also the Dean of Alumni and Development, and worked at organisations like Boston Consulting Group, Oracle, and IBM before joining IIM-B as a faculty. Prof. Mukherji taught me when I was studying at IIM Bangalore, so this was a full circle moment for me to have him be a part of one of our group discussions.

When the question was posed to him, Prof. Mukherji did exactly what I expected from him. He went into the structural aspects of excitement, disruption, and ultimately the way how organisations work, function, and grow. 

He also had some strong views. 

“I think the startup ecosystem, if the driving force behind it is about higher valuation and I quickly cash out, then I think it will go through ebbs and flows. Because it’s very premised on the liquidity situation. Whereas, if you’re driven by the long term, then I think you will also understand that these are part of the game.”

This story that you’re reading is a narrated account of an opinionated and sprawling discussion that took place over an hour and fifteen minutes at Indiranagar in Bengaluru. Quotes from the conversation have been condensed and mildly edited for brevity and flow. If you’d like to listen to the full episode, here’s a link.

Listen to episode

“Don’t do a startup, please”

There’s significant evidence that startups are going through a lean period right now. Funding and capital has diminished considerably in the last couple of years. Last year, in 2023, startup funding fell 72% to a six-year low. Even in 2024, there’s a DC Advisory report that states that 62% of funding rounds in the US$50–500 million range in the January-June period were secondary transactions or buyouts, with external primary investment rounds making up only 13%. 

Consequently, the ability for startups to disrupt “old-world” businesses also appears to have halted. Edtech companies like Byju’s, Unacademy, and Physicswallah are either going offline or being bested by offline competitors. That’s true for retail as well. The share of offline retail in smartphone sales jumped to 61% in the first quarter of 2024, which is the highest in the last five quarters. India’s fastest growing fashion brand is not a startup, but Tata-owned Zudio. Banks are beating fintechs in credit cards, lending, and financial services.

Ashish believes this is happening because people forgot to build products and got obsessed with building startups. 

“I’ve been to a lot of B schools, a lot of IITs, you know, for guest lectures and all, my first slide is, ‘Don’t do a startup please’. And it’s fundamentally for one simple reason—that when you do a startup, the first thing is, okay, what is my business model going to be? What is my valuation? How do I raise money? You don’t say what I’m going to build, right?”–0:10:19 minutes

And then, the kicker.

“There’s no Indian tech ecosystem anymore. It’s now the Indian startup ecosystem.”–0:10:47 minutes

Telling people not to build startups may sound like radical advice, but these are radical times.

Who killed excitement and disruption? The VCs did

If there’s one singular culprit whom we identified as the most responsible for killing disruption and excitement inside startups, it was the venture capitalists. Surprisingly, the strongest case against VCs did not come from Ashish, Rohin, or me, but from Prof. Mukherji.

He started with, “I don’t have great views about VCs…” 

And then he explained why.

“I get very concerned when I see, for the startup capital is important. But also important is advice, and also important is mentoring. And I get very worried about some of the advice and mentoring when I listen to the VCs.”–0:27:30 minutes

And then a bit later, he repeated his point, in case we missed it.  

“In general, VCs are the last people whose advice you should take about running organisations.”

Prof. Mukherji’s argument was essentially this—you don’t have to take advice from the people you take your money from. Sure, VCs are in the business of risk capital, and hence have an incentive to be overtly positive about the future. However, the influence of VCs also led to other eventualities, such as inflated valuations that fundamentally altered incentives inside startups, making them less likely to create disruption.

Sometime last year, Nithin Kamath, the CEO of Zerodha, said, “I think the number of founders and leaders, especially at late-stage startups quitting will only increase, making it harder for businesses to survive this funding winter. This is because of liquidation preferences and the disconnect between valuations and business fundamentals.” A partial list of companies whose leaders have quit recently include Swiggy, Unacademy, Ola, Dealshare, Upgrad, Teachmint. 

This then led to another provocative line of discussion, started by Rohin—was there a way for anyone to “short” a startup investment?

“Generally, in any market, there should be an opportunity for both a bull and a bear. You can go long or you can go short. A hedge fund can come in and say—this stock is overvalued. I’m going to short sell it. I can profit on the fact that this is overvalued. 

“What is the counter view to a VCs exuberance? If we feel that VCs are over optimistic and investing too much money, is it possible for any kind of a player to come and take an opposite position and make money from it?”–0:42:07 minutes

It’s an interesting question. 

What does that look like?

The most dangerous time to build a startup is now

When we delved deeper into the external reasons why disruption has gone out of startups, we went into three specific areas—duopolies, regulation, and market size. 

All of these have had their impact on reducing disruption. 

However, Rohin had a stronger point of view. In his words, I was being “too soft”.

So he went all in. 

“I don’t think there’s been a more dangerous time to be a disruptive startup than India today.”

He explained.

“You can look at sector after sector where, I mean, it could be something like bike taxis, where a regulator comes in. Like just last week, a few days ago, rows upon rows of bikes have been impounded in Bengaluru. You could look at the number of fintech apps that have been shut down, put on hold. You could look at credit card companies that have been forced to, or banks that have been forced to stop onboarding. So I feel like I’m fully on the side of startups, here.”–0:47:25 minutes

The argument is that startups aren’t disruptive anymore because it’s far too risky to be disruptive. And that risk hasn’t been created by VCs, but by regulators. 

“The closest analogy I can think of is how Bengaluru’s IT services sector got built. There is a very famous saying that it got built when the government wasn’t looking. So when it scaled, that’s when the government realised, ‘Oh, something was happening here’. It was too late. They’ve already scaled and gone public. I think that window closed very quickly for startups. Large companies have figured out that, you know, they’re not gonna allow this. Regulators are figured out. So if you are a truly disruptive company trying to do anything, you’ll find very quickly that regulators or large competitors are going to come down on you. And to be fair, I think it’s really risky and dangerous.”–0:48:04 minutes

He ended with a… vivid metaphor. 

“You don’t stick your neck out if somebody’s going to chop it.”

Hope

One way to argue about how this will end is to state that this is ‘cyclical’, i.e., all organisations and sectors go through a lull period and a boom period. However, there are reasons to believe that this ‘lull’ is different. For example, online e-commerce fundamentally changed due to Amazon in the US, and there’s no reason to believe that disruption will come back to that sector. In fact, there’s a fantastic paper titled Coopting Disruption, written by a couple of researchers at the Stanford School of Law. They argue that disruption is much harder, because it’s being ‘coopted’ by Big Tech in the West. The abstract reads:

“Our economy is dominated by five aging tech giants—Alphabet, Amazon, Apple, Meta, and Microsoft. In the last twenty years, no company has commercialized a new technology in a way that threatens them. Why?

We argue that the tech giants have learned how to coopt disruption. They identify potentially disruptive technologies, use their money to influence the startups developing them, strategically dole out access to the resources the startups need to grow, and seek regulation that makes it harder for the startups to compete. When a threat emerges, they buy it off. And after they acquire a startup, they redirect its people and assets to their own innovation needs. These seemingly unrelated behaviors work together to enable the tech giants to maintain their dominance in the face of disruptive innovations.”

Sure there may be something similar playing out in India, where disruption is coopted by regulators, governments, and even large companies. But there’s also reason to believe that things will change for the better. 

Prof. Mukherji explained that there are some fundamental forces at play. 

“I remember one of our partners in BCG, he had this slide that said we always overestimate the short term and underestimate the long term, right? As human beings, that’s always the case. We even know what is the long term implication of AI and generative AI, but that is going to be very different from the hype that we are generating today.”–1:03:46 minutes

Ashish, on the other hand, had other reasons to be optimistic, and spoke about something specific called micro SaaS, which is a portfolio approach to startups. 

“I think that is also one of the things that I’m seeing, that a lot of great engineers and, you know, makers, are building these micro SaaS businesses, which they are quite happy with. I mean, it’s making money. For example, you launch a simple tool—let’s say, a notion plugin. Or, you know, Figma plugin. And it gives, you, say, 100,000 MRR, right? You launch 10 such products. You launch for Shopify, etc.? There’s a lot of opportunity and interest in that space, as compared to, say, launch one big product, raise VC money.”–1:08:21 minutes

And finally, the last word went to Rohin. 

He stuck his neck out and predicted not just the change, but who would drive the change.

“I feel change and disruption is going to come from the 22–25 year old segment, because they’re not encumbered by, you know, EMIs.”–1:11:49 minutes

Essentially, the youth shall not just inherit the earth. 

They’ll be the ones who’ll create it.

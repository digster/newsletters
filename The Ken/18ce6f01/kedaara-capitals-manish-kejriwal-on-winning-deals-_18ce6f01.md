Good Morning Ishan,
Venture capital makes private equity seem tame in comparison.
VC is often associated with self-assured, twentysomething entrepreneurs and the disruption they intend to unleash. PE, on the other hand, is to do as much with a decades-old family business as with a tech startup on the verge of a billion-dollar valuation.
But this impressive range is partly why the world of PE makes for a fascinating study.
Take Kedaara Capital, for instance. The 11-year-old PE firm, one of India’s largest, has invested in a construction-equipment manufacturer (Ajax), an eyewear retailer (Lenskart), and an education financier (Avanse Financial Services), among others.
As you read Seema’s rewarding interview with one of its three founders, Manish Kejriwal, you’ll realise what Kedaara—with US$3.6 billion in assets under management—doesn’t do is as important to its success as what it does.
- It doesn’t back a company based only on the potential return on its investment, no matter how juicy the multiple is.
- It doesn’t believe in outbidding other investors to land a deal.
- It doesn’t bet on turnarounds, which other PE firms love.
Kejriwal, who spent two decades at McKinsey and Temasek, is unusually candid not just about his investing style but also about himself: “If it weren’t for Ho Ching [former Temasek CEO], I would be an obnoxious, arrogant man. (Maybe some people still think I’m that, but leave it aside for now.)”

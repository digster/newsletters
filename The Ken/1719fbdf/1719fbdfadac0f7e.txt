In the comic Asterix, the Gaul chieftain Vitalstatistix lived in perpetual fear that one day the sky would fall on his head. If he were in the travel business, he'd be forgiven for thinking that day has come.

Indeed, the novel coronavirus has disrupted the travel sector like nothing has before. In India, which has been under lockdown for almost a month now, industry bodies estimate the fallout from the pandemic will cost the sector around $65 billion.

But while the Covid-19 storm is set to tear through the space, it's set to impact homestay pioneer Airbnb more than most. After all, trust—the glue that holds the homestay business together, is likely to be one of the casualties of the pandemic. 

When travel does finally open up, guests will be paranoid about the hygiene standards of homestays. Hosts will be worried about guests being potential carriers of the virus. Already, many property owners have stopped taking booking requests. Others have left the platform altogether, choosing instead to enter the conventional, long-term rental market. Lower returns, sure. But fewer worries as well. 

This is a body blow for Airbnb, which has laboured to reach 80,000 listings in India over seven years. For perspective, the Australian state of New South Wales had almost 60,000 listings in 2019. How will Airbnb survive? That's what Vandana's story is about.

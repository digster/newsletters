Just in case you have forgotten the euphoria surrounding lifestyle e-tailer Nykaa’s IPO in late 2021, allow me to quote me from a [story](https://the-ken.com/story/how-nykaa-went-from-investor-darling-to-tech-laggard/) I wrote just a little over a year after its listing:
The lifestyle e-tailer’s Rs 5,350 crore (~US$660 million) initial public offer (IPO) was a standout in 2021. Subscribed 82X in November that year—compared to food-delivery platform Zomato’s 38X in July—Nykaa listed at ~80% premium to its issue price, versus the foodtech’s 53%. And even as the stock market entered choppy waters in early 2022, Nykaa navigated them better than Zomato or fintech Paytm*.
Nykaa was different from other VC-funded tech ventures in more ways than one. Its founder Falguni Nayar was almost 50 when she set up Nykaa, and had spent a little under two decades in investment banking and broking. Plus, she had managed to turn Nykaa profitable before taking it public, something none of its peers could boast of.
Nykaa couldn’t have had better credentials, and its shares promptly soared, valuing the company at Rs 1.15 lakh crore, or US$14 billion. But the e-tailer soon began to crumble under the weight of investors’ expectations. Slowing growth, especially in its fashion business, had knocked 70% off its market capitalisation by January 2023.
Don’t get me wrong, Nykaa hasn’t had a single loss-making quarter since it went public. But in the year ended March 2024, its profits were the same as two years earlier: Rs 40 crore (US$4.8 million). On the other hand, Zomato, in its first year in the black, made Rs 350 crore (US$42 million).
In terms of market capitalisation, Zomato is three Nykaas and then some.

It’s a time of bonfires. Of controls and decontrols, depending on where you look.
For engineering education, mid-May marked the end of Inspector Raj after the apex regulatory body, AICTE, decided to do away with physical inspection of colleges. An approval for a new college or institute in India would now rely solely on self-disclosure and online checks.
Good riddance to bad rubbish is how most private and public institution chiefs viewed this. The past two decades have seen the rise and fall of the engineering degree in India. Colleges popped up sooner than one could count the engineering branches, reaching a tipping point when AICTE had to close scores of them down. Still, there are at least 6,200 AICTE-approved engineering colleges, half of whose graduates go without a job every year.
With inspection giving way to accreditation, India is at least directionally moving closer to global best practices.
Even though the yardstick—via the National Board of Accreditation (NBA)—might still be controlled by AICTE, the possibility of third-party accreditation agencies is now ripe, says Shreedhar in his story today. Colleges could be in a position to choose their accreditation body, signalling an important shift in the power dynamics of the education sector.
Also unrealistic is AICTE's expectation that by 2022, NBA accreditation would be embraced by all. In fact, it’s deceptively straightforward. “But what does accreditation mean when the quality of institutes itself is poor? Any accreditation that we will do, if it is honest, will have to close colleges down,” says the director of Punjab Engineering College.

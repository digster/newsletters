Good Morning Ishan,

Can a peer-to-peer loan be considered a realistic alternative to investing in mutual funds? On the surface, it seems like an asinine question. The two are antithetical. One promises high returns with high risk; the other offers safer investments with respectable returns.

In 2018, when we'd last reported https://the-ken.com/story/peer-to-peer-lending-claims-its-first-victims/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter on India's P2P lending sector, the concept had just claimed its first victims. These were investors who were lured with the promise of high returns, and promised that the risky borrower profiles they were lending to had been adequately vetted by the various P2P lending platforms.В 

Turns out, this wasn't always the case. Defaults exploded, and lenders were left carrying the can as platforms weren't liable for the losses or even for collections.

India's central bank even recognised the risk inherent in these platforms, and stepped in with regulations to limit lenders' risk. The damage, though, had already been done. P2P lending went from an investment opportunity to a roll of the dice.

Today, P2P platforms no longer enjoy the sheen they once did. Growth is steady, but hardly impressive. Borrowers are as eager, but lenders not so much. This imbalance is a problem, and platforms are trying to fix it. They're trying to lure investors back to the space. Not by promising higher returns, but safer investment opportunities. They're aspiring to be seen as a real alternative to safe, traditional investment avenues such as mutual funds, while also taking a leaf out of the mutual funds playbook.В 

It's a fascinating rebrand of a sector with more than its fair share of infamy. Will it actually yield results though?

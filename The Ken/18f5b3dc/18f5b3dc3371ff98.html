A rising wave of engineering-exam toppers is gravitating towards courses focussed on artificial intelligence. It is to the benefit of (almost) everybody

Delivered to Ishan, Subscriber to The Ken.

Thu, 09 May 2024

VIEW IN BROWSER
The Spotlight by Arundhati Ramanathan
Deputy Editor

Good Morning Ishan,

Every year, like clockwork, the cream of the crop from the JEE (Advanced) exam enters the various Indian Institutes of Technology (IITs) to flock to computer science programmes. But a growing number of IITians across engineering disciplines are ditching traditional routes.

They are now leaning towards courses and electives focussed on artificial intelligence (AI) or machine learning (ML). Nowhere is this more obvious than the clamour for such classes. Professors in engineering institutes who teach anything remotely related to AI are being seen as rock stars.

In a post-ChatGPT job market where the most lucrative software gigs demand AI/ML skill sets, students from computer science and beyond are lining up to enrol in these courses.

Abhirami has a rich story chronicling this big shift happening in computer science education at India’s premier engineering colleges. This is especially important as many of the IITs, such as the ones in Delhi and Kanpur, are putting AI and ML at the front and centre for their ongoing curriculum overhaul. Don’t miss reading this.

Over in Indonesia, the country has had some great PR over the last few months with Microsoft and Apple planning to bring foreign investment to the country. But the plight of Baru Gold, a Canadian gold-mining company underscores the ongoing risk some foreign investors face doing business in Indonesia.

The company got the green light to begin mining in 2021 on Sangihe Island, an outpost about the size of Singapore almost equidistant from Sulawesi and the Philippines’ southernmost island.

A year and a half from the start of production, the company had hoped to be producing as much as US$9 million worth of the precious metal a month. But illegal miners are making off with US$1 million a month.

Jeff Hutton writes about how the company is resorting to international arbitration even as Indonesia aims to transition into an advanced economy by mid-century.

Boring or bling?

Remember the time when financial services were boring? Fintech has been jam-packed with action with companies getting banned and things breaking. Globally, too. Bloomberg Businessweek wrote about how after the Silicon Valley Bank collapse, regional banks like PNC Financial Services Group Inc. have introduced TV ads and plastered signs across its branches arguing that it’s “brilliantly boring”.

Boring, they argue, is synonymous with being dependable and steady.

In India, the flashiest of fintechs has been Cred. It spent Rs 713 crore (US$85.4 million) in the financial year for 2023 on marketing and promotion—this is definitely an improvement on the Rs 975 crore (US$116.8 million) it spent in 2022—but that’s still about half of the revenue it made for 2023.

On the other hand, HDFC Bank, India’s largest private lender, spent Rs 236 crore (US$28.2 million) on advertising—a rounding-off error on the Rs 1.9 lakh crore (US$22.7 billion) total income for 2023.

It’s clear that boring brings in money, but what does bling bring?

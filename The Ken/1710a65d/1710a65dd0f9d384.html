Innovations in the bus industry globally have been few and far between, and nearly absent in India. There are two that catch the eye though—Olli and TEB.
 
Olli is a 3D-printed, self-driving minibus created by US-based Local Motors in 2016. That same year China unveiled its Transit Elevated Bus, or TEB—an electric bus that glides 16 feet above the road on stilt-like appendages that hit the ground on the road edges. All very futuristic.
 
On the service side, the last major upgrade was in 2013, by Germany's FlixBus. Their intercity bus service model is a mix of Uber-like bus aggregation and OYO-like standardisation of services such as Wifi, GPS tracking and even onboard toilets.
 
It's a model introduced in India exactly one year ago. Not by FlixBus, but by IntrCity—a unit of rail and bus ticketing platform RailYatri. In this, a bus operator manages the nuts and bolts of supply, like buying the bus, paying for fuel and driver. IntrCity foots the bill for the spit-and-polish of service and ticketing, i.e. the demand side. It pays operators monthly rent, easing their pain of fluctuating cash flow due to seasonality. A business model match made in heaven? Not quite!
 
IntrCity has signed up only around 90 buses in a year, a fraction of the 250,000 AC buses plying in India. Why? IntrCity isn't listed on RedBus, comfortably the country's largest ticketing platform. What's happening there? A trickle of rivals could soon gather pace, including with the pioneer's entry. Can IntrCity keep up?

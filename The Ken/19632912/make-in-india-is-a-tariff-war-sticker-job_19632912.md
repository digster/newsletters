Make India Competitive Again
Mon, 14 Apr 25
What India is doing, will do, and should do—to not just survive but thrive in the chaos unleashed by Trump.
Good Morning Ishan,
In the time it took for these words to travel from my keyboard to your screen, Donald Trump might have changed his position on tariffs five times and named the countries that are “kissing my ass”.
It’s impossible to keep up with the US President’s beautiful, beautiful mind.
First the shocking tariffs, then the 90-day pause, and the Mexican standoff with China in between—not to mention the exemption for smartphones, laptops, and memory chips from China over the weekend. Nobel Prize-winning economists have their heads in their hands trying to make sense of the Molotov cocktail Trump has thrown at global trade as we have known it for decades.
India can be sanguine right now looking at the US’s 145% tariffs on Chinese imports (and China’s 125% levies on US goods). The US has more than halved the newly announced tariffs on most countries, including India, to a baseline levy of 10%, even as it drives a hard bargain with them.
But with Trump, everyone knows always expecting the worst is actually business as usual.
What India does, and chooses not to, over the next few years could alter the course of its economic history. Many commentators have rightly called this India’s “second 1991 moment”.
India had its back to the wall then, which made it realise liberalising its economy was its only shot at escaping a crippling future. There’s perhaps a lot more at stake now. The US will not continue to be an all-weather friend to India, as it worries about its own economy and lowered status in the global pecking order. Adversarial neighbour China will unleash more of its manufacturing and technological might on the world.

With the prologue out of the way, here’s our first edition.
One of the first reactions to Trump’s tariffs in the Indian media was: let’s have more of ‘Make in India’. Launched in 2014, months into Narendra Modi’s first term as Prime Minister, Make in India was our MAGA (Make America Great Again) even before Trump claimed MAGA from Ronald Reagan.
The rationale behind the calls for stepping up domestic manufacturing is fairly obvious. The pain the US inflicts on China will work to India’s advantage, given the wide gulf in tariffs on the Asian giants.
This is perhaps prompted by the success India has lately had in assembling [smartphones](https://www.moneycontrol.com/news/telecom/india-s-smartphone-exports-hit-record-rs-2-lakh-crore-becomes-country-s-top-export-commodity-12992002.html)—now India’s single-largest export commodity. But drawing a straight line from that to India’s supposed manufacturing resurgence is a case of missing the forest for the trees.
The first decade of Make in India is quite telling.
In FY24, India channeled a slightly higher share of its gross domestic product to assets such as factories, equipment, and infrastructure than 10 years earlier. That’s certainly a sign of intent to invest in long-term growth.
But manufacturing is no more important to the Indian economy now than before Modi took office. Even the introduction in 2020 of production-linked incentives hasn’t helped matters. India is nowhere close to its target of having manufacturing contribute 25% of its economic output by 2025.
Unsurprisingly, days before Trump’s reciprocal tariffs, [Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/markets/emerging/indias-23-bln-plan-rival-china-factories-lapse-after-it-disappoints-2025-03-21/) reported that India plans to let the subsidies lapse.
Many firms that participated in the program failed to kickstart production, while others that met manufacturing targets found India slow to pay out subsidies, according to government documents and correspondence seen by Reuters.
As of October 2024, participating firms had produced $151.93 billion worth of goods under the program, or 37% of the target that Delhi had set, according to an undated analysis of the program compiled by the commerce ministry. India had issued just $1.73 billion in incentives – or under 8% of the allocated funds, the document said.
If weaning itself off Chinese imports was the first motive behind India’s manufacturing push, the second was to find jobs for its burgeoning workforce.
But manufacturing has come a cropper here.
In the nine years to FY23, the last year for which sector-wise numbers are available, manufacturing added 8.2 million jobs. But its share in India’s employment slid; it accounted for just over one in 10 jobs.
It’s no coincidence that the ranks of gig workers making privileged Indians’ lives easier by instantly bringing them groceries and doing odd jobs in their homes are [swelling](https://the-ken.com/story/vcs-and-techies-are-turning-india-into-a-nation-of-hired-hands/).
India’s yearning for a manufacturing renaissance is grossly misplaced. Even Germany, one of the world’s most industrialised nations, has witnessed the share of manufacturing in its economy fall since the 1990s, as economist Paul Krugman pointed out in his excellent Substack [newsletter](https://paulkrugman.substack.com/p/no-trump-cant-make-manufacturing).
This is before Chinese automakers such as SAIC and BYD took a [wrecking ball](https://www.economist.com/business/2025/02/13/chinese-cars-are-taking-over-the-global-south) to Germany’s largest industry.
It’s not just India; even Germany has been running twice as hard to stay in the same place in the global goods trade.
As much as countries don’t want to accept it, Chinese manufacturers do have nine lives. The US wanted to crush Huawei with sanctions, but the company, with ample support from the Chinese government, continues to dominate the global [telecom-equipment](https://www.wsj.com/business/telecom/huawei-china-technology-us-sanctions-76462031) market; has staged a spectacular comeback in [smartphones](https://www.wsj.com/tech/apples-smartphone-sales-slump-in-china-as-huawei-gains-market-share-b1a986df); and is making swift advances in [artificial-intelligence chips](https://www.ft.com/content/f46b7f6d-62ed-4b64-8ad7-2417e5ab34f6).
Despite measures by large buyers such as the US and India to cripple Chinese solar-panel makers, exports from China increased [13%](https://www.pv-magazine.com/2025/02/07/china-exports-235-9-gw-of-solar-panels-in-2024/) in 2024. In fact, China was able to use its scale to cut costs and sell its modules cheaper than Indian producers despite a 40% import duty. Not only did it not help local manufacturing, it also hampered the construction of solar farms at the pace India wanted.
What’s more, just as India thought its incentives for Indian module makers would drive up production capacity, the Joe Biden administration offered them subsidies that were [10X](https://the-ken.com/story/why-make-in-america-when-make-in-india-has-a-3b-sweetener/) what India promised.
Trump may have torched most of his predecessor’s clean-energy initiatives, but India’s experience in solar panels points to a quandary. Its tariffs can never be high enough to be a deterrent, and its sops pale next to those of a developed country just as eager to keep its shop floors humming.
Little wonder, then, that Indian economists have exhorted India to use this opportunity not to raise tariffs but to slash them instead.
As a general rule, we should resist action under pressure. However, in this instance, there is a good case for lowering tariffs because they are too high for our own good. Reducing our high tariff levels would reduce domestic production costs and improve our competitiveness. Small- and medium-scale industries will be the biggest beneficiaries because they purchase intermediate goods produced by large industries in India, which are able to charge high prices because of protection.
Those who worry that lower import duties will hurt the ability of Indian industry to compete with imports should be persuaded that this can be countered through appropriate exchange rate policy.
Weathering the Trade Tsunami, by Montek Singh Ahluwalia, [India Today](https://www.indiatoday.in/magazine/guest-column/story/20250421-weathering-the-trade-tsunami-by-montek-singh-ahluwalia-2707397-2025-04-11)
But Indian policymakers refuse to see the writing on the wall, which Krugman [articulates](https://paulkrugman.substack.com/p/no-trump-cant-make-manufacturing) in the context of the US.
Anyway, the bottom line is that we are never ever getting back to being the manufacturing nation of yore. But why should we want to go back?

Why do people imagine that manufacturing offers ordinary workers good jobs? In part because it used to — not because there’s something inherent about working on an assembly line that leads to good wages, but because once upon a time many manufacturing workers were represented by strong unions. Now they aren’t, and manufacturing jobs are nothing special.
India’s interventions in recent years have at best halted manufacturing’s inevitable decline. Of course, there will be silver linings, like the seemingly juicy opportunity in smartphones. But there’s only so much production capacity you can wrest from other countries, especially when the global market for handsets is hardly on a tear. After two years of decline, smartphone shipments grew 5% in 2024.
Even while India continues to press for localisation in products of strategic importance, such as semiconductors, it cannot ignore that manufacturing is no saviour.
But with a workforce three times that of the US, and an employment crisis only exacerbated by the rising productivity of workers and increased automation, wishful thinking is the first predictable response.

Good Morning Ishan, 

Here’s a completely made-up scenario. 

 

Imagine that there’s a brilliant, young, fintech company that built a UPI payments app. UPI payments is more or less a commodity, and probably even loss-making. So this company desires to expand its offerings to provide other financial services, like credit, insurance, and some such. 

 

Normally, product expansion would be pretty straightforward, but this fintech company has one big concern—India’s financial regulator. The RBI is basically in-charge of making sure fintechs aren’t too overzealous and don’t cut corners in their quest to acquire new customers or set up new businesses. And sometimes, this is a grey zone—there isn’t a comprehensive rulebook somewhere that says what you can or cannot do. Sometimes, the only way you can find out if the thing you want to do is above board or not with the regulator is to just do it, and hope nobody gives you a call. 

 

Of late, this fintech company has noticed that the RBI and other regulators have been especially strict. Companies that tried to innovate credit card business models were told to stop. This fintech company’s closest competitor essentially got its entire business shut down by the RBI. Another competitor was restricted from onboarding new merchants. 

 

To make matters worse, there’s a proposal going around to cap the market share of UPI payment apps, which if implemented, places this fintech company in an even worse position.  

 

Tough times. 

 

And this fintech is ambitious. It wants to get into lucrative areas like stock-broking, insurance, mutual funds, mobile advertising, e-commerce, and much more. So it starts to make steps in these directions. To make matters complicated, this fintech is a wholly-owned American company. It’s not even registered in India. So the first thing that it wants to do is to show that it is an Indian company. And to do this, it takes some extreme steps. It starts talking about how Indian it really is, despite its ownership structure. It changes its domicile from Singapore to India and pays a cool billion dollars in taxes to the Indian government for the privilege of doing so. It launches an e-commerce app on a platform backed by the government. It tattoos the Indian flag on its face.

 

It’s making progress, but it’s still not enough. 

 

So it decides to do one more thing. It launches a new app store, and says what makes it better than everyone else is that it’s an Indian product. “We love India so much that we decided to do this out of the goodness of our heart,” says the company. 

 

“India deserves better. A billion people deserve better,” it continues. It’s also hoping that the regulator is listening. 

 

And when you nod in appreciation, it whispers, “By the way, as a fellow patriotic Indian like myself, would you like to buy some stocks on my platform?”

 

So, here’s what happened last week.
Walmart-owned payments company PhonePe on Wednesday formally launched its mobile app marketplace, Indus Appstore, in an attempt to rival the Google Play store and Apple’s App Store.
 
“Indus Appstore challenges the status quo, ushering in an era of more healthy competition in the mobile app marketplace, which in turn should help create a more democratic and vibrant Indian digital ecosystem,” PhonePe founder and chief executive Sameer Nigam said.
 
The launch happened in New Delhi in the presence of communications and IT minister Ashwini Vaishnaw, former Niti Aayog CEO Amitabh Kant and several startup founders.
 
The company had released the developer platform for the Android-based app marketplace in September 2023. At the time, PhonePe had said the platform would be free for developers for the first year, after which a nominal annual fee would apply.
 
“If you go just as recent as 15 years back, the world was looking at us as a consumer of technology…Now the world is looking at us as a co-creator, as a partner in development, as a leader in digital, as one of the largest ecosystems of every emerging technology, whether it is AI, whether it is content, whether it is communications, whether it is developing something new,” minister Vaishnaw said.

It really is the season of companies launching random products to advance a narrative. 

 

Last month, I wrote about this in the context of Ola launching Krutrim, which it claimed was an Indian AI company. My point back then was essentially that it’s irrelevant whether Krutrim is actually better than Open AI—all that matters is that it enables Ola to make all kinds of claims about how it's using Krutrim in its products to advance its narrative and valuations.

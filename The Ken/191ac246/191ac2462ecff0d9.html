Good Morning Ishan,

Sunday mornings are no different than the rest of the mornings in a week. It’s just that we are in a frame of mind to appreciate their calmness, beauty, and meaning.

“It may be that when we no longer know what to do we have come to our real work, and that when we no longer know which way to go we have begun our real journey. The mind that is not baffled is not employed. The impeded stream is the one that sings.”

Wendell Berry

I am not lying when I say that I’ve become a more conscious connoisseur of Sundays after I started writing First Principles.

And 249 of you responded to my request last week to help figure out which day and time of the week we largely read First Principles. I was holding my breath, because I was afraid of how I would need to change the way I pictured you.

Phew. We are definitely a Sunday morning habit.

View in Browser

I have some good news and some sad news. After a nerve-wrenching 8-day disappearance, I found the mama dog earlier this week (read our last edition if you aren’t caught up with the story yet). She had her litter in a small outdoor shed belonging to the caretakers of a nearby empty lot.  

I had gone to feed “Brindle Boy” (as described by reader Jude D’Souza), her son-from-the-last-litter. Unable to find him in his usual spot, I walked around the place whistling my characteristic whistle, the one that both mama and son recognise and respond to.

Suddenly, the flimsy shed made of corrugated metal sheets started shaking violently, as though someone was trying to push it down. I went and peered inside it, and there she was, thrilled to see me and wagging her tail furiously. She wolfed through the food in minutes, obviously hungry. Her few day-old pups were asleep in a faraway corner.

The next day, I saw her running around and playing with Brindle Boy too.

My thanks to the multiple readers who wrote in offering tips and contacts to get the duo sterilised and vaccinated. I’m going to ensure that this time.

The sad news is that Anushka Mukherjee, who originally started with us as a podcast producer before also becoming the books editor for FP Sundays, is leaving us. And Anushka uses a book that was one of our son’s (and our) favourite books as a child to say goodbye.

1. The joy of investing in yourself

In our 18 August edition (“The Sunday Dweller Community”), I asked you for your favourite “investments”.

We “invested” in an espresso machine within a few months of the pandemic of 2020. Hands-down, it was one of our best investments, reliably delivering impeccable coffee every morning (and through the day).

Honestly, I think the best test of what a great investment (not monetary) is, is how often you use it in a day or week, and how much joy it gives you.

What is it for you? I’d love to know.

One-hundred-and-eighty-seven of you responded with a list of some really interesting choices. For those of you who did not check the survey out, here’s what I asked:

Q1. What’s the best “investment” you’ve made in a product recently?

Q2. How often do you use it?

Q3. What joy does it bring you?

Adhithi Priya, my colleague who leads our wonderful in-house editorial design and illustrations team, helped me make sense of the responses.

2. From the First Principles Community

Longtime reader and part-time poet Sandeep Gautam returns.

A Market For Poems?

Everyone who is sensitive, or appreciates beauty,
Is a potential connoisseur, is bound by duty,
To read, reflect, and mull, let a chord strike his very being,
That the whole world can be a market- believing is seeing. 

Everyone who can read well, is proficient in English,
Is part of my target segment, the swamp in which I fish.
Sea may not be my oyster, I can always cry a river,
And pin by slings and arrows, shooting from my quiver.

Everyone who likes or loves me, our paths crossed perchance,
Whose mind share I have earned, who’s ready to finance.
Buys my books or coffee, is a patron in true sense,
Likes my poems on Facebook, be they obtuse or dense.

What should I optimize for, to grow TAM, SAM or SOM?
I write for you my dear, not to please harry, dick or tom.

3. Goodnight reader, goodnight books, goodnight moon

Hello everyone, welcome to this week’s First Principles books section. This is Anushka, as always. 

Before we get to the very interesting list of book recommendations from the community this week, I want to indulge myself for a minute and use this space to say goodbye. This is my last week at The Ken, and hence, my last time writing this books section.

But, I don’t want to write reams about what a great year it’s been for FP books (and well, it has been, with over a hundred submissions behind us since we began). Because the section will live on, continuing to seek and share recommendations from this vibrant community. As has always been my goal with this section, it will (hopefully) continue to encourage you to think about reading, writing, books, and literature a little differently, every Sunday. I will take the liberty, though, to leave you with a personal ‘goodbye’ recommendation. I really hope you check this one out. 

I’ve been scratching my head a little thinking about what a good “goodbye book” would be, because it’s truly such a broad (and difficult) term to contend with. What comes to your mind when you hear it? Leaving home? College farewells? The end of a vacation? I’ll tell you what came to my mind first when I carried out a word-association exercise: the word ‘goodnight’. And once I thought of that, I could only recommend what is perhaps one of my favourite “books” of all time—the children’s bedtime story Goodnight Moon by Margaret Wise Brown.

View in Browser

If you’ve never come across it before, Goodnight Moon is a short story of a little bunny who, as he’s preparing for bedtime, says goodnight to everything he sees in his room—a balloon, mittens, kittens, yarn, an old lady knitting on the rocking chair, and the moon, among other things.

View in Browser

At the first, second, and many following glances, this is a painfully simple, even mundane, book. Especially for a children’s story. You’d expect children’s books to be ripe with imagination, colour, magic. This has none of that. On every page, there’s only the rabbit saying good night to everyday objects. Even the illustrations are fairly drab. 

And yet, the internet is awash with the wisdom of Goodnight Moon. Parents love it for its ritualistic vibe—the repetitive sounds that soothe babies to sleep; the refrain of good night, good night, good night. Perhaps it is even habit-forming. In this wonderful review, a father zooms out and talks about how children’s books change parents who read them night after night. His focus is not on the bunny but on the “quiet old lady”, and he ponders on the state of the caregiver, who eventually has to leave the room. Where does she go? 

But me? I just relate to the bunny. 

A goodnight is not really an ending, just a small break from life. And yet, for children, it is a big deal. The bunny says goodbye to everything—even the air, even to “nobody”. 

I have the tendency to do the same whenever a goodbye is imminent. I say farewell to the place, the objects, the routine, even my commute—all ‘inanimate’ things, but ones that nevertheless lent a shape and structure to this part of my life. There is an enduring wisdom in the bunny’s nighttime routine. 

I’d recommend everyone—kids, adults, parents and non-parents—to read the book, or listen to this reading of it. Do let us know by writing to fp@the-ken.com if you end up reading Goodnight Moon! As always, if you have any recommendations for the community, please send them here.

4. Two by Two: Google Pay is a great product. But, is it also a great business? Does it need to be? 🎙️

In the latest episode of Two by Two, we discuss Google Pay’s precarious position, and how it stands out as an anomaly. Google Pay is India’s second-largest UPI payments app with a market share of 38%, with 500+ crore transactions a month. It’s one of the world’s mightiest companies, and yet, we argue that it’s possibly in a vulnerable, strange position. By this, we don’t necessarily mean that it will disappear overnight, but that all kinds of competitors are coming for it. Already, its market share has declined from 44% to 37%. It’s an outpost of an empire that’s fighting a global war. And most importantly, the first wave of UPI is over, and the second phase is starting. UPI itself is changing and going through some transitions, and there are signs that Google Pay won’t be able to keep up.

To discuss Google Pay’s position, my co-host on the podcast Praveen Krishnan and I invited two incredible guests, both sharp fintech operators, with a wealth of experience behind them building UPI products. 

First, there’s Abhishek Madan, Vice President of Product at Paytm. Abhishek is responsible for building and scaling Paytm’s UPI product, and understands a thing or two about how consumer apps work. Our other guest is someone who is building a company that’s actively trying to disrupt UPI. Vasisht S Ravichandran is COO of Pop, a new UPI app that is inverting the way we’re looking at UPI and commerce.

View in Browser

Our full conversation goes on for nearly 90 minutes, and we have a spirited discussion on stuff like: 

  • How consumers discover, use and stick to UPI apps 
  • Google Pay’s rise and dominance in India
  • The internal organisation of Google Pay that makes it hard to innovate
  • Google India’s push and pull with Google the parent company 
  • Why banks are the zamindars of Indian FinTech
  • The real innovation in UPI is happening outside the apps 
  • How commerce is going to disrupt UPI
  • Google Pay’s real power—trust

6. Sunday reads and links 🔖

“Think better”—by Mike Loukides

“AI’s ability to imitate it is technically interesting, but really doesn’t say anything about creativity. AI’s ability to create new kinds of artwork under the direction of a human artist is an interesting direction to explore, but let’s be clear: that’s human initiative and creativity.

Humans are much better than AI at understanding very large contexts—contexts that dwarf a million tokens, contexts that include information that we have no way to describe digitally. Humans are better than AI at creating new directions, synthesizing new kinds of information, and building something new. More than anything else, Ezra Pound’s dictum “Make it New” is the theme of 20th and 21st century culture. It’s one thing to ask AI for startup ideas, but I don’t think AI would have ever created the Web or, for that matter, social media (which really began with USENET newsgroups). AI would have trouble creating anything new because AI can’t want anything—new or old. To borrow Henry Ford’s alleged words, it would be great at designing faster horses, if asked. Perhaps a bioengineer could ask an AI to decode horse DNA and come up with some improvements. But I don’t think an AI could ever design an automobile without having seen one first—or without having a human say “Put a steam engine on a tricycle.””

In our 18 August edition (“[The Sunday Dweller Community](https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/the-sunday-dweller-community/)”), I asked you for your favourite “investments”.
We “invested” in an espresso machine within a few months of the pandemic of 2020. Hands-down, it was one of our best investments, reliably delivering impeccable coffee every morning (and through the day).
Honestly, I think the best test of what a great investment (not monetary) is, is how often you use it in a day or week, and how much joy it gives you.
What is it for you? I’d love to know.

Before we get to the very interesting list of book recommendations from the community this week, I want to indulge myself for a minute and use this space to say goodbye. This is my last week at The Ken, and hence, my last time writing this books section.
But, I don’t want to write reams about what a great year it’s been for FP books (and well, it has been, with over a hundred submissions behind us since we began). Because the section will live on, continuing to seek and share recommendations from this vibrant community. As has always been my goal with this section, it will (hopefully) continue to encourage you to think about reading, writing, books, and literature a little differently, every Sunday. I will take the liberty, though, to leave you with a personal ‘goodbye’ recommendation. I really hope you check this one out.
I’ve been scratching my head a little thinking about what a good “goodbye book” would be, because it’s truly such a broad (and difficult) term to contend with. What comes to your mind when you hear it? Leaving home? College farewells? The end of a vacation? I’ll tell you what came to my mind first when I carried out a word-association exercise: the word ‘goodnight’. And once I thought of that, I could only recommend what is perhaps one of my favourite “books” of all time—the children’s bedtime story [Goodnight Moon](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/32929) by Margaret Wise Brown.
If you’ve never come across it before, Goodnight Moon is a short story of a little bunny who, as he’s preparing for bedtime, says goodnight to everything he sees in his room—a balloon, mittens, kittens, yarn, an old lady knitting on the rocking chair, and the moon, among other things.
At the first, second, and many following glances, this is a painfully simple, even mundane, book. Especially for a children’s story. You’d expect children’s books to be ripe with imagination, colour, magic. This has none of that. On every page, there’s only the rabbit saying good night to everyday objects. Even the illustrations are fairly drab.
And yet, the internet is awash with the wisdom of Goodnight Moon. Parents love it for its ritualistic vibe—the repetitive sounds that soothe babies to sleep; the refrain of good night, good night, good night. Perhaps it is even habit-forming. In this [wonderful review](https://lareviewofbooks.org/article/goodnight-moon-almost-75-years-later/), a father zooms out and talks about how children’s books change parents who read them night after night. His focus is not on the bunny but on the “quiet old lady”, and he ponders on the state of the caregiver, who eventually has to leave the room. Where does she go?
But me? I just relate to the bunny.
A goodnight is not really an ending, just a small break from life. And yet, for children, it is a big deal. The bunny says goodbye to everything—even the air, even to “nobody”.
I have the tendency to do the same whenever a goodbye is imminent. I say farewell to the place, the objects, the routine, even my commute—all ‘inanimate’ things, but ones that nevertheless lent a shape and structure to this part of my life. There is an enduring wisdom in the bunny’s nighttime routine.
I’d recommend everyone—kids, adults, parents and non-parents—to read the book, or listen to this [reading](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=1ZTnwKLKVhc) of it. In the latest episode of Two by Two, we discuss Google Pay’s precarious position, and how it stands out as an anomaly. Google Pay is India’s second-largest UPI payments app with a market share of 38%, with 500+ crore transactions a month. It’s one of the world’s mightiest companies, and yet, we argue that it’s possibly in a vulnerable, strange position. By this, we don’t necessarily mean that it will disappear overnight, but that all kinds of competitors are coming for it. Already, its market share has declined from 44% to 37%. It’s an outpost of an empire that’s fighting a global war. And most importantly, the first wave of UPI is over, and the second phase is starting. UPI itself is changing and going through some transitions, and there are signs that Google Pay won’t be able to keep up.
To discuss Google Pay’s position, my co-host on the podcast Praveen Krishnan and I invited two incredible guests, both sharp fintech operators, with a wealth of experience behind them building UPI products.
First, there’s Abhishek Madan, Vice President of Product at Paytm. Abhishek is responsible for building and scaling Paytm’s UPI product, and understands a thing or two about how consumer apps work. Our other guest is someone who is building a company that’s actively trying to disrupt UPI. Vasisht S Ravichandran is COO of Pop, a new UPI app that is inverting the way we’re looking at UPI and commerce.
Our full conversation goes on for nearly 90 minutes, and we have a spirited discussion on stuff like:
- How consumers discover, use and stick to UPI apps
- Google Pay’s rise and dominance in India
- The internal organisation of Google Pay that makes it hard to innovate
- Google India’s push and pull with Google the parent company
- Why banks are the zamindars of Indian FinTech
- The real innovation in UPI is happening outside the apps
- How commerce is going to disrupt UPI
- Google Pay’s real power—trust

[“Think better”](https://www.oreilly.com/radar/think-better/)—by Mike Loukides
“AI’s ability to imitate it is technically interesting, but really doesn’t say anything about creativity. AI’s ability to create new kinds of artwork under the direction of a human artist is an interesting direction to explore, but let’s be clear: that’s human initiative and creativity.
Humans are much better than AI at understanding very large contexts—contexts that dwarf a million tokens, contexts that include information that we have no way to describe digitally. Humans are better than AI at creating new directions, synthesizing new kinds of information, and building something new. More than anything else, Ezra Pound’s dictum “Make it New” is the theme of 20th and 21st century culture. It’s one thing to ask AI for startup ideas, but I don’t think AI would have ever created the Web or, for that matter, social media (which really began with USENET newsgroups). AI would have trouble creating anything new because AI can’t want anything—new or old. To borrow Henry Ford’s alleged words, it would be great at designing faster horses, if asked. Perhaps a bioengineer could ask an AI to decode horse DNA and come up with some improvements. But I don’t think an AI could ever design an automobile without having seen one first—or without having a human say “[Put a steam engine on a tricycle](https://www.history.com/news/who-built-the-first-automobile).””

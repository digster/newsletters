Good Morning Ishan,
Unstoppable force is set to meet immovable object. India’s equity derivatives—specifically futures and options (F&O)—market faces an imminent squeeze by market regulator Sebi. Sparks will fly, and many will be singed.
Just a few days ago, Sebi chairperson Madhabi Puri Buch [said](https://www.moneycontrol.com/news/business/kal-ho-na-ho-sebi-chairperson-open-to-taking-some-derivative-products-off-the-market-says-market-understands-regulatory-risk-12757882.html) she was open to taking some derivative products off the market. “Not at all,” Buch asserted when asked if any move that caused a contraction in trading turnover would be considered regressive.
That the regulator has soured against equity F&Os is no secret. It’s been regularly voicing concerns about the exponential rise in options trading volumes, much of which is speculative activity driven by retail traders whose numbers have grown by leaps and bounds. This unbridled casino has set off alarm bells.

The ball is already rolling on slowing down the casino. A couple of days ago, an expert committee appointed by Sebi [reportedly](https://www.moneycontrol.com/news/business/markets/mc-exclusive-fo-crackdown-only-one-weekly-options-contract-per-exchange-min-lot-size-rs-20-30-lakh-derivatives-panel-suggests-12765081.html) suggested a host of measures to rein in speculative trading in derivatives. Moneycontrol lists them out:
– Increase in derivative contracts lot size to Rs 20-30 lakh from Rs 5 lakh now.
– Only one weekly options contract per stock exchange
– Limiting number of strike prices for options contracts
We could also have upfront collection of option premiums from option buyers, intra-day monitoring of position limits, and an increase in margin requirements.
Without getting too deep into the technical details, it’s enough to say that when implemented, these measures could see derivative volumes, especially in options trading, taking a hit.
Sure, these recommendations need to pass through a couple more hoops—another committee and some public consultations—before being taken up for approval by the Sebi board. So there could be a few slips between the cup and the lip. But the writing on the wall is clear: Sebi’s squeeze is coming.
It’s a question of when, not if. And it won’t be too long, going by the regulator’s mood.
There has been talk of Sebi introducing eligibility criteria for individual traders to play in the F&O market. That will be a dampener on volumes too.
And the Union Budget—which is due in a couple of weeks—could also tighten the screws on F&O trades. As of now, income from these transactions is considered ‘non-speculative’. The Budget [could change](https://www.business-standard.com/markets/news/capital-gains-tax-rejig-on-share-sale-in-modi-3-0-budget-can-spook-markets-124070900236_1.html) the rules and classify it as speculative income, which is taxed at much higher rates. Ouch.
Retail investor protection is what the regulator and the government seem to be shooting for. And with good reason. The regulator has already come across cases where folks have borrowed to trade F&Os and ended up losing hearth and home. Nine of 10 individual traders in equity F&O incurred losses, with an average loss of Rs 1.1 lakh (US$1,320) in the year ended March 2022, a study by Sebi shows.
But some of those whom Sebi seeks to protect are not amused. The memes have been coming in thick and fast.

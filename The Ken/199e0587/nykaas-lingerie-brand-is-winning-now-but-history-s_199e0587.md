Good Morning Ishan,
Priyanka Sharma remembers the exact moment she stopped shopping for lingerie at her neighbourhood store. It was in 2017, when she walked into the same shop in Delhi’s Lajpat Nagar that her mother had frequented for years. There, she encountered a longstanding problem firsthand—what Preeti Gupta, Nykaa’s chief business officer, calls “the harsh reality” of lingerie shopping in India.
Uncle ji—as everyone called him—just pulled items from dusty drawers, guessed your size with a glance, and packed them in black plastic bags, all before you could even look properly. And what Uncle ji picked was what you got. That day, she walked out with two ill-fitting bras she never wore, vowing never to return.
Now, in 2025, she shops differently. She browses, asks questions, and tries on things in well-lit trial rooms with female staff who understand sizing. She takes her time. And remarkably, she pays full price without flinching. “I never thought I’d spend twenty minutes in a lingerie store,” she tells me. “But now I do, and it doesn’t feel weird at all.”
Zivame, launched in 2011, [initiated](https://www.financialexpress.com/life/lifestyle-meet-richa-kar-founder-of-indias-largest-online-lingerie-shop-zivame-acquired-by-mukesh-ambani-who-amassed-rs-749-crores-3172528/) this shift, with a promise to transform lingerie retail in India. It distinguished itself by positioning lingerie as a woman-centric category—designed by women, for women—emphasising personal comfort and confidence over external validation. This perspective resonated strongly with its audience; Zivame eventually became the country’s most popular lingerie brand.
However, it has struggled since then. In FY24, it reported a [revenue](https://in.fashionnetwork.com/news/Zivame-sales-dropped-and-losses-increased-in-fy24,1678019.html#:~:text=By-,Published,space%20including%20Amante%20and%20Clovia.) of just Rs 193 crore—a 42% decline from the previous year’s Rs 330 crore, as per data from market-intelligence platform Tracxn. The brand has pivoted often, transforming from a marketplace to a private-label brand to a hybrid model through which it now sells two brands: Zivame and Rosaline.
After Zivame came Clovia in 2015, launching as its own private-label brand. But despite rapid revenue growth, it has struggled with profitability. The brand, in which Reliance Retail acquired an 89% stake in 2022, posted net losses of Rs 80.93 crore in FY23 and Rs 28.53 crore in FY24, as per the latest data. And its revenue fell from Rs 295.51 crore in FY23 to Rs 262.93 crore in FY24.
Reliance Retail acquired Zivame too, in 2020, giving the retail giant a presence across the price spectrum, from the mass-market Hushh to the premium Marks & Spencer.
Yet even Reliance hasn’t been able to stem Zivame’s decline.
It is into this field of struggling rivals that a new star has stepped in.
Nykaa’s in-house brand Nykd—launched in 2020, a full nine years after Zivame—has quietly become the highest-grossing label among Nykaa’s 16 fashion brands, crossing Rs 170 crore in GMV as of FY25 and Rs 100 crore in revenue as of FY24. According to the company’s investor presentation this year, it is the third-largest selling lingerie brand on Amazon and the largest on Nykaa’s own platform.
Nykd is clearly flying high.
But that also means it’s getting closer to a ceiling its older rivals have struggled to break.
A key advantage for Nykd is Nykaa’s existing infrastructure. “Nykaa already has the customer data,” notes Satish Meena, an e-commerce analyst and vice president at Datum Intelligence, a data-consultancy firm.
As of early 2024, Nykd operated 26 exclusive brand outlets and was present in over 1,400 general trade stores across 22 states.
And it maintains 80% full-price sales in a category notorious for discounting. While Zivame’s basic t-shirt bra starts at Rs 299 only after discounts, Nykd’s slip-on bra is priced at Rs 399–and women are paying full price.
“They work with contract manufacturers, but have better control over the supply chain, as (Nykaa) has been working with them for all their other 15 private labels,” says Meena.
With 33% repeat customers—an enviable figure for a young brand in this category—Nykd has also cracked the code on customer retention, he adds.
But the economics grow brutal as brands attempt to scale downmarket. Strategies that succeed in Delhi and Mumbai—full-price sales, premium positioning, and digitally-driven customer acquisition—falter in tier-2 and tier-3 towns, where consumers expect sub-Rs 300 price points and resist online-only discovery. “The moment you move to smaller markets, your entire cost structure changes,” explains a retail analyst, “you’re competing with unbranded alternatives at half your price, and your distribution costs spike because volumes per store are much lower.”
Even today, 70% of India’s lingerie market remains unorganised, dominated by neighbourhood stores selling unbranded products. Zivame learned this the hard way; its aggressive expansion into 200 multi-brand outlets and 20 exclusive stores incurred heavy operational costs, while price-conscious customers still gravitated toward cheaper alternatives.
Producing a single bra for such brands involves 12 components, manufactured at a base cost of Rs 150–200, according to a Delhi-NCR-based women’s wear manufacturer. The fabric—a blend of polyester or nylon and costly spandex yarn—is the most expensive element, requiring specialised machines and skilled operators. The final price to the customer comes after a standard retail markup of 2X to 5X on the cumulative cost of machinery, labour, and materials.
Then, there’s sizing. While a basic clothing line offers sizes from XS through XL, a core bra line sells 32–36 band sizes across A–D cups—that’s 12 core sizes per design. Each requires different construction, driving up both design and production costs for inventory that may not sell, squeezing margins from the outset.
Despite this complexity, India’s women’s innerwear market is the fastest-growing segment in women’s apparel, outpacing even the massive ethnic wear category, as per a clothing manufacturer from Delhi-NCR. The premium lingerie segment alone hit $1.73 billion in 2024, per data from IMARC Group.
But expanding beyond the mostly urban, higher-income markets still remains a challenge no brand has cracked.
According to Meena, there’s a pattern: “All brands struggled after reaching the Rs 100 crore revenue mark.” Zivame hit this milestone in 2019, only to see revenues fall. Clovia faced similar headwinds after crossing the Rs 100 crore mark in FY20.
Data and distribution got Nykd to Rs 100 crore faster than anyone expected. But it remains to be seen whether it can solve the profitability puzzle that has trapped its predecessors. To break the curse, it must find a way to serve a vast, price-conscious market without sacrificing its margins entirely.
In India’s intimate apparel revolution, being the current winner and the eventual champion may be very different things.

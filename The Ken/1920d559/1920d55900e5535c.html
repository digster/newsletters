To call what’s happening in India’s stock market “bonkers” is putting it rather mildly.

It appears that any company, regardless of what it does or how much money it makes (if at all), can go public to a rapturous response. And the market cap of many that are already listed is reaching new heights, even if otherwise-articulate investment pundits don’t have the faintest idea why.

The only reasonable explanation: there’s so much money to go around. Just ask India’s 43 mutual-fund firms. Their equity assets under management (AUM) grew about 50% to nearly Rs 36 lakh crore (US$430 billion) in a year to August 2024.

Only 5% of that is uninvested in stocks. But one fund house is happy not to join its rivals in this wild party. The flagship scheme of PPFAS Mutual Fund is sitting on Rs 14,000 crore of cash—almost a fifth of its AUM.

As Anand writes in today’s story, the fund house’s strategy of “high on cash, low on stocks” has lately tarnished its stellar track record.

“We are fine with short-term underperformance and pain in exchange for long-term outperformance and gain,” Neil Parikh, the fund house’s chief executive, told The Ken.

Would investors agree, though?

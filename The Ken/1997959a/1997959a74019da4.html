Chennai-based Five Star Finance has ended up creating too much of a good thing, and it’s now feeling the heat from it. The lender’s stock hit a 52-week low yesterday. 

Forty-one-year-old Five Star is the largest lender of its kind. It found a niche in giving Rs 3–5 lakh micro loans to “new-to-credit” and self-employed people, such as vegetable sellers or barber-shop owners. And it built an operation with Rs 12,500 crore in assets under management (AUM) as of Q1 FY26 by charging these borrowers interest rates upwards of 20%, even though these loans are backed by property as collateral.

Before going public, Five Star also raised about $240 million in funding from the likes of TPG Partners, Z47, Norwest Venture Partners, and Peak XV. But once it listed in 2022, its expensive valuations became a bit apparent. The non-bank reached its peak valuation in 2024, when its price-to-book multiple was 5.5X. 

This was thanks to its industry-beating return on assets (7%) and return on equity (20%), said an analyst who tracks the company, at a time when other NBFCs were at 13–15% ROE.

But over the last few years, Five Star’s niche segment has drawn in more competition from both old and new sets of players.

L&T Finance entered this market in 2023. So has Bandhan Bank, looking to expand its micro LAP (Loans Against Property) portfolio. The likes of Vastu Housing Finance, Shubham Housing Finance, and Finnova Capital are all widening their presence in the space. 

This influx means more growth capital. For instance, Warburg-backed Vistaar has raised Rs 1,360 crore from private-equity firms like Motilal Oswal Alternates. Peak XV and Norwest-backed Finova Capital raised $135 million in 2024 from Avataar Venture Partners, Sofina, and Madison India Capital.

All this competition has resulted in the micro LAP segment growing by 60% to Rs 1.6 lakh crore over the two years ending September 2024, according to a Careedge report.

Lending to this profile requires a special kind of skillset, something that the likes of Five Star have sharpened over the years. The company does not use intermediaries to source loans, but instead, has its own salespeople who double up as collection officers.

However, as lenders flush with capital and greater growth ambitions have waded into the company’s niche, they’ve begun poaching the trained talent from Five Star and other housing finance lenders, leading to high attrition over the last three years.

As Ambit Capital’s research analyst Raghav Garg wrote in a September 16 note, attrition is a key challenge for micro LAP non banks due to a lack of seasoned, long-vintage sales staff in the industry. That’s unlike, say, the prime loans (aka lending to the creditworthy) segment, where lenders can underwrite loans easily, and you don’t have any complicated assessment. 

“In the case of micro LAP customer profiles,” says Garg, “the frontline staff have a lot of credit assessment to do as the borrowers’ incomes are not documented, there are challenges to income stability, and you need a different skillset to evaluate income.”

Non-banks like Five Star, and even other housing finance companies like Aavas, have an already trained pool of talent that other NBFCs with growth capital are now eyeing. And Five Star’s nearly 12,000 employees are now fair game for everyone.

This kind of attrition can have a direct impact on AUM growth. 

“Each time an NBFC brings in a new person, it ends up impacting productivity. 

The thumb rule is, when a fresh loan officer joins an affordable-housing loan NBFC, it takes three to four months to source their first file and have it approved and disbursed.”

Raghav Garg, Ambit Capital, research analyst

Roughly, a 25% attrition rate means the effective capacity of the sales workforce is only 75%, which ends up hurting the NBFC’s ability to absorb operating expenses and subsequently impacts the bottom line. 

The increased competition has also resulted in another complication. 

With more players entering the space, borrowers have more choice and are taking on more loans. And the lending is becoming riskier.

According to Ambit Capital, with small-ticket property loans, non-banks are differentiating themselves by focusing on customers who lack income documentation and properties that fall outside the purview of the municipal corporation. “A few companies are taking higher-risk calls on either customer profile, property type, or both,” it notes. 

That means non-banks also have to contend with a bad loan problem. 

Five Star has had a track record of low non-performing assets (NPA) considering the type of loans it extends. Micro LAPs have a higher proportion of new-to-credit customers, and the company relies on credit evaluations based on assessed income and borrowers’ ability to manage cash flow mismatches. And since 2017, Five Star has maintained a streak of around 1% gross NPAs (loans due for over 90 days). 

But in Q1 of FY26, that streak ended. Its bad loans shot up to 2.5% from 1.8% in the previous quarter. 

A lot of this is because the sector is facing pain, with its segment of borrowers becoming overleveraged. 

Five Star’s chairman and managing director D Lakshmipathy said in a conference call in August that over the last 18 to 24 months, the company has seen a high spurt in credit moving towards this segment in the form of microfinance, personal loans, and credit cards. “The problem has cropped up with customers overleveraging themselves after taking our loans,” he said. Five Star is increasing the ticket size of its loans and has sharply cut down on loan disbursals (see the graph above) to tide over this phase. 

Still, the company has not cut its 25% AUM growth guidance for the 2026 financial year. (Its AUM grew 23% between FY24 and FY25.)

For a lender that was once the poster child of secured micro-loans, Five Star’s valuation has eroded by 40–45% in the last 12 months, with its share price down by 33% in the same period.

And while it has managed to stem its attrition a bit and hold steady for the last three to four quarters, it’s still not where it was pre-covid. 

Which is a challenge, because for Five Star to get to double-digit AUM growth from where it currently stands, it needs its people more than ever.

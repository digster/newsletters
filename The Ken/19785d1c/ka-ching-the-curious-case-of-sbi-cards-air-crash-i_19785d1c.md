Ka-Ching!
Thu, 19 Jun 25
A weekly newsletter about how finance is getting supercharged by tech in India, and how you can make money work for you..
Good Morning Ishan,
This week, headlines across major business dailies like The Economic Times and Business Standard featured a piece of news that no one would have batted an eyelid at, were it not for one of the country’s worst air crashes in history on 12 June.
Four days after the disaster, The Economic Times [ran this header](https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/wealth/spend/major-sbi-credit-card-update-no-complimentary-air-accident-insurance-for-these-cardholders-from-july-15-2025/articleshow/121848382.cms?from=mdr): “Major SBI credit card update: No complimentary air accident insurance for these cardholders from July 15, 2025”
SBI Card will discontinue its complimentary air accident insurance coverage of Rs 1 crore, which is currently offered on select premium SBI credit cards, effective July 15, 2025. This change applies to the following cards: SBI Card Elite, SBI Card Miles Elite, and SBI Card Miles Prime. Customers using these variants of credit cards will no longer be eligible for this benefit after the specified date.
SBI Card, India’s second-largest credit-card issuer, offers an air accident insurance cover of up to Rs 50 lakh on many of its credit cards, and up to Rs 1 crore on premium credit cards. Effectively, this means that the kin of those who died tragically in the 12 June crash will be able to claim an insurance cover of between Rs 50 lakh and Rs 1 crore if the victim had booked their flight using an SBI Card credit card.
But the card company will no longer offer this to future passengers, rolling the feature back alongside a [laundry list](https://www.sbicard.com/en/customer-notices.page) of other changes to its benefits program.
Immediately, social-media warriors took to their keyboards, calling it a cold, heartless, calculative move from SBI Card. The Ken has learnt that even the State Bank of India’s top management—the bank has a stake in the card company—enquired about the timing of the move.
But most reports on the development missed a small yet important detail.
SBI Card had rolled out these changes a month ago, well before the tragedy on 12 June. The notice to cardholders intimating them of this rollback can be traced back to 31 May with the help of the Wayback Machine, a digital archive of the World Wide Web. SBI Card, unfortunately, did not carry this date on its notice, a step that could have easily avoided this bad PR.
Credit-card companies constantly evaluate which new benefits to offer their customers and which ones to take away. And before the crash, hardly anyone would have noticed the insurance cover among more sought-after and glitzy perks like free lounge access and golf lessons.
In fact, a former SBI Card executive I spoke to said that although the air-accident cover has been available for many years now, there have barely been any claims made in that time. Air-accident insurance, they said, is a relic of the past, when general insurance coverage was low and insurance of all types were offered as perks. Now, it’s likely that most cardholders have some kind of insurance, making such benefits go largely unclaimed.
Credit-card issuers spend about Rs 15–20 per card on premiums for such covers, according to the executive. Given SBI Card has about 20 million credit cards, and assuming around 60% of them had this benefit, that’s a total premium of Rs 18–24 crore every year.
And every penny saved counts for the card company, which is already having a tough time with profits dipping 20% year-on-year in FY25.
In fact, SBI Card’s move will likely turn out more favourable than it intended, because overall premiums are bound to rise sharply after the $475 million in claims that insurers may have to pay victims of the crash, making it India’s [costliest](https://www.newindianexpress.com/business/2025/Jun/17/ai-171-crash-475-million-insurance-claim-ranks-among-indias-costliest) insurance settlement.
Usually, when one credit-card issuer rolls back a feature or adds a fee, the rest of the industry follows. Others such as HDFC Bank, ICICI Bank, and Axis Bank also offer air-accident insurance as a benefit on their credit cards, though given the PR fire SBI Card is having to put out, it’s likely they will not take any action—at least in the short term.

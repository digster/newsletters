Except, it might be the one paying! And the ones hitting a $1 billion payday might be Devas Multimedia, Deutsche Telekom, Columbia Capital and Telecom Ventures. For they took the Indian government to international arbitration court for reneging on a 2005 decision to lease two transponders on ISRO’s satellites to Devas so that the latter could launch hybrid satellite-terrestrial broadband services using spectrum in the 2.5 GHz or S-band.
 
In 2015, the Indian government lost the first of these cases for $562.5 million. With further penalties and interest that fine now stands at close to $1 billion. India has also lost two other cases slapped on it by the Deutsche Telekom, Columbia Capital and Telecom Ventures—the three investors in Devas. All put together, Indian taxpayers might end up footing a bill of nearly $1.5 billion.
 
Which might or might not explain the birth of Newspace India Limited, a new company created by India’s Department of Space on 6 March. Newspace was born after a cabinet approval in February which empowered it “to commercially exploit the research and development work carried out by Indian Space Research Organization (ISRO) Centers and constituent units of DOS.” 
 
But the catch is that India already had a government-owned space company dedicated to doing just that—Antrix Corp. For 26 years, Antrix Corp has been the sole commercial arm of ISRO.

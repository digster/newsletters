In 2012, when the world was a simpler place and when Indian e-commerce companies were from India, a new competitor arrived from overseas. Not Amazon from the US. Not Shein or Club Factory from China.
Instead, it was a German firm called Rocket Internet—which set up a fashion e-commerce company called Jabong.
Rocket Internet’s playbook was straightforward. See what works in the West or China. Duplicate in fledgling markets in Asia. Headquartered in Germany, it went from country to country, setting up one e-commerce website after another—most of which looked like carbon copies of each other. Lazada in Southeast Asia. Dafiti in South America. And Jabong in India.
At that time, the market leader in e-commerce fashion was Myntra, where I worked as a product manager. We were paranoid, but the conventional wisdom was that Jabong could never threaten Myntra. Copy-pasting products across continents? A centralised global product roadmap in Germany? It would never work.
We were all wrong. At first. Jabong put up a hell of a fight, and came within touching distance of overthrowing Myntra as the market leader. After a bruising battle, Myntra would go on to acquire Jabong for a paltry $70 million in 2016, and after some half-hearted efforts, would proceed to wipe it off the face of the earth. If you visit Jabong’s website today, you automatically get redirected to Myntra.
There are many theories why Jabong didn’t work in India. For several of my colleagues who were in the trenches at Myntra at the height of the fashion e-commerce war, this was seen as a repudiation of the copy-and-clone model. You can never win by copying products. Not in India.
Perhaps this was true back then. I’m not sure if this is true anymore, because it feels like something has changed. And not just in India.
That’s because over the last couple of weeks, here’s what has happened:
a. Reliance Jio launched a beta version of a product called JioMeet, which is a screen-by-screen copy of Zoom, the videoconferencing product from the US, and the de-facto market leader.
b. Instagram launched a product called ‘Reels’ in India—a clone of TikTok, which finds itself banned in India.
Strangely, it’s not just tech. This has also spilt over to adjacent sectors, notably FMCGs...and to a product category that has been under some scrutiny of late—fairness creams.
All of which leads me to ask—what has changed?
I’m not sure.
But I have a few guesses.
Let’s dive in.

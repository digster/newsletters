Make India Competitive Again
Mon, 18 Aug 25
What India is doing, will do, and should do—to not just survive but thrive in the chaos unleashed by Trump.
Good Morning Ishan,
Public investors have done a 180 on telecom-gear maker Tejas Networks.
Just 10 months ago, the share price of the Tata-owned company was at a crazy high, resulting in a market cap of Rs 22,000 crore ($2.5 billion). Today, that has more than halved to Rs 9,800 crore, just a little north of the company’s consolidated net revenue of roughly Rs 8,900 crore for FY25, a year during which it also recorded a remarkable year-on-year revenue growth of 260%.
It can’t be that the markets have punished it for reporting a net loss of Rs 194 crore in the June quarter. The stock price has been steadily declining since January. As of the end of June, foreign institutional investors have pared their shareholding to 6.14%, down from 10.20% a year ago.
Evidently, investors have booked profits. When we [wrote](https://the-ken.com/story/the-tata-wind-beneath-tejas-networks-wings-carries-investors-away/) about Tejas in late 2022, the stock had rallied and the valuation was off the charts, with a price-to-earnings multiple of more than 2,000X. One analyst had then called it a “hope” rally.
There’s nothing grave in the financial numbers or company projections to suggest that the hope has turned into hopelessness. Perhaps it’s just investors, to use the cliché, “buying the rumour and selling the news”. But India’s only sizeable intellectual property (IP)-driven telecoms gear company getting battered by public investors—private investors are not interested in telecoms companies in any case—must be a canary in the coal mine.
Tejas’ plight is especially worth studying when the Department of Telecommunications (DoT) has floated a draft for a new [National Telecom Policy 2025](https://dot.gov.in/sites/default/files/NTP_2025_0.pdf?download=1), taking its [2018 vision](https://dot.gov.in/sites/default/files/National%20Digital%20Communications%20Policy-2018.pdf?download=1) forward.
First, without evaluating the earlier policy and sharing it publicly, any new draft policy is a futile bureaucratic exercise.
But then, the three-week public feedback window also closed last week.
Is the bureaucracy listening to the canary?

In its latest quarterly results, Tejas [reported](https://www.tejasnetworks.com/press-and-news/tejas-announces-q1fy26-results/) that the Rs 194 crore net loss it recorded was “largely due to lower revenue” of Rs 202 crore, compared to the Rs 1,563 crore it had clocked during the same period last year. But the company closed the quarter with an order book of over Rs 1,200 crore, a healthy 22% quarter-on-quarter growth. It’s also expecting a new BSNL order of over Rs 1,500 crore for 4G expansion.
However, it’s the receivables that explain the investor flight—at least part of it.
As I have [written](https://the-ken.com/long_and_short/tatas-tejas-networks-soars-on-indias-costly-efforts-to-keep-bsnl-afloat/) earlier, working with the government can be a double-edged sword for Tejas.
Its trade receivables are pretty high—Rs 4,453 crore in June. And though the company has received some advances against them, effectively lowering overall receivables, it doesn’t really help its working capital situation. The company’s borrowings are at nearly Rs 4,000 crore, and delays in government orders or payments can leave it scrambling for capital to fulfill private orders or capex requirements.
Capital-market investors who came in when Tejas got acquired by the Tata Group, and was on the cusp of bagging big government orders, have now booked profit and left.
In this context, what the new telecom policy intends to do with telecom product and services companies is worth pondering.
To kick things off, telecom bureaucrats seem to be on double espresso.
Without specifying the start and end dates or figures, the draft NTP 2025 wants to double a bunch of things—the telecom sector’s contribution to India’s GDP, export of telecom products and services, the number of telecom startups, public and private spending on R&D.
That’s a lot, if the stop date isn’t somewhere beyond this decade.
Separately, the policy document, which unarguably is the north star for any administration, wants to handhold 500 startups and MSMEs specialising in emerging technologies in telecom.
But as I [wrote](https://the-ken.com/story/not-airtel-not-jio-bsnl-is-the-front-runner-in-private-5g/) in November, soon after the mega India Mobile Congress, small telecom companies don’t really have a red carpet rolled out for them right now:
The trickle-down effect is that smaller private 5G use cases are stalling. Unlike in other countries, India’s Department of Telecom (DoT) only hands out 5G spectrum to enterprises, system integrators, or service providers for proof-of-concept (PoC) trials. For commercial rollouts, companies are forced to go through the telcos.
That has created a structural divide—either there’s a large market for Jio or Airtel or there’s nothing.
“The reality today is that smaller use cases are not taking off in private 5G primarily because there is no spectrum for people who are interested; and those who have spectrum, are not interested because the opportunity is too small,” said Ramu Srinivasaiah, founder-CEO of Lekha Wireless. After 14 years of building out its wireless-communication portfolio, this bootstrapped, profitable company saw an over 50% revenue spike to Rs 72 crore (US$8.5 million) in FY2024.
Airtel and Jio won’t touch this 5G goldmine. So why is BSNL stepping in?, [The Ken](https://the-ken.com/story/not-airtel-not-jio-bsnl-is-the-front-runner-in-private-5g/)
If you are wondering whether things have moved fast since then, you are missing a beat or two on how Indian bureaucracy works.
A month-long [demand survey](https://www.pib.gov.in/PressReleseDetailm.aspx?PRID=2140830) to identify frequency bands for assigning spectrum to captive non-public networks or private networks ended on 31 July. It took the DoT three years from the time guidelines for private networks were made known, in June 2022, to even start gauging the demand. It’s anybody’s guess how many more months it will take to finally assign spectrum.
“It is a painful process for industry to spare time for these [survey] things. It is hard to get the information percolated [down to execution]. Ideally, they should have taken an intelligence report or global trends and decided,” said the chief executive of a telecom products company.
If assigning spectrum, no doubt a finite and scarce resource, for private networks is proving to be such an industrious exercise, why isn’t the DoT fulfilling its NTP 2018 promise of creating a spectrum portal? A public and accessible repository of all spectrum allocation and use in the country, one that would show who is using what spectrum and flag mismanagement or lack of use.
For instance, BSNL is deploying 4G in 5G bands. That’s utterly wasteful. The country’s largest telecom operator, Reliance Jio, is [deploying fixed wireless](https://telecom.economictimes.indiatimes.com/news/industry/reliance-jio-turns-to-ubr-to-lower-5g-fwa-deployment-costs-deploys-private-network-in-jamnagar/120820104#:~:text=NEW%20DELHI:%20Reliance%20Jio%20has,most%20recent%20post%2Dearnings%20call.) in unlicensed spectrum because using 5G spectrum is expensive. One telco is state-owned; another is a private behemoth hoping to list at a $100 billion valuation. Both examples epitomise suboptimal use of India’s radio frequency spectrum.
Instead of fixing the spectrum mess and making it affordable, the new policy document goes mum on these specifics. BSNL has deployed private proof-of-concept 5G networks in a few places in north India which are working, but no participating company has received any commercial contract yet.
As it is, investors, private or public, stay away from IP-seeking and research-driven telecom companies. If the DoT doesn’t help them develop products and services, and deploy them in a remunerative manner, then policy documents are a vanity exercise at best, and a distraction at worst.

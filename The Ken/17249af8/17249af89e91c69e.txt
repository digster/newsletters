Good Morning Ishan,

Three months ago, testing threw up medical dilemmas for health professionals. Today, it’s drugs.

The only partially effective drug available, Gilead’s remdesivir, is now being shipped to some countries. Healthcare providers are back to battling the treatment dilemma: first come, first served? Anonymise applications, reduce bias? Or just take the (non-random) lottery route?

In India, for now, it’s a different dilemma altogether. Which drug to prioritise for development, how to align pharma companies, what to promise in procurement so they buy into the gamble this Covid-19 treatment has turned out to be, and so on. It’s terrifyingly unprecedented. 

Some 254 therapies are being explored globally to treat Covid-19, making any investment at this point a stab in the dark. There are also around 100 projects to develop a vaccine in the works, which would drastically shrink the market for remdesivir or any other drug that comes soon after.

Earlier this month, the Modi government called the pharma companies and made clear its intent: 1) The drug should be available soon; 2) It must cater to mild and moderate cases, which make up the majority of Covid-19 infections. This is unlike remdesivir, which is recommended for moderate to severe cases; 3) The drug should be administered at home. Again, unlike remdesivir, which is an injectable, the government is keen on oral pills.

That got over half a dozen Indian pharma companies, including Cipla, Hetero Labs and Zydus Cadila, busy with their permutations. Only one factor is known: India will see a surge in Covid-19 cases in the next two months.

Being used to planning investments and pricing based on disease prevalence and future incidence, they must now work in reverse. 

If any of these drugs work well, Indian companies will have a handsome lead in the global market. Ruhi lays out the landscape in which Indian pharma is rolling the dice.

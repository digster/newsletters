Good Morning Ishan,
If you’ve been following how astroturfing campaigns influenced the US elections, or if you generally keep a tab on Facebook in the age of “patriotic trolling”, you’ll appreciate today’s story.
Stolen credit card details. Facebook ads. A payment setup with no two-factor authentication. All of these make the social media giant the perfect playground for anonymous astroturfing and phishing campaigns.
That Girish Mallya was a victim of fraudulent transactions amounting to $4,859 is only part of the story. There are many like him. The real story is what the money was used for—to run ad campaigns. Because for every successful astroturf campaign, the face behind the finances wants to remain incognito.
Here’s the beauty of this operation. The ones pulling strings from behind-the-scenes achieve their objective, individuals whose card details are stolen get their money back after a few months, and the cycle goes on.
To gain some first-hand experience in the matter, Sidhartha, writer of today’s story, tried floating a dummy ad campaign on Facebook. In a few clicks, Facebook informed him if he wanted he could potentially target 83 million people with it. If he paid up, that is.
Now, this assumes significance in the light of the fact that Facebook has just tied up with the Indian Election Commission to help citizens register and vote.
The misinformation campaigns or hyperpartisan content aren’t going to go away anytime soon. However, our story gives a glimpse into how phishing ads are possibly run.

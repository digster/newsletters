Selling your voice to AI is now a side hustle, but a risky one. Perhaps it pays to think about what your voice might be used for

Good Morning Ishan,

I want to begin today’s edition of Ka-Ching! by being grateful to everyone who responded to last week’s little experiment. I was sceptical and nervous about ending the edition with a question, but the thoughtful and very detailed emails I got from our subscribers made it all worth it. Thank you! I intend to use your perspectives and leads to further probe how Flipkart’s Super.money is managing to clock such rapid growth. You’ll also find a few of the best responses I received featured at the end of today’s issue.

Speaking of experiments, allow me to introduce you to a new passive income option this week—selling your voice to AI. 

I don’t know how many of you already know about this. I didn’t. But the very first time I came across the idea, I knew I’d be writing about it here.

My group of friends and I have all just about crossed into our thirties, so we increasingly find ourselves discussing personal budgets, income, long-term career plans, lower-back pain, pivots, business ideas, digestion, and other such momentous topics. During one such discussion last week, a friend brought up Elevenlabs, a US-based company that makes AI tools for audio applications. Reportedly, it raised a Series C round in January at a valuation of US$3.3 billion.

But the company’s valuation was incidental to the conversation. Instead, the central subject was my friend’s claim. 

“I’ve earned Rs 50,000 in three months from Elevenlabs”

And apparently, almost all of that was “passive income”, derived from an “investment” of just an hour or two of work. 

Huh.

What’s the deal?

Of course, initially, we didn’t believe him; all of us have heard enough horror stories about online-income scams that our BS radars went up immediately. Add to that the fact that you need to pay a monthly subscription to Elevenlabs to keep earning from the platform and we had dismissed him within the first five minutes. 

He soon pulled out an ace, however—he showed us payments from Elevenlabs he’d received through Stripe, a US-based fintech. I cannot post those screenshots here because he requested me not to, but as journalists, we’re trained to be skeptics. So I decided to give it a shot myself and see what was going on. 

Here’s what you see when you land on Elevenlabs’ homepage.

View in Browser

The basic premise is pretty simple. After registering with an email address, you are supposed to upload your voice recordings to the platform. You need to read aloud or speak for at least 30 minutes, record it in good quality (preferably studio quality), and send it to Elevenlabs. People who have already used the platform (like my friend) recommend sending at least 2–3 hours of recordings to make sure the AI can be better trained. 

What does Elevenlabs do with your recording? 

Well, it’s processed for about 4–5 hours, the platform’s AI model is trained on it, and eventually your voice is cloned and uploaded on Elevenlab’s internal marketplace for sale. 

And that’s it. 

Anyone on Elevenlabs’ platform can now buy your voice and use it for their own purposes. They can just type out what they want, and—tada!—Elevenlabs will convert the text into speech, in your voice. 

That’s when I chickened out. 

Because I found the whole thing beyond creepy, and quite dangerous. 

I thought it odd that my friend wasn’t as concerned, so I asked him a few questions. 

“What can buyers use my voice for?”

“Everything.” 

“To dub movies?”

“Yes.”

“For audio books?”

“Yes”

“Porn?”

“Hmm.”

“On top of an ISIS video?”

“Um, maybe. But who will know it’s your voice? And I’ve made the money with zero effort.”

Right. 

Maybe that is enough for my friend (though I believe we will have another conversation about that soon), but it wasn’t enough for me. Elevenlabs’ website does include a set of usage guidelines and conditions for its customers, but how enforceable is that really?

View in Browser

There’s also how the payments are structured. Once you’ve uploaded your voice and set up payment details, you will get weekly payments through Stripe every time someone uses your voice. But you need to be on Elevenlabs creator-tier subscription plan in order to do this, which means paying $22 every month. So you have to hope that you make more than $22 per month from your voice sales in order to turn a profit. Elevenlabs also seems to have some kind of referral model in place, where creators send you sign-up links and ask you to pick up a subscription in exchange for discounts. 

Anyway, it isn’t just my friend who seems okay with this; I also found several Reddit posts that endorse the idea. Frankly, I’m amazed at how nonchalant some people seem with having their voices put to use for purposes they have no clue about. 

Me? I find it all too risky. And I don’t think I’m overreacting. But what about you? Write to kaching@the-ken.com and let me know.

Subscriber views: What’s Super.money’s secret sauce?

I mentioned that we received some insightful responses to my question last week about why Flipkart’s payments app Super.money is growing so rapidly. We received quite a few emails, but here are two I found interesting. 

Sree Sai Ganesh Atmuri writes:

Simplicity of the App—When you open the Super.money app, it immediately activates the camera to scan the QR code—even before you authenticate…

Incentivized Transactions—Unlike traditional UPI transactions, where you earn nothing back, Super.money rewards you with a few paise per transaction…

Virality and Organic Growth—The app’s unique UI sparks curiosity. When people see someone using Super.money, it naturally leads to questions and recommendations…

Mahesh Karuveli writes:

…The app is laser focussed on UPI payments while the market leaders have become bloated all-in-one apps that are hard to use.  

For example, every time I open Phonepe to scan a QR, I have to dismiss five different popup reminders for my utility and credit card bill payments. On Super.money it is as simple as just opening the app with my fingerprint and I am ready to scan. Even though Super.money has other offerings like credit cards and FDs, they are not in your face. This focus on JTBD is why I have switched to Super.money. Hope it stays this (way for a) while before the eventual enshittification.

And now, here’s my next question for you: 

What’s the most unimaginable thing you’ve taken a loan for? 

Someone once told me that they had borrowed to fund a fancy pre-wedding photoshoot. Apparently, the wedding was sponsored by the parents, but they refused to participate in any pre-wedding shenanigans. What about you? Have you taken loans to do things people wouldn’t usually borrow for? Maybe a super-expensive annual subscription? Clothes? A watch? Shoes? 

And also, why? 

Let me know by writing to kaching@the-ken.com.

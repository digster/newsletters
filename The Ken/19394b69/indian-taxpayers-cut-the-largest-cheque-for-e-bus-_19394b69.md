Good Morning Ishan,
Reading today’s draft reminded me of the movie Ratatouille. For those who haven’t watched the 2007 French drama-comedy, it’s about a super untalented kitchen worker who ends up learning to cook from a rat who can and wishes to be a renowned French chef one day. It’s about how the two align to create their own identities and fulfil their passion.
Something similar is up with EKA Mobility. If the EV industry were a Pixar movie, EKA might just be the protagonist doing its best to punch above its weight. A five-year-old R&D startup that somehow transformed itself into an electric-vehicle (EV) manufacturer—not just any EV, but the biggest one. EKA is now in the e-bus business, a space already claimed by players with deep pockets and even deeper R&D budgets.
But EKA isn’t flying solo. It’s a subsidiary of Pinnacle Industries, which has long supplied automotive seating systems to the likes of Tata Motors and Ashok Leyland. Add to that a shot of credibility from Japanese investment giant Mitsui & Co. and a boost of know-how from Dutch e-bus maker VDL Groep, which has been perfecting this game in Europe for over a decade.
At the outset, it looks great, too. From making seven buses in 2023, it now claims an order book of 1,500 e-buses worth Rs 3,000 crore (US$354 million), all within a month of the launch of the PM E-drive subsidy in September—the biggest push towards electrifying the country’s public transportation system.
And EKA’s timing couldn’t be any better. “The startups entering the booming Indian e-bus ecosystem need to remember this is no longer a B2B or B2C play—it’s B2G [business-to-government],” Mahesh Babu, global chief executive of Switch Mobility, a maker of e-bus and light commercial vehicles, told Nathan, the author of today’s story. Switch Mobility, too, has been in the business for about three years now, across 15 cities.

In Tolstoy’s short story—The Imp and the Peasant’s Bread—a simple farmer is tricked by a sly little devil. The imp plants doubt, greed, and ego in the farmer’s mind and ruins him.

In 2025, that imp now has a smartphone.

To ruin you, your family, and your lives.

Earlier this month, a Mangaluru-based man lost Rs 46.5 lakh over Whatsapp. He had received a message on 7 May from someone claiming to be Tom Harris, a service manager at the investment group Mayfield Trading Investment Group. Harris introduced himself politely and sent the man a registration link. It looked official. The site asked for the man’s name, address, and bank details.

What followed was a slow drain of money: Rs 46.5 lakh bled out, transferred in instalments across 25 days to various bank accounts, as the man continued to “invest”.

The link, of course, was fake. 

So was Tom Harris.

A game of digital disguise

This is not a rare case. Nor is it new. Last year, Rs 1.3–1.5 lakh was lost to scams every minute, with nearly one in three Indians falling victim to real-time payment scams.

But such swindles are getting increasingly more devious, especially with the rapid proliferation of artificial intelligence (AI) and machine learning (ML) technology. 

In the last few months, people in Nagpur, Bengaluru, and other cities have been receiving fake traffic challans on Whatsapp stating that they have violated some traffic rule or the other. These messages had a link to a file which, once downloaded, promptly initiated unauthorised transactions, siphoning off the entire balance in one’s bank account.

I have reported on such scams since 2021, including several deep-dive investigations that traced the Whatsapp investment scam industry back to Myanmar, Cambodia, and Laos. In some areas of Myanmar, territories not under the control of the country’s own military, entire scam factories now exist. Indian nationals are lured with job offers, then kidnapped and forced to run these operations. They are given targets. 

“You have to bring in Rs 10 lakh this month,” their captors say. And if they don’t, they are threatened with torture—in some cases, even starvation. 

Some of these scammers are thus also victims of extortion, kidnapping, and mental and physical harassment. In March 2025, 549 Indians were rescued from cyber-scam centres along the Myanmar-Thailand border. 

In other cases, local operators run the scam solo. Just 10 days ago, Bhubaneswar’s cyber police arrested a man who stole over Rs 12 lakh by pushing fake investment schemes on Whatsapp.

And many of these scamsters act as influencers and tipsters on social media. Over the past year, Sebi has banned and penalised dozens of accounts for peddling investment advice and trading tips through Telegram and Whatsapp. In fact, the regulator launched a new UPI security feature just this month restricting legitimate transactions to verified UPI IDs, all of which are supposed to follow this format: brokername@validbank. 

This is because many scamsters now use the names and logos of well-known Indian firms to appear legitimate. Scams impersonating brokerage firms like Zerodha, Angel One, and wealth management companies like 360 One have all come to light recently.

In April, Nithin Kamath, co-founder of Zerodha, was forced to tweet that the company “does not offer stock tips, investment advice, or run Whatsapp/Telegram trading-signal groups” and that “all official communication comes only through verified channels”.

More recently, on 24 June, Karan Bhagat, founder and MD of 360 One, also issued a public warning on Linkedin: 

“We have recently come across a number of fraudulent attempts where unknown individuals are misusing the 360 One brand name and the identities of our employees, including myself, to deceive and defraud the public […] We do not solicit investments on Whatsapp, Telegram, or public messaging platforms.”

Add increasingly more accessible AI and ML tech to the mix and things get even more dangerous. 

Let me give you an example. With the use of AI, scammers can now immediately share an authentic looking company ID badge on Whatsapp to gain trust.  And in multiple cases, scamsters used AI to mimic the voices of close family and friends, such as parents, to trick their marks.

Source: Linkedin

Whatsapp scams today go beyond just fake investments. The most common ones include job offers, crypto schemes, Ponzi tricks, and impersonations.

According to Whatsapp, the platform banned over 9.7 million Indian accounts in February alone. And over 92 million Indian accounts were banned in 2024.

Some tech players are trying to keep up. Google, which flags more than 500 million scam messages every month in India, launched a “Safety Charter” for the country last week and is rolling out AI-powered tools to detect and neutralise scams across its services.

Action against scamsters, however, remains patchy. While Indian law agencies and police cyber cells occasionally make arrests, most cases end up forgotten or unresolved. The bigger networks—the ones operating across borders or through sleeper agents—rarely get touched.

And it is not just about poor policing. These scams thrive because they play on emotions: hope, fear, and sometimes greed on the part of the scammed—none of which can magically be wished away. 

That is why some of my colleagues have now gone back to using Nokia feature phones— models like the 105 or 130. A smaller digital footprint, fewer avenues of attack for scamsters. 

Today, if you get a message from a “Tom Harris” promising easy money, you may want to ask yourself:

Are you the peasant Tolstoy warned about?

Year-end means everyone is planning trips if they can afford it, some to Goa, migrants to their native towns. A group chat with my friends was buzzing with discussions about where to visit and what to pack. My eyebrows shot up, however, when a male friend shared the contents of his toiletries bag: shampoo, conditioner, body wash, sunscreen, toothpaste, brush, and moisturiser. It was a small, personal detail that mirrored an ongoing market shift I had completely overlooked. 

And now, I started noticing this shift everywhere. Just last week, Honasa Consumer, which owns Mamaearth, announced a Rs 195 crore deal to buy Reginald Men, a direct-to-consumer (D2C) skincare brand for men. Before them, in November, Godrej Consumer also announced a Rs 450-crore acquisition of the men’s grooming brand, Mucchstac. When two FMCG giants spend crores to buy men’s skincare brands, you realise my friend’s toiletries bag isn’t an outlier; it’s the target demographic. 

But is this really a new trend? 

It’s not. In fact, the chase to encourage Indian men to adopt quality skincare on a mass scale began back in 2017. That’s when Marico, another FMCG major, acquired a strategic stake in Beardo—now one of India’s largest men’s grooming brands. The same year, Emami also took a stake in The Man Company.

This is not a new spree. It’s part of FMCG’s plan to diversify their portfolios and to essentially establish men’s grooming as a category within their beauty & personal care (BPC) play, explains Ankur Bisen, senior partner at The Knowledge Company, a consultancy firm. 

Take Mamaearth, for example. Its parent company houses seven skincare and cosmetics brands, such as Dr. Sheth’s, The Derma Co., that are all primarily focused on women. Similarly, Godrej Consumer has a strong presence in hair colour and hair oils but lacked a dedicated play in men’s grooming.

So what has prompted giants like Honasa and Godrej to acquire these new-age men’s brands at 11X (for Reginald) and 15X (for Mucchstac) EV/Ebitda multiples, respectively? EV is enterprise value, which is a company’s equity value plus debt minus cash. Ebitda—earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortisation—measures operating performance. 

The market has completely changed post-Covid, says Arush Chopra, founder of the D2C brand Just Herbs (which was also acquired by Marico). Consumers have become much more accustomed to taking care of their skin, and all of this has been boosted by the quick availability of products through quick commerce, he explains.

For brands, this is now an opportunistic play in a maturing market. 

“If you compare to when we started, there are more tools available at their [brands] disposal. Logistics, e-commerce, sourcing, marketing, everything is one tap away.”

Arush Chopra, founder of Just Herbs

The glow-up in numbers

That advantage is visible in the numbers. New brands now take significantly less time to scale compared to the pre-Covid era. 

For example, Beardo, founded in 2015, took five years to reach a scale of about Rs 80 crore. In comparison, Reginald, launched in 2022, scaled to Rs 74 crore in three years.

Their financial profiles also differ. Beardo operates at an Ebitda margin of 9.2% as of fiscal year 2025, according to a JM Financial report. Reginald, operates at a significantly higher margin of 24% over the trailing 12 months, according to Honasa Consumer. 

Mucchstac, which started earlier in 2018, has also scaled to about Rs 80 crore in revenue but with an impressive Ebitda margin of 37.5%, as per Godrej Consumer.

This makes the timing of these acquisitions particularly strategic for Honasa and Godrej. They did not have to spend on building the market from scratch, nor wait for the brands to become profitable, a path that has been choppy for pioneers like Beardo, which only achieved consistent profitability around 2024. 

In a recent Linkedin post, Sudhir Sitapati, CEO and managing director of Godrej Consumer, explained the rationale. He noted that while Muuchstac’s top line was smaller than typical strategic targets, its bottom line was particularly attractive. “The business, expected to hit a revenue of INR 80 crore in a few months, was indeed smaller than what strategics usually like. But a number that stood out was the Ebitda of INR 30 crore,” he wrote.

This detail was significant for Sitapati, as he had previously told The Ken many D2C brands falter by letting their bottom lines erode. Both the above-mentioned acquired brands, however, managed to avoid that pitfall and achieve scale—albeit through very different strategies. 

Reginald Men, whose sunscreen is its top-selling stock keeping unit (SKU), plays in the premium segment—its products are priced almost 2X as high as those of other D2C brands. It scaled massively by diverting traffic to its D2C website, explains a founder in the BPC space.

In comparison, Mucchstac, which counts its face wash as its bestseller, prices products even lower than FMCG giants like Nivea and Garnier. They grew on marketplaces through a very sharp pricing strategy, the above cited founder adds.

However, scaling these brands further will still be challenging. Most men’s skincare lines are nowhere near the scale achieved by women-focused or even unisex brands.

“The problem is that most men look at skincare products for functionality. They don’t experiment with the products like women do.” 

Pritam Kudev, founder of the men’s BPC brand Mannlich

I know so many men around me who have used the same face wash since they were 16, and still use it in their 30s, he added.

That explains why, even as first movers with nearly a decade in the market, Beardo and The Man Company are still at a scale of only around Rs 200 crore—a modest figure compared to, say, the unisex brand The Derma Co., which crossed Rs 500 crore in revenue in April 2024.

That said, behaviour is slowly changing. Earlier most men’s BPC purchases were made by their mothers, wives, or girlfriends, but now 80–90% of men’s product purchases are done by men themselves, Kudev notes, citing data shared with him by a beauty marketplace platform.

Still, some remain cautious. 

“It will take a lot of money and time to change how men behave culturally and socially,” says Bisen. Especially when the goal is to make them care about skincare the way women do.

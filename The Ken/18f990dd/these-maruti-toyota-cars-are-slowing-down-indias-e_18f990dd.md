Good Morning Ishan,
The tide seems to be turning against electric vehicles as their growth rate is not what it used to be.
Replacing them fast on the streets are hybrid cars. This year till early May, hybrid car registrations in India were almost 4X of EVs.
And the largest beneficiary of this trend in the country is the Maruti Suzuki-Toyota Kirloskar Motor duo—who came together in 2021 to develop hybrid cars for Indian buyers. The deal was that Toyota would share its hybrid technology with Maruti Suzuki. In exchange, Toyota gets to re-enter the segment of cars costing less than Rs 10 lakh (US$12,000).
This sure seems like a win-win situation. But the win matters more for one company over the other. Read Nathan’s [insightful story](https://the-ken.com/story/these-maruti-toyota-cars-are-slowing-down-indias-ev-adoption/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter) to know how this is playing out between the two auto majors even as EVs led by Tata Motors are trying to fight back.
If there is no such thing as a bad student, how come no one has been able to teach Byju’s a lesson?
Joining the list of those attempting to fix Byju’s and its laundry list of problems and giving up on the idea are Rajnish Kumar, former State Bank of India chairman, and Mohandas Pai, an early investor in the edtech. On 19 May, the two reportedly stepped down as advisers to the battered startup.
Kumar and Pai believed they could advise and get Byju’s to do what even its board couldn’t.
They had a simple 3-point agenda. Get the company to disclose its audited financials and communicate better with its shareholders. Plus, help Byju Raveendran rebuild the team. But even before the duo could get the company to do one of those, they quit. Kumar and Pai, in a joint statement, said their role as advisers to Byju’s was always meant to be only for a year.
For an edtech, it seems like Byju’s first needs to learn to be a better student.

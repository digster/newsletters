WhiteHat Jr's tryst with genies
 
Olina
 
Three things happened in the space of 12 months. Rapidly.
 
Twelve months ago, ex-Discovery India head Karan Bajaj created a PowerPoint, with his vision for a company that would teach Indian kids coding. He named it Whitehat Jr.
 
Six weeks ago, Bajaj met Byju Raveendran, whose company Byju’s just raised $400 million from an investor. Who? Doesn’t matter. At this point, only Jio hasn’t bitten the Byju’s bullet. 
 
Two days ago, Byju’s acquired WhiteHat Jr in an all-cash deal for $300 million, putting its recent fundraise to good use.
 
In a matter of 12 months, WhiteHat pulled off the quickest exit in Indian edtech, at seven times its last known valuation—$40 million. 
 
So what exactly happened between February 2019 and six weeks ago, when Bajaj met Byju’s?
 
When Bajaj met Byju’s
 
Bajaj found a Covid lamp, and three wishes fell out of it. Three events that he likely didn’t see coming:
 
  1. A pandemic hit the world and children ended up stuck at home. As a 21st century skill, coding was on the anvil. WhiteHat used this time to double down. It ran ads across media platforms, linking coding and the astronomical success of Bill Gates and Sundar Pichai. Nothing entices the Indian urban middle class more than an engineer, upward mobility, and a US green card.
     
  2. Kids have time on their hands now. There’s no extra-curricular activity they can do outside. Why not create an app from your living room. Edtech startups have spent years trying to sell their value proposition to parents, and dip into the generous education wallet share. Now buyers have switched places with sellers. Online tuitions went from “good-to-have” to necessities, and coding moved up from “also-have” to a “must-have”. Whitehat’s user base grew to 20,000 in less than a year.
     
  3. WhiteHat’s aggressive growth was never not a subject of discussion in closed-door edtech meetings. The choice was either to “be a Byju’s” or “be bought by Byju’s”. By the time Bajaj met Byju’s six weeks ago, Covid had likely made the decision for WhiteHat. It fattened up the year-old start-up into the perfect acquisition vehicle for Byju’s. Byju’s had started its own coding vertical not long ago, but WhiteHat was just too ripe for the taking. Now does that count as a wish?
 
Covid has undeniably changed the shape and structure of the edtech market in India. It has beefed up younger start-ups with organic growth and sky-high valuations, all thanks to the Covid genie that shrunk timelines for everyone. 
 
But what if Covid wasn’t the real genie. What if it was someone else all along? Someone with a big appetite to grow, millions of dollars at its disposal and the best marketing spiel in the business? 
 
You’ll never guess.
The digital payments monster must be caged
 
Arundhati
 
This conversation happened among central bank officials, earlier this week. 
 
"Banks are less active in electronic payments.”
 
A former central bank official said the digital payments companies were allowed “to grow into monsters”.
 
"They (digital payments companies) got special love...,” the person said. 
 
“All the banks.... complained. Now it is incredibly difficult to rein them in.”
 
“.... want a more level playing field for the banks" 
 
Digital payments companies growing big is a concern. Banks want to strike back.

Good Morning Ishan,
If you’re a discount broker making most of your money off futures-and-options trading—something that the regulator loves to crack down on—what do you do? You sprint toward a billion-dollar IPO, of course.
That’s Groww.
Yes, it has more clients than any other Indian broker. Also yes: it trails behind Zerodha* and Angel One on every financial metric that investors care about—revenue, profits, ARPU, all of it. And its customers are mostly young, first-time investors. The kind that ghost when the market gets rocky.
But whatever—IPO season, baby!
Groww is doing the pre-listing dance: letting Singapore’s sovereign wealth fund in, jacking up fees, and—because no IPO is complete without a narrative pivot—buying Fisdom, a tiny, loss-making wealth-tech firm, in a $150 million all-cash deal to prove it’s not just a broker. Also in the works is a wealth-management unit for the rich.
Because being “just a broker” in 2025 is radioactive.
If you need a case study, just look at Angel One. Flying high a few months ago, the broker’s profits tanked 50% in the March quarter. The stock followed.
So Groww is spinning this new story: We’re a full-stack platform. The kind that gets better multiples, steadier earnings, and VC heart emojis. There’s just one issue: It’s not completely true. Or not yet. Still, the company wants public-market investors to ignore the cracks. The comps. The clock ticking on its business model.

Zydus Lifesciences, a company best known for its generics and consumer-health brands like Sugarfree and Glucon-D, has just made one of its biggest moves yet—a €300 million acquisition of a French orthopaedics firm. It’s the first time an Indian pharma major has ventured meaningfully into medical technology, a space with faster regulatory cycles, steadier margins, and radically different playbooks. The leap could redefine how Indian pharma thinks about adjacent bets—and what scale and ambition look like in a post-generics world.
In this week’s Long and Short, Seema unpacks why Zydus is straying from the script—and what it’ll take to make medtech more than a one-off experiment.

Stories of employee stock options come in different shades. In India, Infosys introduced many of us to this fantastic wealth-creating machine. Many thought it was just a fad.
 
Two years ago, when I asked Infosys co-founder, NR Narayana Murthy, if he regretted giving away so much equity, he said: “I come from a socialist background and when I turned to capitalism, I became a compassionate capitalist. We gave, excluding the so-called co-founders, 35% of equity which is worth $11-12 billion today. Never in the history of India has it happened and I don’t think it will ever happen again.”
 
Founders have since then embraced the idea of ESOPs but often it’s not designed or distributed in the best interests of the company or the employees. The tax laws throw in a wrench too. Which is why sometimes employees don’t just leave the company but also leave money on the table.
 
When it comes to founders’ interests, startups have found a way around the hurdles. Like this messaging company did by issuing differentiated Class A shares which carry 10 weighted voting rights per share. A few are now paying more attention to employees’ interests. Like this online property rental company has done by ensuring that in the case of an acquisition, if an employee’s role is demoted or salary lowered, the shares of that employee vest immediately, irrespective of the discretion of the new management. 
 
In our third and concluding part of the series on ESOPs, Payal looks at the good-to-have, must-have and strictly forbidden map of the ESOP journey.

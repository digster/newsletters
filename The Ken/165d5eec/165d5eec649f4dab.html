Good Morning Ishan,
 
Earlier this week, I stumbled upon this tweet from Saumya Swaminathan, deputy director general of the World Health Organisation: “How could healthcare be anything other than high quality?”
 
Turned out, it was from an editorial in The Lancet, by none other than the WHO chief, Tedros Adhanom Ghebreyesus. (The journal has just commissioned a report on the state of quality care globally.)
 
Ghebreyesus hopes that in the years to come, the term “quality care” will fall into disuse—because there is no other kind. Beautifully said, except that keeping relentless focus at the point of care is more than a Herculean task. 
 
Our story today is about two entrepreneurs struggling with this dilemma, because in India, unfortunately, investment in the quality of care doesn’t automatically translate to better business. Even in renal care, where only about 15% of the people who require dialysis can afford it today.
 
NephroPlus, India’s largest dialysis chain, and also one of the first single specialty chains to be backed by private equity, is at a crossroads. All because of its steadfastness to quality. Intriguing, isn’t it?
 
Dialysis is a form of surgery; it needs high adherence to SOPs and yet is not a high ticket item for the hospitals. Hospitals are happy to outsource to centres like NephroPlus. In other markets, such chains are rated; and higher the rating, better the business. In India, everything is self-proclamation.
 
Ruhi’s story goes deep into the challenges and opportunities of the dialysis business. NephroPlus seems like a star achiever, especially when the international players are not keen on India. But, in the midst of Series D fundraise, it knows very well that this business is not about the capital, but about making unit economics work with quality service. Can NephroPlus make it work as it goes from 149 centres to 300? Let’s dive into the story: https://the-ken.com/story/nephroplus-largest-dialysis-chain/

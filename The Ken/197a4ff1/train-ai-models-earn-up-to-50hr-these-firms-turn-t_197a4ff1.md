Good Morning Ishan,
If I were to ask you to think of a data labeller, there’s a good chance your mental image would be something like this: someone sitting before a monitor, right hand resting on a mouse, clicking and dragging to place boxes over specific objects in a collection of images, over and over again. They would be slowly training a piece of software to “see”—maybe something simple like a cat or dog. It’s a low-skill, low-pay, low-satisfaction job.
But there’s a new crop of AI trainers now. They’re highly specialised—think of physics researchers and climate scientists who hold PhDs, or the voice actors behind popular ads you’ve encountered hundreds of times. And they’re crucial to the process of sharpening AI models that are becoming more advanced every day.
Deccan AI is headquartered in the United States, but its network of 900,000 freelance contributors is in India. The company specifically taps graduates of major institutions—the IITs, IIITs, NITs, and the like. These contractors hold degrees in engineering, medicine, and law, among others.
They end up in highly sought-after positions. Specialists can earn up to $50 per hour, compared to $11 an hour for the average physics PhD student in India. Other firms that are in the same space have a similar setup. Turing’s freelance cohort of 4 million people is also majorly from India.
It’s a better-paid version of what IT-service companies have been doing for decades—source labour in India, then service projects and clients abroad.
This is a development that is changing the nature of AI training as a profession.

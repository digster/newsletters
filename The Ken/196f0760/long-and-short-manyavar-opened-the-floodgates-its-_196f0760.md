Long and Short
Wed, 21 May 25
The story behind the numbers and motivations driving India's stock market..
Good Morning Ishan,
Within eight months of its February 2022 listing, Vedant Fashions’ market value had reached an all-time high of almost Rs 36,000 crore ($4.2 billion).
The owner of apparel retailer Manyavar surged to such levels again in December 2023 and a year later. It has since careened downhill, losing more than half its market cap. A fall in its profits in the March quarter has only hastened Vedant’s plunge.
Shares in the company are now trading well below their issue price.
A turn of events few would have predicted for what was once the envy of the retail world, as I [pointed out](https://the-ken.com/tradetricks/why-3b-worth-manyavar-is-a-retailer-like-none-other/) in 2022.
There are other retailers that have built a business out of selling ethnic apparel, including Fabindia and TCNS Clothing Company, which owns brands such as W and Aurelia. But no company has managed to get this far by staying focussed on a subset of that market–clothes for special occasions.
What makes Vedant Fashions’ (VFL) rise even more laudable is that it was largely made possible by catering to an even smaller subset–men’s apparel–that accounts for just a tenth of India’s ethnic wear market. (VFL unveiled Mohey, its women’s celebration wear label, only in 2o15, a decade and a half after the launch of Manyavar.)
But that was only half the story.
Unlike its listed peers, VFL never discounts Manyavar merchandise—Manyavar brings in 85% of its revenue—and doesn’t have an end-of-season sale. Fabindia is somewhat similar, but it’s not completely immune to discounts. In the year ended March 2021, 8% of its revenue was adjusted for discounts and returns.
VFL doesn’t have to worry about not having a big sale because its unsold Manyavar stock is less than 3% of its catalogue, according to brokerage Mirae Asset Capital Markets. And it is able to keep a close watch on its inventory and costs by dispatching all its products from its central warehouse in Kolkata, where VFL is headquartered. In addition, the 25 designers on its payroll create 90% of its merchandise.
The fact that most of Vedant’s 670 stores are franchisee-run means Vedant doesn’t have to spend hundreds of crores every year expanding its footprint. The industry-beating profitability that results from this is naturally catnip for investors.
It’s common for top executives of listed companies to be nonchalant, at least in public, about conspicuous swings in the stock price. They often trot out a variant of this: “Any short-term volatility doesn’t affect us. We are focused on creating value for our shareholders in the long run.”
But Vedant Modi, chief revenue officer of Vedant Fashions, did refer to past investor love for the company as a possible reason for its current woes on its recent earnings call.
Well, on the lighter side of things, people say it was our market cap three years ago, and that might have attracted a lot of people. But overall, I think this space just seemed interesting and without many brands, with only one large player operating. So, probably why it attracted a lot of competition.
The son of founder Ravi Modi, Vedant may have meant this as a quip, but there’s certainly some truth to it. A fashion retailer that didn’t exist before 2022 was worth considerably more than an older peer such as Shoppers Stop and as much as a giant like Aditya Birla Fashion and Retail.
So Vedant Fashions must have been doing something right.
In fact, even before Vedant Fashions’ listing, Reliance Retail, India’s largest retailer, had rolled out Avantra by Trends, an Indian-wear chain for women.
Months later, Nykaa Fashion followed suit with an in-house brand. Then, Aditya Birla Fashion teamed up with fashion designer Tarun Tahiliani to launch a men’s ethnic-wear label. The number of stores catering to men’s Indian wear alone have tripled in 2–3 years, Vedant Modi said on the earnings call. And it’s not merely thanks to prominent retailers.
It’s a large number of players opening a few stores, that is what we are seeing on the ground majorly. And if I give you an example of a city like Raipur, we used to have one exclusive brand outlet which we operate. And maybe another 25–30 stores would have opened in that market.
Vedant Fashions’ lukewarm performance is hardly limited to a single quarter. Even for the year ended March, its profits slumped even as it struggled to grow its sales.
Part of the company’s appeal is the widely held belief that weddings are immune to consumption slowdowns. But Vedant Fashions’ financials have driven a truck through that assumption, and Vedant Modi broke down his company’s conundrum on the call.
If you look at our revenues, 40%-odd revenue comes from the grooms and the brides, and the remaining 60% comes from the family/wedding attendees. Now when we say consumer sentiments impact us, it will impact how much 40% is willing to spend, but they will still continue to buy. But the 60% behaves very similar to the other lifestyle markets of India, where consumer sentiments demand how much they will actually buy, will they buy a brand or what will they sort of consume during this festive period as well.
Vedant Fashions has tried to calm the market with its attempts to unyoke its business from weddings, including the launch of a festive-wear label, and more than doubling the designs it launched last fiscal.
But, with the company no longer one of its kind, investors won’t be quite as wide-eyed as before.

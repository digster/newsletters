By now, you’ve surely heard of Thrasio, the 2-year-old company that inverts the laws of startup exits and venture capital. Instead of building a better mousetrap that customers want, it simply finds those that already exist on Amazon and offers to buy them from their owners, usually for around $1 million in cash. Having bought them, it spends its efforts on growing them further. Where does it find the money to give out? Venture capitalists and debt investors. Why do they give Thrasio money? Because it uses their money to buy, grow and repeat really fast.
Like zero to $1 billion in valuation in 2 years. And a rumoured SPAC merger and IPO at a valuation of $2-10 billion by the 3rd year.
Why build if you can buy? Why indeed.
Due to Amazon’s sheer dominance in the US market, Thrasio has chosen to focus largely on buying out Amazon sellers. But the story is different in India. Which is why Mensa Brands, arguably the most strategic of its ilk in India, is looking to buy independent brands from Amazon, Flipkart, Myntra, Jio, and Nykaa. It helps that its CEO is the former CEO of Myntra. Mensa raised $50 million last month.
But it's not just the fracturing of marketplaces that Thrasio’s Indian avatars will need to figure out. There’s also the fact that Indian entrepreneurs are very different from American ones.
Aside from the hard-nosed scepticism about new age VC-funded startups offering to buy out their businesses, Indian entrepreneurs find it hard to sell out, take cash and then have a nice time. Selling their businesses completely feels to many like selling their babies. Sitting idle is looked down upon. Letting go is hard.
So what will the Thrasios of India do? How will they alter their strategy to Indian conditions? What categories will they choose? Can they avoid the need to show rapid acquisitions in order to validate their model and show growth?

Good Morning Ishan,

Whoever first came up with the super app model must also be credited with fanning gladiatorial fights in the digital economy.

The one playing out between Indonesia’s ride-hailing company Gojek and Singapore’s Grab is of epic proportions. The prognosis is so dire that media reports last month about a possible merger of Gojek and Grab didn’t look outlandish to most.

Although Gojek has rebuffed that report, fact is, it hasn’t been able to replicate its home run in any other country. It is present in five countries, but 90% of its revenue comes from Indonesia, according to information sourced by The Ken. Then, what was the boil-the-ocean-strategy until now all about? Should it be retreating from some cities?

The writers of today’s story, Jon and Jum, say Gojek’s regional expansion was as much about distracting Grab away from Indonesia as it was about landing in new territories and shoring up investments. Aware of its financial mortality, it is making cutbacks. It is shutting down most of its GoLife services in Indonesia, including laundry, beauty and home services on-demand.

Grab, meanwhile, is on the offensive. Its famed investor, SoftBank, has committed to the Indonesian government of spending $2 billion towards developing national infrastructure. It may even help fund a new capital city. (Anything is possible with SoftBank.)

In food delivery, which, everywhere, is a battle for market share and fought with piles of cash, the steep discounts between the two companies have made food prices more affordable, keeping Indonesian inflation low. Talk of a living socio-economic laboratory!

Grab’s strategy is starting to show tangible results, according to downloads data from App Annie. (In another app download race, India’s Ola is leaving Uber behind in London. Driven, of course, by cash incentives.)

In a ‘super’ insightful story on the organised chaos across the region, Jon and Jum ask whether Gojek can ‘still’ win in Indonesia without Southeast Asia.

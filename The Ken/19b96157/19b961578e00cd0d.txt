Siddhartha Bhaiya’s untimely passing endangers an already rare species—the straight-talking contrarian—in the Indian stock market

Siddhartha Bhaiya was not one to mince words. In December, in his last interaction https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5sxJJ0OjWWo with the media, Bhaiya offered this sharp take during a panel discussion (edited excerpts):

“We are in a bubble of epic proportions. 

Excluding that [some big PSU stocks], Nifty PE [price-to-earnings ratio] is more than 40. In mid-caps and in small-caps, PE multiples are in excess of 50.

What happens in a 50PE environment? The promoter comes and sells. 

I have said this a lot in private, I am going to say this in public. It is not SIP. It is SWT – systematic wealth transfer from India’s middle class to the rich. The SIP amount has been matched with promoter offloading.

What I am seeing is not a healthy bull market, not a healthy capital market. 

Everybody talks about asset allocation. When a bull market comes or a bear market comes, your asset allocation is thrown out of the window. 

All I want to say is: Be careful. Don’t listen to narratives. Go back and look at your portfolio. What is the PE multiple of stocks in your portfolio?”

Bhaiya had added laughingly, “I have been warned by my team to control my emotions today.”

Thankfully, Bhaiya didn’t control his words, even if he restrained his emotions. He said what he saw, having put his money where his mouth was. An outlier in a market filled with consensus sticklers, conformists, and crowd pleasers. 

His untimely passing on 31 December 2025, at the age of 49, is a big loss not just for his loved ones and investors, but for the Indian stock market as well. A rare species—a vital one in the market ecosystem—has shrunk further. 

As managing director and chief investment officer of Aequitas Investments, a boutique portfolio management services (PMS) firm, Bhaiya was a straight-talking contrarian.  Kotak Institutional Equities’ Sanjeev Prasad, who debunked the mutual fund hype in an interview https://the-ken.com/story/kotaks-sanjeev-prasad-debunks-mutual-fund-hype-40-of-retail-flows-since-2021-yielded-zero-returns/ with The Ken in September, is among the very few in this small group. So is ICICI Prudential MF’s S Naren https://the-ken.com/story/icici-prudential-mfs-investment-chief-warned-of-a-crash-did-the-fund-house-listen/?utm_source=newsletter&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=collection. They are, however,  just a handful. 

The Ken got a first-hand glimpse of Bhaiya’s plain speak and contrarian streak when my colleagues Nuha, Seetharaman, and I met with him about a year ago. It led to this story https://the-ken.com/story/siddhartha-bhaiyas-aequitas-is-the-fund-for-the-ultra-rich-that-can-win-by-not-playing/: “Siddhartha Bhaiya’s Aequitas is the fund for the ultra-rich that can win by not playing.” 

Bhaiya’s reputation as a small-cap multi-bagger expert over the past 12 years had been built on stocks like Avanti Feeds (>100X returns) and Apar Industries, and Sanghvi Motors (>50X). But he had taken a step back since 2024, when the market was pushing valuations up and away. 

The Ken’s story delved into why Aequitas’ small-cap-focused PMS had chosen to sit out a roaring bull market, and how that patience had paid off handsomely. 

Bhaiya’s candour and willingness to go against the grain stood out.

Back in April 2024, when the stock market was still riding high, Aequitas had already stopped accepting new inflows and was steadily moving into cash… By December 2023… Bhaiya was certain—small- and mid-cap valuations had become a bubble waiting to pop…September [2024] brought the vindication. The Indian markets have been bleeding, with small- and mid-caps taking the biggest hits. Meanwhile, Bhaiya has been watching from the sidelines, with Aequitas’ small-cap fund paring its stock holdings to 14% from nearly 63% between May and December 2024.

Its sharp focus on valuations led Aequitas to take this bold bet, one that proved painful for its investors in the near term but paid off generously in the end.

Aequitas has just the one scheme. “And you can measure us on the basis of what we’ve done. I don’t believe in multiple strategies—it’s a good way of fooling people,” Bhaiya said…..As of December 2024, Aequitas’ India Opportunities product boasted a five-year annualised return of over 40% and a ten-year return exceeding 26%, according to PMS Bazaar. Among the 52 PMS schemes with a decade-long track record, Aequitas leads the pack. Only two others focus on small caps, and Aequitas’ ten-year return stands apart from the competition.”

[…]

“Conviction is conviction,” said a Barclays manager who has followed his career. “If he’s sticking to it, all we can do is wait and watch.

Over the past year, in 2025, the Aequitas India Opportunities fund stuck to its guns, staying big on gold and cash. Unlike most small-cap-focused PMS funds that posted negative returns over the year, the Aequitas fund delivered a 36% gain. Its portfolio now holds just five or six stocks—including Technocraft Industries, Petronet LNG, and JK Paper—down from 22–25 stocks it held in earlier years.  

His investors are not complaining. And they will likely miss https://x.com/malpani/status/2007652783017918675 him.

Many others in the Indian stock market will miss Bhaiya. Whether his calls will continue to deliver is beside the point. What really matters is the variety in thought and richness of debate he brought to the table. The importance of free thinking and independent action in developing a healthy capital market cannot be overstated. Especially in an environment where most fund managers in the country seem reluctant to go against the consensus, or to speak up when they do. Very few have the conviction and courage to do both. 

The average investor is worse off for it, bombarded with views and opinions that all sound the same. Only the cheerleaders shuffle. This herd mentality is also seen in the analyst bias towards “buy” calls, or at best “hold” calls, while “sell” calls remain far fewer.

The Indian capital market needs many more like Siddhartha Bhaiya, contrarians not afraid to call a spade a spade.

Trade Tricks
Tue, 01 Jul 25
A weekly newsletter about the biggest changes in commerce—focusing on shifts that matter to you..
Good Morning Ishan,
In December 2022, Nike launched a limited-edition sneaker in collaboration with American rapper Travis Scott. Called the Air Jordan 1 Low Og Black Phantom, it was priced at $150 (Rs 12,500) a pair. A stunning triple-back shoe, it also had a wing and bee embroidered on its heel tabs, dedicated to his daughter Stormi.
Today, if you wanted to buy a pair in India, they’re out of stock on Nike’s official website. But if you are okay with shelling out Rs 75,000–1,00,000, over 5.5–8X their original price, you can buy them on sneaker marketplaces like the Culture Circle. Just for fun, I checked how much the stock markets have grown in the same period, and unsurprisingly, gains are far lower—the Sensex is up 1.3X in that time; the Nasdaq 2X.
This obsession with streetwear fashion is what gave birth to operations like Growthx, Goat, and Realreal in the United States. They’re essentially matchmakers, connecting enthusiastic buyers with a range of sellers who have the desired models in the correct sizes (which is tougher than it sounds for some of the rarer lines).
A clutch of players in India have now adopted the same market strategy, and streetwear marketplace Culture Circle is one of them. Think an E-bay for luxury streetwear, where buyers—mostly in the 15–30-year age group—seem more than ready to pay above retail price for the latest edition of whatever it is that catches their fancy. Brands and products like Yeezy, Louis Vuitton, Labubu dolls, etc. The difference is, Culture Circle doesn’t feature used products or sell via multiple intermediaries. They connect buyers directly with sellers.
Investors see something in it. Culture Circle has backers such as Sanjeev Bikhchandani, Kunal Bahl, and Ritesh Agarwal*, who have invested a total of $2.45 million at a $9.5 million valuation (Rs 80 crore). Their rival, Main Street, raised $2.34 million from Nikhil Kamath’s Gruhas and Silverneedle Ventures in 2023, according to data from Tracxn. Then there are others like Vegnonveg, Superkicks, and Crepdog Crew.
“The desire to stand out is biocoded in us. And wearing luxury is a sort of social status signalling that says, ‘Yes, we have arrived,’” notes Devansh Jain, one of Culture Circle’s founders. His co-founder Ackshay Jain adds: “People are talking about a slowdown in consumption, but for us, since we started our website in January 2024, we haven’t seen one.”
In January 2024, Culture Circle clocked a monthly gross merchandise value (GMV) of Rs 40 lakh; that number has gone up to Rs 8–10 crore a month now, according to the marketplace. And by FY26, they expect a NMV of Rs 300 crore.
The founders, at least, think they have a hit on their hands.
Not long ago, even if some of these luxury brands were available at fancy malls in Indian cities, the collections used to be old. And there were certain models that were not available at all in India, even at duty-free shops at airports.
So your only choice if you wanted to get your hands on them was to either visit luxury outlets overseas, or rely on websites like E-bay to find sellers who would ship to India.
Things are a little different now. Earlier, international sneaker and streetwear collections reached India with a delay of three to four seasons. Today, that gap has narrowed to just one or two—both in terms of product availability and trend adoption, according to Kashika Malhotra, chief marketing officer at Brandman Retail, the exclusive distributors for premium athleisure and lifestyle brands like New Balance in India.
But some lines and brands are still pretty hard to lay hands on. And marketplaces like Culture Circle are solving for that—organising the reselling market in the process.
The key is exhaustive seller verification. Culture Circle has about 3,000 of them, selling products priced between Rs 5,000–5,00,000.
For the Travis Scott Black Phantom Jordan, for instance, there are at least nine sellers offering the model on the Culture Circle website. And each of them is verified, including via Checkcheck, an app-based platform for checking the authenticity of items such as sneakers and handbags.
Earlier, Ackshay says, the industry standard for order cancellations used to be around 30%, and most orders were cancelled by sellers. This number has now come down to less than 2%. “Cataloguing and record-keeping were not done properly, it was manual and not automated… But the introduction of an AI engine, called Sourcex, has helped reduce that number,” says Ackshay.
To quote the founders:
“Sourcex platform operates through a comprehensive five-layer authentication system unprecedented in India. We begin with complete vendor KYC verification and business license validation, followed by initial product verification including SKU matching and serial number checks. Our proprietary AI then conducts material verification and manufacturing pattern analysis, complemented by Checkcheck’s global authentication system featuring expert review and UV light inspection. Finally, we perform quality inspection covering packaging and accessories.”
The founder duo shared an anecdote: Culture Circle also has stores, and sometime earlier, a woman stopped by one in Hyderabad with a bag stuffed with Rs 15–20 lakh in cash. She asked the store executives to show all the limited-edition shoes available in her size, and bought all of them. Her reasoning was simple—prices of limited-edition shoes, she believed, would only go up.
This is a market segment, says Devansh, where consumers are quite willing to pay above MRP for the items they want. Most of the products are limited-edition, with only around 100–500 units ever made, and when their price increases, they only become more desirable. The founders believe their product range may even be recession-proof, powered by the desire for little luxuries that survives even during the bad times. A little like the [Lipstick Effect](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/l/lipstick-effect.asp).
And high [import duties](https://www.financialexpress.com/business/industry-asics-to-boost-india-manufacturing-to-40-amid-import-roadblocks-3881405/) and restrictions on footwear imports have only benefited Culture Circle, the founders claim, because “consumers are shifting from individual imports to trusted marketplaces for authentic products”.
Today, the Indian streetwear and luxury resale market is estimated to be growing at 40–45% annually, with the size of the sneaker reselling market at around Rs 1,000 crore. And while global peers like Realreal have seen sales slow down, Culture Circle’s founder-duo believes the growth in India is primarily organic and community-driven rather than marketing-spend heavy, like in other markets.

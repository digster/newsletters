Long and Short
Wed, 08 Jan 25
The story behind the numbers and motivations driving India's stock market..
Good Morning Ishan,
Gautam Adani is no stranger to working alongside strategic foreign investors of note. French giant Totalenergies is a major shareholder in two of the billionaire’s key companies, Adani Total Gas and Adani Green Energy.
But he hasn’t had as long an association with any global company as with Singapore’s Wilmar International, an agribusiness group with operations across Asia and Europe. Adani Enterprises, the flagship of his sprawling empire, has had a joint venture with Wilmar since 1999.
That partnership will soon be history, though. And with it, the Adani Group’s years-long desire to build a consumer brand that would be the perfect foil to the ports, airports, power plants, and city-gas-distribution networks it has staked its reputation on.
On 30 December, Adani Enterprises announced that it will sell its entire 44% stake in Adani Wilmar, which is best known for its Fortune brand of edible oils. Of this, Wilmar will buy 31%, with the rest to be offloaded in the open market. Adani Enterprises said it intends to use the US$2 billion proceeds to “turbocharge” its investments in “core infrastructure”. In 2023, Adani Enterprises [reportedly](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2023-08-08/adani-enterprises-said-to-mull-exiting-6-billion-wilmar-venture) considered selling its stake in Adani Wilmar for those same reasons.
It isn’t a surprise that Gautam Adani wants to once again focus on how he made his billions in the first place.
He has been under attack—first from short-seller Hindenburg Research, which accused his group of share-price manipulation and accounting fraud, in early 2023, and then from the US Justice Department, which recently indicted him and other group executives for being part of a scheme to bribe government officials to secure power-supply contracts.
It’s an all-hands-on-deck situation, and Gautam Adani needs to do everything, including freeing up capital tied up elsewhere, to build a moat around his infrastructure fort.
Adani Enterprises, as we have [written](https://the-ken.com/story/gautam-adanis-oldest-company-is-not-his-most-valuable-but-it-dictates-his-future/) earlier, is integral to most of the group’s newer businesses, including roads and airports. It not only bankrolls them with equity but also lends to them and even guarantees their borrowings from banks.
When Adani Wilmar listed in February 2022, Adani Enterprises’ plans for it were the exact opposite of eventually sacrificing it at the altar of its mainstay. In fact, it wanted to turn a company mostly reliant on a low-margin commodity (edible oil) into a diversified foods maker, as I [wrote](https://the-ken.com/story/adani-wilmar-fmcg-oil/) then:
In its IPO filing, the billionaire Gautam Adani-owned company says it’s looking to “derisk our business from dependency on any single product or category”. Indeed, if it doesn’t want to be seen as a mere commodity company that happens to have one well-recognised brand, diversification is not an option but a necessity.
That’s why Adani Wilmar is venturing into a range of new categories such as noodles, pasta, and biscuits. In doing so, the company seems to be taking a leaf out of ITC Ltd’s playbook. The FMCG major has created strong brands such as Aashirvaad wheat flour, Sunfeast biscuits, and Yippee instant noodles over the past two decades.
The parallels with ITC don’t end with foods. Like ITC, Adani Wilmar wants a personal and home care portfolio to play second fiddle to its foods business. It launched a soap brand, Alife, in 2019, and added handwashes and sanitisers to the same brand the following year.
While non-oil FMCG has almost doubled its contribution to Adani Wilmar’s top line since the IPO, its share is still under 10%.

Small wonder, then, that investors don’t see it as a food company they way do Tata Consumer Products, a prominent rival in pulses. The duo’s profitability is one reason why. Tata Consumer had an operating margin of 11.4% in FY24, compared to Adani Wilmar’s 2.7%.
The latter’s inability to transcend its edible-oil origins has also hurt its market value. Adani Wilmar shares have lost 11% of their value in the past year, while the S&P BSE FMCG index has gained 2%.
Adani Wilmar’s struggles have been thrown into sharp relief by Tata Consumer’s and ITC’s ambitious “[whole-pantry](https://the-ken.com/tradetricks/the-tata-consumer-itc-fight-to-take-over-your-entire-kitchen/)” strategy.
But for all that Adani Wilmar is yet to achieve, it offered its Indian co-owner its one true shot at forging a household brand—much like Aashirvaad or Surf Excel.
In the first edition of this newsletter, I [wrote](https://the-ken.com/long_and_short/the-retailer-taking-shape-in-adani-enterprises-airports-business/) about the budding retailer emerging within Adani-run airports. That’s a consumer bet, too, and one with a (at least temporarily) captive customer base. But it’s limited to a sliver of the population that can afford to fly.
The Adani Group may be unrivalled in its ability to repeatedly raise billions of dollars and deploy them in such a way that its own interests are yoked to India’s infrastructure needs.
But it has proved no match for the average Indian’s fiercely contested kitchen shelf.

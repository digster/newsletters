Good Morning Ishan,

No, not a bar. But into India’s growing digital banking system. Instead of a bartender there’s a banker, who says, “Would you like a bank account?”

Ram Mitra*, the husband and the owner of a small carpentry workshop, replies, “Sure. I’d love that. Nobody’s ever offered me a bank account.” 

So the bank, Kotak Mahindra Bank—one of India’s largest private banks—gives him a savings bank account. 

A few months pass. Another banker, also from Kotak Mahindra Bank, asks him, “Would you like a current account, too?” 

Sure, he replies.

"Would you also like an insurance policy to go with your accounts?” asks another Kotak Mahindra banker a month later.

Okay, he replies. 

By now, it's a party. Yet another Kotak Mahindra banker asks, “Surely you must want our credit card, too?” 

I guess, the man says. 

His business has gone to the dogs. He barely has any income to talk about. His wife works as a household help to make ends meet. But he has three bank accounts, a credit card and an expensive life insurance policy with a return of under 4% over 15 years.

The Mitras had just entered India’s formal and increasingly digital banking system. Except, they took the wrong turn.

In a deeply-reported story about the hapless travails of a single lower income family, Arundhati and Shreedhar lay bare the extent of misselling and fraud that is taking place among people who have no way of spotting it. Much less doing something about it.

From banks to e-commerce portals to online education companies, the Mitras walked straight into a quagmire that consumed every Rupee of their savings and then some more. On late fees, bounce fees, minimum balance fees, loan fees and test fees. 

“I’m now convinced that the chances of my money getting stolen from my bank account is higher than if I keep it at home,” admitted Mitra’s wife finally, defeated.

This is an important story that deals with the challenges faced by India’s low income banking users. A segment that risks losing trust in formal banking because existing systems aren’t built to truly engage with them honestly.

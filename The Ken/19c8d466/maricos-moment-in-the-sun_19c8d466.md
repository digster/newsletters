Good Morning Ishan,
How was your day yesterday? Well, if you ask Marico, it was a pretty good one.
The Mumbai-based FMCG company has been on an acquisition spree, and investors are taking notice. Its stock is trading at an all-time high, fueled by strong third-quarter results. All signs point to a perfect start to the week for the company.
In fact, Marico broke records twice this month. First by hitting a 52-week high of Rs 798 last Thursday, and then surpassing it to reach Rs 805 yesterday. It seems the season of abundance has arrived early for Marico.
| Source: Screener |
In the December quarter, Marico’s profits grew 13% from a year ago. Rival Emami fared a tad better, while Dabur’s profit growth was in the single digits.
Where Marico truly outperformed its peers was in sales, which expanded by a quarter. In comparison, Emami’s growth was less than half, and Dabur’s was merely one-fourth of Marico’s.
This makes it the third consecutive quarter of industry-beating revenue growth.
Peel back the numbers, and the story is not so straightforward.
Volume growth for flagship brand Parachute is unimpressive. Saffola edible oils, in particular, saw a marginal decline in volumes this quarter. This poses a long-term risk. Relying too heavily on slow-growing core categories can cap the company’s future potential.
Marico recognised this early in 2017 and became one of the first FMCG players to aggressively acquire D2C brands.
A D2C strategy may not be an immediate priority for an FMCG behemoth like Hindustan Unilever (HUL), whose revenue in the year ended March 2021 was ~Rs 47,000 crore ($6.2 billion). But for smaller companies like Emami and Marico, having a D2C portfolio is crucial.
For one, these legacy companies have a huge blindspot when it comes to figuring out how to sell to Instagram- and YouTube-influenced twenty-somethings, who are more comfortable browsing an online catalogue than walking into a store. Nearly half of India’s 1.3 billion population is under 25 years old. A D2C portfolio is also a way to reduce their dependence on one or two business verticals, or to make up for their lacklustre revenue growth in recent years.

What started as a strategic move is now becoming a mutually beneficial trend between FMCG giants and digital-native startups, especially in the food space, where scaling remains a significant challenge.
Gross margins in food, at around 40%, are also lower than beauty-and-personal-care’s 70–80%, according to three industry veterans. “This means that while a lipstick brand can spend as much as 50% of its revenue on marketing, an oats brand doesn’t have that luxury,” said Moolayil.
Offline distribution is another headache. “This is where 75–80% of the market is, and to scale, you have to ensure you’re there,” said Sampath. Yoga Bar, for instance, has grown 7X since the acquisition by ITC, with access to over 2.5 million kirana touchpoints. It now ranks first in protein bars and second in cereals, only after Kellogg’s, Sampath claimed.
The art of building a D2C food brand to be acquired, [The Ken](https://the-ken.com/story/the-art-of-building-a-d2c-food-brand-to-be-acquired/)
Where FMCG companies solve for scale, D2C brands bring the advantage of experimentation and diversification.
Between enough inventory for that many outlets and a marketing blitz to create curiosity about the product, the company would spend Rs 30–40 crore ($3.9–5.3 million) on the launch, the senior executive adds. “But a D2C brand will only have, say, 3,000 pieces of initial inventory and they will spend Rs 25,000–50,000 ($330–660) on online ads.” In all, a few lakhs is all a D2C brand needs to get a launch off the ground.
“Whether the product quality is good or not, all they care about is what is trending,” says the senior executive. “The reputational risk is much lower for them.” Given the really high stakes, FMCG companies will spend three-quarters of a year trying to gauge how consumers will react to a new product and what kind of sales volume they need to prepare for.
Marico Emami’s quest to catch the D2C lightning in a bottle, [The Ken](https://the-ken.com/story/marico-emamis-quest-to-catch-the-d2c-lightning-in-a-bottle/)
So, diversifying in-house is not an easy thing to do. Marico learned this firsthand. Its early attempt to diversify organically, through the launch of Set Wet, saw modest success, but the real breakthrough came from investing in D2C brands that already had a strong digital presence, such as Just Herbs and Plix. The impact of this strategy is visible in the investor presentation, which highlights the clear difference in expected accounting rate of return (ARR) between its in-house personal care brands and the ones it acquired.
| Source: Marico's investor presentation |
Now, Marico is sharpening its focus further. With its recent acquisition of a 60% majority stake in Cosmix, following earlier investments in Plix and True Elements, the company is steadily expanding its footprint in the healthy-food category.
Together, with its many adopted brands, Marico is laying the foundation of what it thinks will make it a top-tier consumer-goods company.

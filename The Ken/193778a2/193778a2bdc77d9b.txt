Authenticity is the glue that holds brand stories together. What a company does isn’t as exciting as why or how they do it 
 By Ruhi Kandhari https://the-ken.com/writers/ruhikandharigmail-com/

“This product is too complex…”

“The subject is too dry…”

“It is only really meant for businesses, not individual consumers…”

All of these are things I’ve heard from business leaders sceptical about the value that storytelling brings to the table. 

In fact, these are three of the most common reasons I’ve come across for why some businesses don’t take storytelling very seriously. 

The assumption is: a good, compelling narrative works for advertising and branding fun, simple consumer products—in segments such as skin care, beverages, electronics, etc. But it doesn’t really matter for a B2B product. After all, which company is deciding on vendors based on how emotion-laden their brand story is? 

Well, in today’s edition of Inciting Incident https://the-ken.com/inciting-incident/, I want to share an example of a 60-year-old company that has constantly defied this myth. 

If you are not a child or adult who is into crafts, you probably won’t find its product range very fun or exciting. In fact, it is likely that a large portion of its consumers only ever “buy” its products indirectly, through a contractor of some kind. 

But if you live in India, I’m quite sure that its name and that of its most famous product actually evokes some emotion in you. 

I’m talking about Pidilite Industries, and Fevicol.  

The product is only the first thing you build

Pidilite is a pretty good example of a company that has doubled down on the power of narratives for decades. 

Take, for instance, annual reports—a document meant to build relationships with a company’s investors/shareholders. Indian companies rarely use these releases to foster any meaningful emotional connections. But Pidilite, which has a market capitalisation of over Rs 1.5 lakh crore (US$17.75 billion), did just that in its latest annual report https://www.pidilite.com/content/dam/pidilitecorporatewebsite/financial/annual-reports/Annual%20Report%202023-24.pdf, where it commemorated the hundred birth anniversary of its founder Balvant Parekh. 

The report has a lot of memorabilia, but what I want to point you towards is a diary entry written by Parekh before he founded the company—a note that not only communicates where Pidilite actually came from, but is also an intimate snapshot into Parekh’s inner conflicts, the questions he wrestled with when making tough decisions.

Source: Pidilite Annual Report

Over the next several pages, the document lists the lessons the founder of the company ingrained in its culture, before turning the lens towards what the people who once worked with Parekh thought about him. 

Now, many companies do go beyond ‘what we do’ in their communications, but when they share profiles of leaders, it is generally to paint a more guarded picture—templatised, tropey, and rarely with any hint of vulnerability. 

But templatised and tropey can’t create emotional connections. If a deep look into the heart of the person who built the company is one way to that, then ‘why’ and ‘how’ are others. 

Why and how over what 

Here’s author and business mentor Simon Sinek’s model for the most impactful ways a business can communicate. A concept he calls the ‘Golden Circle’, where impactfulness increases as you get closer to the centre. 

And telling your customers what you do is really the least effective way of going about it. 

Instead, he believes that getting into the how and the why is how you communicate with conviction.

Pidilite is quite accomplished at doing this, especially for its flagship brand of adhesives—Fevicol. In an article titled ‘How Fevicol Became The Ultimate Adhesive Solution For India https://worldbrandaffairs.com/how-fevicol-became-the-ultimate-adhesive-solution-for-india/’ published in World Brand Affairs last year, the author concludes that it is the ability to craft memorable ads that has made Pidilite’s marketing strategy tick. 

The brand has always focused on creating a connection with its customers by showing them how Fevicol can help them solve their problems… 

And this is true even in the B2B business, where its goal has been to highlight how ‘dependable’ it can be as a vendor. 

For instance, The Economic Times wrote last year https://cio.economictimes.indiatimes.com/news/brand-solution/pidilite-uses-salesforce-to-build-powerful-apps-to-deepen-contractor-and-customer-bonds/100837246 about how, while upgrading its sales processes, Pidilite took an audience-first approach even in its B2B relationships. The automation has meant that Pidilite’s first response time has improved by almost 90%, but its conversations with customers are also now even more personalised. It has also focused on showing how it can make things easier https://www.manufacturingtodayindia.com/8750-pidilite-forms-new-bonds-with-customers for its B2B customers by rolling out a platform called Genie, whose entire purpose is to make order placement and fulfilment as seamless as possible—always a huge selling point for any enterprise business. Last year, Pidilite announced https://www.rprealtyplus.com/allied/pidilites-online-b2b-platform-genie-crosses-1000-crore-in-sales-110615.html that Genie had crossed over Rs 1,000 crore in sales.

Pidilite’s record isn’t spotless, sure, which is to be expected from any business that has been in operation for several decades. 

But the one thing it has done very well is understand the power of a well-told, emotionally appealing brand story https://www.marketingmonk.so/p/how-fevicol-sticks-to-its-customers-hearts, even for its B2B operations.

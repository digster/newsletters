Good Morning Ishan,

Welcome to the first weekend of the year. The holiday after the holidays. I hope 2019 has treated you well so far.

Someone reminded me today of a grand corporate exercise that happened at the last place I worked, a newspaper. Consultants were hired, workshops were held, all to instil in us a sense of tidiness—the famous Japanese 5S system (Sort, Set in order, Shine, Standardise, and Sustain, Wikipedia tells me). No eating at our desks, no mountains of paper, no clutter anymore, we were told. Sounds good, on paper.

I mention this because, as is the tradition for us now, the first Saturday of the month we have for you the latest from our long-running Simplicity Paradigm series, by our stock marketeering experts Saurabh Mukherjea and Anupam Gupta. Decluttering is at the core of their philosophy.

We sneered at the 5S thing back in the day, and (like most every expensive, “productivity-enhancing” HR exercise) it died in a matter of weeks. My desk once again was covered by dozens of A3 printouts, orange and banana peels, discarded peanut packets. Victory.

All that, ironically enough, changed on its own. I came around to decluttering; my desk at The Ken’s Delhi office is spotless, and it works so much better. The expensive consultants had a good idea after all—where they went wrong was that they made decluttering, well, complicated. A few Ss too many. Saurabh and Anupam, in contrast, put simplicity front and centre (the series' name gives it away).

The other thing that sets their Simplicity Paradigm apart is that they break it down into a series of concepts, and delve deep into each one. It’s a bit of journey, and today’s stop is, wait for it, spirituality.

Before you jump to conclusions, no, there is no mumbo jumbo here. What they’re talking about, essentially, is shifting focus away from material things and relationships—at least, all that which does not add value to your life, i.e. the clutter. “It is impossible to make a decision which is just based on numbers, because for tomorrow’s world we have no numbers, no ‘future facts’. Our decisions, therefore, have to balance the emotional and the rational,” they say. Which is where their construct of spirituality comes in.

It’s an interesting concept, to say the least. Give it a read and let me know what you think.

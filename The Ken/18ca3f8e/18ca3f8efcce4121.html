Good Morning Ishan,

Hope you had a great Christmas! This is the first year since 2020 that we can reflect on without the long shadow of the pandemic.

In a sharp departure from the days of Covid, companies that stared at obliteration three years ago rebounded with aplomb in 2023—both PVR Inox Leisure, India’s largest operator of cinemas, and Interglobe Aviation (Indigo), the largest airline in the country, had their best quarter ever. And businesses that took off like a rocket during the pandemic came back down to earth just as swiftly. Such was the story of edtechs (even while keeping aside the train wreck that was Byju’s), social-commerce startups, and those offering short-video apps.

This made 2023 a particularly eventful year for business journalists. And writers at The Ken sank their teeth into a range of companies and subjects—from how India bent over backwards to convince an American memory-chip giant to set up shop here to why the Mamaearth IPO signals the end of the direct-to-consumer (D2C) gold rush.

We’ve been able to do what we do for seven years because you, our subscribers, value our journalism. So, thank you!

It goes without saying that no year-ender would be complete without the features you loved. So here are our ten most read and gifted long-form stories from India this year:

India’s startup workplaces confront the rise of the ‘unhireables’
Overfunded startups led to overvalued talent just after the pandemic. Then, when these companies suddenly didn’t have too much money to throw around, many tech professionals found that they were rendered unwanted, as Arundhati argues in this important story.

The domino effect of Adani stocks’ $US51B meltdown
Anand and I dig deep into the ramifications of US short-seller Hindenburg Research’s explosive report on the Adani Group, alleging stock-price manipulation and accounting fraud.

When credit cards made airport lounges too much of a good thing
In her sharp story on another unmissable phenomenon, Arundhati lays out how banks have come to regret their decision to make free access to airport lounges a big draw of their credit cards.

You can retire at 40. And that’s a lie
Linkedin bros don’t tire of telling you how doable it is to retire early. But Anand takes a sledgehammer to that idea in his analysis.

A job with McKinsey, Bain, or BCG trumps everything. Or it used to
The “big three” strategy-consulting firms have for years been the most sought-after employers at India’s top engineering and business schools. But ask my colleague Vanita, and she’ll tell you that’s no longer true.

A CA and an MBA walk into a boardroom. Who emerges as CFO?
For years, chartered accountants were considered the finance world’s elite. It’s a new reality these days. Vanita makes a strong case for why they are losing out to MBAs.

Why a mere 2km stretch threw the Bengaluru Metro into chaos

No big city in India has worse public transport than Bengaluru. So imagine its residents’ frustration when the addition of a tiny stretch to its fledgling metro network led to stations getting overcrowded and riders spilling out of trains.

Young Indians are driving credit growth by riding premium, living swanky. But there’s a catch
Keen to buy luxury homes and pricey cars, younger consumers are responsible for a surge in loans. But Rounak also looks at the fault lines in this trend.

Elite UK schools land in India, get schooled in local realities
As China grapples with its worst economic slowdown in years, renowned British schools, such as Harrow and Wellington, have found a lucrative outpost in India. But, Alifiya reports, they didn’t expect to deal with fussy parents and strict state laws.

Lenskart’s CEO and chief people officer Peyush Bansal has a people problem
Bansal was the reason the eyewear retailer saw many high-level exits. But he also took it upon himself to fix that very problem. In a nuanced profile, Aayush captures what it takes to steer a $4.5 billion-worth company in flux.

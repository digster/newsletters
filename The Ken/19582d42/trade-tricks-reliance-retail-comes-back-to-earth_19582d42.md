Trade Tricks
Tue, 11 Mar 25
A weekly newsletter about the biggest changes in commerce—focusing on shifts that matter to you..
Good Morning Ishan,
A few months ago, as I was thinking aloud about Reliance Retail Ventures’ much-anticipated [listing](https://the-ken.com/story/no-ipo-talk-at-agm-yet-reliance-retail-will-be-stock-pundits-biggest-puzzle/) in a chat with an equity analyst, he interjected to correct me on a tiny detail.
He was right.
RRVL sells everything—from Parle-G biscuits to Apple iPhones to Bottega Veneta bags. And it runs almost 19,000 stores and half-a-dozen e-commerce ventures. Plus, it has made over 20 acquisitions since the pandemic—including e-pharmacy Netmeds, furniture seller Urban Ladder, grocery-subscription service Milkbasket, and local-search platform Just Dial.
Forming a neat little bow on RRVL’s unwieldiness are its 75 subsidiaries and associates, including Reliance Brands Ltd (which is the India franchisee for a number of global fashion brands) and Tira Beauty Ltd, which runs an eponymous online store for personal-care products.
India doesn’t have a retailer that even remotely matches RRVL in size or complexity—nor will it have one in the foreseeable future.
This is why Reliance Retail, owned by billionaire Mukesh Ambani, has featured more often than any other company in this newsletter.
No retailer turned the pandemic to its advantage quite so deftly as Reliance Retail. It launched e-tailer Jiomart and bought e-pharmacy Netmeds, furniture seller Urban Ladder, e-grocer Milkbasket, and online-classifieds platform Just Dial. Then it purchased the Indian unit of wholesaler Metro Cash & Carry. Reliance Retail even managed to [hoover up](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2022-02-27/reliance-takes-over-200-future-group-stores-business-standard) Future Group’s retail outlets despite its messy and abortive takeover attempt.
What’s more, armed with US$8.2 billion raised from big-name private-equity firms and sovereign-wealth funds between 2020 and 2023, it opened stores at a blistering pace.
In FY23 alone, Reliance added almost 2,850 outlets. That’s more than the entire footprint of rival retailers.
But that’s all in Reliance’s rear-view mirror now.
The following financial year, the company opened just under 800 stores. Kotak Institutional Equities, a broker, expects it to close the current fiscal, which ends this month, with a mere 350 additions.
Also, perhaps for the first time in its history, the total area Reliance Retail’s stores occupy will shrink—from 79 million sq ft to 78 million sq ft. This is not the only fall that was previously unthinkable. In the quarter ended September, Reliance Retail’s mammoth top line [slid](https://www.livemint.com/companies/company-results/reliance-retail-q2-results-net-profit-rises-1-3-to-2-836-crore-adds-464-new-stores-during-the-quarter-11728913152462.html) too.
What was once the [most formidable](https://the-ken.com/story/why-investors-are-buying-what-reliance-retail-is-selling/) part of Reliance Industries is now the shakiest.
Sure, consumers have over the past year tightened their purse strings. That has hurt retailers across the board, save value-apparel chain Zudio. An Economic Times [analysis](https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/industry/services/retail/reliance-dmart-and-other-top-retailers-go-easy-on-store-launches-as-slowdown-hurts/articleshow/118742717.cms?from=mdr) of 12 fast-food operators, grocery, and clothing retailers revealed that they opened just three outlets a day in 2024, compared to 10 the previous year. Last year, the head of Nestlé India [referred](https://www.financialexpress.com/business/industry-shrinking-middle-class-hitting-fmcg-firms-nestle-india-3646982/) to the impact of a “shrinking middle class”.
But Reliance Retail’s woes also have a source closer to home. It expanded faster than it could persuade consumers to keep walking into its nearly 19,200 stores, which Ambani acknowledged to investors, according to a recent Bloomberg [report](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-03-04/ambanis-overhaul-retail-unit-in-quest-of-125-billion-valuation) last week.
To fix things, Ambani, together with daughter Isha Ambani, is overseeing job and cost cuts, the people said, asking not to be named because the discussions are private. That entails limiting physical store expansion, slashing marketing budgets, merging unit[ Reliance Brands Ltd.](https://www.bloomberg.com/quote/1008481D:IN) with the umbrella retail entity and reviewing global brand partnerships. The hiring of employees above a certain pay scale also now requires approval from the chairman’s office, they added.
The timing couldn’t have been worse for Reliance. It needs to give its investors an exit at a lofty valuation sooner rather than later. And equity analysts don’t seem to be quite so enamoured of it as they used to be. Ambit Capital, a broker, recently valued it at US$50 billion. This could be the canary in a coal mine for other brokers, who have routinely pegged Reliance Retail’s worth at US$100 billion or more.
Last year, Reliance Industries tried to give Reliance Retail [a neat little boost](https://the-ken.com/tradetricks/mukesh-ambani-is-using-jio-financial-to-beef-up-reliance-retail-before-its-listing/) through another subsidiary, Jio Financial Services.
It’s still early days for JFS, but Ambani pulled another rabbit out of his hat last week when JFS sought shareholder approval for one of its units to buy “customer premises equipment / devices and telecom equipment” worth Rs 36,000 crore (US$4.3 billion) from Reliance Retail (RRL) over two years and lease them to Reliance Jio’s customers. The announcement didn’t specify the kind of equipment, but Reuters said the deal could include mobile phones and routers.

The deal with JFS won’t hurt for sure. It will add a neat 6% to RRVL’s top line. Even better is the possibility of a two-year deal becoming a multi-year arrangement, which would mean predictable, recurring revenue. Not what a typical retailer can count on.
But 10 months on, there’s been [no update](https://the-ken.com/long_and_short/the-curious-entry-of-jio-financial-services-into-the-nifty-50/) from Jio Financial on this. Reliance Retail is the opposite of Jio Financial, as I [wrote](https://the-ken.com/story/no-ipo-talk-at-agm-yet-reliance-retail-will-be-stock-pundits-biggest-puzzle/) last year.
When Jio Financial Services Ltd was spun off from Reliance Industries and listed last August, analysts and institutional investors faced a conundrum: how does one value a company that is yet to get off the ground?
With RRVL, the stock market will ask: what should the worth of an entity with a dizzying assemblage of businesses be?
The question will now be slightly different: what should the worth of an entity with a dizzying assemblage of slowing businesses be?

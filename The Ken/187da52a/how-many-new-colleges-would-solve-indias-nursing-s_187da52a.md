To treat the issue of an acute nurse shortage, the Indian healthcare system is staring at a potentially wrong diagnosis.
It's well-known that India's nurses are underpaid, overworked, and underappreciated and, therefore, are prone to migrating to other countries. So much so that if India needs 34.5 nurses for every 10,000 of its people, it only has 24.5 nurses.
To fix this gap, the Union cabinet approved the plan to set up 157 new nursing colleges on 26 April. The colleges will be established alongside the existing 5,000 medical colleges to increase the availability of qualified nurses.
But guess what? The government is implementing this step at a time when the country is running out of even nursing educators.
The regulatory body for nurses and nursing education mandates having a teacher-to-student ratio of 1:10. But private nursing schools often recruit only four or five nursing faculty and bring more people in to match the regulator-prescribed ratio during inspections, finds out Vanita, the author of today's story.
One of her sources told her that in 2016-17, nursing colleges would close a vacancy for a nursing professor within three months. Now, it takes more than a year to find a good qualified teacher.

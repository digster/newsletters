Being called an ‘early stage startup’ is usually a sign of promise or agility. But for a 10-year-old company, valued at $16 billion, it isn't what you want to hear.

With $1 billion and many false starts in the bank, Paytm finds itself back at the drawing board, desperate to figure fresh growth paths that’d justify its valuation.

Its largest investor and mentor, Ant Financial, which is aiming for a valuation of $200-300 billion in an upcoming dual listing in Hong Kong and Shanghai, has made it apparent in its filings that Paytm is not just another investment. It’s a joint venture. Ant Financial is what Paytm set out to be; the former did everything it could to make Paytm India’s Ant Financial.

In 2020, Paytm’s revenue grew 1.5%, a crushing fall from 2018 when the fintech was firing on all cylinders, recording 398% revenue growth. Payments was Paytm’s game to lose, and it did— today it’s a distant fourth behind Google Pay, PhonePe, and even Amazon Pay when it comes to UPI transactions.

Now, Paytm has turned to another page in Ant’s playbook—financial services. It had two good starts with lending and mutual funds—Paytm Postpaid and Paytm Money. Former executives say if there was one company that could build a unicorn in lending, it was Paytm. (Past tense, already?)

In her deeply reported story today, Arundhati brings you a worm’s-eye view of Paytm’s financial services, its many missteps, and the still-holding promise of redemption. Throwing cashbacks at financial services won’t build a product, Paytm has learnt the hard way.

Paytm Money will now focus on stockbroking, but at the cost of mutual funds. Promising as this may sound, its success will not depend on the size of its user base so much as the quality. And that is where its financial services dreams risk coming undone, says Arundhati.

In corporate strategy they say, if you are not lead dog, the view remains the same. Riding on Ant’s vision, how far is Paytm able to see?

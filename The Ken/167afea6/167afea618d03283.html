Good Morning Ishan,
 
It’s that time of year again. Specifically, the last days of one. And like most arbitrary milestones on the calendar (birthdays, festivals, New Year’s, you name it), we associate certain things with the year-end—and one of those things is a tendency to lean back and reflect on the year gone by.
 
For us at The Ken though, preparations for a new year are already underway: What new thing should we do? How do we start 2019 with a bang? Do we have stories lined up for early January, before people start disappearing on holiday?
 
Curious about the answers to those questions? Well, you’ll just have to wait and see. In the meantime, why not check out what we’ve been doing over the past week? There was some amount of reflecting, but also, I’m glad to say, a fair bit of dissecting.
 
The best example of that is probably Sumanth’s Tuesday piece on OYO, a company he refers to rather delightfully as one of SoftBank’s “Trojan pigs”. A startup that’s been force-fed capital and global dreams even as it’s yet to create a viable business at home.
 
More dissection happens in Arundhati’s story on Ezetap co-founder Abhijit Bose’s move to WhatsApp. Founders leave the companies they start all the time, but, well, usually not because someone walks up with a job offer. And Bose’s sudden exit comes at what may be a make-or-break moment for the startup, writes Arundhati.
 
Then we have Sidhartha’s Wednesday story, in which he takes a look at what happened to MoneyOnMobile. A payments company, it started off this year looking like it was just a step away from profitability, but not for long. Now, the US-based chairman says the company has been hijacked by his Indian partners, there are accusations of fraud and forgery all around. What went wrong? Read on and find out (it’s our free story of the week).
 
Right after that, on Thursday, we had Ruhi writing on the spa chain O2. For years, there seemed to be no end to its growth—over 120 spas at its peak, O2 became the largest spa chain in all of Asia, and had even raised over $10 million in private equity funding. But this autumn, things went south, and the company has had to shut dozens of spas. Smaller, nimbler competitors are eating into its share of the pie.
 
Leaning a little to the other side on the dissect-reflect scale, we have Ashish’s Friday interview with Satyen Kothari, the founder of fintech firm Cube. And also of Citrus Pay, the payments company bought by Naspers-owned PayU in 2016—a deal that came at a time when Citrus was in the middle of an existential crisis. Kothari talks, at length and very candidly, about the early days at Citrus, about the mistakes made along the way, and about what the future holds for the payments business in India.
 
And finally, there’s our Sunday piece, which is firmly in the reflect camp. In the second part of our weekly Going Home series for this month, journalist and writer Prem Panicker takes us through a series of homes, across cities and countries, looking at just what “home” means. And what it feels like to not have one. It’s a lovely piece, and also a free read, so do read and share.
 
Well, that’s all for now. Have a good weekend (and come back tomorrow for our next Going Home story).
 
Cheers,

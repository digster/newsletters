Good Morning Ishan,

Earlier this month, a private equity fund run by Motilal Oswal decided to invest $32 million in a two-decade-old Bengaluru company that has been primarily bootstrapped till now—MolBio Diagnostics. 

Now, a hard-nosed PE fund investing such a large sum in an old and bootstrapped company would be odd even in the best of times. And these are not the best of times. Private funding for companies of all shapes and sizes has fallen off a cliff.

The reason Motilal Oswal was interested in MolBio was because of TrueNatTM, the world’s first handheld, battery-operated real-time PCR device. PCR, or polymerase chain reaction, is the fastest method to check the genetic material of viruses. Nearly all of the tests for Covid-19 being done globally at scale are PCR-based. And TrueNat is perhaps the only field test for screening available anywhere today. 

It can be used at the airport, in villages, or any other public place to give results within an hour. There are already over 1,000 such devices running in public and private healthcare facilities across India, to test for tuberculosis. The same devices will be able to screen for Covid-19 once MolBio’s new kits are validated and approved by regulators.

Meanwhile, Xcyton, another Bengaluru based company has a lab-based PCR test that can only detect the presence of Covid-19 in samples. And in its absence, can identify other viruses like influenza A or B, SARS, MERS, rhinovirus or three other types of coronavirus—CoV 229E, CoV OC43, CoV NL63. 

As India gears up to confront Stage 3 of Covid-19 infections (community transmissions), the number of tests will have to scale dramatically. MolBio and Xcyton are among 16 different tests being tested by India’s National Institute of Virology (NIV) in Pune, Maharashtra. 

This could be their breakout moment.

But even with their help, India has miles to go unless it starts thinking strategically about the reagents used in these tests. They’re all imported. “Stockpiling of reagents will begin now as the whole world is looking for reagents,” says one of the people in today’s story.

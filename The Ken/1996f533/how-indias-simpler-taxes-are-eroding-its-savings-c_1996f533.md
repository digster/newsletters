Good Morning Ishan,
Updating my PPF passbook is one of my core childhood memories. I’d walk into the SBI branch near my school, hand in a rectangular blue-and-white booklet at the window, and see them stamp it with their approval. Though I didn’t understand what was happening, it gave me a rush—some combination of control, safety, and power.
Because, like my parents had explained, we were doing the “smart” thing by squirrelling away money for the future. My future.
But as people flock from the old tax regime to the new one, such long-term saving habits are no longer incentivised.
By offering deductions for instruments such as PPF, ELSS, NPS, and insurance, the old tax regime ingrained saving habits in countless young and old individuals. In contrast, the new tax regime may boast lower tax slabs and ease of filing, but sidesteps the same nudge towards a sustained saving culture and well-rounded investments.
The result? Instead of putting money in long-term saving instruments, people are spending it on, say, a new car, and paying less taxes to boot. Many are flocking to “mutual funds as [if it were a] fixed deposit”—a risky proposition given over-valued public markets. Combined with inflation and stressed incomes, household savings have dropped to 22.8% of personal disposable income—the lowest in 27 years.

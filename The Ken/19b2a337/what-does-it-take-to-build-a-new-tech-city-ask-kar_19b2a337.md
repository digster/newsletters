Good Morning Ishan,
There is a structural shift taking place in the way India’s tech and industrial capacity is organised and built out.
Since the beginning of 2024, Hyderabad has added more global capability centres (GCCs) than Bengaluru; Tamil Nadu has overtaken Karnataka in electronics exports; and mega investments such as Google’s $15 billion data-centre project and Foxconn’s electronics-assembly plants are heading to Visakhapatnam.
There’s a logic to these choices—one that makes a lot of sense for Telangana, Andhra Pradesh, and Tamil Nadu. Every city in this geography has a specialisation, and the cities within a state work in tandem to form tech hubs.
All of this is taking place while Karnataka has been running its Beyond Bengaluru programme since 2021 in hopes of directing talent and investment to tier-2 cities. But the state is being outclassed by its neighbours.
In today’s story, Mrunmayee covers the way tech hubs are being planned and built in India—and more importantly, what it takes to get these hubs up and running. [Read it](https://the-ken.com/story/tech-city/) to find out what’s happening now and what the future will look like.
Of the 500 largest companies by market value in 2008, only 35 have consistently grown their revenue in line with India’s nominal GDP while also outperforming the Nifty 50 index. More striking is that 27 of these, over 75%, were already market leaders in 2008. 11 belong to the influential business groups that form 15–20% of India’s GDP.

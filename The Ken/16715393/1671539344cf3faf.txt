Good Morning Ishan,

Of all the things one can buy online these days, I never thought sewage treatment plants would have a thriving market to themselves. A grey market for grey water, as it were.

But that’s what it is, complemented by online housing forums where desperate residents are posting queries, seeking advice on brands to buy or not to buy. With no mandated standards, builders—who are tasked with installing STPs at the time of construction—opt for cheaper systems. They fail far too early in the life of a building. And now with state governments and civic authorities strictly enforcing polluter-pays principle in wastewater treatment, city dwellers are forced to a new aqueous awakening. 

It’s a stinker. Some 78% of domestic sewage is left untreated in the country. It is either dumped into rivers and lakes or let out into fields, where it leaches into the soil, contaminating our groundwater. 70% of groundwater is already contaminated. At this rate, 21 Indian cities are well on their way to having no access to groundwater by 2020.

In its rush to mandate decentralisation, the utilities have paid little attention to laying out any detailed standards for these machines. So, in a jugaad nation, what do you expect? A market full of small operators who’ve broken away from bigger firms and use their basic knowledge to assemble these domestic STPs.

Few realise that water runs in unforgiving ecological loops. What you give is what you ultimately get back.

Olina’s story discusses how decentralised STPs are certainly the way forward—many more governments are considering making it mandatory—but the retail plug-and-play systems that have flooded the market will do more harm.

Good Morning Ishan,
Ask any football fan why Francesco Totti, Carles Puyol, or Paul Scholes are legends of the game, and you’ll get an answer along these lines: “They weren’t just world-class players. For their entire careers, they lived and breathed for their boyhood clubs.”
They just don’t make them like that anymore, right? Today’s footballers are mercenaries—chasing clubs that massage their egos with lucrative contracts.
Still, who can blame them? Loyalty is a virtue, but self-interest is only rational—as the likes of Hindustan Unilever, Dabur, and Larsen and Toubro are finding out.
Companies built on the backs of lifelong employees need a new playbook for an age where youth are seeking “a job, not a career”, as one chief human resources officer told Debanjali. That’s why firms are dishing out perks like more flexible workdays, and setting up management one-on-ones to hear Gen Z voices.
After all, what’s the alternative—spend resources and cash on plugging holes? Not an option when failing to keep up can be an existential threat.
In today’s [story](https://the-ken.com/story/how-to-build-companies-that-last-when-their-employees-dont/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter), Debanjali chronicles how some of the most rigid employers in the country are now changing with the times.

‘D2C’ brands are finally growing into their name. There are clear signs in property-leasing data

You know, I love railing against the term “direct-to-consumer” (D2C) as it’s used in India. Not because I have anything against it, but because it’s wrong. Plain and simple. 

The origin of my pet peeve https://the-ken.com/tradetricks/boat-allbirds-and-what-it-means-to-be-a-d2c-brand/ was in wearables brand Boat’s attempt at going public in 2022 and what its IPO document revealed.

Considering it’s a D2C brand, most, if not all, of Boat’s revenue should be coming from its own website, right?

Nope.

Marketplaces such as Amazon and Flipkart were responsible for 86% of Boat’s sales in the year ended March 2021. And Boat depends on 51 distributors and 180 sub-distributors to reach 23,000 retail stores, which brought in another 12%. Boat’s own website accounted for just 2% of its sales.

At that point, you could replace Boat with any other “D2C” brand—in FMCG, fashion, beauty, or electronics—and the story wouldn’t be any different. Instead of selling mostly through an independent retail store or chains like Croma, Dmart, or Pantaloons, they rely on Amazon or Myntra. 

They reach their consumers through an intermediary, as has been the case for most products for years. In other words, there is nothing direct about that relationship. 

But that is now less true than it has ever been. 

It’s not that people suddenly love shopping on the apps of different brands. Instead, several brands are now increasingly found elsewhere too. And I’m, of course, not talking about online marketplaces or department stores. 

Digital-first brands accounted for a fifth of all retail space leased in India in the first six months of 2025, according to a report published by property firm CBRE last month.

And these firms naturally want to be where the footfall is highest.

It was only a matter of time before internet brands began to reluctantly accept the inevitability of physical retail. When it went public in 2023, cosmetics maker Honasa Consumer made sure to point out in its IPO filing that selling offline was more profitable. 

Honasa’s attempts to replicate the distribution might of FMCG giants have been fraught with trouble, as my colleague Aakriti wrote https://the-ken.com/story/mamaearth-sold-investors-on-its-fmcg-dreams-consumers-had-other-plans/ last year. 

On 14 November, in its second-quarter results for the 2025 fiscal year, the company reported a 9% drop in sales, down to Rs 417 crore (US$50 million), and a Rs 15 crore loss—the first since its initial public offering (IPO) last November. 

The company estimated a one-time hit of Rs 50 crore due to the inventory losses. But that was optimistic: the real damage was closer to Rs 70 crore, as CEO and co-founder Varun Alagh admitted in an interview with The Economic Times. 

In a November analyst call, Alagh acknowledged that Honasa was able to build a Rs 1,000 crore brand with its online strategy, but replicating the same “playbook” in offline channels to hit Rs 2,000-crore revenue did not work.

Honasa still gets the majority of its revenue from e-commerce, but for its flagship brand, Mamaearth, 60% of the sales are expected to be offline. Honasa had 85 exclusive brand outlets before its IPO. Now, it has 140. Fashion e-tailer Nykaa has quadrupled its store count since 2021 to 240. 

Eyewear retailer Lenskart has added 600 shops in the past two years alone to take its India footprint to over 2,000 outlets. (It operates 700 stores overseas.)

None of these brands can afford to be exclusive to their own stores and apps, unlike apparel retailers Zara and Westside. And most of their customers will continue to buy their products from multi-brand outlets or apps. But to have at least some of them walk into a wide network of outlets carrying the name of the label is still worth something.

Good morning Ishan, what a non-summer we’ve had this year. I’ve always remembered 1 June as the approximate day that the monsoon hits the Kerala coast. This year, it arrived on 24 May.

The truncated summer and early rains (both pre-monsoon and monsoon) have played havoc with mango trees. Farmers are suffering. And the few dozens of fruits we had in our own backyard tree have started yellowing and shriveling without attaining full size.

On the other hand, Bengaluru’s lakes are brimming.

I must say I’m conflicted. I love the rain, but not at the cost of a proper and normal summer.

Let’s see what the next year brings.

Speaking of bringing, I remember my dad bringing week-old issues of international news magazines like Time, Newsweek, and The Economist from his office when I was young. I used to devour the first two (I was a kid in the pre-globalisation era of the ‘80s, The Economist was too dense and boring for me). They were my windows to the world.

I say this because things have come full circle, and I am now the one bringing him back issues of The Economist each week. (Newsweek stopped being a print magazine in 2012. Time, a shell of itself, is alive because of a billionaire benefactor-owner.)

I admire The Economist for standing the test of time. It’s one thing to hear when a newspaper or magazine was originally founded. It’s another when you’ve experienced its continuing relevance across a span of 40 years. It’s something to aspire to.

Learning (and perhaps overcompensating) from our first event, where many attendees told us they wished there were more questions than narrative-driven conversations, we swung the other way a bit too much. Many attendees told us that our questions-first style felt a little discombobulated.

1. Post-victorems and action replays

Arguably, our strongest thread across these years has been the concept of mental time-travel. Within that, we’ve discussed the powerful concept of “pre-mortems”.

A premortem is the hypothetical opposite of a postmortem. A postmortem in a medical setting allows health professionals and the family to learn what caused a patient’s death. Everyone benefits except, of course, the patient. A premortem in a business setting comes at the beginning of a project rather than the end, so that the project can be improved rather than autopsied. Unlike a typical critiquing session, in which project team members are asked what might go wrong, the premortem operates on the assumption that the “patient” has died, and so asks what did go wrong. The team members’ task is to generate plausible reasons for the project’s failure.

Who wants a post-mortem when we can do a pre-mortem and never die, right?

Have you heard of the “post-victorem”, though?

I recently came across it in an online post, which for the life of me I cannot find now. So, I will attempt to explain it from memory. (If you find a reference, please do email it to me. I’ll update this post to include it.)

Unlike a pre-mortem, where teams attempt to imagine the various things that might have gone wrong (leading to the “death” of their project), a post-victorem is where teams imagine the things they did that led to absolute success (“victory”).

Now, you might wonder, aren’t they the same? If you can brainstorm and imagine the potential causes of a project’s failure, you can then fix them. And if you do, the project will be a success. Right?

I don’t think so.

If you fix the things that might cause a project to fail, then all you have done is prevent its failure. Which isn’t anywhere near the same as making it successful. Between success and failure lies the crowded middle where the vast majority of our projects and initiatives exist—middling performance.

By anchoring strongly to the concept of an outright success, the post-victorem forces you to think about the truly innovative and hard things required to ensure absolute success.

I’ll give you an example.

The Ken started doing live events from March. One of the items in our very first pre-mortems was the possibility of one or more guest speakers accidentally forgetting about the event and missing it.

Our pre-mortem fix was to ensure that I personally spoke with all the speakers on the day of the event to confirm the time and venue.

Doing this ensures that the guest speakers will arrive for the event. But is that the best that we can hope for? Is that success?

No.

Success would mean the speakers have been introduced to each other; have a sense of the format and discussion; have communicated their own strong opinions or beliefs to the moderators; and are looking forward to sharing their opinions.

That is never going to emerge from a pre-mortem.

But it would if you do a post-victorem and ask yourself, “What did we do that made the guest speakers comfortable, eager and prepared for the discussion?”

Of course, a successful event has numerous other elements that must be engineered too. But I used the limited point of guest speaker invitations to show how the opposite of “failing” isn’t “succeeding”. It’s “not failing”.

And there is a world of difference between not failing and succeeding.

Finally, it’s easy to get caught in imagining the various futures so much that we forget about learnings from events in the real world. I like to do that via a constructed “action replay”, sometimes by myself and sometimes with colleagues. We force ourselves to “play back” key events that happened in, say, the last few days or weeks. Then, we can “pause” at places when something went off and ask ourselves why that happened, and what we can do to ensure it doesn’t happen again.

There’s strong evidence that replaying events in our brain is essential to learning. While the brain records and holds what takes place in the moment, the learning from what one has gone through — that is, determining what is important and what lessons should be learned — happens after the fact during periods of quiet reflection.

Also, when we slow things down and reflect, we can be more creative about solving seemingly inscrutable problems. Take, for example, a technique called the “second solution method” that I’ve used in the past. If a group was struggling to come up with options to solve a tough problem, we would brainstorm to identify a list of possible solutions. Before switching to prioritizing, making items specific, etc., we tried to identify all possible options. I found the best approach was to tell the group to take a break and when it reconvened to ask, “What else occurs to you?” Inevitably, this simple question resulted in about 50% more items, often of higher quality. By experimenting, I found that the break that took place between the first and second rounds was more important than the question. A journal is an effective, efficient, private way to take a similar break.

The More Senior Your Job Title, the More You Need to Keep a Journal by Dan Ciampa, Harvard Business Review

Both pre-mortems and post-victorems hack our brain’s regular operations by remixing imagination (of a future that has yet to happen) and reflection (of a past that has yet to happen too).

Mental mind-travel is trippy!

It also helped that I am currently reading The Architecture of Happiness, in which author Alain De Botton poses the question: “What makes a house truly beautiful?”

View in Browser

De Botton has always been a fascinating figure to me, though I’ve been more of a listener than reader of his written words and an admirer of his work that’s available on Youtube. In case you are unfamiliar with The Architecture of Happiness, it is equal parts philosophical and scientific argument about the structures we construct in our lives and what makes them beautiful (and sometimes ugly too). It is a book which covers everything from the mundane to the grandiose. And to back up every take he has, De Botton carefully drops an image of whatever he’s talking about to make his case. 

I think a similar land of possibilities exists in the three wonderful recommendations we have received from the community this week, albeit in vastly different contexts. Please switch to our website or app version to see what our recommenders have to say about the books.

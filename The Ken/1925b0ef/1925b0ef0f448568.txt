Who made the Frauduct Manager? And how can we save them? 
 By Praveen Gopal Krishnan https://the-ken.com/writers/praveenthe-ken-com/

19 min read

Good Morning Ishan,

Today’s edition is a rescue mission—of sorts.

First, let’s introduce who we are saving.

Product Management, as a career, is the only one where the people who do it—even the good ones—often struggle to explain their value to organisations, companies, and even their own families. This is the eternal existential crisis that Product Managers go through everyday. Unlike engineering, sales, design, or marketing, Product Management is a newer, younger profession that sits somewhere in between all of them, without any direct power but saddled with the responsibility of success or the ignominy of failure. PMs are told that they need to treat this ambiguity as a feature, and to make what they want out of it. But for a lot of people, it’s simply a bug.

It’s not just the PMs themselves. Even companies struggle to make sense of how to handle this function. They hire PMs, but don’t understand how to empower them. And this crisis has worsened. Of late, thanks to reduced venture capital, the rise of AI, and other factors, companies are increasingly leaning towards getting rid of the Product Management function altogether. Like I wrote a few weeks ago, some founders have told me privately that they believe that over 90% of Product Management roles aren’t needed. Others believe that designers, engineers, or business people can simply extend a bit and play the role of a PM.

However, PMs are generally smart people with strong survival instincts. So most of them just power through and find meaning in their own ways—both to themselves and to others. Some claim credit for how they influenced business metrics through what they did. Some justify their role as an orchestrator of things. Others tell themselves (sometimes a little too loudly) that they are the CEO of their product. Some others believe their purpose is simply to get things done. The list goes on and on.

The ones who fail at doing this are the Frauduct Managers. 

Frauduct Managers work in Product Management, aren’t convinced that what they do has any meaning or purpose, and yet, they still do it. They are the ones whose lofty expectations of Product Management have been dashed and killed by what they’ve experienced in their workplaces and companies. Ask them if they’d recommend Product Management as a career to a friend, and they’ll likely say no. Once they get comfortable, they are more likely to confess something like, “This whole PM thing feels like a scam… it’s all a fraud”.

Meet the Frauduct Manager.

Some of India’s Frauduct Managers are early in their career. Others have been doing it for years. They are the ones who are trapped, disillusioned, and lost. They are your colleagues, peers, juniors, and bosses. They are all around us, writing Product Requirements Documents, making presentations, and filling out appraisal forms once a year. They wake up on most days not sure what they do, why they do it, and insecure about whether they are even needed in the first place. 

This is a nice story, you may say—where is the data that supports this?

Well, today’s edition is a story about Frauduct Managers. And I’m going to tell it primarily using data.

Here’s the backstory.

A few weeks ago, I wrote that https://the-ken.com/the-nutgraf/who-killed-the-art-of-product-management-in-india/ somebody, or something, had killed the art of Product Management. Essentially, my argument was something has changed in Product Management, particularly in India. Product used to be a blend of art and science. Left-brain and Right-brain. Intuition and numbers. However, the more I looked at the products we use everyday, the more I was convinced that PMs are building products from a scientific, utilitarian, metric-driven perspective, and the artistic, intuitive craftspeople were being purged from the function.

I wanted to find out why this happened, and who was responsible for killing it. I had a few hypotheses. Maybe this was because PMs at organisations today have ready access to much more data than they used to, which encouraged a certain kind of decision-making. Or perhaps it was because engineering bandwidth (and salaries) became much more precious, which created a much higher bar for what got picked and what didn’t. Maybe it was because venture capital went away, so product organisations couldn’t afford to take risks and just became much more intentional about what they did. Or maybe it was all GenZ’s fault, who came in with things like lack of ambition, quiet-quitting, situationships, and God knows what else. 

Like any good PM, I needed data to validate (or invalidate) these hypotheses. 

So, I made a detailed survey, and asked Product Managers in India to tell me what they thought. I asked questions about where they worked, their backgrounds, how they built products, their strengths, beliefs, and much more. Some of the questions were quite personal—like how they rated their managers, or whether they’d recommend Product Management as a career to a friend. 

I shared the survey only to subscribers of The Nutgraf, with strict instructions to not post it on social media. Paradoxically, I didn’t want this survey to go viral. Product Managers are incredible at deluding others about their importance, but they are even better at deluding themselves, especially on Linkedin or Twitter (now X). I wanted honesty, not volume. I’d told my colleagues at The Ken that I’d be happy if I got a couple of hundred responses.

Turns out, I had (once again) underestimated my subscribers. Exactly 1,001 Product Managers in India took the survey. These are individuals who work in Product Management across levels—all the way from Associate Product Managers to SVPs of Product Management. Unsurprisingly, the biggest cohort of respondents fell in the middle, with designations as Product Managers (17%) or Senior Product Managers (18%). We even have representation at the highest level—40+ Chief Product Officers across India’s top companies took this survey.

There was diversity across experience as well. 6% of respondents have been PMs for less than a year. On the other end of the spectrum, 10% are veterans, with over 12 years of Product Management experience.

Product Managers from 400+ sectors and sub-sectors in India are represented in this survey. There’s a long-tail effect here—the top five sectors which represent nearly 40% of all respondents work in either Fintech, E-Commerce, B2B SaaS, Media, or Edtech.

However, as I analysed the results of the survey, I found a cohort of PMs who expressed disillusionment, unhappiness, and confusion about what they did. No matter how I looked at the data, this group of PMs stood out in their general attitudes about Product Management, and how they saw themselves.

I must stress that I am not using the term Frauduct Managers as a pejorative. As we’ll see, these are PMs with hopes, goals, dreams, and aspirations—which have been snuffed out by circumstances. It’s not their fault they’re unhappy. Frauduct Managers are likely to be younger, working at Indian privately funded startups, are sceptical about users and data, and have limited power or agency in their work. 

If anything, as we’ll find out, multiple parties bear responsibility for making these PMs feel like their entire profession is a scam.

Today’s edition is about what all these Product Managers told us. 

As always, let’s dive in.

Finding Frauduct Managers and Product Maniacs

Finding the PMs who were unhappy about what they were doing was quite straightforward. I narrowed it down to two questions I asked in the survey:

How well did your actual day-to-day work in Product Management match with your expectations about it before you started?

How likely is it that you’d recommend a career in Product Management to a friend?

As Product Managers can tell you, both these questions are fairly standard questions to test how your audience feels about your product. The first question captures prior expectations before using a product against the actual experience after using it. The second question, more popularly known as NPS (Net Promoter Score) is a higher bar. It measures whether your user loves your product well enough to promote it to others, another powerful measure of Product Market Fit (PMF). I simply asked these questions back to PMs about their own careers. 

These are the two questions I used to measure where the PM taking the survey was unhappy, neutral, or happy. The ones who were negative on both questions—i.e., the ones who said that their day-to-day work in Product Management was less than what they expected and that they wouldn’t recommend it as a career to anybody—were the people who were disillusioned with the profession. They are the Frauduct Managers.

On the other extreme, there was a cohort of PMs who were the exact opposite—they said that their day-to-day work was much better than expectations and that they’d whole-heartedly promote a career in Product to their friends. I don’t really have a clever name for this cohort, so let’s just call them the optimists, or maybe… Product Maniacs. 

Across 1,001 Product Managers, the distribution looks like this.

https://the-ken.com/?post_type=nutgraf&p=1478305?utm_source=ro_hdr&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=View in Browser https://the-ken.com/?post_type=nutgraf&p=1478305?utm_source=ro_hdr&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=

There was another way to confirm whether I’d found the right set of people. It all came down to this question: 

How disappointed would you be if you no longer had the word ‘Product’ in your designation?

Again, as most PMs will tell you, this is a classic test https://productcoalition.com/using-sean-ellis-test-for-measuring-your-product-market-fit-c8ac98053c2c to measure PMF—popularised by Sean Ells, author of Hacking Growth and the first marketer at Dropbox. Of course, in this case, I was measuring how PMs saw their job titles as a “fit” with what they did. According to Sean Ellis, the magic number is 40%. 

Anyway, this is how both these cohorts split up on this question.

https://the-ken.com/?post_type=nutgraf&p=1478305?utm_source=ro_hdr&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=View in Browser https://the-ken.com/?post_type=nutgraf&p=1478305?utm_source=ro_hdr&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=

Simply put, Product Maniacs have an incredible fit with their designations. Frauduct Managers do not. This just confirms that we’re working with two cohorts who see the profession very differently. 

But who exactly are they? 

The young, disillusioned, and (possibly) consultants 

I’ll share three charts that tell the story of who these cohorts are, and I’ll let you draw your conclusions. 

Here’s the first one—which breaks them up by tenure.

And this, about their education. (By the way, I did get responses from PMs who had studied design, humanities, law, and other areas, but their numbers were quite small, and I hesitate to read too much into it statistically.)

Finally, I asked these PMs about the work they’d done before they did Product Management. Here’s what that looked like. 

https://the-ken.com/?post_type=nutgraf&p=1478305?utm_source=ro_hdr&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=View in Browser https://the-ken.com/?post_type=nutgraf&p=1478305?utm_source=ro_hdr&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=

I’m guessing you got to the same conclusions as I did. Frauduct Managers are more likely to be early in their career. Nearly 43% of this cohort are PMs with less than four years of experience, which is above average if you compare it to all PMs who answered this survey. Another thing that the data told me (not visualised here) is that unhappiness has a direct correlation with tenure—20% of all PMs with less than a year of experience are Frauduct. In the one–four-year cohort, that’s 18%. As the tenure increases, the share of frauduct managers reduces. Less than 8% of PMs with 12+ years of experience exhibit disillusionment. 

In some ways, this makes sense. Most people don’t stick around doing something they don’t really enjoy. After a point, they just move out and do other things. So it makes sense that young PMs are unhappy, and older PMs are happy and fulfilled.

The educational background question is mildly interesting. A lot has been written about whether MBAs make for good PMs. We don’t know that, but we do know that Product Maniacs are more likely to have MBAs than Frauduct Managers.

Finally, prior experience in business roles are more likely to create happy PMs. And if you’ve worked as a consultant and then moved to Product, well, you are likely to be more disillusioned. Personally, it’s hard for me to imagine that Product can be considered more fraud than consulting, but the data is the data. 

So, in which companies are these cohorts of PMs more likely to be found? 

Well…

https://the-ken.com/?post_type=nutgraf&p=1478305?utm_source=ro_hdr&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=View in Browser https://the-ken.com/?post_type=nutgraf&p=1478305?utm_source=ro_hdr&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=

By the way, another thing that the survey told us (not visualised) is that unhappiness is negatively correlated with size of company. The bigger the company, the smaller the share of PMs in it who are unhappy and disillusioned. Which sort of makes sense, I suppose. 

But if you’re working in a company with more than 50 people and less than 250, well, you’re really disillusioned. 

But why? I’ll get to it shortly.

Here’s how this breaks down with how old the company is.

https://the-ken.com/?post_type=nutgraf&p=1478305?utm_source=ro_hdr&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=View in Browser https://the-ken.com/?post_type=nutgraf&p=1478305?utm_source=ro_hdr&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=

Just look at the PMs who are working in companies that are older than two years but less than five. Just see how unhappy they are. 

Yup, these are the ZIRP-company PMs. 

If you take these last two charts together, it makes sense. The unhappiest PMs are the ones who work in companies with between 50–250 people, founded two to five years ago. Those were the companies that raised insane funding at crazy valuations and are now stuck trying to figure out what to do. Nobody bears more responsibility for killing the hopes and dreams of young, enthusiastic PMs than these companies. These are the biggest culprits and hotbeds for creating a generation of Frauduct Managers. 

Unsurprisingly, these are also privately funded Indian startups. 

https://the-ken.com/?post_type=nutgraf&p=1478305?utm_source=ro_hdr&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=View in Browser https://the-ken.com/?post_type=nutgraf&p=1478305?utm_source=ro_hdr&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=

The only positive thing about this infographic is that privately funded Indian startups are almost bimodal—there’s a sizable cohort of PMs in there who love what they do, and there’s a sizable cohort who dislike what they do. Few neutrals in there. 

Also, the datapoint about public Indian companies is interesting, and I’ll let you come up with your theory to explain it. 

Happiness comes from getting things done… and working with designers

This is one of the more controversial (and interesting) findings I got from the survey. 

https://the-ken.com/?post_type=nutgraf&p=1478305?utm_source=ro_hdr&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=View in Browser https://the-ken.com/?post_type=nutgraf&p=1478305?utm_source=ro_hdr&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=

Product Maniacs are more likely to enjoy working with designers. Frauduct Managers prefer working with analytics, or business teams. I can’t explain this beyond the possibility that maybe if you enjoy working with designers, you have a more… artistic side? I’m not sure. 

Also, the most interesting aspect of this question is that PMs who enjoy working with engineers are far more likely to be “neutral” about Product Management as a career. Again, I have no idea why, and I’m open to theories. 

Also, Product Maniacs are slightly more likely to enjoy shipping and getting things done. Frauduct Managers don’t exhibit any specific preferences on either of these traits as compared to all the PMs who took the survey. 

https://the-ken.com/?post_type=nutgraf&p=1478305?utm_source=ro_hdr&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=View in Browser https://the-ken.com/?post_type=nutgraf&p=1478305?utm_source=ro_hdr&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=

Product Maniacs believe in users and data… but only to a point

You’d expect Product Maniacs to be strong believers in data and user research. Not true. If anything, this survey suggests that the Product Managers who are the happiest and most fulfilled are just slightly more believers in users and data beyond the average PM. If anything, the ones who really believe in the power of users and data are likely to be neutral about Product Management as a career.

https://the-ken.com/?post_type=nutgraf&p=1478305?utm_source=ro_hdr&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=View in Browser https://the-ken.com/?post_type=nutgraf&p=1478305?utm_source=ro_hdr&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=

Frauduct managers are intensely sceptical (for good reason), and are more likely to distrust users and data. 

If you’ve made it this far, you’re probably wondering if the PMs you work with are Frauduct or Maniacs. Good question. Chances are that you’ll never really know. But regardless of the actual distribution, there are two specific things you can do to fix it. If these two things are in your control, just fix them and you’ll see a change in your team.

The Two Big Product happiness levers

You can cut the data in several ways, but at the end of it, there are two dimensions where the separation between the Product Maniacs (the ones who are happy and fulfilled) and Frauduct Managers (the ones who feel like the whole function is a big scam) comes out starkly. No other dimension captures this divide as much as these do. And luckily, both of them can be changed and influenced by companies, managers, and HR leaders.

The first of this comes down to power and agency. 

https://the-ken.com/?post_type=nutgraf&p=1478305?utm_source=ro_hdr&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=View in Browser https://the-ken.com/?post_type=nutgraf&p=1478305?utm_source=ro_hdr&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=

Happy Product Managers are those who believe they have some power. Unhappy PMs are the ones who are saddled with processes, checks and balances, and multiple things that stand in their way. If you are a company and you want to fix your Product Management function, simply cut things out of their way and let them make some features live quickly without too many questions. Nothing creates happiness more than seeing the thing you conceived go out into the world.

The companies that do this are more likely to have happier, more fulfilled PMs. 

The second most important distinction is also, in some ways, the most expected, and yet, somewhat surprising. 

https://the-ken.com/?post_type=nutgraf&p=1478305?utm_source=ro_hdr&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=View in Browser https://the-ken.com/?post_type=nutgraf&p=1478305?utm_source=ro_hdr&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=

I suppose this is the biggest thing that any organisation that wants to save its Frauduct Managers can do. They can fix the biggest reason why people leave or get disillusioned.

And like most things, it begins by fixing the managers.

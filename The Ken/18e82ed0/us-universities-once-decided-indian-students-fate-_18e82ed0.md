Good Morning Ishan,
Over ten days in late February and early March, representatives from 17 US universities visited different Indian cities meeting university officials, edtech executives, and education mandarins.
This should be no big deal since India sent roughly 270,000 students to the US in 2023. But part of the US delegation was University of California, Berkeley—the country’s top public university. This is not the kind of institution that needs to drum up business.
But that’s the new reality for American universities of all kinds.
In the past 12 years, college admissions have fallen 16%, courtesy of births plunging in the years following the 2008 financial crisis. Between 2025 and 2029, there’s bound to be a similar decline.
More recently, the US is seeing a dip from its top source of international students: China.
So, it’s natural for colleges to turn to their second-largest contributor of international students. But today, Alifiya’s [story](https://the-ken.com/story/us-universities-once-decided-indian-students-fate-now-its-the-other-way-round/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter) is about how they are winning over potential applicants.
Once you are done reading that, I’d urge you to take a look at a [feature](https://the-ken.com/story/canada-makes-life-hard-for-indian-students-and-thats-a-good-thing/?utm_source=web&utm_medium=socials&utm_campaign=social) we published in January about how Canada is dissuading Indian students as much as the US is enticing them. We’ve made this story free for 48 hours, so share it widely.
The humbling of Visa and Mastercard, country by country
That Visa and Mastercard are very profitable wouldn’t raise eyebrows. The card networks are, after all, a global duopoly. But do you have any idea how profitable they are? Roughly half their revenue flows to their bottom line. This is all thanks to the fat fee they charge on processing credit-card transactions. (The fee for debit-card purchases is not as high.)
But, increasingly, that’s not a given.
A decade ago, the European Union capped the swipe fee at 0.3% of the purchase price when the card networks were charging 1.5%. Now, Visa and Mastercard have decided to lower their fee in the US, which averages 2%, by 0.07 percentage points for five years. That’s equivalent to giving up US$30 billion in revenue—a move resulting from years of being sued by merchants.
In India, too, Visa and Mastercard are being hemmed in by the banking regulator. In its quest to level the playing field for homegrown rival Rupay, the Reserve Bank of India recently told banks that co-branded credit cards, which are very popular, cannot be exclusive to any network.
I don’t know how strongly the two giants feel about this move. But I do know they have no love lost for the widely used Unified Payments Interface. Here’s Sachin Mehra, Mastercard’s CFO, at a conference last year: “It is an incredibly painful experience for ecosystem participants who all end up losing money as part of that proposition.”

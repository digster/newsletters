Back in 2013, when I was fresh into the adtech world, I stumbled upon a French company company called Criteo. Criteo was in the retargeting business, and essentially showed ads across the internet to people who have already viewed that brand's product, website or added it to their cart. You’ve probably experienced this phenomenon yourself.

In its initial filing with the Securities and Exchange Commission prior to its IPO, Criteo submitted a document that described its business model, its financials, and how it planned to make money. It’s a tome, spanning nearly 300 pages, with one interesting aspect.

It has zero mentions of the words ‘retargeting’ or ‘adtech’.

There’s a good reason for this. Adtech companies never do well in public markets. Investors are usually quite sceptical about their prospects. In fact, as much as possible, adtech companies prefer to stay private as long as they can, or if they go public, they contort themselves to describe their business as anything but adtech.

Anuj Khanna Sohum, founder and CEO of Gurugram-headquartered adtech firm Affle India, puts it more colourfully—“If you see the public markets in the US, the most unfavoured child would be adtech. Mining would be second”.

Affle is the company that seems to have ignored all that.

It listed itself on India’s bourses in August last year with a share price of Rs 930 ($12.60).

Two weeks back, its share price touched an all-time high of Rs. 3,053 ($41.5), giving Affle a market cap of nearly a billion dollars. It’s India’s billion-dollar company you’ve heard little about.

Affle’s revenue last year?

A little under $50 million.

How is Affle doing this? What is its secret sauce? Will Affle continue to soar, or will it fall down like dozens of adtech companies that came before it? Where does it plan to go from here? And why has it made a bunch of acquisitions in the last year?

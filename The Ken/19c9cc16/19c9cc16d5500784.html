I have a pitch for you.

I’m starting a fund, and I want you to give me your money for the next ten years. I can’t promise you’ll beat the stock market. In fact, I can’t promise you’ll even beat a fixed deposit’s interest. But here’s what I can promise: I’ll take 2% of your money every single year. That’s my management fee, and it doesn’t matter if I make you rich or broke.

And when we finally exit after a decade? I’ll take 20% of whatever profits we made.

Oh, and one more thing. You can’t take your money back during these ten years. Not if you need it, not if you change your mind, and not even if I’m underperforming. It’s locked in.

So, are you convinced?

If your answer is “absolutely not,” hold that thought. Because that’s the tension at the heart of this entire episode.

This week on Two by Two, Praveen Gopal Krishnan, Rahel Philipose, and Rohin Dharmakumar sat down with Mayank Bansal, a UAE-based hedge fund manager, to break down the VC model. Because something doesn’t seem to be working.

Just earlier this month, three senior partners left Peak XV. Ashish Agarwal, who funded Groww; Ishan Mittal, who invested in Razorpay and Mamaearth; and Tejasvi Sharma, who backed Cred and Chargebee. These aren’t people who failed, but ones who made good bets for the company.

And they left over “disagreements on economics and payouts“, as Managing Director Shailendra Singh put it.

Our other guest, Arundhati Ramanathan, deputy editor at The Ken, just published a story breaking down exactly what’s happening inside Peak XV, and honestly, it’s worth reading alongside this episode because the cracks are showing everywhere.

The pitch vs the reality

From my understanding, here’s how venture capital is supposed to work. You give a VC firm your money, and they invest it in startups. Some startups fail, some do okay, and a few become unicorns. The unicorns make up for the losses, and it’s a win-win for everyone.

In exchange, the VC charges you 2% of your money every single year as a management fee. Then, when the fund makes profits, they take 20% of that as carry. This is called the “two and twenty” model, and it’s been the standard for decades.

Sounds fair, right? Except here’s the part nobody talks about.

If a VC fund generates 10% returns, a meaningful chunk of that upside goes to the fund itself once you account for the two and twenty.

Which means that unless venture returns are dramatically higher than public markets, the economics start to look a lot less compelling for investors locking their money away for a decade.

Mayank actually pulled the numbers from Crisil’s Alternate Investment Funds Benchmarks report. He compared Indian VC returns to small-cap index funds and FDs over the last decade.

His conclusion?

“What is happening in the VC industry currently is they are charging the profit shares of that Medallion fund while returning less than index funds, which is blasphemous.”

Most Indian VC funds delivered around 12% returns between 2014 and 2019. A small-cap index fund gave 13.35% over the same period with zero lock-in. You could withdraw your money whenever you wanted.

So, why does anyone invest in VCs?

This is the part I still don’t fully understand. If the returns are this close to the public markets, why do the smartest investors in the world keep putting money into venture capital?

Rohin’s take: it’s about diversification. Large pension funds and endowments manage hundreds of billions of dollars. They allocate 2% to venture capital, not because they expect amazing returns, but because they have to spread their bets across asset classes. Once that decision is made, the money flows in regardless of performance.

Throughout the episode, Praveen ended up cosplaying as a VC defender, trying to argue that surely not all VCs deserve to be painted with the same brush.

Praveen Gopal Krishnan: There are people within this asset class. Like, there are people who give money to Warren Buffett, but not to others, right? Are there Warren Buffett equivalents in VCs?

Rohin Dharmakumar: I’m sure there are, like absolutely no doubt. I’m literally saying, let’s assume 10% of venture capital funds are actually great at returning money and generating returns. What about the remaining 90%?

Mayank Bansal: I think they should shut down. You can’t be taking 2X the risk and generating less-than-index returns. On a risk-adjusted basis, it is pointless.

The problem is that top-performing early funds like Accel and Blume are closed to investors because they don’t need your money. They already have relationships going back years.

So your money inevitably ends up in the other 90%, and these are the ones charging the same fees but delivering FD-level returns. And by the time you realise this, your capital is locked in for a decade.

The most interesting part is that even the people generating the wins are starting to question the model. So when your best performers walk out because the economics don’t make sense for them, what does that say about everyone else?

Now, back to my opening pitch. Would you give me your money?

Yeah, neither would I.

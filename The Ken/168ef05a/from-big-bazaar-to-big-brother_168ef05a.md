Good Morning Ishan,
Once upon a time, all stores wanted was your money.
Those were the days.
Today, no purchase is complete without one ubiquitous, intrusive question—phone number?
I always refuse. My personal information, I tell myself in a smug inner monologue, stays with me.
Then I go home. Log in to Amazon with my Facebook profile because I’m too lazy to remember another login and password, supply the company with my name, address, and phone number for delivery, save my card details for a swifter checkout, and allow the site to access my GPS location for a more seamless experience.
Clearly, my data privacy game needs a lot of work.
But I’m not alone. I imagine a lot of you are in the same boat. Aghast at the blatant intrusiveness of our local supermarkets—who, let’s face, often do nothing with the information—while blissfully sacrificing our data at the altar of Amazon or Flipkart.
But India’s offline retailers are done being left out. After years of desperately hoarding phone numbers with the intention of spamming customers with SMS offers, they now want to know you better. They want to track everything you do in their stores. Watch every move. Listen in to what you say. Track every purchase. And every purchase that you almost made, but didn’t.
They want to know you better than you know yourself. Or, at least, as well as their e-commerce rivals know you. That’s what today’s story by Harveen is about. When Big Bazaar becomes Big Brother.

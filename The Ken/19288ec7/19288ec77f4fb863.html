India’s most feared regulator is turning on the fear factor some more on its regulated entities. The Reserve Bank of India, the country’s central bank, is encroaching, slowly but steadily, upon the turf of those it lords over. That’s sending chills down the spines of scores of fintechs—from account aggregators to fraud-protection startups to invoice authenticators. With good reason. Like The Godfather, the RBI makes offers its regulated entities can’t refuse. Or rather, won’t (dare to) refuse. Where does that leave lesser mortals that make similar offers, products and services? No prizes for guessing.

The Reserve Bank Innovation Hub (RBIH), a wholly owned subsidiary of the central bank, was set up just two-and-a-half years back to “accelerate innovation across the financial sector.” Former executives told Rounak, the author of today’s story, that the body is working with “welfare intentions”. But as many fintechs seem to lament in the story, the road to their hell is paved with the RBIH’s good intentions.

This became clear to them in August when the central bank-backed innovator launched the unified lending interface (ULI). Like its payments sibling UPI, the ULI, too, could upend the ground it walks on. Just that the move blindsided fintechs that were already offering solutions in the space. The RBIH followed it up last month with an offering that could help banks tackle the menace of mule accounts. Again, many fintechs toiling here were left wide-jawed and upset. There’s more coming from the RBIH stable, including—believe it or not—a savings product. Fintechs are staring at business erosion at the altar of a competitor with outsized influence and power.

Is there a method in the madness? What exactly is the RBI’s mandate—and that of the RBIH? Are they exceeding their remit? What does this really mean for fintechs? Should they pivot and grin and bear it, or adapt to the new reality? Just how?

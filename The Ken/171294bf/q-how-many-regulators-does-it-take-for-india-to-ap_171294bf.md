Ans: One apex body, to keep its cards close to its chest. And one institute under the apex body, to insist on 100% accuracy.
The apex body here is the ICMR, or the Indian Council of Medical Research; and the country’s premier biomedical regulator under the ICMR, the NIV. Or the National Institute of Virology.
And between the two of them lies confusion, opacity, and inexplicability.
As a poor, dense, and still-developing country of 1.3 billion people begins to confront the scary possibility of widespread community transmission of Covid-19, it needs to move to a protocol of extensive testing followed by targeted containment and hospitalisation.
Instead, sadly, India is wasting precious days and weeks fumbling its testing strategy.
With 135-odd RT-PCR kit brands floating in India, according to a private pathology lab’s market research, things are beginning to get chaotic. Kits are in short supply; some of them are failing; suppliers are jacking up prices. A few labs are even hoarding kits.
The reasons are many, but ICMR’s lack of transparency and organisation have played a major role in the current morass. Meanwhile, NIV has set an impossibly perfect benchmark for tests to be approved—100% sensitivity (to detect 100% cases) and 100% specificity (to detect 100% negative cases).
“No kit in the world shows 100% sensitivity or specificity. Even a textbook definition talks of anything between 96% to 98%,” said the India spokesperson of a global diagnostics company whose kit is approved.
A curious loophole exists for importers of test kits which are US FDA-approved or have Europe’s CE certification (neither of which requires 100% specificity): they can get approval from the drug regulator to sell those kits in India.
Meanwhile, ICMR has curiously not (yet) included some of India’s leading medical research institutes (Nimhans in Bangalore, Christian Medical College in Vellore, and Sanjay Gandhi Post Graduate Institute of Medical Sciences in Lucknow) in its efforts. Nor has it taken advantage of Southern India’s biotech hubs in Bangalore, Hyderabad, and Chennai, meaning they all have to send their kits to the nearest testing hub (500-1,000 km away) by road because flights are cancelled.
Seema, our editor, reports in depth on a vital institution that should have been more transparent, better prepared, and more organised.
ICMR needs to up its game. For India’s sake.

Good Morning Ishan,
Last week, right before Holi, I travelled to Pune. If you remember, the last time I summed up the week’s action for you, I’d written how celebrating the Republic Day was like a tradition in my family. This time, when all of us cousins got together again, we were reminded of our grandfather and paid the best tribute to him by making some samosas and gujias at home, albeit with less ghee.
It’s in my nature to share food; although this time, it’s only a picture of home-made samosas.
Most of my “rituals” in Pune revolve around food—have guava ice cream at Natural’s in Viman Nagar; binge on vada pav and bhaji pav from Joshi Wadewala; visit Toit’s in Kalyani Nagar for their Tint-In-Wit beer and shrimps; and finally, fill up my suitcase with Chitale’s bakarwadi, Laxminarayan’s chiwda and farsan, and wafers from Budhani’s.
If one were to learn lessons in entrepreneurship, it better be from these regional businesses who’ve been at it for decades now. Most importantly, they’re doing it without burning money.
Upon googling, I find that most of these savouries are available in Delhi through e-commerce platforms. But the proof of the pudding lies in how most brands make a chunk of their sales offline. All this “building a brand” talk has occupied my mind of late, thanks to Shark Tank season 3.
American investor Peter Lynch, in his book One Up on Wall Street, wrote how he invested in a multi-bagger—L’Eggs (owned by Hanes), a stockings brand—upon recommendation, not from a market participant but his wife. She bought some L’Eggs stockings from a supermarket, and told Lynch how they’ve become popular among women.
This was also because Hanes applied a hyperlocal solution to sell their products—they were placed near the billing shelf, a place where women made weekly, if not daily, runs. That made it hard for them to ignore the brand.
Same is the case with brands such as Chitale, Laxminarayan, and Joshi Wadewala—they’ve catered to the hyperlocal needs of their customers and built a profitable brand in the process. And in India, examples are replete. Every gully (lane) has a lesson for you.
With all that talk about money to be made, lessons to be learnt, and opportunities in the market, let’s look at the stories my colleagues delivered last week at The Ken.
It was a non-publishing day in India on account of Holi. But my colleague in Indonesia, Prima, wrote about former rivals in the ride-hailing and food-delivery space, Grab and Goto, trying to pull out of the red. Goto, for instance, made a moderate profit of US$4.9 million in the latest quarter. |
|
On Tuesday, Nathan wrote about an interesting trend he noticed among youngsters registering for financial-certification courses. While there’s been a steep decline in the registration for the CA course, youths have increasingly been opting for the CFA programme. The governing body of CAs noticed this pattern, but are they doing enough to save the once most-sought-after profession? |
|
On Wednesday, Shivani and Shaswata wrote about Urban Company’s push in the water-purifying (RO) market. What’s the value proposition as against the Kents and Eureka Forbes of the world you ask? Two years of free servicing as against one. But the actual game starts after those two years because RO requires recurring services, which means a recurring source of revenue for the company. |
|
Alifiya, on Thursday, wrote about how American universities are turning to India to help them arrest the declining college admissions. Why India? It’s the second largest market of international students for these universities, after China. Tuition discounts, short master’s courses, and other facilities for these students, however, may not be enough, it seems. Read to know more. |
|
We wrapped up the week with a solid follow-up from Anand on the management crisis at the EPFO. The discontent among the working class with India’s top retirement funds manager is a big opportunity for its closest competitor—the National Pension System. Even though long-term returns are better at NPS, it comes with its own set of challenges, argues Anand. |

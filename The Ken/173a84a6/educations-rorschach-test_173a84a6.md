Last week, India’s cabinet approved a new national education policy (NEP).
There’s a lot to unpack here, but let me start with the basic stuff.
First, many believe that this is a big deal for many reasons :
- It’s a massive update to India’s education policy. The last time this was done was back in 1986—before India’s economy was liberalised. That’s the broad framework on which our current education system rests on.
- It’s a culmination of a five-year process that was fairly consultative in nature. Multiple drafts were created. The draft was in the public domain for a while, and it received over
[77,000 comment](https://www.thehindu.com/news/national/over-77000-feedback-letters-on-draft-national-education-policy/article28751808.ece)[s](https://www.thehindu.com/news/national/over-77000-feedback-letters-on-draft-national-education-policy/article28751808.ece) and letters. You may disagree with aspects of the policy but there seems to be little to complain about the process itself.
However, it’s important to note that this isn’t a big deal for many other reasons:
- The NEP is a directional document. Yes, it spells out policy and will have deep ramifications, but there’s a long, long way to go. The history of India is replete with numerous examples of great documents, all with questionable implementation, starting from the Constitution itself. So, let’s not get too excited yet.
- A lot depends on how states and the centre work together. Over time. Education is not a central subject—it’s on the concurrent list, which means that all this comes down to how the central government and state governments work together. And there are many examples of where this has been botched up.
Oh also, the NEP itself believes that some of the things that it hopes to accomplish will be done only by 2040—by when, if current events are any indication, all of us will probably be living in a colony on Mars, ordering masks from Amazon’s lunar warehouse while being employed by Reliance Jio.
The NEP has received some significant attention in the media, particularly on aspects like Hindi imposition and local languages as the medium of instruction. I am not an expert on education, but it seemed to me like this is missing the forest for the trees.
But the NEP is a dense forest. There are aspects like how education will be funded, the creation of a “360-degree feedback” for school report cards and assessments, AI-driven analysis of performance, alongside structural reforms like the elimination of a few boards and the creation of new ones. That’s just the beginning. There’s a lot in there.
So, in this edition, instead of focusing on what this means for the future and how it will pan out, because that’s frankly just guesswork, I thought I’ll highlight three paths that the NEP seems to have made directionally and philosophically.
You can make perfectly good faith arguments why you support them. You can also make good faith arguments about why you don’t. Both are legitimate at this point because it’s far too early to say how it’ll pan out.
But they are significant. And reveal interesting things.

Good Morning Ishan,

An experiment of this scale has never been attempted before. No, not finding a cure or a vaccine. I’m talking about the global scale work-from-home experiment that you, I, and almost everyone we know is participating in, willingly or not.

A whole chorus of voices, including penny-pinching corporates and couch potatoes, is hailing it a success. But, not everyone is convinced—pound-foolish corporates and office couch potatoes. And me.

Your and my vote aside, there is one group that is rooting for offices to reopen asap—co-working spaces. They seem better primed than most for the post-Covid world, considering WeWork’s botched IPO was their Wuhan. The spectacular month-long slow meltdown of WeWork last year made everyone an expert in the fragility of the co-working business model then. Including co-working companies. And SoftBank.

But the winds of change had been flowing in India’s co-working sector for a while. A co-working space is no longer this cool, chic setup where freelancers and startups hang out to work.

Instead, writes Seetharaman in his debut story for The Ken, co-working centres are increasingly looking like regular offices, a collection of enclosed private spaces. In other words, boring but functional—a more solid business model.

BHIVE, for example, had gone from an equal split of desks in cabins and in open areas to now having 90% of its 4,000 desks in private studios. These are the sort of desks that enterprise clients want, and though they push for cheaper rates, they sign longer leases.

The pandemic has also given co-working companies an edge in their negotiation with landlords to switch to revenue- or profit-sharing models, from the lease model. “If you throw me out, you are not going to find anyone for a while,” says the CEO of a large co-working company.

But there are new hurdles: physical distancing and stricter hygiene standards being the most obvious ones. Seetharaman’s story today is about co-working companies and their moment of serendipity, and their plans to continue to adapt.

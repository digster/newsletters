We all like to believe in our own essentiality. That, as insignificant as we may be in the grander scheme of things, we're still essential cogs in the various systems we participate in—be it work, family or even our circle of friends.
Being essential, though, does not amount to being irreplaceable. As Bilbo Baggins mused in The Fellowship of The Ring, "Don't adventures ever have an end? I suppose not. Someone else always has to carry on the story." This holds true in every sphere of life.
Even SoftBank, the Japanese VC behemoth, is not immune to this. In 2018, when it exited India's largest homegrown e-commerce platform Flipkart, it seemed like the end of an era. SoftBank, which had made some of the biggest and boldest bets the Indian startup ecosystem had ever seen, was closing the chapter on its Indian sojourn.
Three years later, SoftBank is back in town and creating unicorns. Except this time, the Indian ecosystem is different. The gap left by SoftBank has been filled, and how. SoftBank is no longer the biggest dog in the fight. There are VCs like Tiger Global as well as PEs that can match it cheque for cheque. This is a far cry from a few years ago, when SoftBank’s backing was a death blow to one's rivals. It’s also not the only one creating unicorns anymore—just a quarter of the 16 Indian unicorns minted this year belong to the SoftBank stable.
It isn't just the ecosystem that has changed. SoftBank has, too. It no longer has the swagger of its US$100 billion Vision Fund. Now, it's investing out of its own coffers, and cheques are more prudent and its choice of investees is also different. While SoftBank still enjoys the safety net that comes with backing undisputed category leaders, it's now also looking to catch companies young to truly get bang for its buck.

Good Morning Ishan,
“It’s the law of the jungle—go for the weakest.”
This is the kind of statement that stops you in your tracks, especially when you’re reading a [story](https://the-ken.com/story/how-many-hospitals-can-pe-firms-buy-before-patients-say-enough/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter) on healthcare.
Private-equity (PE) firms and the hospital chains they back couldn’t scoop up standalone facilities fast enough. Of the 80-plus deals in Indian healthcare since 2020, 18 have been buyouts, most of them in the past year alone. On Monday, PE major KKR bought a controlling stake in Kerala-based Baby Memorial Hospital for US$240 million.
In a country where government health expenditure is a scanty 2% of GDP—among the lowest in the world—isn’t all this money welcome?
How you feel about that depends on your answer to another question: is a PE firm a natural hospital owner?
Especially when the endgame is to take the hospital public, and when the same retail investor who expects juicy profits as a shareholder demands impeccable service as a patient. If only the two could go together.
Seema spoke with a range of people, from private-equity executives and hospital CEOs to doctors who sold their practices and those who held out, to tell the [definitive Big Story](https://the-ken.com/story/how-many-hospitals-can-pe-firms-buy-before-patients-say-enough/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter) of a tectonic shift in India’s healthcare industry and its fallout.

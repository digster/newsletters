Good Morning Ishan,
 
“Great stories happen to those who tell them.”
 
This powerful quote by American radio host Ira Glass is how I began a talk on storytelling for an online community I’m part of.
 
Now, I’m not a storytelling expert. But I happen to work with an organisation that is. And that helps. Everything I spoke about in that session was inspired by either the wonderful stories our writers tell every day or the brilliant narratives I happen to come across in conversations with my team.
 
Whether you’re a writer, editor, podcast producer, marketer, product manager or engineer, if you work at The Ken, you have to tell stories. There’s no other way. It’s probably one of my favourite things about working here. We’re an organisation of storytellers, and we’re obsessed with storytelling (our Slack channels are proof).
 
We brought some exciting stories to you this week. Here’s a short recap.
 
We kicked off the week with six-year-old Bengaluru-based fintech Rupeek and its hunt for the gold loan crown. Before that, though, Rupeek has a lot to figure out—how to reach rural and semi-urban parts of India, the ones that make for 65% of India’s total gold stock. And it has to do this while keeping its pricing lucrative enough.
 
We followed it up with Olina’s story about Apna, one of the fastest Indian startups to enter the unicorn club and its ambitious vision. All at once, it wants to be a social network, a classroom, and a job portal. In its race to achieve all of this, will it end up becoming a master of none?
 
Young health insurance providers believe the answer to India's lack of insurance penetration is subscriptions. However, it’s not easy to get users to subscribe. So, how does a business offer more, charge less, and stay sustainable? That was Maitri’s story on Wednesday.

On Thursday, Anand wrote about one of the country’s largest money managers—the Employees’ Provident Fund Organisation. And how a host of governance issues—from a lack of transparency to how it manages to deliver on its promised returns—threaten both its sustainability and allure. It was also our free story of the week, so do share it widely.
 
Shruti closed the week with a story on Ashok Leyland’s e-bus empire, courtesy of its new subsidiary, Switch Mobility. The world’s third-largest bus manufacturer found that the way to Indian customers’ wallets is through electric mobility-as-a-service (eMaaS) products. Unfortunately, heavyweights like Tata Motors and PMI Electro have the same idea.
 
We had some interesting newsletters as well this week. Seetharaman took us on a nostalgia ride while writing about the curious box packaging that tea and other products seem to come in; Seema wrote about the latest IPCC report and its promise of focusing 50% of climate finance on adaptation, not mitigation; Praveen ended the week with an edition on why Indian food giants are thrilled about the new nutrition label.
 
That’s it from us this week! We’ll see you again on Monday at 8 am.
 
Till then,

Good Morning Ishan,
When I asked Siddharth Jadhav whether he has a green thumb, he smiled and said no.
The research scientist-turned-entrepreneur may not have a knack for growing plants, but the work that his team is doing at Polybee, a company that uses drones to improve yields in controlled-environment agriculture settings, may shape the way food is cultivated in various parts of the world.
But let’s back up a bit: Jadhav hails from Pune, India. He trained to be an engineer at the Goa campus of[ BITS Pilani](https://the-ken.com/story/bits-pilani-is-tired-of-playing-second-fiddle-to-iits/) before relocating to Singapore in 2016 and becoming a research scientist at the National University of Singapore’s (NUS) Temasek Laboratories.
It was at NUS’s Lean LaunchPad where Jadhav got a taste of operating at the intersection of business and R&D. The idea behind this program was for each participant to take a technology stack and figure out its leanest real-world application, with validation from interviews with people who might actually use it. If the idea was solid, then a business plan could be developed.
Jadhav eventually landed on an idea to make pollination more efficient by using aerial drones’ downwash to vibrate flower trusses, releasing pollen so that fruits could form. The Ken spoke with him to find out more about how this works and how Polybee, counterintuitively, isn’t exactly a drone company.
Bearing fruit
As its name suggests, Polybee’s small aerial drones are designed to fulfil the function of bees in pollination. Originally a side project for Jadhav, the concept took shape in a commercially viable form when experienced voices offered encouragement:

At the time, Jadhav was thinking about the number of mouths that would need to be fed by 2050 (9.7 billion) and the rapid ramp-up that’s needed in food production. With that demand in sight, he knew that factors such as climate change, urbanisation, and the depletion of arable land would make it difficult to cultivate the calories that future humans would need.
|
|
Jadhav can speak at length about the different types of pollinators and how they interact with crops. He’s able to rattle off market sizes (US$2.4 trillion globally for farming, for example) and how specific segments are valued (US$900 billion for fruits and vegetables, with US$600 billion depending on pollination). And he can tell you where bumble bees naturally live and where they’re rare or absent (South America, Australia, Southeast Asia).
This all sets the scene for the potential of Polybee’s offering. The company integrates its navigation software into off-the-shelf, consumer-grade drones, making them easy to deploy and use. This is important because Polybee’s drones are meant to accomplish more than pollination for crops like tomatoes, berries, eggplants, and peppers. They perform a function that seeds much more fundamentally important changes in how food is grown.
The real juice
Aside from acting as pollinators, Polybee’s drones also measure traits such as yield, the number of fruits on a plant, fruit size and weight, ripeness levels, expected harvest date, and more. It gathers this data by recording video images—using cameras like ones found in smartphones—then creating a 3D digital twin of the crops and their environment. This virtual model is then used to perform analysis and make projections.

The application being developed by Polybee shows that deep tech isn’t always about heading to space, or reinventing food, or figuring out ways to apply mind-bending equations. It can also be about implementing useful research in immediately impactful ways. My colleague Seema recently wrote about a non-profit doing something [similar in India](https://the-ken.com/greenmargins/sequoia-backed-absolutes-agritech-claims-continue-to-baffle/) in Green Margins, our climate sector newsletter—though not with drones.
Polybee’s progression depended on support from a range of actors, including the Singapore Food Agency, which encouraged academics to develop solutions for pollination and helped Jadhav set up a small indoor farm before Polybee was established. This initial experiment involved just a couple shelves in a controlled room in Sembawang, in the northern part of the city-state.
The engineer made significant developments in these humble beginnings. Jadhav said this setup was where “80% of the research work” behind Polybee was completed, giving him solid ground to stand on when the company obtained a grant from the Temasek Foundation to fund its initial operations and build revenue streams without tapping venture capital.
Polybee is distinct as a firm that hasn’t needed to rely heavily on venture funding to get its research off the ground, moving in a subdued manner but with high-impact purpose. And just like that, an engineer who says he doesn’t have a green thumb might have nurtured the tech that will grow your food someday.

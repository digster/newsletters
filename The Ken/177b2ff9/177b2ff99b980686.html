Good Morning Ishan,
 
Eye-popping cloud news has been pouring in over the past week. Microsoft pipped AWS to the #1 position in cloud revenue by a mile—31% higher than AWS’ $45.4 billion in 2020.

Revealing its cloud earnings for the first time, Google said its $13 billion in revenues came with $5.6 billion in losses. If AWS has been subsidising Amazon’s other businesses, Google’s ad sales will subsidise its cloud business for some time.

And though at #3 in market share, a new report shows Google Cloud outperformed both AWS and Azure (Microsoft) on a number of benchmarking tests.

Amidst all this, if I told you that Google India bagged a $20-million cloud deal from the $13 billion Adani Group, the world’s largest solar power developer, you’ll probably laugh. It’s chump change in the cloud war. Heck, it’s a fifth of what Indian social platform Sharechat paid for Google Cloud.

But the Adani deal is a sign of where Google Cloud is headed in the region and how its strategy differs from both AWS and Microsoft. The Ken has learnt that the search giant has authorised its salesforce to offer significant discounts to persuade clients. (Enterprise clients indeed require persuasion to shed at least the inertia.)

Under Thomas Kurian, GCP is getting creative and bold. And why not. Kurian quit Oracle over disagreements with founder Larry Ellison on the direction of the software giant’s cloud business. Three months after Kurian joined Google, Anthos, a multi-cloud product, launched. And signed up Bharti Airtel, India's #2 telco.

Among Kurian, Karan Bajwa, the APAC VP, and Bikram Bedi, India managing director and former AWS India chief, Google is seeding its cloud business with old and new tricks. It’s adding more skin to the game as its global deals with automotive company Ford and French conglomerate Total reflect. In India, the Adani Group could prove to be a solid and similar case.

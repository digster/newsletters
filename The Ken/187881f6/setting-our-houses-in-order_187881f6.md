My wife and I had the pleasure of driving down to Bengaluru’s Bannerghatta Biological Park a few days ago, and with a bit of time left in the evening, we thought we’d drive out to a waterfall we spotted on Google Maps. The reviews weren’t great, but since neither of us has lived in the city for very long, we thought we’d check it out anyway.
The Thottikallu Falls, less than a kilometre from the Gulakamale Lake, is tiny. But to be honest, the reviews had prepared us for that.
What they didn’t prepare us for was the absolutely unbelievable amount of trash we found there. In the stream, out of it, at the bottom of the waterfall, in the foliage (I seriously do not know how a pair of shorts and a wrapper of Lay’s chips got 15 feet up a tree)...
At first, I was—as always—annoyed with the government and whichever authority it is whose job is to keep the place clean. But really, what can any project to tidy up the Thottikallu Falls achieve if the next visitor just tosses their garbage right into the stream again?
Littering is something no amount of external policing can fully eradicate. It is a matter of personal discipline and consideration for nature that societies have to instil in individuals. It’s about putting our (otherwise beautiful) houses in order.

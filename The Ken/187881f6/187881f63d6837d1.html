Good Morning Ishan,
 
My wife and I had the pleasure of driving down to Bengaluru’s Bannerghatta Biological Park a few days ago, and with a bit of time left in the evening, we thought we’d drive out to a waterfall we spotted on Google Maps. The reviews weren’t great, but since neither of us has lived in the city for very long, we thought we’d check it out anyway. 
 
The Thottikallu Falls, less than a kilometre from the Gulakamale Lake, is tiny. But to be honest, the reviews had prepared us for that. 
 
What they didn’t prepare us for was the absolutely unbelievable amount of trash we found there. In the stream, out of it, at the bottom of the waterfall, in the foliage (I seriously do not know how a pair of shorts and a wrapper of Lay’s chips got 15 feet up a tree)...
 
At first, I was—as always—annoyed with the government and whichever authority it is whose job is to keep the place clean. But really, what can any project to tidy up the Thottikallu Falls achieve if the next visitor just tosses their garbage right into the stream again?
 
Littering is something no amount of external policing can fully eradicate. It is a matter of personal discipline and consideration for nature that societies have to instil in individuals. It’s about putting our (otherwise beautiful) houses in order.
 
Our first story last week by Rounak was also about a house that needs to be put in order. India’s offshore financial hub, GIFT City in Gujarat, has seen a big jump in the number of businesses registering after 2020. This was after the Gujarat government took over the development of the long-delayed project. But a host of factors are holding businesses back from taking the best advantage of the incentives that come with the location.
 
On Tuesday, Anand and Anirudh explored how the Securities and Exchanges Board of India’s new rules have caused turmoil in India’s community of wealth portfolio managers. Accustomed to little regulatory meddling and high flexibility in their operations, they are now finding many of the guidelines (such as benchmarking) particularly irksome. What does the future look like?
 
Wednesday’s story by Katrina was about how a ubiquitous mode of public transportation in the Philippines—the jeepney—is facing an epochal shift. The government is pushing to electrify the country’s transportation infrastructure, but many jeepney drivers and operators just can’t afford the upfront cost of an electric vehicle (EV). Francisco Motors, however, is trying to change things with locally manufactured batteries and other cost-saving measures.
 
On Thursday, Alifiya delved into an important and, by now, extremely popular subject—health supplements and nutraceuticals. The market for nutraceuticals could become worth a whopping $18 billion by 2025. But India’s food-safety regulator is starting to increase scrutiny on the sector as many products are not following its standards.
 
On Friday, we had our editor, Seema Singh, speaking to Ravikiran Annaswamy and Siddharth Sreenivasan, the founders of Numocity—a software-as-a-service (SaaS) firm specialising in EV charging—on how they’re breaking the mould.

Sometimes the best way to spot patterns is to step back and zoom out. And as you do that, the pictures start to form. The lines start to connect. The trends start to reveal themselves. 
 
When it comes to VC funding and startups, far too often, the news is about the here and now. This company raised money. That fund got an exit. This sector is “hot”. That startup became a unicorn.
 
For instance, if I told you that 2018 was the first time in India’s history that the total money returned by VCs to their LPs via exits exceeded the total money invested by them, that would be an amazing tree story indeed. But zoom out and you realise the returns were skewed by a once-in-a-generation exit, as Walmart bought Flipkart for $16 billion. Take that away and exits were as anaemic as ever. That’s the forest.
 
Or that the most prodigious VC fund in India was Sequoia, with investments in 35 companies in 2018. And that with its sixth fund of $700 million, it had $4 billion of cash under management. Another great tree. Heck, it's even called Sequoia. But zoom out and you realise that three of its partners quit to start their own fund, while some others moved base to Singapore. Leaving only a shadow of what one would expect from a fund of its size.
 
That’s today’s story, our first one where the narrative plays only a supporting role.

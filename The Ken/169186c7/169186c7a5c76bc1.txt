Good Morning Ishan,

Every evening, while the rest of us monkeyed around campus, playing badminton, smoking, having ridiculous pointless conversations, she'd be cooking in the kitchen on her hostel floor.

She couldn't eat the hostel food. And not just because it was sometimes really unappetising, but because to her it was poison.

This smiley, friendly collegemate of mine suffered from a rare disease that put her on extreme dietary restrictions. The last I'd heard from her, she was working at an exceedingly prominent hospitality company in India. 

There are about 100 million people like her in India. 100 million.

And very few have the resources to fight a rare disease like my friend, for the medicines, called orphan drugs, can be impossibly expensive. Fewer still in India. We need an effective policy on it. Like yesterday.

The last one we had did little. India’s health ministry in 2017 put in place a national rare disease policy, which would use “a multipronged and multisectoral approach to build India’s capacity to tackle rare diseases comprehensively”.

The government, as of January, has announced, that any orphan drugs registered in India would be exempt from price control for five years. The step is meant to incentivise both large global pharma companies to market orphan drugs here, and domestic companies to develop such drugs locally, as importing them can cost a bomb.

But it's not all that easy. Especially because the government hasn't set the best precedent on this front. Just three months ago, in December 2018, it dropped the rare disease policy, telling the Supreme Court that it was 'unimplementable'.  What makes this worse is that a Rs 100-crore fund to cover the state-sponsored treatment for these diseases never actually got allocated. Why? Due to an "honest mistaken belief" on the part of the government.

“I felt if tech companies with little to offer can create so much value, why can’t we create more value,” says Kiran Mazumdar-Shaw, the founder of India’s biotech giant, Biocon.
And so she did.
Biocon Biologics, the four-year-old company that Mazumdar-Shaw spun out of Biocon to manufacture and market “biosimilars”, was valued in January at an eye-popping US$4.1 billion, after an investment of US$75 million from Abu Dhabi’s state-owned holding company ADQ. Biosimilars are biological drugs that offer nearly the same efficacy of original drugs, even though their composition might be different. This is unlike generics, which are usually the same chemical composition as the original they’re copying.
Just a year ago, Biocon Biologics was worth just US$3 billion.
But using the tech playbook to build investor interest and valuations for the startup was the easy part. Executing on expectations was the hard part.
Its target of reaching US$1 billion in revenue by March next year might fall short by nearly 35-40%. Its CEO Christiane Hamacher, who had joined less than two years ago from Swiss pharma giant Roche, is stepping down.
And on Biocon’s most recent analyst call, Mazumdar-Shaw had to admit that the path her unicorn subsidiary had taken wasn’t working out, and that it’d need a “course correction”.
That’s the setting for today’s high-stakes story by Seema Singh, our editor.
Because biologics is a US$40 billion opportunity today and growing rapidly, with some estimates placing it to be US$121 billion by 2023. And Biocon's work in the space goes back all the way to 2000.
Analysts and investors feel Mazumdar-Shaw and Biocon have been overconfident about their biosimilars future. That the competition is fiercer. That the risks are greater.

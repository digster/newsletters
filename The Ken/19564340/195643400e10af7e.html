The stock markets in India continue to correct.

The regulator continues to press the brake on F&O trades.

Meanwhile, the loudest and clearest voice in the broking industry has dropped a bomb—there’s a “massive drop” in both the number of trades and traders.

In the midst of all this, two sizeable private-equity firms are chasing Dhan, a four-year-old discount broking company, to invest a sizeable amount. Why?

The short answer: PEs know how to make money.

The long answer: in a cut-throat industry, Dhan has not even broken into the top five in terms of active clients, will cost a pretty penny, and yet offers a chance to bet on a professional who’s done this before. Not exactly in discount broking, but in the mutual-fund business.

At Paytm* Money, CEO Pravin Jadhav garnered over 50% market share in all SIPs in less than 18 months. He isn’t clocking that speed at Dhan yet, but he’s comfortably found a place for his startup between other discount brokers and Zerodha*, the market leader. Its ARPU, or the average revenue per user, stands out for its size and age.

To The Ken’s list of questions, Jadhav had a clipped response, “I am heads-down with execution.”

But that didn’t stop Archishma from trying to understand why Chryscapital and Hornbill find Dhan attractive in this brutal environment. Read it here.

*Paytm founder Vijay Shekhar Sharma and Zerodha’s perennial fund, Rainmatter Capital, are investors in The Ken.

The Aditya Birla Group ventured into the decorative-paints market in 2024 with Birla Opus. The incumbents and their investors were predictably worried. Next, the empire’s crown jewel, Ultratech Cement, announced its entry into the cables-and-wires business last week, hurting the stocks of several companies.

There‘s yet another diversification that has preceded these. The group rolled out an online store called Birla Pivot in 2023, selling everything from cement and steel to bath fittings and switches—just like startups Infra Market and Ofbusiness. Their startling rise was reason enough for the Birlas to jump in.

Yet, this particular move has gone largely unnoticed. Why? Find out in this week’s Long and Short.

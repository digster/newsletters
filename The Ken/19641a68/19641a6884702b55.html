What do you do when you bomb a test so badly it haunts you for over a decade? If you are India, you just stop showing up.

Consider these two actual exam questions:

1. What are the three main features of the rabi crop season?

(If you’ve survived an Indian board exam, this one probably triggers muscle memory.)

2. Here are images of two hills. One looks greener. Here’s data from solar radiation sensors, soil moisture meters, and rain gauges. Two people are arguing about which hill has more vegetation. Pick a side. Defend it.

(You can’t memorise your way out of that one.)

The second question comes from PISA—the Programme for International Student Assessment. It’s a global test for 15-year-olds, designed not to check what they remember. It’s a proxy for how well your future workforce can think, solve problems, and adapt to real-world scenarios.

This year, 90 countries took it. India did not. Again. In fact, it hasn’t participated since 2009, when it came second…from the bottom.

But just because it isn’t taking the test doesn’t mean it isn’t trying to fix the scorecard. Behind the scenes, a little-known agency called Parakh is trying to drag the country’s 69 school boards into the world of critical thinking. It’s brought in the GRE and TOEFL people. It’s holding teacher workshops. It’s dangling money in front of states. And yes, some boards are already setting 50% of their questions in the “please-think-before-you-write” category.

So, will India show up at PISA 2028? Seems unlikely, given the reform’s current pace. Because the real test isn’t taken by students; it’s in convincing thousands of teachers, dozens of state boards, and one very cautious ministry that change is worth the headache.

Why build when you can buy and then build? Here come the search funds

Chances are, many of you haven’t heard of ‘search funds’. It’s a relatively new concept in India, and it inverts and twists many of our conventional ideas about how people start, build, scale, and fund businesses. But for tens of thousands of small and medium enterprises that have achieved some measure of success and want to take themselves to the next level, search funds might just be the answer.

In this week’s episode of Two by Two, co-hosts Rohin Dharmakumar and Praveen Gopal Krishnan sit down with Anurag Sinha, managing partner of Milestone Search Capital, and Rehan Netarwala, founder and managing partner of Okintek Capital, to break down why and how search funds do what they do.

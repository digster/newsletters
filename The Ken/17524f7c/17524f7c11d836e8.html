Good Morning Ishan,
 
The Indian Premier League (IPL) is seemingly unkillable.
 
It has survived:
 
A fugitive founder
Match-fixing cricketers
Team owners illegally betting on the tournament
General elections, which saw it move to South Africa
And a million other scandals that have, by now, become a hazy blur to most
 
This year's edition was delayed, and then moved to the United Arab Emirates to get around the pandemic still tearing through India. Even positive cases that popped up shortly before the tournament began couldn't derail it.
 
Despite everything, the IPL still maintains a viewer base like little else—sports or otherwise. The opening match of this season saw 200 million viewers tune in. For advertisers looking to reach the Indian masses, this is an unparalleled canvas.
 
Unsurprisingly then, even as some sponsors opted out of this edition of the IPL for different reasons, many more flooded in. Startups like Byju's, Unacademy, Dream11 all broke the bank in their quest for India's attention. As did CRED. Except, unlike its startup peers, CRED has always avoided the mass market, claiming to target only India's most elite.
 
So why did CRED spring for the massiest advertising avenue available, and with ads steeped in even more mass appeal through the allure of Bollywood? Sumanth does the math to see just what CRED gains by breaking from convention. Spoiler alert: It isn't revenue.

Long and Short
Wed, 22 Jan 25
The story behind the numbers and motivations driving India's stock market..
Good Morning Ishan,
“Building the world’s most modular, large-scale, affordable, and modern green energy business.”
Reliance Industries’ description of its new-energy vertical in its FY24 annual report is, at once, hyperbolic and hard to dismiss—a function of its track record in blitzscaling its retail and telecom operations.
But the clean-energy division expectedly found no mention in Reliance’s 65-minute presentation on its October–December financials last week. Its 10-gigawatt solar-panel factory is yet to commence production, followed by a 30-gigwatt-hour battery facility—both part of a sprawling complex in Jamnagar in Gujarat, home to Reliance’s oil refinery.
The final piece of the renewables puzzle that Reliance is assembling is hydrogen made by splitting water into hydrogen and oxygen using renewable electricity. Electrolysers are the devices used for the process.
In 2021, Reliance outlined a US$10 billion investment in clean energy. It has since made several local and global acquisitions and inked partnerships to fuel its plans. I found a nifty chart on how they all come together in a report by brokerage Sanford C Bernstein from June 2023.
Bernstein also made some bold projections.
But much has changed since the report. Sustainability, for one, has shed its allure as an investment theme.
If investors were obsessed with sustainability in 2020, they are now indifferent. Oil majors are no longer trumpeting their clean-energy transition; instead, they are[ scooping up](https://www.wsj.com/business/energy-oil/big-oil-companies-will-just-keep-getting-bigger-ac194ae7) smaller rivals for billions of dollars. Before the damning report by short seller Hindenburg Research, Adani Green was worth three times as much as its coal-dependent sibling, Adani Power. Now, Adani Green is just 12% bigger.
A year ago, RK Singh, then India’s minister for power,[ reportedly](https://www.thehindu.com/business/Industry/union-government-asks-private-firms-to-ramp-up-investments-in-coal-fired-plants-despite-global-pressure/article67564574.ece) told private power producers to invest in coal-fired capacity and “not miss the growth opportunity”.
NTPC is enjoying the renewables party. But the music has stopped, [The Ken](https://the-ken.com/long_and_short/ntpc-is-enjoying-the-renewables-party-but-the-music-has-stopped/)
Banking giants like JP Morgan, and Goldman Sachs, and Blackrock, the asset manager that led the discourse around clean-energy transition during the pandemic, have all quit key climate-action networks. In one of the first decisions in his second term as US President, Donald Trump withdrew the US from the historic Paris climate deal.
He has also asked government agencies to [stop](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-01-21/trump-orders-agencies-to-halt-spending-from-biden-s-climate-law) spending money from his predecessor’s ambitious initiative to subsidise clean energy. Plus, the possibility of a tariff on solar-panel imports is very real now.
This will surely throw a spanner in the works for Indian clean-energy producers, as the US is the top overseas destination for Indian-made modules. What’s more, the flurry of announcements on new module-making capacity, including Reliance’s plan to double its own, means that in three years, India could have 40% higher capacity than is needed, according to one estimate. The battery industry is facing even stronger headwinds globally, with an inventory [glut](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2024-08-06/china-battery-makers-face-overcapacity-weaker-demand-bnef-says) and weak demand threatening to put all but the biggest out of business.
Green hydrogen, on the other hand, is yet to make the leap from potential on paper to viability on the ground.
I don’t doubt Reliance’s ability to dominate all these businesses. But what rewards await it once it reaches the top? Even analysts tracking Reliance don’t concur on much.
Morgan Stanley recently valued the new-energy business at US$19 billion. (Reliance’s market cap is more than 10X that.) Nomura pegged its worth at US$13 billion. Bernstein went even lower, at US$10.8 billion. I’m sure no one has a clue how this bet will shake out. But it’s analysts’ job to tell their clients (institutional investors) what each major part of a company is worth and, consequently, what they should pay per share. What makes their task even harder here is that Reliance’s new-energy business has no valuation benchmark.
Both Jio Platforms, which houses the telco Jio Infocomm, and Reliance Retail Ventures raised billions of dollars from tech giants, private-equity firms, and sovereign-wealth funds. So, brokerages have since anchored their valuations of these companies in the fundraises.
But their estimates for the clean-energy vertical are at best a shot in the dark.

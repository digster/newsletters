What do aerospace components and kitchen utensils have in common? The Karnataka-based precision-manufacturing company Aequs Limited.

The company lists on the bourses today with an IPO, valued at Rs 8,400 crore and subscribed 101X.

Most of this investor interest is concentrated in the company’s aerospace vertical. After all, Aequs is one of the largest aerospace-parts manufacturers in the country. It makes around 5,000 of the 6 million or so parts in a commercial aircraft, which, in turn, comprises 90% of the company’s top line.

But there’s a problem. Aequs’ business is largely dependent on demand from aircraft makers such as Boeing and Airbus. So any fluctuations, due to, say, the recent crash of Air India’s Boeing aircraft in Gujarat, impacts the supplier.

The fact that the aerospace segment has a longer payback period than consumer electronics is another point of concern.

The solution? Since 2020, the company has been expanding into manufacturing portable computers, smart screens, and kitchen pans, and it hasn’t ruled out getting into other sectors such as med-tech and semiconductors, either.

That’s because the essentials are the same, whether for aerospace components or a humble category like cookware.

In today’s story, Aakriti delves into this unusual business strategy to understand what it will take for it to work.

Amagi’s* IPO isn’t the usual investor-exit story players in the Indian markets love to talk about. The company is raising fresh capital to scale the cloud plumbing that powers thousands of TV channels worldwide. The real question is whether Indian public markets can price a deep-tech, export-led hybrid that refuses to fit into SaaS or infra templates.

In this week’s Long and Short, Sumit explains why valuing Amagi is really a test of whether the bourses are ready for deep tech that doesn’t look or behave like “Indian tech”.

Every single other Indian Fund Houses has said ‘Active is better than Passive so we focus on 100% Active (including Factor Funds)…’. A majority of Indian Funds Houses have also said: ‘…but if you falsely think otherwise, then here are one or two 100% Market-cap weighted Passive Index Funds’.

Parag Parikh MF has effectively said ‘We do not claim that for the Industry overall, Active is beating Passive.

Good Morning Ishan,
Past success is no guarantee of future popularity. This is the reality pizza chain Domino’s has been grappling with in the post-Covid era. Now, the Jubilant Foodworks-operated chain is fighting back with a recipe for resurgence, rolling out a series of initiatives designed solely to keep customers happy—from revamping its menu to enhancing the digital experience.
For starters, earlier, only orders above Rs 250 qualified for free delivery. But since Domino’s introduced free delivery for orders above Rs 150 in 2024, there has been a clear jump in online sales volumes. In fact, in some pincodes, Domino’s has lowered this threshold even further, offering free delivery for all orders above Rs 100.
Even walk-ins have jumped for the fast-food chain after it introduced a special lunch menu—a four-course meal—priced as low as Rs 99. This includes a small pizza, a snack, a drink, and a dessert. For the non-vegetarian variant (which they call a “lunch feast”), there is chicken rice and two chicken snacks, along with a drink and a dessert. Takers for these meals include both college students and office-goers who don’t want to have lunch under the hot sun, but also want a decently priced meal, one Domino’s customer in his early 20s told The Ken.
Now, Domino’s rivals such as KFC, Pizza Hut, and Wow!Momo have all started their own version of this lunch menu to boost their sales. But none have shown the kind of results that Domino’s has.
For the last three quarters, Domino’s like-for-like sales growth (also called same-store sales growth) has been between 11.6–12.5%. In the latest June quarter, this number stood at 11.6%, while for its rivals like Devyani International’s KFC and Pizza Hut, it stood at -0.7% and -4.2%, respectively. Mcdonald’s and Burger King’s SSSG came in at 0.5% and 2.6%, respectively. In fact, Domino’s has outperformed its peers in this metric for the last eleven quarters, per a September report by brokerage firm Antique Stock Broking.
This is a clear reversal from where Domino’s found itself about two years ago. This is from our January 2024 story titled [“Are Indians falling out of love with Domino’s?”](https://the-ken.com/story/pizza-dominos-thats-no-longer-true/):
“Under the tagline “It happens only with pizza”, the new Domino’s pizza box reveals the brand’s facelift. But the two-month exercise isn’t just another routine effort to drive up pizza sales for local franchisee Jubilant Foodworks.
Instead, it’s unfolding against the backdrop of a broader slowdown in fast-food consumption. What’s more, the American chain, seemingly immune to such slumps, has not had it so bad in years.
Jubilant Foodworks’ same-store-sales growth (SSSG) declined 1.3% in the quarter ended September 2023. Its burger-making rivals Westlife Foodworld (Mcdonald’s franchisee) and Restaurant Brands Asia (Burger King’s franchisee) posted 3.5% and 1% growth, respectively, during the same period.”
At that time, it was battling competition that had barged in on the casual-dining front through specialised pizza chains. It was also facing challenges from regional rivals such as La Pino’z, which were growing rapidly during the pandemic thanks to food aggregator platforms like Zomato and Swiggy.
But Domino’s has greased its squeaky wheel well since then.
It has doubled down on its strategy of making deliveries more attractive to customers. On the app, Domino’s now clearly markets a 20-minute delivery guarantee, down from 30 minutes earlier. It has introduced the fast-growing chicken category into its product range, something rivals like KFC and [Mcdonald’s](https://the-ken.com/story/mcdonalds-broke-with-the-past-and-stepped-into-kfc-territory-investors-are-lovin-it/) have been benefitting from for a while now. And it has been innovating with healthier options like [sourdough pizzas](https://www.afaqs.com/news/advertising/dominos-rolls-out-new-sourdough-pizza-with-new-ad-film-10501132), which it launched just last week.
Domino’s has also started playing the “discounting” game. If you open the Domino’s app on your phone, you will now see discounts of as much as 50% on some meals. For instance, its “Big 6-in-1” pizza is priced between Rs 1,579 and Rs 2,079, but is always available at half that rate on its app. Similarly, their chicken platter, priced at Rs 499, is available for Rs 299—a flat 40% discount.
These offers are very similar to the playbook adopted by brands on food aggregator apps like Zomato and Swiggy, where many popular, emerging food outlets perpetually sell at a discount.
Sameer Khetarpal, the CEO of Jubilant Foodworks, who joined the chain in 2022 after spending almost seven years at tech-giant Amazon, has spoken about how the trend of ordering food has continued since the pandemic, fuelling sales through online channels. This was Khetarpal in an analyst call last month:
“Firstly, eating at home from QSRs, whether through carryout or delivery, is a worldwide trend, right? And aggregators have obviously accelerated it. Covid boosted it. And consumers are looking to eat at home more and more; the QSR food, which stands for value…
And then, when it comes to delivery accuracy, delivery comfort, ease of ordering, and the value that we give—these are underlying factors that are fueling delivery. So, if I’m pressed for time, which is happening more and more in our cities—we are becoming more urban, more nuclear—those underlying demographic trends are there. That is what is fueling delivery.”
In the latest June quarter, 72.9% of the pizza chain’s orders were from online channels—a marked jump from 62.4% two years ago.
Even in terms of capex, the company’s focus has been geared towards technology; it has been spending more on tech and improving its app than on enhancing the look and feel of its stores, according to a consumer analyst. Domino’s usually spends Rs 1 crore to upgrade a store (compared to Rs 60 lakh for rival chain La Pino’z) and has plans to reduce its capex per store by 15% each year, per an Antique Stock Broking report.
But having said that, while its sales have grown at a compounded annual growth rate of 22.8% in the last three years, profits have seen a negative CAGR of 20%, according to data from a financial data platform Screener. Even in terms of operating margins, Domino’s is hovering around 19%, down from a peak of 25% in March 2022.
This is something that analysts at Dalal Street are worried about.
The chain’s management attributes some of this to competition. Since the number of players in the space have increased in the last three to four years, “consumers” are now “spoilt for choice”, and hence the company has to be very “efficient on the pricing (front)”, Khetarpal told analysts in response to a barrage of questions, adding that some of those past margins were “not sustainable if you want to protect your consumer franchise”. But the company does plan to improve its margins going forward.
In the meantime, though, it’s clear the delivery fleet in yellow helmets and blue-and-red shirts will continue to take fewer minutes to deliver orders in increasingly more locations, and that Domino’s will continue to focus on keeping all its customers happy: whether they are value-eaters, chicken maniacs, or sourdough enthusiasts.

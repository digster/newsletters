Good Morning Ishan,

About 18 months ago, when the world was a very different place, I was looking to buy a two-wheeler for my work commute. I really wanted an electric bike. More than its environmental benefits, I was really drawn to the sound of an electric motor—it's like a spaceship from Star Trek at warp speed.

Unfortunately, I couldn't afford one back then. A scooter from Ather, India’s leading high-performance electric scooter maker, cost around Rs 1.5 lakh (US$2,000)—almost double the price of its premium petrol cousins. Considering India's almost non-existent charging infrastructure at the time, the investment didn't seem worth it. And I didn't have much choice in the EV market. Other electric two-wheeler startups had launched some very cool prototypes, but that was it.

Today, the world has flipped—in favour of electric two-wheelers. Petrol prices are touching Rs 100 (US$1.35) per litre in many Indian cities, and the Indian government recently increased its subsidy for electric two-wheelers by 50%. An Ather bike is now just 15% more expensive than comparable high-performance petrol scooters.

One thing that hasn't changed, though, is that Ather still does not have much competition. EV startups like Tork Motors (founded in 2009), UltraViolette (2015), Orxa Energies (2015), and Emflux Motors (2016) have been around for over half a decade but have still not launched any bikes for sale. In fact, as Pranav reports, they've been pushed to the brink, no thanks to the pandemic. Emflux Motors, which showcased a sleek and cutting-edge electric super bike in 2018, is now making ceiling fans and face shields to earn revenue.

It's not like India isn't a conducive market for electric two-wheelers. Ather, and Ola Electric, from the house of ride-hailing unicorn Ola Cabs, have raised around US$130 million and US$326 million, respectively. Ather's new production facility is capable of producing up to 110,000 scooters and 120,000 battery packs a year. Ola Electric's Future Factory hopes to churn out 10 million electric scooters a year by 2022.

However, the funding gap between Ather and UltraViolette, the next highest-funded EV startup that’s also involved in R&D in design and manufacturing, is US$133 million.

Why? Will the bikes of these other EV companies ever see the light of the day?

Good Morning Ishan,
In sci-fi, the future usually kicks in with flashing lights, moody synths, and a big guy in a leather trench coat delivering a baritone whisper, “We’re not so different, you and I.” In real life, it’s quieter. Less dramatic. Sometimes, the future looks like a black ring of lab-grown retinal cells at the bottom of a vial.
That’s what’s happening in a 1,200-square-foot office in Bengaluru, where a 14-person company called Eyestem is trying to cure a form of degenerative eye disease that slowly blinds nearly 200 million people. A disease with no cure. And it has raised just about $20 million, which couldn’t cover snacks at a midsize Boston biotech if it tried.
Naturally, at least five global pharma companies are circling. But hang tight—we’ll come back to that.
If you’re in Bengaluru today, we are doing a live event—on the future of work—you’ll want to attend. To be honest, the idea of a stable, linear career path is looking … not so stable or linear. AI is rearranging jobs, layoffs are more frequent, and the old-school career ladder has turned into something more like a jungle gym. So, how do you build a “career lattice”—something flexible, something resilient?
My colleagues Rohin Dharmakumar and Praveen Gopal Krishnan will be unpacking that with Harshil Mathur (Razorpay), Vasuta Agarwal (Inmobi), and Sourav Mukherji (IIM Bangalore).
Time: 5 pm to 7:30 pm | Venue: Bangalore International Centre Auditorium | [Get your ticket here](https://the-ken.com/event/building-unique-career-lattices/?utm_source=spotlight&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=bfo_event). (We’re almost sold out—just a handful of seats left.)
Now, back to Eyestem.
Led by Jogin Desai, a former pharma executive who turned a “private grief to a public cause”, the company is using retinal cells sourced from an NIH bank, scaled up in Indian labs, manufactured in Hyderabad, and priced as if they actually want people to use it. The entire treatment course is targeted to cost under $10,000. Not per shot. Per treatment.
One trial patient, legally blind, walked down a hallway. That alone should’ve-could’ve made headlines. Because Eyestem isn’t some scrappy biotech—it’s one of only three companies in the world to show real results for cell therapy. And it’s a genuine attempt to solve a massive medical problem at scale.

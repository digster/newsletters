Are you familiar with Match Day?

No, it has nothing to do with dating or sports. Each year, medical students in the US await the third Friday of March with keen anticipation. It’s when they learn where they will be doing their residencies for the next several years. 

From what I gather, it’s a fairly complicated algorithm, but this is how it loosely works.

Graduating students explore all the different residency programmes available to them, check out the facilities, and meet faculty members. They then register themselves on a portal, rank the programmes in order of preference, and submit an application. The programmes then go through these applications and rank the students in order of their own preference. 

After both sides are done, the algorithm does its magic and matches all the students with residency programmes. The third Friday of March is when the students decide which programme they want to go with. Hence, Match Day. (If you’d like to get a clearer picture on how it all works, I’d recommend watching this Youtube video.)

The reason I bring this up is because it’s very closely linked to the subject of our latest Two by Two podcast. In fact, this episode was my first time hearing about such an algorithm; our guest for the week—Varun Nagaraj, Dean and Professor of Information Management & Analytics at S P Jain Institute of Management and Research (SPJIMR), Mumbai—used it to illustrate how B-school placements could change for the better in India. 

With Two by Two hosts Rohin Dharmakumar and Praveen Gopal Krishnan adding their own MBA experiences, this episode is a fun, candid conversation on how Indian B-school placements have evolved over the years, and what needs to change to drive better outcomes for students.

Let the ‘grown-ups’ do it

In India, B-school students are a critical part of placement committees and have to take on much of the responsibility for getting everyone in their batch placed. In the US, most leading B-schools have professional teams that run the entire process, finding quality jobs for hundreds of students each year. 

Prof. Nagaraj, who came back from the US to take up his position at SPJIMR three years ago, admits he remains puzzled by how most Indian B-schools go about placements even now.

Prof. Nagaraj: I’d been out of the country for 30 years, and certainly, this was not how things were done at IIT-Bombay back in 1986.

These are all new trends that happened over the last 30 years that I was blissfully unaware of.

And so I must say it was actually quite a very big surprise to me that this is how placements—or what one might call career services—are delivered in Indian business schools and other institutions.

At SPJIMR, according to the professor, the system consists of around 25 students on the placement committee working with four to six professionals (or “grown-ups”) who are in charge of the whole process. However, he notes that he would love it was run entirely by professionals. 

One reason is relationship management.

Prof. Nagaraj: The idea of relationship management goes beyond placements, and this is something that Shiv refers to in his Linkedin post.

What you’re looking at is an institution that has a set of relationships with its ‘customers’. And this goes beyond just the industry in which we place people. I would say our customers are societies and organisations at large.

If you take a long-term view, it is the society that’s our customer. Companies are a form of societal organisation. 

So if that’s the case, then all the connections and relationships that an institution has—whether it is for purposes of the first job, whether it is for purposes of ongoing consulting, whether it’s for purposes of being in advisory roles with boards, whether it’s for purposes of executive education—these represent relationships that an institution like SPJIMR, or any other management school, should cultivate and nurture carefully.

Let’s not call it ‘placements’

Prof. Nagaraj: The second point I’m going to make is that I think we shouldn’t even be using the word ‘placements’ as the driving factor.

Rohin: Thank you, Professor!

Placements are probably one of the most common metrics that B-schools use to woo students. But it’s not a very open process. And the word itself may signal the wrong priorities.

Prof. Nagaraj: People hold on to their first job these days for a relatively short period of time.

And yes, the first job matters, because perhaps the salary there determines your second job salary and so forth.

Back in the day, there was this movie called ‘Deewar’, where Amitabh Bachchan is called, ‘Yeh lambi race ka ghoda hai’ (A horse that can win the long race)

To be a ‘lambi race ka ghoda’ is what we’re trying to create here. If society is to benefit from the efforts we are putting in to train and enable the next generation of leaders, it should be over a sustained period of time.

Placements just put a very short-term and instrumental layer on what ought to be a much more joyful, long-term, satisfying process. 

Let’s just call it professional development or career development.

Just a few days ago, both The Economist and The Wall Street Journal published stories about how the job market for B-school grads has started to shrink, even for candidates from the most elite universities. 

Going to a top B-school isn’t cheap, and if they have a loan to repay, students can become risk-averse and take jobs they might not necessarily want to. Having a team of professionals managing the “professional development” of graduating students could help them find better, more desired prospects.

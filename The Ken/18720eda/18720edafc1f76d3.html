Good Morning Ishan,
 
A major transition is underway in India’s power sector. The country—one of the largest greenhouse-gas emitters—has taken the bull by the horns and decided to plunge headfirst into the green-energy arena. 
 
Of course, energy giants like the Adani Group, Reliance Industries, L&T, and Indian Oil are all jostling for a piece of the renewables pie. But the biggest testament to this metamorphosis is the story of Mohit Bhargava and his renewable-energy division at the country’s largest thermal-power producer NTPC.
 
Upon taking the reins in 2019, Bhargava faced the Herculean task of steering the largest shift in the company's half-a-century existence. A lot has happened since: with a rise in the investment ceiling on NTPC Green Energy and oil-and-gas giant Petronas reportedly offering to invest US$462 million in it, the transition is in full swing. As a result, NTPC has tripled its operational clean-energy capacity to 3.2 GW since 2020, and is targeting 60 GW by 2032.
 
Even as it was late to the renewables party, the company made up for the lost time by bidding big and lower than its relatively older rivals. 
 
And the ability to outmanoeuvre its competitors and access cheaper capital has made NTPC a top choice for investors betting on India's energy-transition story. Its shares have risen by ~30% in the past year, while Adani Green and Tata Power have seen their value decline by 50% and 20%, respectively. 
 
This new commitment is also creating a new identity for the company. As Bhargava tells Seetharaman, the author of today’s story, “We were kind of a late entrant. We had to adapt to the new system and bidding process.”
 
NTPC's green move must now sustain its success while becoming more than just a clean-energy producer, as Tata Power—a major player in EV charging and solar manufacturing—has done to great effect.

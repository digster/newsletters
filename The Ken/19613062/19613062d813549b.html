HUL is obsessed with “wellbeing”. It needs something new to pick up the slack in one of its core businesses
By Seetharaman G
6 min read

Last week, Hindustan Unilever (HUL) launched a US-based brand in India that its British parent had bought in 2020. Liquid IV is a powdered hydration beverage that HUL claims is “better than water” and has  “3X the electrolytes of the leading sports drink and 7 vitamins and nutrients”. 

The launch is of a piece with the fast-moving-consumer-goods powerhouse’s newest obsession: “health and wellbeing”. Over two years ago, HUL bought into two nutritional-supplement brands—Oziva and Wellbeing Nutrition. 

At first glance, this is commonsensical. It’s hard to ignore wellness regardless of whether you sell something that people put on their skin or into their bodies. 

But HUL has never been like Marico, quick to latch on to a “trend” by making acquisitions. HUL’s size dictates the choice of bets that are worth its while. 

Its top brand alone, Surf Excel (detergent), could have sales nearly equal to Marico’s revenue in FY25. So any diversification or acquisition has to lead to an outsize outcome. Which is why, when rivals like Marico and Emami were buying digital-first cosmetics brands for a few hundred crores, HUL bought Minimalist for almost Rs 3,000 crore, in January 2025.

Minimalist stands out in the morass of VC-backed also-rans. It steered the conversation in skincare from just making lofty claims to actually talking about a product’s active ingredients. Minimalist even managed to force HUL, well before the acquisition, to change how it sold Pond’s anti-ageing cream, as my colleague Nuha argued in a story last year.

If HUL likes swinging for the fences, then the Oziva and Wellbeing deals were anything but. They are so tiny as to be inconsequential relative to HUL. The investments valued the companies, respectively, at Rs 530 crore and Rs 350 crore.

So why pursue them in the first place?

I believe it has to do with finding a new business to pick up the slack in its foods business. It just doesn’t measure up to personal care and home care. It is considerably smaller than the other two in both sales and profits.

Foods will matter even less to HUL with its decision to spin off its ice-cream business, in line with its parent’s global strategy. It’s a telling example of HUL’s struggles in this category. HUL ventured into ice creams three years before Amul in the 1990s, but has only managed to scale to one-fifth the size of the latter. 

But can wellness deliver what foods couldn’t? Vitamins, minerals, and supplements are a Rs 30,000 crore market, according to research firm Euromonitor and HUL. The fact that no one single name towers over it like HUL in FMCG is just as appetising for the company. 

Much like why Amul is interested in protein. 

Since 2022, India’s largest fast-moving consumer goods brand has rolled out a slew of whey-infused products, including lassi, buttermilk, blueberry shake, and protein powder. The latest in line is its high-protein dahi (curd), launched in January. The 400 ml pack offers 25g protein for Rs 70 (US$0.84).

For Amul, launching a protein-focussed line-up is intended to fill a gap in the nutritional needs of India’s protein-deficient and significant vegetarian population, said [Amul’s managing director Jayen] Mehta.

And he’s not wrong; about 73% of Indians have insufficient protein levels. “There can’t be a better [option] than whey protein” to include the essential macronutrient into vegetarian diets, Mehta told The Ken.

So, Amul, India’s largest manufacturer of cheese and paneer (cottage cheese), is repurposing the protein-rich byproduct of its dairy production—for the masses this time. It’s been around the whey block since 2005, supplying demineralised whey powder (also known as DM 40) to bakeries in 25-kg sacks. This is commonly used in biscuits, infant formulas, and soft baked goods such as bread.

Amul made India the world’s top milk producer. Its next target is protein, The Ken

As with Amul, HUL’s most obvious edge over rivals is distribution. Even if it can sell its supplements and serums only in a fraction of the outlets that stock its soaps and teas, that number could still be sizeable—HUL’s current total reach is a stonking 9 million stores. 

Wellness is clearly not a business where volume is the path to dominance for any company, simply because there isn’t yet a large enough section of the population that cares for it. Liquid IV, for instance, retails at Rs 2,400 for a pack of 24 sachets, and is currently sold only on HUL’s own website, Amazon, and Blinkit.

But HUL, despite its range of mass-market products, is no stranger to success higher up the price pyramid.

If HUL’s last decade was powered by the astonishing growth of detergents, it was partly thanks to shoppers opting for pricier variants. 

But in wellness, HUL has to first persuade consumers that they need these products.

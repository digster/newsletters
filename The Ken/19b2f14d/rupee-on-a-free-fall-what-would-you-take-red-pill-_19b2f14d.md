A weekly newsletter about how finance is getting supercharged by tech in India, and how you can make money work for you.
Good Morning Ishan,
Pankaj Vidyarthi, an IT professional, is counting down to his wedding day. Like most Indian grooms, his biggest worry isn’t the venue or the guest list. It’s the gold. He needs Rs 10–15 lakh worth of jewellery for his bride. Bad timing. Gold has climbed to an all-time high of Rs 1.37 lakh per 10 grams as of 15 December. With the rupee depreciating to its lowest value, things have only become more difficult for Vidyarthi.
There’s more. His younger brother studies at an Ivy League university and has asked for an extra Rs 5 lakh this coming year.
The reason is simple and brutal: the rupee. It has depreciated over 7% in a year, from Rs 85 in January to roughly Rs 91 this week. In five years, it’s fallen nearly 19%.
“If I am buying jewellery worth Rs 15 lakh, it’s almost Rs 1 lakh costlier simply due to rupee depreciation,” says Vidyarthi.
For the past year, the rupee has been Asia’s worst-performing currency. In recent months, it’s performed even worse than the Pakistani rupee.
Three economists associated with various banks, whom we spoke to, believe the rupee could cross 95 next year, driven mainly by India’s import-heavy economy and fading foreign investor confidence amid Trump-era uncertainties.
India’s current account deficit fell to 0.6% of GDP in FY25 due to strong services exports. Foreign investment inflows weakened, the rupee came under pressure, and foreign exchange reserves remained comfortable despite fluctuations.
Finance minister Nirmala Sitharaman’s familiar stance—that it’s not a weak rupee but a strong dollar—is now wearing thin.

Most of us choose the “blue pill”. We shrug, talk about exports doing better and IT companies earning in dollars, and move on. But take the red pill, and the impact shows up everywhere.
India imports most of its gold. In November 2025, gold imports fell by 60% despite a $42 [reduction](https://www.india.com/business/will-gold-and-silver-get-cheaper-govt-cuts-base-import-price-what-does-this-mean-8161073/) in base prices, largely due to international price increases, and the Indian government’s consistent effort to reduce the current account deficit.
Vegetable oil—of which India is the world’s largest importer—saw imports fall by over 28% last month. The primary reason was reduced imports of refined, bleached, and deodorised (RBD) palmolein, a key processed palm oil derivative. Rupee depreciation is also [said](https://www.business-standard.com/article/markets/vegetable-oil-import-falls-23-in-june-due-to-rupee-depreciation-118071600382_1.html) to have contributed to the decline.
Electronics are getting 3–7% costlier. Havells and Godrej Appliances are planning hikes of up to 10%. Mercedes-Benz India is considering price corrections starting in January.
Even pulses, which have already [reached](https://vajiramandravi.com/current-affairs/indias-import-burden-rising-costs-of-pulses-and-edible-oils/) an all-time high in imports, along with fresh and dry fruits, are being impacted by the weakening rupee.
Not to mention crude oil-based products, which India mostly imports.
Beauty and personal care products—either imported directly or made from imported raw materials—are also being hit.
Back to Vidyarthi. He’s quietly switching from 22-carat jewellery at Tanishq to 18-carat options at Caratlane or Mia. A small decision. A telling one.
The red pill doesn’t stop there. It changes how you invest. My portfolio advisor’s counsel now is to consider export-oriented companies that earn in dollars is one rational response. However, the story isn’t so simple; a weaker rupee doesn’t automatically benefit all exporters. But it does seem like the safer bet.
Some investors are nudging allocations toward foreign stocks or even dollar-pegged stablecoins as diversification. Not because it’s trendy, but because the math demands it. India’s diaspora has increasingly turned to stablecoins like USDC and Tether (USDT), which have significantly reduced transaction fees, often below 3%, and accelerated transfer times, according to various [reports](https://www.fgveurope.de/wp-content/uploads/2025/02/Research-Study-Emily-Ekshian.pdf).
Investments in gold have always been an age-old tradition for Indians. The depreciation in the rupee has only made this more lucrative.
At the core, diversify with a little more focus on earning in dollars, my advisor says. It’s worth mentioning that under the Liberalised Remittance Scheme (LRS), Indians can remit up to $250,000 a year for offshore investments. Multiple platforms, like INDMoney, now offer access to US stocks starting from just $1.
What about policy? How concerned is the Reserve Bank of India (RBI) about rupee depreciation?
The RBI governor has stated the bank doesn’t target specific levels, only volatility. Responding to question about the “falling rupee” in a press conference, governor Sanjay Malhotra said,
“We saw this earlier in February; the rupee-to-dollar rate had climbed up to almost 88. And within a period of three months, it came back to below 84. So, these fluctuations, this volatility does happen, can happen. Our effort has always been to reduce any abnormal or excessive volatility.”
Until India fixes its export-import imbalance and rebuilds confidence, the dollar will keep winning. The rupee’s fall is no longer an abstract concept. It’s reflected in wedding bills, college fees, grocery prices, and the daily choices we’re forced to make.
Red pill or blue pill? That’s our only choice in this matter.

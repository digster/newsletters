Not B2B, not B2C, Nitara’s Manish Jain is betting on 80 million farmers and his F2F platform to correct the dairy market asymmetry
India is a world leader in milk production. Yet, the market is largely unfocused, fragmented, and riddled with misconceptions. A former investment banker, Manish Jain, has spent the past eight years trying to understand the 80 million farmers behind India’s $30 billion dairy industry. Nitara, his platform, pioneers a farm-to-friend approach, promising productivity, transparency, and efficiency. By focusing on the cow and the farmer first rather than the milk’s contents, Jain aims to fix a dysfunctional system from the bottom up. “At every stage, I saw a system trying to consistently mine the farmer, and that farmer mining the cattle,” he said. “Our vision is to upgrade the market as we go along, correct the information asymmetry that exists between the buyer and seller today.” Grounded in the science of genetics, nutrition, and management, he counters the existing industry norms by bringing data and digitisation, believing that to milk the situation, he needs to first fix the ecosystem. Short of faculty and strapped for money, public universities are hiring a great many guest teachers to plug the gap. Much to the chagrin of academics and students alike
Indian universities, even the better ones, are increasingly reliant on contract teachers. The higher education regulator, UGC, had imposed a 10% limit on employing contract teachers, but the rule was widely flouted. Instead of coming down on the institutions, the UGC removed the limit in its latest draft regulations. By thus giving universities a free rein to hire temporary teachers, the UGC has put higher education in a precarious position. The Supreme Court’s recent guidelines and a state’s AI mandate hint at a fragmented overhaul—one testing whether tech can cut through decades of procedural drag
AI tools are entering Indian courts unevenly, with some states pushing ahead through mandates and others relying on scattered pilots. Startups such as Adalat and Nyaay are automating chokepoints, but their solutions touch only a fraction of the systemic delays. Legacy workflows, uneven capacity, and missing governance frameworks limit how far and how fast AI can scale across 18,000+ district courts and 25 High Courts. New policy signals, including the Supreme Court’s White Paper, show the judiciary finally grappling with AI’s risks and potential.

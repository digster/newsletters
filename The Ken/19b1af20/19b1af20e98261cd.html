Parag Parikh MF: the rebel that finally conformed

A former i-banker’s productivity fix for India’s $30B dairy industry: listen to the farm, not the farmer

Not B2B, not B2C, Nitara’s Manish Jain is betting on 80 million farmers and his F2F platform to correct the dairy market asymmetry

India is a world leader in milk production. Yet, the market is largely unfocused, fragmented, and riddled with misconceptions. A former investment banker, Manish Jain, has spent the past eight years trying to understand the 80 million farmers behind India’s $30 billion dairy industry. Nitara, his platform, pioneers a farm-to-friend approach, promising productivity, transparency, and efficiency. By focusing on the cow and the farmer first rather than the milk’s contents, Jain aims to fix a dysfunctional system from the bottom up. “At every stage, I saw a system trying to consistently mine the farmer, and that farmer mining the cattle,” he said. “Our vision is to upgrade the market as we go along, correct the information asymmetry that exists between the buyer and seller today.” Grounded in the science of genetics, nutrition, and management, he counters the existing industry norms by bringing data and digitisation, believing that to milk the situation, he needs to first fix the ecosystem. Gig work takes over the college classroom

Short of faculty and strapped for money, public universities are hiring a great many guest teachers to plug the gap. Much to the chagrin of academics and students alike

Indian universities, even the better ones, are increasingly reliant on contract teachers. The higher education regulator, UGC, had imposed a 10% limit on employing contract teachers, but the rule was widely flouted. Instead of coming down on the institutions, the UGC removed the limit in its latest draft regulations. By thus giving universities a free rein to hire temporary teachers, the UGC has put higher education in a precarious position. [Read the full story]

Boeing to Wonderchef: Aequs’ disparate clientele tests its manufacturing edge

Building parts for Airbus and Boeing gave Aequs 90% of its revenue. To hedge risks, it’s now tilting toward making more frying pans, casseroles, and toys

Aequs, an aerospace-parts maker, listed on the bourses on 10 December, coming off an IPO that got subscribed 101 times. Despite serving marquee aerospace clients, the company took nearly two decades to reach a top line of nearly Rs 1,000 crore. That’s largely because the demand in aerospace is cyclical. If a planemaker sneezes, its suppliers catch the cold, too. So, as Aequs goes public, it’s betting on its other business—making kitchenware and toys—to scale. [Read the full story]

Handwritten chaos vs machine order: how AI is splitting India’s judiciary

The Supreme Court’s recent guidelines and a state’s AI mandate hint at a fragmented overhaul—one testing whether tech can cut through decades of procedural drag

AI tools are entering Indian courts unevenly, with some states pushing ahead through mandates and others relying on scattered pilots. Startups such as Adalat and Nyaay are automating chokepoints, but their solutions touch only a fraction of the systemic delays. Legacy workflows, uneven capacity, and missing governance frameworks limit how far and how fast AI can scale across 18,000+ district courts and 25 High Courts. New policy signals, including the Supreme Court’s White Paper, show the judiciary finally grappling with AI’s risks and potential. [Read the full story]

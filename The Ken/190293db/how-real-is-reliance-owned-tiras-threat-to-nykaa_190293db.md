Good Morning Ishan,
A vending machine that dispenses free samples, another that personalises gift products, an app on a tablet that analyses your skin, a fragrance finder…
These are meant to be not just tech touches but little fairy dusts to take Reliance’s newest bet, Tira, into the top league of beauty-products retailing.
Surveys from Google, Kantar, and WPP show that Indian consumers of beauty and personal-care products are open to interacting with new technologies. Nearly seven of every 10 consumers are “interested” in virtual reality.
Tira set out to capitalise on this. A year after its launch, hoping to capture consumer imagination both offline and online, the beauty retailer is caught between Nykaa (with more than a third of the market share) and Myntra (which holds 9% of the market just by dint of being in the fashion business).
To its credit, Tira has onboarded several exclusive brands, but it’s not opened as many offline stores as many had expected. Nuha keeps a hawk’s eye on this sector and says Tira needs more than Reliance’s name to beat Nykaa. Voda-Idea’s debt evergreening
The struggling third-largest telecom operator, Voda-Idea, whose stocks have been comatose for years, is suddenly doling out equity. Vi issued preferential shares to Nokia and Ericsson to pay off its dues.
Given the financial health of the vendors themselves, the two European companies would have any day preferred cash to equity. Why? I’ll let the Vi share price history answer this—it’s stayed in the Rs12-16 range for a full five years.
As we’ve written before, a company with a negative net worth giving away equity is not a matter of celebration. As much of an anomaly as it is in the business world, it is also pushing a financial crisis down the road. Vi will continue to bleed customers and lose market share, say all analysts tracking Indian telcos.
But Vi is a public sector company, at least by ownership, and the third term of the Modi government is perhaps instilling confidence in the vendors who’ve taken Vi’s offer. By taking equity in Vi, instead of its outstanding dues, the government “provided sectorial relief” and allowed Vi to “survive”, a telecom official had explained this to us last year. The government expected other “chips to fall in place”.
A business daily reported on Monday that Vi is considering issuing more shares to its vendors to clear more dues. The promoter stake is being diluted. Who’s really placing chips on Vi is an interesting story to follow.

Long and Short
Wed, 06 Aug 25
The story behind the numbers and motivations driving India's stock market..
Good Morning Ishan,
Tata Capital’s much-awaited IPO, reportedly an over Rs 17,000 crore offering, took a key step—a giant leap if you will—on 4 August. The company filed its updated draft red herring prospectus (DRHP) with market regulator Sebi, and it confirmed what The Ken [reported](https://the-ken.com/story/tata-capitals-ipo-has-a-tata-motors-problem/) on 25 March.
Tata Motors Finance is indeed a mighty drag on Tata Capital.
The auto financier’s proposed merger with the IPO-bound company got the go-ahead from the company court in May, effective from April 2024. Essentially, Tata Capital’s financial performance for FY25 includes that of Tata Motors Finance. Thankfully, the 795-page DRHP—unlike the confidential one the company filed in April—is on public display with a lot of details. Even Tata Capital’s financial performance, both including and excluding Tata Motors Finance.
Compare the numbers, and it’s clear why Tata Capital would have been much better off without the vehicle financier. Sure, it adds to the loan book size and revenue, but Tata Motors Finance chips away at Tata Capital’s profits and messes with its asset quality. Here’s how:
Excluding the vehicle financier, Tata Capital’s profit for the year ended March 2025 was about Rs 3,710 crore. Including it, it fell to Rs 3,670 crore. Simply because Tata Motors Finance incurred a loss.
By itself, Tata Capital’s non-performing assets (NPA)—a measure of bad loans—in FY25 were well-contained at 0.5% of its total loans. Add Tata Motors Finance to the mix, and the number increased to 0.8%. That’s because by itself, the latter’s NPA during the year was as high as 4.4%.
Tata Capital’s return on equity—a key profitability metric—was 14.2%. But Tata Motors Finance dragged it down to 12.6%. Why? Because the latter’s return on equity was a negative 4.1%. Tata Capital’s credit cost for the year, too, nearly doubled from about Rs 1,500 crore to Rs 2,800 crore—thanks, again, to Tata Motors Finance.
It goes on for a host of other financial and operational parameters, too.
As an analyst told The Ken in March, a below-average Tata Motors Finance will turn an above-average Tata Capital into an average business overall. Tata Motors Finance is below-average because of the nature of its business—a “captive” financier exposed to high-risk borrowers and with low margins. The vehicle financier’s size boon becomes a quality bane.
The trouble really is that Tata Motors Finance isn’t really built to be a moneymaker. It’s a captive lender, existing not to maximise its own profits but to boost vehicle sales for Tata Motors. That’s just how these things work—Hinduja Leyland Finance, which funds Ashok Leyland trucks, faces the same problem.
Tata Capital’s IPO has a Tata Motors problem, [The Ken](https://the-ken.com/story/tata-capitals-ipo-has-a-tata-motors-problem/)
This has costs that Tata Capital is now bearing after the merger.
Captive financiers like Tata Motors Finance are exposed to all kinds of borrowers, especially those that others won’t service, and this results in adverse customer selection. Such borrowers are more likely to default on loans than others in tough times. It doesn’t help that the commercial-vehicle market, where Tata Motors is a major player, is now going through a rough patch.
Despite the higher risk, captive financiers are unable to charge such borrowers commensurately higher rates. Simply because if the customer walks away due to the higher cost, it will be a loss of business volumes for the vehicle maker, Tata Motors in this case.
Experts The Ken had spoken to said that the captive business of Tata Motors Finance will continue even after its merger with Tata Capital.
The IPO is a highly anticipated one—coming from a group that’s among India’s most trusted, with a diversified business model, good growth, and profits to boot. The IPO will likely count among India’s five-largest IPOs so far.
The issue’s pricing and dates also shouldn’t take too long to come—Tata Capital is racing against time. The company, as one of India’s largest non-banks, has to list before September 2025, thanks to the RBI’s diktat.
Tata Capital may not have had a choice about taking Tata Motors Finance into its fold. Tata Sons, the ultimate parent company of both firms, would have called the shots on this. Group compulsions would have forced Tata Capital to go along and take one for the team.
Had it chosen to list without the merger, Tata Capital could likely have commanded a better valuation. But now, a “captive” discount will apply—that is, the IPO’s pricing should be lower than what it could have been to account for the negatives. Investors should factor this in.

Poll results: How Indian kids reacted to the pandemic

Olina Banerji

One of the trickiest conundrums to emerge out of the pandemic in India is: what exactly do you do with children? They’re (still) too young to be vaccinated. Their schools have been shut, on and off, through the last 15 months. They’ve stewed inside through two summers now, away from the school playground and their friends. The trickle towards normalcy for kids, arguably, has been much slower than us vaccinated, working adults. 

Let’s face it. No one likes online classes. So, the demand for school reopening is growing stronger by the day. There’s definitely an academic incentive, since most kids are going to fall behind a grade level or two because online classes can’t really replicate everything from a physical one. But the larger worry is the healthy development of a young mind that’s been shuttered inside cramped homes during one of the worst health crises the world has ever seen. 

It’s a defining moment in terms of what matters the most in a child’s life. Last week, The Ken conducted a survey for its readers to find out what exactly has been the impact on children’s behaviour, screen time, grades, etc. The survey, designed to capture a broader impact than just academics, also focused on what kids fear the most, and what help parents have sought to handle the 24 hours kids now need to spend indoors. A few disclosures about the survey, before we get into the results:

  1. Indian children come from a wide economic and societal span. The results from this survey will pertain to only a sliver of this much larger group.
  2. We had 475 parents/guardians respond to this survey. While the sample size is small, there are stark observations that were interesting enough for us to put forward in a piece, and try and make sense of what this means for children’s well being.

For a country obsessed with good grades and spending hard-earned income on tuition classes, academics took a bit of a back seat during the pandemic. The survey indicates that 39% of the respondents thought their kids’ academic performance stayed intact, and 13% even believed it “improved” throughout this massive experiment with online learning. 

This is a complex metric to unpack, since there’s no third-party assessment of grades in private schools to determine if these metrics hold true. For government or low-income private schools, however, the loss of in-person school days have had a severe impact on learning levels, as The Ken reported earlier.

There’s also the possibility that schools in these economic strata have the means to conduct better online classes, adapt curriculum to virtual lessons better, and create opportunities for non-academic discussions online. Unmitigated access to devices also plays a huge role in how well virtual education can be managed.

So, where is it that kids—according to their parents—have been impacted the most? When asked about “physical” or “mental” health, the survey results were inconclusive. While parents definitely see the impact in both cases, their top concern is their kids’ “ability to make friends”. In parallel, the biggest concern for kids is the “restrictions on going out to play”. In both lists of concerns, those around academic growth rate were dead last.

The first and fourth positions in this list of concerns is telling. It also lines up with other findings from the survey, which indicate that for 31% of the respondents, their kids’ non-academic (or non-school) screen time had increased by 100%.

Screen time, at least under a complete lockdown, was a necessary evil. But the impact of experiencing the outside world through a screen is going to have long-lasting implications. Ironically, it’s going to weaken ties with the very thing it was looking to emulate. One of our readers also took time out to tell us their specific experience, which succinctly captures this impact:

“My kids, who have always loved being outdoors and playing sports, are now getting very comfortable being at home. They are now entering a comfort zone with being along with their devices… And then we noticed the obvious—even when present physically with friends, all that the kids did was pick up their devices and play games online, with each other. The children had nothing to talk or share, they had no ideas for physical games. It is like they were struggling to have a face-to-face interaction with each other but as soon as they picked up their devices, everything seemed well.

So, while I believe that we have raised our children well during these trying times and they have not shown severe signs of anxiety or depression, this severe dependence on devices and their virtual life is worrying. I don't know if they feel anxious about in-person conversations but the ease with which they can lead a virtual life is disheartening to us as parents and we only wonder about the impact these two years of solitude is going to have on them in the long run.”

The world inside the screen is the comfort zone. Outside it, kids are having trouble connecting to their old physical world. When asked about shifts in their children’s behaviour, over 70% of parents thought their kids were exhibiting signs of aggression, or dealing with issues like irregular sleep or anxiety/depression.

The government’s stance on school closures is slowly thawing. Yesterday, the state of Punjab reopened schools for all classes. The first day back, reported Hindustan Times, was a happy time for the children, who were glad to meet their teachers and friends. Parents, naturally, are holding their breath. Attendance in rural areas has surpassed the bigger cities, where only 50% of students have made it back to their classes. For them, online classes are still the preferred option.

When schools do finally open up across the country, they’re going to be dealing with shifts that happened, and maybe were crystallised during this long absence from the classroom. Schools might have to spend a few semesters just getting to know this new, post-pandemic generation better. There could be less focus on academics initially, and more on re-socialising kids to a classroom and their peers. They’re also going to be dealing with kids who’ve spent the better part of two years behind a screen, uncertain and fearful about the world around them.

e-RUPI: When disruption comes from within

Arundhati Ramanathan

Direct benefit transfer (DBT) is a massive machinery in India. Nearly 420 schemes under 56 ministries rely on DBT. And since 2014, the government has disbursed a whopping Rs 822,000 crore (US$110 billion)—close to 60% of the welfare and subsidies budget of the government—directly to the bank accounts of beneficiaries. People rely on this to get their cooking gas subsidies, their share of money to buy rations from the Public Distribution System, and wages from the social security schemes like the Mahatma Gandhi National Rural Employment Guarantee Act, among others. 

About 50% of DBT runs on an Aadhaar-based system. Aadhaar is a 12-digit unique identification (UID) number issued to all Indian citizens. And bank accounts are linked to the biometric Aadhaar number. The National Payments Corporation of India (NPCI), India’s retail payments body, facilitates this linkage. So direct benefit transfers are made to an Aadhaar-linked account. But this has not been the smoothest implementation. 

“Often real beneficiaries complain about not receiving the amount transferred via DBT in their bank accounts, as government departments create multiple bank accounts to transfer subsidies to, without the knowledge of the person in whose name the account has been created.”

Aadhaar Continues to Trip Up Direct Benefit Transfer Schemes, Duping Thousands: Report, The Wire

The flaw in the design is that any time a new bank account is created, it gets linked to the unique Aadhaar number, and the subsidies could flow to the last linked bank account, unless the beneficiary intervenes. The Wire report published in December 2020 says:

“Although beneficiaries’ consent is supposed to be a prerequisite to this linking or mapping”, it is totally absent in practice, leaving beneficiaries in the dark about which accounts have been created in their names or linked to their Aadhaar numbers.”

In a system like this, identifying the bank account that the subsidy is routed to is the problem. What if the government can deliver subsidies, even if they don’t have a bank account? 

Enter e-RUPI. 

Prime Minister Narendra Modi on 2 August unveiled a new channel of delivering welfare benefits, called e-RUPI. 

It’s a voucher delivered to mobile phones of beneficiaries as a QR code or as a string-based e-voucher on SMS. The users of this one-time payment mechanism will be able to redeem the voucher without a card, digital payments app or internet banking access, at the service provider. 

So, that means, if a farmer wants to avail a fertiliser subsidy, she is likely to get a voucher for it from the agricultural ministry. On displaying that voucher to the agro dealer, who will scan the QR code, the farmer can get the fertiliser.

As for the service provider—in this case the agro dealer—she gets her payment on time as the voucher is already paid for. 

Win-win-win. 

What this system improvises on is the delivery of benefits. But the first hurdle of direct benefit transfers still remains unaddressed. The identification of the beneficiary.

The most fundamental, and troublesome, aspect of DBT is the identification of beneficiaries. Most of the DBT schemes are managed by states, except a few like MGNREGA, PM-KISAN or PMUY, where money is transferred to beneficiaries’ accounts directly by the Centre. For each DBT scheme the government has a different criteria, beneficiary list and delivery channel.

For example, MGNREGA has 90 million workers registered; the National Food Security Act (NFSA) has 810 million; PM-KISAN over 140 million; and PMUY over 80 million. The problem is that during a crisis, the government randomly selects beneficiary lists to transfer benefits, which leads to exclusions.

Cash, on delivery: How India has taken up DBT in the times of COVID-19, DownToEarth

e-RUPI will also open a new conundrum for the government. 

Since these digital vouchers could make redundant the need for bank accounts, what happens to the millions of accounts opened under the PM Jhan Dhan Yojana, whose primary use case is to receive subsidies? It was a scheme launched for easy access to financial services. But given the profile of those who have these accounts, most deposits in these accounts are subsidies. There is nearly Rs 143,370.58 crore (US$19.3 billion) in deposits as of July 2021. For the banks that operate these accounts, e-RUPI’s success could suddenly result in higher operational costs from maintaining millions of zero-balance accounts. 

As with any tech, you win some, you lose some.

Planes vs their engines

Benjamin Cher

Air travel is recovering in markets like the US and China, and aircraft manufacturer Airbus wants to capitalise on it by boosting its narrowbody output to 75 jets a month. But engine suppliers are saying, “Whoa, hold your horses.”

This comes from the different business models of engine suppliers and aircraft manufacturers. Airbus gets payment for its delivered planes upfront, recouping the fixed costs immediately. Not so for engine suppliers, who often lease their engines to airlines, raking in money mostly from engine overhauls and servicing, of which it hasn’t seen much as airlines kept the engines off.

This business model is in peril should airlines buy new planes rather than keep their older ones running. Newer engines see less servicing, and costs can’t be recouped on existing engines. There’s also the worry that recovery could dive with a supply glut of new planes, as airlines might hesitate to take on new deliveries, with no one getting paid.

Seems that even if air travel recovers, the recovery won’t be the same across the entire industry.

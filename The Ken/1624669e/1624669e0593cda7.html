At an event in Delhi over the weekend, Smriti Irani, the minister for Information and Broadcasting, made a case for regulating online content and said that the government is seriously considering it. So, is the government of India going to become a troll monitor?
 
The minister gave a long-winded response which boils down to this: That like Press Council of India (for newspapers) and News Broadcasters Association (for television), online content, including social media stuff, should have a regulator. This, despite the fact that India does regulate online content under its IT Act of 2000.
 
But all the three mentioned above are failed models.
 
A new model is being adopted by Germany. Recently, it brought in a new law: Network Enforcement Act. It makes social networks with at least two million users or more in Germany responsible for taking down illegal content posted on them by users, and for putting in place a mechanism where illegal content can be reported and acted upon. Failure to do this invokes hefty fines.
 
The German law comes at a time when social media is no longer seen as a benign tool, let alone a force for good. But the catch is, social networks have by and large done a terrible job of monitoring their own platforms for hate speech, intimidation and “fake news” but willingly kowtowed to government diktats on free speech.
 
In today’s story, lawyer Alok Prasanna Kumar argues that in India, what we have is the worst of both worlds: harmful speech inciting violence, defaming or intimidating someone is rarely stopped on social media, but criminal action is taken against those who dissent (usually at the behest of someone in power). He suggests a fix.

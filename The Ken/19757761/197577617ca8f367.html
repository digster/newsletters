It’s time for another quick roundup this week. The heated world of retail and commerce unleashes new developments every other day, many of which we haven’t had a chance to talk about in Trade Tricks yet. 

So, let’s catch you up on a couple of interesting things we came across recently.

Quick commerce—suffering, expanding, encroaching

Aggressive expansion in the quick-commerce space is seeing the largest players rack up even bigger bills (both Swiggy Instamart and Blinkit recorded widening losses in the previous quarter), but it’s certainly helping them grow their toplines. 

The problem for the rest of India’s retail and commerce sector is that most of this sales growth is coming at their expense. 

To quote a recent report from global management consulting firm Kearney:

“…of all sales happening via quick commerce, a 6–8% share is purely incremental, while the rest is coming at the expense of sales from other channels (predominantly modern trade and e-commerce followed by general trade outlets).”

Last year, The Ken wrote about this encroachment and the threat quick commerce may pose, especially to major e-commerce platforms and neighbourhood grocery stores. Local shops were, even then, starting to feel the impact of quick-commerce platforms on demand.

According to a survey of 900 retailers in nine cities done by Kirana Club in February, about 40% of them located in Delhi said that their sales dropped in the last one and a half years owing to quick-commerce apps. However, as high as 60% of surveyees in some tier-2 cities like Kanpur and Lucknow felt the impact. All of their sales were hit by 10–30%.

Kearney expects quick-commerce to continue along the same trajectory, with the segment expected to grow 3X between 2024 and 2027 and reach a size of Rs 1.5–1.7 lakh crore. 

Much of this is being driven by a significant shift in consumer behaviour, especially in segments such as snacks, gifting, staples, and other household needs. Discounts, of course, help. According to the Kearney report, while quick-commerce platforms do not discount as much as other e-commerce players, they do offer bigger discounts than general stores, at 6–9% versus 2–5%. 

The report notes that before quick commerce, only 33% of consumers surveyed approved of online shopping. But that number has now grown to 87%.

No doubt, this is why an increasing number of retail and commerce companies are frantically trying to build up their own quick-commerce muscle. Flipkart last month said that it wants to set up 800 stores by the end of this year that will cater to new markets in tier-2 and -3 cities for its quick-commerce wing, Flipkart Minutes. Even cosmetics brand Nykaa is wading deeper into these waters, announcing in its post-earnings call last Friday that it wants to expand its quick-commerce offering Nykaa Now, currently present only in Mumbai, to other metro cities. 

It seems no one wants to be left behind, even if that means drawing the ire of an ever increasing pool of small brick-and-mortar stores.

Defence against the dark patterns… and dark stores

Just two weeks ago in this newsletter, we wrote about how “dark patterns” have now become quite common on e-commerce platforms, including in the quick-commerce space. Well, last week, the Indian government moved to quell their proliferation, directing over 50 online platforms to check for “dark patterns” on their websites.

According to a 7 June statement from India’s Central Consumer Protection Authority (CCPA), e-commerce platforms have been asked to “self-audit within three months to detect dark patterns and ensure its resolution”. The CCPA also said that it has already noticed the use of such dark patterns on certain platforms and that notices have been sent to companies found violating its guidelines. 

It isn’t just dodgy cart totals that are coming under scrutiny though, because the Maharashtra Food and Drug Administration (FDA) suspended the licence of one of Zepto’s dark stores in Dharavi, Mumbai, last week after expired food was discovered in the warehouse. Inspectors said they found fungal contamination and unsanitary conditions at the facility.

Joint Commissioner (Food) Mangesh Mane said the suspension will remain until Zepto achieves full compliance. “Till the contraventions are cleared, the facility will remain shut. We will also be inspecting other Zepto warehouses and similar quick-commerce setups to ensure hygiene and compliance with food safety norms,” he added.

On 5 June, authorities also inspected a Blinkit warehouse and found it operating without the required FDA licence, with no pest control or calibration certificates, food on the floor, and dirty racks. 

Is the sector’s hasty expansion leaving holes in processes and systems that should be airtight?

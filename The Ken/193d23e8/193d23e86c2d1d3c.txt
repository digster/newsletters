Domino’s is the dark horse in the next food-delivery race. The fast-food chain now delivers 70% of its orders within 20 minutes 
 By Seetharaman G https://the-ken.com/writers/seetharamang/

Over the past few days, you must have seen variations of a sermon on “you are what you eat” on social media. They were all in response to instant-delivery company Zepto spinning out Zepto Cafe as a separate app, and Zomato-owned rival Blinkit launching its own 10-minute food-delivery app, Bistro. 

Linkedin pundits fell over each other to warn people away from ordering “defrosted” foods that are “not good for your gut”, but they missed the point. The debate around the quality and nutrition of foods that you buy rather than prepare yourself is hardly unique to these apps. It’s been around as long as restaurants have existed. 

If you’d like to read a sharp take on what’s prompting Zomato and competitor Swiggy to disrupt themselves in food delivery, you should head to my colleague Praveen’s recent piece https://the-ken.com/the-nutgraf/10-minute-delivery-is-eating-40-minute-food-delivery/ from his newsletter, The Nutgraf:

Groceries. Toys. Electronics. Mobiles. Medicines. Pet food. Alcohol. And now, we’re finally at the end. There are no more new categories left to conquer. So quick-commerce companies have decided to try to create associations in categories like food delivery, hoping to sway users who automatically recall Swiggy and Zomato when they think of their next meal.

But there’s one company that fascinates me more in the context of instant delivery than Blinkit, Zepto, and Swiggy. 

People often make the mistake of thinking of Domino’s Pizza as just a fast-food chain. But what has really made Domino’s stand out for decades is its ability to take reasonably edible grub in a cardboard box from any of its restaurants to a customer quickly enough for the food to still be warm. In India, too, Domino’s franchisee Jubilant Foodworks had been delivering pies in 30 minutes for years before Swiggy and Zomato burst onto the scene. 

Such was the loyalty of the Domino’s customer that as recently as 2021, half https://the-ken.com/story/how-dominos-defied-the-might-of-zomato-and-swiggy/ its online orders came through the brand’s own app and website. There’s no reason for me to believe that has changed dramatically. It has retained the appeal of its own online channels by keeping certain offers exclusive to them. Whatever may now be different about Zomato and Swiggy, the experience of ordering food on them, or the incentive to do so, is no better than it was three years ago. 

Plus, Domino’s is particularly good at skating to where the puck is going. In March 2023, when instant food delivery was still being discussed in hypothetical terms, it rolled out 20-minute delivery in Bengaluru before making the same promise in other cities. 

Analysts believed it was little more than a pilot. But now, Domino’s delivers seven out of 10 orders within 20 minutes. This is a staggering feat; delivery contributes 70% of the sales from the chain’s 2,100 stores in India. 

Domino’s added some ballast to its 20-minute bet by waiving its delivery fee earlier this year. It was happy to forgo some of its profits in the short run because it couldn’t afford to be yet another fast-food chain dependent on the Zomato-Swiggy duopoly. 

What also works for Domino’s is that it’s dealing with a food that can be relentlessly optimised. There is no fretting over crumbly burger buns or soggy fries or fried chicken that lost its crunch. That pizza is middle-class Indians’ gateway to Western fast food helps too. 

I’m sure this is not lost on Zomato or Swiggy. The 10-minute experiment in food delivery is currently about puffs, samosas, Maggi, and chai, but I expect pizza to be a big part of the menu. In fact, Zepto Cafe already offers it. Swiggy Bolt already delivers from McDonald’s, KFC, and Burger King in 10 minutes in select locations. 

This is reason enough for Jubilant Foodworks investors to be anxious. But their nerves should be soothed since the company knows better than to be dismissive of the 10-minute promise, as its chief executive Sameer Khetarpal told https://www.jubilantfoodworks.com/Uploads/Files/1127foodfile-Transcriptq2H1.pdf analysts recently: 

See, customers love free speed. I am always a very big believer of that. So, firstly, we have the network to boost the limits of speed on a very large menu. Our philosophy on technology has been that we have to ride on all sorts of technology, whether it’s aggregators, ONDC, our own apps—we are present everywhere… I genuinely believe if somebody can deliver faster speed with a 2,100-store network in about 450-odd cities, it’s Domino’s. So I worry less about it. I love that challenge… There’s no reason why we will not participate in it.

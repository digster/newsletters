Good Morning Ishan,

It’s a bit unfair to compare the payments landscape with the sci-fi action film The Matrix. If the latter was a simulated world to which humans were connected, payments is a pulsing sector in India with blazing growth. Over a billion transactions happen a day. Going by the last week’s RBI vision document, the transactions turnover would contribute nearly 15% of GDP by 2021, up from the current 10%.

Yet, what companies here need to stay ahead of the next challenger is nothing short of a matrix. It’s no longer a price war; it’s a tech war in which, ironically, tech is what is getting commoditised.

Five-year-old Razorpay, which aggregates and processes all payment options, is challenging the industry leader PayU. The difference in volumes the two payments gateway companies process is sharply declining. Unseating PayU from its top position will mean getting to some of its top merchants like Airtel, Ola, Ibibo, Zomato, Indigo, Oyo and the like.

Razorpay says it is already processing most of the volumes of these companies; PayU denies this claim. Merchants’ responses, meanwhile, have been mixed. Perfect elements for a riveting tale.

Arundhati’s story, however, is not just about this. After all, no feature in payments is a moat. Max it can get you is a few months of stress-free operations. Then, in its payments matrix, will becoming a ‘neo’ bank save Razorpay? The company thinks so. And so do Open Financial Technologies and, yes, NiYo. The two other self-proclaimed neo banks in India.

Some 60 such banks exist globally today. Fintechs are prefixing the word ‘neo’ to bank to imply a revival for some parts of banking. Especially the profitable bits and services. Incumbent banks are reacting; fintech investors are on fire.

Good Morning Ishan,
...Would still be exclusivity.
Depending on where you are, the biggest e-commerce players in your region could be Amazon, Shopee, Flipkart, Lazada, Tokopedia... It's a long list.
A simple binding factor for all of them is how they lean on discounts on sale days and festive periods. Maybe even a regular Thursday like today. Discounts drive online sales—or at least used to. But like with much else, Covid has thrown a spanner in the works.
At a time when sellers find inventory hard to get rid of, everyone is offering steep discounts. So how then does an e-commerce player—the platform these sellers use to retail online—compete? By signing exclusivity pacts. "You sell your product exclusively on my platform and I'll make sure it gets high visibility for ___ number of days." Exclusivity is a two-pronged weapon for e-commerce players. It drives traffic to the platform while holding competition off.
Except in India, e-commerce players—primarily Amazon and Flipkart—are not allowed to go exclusive, as per regulations instituted last year.
Instead, Appario Retail—one of Amazon India's leading sellers—has reached out to smaller electronics suppliers for exclusivity deals for Amazon's upcoming Prime Day sale (for at least 30 days from the launch date). Appario, incidentally, is a joint venture between Amazon Asia Pacific Holdings and the Ashok Patni Group, but isn't considered an Amazon group entity, allowing it to do what Amazon India cannot.
Amazon is no stranger to exclusivity, though. Globally, it has an Accelerator program, where third-party sellers develop Amazon-exclusive brands that it can buy out for US$10,000. In India, though, the company is choosing to rebrand 'exclusivity' as Amazon Specials. Flipkart's version of this name change is its "just here" category.
Abinaya's story today compares exclusivity on e-commerce platforms with its counterpart in the foodtech space. After all, exclusivity is a huge part of the foodtech business—such tie-ups accounted for 30% and 12-13% of overall sales for India's leading footech players, Zomato and Swiggy, last year. The question is when the dust settles, would e-commerce exclusivity look more like that of foodtech...or vice versa?

Every once in a while comes a company that rules the public toxicity department; then it makes way for another. But few have a sustained (toxic) reputation like the agro-chemical behemoth Monsanto, a company that pioneered genetically modified seeds with its Bt cotton tech. (The brand name Monsanto is on its last legs after the company got acquired by Bayer in July.)

So to find a new company that actually wants to be a mini-Monsanto is surprising. A little-known startup in Hyderabad, Tierra Agrotech, bought the Indian cotton businesses of Monsanto and DuPont-Pioneer last year. Today, it is the *second largest cotton seed company in the world*, after Bayer-Monsanto.

Whatever the outsiders may think of Big Ag, Monsanto is highly admired in the seed sector. Mostly for demonstrating the tech to create a new crop/plant with desired traits. The three founders of Tierra dream of creating new seeds and a thriving business, using their own IP as well as the seven truckloads of seed that arrived on their premises after the Monsanto-DuPont deal.

In the free-for-all farming that exists in India today, where seed companies are habituated to stealing others’ products, how Tierra commercialises its IP will shape Indian agriculture. The acquisition of the MNC portfolio has vaulted it in the Top-10 league in India.

In her story today, Gayathri says it’s not clear why Monsanto and DowDuPont chose to sell to Tierra. One of the co-founders told her it might be partly because of their acrimonious relationship with older Indian companies; the annals of the Indian court system are littered with suits and counter-suits between MNCs and domestic companies.

Meanwhile, the anti-GMO activist Vandana Shiva has her own theory on why this sale happened.

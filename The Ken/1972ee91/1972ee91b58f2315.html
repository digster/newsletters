Look, Wi-Fi is kind of a miracle. 

You turn on your phone, it magically connects to a router that someone probably named “D-Link_5G_EXT_20”, and you start watching cricket clips on Youtube without thinking too hard about it. It’s a miracle because nobody paid a spectrum licence fee. There was no bureaucrat allocating frequencies to your local café. No one filed a network-rollout plan. It just works.

This magical system exists because of “unlicensed spectrum”. Specifically, the 2.4GHz and 5GHz bands—radio frequencies that governments had the good sense to leave open. Now, India wants to add another slice to that unlicensed buffet—a 500MHz portion of the 6GHz band, from 5925 to 6425MHz. For years, that spectrum has been lying fallow. India’s Department of Telecom (DoT) wants to put it to work.

In May, the DoT proposed draft rules to delicense this chunk of airwaves. No need for a licence, no auction or red tape. Follow basic interference rules, keep the power levels down, and don’t blast your neighbour’s router into oblivion. You are good. 

It’s India’s first real systemic reform for Wi-Fi, arguably ever. And that’s exciting.

But not exactly spontaneous. The Telecom Regulatory Authority of India—yes, Trai, the institution telcos usually love to ignore or blame—actually called this move years ago. Back in 2022, it floated consultations asking how best to use the 6GHz band. Next year, it laid out three options: go full-China and license the whole thing to telcos, go full-United States and delicense everything, or split the baby.

Trai recommended the baby-splitting option: open 500MHz to unlicensed Wi-Fi, and reserve the rest for future 5G or IMT (International Mobile Telecommunications, the UN’s umbrella term for global mobile broadband systems). It even warned that Wi-Fi in India had long been throttled by “limited availability of public spectrum” and said delicensing was the only “globally harmonised and economically efficient” way forward.

So, if the DoT’s May 2025 draft rules feel surprisingly progressive, it’s because they are Trai’s 2023 roadmap brought to life. Every once in a while, our institutions do what they’re designed to do. We just don’t always notice it through the noise of industry lobbying.

According to the telecom minister, this could “boost innovation” and next-generation Wi-Fi 6E and Wi-Fi 7 deployments. And—if you look past the jargon—maybe even finally give your local government school usable internet speeds.

Tech companies are thrilled. Meta, Google, Microsoft, and equipment makers have all been lobbying hard for India to open up this band. So have digital access groups like the Broadband India Forum. 

Their argument is simple: Wi-Fi is cheap, flexible, and it solves the “last 100 metres” problem in a way that 5G just doesn’t. If you want to give hundreds of millions of Indians fast internet in homes, schools, shops, and bus stops, you can’t rely entirely on licensed mobile networks. 

India’s telcos, naturally, are not thrilled.

They see the same 6GHz band and think, “Wouldn’t that be great for future 5G expansion?” 

The Cellular Operators Association of India—representing Airtel, Reliance Jio, and Vodafone Idea—is adamant that India’s mobile networks are starving for mid-band spectrum. International 5G benchmarks suggest you need around 2,000MHz of mid-band capacity. India barely has 700–800MHz in hand. So giving away a full 500MHz chunk to Wi-Fi? Without charging auction fees? While your mobile data demand keeps surging?

To a telco executive, this is madness.

Telecom companies want the government to reconsider delicensing, warning it could hurt the 5G rollout. In telco speak, “interference” doesn’t seem to be just about spectrum, but also control. And Wi-Fi, if allowed to thrive on its own terms, is a threat to the licensed order of things.

If you believe that India’s digital future is fundamentally a telco-driven one—that national-scale infrastructure can only be built top-down, with private capital bidding for spectrum and deploying towers—then yes, it kind of is madness. Why let Wi-Fi providers freeload on public airwaves while telcos spend lakhs of crores building networks?

But the counterargument is this: Wi-Fi is not freeloading. Wi-Fi is infrastructure.

It’s just a different kind.

It’s not centralised, metered, or vertically integrated. It’s small-scale, fragmented, and local. But it’s still vital. And in India, it’s the part that has suffered a decade of benign neglect.

Wi-Fi underdogs

India has seen wave after wave of Wi-Fi startups and grassroots models that aimed to bring users local, affordable internet. 

Joister Connect promised free Wi-Fi at colleges and railway stations in Maharashtra and Gujarat. Ozone Networks once claimed it would build 1 million public Wi-Fi hotspots. i2e1, a startup that raised VC money, wanted to put analytics-backed Wi-Fi access points in kirana stores and street corners.

But there were tollbooths, and telcos stood at the gates. 

Under PM-WANI, thousands of Public Data Offices (PDOs) were supposed to spring up and democratise access. But PDOs had to buy wholesale bandwidth from telcos, who priced it at Rs 6–8 lakh a year per hotspot. Unsurprisingly, India now has just 280,000 hotspots, versus the 10 million target that was set for 2022.

The technology didn’t fail. The model did. Because it was built in an economy where three telcos could set the rates.

That’s why the new draft rules matter. They essentially say: “Fine. You don’t want to sell to small providers? We’ll give them their own sandbox.” 

No licence. Just plug in a certified router, follow the emission caps that govern how strong signals can be, and get to work.

In a way, it’s India’s first anti-incumbent Wi-Fi policy.

You see, Jio and Airtel already use unlicensed spectrum every day. Their home Wi-Fi services—Jiofiber, Airtel Xstream—all run on the same 2.4 and 5 GHz bands that every café and school Wi-Fi does. No licence. No auction. Just spectrum that’s free for all. The difference is that they own the backhaul: fibre and pipes. So, while the airwaves are technically open, the road that feeds them still runs through telco toll booths. 

That’s what makes the 6GHz move feel threatening. It’s not just more free air; it’s the first time other companies might be able to breathe it without buying oxygen from telcos.

There are still rules

The government’s notification issued on 16 May sets clear technical limits for how powerful wireless devices can be, depending on where they’re used. 

For indoor devices, the maximum allowed transmission strength is “30 dBm EIRP (Equivalent Isotropically Radiated Power), with a power spectral density (PSD) capped at five dBm per MHz”. That’s strong enough to blanket your house with its signal, but not so strong that it leaks into your neighbour’s Zoom call. 

Outdoor devices, which can cause more mischief, get only 14 dBm. And everyone’s expected to colour within the lines; any signal that drifts outside the assigned band must drop off sharply.

You also can’t use them on oil rigs, inside vehicles, or with drones. And everything needs in-built antennas and type approval.

This is liberalisation, yes—but of the bureaucratically-regulated, make-sure-you-submit-an-Annexure kind. No one’s saying you can launch rogue mesh networks across Lutyens’ Delhi.

But the freedom is real. And importantly… you can be kicked off. 

If your unlicensed router messes with a licensed telco or satellite operator, the government can order you to shut it down. This is not Wild West Wi-Fi. The rules operate on a “non-interference, non-protection” basis. Use the spectrum, sure, but don’t expect to win if there’s a fight.

This is a clever compromise. It gives Wi-Fi entrepreneurs access while keeping incumbents comfortable. Think of it as Airbnb spectrum: you get to use the flat, but the landlord can evict you if you cause a scene.

And that’s fine. What Wi-Fi players get isn’t total freedom, just permissionless innovation with accountability. In an industry where every move needs a nod, that’s no small win. 

Telcos, for all their lobbying muscle, do raise a point that’s harder to ignore: unlicensed spectrum isn’t magic if the infrastructure underneath it is broken. 

According to GSMA’s 2024 analysis, only 13% of Indian households have fibre connections, and most of those are well below the speeds where Wi-Fi 6E/7 can really work. If India fully opens up the 6GHz band for Wi-Fi, the projected GDP boost by 2035 is about 0.42%. If it’s kept for 5G, that number jumps to 0.7%. A hybrid approach delivers 0.6%. 

In other words, there’s some upside, but no version of this is a silver bullet.

There’s also the cost of not solving this at scale: GSMA estimates that in Mumbai alone, a shortage of mid-band spectrum could require 195,000 additional small cells, driving up network costs by 4X and power use by nearly 3X over the next decade. 

So, when telcos say they need more spectrum, they’re not just being greedy. They’re protecting a model that, however vertically integrated and expensive, works at a national scale. And that’s where the government has to play referee, weighing the efficiency of top-down infra against the openness of bottom-up access.

The irony in all this is that even mobile connectivity—the telcos’ turf—isn’t growing like it used to. India used to add millions of wireless subscribers every month. Now, it’s mostly flat.

In the first half of 2025, Trai data showed that the total number of broadband subscribers declined by 5 million. Smartphone shipments, too, dropped about 8% year-on-year in Q1, the biggest contraction in recent memory.

For the first time in two decades, we’re not growing into digital India. We’re circling inside it.

And it’s not that India’s doing anything revolutionary here. It’s finally doing what most of the world has already done. The US and South Korea opened up the entire 1200MHz of the 6GHz band to unlicensed use. Europe and the UK took the hybrid approach: opening the lower 500MHz for Wi-Fi and keeping the upper 700MHz for licensed 5G networks. (China, of course, did the opposite, and all 6GHz spectrum is reserved for licensed IMT.)

Meanwhile, as the 6GHz turf war plays out close to Earth, another battle is unfolding above the clouds. 

India is opening its satellite broadband sector to competition. Starlink, Amazon, and Bharti’s Oneweb are all lining up licences to beam internet from space. And they need spectrum too.

The 6GHz debate fits into a broader rethink of how India manages spectrum. Satellite spectrum isn’t auctioned, not merely because of non-interference clauses, but because of how the rules set by the International Telecommunication Union—a UN agency—define the global use of airwaves. These bands are typically harmonised across countries and allocated administratively, not licensed competitively. India’s satcom policy reflects that reality. 

Yes, it includes “non-interference, non-protection” provisions for coexistence, but the deeper structure comes from global coordination, not just domestic deregulation.

So the 6GHz band is more than just spectrum. It’s also a proxy for a much bigger question: who gets to build India’s digital future?

Telcos want it auctioned, gated, monetised. 

Wi-Fi advocates want it open, shared, unlicensed. 

And satellite firms want it regulated but flexible.

The government’s answer, for now, is a cautious yes to openness. A signal that not all innovation must be licensed. It took a decade of failed Wi-Fi pilots, telco turf-guarding, and international peer pressure to get here.

But it’s a start.

Good Morning Ishan,
I’ll begin with a case-study.
It’s been a long time since I’ve left B-school, so I’ve forgotten how to write case-studies, but here’s my best attempt:
Let’s say you are the founder of a startup. You want to raise some money. So you take, say, US$100 million from a VC at a valuation of a billion dollars, giving them a 10% stake in your startup. A few days later, when another investor comes to you expressing a desire to invest in your startup, you say that the valuation is now US$2 billion, and so their US$100 million investment only gives them a 5% stake. The investor doesn’t understand why your company doubled in value in a week, but they don’t really care because they badly want to get a slice of the action. So they rush in and give you the money. The next week, to another investor, you say that the valuation is now US$5 billion. You do this again. And again. At each round, you also take money from existing investors, who have to keep adding money to retain their existing shareholding.
Suddenly, one day, you hit a ceiling. Obviously. Your startup’s valuation reaches a point where you can’t find anyone who wants to invest in it. I know it’s hard to believe, but even VCs start to act sensibly… eventually.
But you need more money. You need money all the time. Some people say it’s because you are now addicted to raising money. Others say it’s because your startup is actually a nice machine that burns cash everyday and keeps you warm.
You consider raising debt—basically a loan. But lenders have unreasonable demands like repayments, and that doesn’t really suit you. You want cash in return for equity—which is much less annoying, and you can basically do whatever you want. However, nobody wants your startup’s equity at its current valuation.
So, here’s the question to you—where do you get the money from?
You’re probably tempted to say common sense things like, “We should do things that justify the valuation of the startup, like increase revenue, or acquire a competitor, or expand into a new business line”. No no no. Write all that nonsense on Linkedin. This is the real world we are talking about.
Do you want to know the answer?
According to Byju’s—the world’s most valuable edtech and the world’s most innovative financial engineering company, the answer to the case-study is blindingly obvious: You just flip the table, knock all the pieces off the board and start again from zero.
What?
Yes.
Wait, but—
Yes.
Byju’s knows the correct answer to this case-study because this is exactly what it did back in January this year. The best explanation on how this works comes from my good friend Matt Levine, columnist at Bloomberg:
Last week Bloomberg reported a 90% cut — “a price that values the firm at less than $2 billion, … down from $22 billion at its previous round in late 2022” — but I guess things are moving fast, and in the wrong direction? On these numbers, the people who put in $5 billion back in the good times will own less than 10% of the company, while the people who put in $200 million in the bad times will own 90%.
The point of a rights offering is to be coercive: Existing investors have the right to put more money in at the rights price, which here is basically zero, and if they don’t put more money in then they get diluted by the people who do. Here, if you put up $1 billion for 5% of the company back when it was worth $20 billion, Byju’s will come to you and ask you to put in $10 million more to keep your 5% stake; if you say no, you’ll be diluted down to about 0.5%. If you’ve already thrown away $1 billion, what’s $10 million more? On the other hand, if Byju’s has already reduced your $1 billion investment to zero, why would you give them any more money? Getting diluted doesn’t matter if the stock is going to zero; owning 5% of a company worth zero is no better than owning 0.5% of it.
The main questions in the rights offering are: Do you trust that the company has a plan to turn things around, and that this one last contribution is enough to see it through?
And also: How mad are you about what happened to the rest of your money?

2023 has been a hell of a year for Byju’s. Back in June last year, I summarised [their predicament](https://the-ken.com/the-nutgraf/byjus-has-one-escape-route/) as, “It’s a company that nobody wants to touch. Investors don’t want to be on the board. Auditors don’t want to certify their revenue. Lenders don’t want to lend to their customers”. In fact, in my predictions for 2024, [I wrote that](https://the-ken.com/the-nutgraf/2024-predictions/) I expected Byju’s to retreat into the background and go into a quiet rebuilding phase as it becomes a zombie company.
Well, halfway through the year, it’s now clear that Byju’s wants to become a zombie company and fade into the background, but everyone is refusing to let it go quietly into the night. For nearly six months now, Byju’s has been doing its best to go ahead with a rights offering that effectively transforms who owns the company.

UPI delegates payments, and with it, three headaches. The new feature opens up attractive possibilities for payment firms, but comes with a few challenges too
By Arundhati Ramanathan
6 min read
View in browser

Good Morning Ishan,

UPI is a transaction-hungry monster, and anywhere it sees even a glimmer of potential to add to its billions of transactions, it wants to gobble that up. 

Thus far, the Unified Payments Interface (UPI) could only be used by bank account-holders, but National Payments Corporation of India (NPCI), the retail payments body that runs the digital payment service, now wants to allow non-UPI users to also join and partake in the digital payments revelry. 

Last week, Reserve Bank of India Governor Shaktikanta Das announced that non-UPI users, such as teens or senior citizens or anyone who does not have a way to transact via UPI, can piggyback on a UPI user and spend out of their bank account. 

Think add-on card, but for UPI.

“It is proposed to introduce a facility of ‘Delegated Payments’ in UPI. This would enable an individual (primary user) to allow another individual (secondary user) to make UPI transactions up to a limit from the primary user’s bank account without the need for the secondary user to have a separate bank account linked to UPI.”

Shaktikanta Das, RBI Governor

Payment platforms are upbeat about the feature’s prospects. Phonepe co-founder Sameer Nigam, for instance, believes that such delegated payments will “significantly accelerate UPI adoption by the next 300–400 million Indians.”

Everything may not work as rosily as you’d think, though.

Three new categories, three headaches

I spoke to a payments executive who laid out how the new feature is expected to work. 

So far, you couldn’t participate in the UPI network if you didn’t have a UPI ID linked to your bank account. And usually, having a bank account was the first step in creating one.

But now, users can set up UPI IDs without having a bank account at all. 

They can simply link their profile to a primary bank account, either by scanning a QR code, or adding the UPI ID or mobile number of the primary bank account-holder. It is up to the primary user to accept this request to link. 

Once linked, the bank account-holder will have the option to set it up either as a recurring use case or a one-off use case, much like how recurring mandates operate. 

In case it is a one-off, it will require the primary account-holder to punch in an OTP to authorise the transaction. But for a recurring use case, it won’t even need an OTP sign off—all the primary account-holder needs to do is set spending limits. 

NPCI is set to launch this feature officially at its flagship global Fintech Festival, to be held later this month. And the executive I spoke to said companies are seeing this as a way to target three sets of people: senior citizens, 18–22-year-olds, and unemployed spouses. Sandeep Srinivasa, a fintech founder, posted on X (formerly Twitter) that he believes this unlocks the potential to build companies that can better serve senior citizens. 

But where there are senior citizens and the vulnerable, there are also fraudsters—all of whom are already hard at work figuring out their next social engineering scheme, I’m sure.

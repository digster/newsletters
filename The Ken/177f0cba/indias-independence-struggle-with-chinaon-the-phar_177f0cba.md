In the early 1940s, when MK Gandhi and his wife Kasturba Gandhi were imprisoned by the British in Aga Khan Palace near Pune, Kasturba fell ill with pneumonia. She then suffered two heart attacks and was in pain. The doctors advised the antibiotic drug penicillin. Multiple accounts state that Kasturba didn't get the drug and died subsequently because her husband didn't allow it. Some others note that the drug would've been hard to access during World War II.
Whether Gandhi came in the way of penicillin or not, it was Pandit Jawaharlal Nehru—12 years year after Kasturba Gandhi's death—who formed a committee to manufacture Pen-G, a penicillin drug.
Almost 70 years since, India imports all of its Pen-G from China.
Penicillin isn't the only drug ingredient that India is relying on China for, either. China accounted for 68% of India's active pharmaceutical ingredients (API) imports in the year ended March 2020, according to data sourced from the Ministry of Commerce.
The Indian government wants to do away with this dependence on China. To that end, it has instituted a production linked incentive (PLI) to get Indian pharma to also produce the raw material or API that goes into drugs. The government, which had initially announced a Rs 10,000 crore ($1.3 billion) scheme in July last year, has upped that amount to Rs 15,000 crore ($2 billion) on 24 Feb—with seemingly fewer export regulations.
But, so far, the government hasn't budged on allowing companies to use existing production facilities. It insists that new plants be set up for something that could, eventually, sort of, get the company subsidies. Hmm, why would pharma be keen again? More so since Chinese imports are cheap?

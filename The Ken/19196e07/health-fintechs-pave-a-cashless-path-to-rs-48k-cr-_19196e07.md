Good Morning Ishan,
We have two stories for you today.
The first is about a new class of fintech startups. Mostly in stealth mode or in their early stages, these companies are setting out to be the connector between hospitals, insurance providers, and patients.
They want to solve an awkward situation that India’s hospitals are in: nearly 60% of their incoming payments are locked in promissory receivables at any given time. That isn’t a great position to be in.
The solution offered by these firms is to finance the receivables of the “missing middle” hospitals—medical institutions that aren’t funded with public money and don’t have the financial cushion of corporate backing. This happens to be most of India’s small and mid-sized hospitals, which account for 85% of the country’s healthcare facilities.
Stabilising that cash flow, these health fintech startups believe, would be of immense value. They offer 0% interest and manageable equated monthly instalments to patients. If uptake becomes wide enough, the major advantage is that hospitals would be less likely to refuse treatment for patients.
These medical-financing platforms see the Rs 48,000 crore, or more than US$5.7 billion, of government schemes as their total addressable market. The biggest among them is Gmoney, which has already raised US$10 million from a family office and will soon start another round.
For her debut, Sudeshna spoke to the founders or co-founders of startups in the space, including Gmoney. [Read her story](https://the-ken.com/story/health-fintechs-pave-a-cashless-path-to-rs-48k-cr-for-indias-missing-middle/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter) to find out how it all works.
Over in Indonesia, a new administration under Prabowo Subianto will take the reins in less than two months. It’ll mark the end of a decade under President Joko Widodo, who charted aggressive renewable energy targets for the country, laying the groundwork that would attract more than US$10 billion into this space for the country.
There’s no doubt that Indonesia has transformed—and continues to do so. Electricity generated from solar energy costs 800 rupiah/kWh, 20% less than what businesses currently pay for power from fossil fuels.
Yet, things are hardly going swimmingly. The targets for Indonesia’s transition to renewables are being amended to reflect a slower progression than initially anticipated. Even while renewable energy projects are being added to the pipeline, so are coal-fired power plants—with double the electricity generation compared to the output of facilities that are being shut down.
Besides, Prabowo is closely linked with coal tycoons, adding to the worries of renewable energy investors and environmentalists alike.

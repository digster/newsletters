Year-ender lists have a job. And the job is to feign that the year gone by made sense. The writer lines up events/stories, ranks outcomes, and assigns winners and losers. The year becomes a listicle.

In that sense, 2025 was not especially cooperative. The growth numbers looked good, as long as nobody asked the annoying follow-up question, which is usually “for whom?” Technology kept promising the future, while also creating new things to worry about. Capital kept flowing, though not always to places where outcomes visibly improved. Scale, which used to be the north star, suddenly needed explanation, even justification.

In a year like that, interviews became one of the more honest forms of reporting. Not because interviewees suddenly grew candid, but because they showed how to think when old frameworks stop delivering answers. They exposed the tensions leaders are sitting with, the trade-offs they can no longer hide, and the assumptions they are beginning to abandon.

The conversations chosen here aren’t united by sector, seniority, or scale. What they do share is a common problem: each interviewee is dealing with a system that has reached some sort of limit. Healthcare, where outcomes are rising but affordability isn’t. Markets where participation surges, but returns don’t. Consumer businesses discovering that India now behaves like several different countries at once. Technology founders realising that engagement and impact don’t always point in the same direction. Investors admitting that patience, not pattern recognition, might be the scarcest resource of all.

Read individually, these interviews look like stories about cancer care, mental health, venture capital, FMCG, hotels, markets, and dairy. Read together, they tell a story of how power, capital, and credibility are being renegotiated in India. 

In many of them, the shift was visible in a single exchange. A question asked plainly, and an answer that didn’t reach for cover.

Here are The Ken’s best Q&As of 2025:

The man who refuses to let cancer become a luxury product

BS Ajaikumar, founder of Healthcare Global (HCG), runs India’s largest cancer-focused hospital chain while owning barely 10% of it. Private equity controls the rest. Another PE firm is about to replace it. The stock market thinks the company is “pricey”. None of this seems to interest him much.

What does is this: mortality rates halved, outcomes published when peers won’t, and cancer care that allows patients to go home and sleep in their own beds instead of relocating their lives to metros.

Ajaikumar’s argument is less anti-capital and more anti-confusion. In a healthcare system increasingly tempted to chase margins and multi-speciality sprawl, his insistence on single-mindedness feels almost subversive.

The Ken: “HCG was the first, and is probably the last, cancer-focused chain in India. Now multi-specialty hospitals are beefing up their cancer offerings. Is single specialty better for cancer care or multi-specialty? Your latest investor presentation quotes a US study that says it’s the former.”

The AI founder who refuses to optimise for engagement

Jo Aggarwal spent eight years building Wysa, an AI-led mental-health platform, while avoiding the metrics most startups chase. No daily active users or infinite scroll logic for their own sake.

Her core claim is uncomfortable but hard to dismiss. That mental health is broken not because it lacks money or technology, but because it’s been treated as episodic when it is actually chronic. 

What makes this interview linger is the diagnosis of a generation shaped by algorithmic attention, job precarity, and absolute goals in a relative world. Wysa’s bet is that resilience can be trained, routinised, and coached at scale.

In a year when AI was mostly discussed in terms of productivity and layoffs, this conversation asked sharper questions.

The Ken: “AI has undergone step changes since Wysa started in 2016. How does that affect an AI-driven product like yours, especially now that people are turning to ChatGPT and the like for mental-health support?”

A venture capitalist who insists there is no playbook

Most VCs talk about pattern recognition. Vishesh Rajaram talks about its limits.

After a decade as a conventional VC, Rajaram chose to invest only in deep tech—humanoid robots, electric aircraft, and space launches—areas where failure is frequent. Also, timelines are long.

His most radical statement is also the simplest: if there were a playbook, large funds would already be using it. The absence of a playbook is the feature, not the bug.

This interview works because it strips venture investing of its mythology. Deep tech, Rajaram argues, is about the nerve to be patient and the willingness to define milestones that don’t resemble revenue. In a year obsessed with quick exits and AI multiples, this one reminds that some forms of progress still move at geological speed.

The Ken: “What kind of mental math do you do in choosing companies to back? Does that vary from company to company?”

Selling to India by ignoring most of India

Sudhir Sitapati’s diagnosis of India’s consumption slowdown is unrestrained: the top 20% are fine; the rest are not. Godrej Consumer Products is reorganising itself accordingly.

What makes the interview interesting is how consumption is being rebuilt around closed circuits—quick commerce, connected TV, modern trade, and targeted advertising—while the old idea of “mass” fractures. 

Sitapati’s real provocation is this: brand-building hasn’t changed nearly as much as people think. What has changed is who you can afford to talk to, and where they can see you.

In a year full of hand-wringing about India’s demand story, this interview explains why some companies are still growing, and why that growth may not feel evenly distributed.

The Ken: “For a while now, it’s been impossible to discuss FMCG without talking about how consumers aren’t buying enough, even though there are signs of recovery now. What exactly is happening, in your opinion?”

The market veteran who called retail euphoria a vanity metric

Sanjeev Prasad of Kotak Institutional Equities poured cold water on a rising market without sounding theatrical.

His central point is almost offensively simple. Secondary markets do not create money. They transfer it. When domestic investors buy, someone else is selling…and often taking capital out of the country.

The statistic that stays with you is that roughly 40% of retail money that entered equity mutual funds since 2021 has produced zero returns.

In a period dominated by narratives of financial democratisation, this interview restores an older, less flattering idea that valuation still matters, even when belief is widespread.

The Ken: “There’s the narrative that domestic institutional investors (DIIs) are acting as a buffer to foreign institutional investors (FIIs), and that’s a good thing. Does that hold, or is it a vanity metric?”

When moving downmarket becomes a promotion

Puneet Chhatwal’s turnaround of Indian Hotels is often told through market-cap charts. The more interesting story is cultural.

At some point, sending senior executives to run Ginger, once seen as a budget backwater, stopped being a punishment and started being a mark of trust. That shift, Chhatwal suggests, mattered as much as asset-light strategies or post-Covid travel demand.

The interview reads like a case study in unglamorous reform. Monetising forgotten assets, fixing food and beverage economics, and insisting that every Tata business be treated as a business.

The Ken: “A year ago, we wrote about Indian Hotels rebounding by doing something odd in hospitality: being all things to all people. It’s like you have Hermès, Mokobara, and everything in between. How does that work?”

Listening to the farm, not the farmer

Manish Jain left a successful investment career to work with dairy farmers, then discovered that advice was the least useful thing he could offer. Data, genetics, and listening worked better.

Nitara’s farmer-to-friend model maps cattle genetics, disease, nutrition, and pricing asymmetries across villages, correcting a system that has long prioritised volume over viability.

What elevates this interview is the humility. Solutions arrive only after trust does. Productivity gains follow dignity. In a year full of grand platforms and national missions, this was a grounded lesson in how scale actually happens at the bottom.

This wrap doesn’t claim the year was orderly, but it comes closest to making sense of it. We look forward to bringing you more such interviews—and insights—in the year to come.

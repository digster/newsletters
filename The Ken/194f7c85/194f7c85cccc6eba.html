Mahindra figured out the urban buyer. And soared. Its cars have for years been a fixture on hinterland roads. Now, even city folk have taken a shine to them

A company that earned its stripes as a carmaker with boxy yet sturdy sport-utility vehicles, Mahindra & Mahindra stunned everyone late last year when it unveiled two new electric cars.

Function over form was out the window.

The reaction Mahindra was going for was not “They are not bad” but “They are bonkers”, even if it wasn’t necessarily accompanied by adulation.

The curiously named BE6 and XEV 9e, the bookings for which open this week, are all sharp edges and abundant slopes. One of them even has headlights resembling a moustache that sits between a horseshoe and a Fu Manchu.

The launch capped a sublime year for the 80-year-old company. In 2024, Mahindra’s car sales rose over 20% from the year before—second only to Toyota Kirloskar Motor among large automakers. Hyundai Motor India and Tata Motors, whose heels Mahindra is snapping at, grew their sales in the low single digits.

Mahindra’s impressive run continued in January, too. On Monday, its shares reached an all-time high after its revenue and profits in the December quarter beat analyst estimates. At Rs 3.9 lakh crore ($45 billion), Mahindra’s market cap is not far behind Maruti Suzuki’s.

The stock has far outpaced the S&P BSE Auto index over the past year.

The story behind the numbers is one of reinvention. Not of a struggling company but of one that was coasting for years.

Even if its new cars didn’t excite buyers, Mahindra had its reliable workhorses from the early 2000s, Scorpio and Bolero. Their nondescript designs screamed utility and they thrived, thanks to staunch fans in India’s hinterlands, whose jerky roads the cars were made to weather.

Mahindra’s rural PV play relies primarily on two SUVs—Bolero and Scorpio. Over the years, they have remained fairly profitable for the company despite little investment and change.

“Contributing over half of Mahindra’s UV volumes in passenger cars, the Bolero and Scorpio face little competition in rural markets, so a definite jump is expected …” said Priya Ranjan, vice-president at Systematix Institutional Equities, a Mumbai-based brokerage house.

Mahindra plans expansion to protect rural turf, Mint

In FY18, these two cars alone accounted for 60% of Mahindra’s sales volume. Mahindra is a household brand in rural India, thanks to its market-leading tractors and small trucks. Small wonder, then, Mahindra was happy giving these customers what they wanted even if rivals were more attuned to city-dwelling car-owners’ changing preferences, especially for smaller SUVs.

But that changed in 2019 when it launched XUV 300 in the wildly popular sub-4 metre category. The following year saw the launch of the new three-door Thar. A year later, Mahindra rolled out XUV 700 to take on Hyundai Creta, the mid-size SUV market leader.

All these cars were squarely aimed at urban consumers. Feature-rich, aggressively priced, and loved for their designs and drive quality, the vehicles caught on quickly. It must have come as a surprise even to Mahindra how many bought the Thar for more than just off-roading. You will find more Thars than honeymooning couples in Goa. And you are just as likely to see a parent dropping their kid off at school in a Thar in Mumbai.

Mahindra chose to feed that frenzy by making a five-door successor—the Thar Roxx—in 2024. The two Thars contributed 15% to Mahindra’s sales in January. In fact, the cars launched in the past five years now account for half its sales.

Mahindra will hope to replicate the success of these vehicles with its new electric cars. MG Motor India resuscitated the market for electrics with Windsor and the option it gives customers not to pay for the battery while buying the car, making it more affordable. In the three months to December, MG sold over 10,000 Windsors, which made up half its total volume.

The renewed interest in electrics is great news for Mahindra. Motilal Oswal, a brokerage, expects its EVs to attract a new group of customers, “many of whom would not have considered purchasing an M&M vehicle earlier.” Several analysts have raised the target price for Mahindra after the latest quarterly results.

The stock is already the priciest in its sector and trades at 30X its forward earnings, miles ahead of Tata Motors’ 11X.

If there’s one thing Mahindra may be losing sleep over, it’s the automaker’s ability to keep up with the surging demand. It’s the kind of problem most companies would kill to have, but Mahindra has admitted it is “totally constrained” on capacity for some of its models. As for its electrics, anyone looking to buy the cheapest variant will have to wait six months to drive it.

It would be anticlimactic for Mahindra to have spent years winning over customers, only to have them turn on it now.

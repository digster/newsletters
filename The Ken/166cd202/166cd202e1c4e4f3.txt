Good Morning Ishan,

Direct current. Betamax. Wimax. Each of them was a casualty of what we call “format wars” - a fight between competing technology standards to win the race for adoption and critical support, ultimately forcing an entire market and ecosystem to adopt the winner. The examples I quoted lost out to alternating current (electricity transmission), VHS (video cassettes) and LTE (4G). 

Vendors who lose often end up not just losing years of effort and huge sums of money, but orders of magnitude more in terms of lost revenue and future profits. Thus, fights are often bitter and no-holds-barred, as they’re often death-matches. And because losing a format war is so existential, vendors are reluctant to admit defeat even as it becomes clear to the rest of the world that they ought to give up.

Survival instinct doesn’t listen to logic or facts.

Something similar is happening in a space that couldn’t be further removed from high technology - cookstoves. Yep.

Gayathri goes behind the global philanthropic & governmental effort to convert the 3 billion people who still cook their food on open fires or traditional stoves, a quarter of whom live in just one country - India. The emissions have been linked to pneumonia, stroke, heart and respiratory disease and cancer. “Clean” cookstoves that burn less wood and give out lesser smoke were the better “format”, said the organisations involved. Beginning in 2010, they wanted to distribute 100 million cookstoves by the end of the decade. 

But somewhere along the way, the tail started wagging the dog. As in, everyone involved - development organisations, funders like Shell Foundation, the US Government, Clinton Global Initiative and various social enterprises - started pushing cookstoves even though evidence started to emerge that perhaps they weren’t the best solution. Perhaps LPG stoves were. 

I’ve obviously taken editorial liberty to liken Gayathri’s deeply reported, nuanced story to a “format war”. But you know I’ve a weakness for analogies. Do excuse me.

Good Morning Ishan,
 
Before I joined The Ken, I was fairly certain I knew what the newsroom would look like—old, fat, balding men. Most with spectacles. All in suspenders. Rohin would be appalled.

It's an odious stereotype when it comes to business journalists, I know, but it isn't hard to see why it exists. A recent report points out that India's newsrooms are overwhelmingly male-dominated. The Economic Times, which has a competing product, had the best representation for women in terms of bylines. At 28.2%. In English dailies as a whole, only 20% of bylines were by women. And most were in "soft" beats.

So when I expected a similarly gender-skewed newsroom here, can you really blame me? The truth, though, was very different. Our editorial team is dominated by women. Inspiring, hardworking self-starters. In fact, up until we made our campus hires, you could count the men on the team on one hand and still have a few fingers free. Consequently, our bylines are usually dominated by women.

This past week though, our bylines were equally divided between the men and women, who produced uniformly great stories across a variety of sectors. And often, together.

We started the week with Jon's debut at The Ken. Based out of Bangkok, he turned his focus to the two companies looking to become the gatekeepers of Southeast Asia's digital economy—GoJek and Grab. The two are going head-to-head, and its having huge ramifications on the region's startup ecosystem.

On Tuesday, we had contributor Priyanka Pulla writing about AFP. What is AFP? These are cases of paralysis which present in ways similar to polio but have other causes. India saw over 36,000 cases of AFP in 2018. Worryingly, Priyanka points out, no central Indian agency is tracking non-polio causes of paralysis in India. It was our free story of the week, do read and share it widely.

Wednesday saw Durga and Pramshu team up to run the rule over OYO. Its many, oftentimes disparate, businesses, its valuation, its expansion. The whole spectrum of its existence. All, of course, with the aid of Prajakta's data visualisation wizardry. 

Then Ruhi re-visited the telemedicine space. A few years ago, she'd written about how Lybrate, India's first VC funded telemedicine startup, might feel the heat thanks to Jio. Now, she and Pranav write, Lybrate is a shadow of its former self. Not because of Jio, but because of the dynamics of India's telemedicine space.

We ended the week with a contributor copy from Nilesh Cristopher on Savaari. If you've rented an intercity cab before, you may have come across Savaari. The company has done the unthinkable in the intercity cab space—it recently turned a profit. Savaari, however, isn't quite out of the woods yet.

Before I end, I'd like to explain my focus on gender this week. A commenter, whom I will not name, disparaged a piece saying it seemed like it was by "a suspicious housewife, not a business reporter." The implications of this statement are manifold, but they're steeped in outdated, gendered thinking. Quality journalism knows no gender.
 
Have a great Sunday,

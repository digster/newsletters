Put some money in a box every month, forget about it, come back in 40 or 50 years—that’s the general idea of a pension fund.
You’re meant to have a little discipline, take advantage of compounding, and retire with financial security, all with some tax advantages sprinkled in. At the same time, you have limited options in how that money is invested. There might be regulatory red tape, and there’s still a dose of risk involved.
Some of that is changing for Indians putting their money into the National Pension Scheme (NPS). Anyone paying in can now opt for 100% exposure towards equities. If you’re truly, confidently long on India, the new rule is like manna from heaven because it could give you higher returns.
Besides reducing the vesting period from the age of 60 to a minimum of 15 years from the investment, the Pension Fund Regulatory and Development Authority (PFRDA) has also mooted the idea of halving the mandatory annuity limit to 20% of one’s retirement corpus.
This makes pensions look more like mutual funds. In fact, major asset categories, including equities, government securities, and corporate bonds, under the NPS have all given higher returns than under most mutual funds, even though the scheme lags in popularity.
The adjustment is meant to encourage higher participation in the NPS. In fact, there’s a goal for the scheme’s assets under management to grow by 10X, according to a senior pension-fund executive.
That’s a high target, and the NPS’s latest changes are meant to nudge it in that direction. But there are still conditions that weigh it down.

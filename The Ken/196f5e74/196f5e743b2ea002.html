I wonder which was the bigger U-turn: the US-China agreement on tariffs or Jio Infocomm and Bharti Airtel’s newfound love for Elon Musk’s Starlink.

After fiercely opposing SpaceX-owned satellite-internet service entry into India, the two telco giants struck commercial deals with it in March. And then on Tuesday, Starlink launched operations across Bangladesh.

Given its relentless rise—its revenue this year is expected to surge 75% to $16.3 billion—India couldn’t have stalled it for much longer. But, as Seema argues in her perceptive story today, the market reality might have left the telcos with little choice but to embrace Starlink.

Mobile-wireless growth is stalling, and servicing remote corners of the country is best done with satellite connectivity.

But Jio and Airtel sell data at the lowest rate in the world, and Starlink could charge them 10X what they can afford to pay for satellite-communications bandwidth.

Even if there was agreement on the price, what would Starlink gain from having the telcos as its customers, when 80% of its business in over 100 countries is reaching users directly?

Reliance Retail pays the price for size

Reliance Retail is a behemoth that sells everything—from the recently revived Campa Cola to luxury jackets. And the more than 19,300 stores across its verticals makes it the largest retailer in the country. But Reliance Retail seems to have run the race a bit too fast, and is now plagued by its very efforts to dominate every market it enters.

Good Morning Ishan,
Sometimes, we long to escape the urban chaos with a hillside cabin or beach day for a taste of solitude. But for full-time workers like me, such getaways could be a stretch.
So, occasionally, I take the metro in Chennai just to catch a breather—the nearly empty seats and the cityscape zipping by have an oddly soothing effect.
Now, my friends in Bengaluru may not be as fortunate. Why? Well, that’s the story for today.
Around 12 years ago, when the Namma Metro rolled into action, the Bangalore Metro Rail Corporation hoped it would offer the residents of one of the most traffic-congested cities in the world some reprieve from its jam-packed streets.
For a while, it did. But matters changed on 9 October. The opening of a highly anticipated, albeit only 2-km long, extension—connecting the commercial hub of Whitefield in the city’s east to Challaghatta in the west—has tied Namma Metro in knots.
Commuters say the coaches are so packed during office hours that even finding some space to stand is a real challenge.
A dearth of trains, a “Make in India” mandate, and an oversight in future-proofing serve up a cocktail of unsolved conundrums that threaten to make Bengaluru’s infamously crowded atmosphere a permanent fixture in its metro’s coaches.
So, how exactly did the new stretch manage to throw a spanner in the works of an entire metro network? Cheer up, Bangaloreans. At least the weather ain’t so bad.

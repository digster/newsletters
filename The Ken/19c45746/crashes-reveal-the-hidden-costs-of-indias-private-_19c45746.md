Good Morning Ishan,
The world of private jets and charter flights is so out of reach that what it takes to be in this business has been, well, under the radar. But that is by no means an indicator of just how teeming this space of non-scheduled flight operators has been.
There are over 80 domestic private-jet operators, and the number of non-scheduled aircraft operating in India rose by a third between 2021 and 2025, to over 430, according to the Directorate General of Civil Aviation (DGCA). But then so have the accidents. Between 2014 and 2020, non-scheduled operators accounted for nearly 40% of the total 56 aviation accidents.
One such accident that drew attention to this space was in late January in interior Maharashtra, which killed the state’s deputy chief minister, Ajit Pawar. Pawar was in an aircraft run by VSR Ventures, which is the largest in this space, with over 17 aircraft and over 70 pilots on its payroll.
For the pilots who work in these operators, helping the rich get around is a stressful business. Unlike commercial flights, where the airlines set the terms on what customers get, here it’s the reverse.
Nuha has a [timely story](https://the-ken.com/story/crashes-reveal-the-hidden-costs-of-indias-private-jet-boom/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter) that examines what it takes to run such an operation, how it’s prone to safety risks, and why even all these risks don’t deter the elite from flying private jets.

For the longest time now, one of the gripes of entrepreneurs in India has been the broken public procurement process. Elsewhere, as buyers, governments have made king-size companies. In a fair manner, of course. (No cronyism implied.)

In India, we’ve missed making procurement a lever for change and innovation. In diagnostics, state governments—all 29 of them—have made a start as they begin to offer free diagnostics. Together they’d be outsourcing lab tests worth over $1 billion every year.

Governments want the big diagnostic chains to bid for these tenders but, alas, they are not keen. “The government is not just a bulk buyer, it is a bully buyer,” says one. Then you have those like Wipro-GE, which have aced this, garnering nearly a quarter of their revenue from public contracts.

Now, a new, mid-size segment is emerging, from Punjab to Odisha, which is happy to adhere to government rules (even idiosyncrasies), costs, and surprisingly, quality demands. Ruhi’s story is about how these companies view the opportunities in the government and why they are managing the ‘lowest cost’, or L1, barrier which has kept many entrepreneurs away from doing business with the government.

Having seen much of health tech get obsolete in pilots, I think this trend gives hopes to small entrepreneurs to find a new distribution channel.

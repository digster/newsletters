Good Morning Ishan,

What connects Byju’s, Bharatpe, Go Mechanic, and Zilingo beyond just the headlines? Sure, you must think “financial irregularities” and you wouldn’t be wrong. But if you peel back the layers, there’s a question that probably crossed your mind too: “Where were the auditors?”

So, imagine this: you are a fly on the wall in any of the 350,000-plus auditors’ offices in India, where chartered accountants review financial statements and offer guidance to businesses. These days, what you would see or hear is not the hum of business as usual, but rather the nervous shuffle of papers and anxious murmurs.

Consider these snippets from Vanita’s conversations with CAs:

  • “There is risk, there is fear, and not many people want to practice chartered accountancy anymore.”
  • “We are not Lord Brahma who knows everything about what is happening in the company.”

What’s causing this sudden unease? Enter the National Financial Reporting Authority (NFRA), the auditors’ auditor. With financial irregularities becoming increasingly common, this regulatory body has been on a spree, dropping fines left and right. Since 2022, it has debarred 78 auditors and imposed penalties totalling over Rs 16 crore (US$1.9 million). The message is clear: NFRA means business; and it’s different from the Institute of Chartered Accountants of India (ICAI), the self-regulatory body.

But being an auditor isn’t easy—especially when you are expected to safeguard financial integrity while keeping your clients’ content. So, resignations in the profession have skyrocketed.

In today’s story, Vanita dives deep into the auditor-regulator standoff, exploring how much of the tension stems from the scope of auditors’ responsibilities.

It’s hot, over in Southeast Asia. No, not the weather. I am talking about the battle for fintech dominance, with Sea Limited’s financial-services arm—Seamoney—stepping into the ring as a worthy contender. In fact, it’s the only one among the other financial-services arms of the region’s tech giants, including the likes of Goto and Grab.

At Sea’s earnings call, the spotlight was on Seamoney. After all, the announcement said that it clocked nearly US$150 million adjusted Ebitda for the March quarter, and solidified its spot at the head of the profitability table.

But Seamoney didn’t stumble into success. Starting small, just greasing the wheels of transactions on Sea’s Shopee platform. But then it did what any good fintech player does: it evolved. Strategic pivots, diversified financial products (read: loans), and a calculated expansion beyond its e-commerce roots.

But the competition is heating up from other directions, and Seamoney knows it. Katrina today breaks down Seamoney’s sidekick-to-budding-star story, its ambitious plans for expansion, and the urgent need for innovation.

Meme stocks strike back(?)

Meme stocks, such as Gamestop, just keep on giving, don’t they? It’s 2024, with a Netflix series dedicated to the madness and regulators having already scratched their heads, yet the video-game retailer remains a sensation.

This Monday, the stock shot up over 70%, seemingly sparked by Keith Gill, the (in)famous “Roaring Kitty”, and his legion of small-time investors. Reminds you of 2021 Reddit, doesn’t it?

Once again, short sellers got burned—reports suggest losses north of US$1 billion. And it wasn’t just a US affair; Gamestop’s shares took off even on the Frankfurt exchange, hinting at a broader, possibly global, frenzy among retail investors.

For many, Gamestop represents more than a stock; it’s a movement, a rebellion against Wall Street. It’s financial populism. (Though, whispers suggest even hedge funds, traditionally guardians of Wall Street, may have joined the party.)

But let’s not romanticise this. There’s a thin line between populist investing and recklessness. This surge is fuelled by momentum and good old FOMO. In this theatre of absurdity, social media and influential figures play key roles. Tweets, memes, and viral posts get the power to move markets as much as quarterly earnings reports.

While it’s all exhilarating when stocks like Gamestop go to the moon, the landing can be brutal. Retail investors who jump in at the peak often find themselves nursing burly losses.

Sure, this week’s meme rally may have lost steam quicker than in 2021, but Gamestop is still a symbol—one of rebellion, community, and at the same time, the wild nature of modern markets.

As long as there are shorts to squeeze and memes to spread, this saga will continue.

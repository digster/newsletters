Good Morning Ishan,
Sometime last year, there was a moment when it felt like everyone was announcing the death of D2C. Some proclaimed that it was no longer a get-rich-quick model, while others said an offline presence was increasingly a must to survive in a digitally saturated market.
But proving those naysayers wrong, D2C, it seems, is back with a vengeance.
Just look at Marico’s back-to-back acquisitions of gourmet popcorn brand 4700 BC, skincare brand Skinetiq, and plant protein brand Cosmix—all in a three-week window. The bets seem even grander when you realise that the FMCG major spent nearly 10% of its FY25 revenue on them.
I don’t know if it’s just me, but I’ve also recently had cousins popping up across the country, announcing their intention to start their own D2C brands, whether in ramen or protein powder. All I can say is that they couldn’t have picked a better time.
The rampant rise of quick commerce platforms, not to mention younger generations’ growing affinity for ordering from the comfort of their couches, means D2C food brands, in particular, are having their moment in the sun.
In such a frothy market, what does it take for a D2C brand to be acquired? Aakriti asks in [today’s story](https://the-ken.com/story/the-art-of-building-a-d2c-food-brand-to-be-acquired/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter). Clue: the magic number may be 100,00,00,000.

Three keys to a differentiated vision. All stakeholders, from angel investors to stock markets to employees to customers, want to invest in companies with vision

It can take us a lifetime to accumulate experiences, discover preferences, and formulate beliefs about the world, people, and life. But there are occasionally moments that force us to unlearn everything we thought we knew. 

Edgar Rubin, a Danish psychologist from the early 20th century, proved this point in his dissertation with this one image:

Do you see a vase? Or two faces? 

Take a moment and look again. Whichever image was foregrounded for you is also the background for the other image.

In this investigation, Rubin’s major aim was to describe the visual experience of figure and ground. He analysed his own perceptions and those of numerous participants, written down in detailed verbal protocols. Here Rubin established that there are several aspects to the figure–ground distinction.

The most important of these is the fact that the experienced figure and the experienced ground are not shaped in the same manner – indeed the ground, in some sense, is without shape. Sometimes the ground, when it changes into a figure, takes us by surprise, due to that fact that it now becomes a specific shape.

Looking back: Figure and ground at 100, The British Psychological Society

Therapists, psychologists, and mental health counsellors witness such shifts with what they call breakthrough moments during sessions with clients. A sudden shift in perspective where the client, used to seeing their life, memories, and experiences in a certain way, suddenly experiences a change in perspective. On the most fundamental level, this shift often gives birth to empathy—the ability to see the world from another’s shoes. And on the most profound level, it leads to what Buddhist monks call Satori—a moment where illusions of form and phenomena disappear.

This ability to shift perspective is crucial for leaders, because people look to them for vision—a glimpse of a world that could be—and thus, hope. In the world of business, such visions are usually documented and expressed in the form of vision statements. 

All stakeholders, from angel investors to stock markets to employees to customers, want to invest in companies with vision. 

So how do you craft a truly compelling one? 

Let’s unpack.

The three elements of a differentiated vision 

Forbes argues https://www.forbes.com/councils/forbesagencycouncil/2023/07/19/the-big-mistake-of-mission-and-vision-statements/ that most companies get their vision statements wrong because they are learning the wrong lessons on how to create one.

Take a look at Indeed, the job site which offers this explanation: “A vision statement is a declaration of a company’s goals for the future. It outlines what it plans to become and how it aims to reach its goals. An effective vision statement promotes growth and innovation, answering the question, ‘Where does a company want to go?'”

[…]

Harvard Business Review offers a set of definitions that add to the noise: “A vision statement says what the organization wishes to be like in some years’ time. It’s usually drawn up by senior management, in an effort to take the thinking beyond day-to-day activity in a clear, memorable way.

[…]

It’s actually pretty simple. A vision statement should answer a fundamental question: What does the world look like if we succeed? This is what a lot of the definitions miss. The fact is that a vision statement has to be bigger than your brand. It’s the ultimate “why” for a company existing, which means it has to go beyond self-aggrandizement to a larger significance in the world. This is even more important today, when customers want to see that you are devoted to more than revenues and share price.

Visions stand out, and actually inspire and move people to act, when they transcend inward-facing identities and goals. When it is of larger significance to the world and life in general. 

To be most successful, visions have to crack three specific elements: 

First, it needs to be bigger than you and me, and focused towards achieving a greater good—one that is universal and inclusive. Linkedin and Ikea, for instance, have visions to create economic opportunities for every member of the global workforce, and a better day-to-day life for customers, respectively. 

Second, a strong vision promises the death of the status quo, but in a realistic way. Disney and Spotify are two companies that have imagined a world very different from the one that exists right now. 

Disney: The mission of The Walt Disney Company is to entertain, inform and inspire people around the globe through the power of unparalleled storytelling, reflecting the iconic brands, creative minds and innovative technologies that make ours the world’s premier entertainment company.

Spotify: Our mission is to unlock the potential of human creativity—by giving a million creative artists the opportunity to live off their art and billions of fans the opportunity to enjoy and be inspired by it.

Disney is specific about the emotions it wants its stories to evoke, but also ambitious about the power of storytelling to effect change. Similarly, Spotify focuses on the intrinsic need for artists to make a living off their art, and makes that the core of the future world it promises. 

And finally, a true visionary changes their audience’s perspectives, but not by relying on illusion. Instead, the most powerful visions are those that seem real and achievable. The best example for this is Steve Jobs and how he went about articulating his vision:

“What is your vision for the personal computer?” Campbell asked Jobs.  Campbell said what happened next still gives him goosebumps. “Steve Jobs was a magical storyteller,” Campbell told me.  “For the next hour, he talked about how personal computers were going to change the world.  He painted a picture of how it would change everything about the way we worked, educated our children and entertained ourselves.  You couldn’t help but buy in.”  Vision, said Campbell, was the one thing that separated Steve Jobs from the others.

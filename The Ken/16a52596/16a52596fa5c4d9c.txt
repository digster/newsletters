Good Morning Ishan,

Everyone has a story about the most offbeat travel experience they have ever had. Scuba diving at Galapagos Islands. Biking in Ladakh in winter. Taking a cab to Bangalore Airport. 

Mine is when I was in Cape Town, South Africa for two weeks last year. When it was days away from becoming the first major city in the world to completely run out of water. 

I saw the city do strange things. A special in-flight announcement was made when we landed. Restaurants politely declined to serve us water with our meals. Swimming Pools were emptied and filled with sea-water. At our Airbnb, showers couldn’t be longer than two minutes (They had ad campaigns to show us how to do it). Toilets had to be flushed just once a day. The city even had a website with a map marking out homes which met their quota of consumption. You could check if you or your neighbours were one of them. And huge hoardings all across Cape Town counted down the number of days left for Day Zero - when water would finally run out. 

But there was one interesting aspect I noticed. It’s hard to miss, especially if you are from India. 

The taps were never turned off. Not once. 

Surely the best way to do this would be to ration water supply by turning it on for a few hours every day? Happens here all the time. I tried finding out why Cape Town didn’t do this - not even when things were extremely dire. I never got convincing answers. 

Until I read today’s story by Olina. 

She writes how over 20 cities in India are going to face what Cape Town did. The story posits that one key part of the solution may be something called smart meters. To measure consumption patterns. At a unit level. At an apartment level. At a home.

In other words, water shortages can’t just be solved by curbing supply. You need to understand demand first. Which is exactly what Cape Town did. It measured. It understood. It created disincentives. It didn’t just turn off the taps. 

But smart meters aren’t a done deal yet. Many obstacles exist along the way.

A lawyer friend once told me a joke about antitrust. 
 
Three CEOs meet in a bar after being assessed by the competition commission for antitrust practices. The subject turns to what they had done to merit an investigation. 
 
The first says, "I charged higher prices than everybody else and they accused me of profiteering."
 
The second says, "I charged lower prices than everybody else and they accused me of predatory pricing."
 
The third says, "I charged the same prices as everybody else, and they accused me of collusion and price fixing."
 
It’s likely the only joke about antitrust ever told. Antitrust law is usually dense, complicated and incredibly nuanced. However, the parallels between the joke and what’s happening right now with Google, Amazon and Facebook couldn’t be starker. Consider this. 
 
In 2017, a 28-year-old law student, Lina Khan caught the attention of the community with her Yale Law Journal paper, "Amazon's Antitrust Paradox", which demonstrated how companies like Amazon could crush competitors, employees and suppliers all through the guise of lower prices, which seemingly work for the consumer.
 
Then two months ago, the European competition commissioner, Margrethe Vestager, levied a 1.49 billion euro fine on Google, for placing anti-competitive contractual restrictions on publishers. Even though, technically, Google wasn’t playing on price.
 
In February, Dina Srinivasan, an ad-tech entrepreneur, wrote a paper titled "The antitrust case against Facebook". In it, she made the case how Facebook violates competition law—not because it charges higher prices than others—which it does, but also because it lowers quality for consumers as a whole, by using privacy as a differentiator. It’s brilliant. 
 
Three companies. Three women. All fighting to redefine the boundaries of what makes something anti-competitive, why, and how price really has little to do with it. That’s the old way of looking at antitrust. These are the new ones. 
 
Then there’s India. 
 
In our story today, Arundhati deftly breaks these arguments down and illustrates how they can be applied in the Indian context by the Competition Commission of India against Facebook, Amazon and Google. Three women have shown the way. Will India follow?

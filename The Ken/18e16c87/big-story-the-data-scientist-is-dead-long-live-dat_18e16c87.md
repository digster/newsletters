Good Morning Ishan,
There’s a scene in Minority Report where the protagonist, John Anderton, played by Tom Cruise, paces by futuristic billboards and displays. Sensors scan his eyes, and holographic advertisements beckon him to imagine himself driving a Lexus, buying diamond jewellery, and investing in real estate.
The scene is meant to caution us about surveillance, but it’s also a prediction of how retailers might target us when they have massive troves of data on hand.
We aren’t close to that version of events yet (I hope!), but the ways of working with data are already changing because of generative artificial intelligence, as you’ll find out in today’s Big Story.
As large language models become increasingly accessible and customisable, enterprises can do more with less, and the halos of data scientists are dimming while billable hours get cut.
“There are reasons to think that generative AI could be to data scientists somewhat analogous to what ATMs were to bank tellers,” H James Wilson, a global managing director at IT consulting firm Accenture, told The Ken.
The result? Data scientists who can roll with the punches will become relevant in new ways.
The lines between disciplines and skill sets are blurring, so data scientists need to get creative with use cases, develop deeper domain expertise, and collaborate with business leaders. Check out Seema’s coverage about the [changing nature](https://the-ken.com/story/data-scientist-is-dead-long-live-data-science-how-indias-largest-analytics-companies-are-coping-with-genai/?utm_source=daily&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter) of this line of work.
Most of us care about how AI will change the way we work and whether we’ll be replaced by automated systems soon. Even though economists and futurists speak of higher personal efficiency and the jobs that AI will spawn, the fear is real.
I recently spoke with a Hong Kong-based news editor who is required to upload drafts and help train a tool that will someday rewrite articles with little to no human assistance.
There are few jobs that will be untouched by AI applications, and money is pouring into the space at a brisk rate.
The Financial Times reported that Singapore’s Temasek Holdings is weighing an investment in OpenAI. If a deal is sealed, it’ll be the first time for a state-backed fund to write a cheque for the AI developer. Last month, OpenAI chief Sam Altman was in talks with the UAE government and other investors to raise trillions of dollars for a semiconductor and AI initiative.
If there were a Fear and Greed Index just for the AI space, it would be fair to say we’re squarely on the “greed” side of the spectrum!
This week, in his column for The Information, Tim O’Reilly made the point that there’s a land grab happening in the AI space, leading to doubts about fair competition and an overfunded market. In practice, AI will probably bring everyone some benefits, even if it displaces workers before they settle into new roles.
For most of us, AI will become part of our daily tools. But for the select few who had the capital to flush into a handful of leading developers, it’s a path to dynasty-forming wealth.

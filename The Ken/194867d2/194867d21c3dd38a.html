After kiranas, quick commerce has come for restaurants. 

In select pincodes of cities like Gurugram, Bengaluru, and Mumbai, a huddle of riders are even now delivering quick snacks such as samosas, thai chicken momos, and bhel puri to satisfy the pangs of impatient consumers who want it all under 10 minutes. 

Zomato, Swiggy, and Zepto, the leaders in 10-minute grocery delivery, are all now doing 10-minute food delivery too. In fact, last week, Zomato announced an additional investment of Rs 500 crore (US$58 million) for its quick-commerce business, which analysts believe will also be spent on setting up new cloud kitchens of its own. 

In a letter to investors on Monday, Zomato founder Deepinder Goyal admitted that the company’s earlier attempt at quick food deliveries—Zomato Instant—had to be shut down because of a flawed economic model. But this time around, with quick-commerce unit Blinkit’s new Bistro, he said, they would put their focus on building denser kitchen networks to reduce travel time, and pare down food preparation time by working with food researchers. 

Through the Zomato app, he added, they would also launch deliveries under 15 minutes for restaurants listed on the aggregation platform—Zomato will curate their menus and provide a dedicated delivery fleet. 

Now may seem like a good time for Zomato and Swiggy, which launched its own 10-minute food-delivery app Snacc earlier this month, to make sure they break into instant food deliveries. After all, quick commerce is one of the fastest growing segments in India’s consumer economy. 

But there’s more to it than just market expansion. 

According to two Dalal Street analysts tracking this space, there may be a certain amount of “panic” involved too. 

“It is a natural segway, yes. But I think more importantly, at this point, both Swiggy and Zomato cannot afford to let Zepto have a free run,” one analyst at a Mumbai-based brokerage said.

The Zepto Factor

Zepto’s instant food-delivery service, Zepto Cafe, was launched in 2022. But it only started gaining traction last year. 

Since it kicked into gear, though, its growth has been rapid. Today, Zepto Cafe receives over 50,000 orders a day, representing roughly 3–5% of Zepto’s total orders. And earlier this month, the company launched a separate app for the service.

“Zepto Cafe has a completely different team that runs it. It’s almost like a separate company within Zepto, personally overseen by co-founder Aadit Palicha.”

A former executive at Zepto

Zepto prepares the food and beverages in a cut-out space within existing dark stores, according to a restaurant owner aware of its operations. That is a pretty nifty advantage, because when food is prepared in-house, or is private-labeled, the margin profile is quite attractive. 

As per the former Zepto employee quoted above, the net margin for the grocery delivery business is currently about 2–5% of order value. But for private labels in the food business, this can be anywhere between 25–60%. 

Which is why even platforms that had given up on the cloud-kitchen and private-label business have now taken a U-turn on it. 

Goyal touched on Zomato’s failed first attempt at instant food delivery in his letter, but Zomato and Swiggy have also taken a shot at building cloud kitchens. Zomato Infrastructure Services and Swiggy Access were cloud-kitchen verticals that provided their restaurant partners with kitchen infrastructure and were supposed to make food deliveries faster. Swiggy also ran a private food label, The Bowl Company, before shutting it down in 2022. 

But this model was a loss-making one, according to analysts and restaurant owners that The Ken spoke to. The real estate for the kitchens was expensive, and it was drawing flak from restaurant partners, who felt it was anti-competitive for aggregators to start selling their own private labels on marketplaces while feeding on the restaurants’ data.  

In the end, both Zomato and Swiggy wound up their cloud kitchens. 

Quick commerce, and Zepto Cafe’s success, is luring them back in. The real estate wouldn’t be as expensive if the kitchens were located inside existing dark stores, for instance.

Restaurants still miffed

The other problem remains, though. The National Restaurant Association of India (NRAI) on 9 January said it was considering legal action against the food aggregators for allegedly violating platform neutrality and “creating an uneven playing field”. 

The NRAI claims that prices of various food items such as momos, samosas, biryanis, idlis, and tea are as much as 40% cheaper on the 10-minute food delivery apps compared to restaurants in the same space. In addition, the restaurants body says unlike regular food delivery apps, there is no delivery fee on 10-minute food delivery apps. 

However, Albinder Dhindsa, the chief executive of Blinkit, claimed last week that the new Bistro service is not being built within the Zomato platform, and that no Zomato restaurant data has been used.

Right now, it isn’t clear whether 10-minute food-delivery services will lean towards cloud kitchens and in-house food labels or collaborating with restaurants. 

According to analysts, it’s unlikely 10-minute food delivery can ever entirely replace regular food delivery, for the simple reason that only select snacks and beverages—like juices, coffee, burgers, vada pav, momos—can be prepared in under 10 minutes. 

The food business is also operationally intensive and requires a lot of branding, noted Kawaljeet Singh, founder of food chain Khadak Singh Da Dhaba. A customer is more likely to order food from an established brand than an unknown name. 

Regardless, many restaurant chains have started to work on “reducing the KPT” (kitchen preparation time) in the last few months. For instance, momo chain Wow! Momo started working on quicker food preparation around nine months ago, according to founder Sagar Daryani, who is also the president of NRAI. 

“We did our field trips and brought equipment from China and Taiwan to reduce time for momo preparation. The time taken to prepare a momo has already been reduced from four minutes to two minutes.”

Daryani believes 10-minute food delivery is here to stay. The NRAI’s only ask, he says, is that aggregators “build it with restaurants rather than on the backs of them”.

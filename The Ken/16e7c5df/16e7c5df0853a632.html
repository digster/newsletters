It was just over two years ago that Disney stunned the world by announcing it would pull all of its content from Netflix. Last week, Disney launched its much awaited streaming service, Disney Plus. And promptly signed up 10 million users within 24 hours!
 
Last week, Nike decided to pull all of its products from being officially listed on Amazon. Nike said it did this to focus better on "consumer experiences through more direct, personal relationships.”
 
Tim Armstrong, former CEO of AOL, called Nike’s decision the tip of the iceberg when it came to brands going direct-to-consumer after shunning platforms. "If they have the option to go direct, they are going to go direct,” he said.
 
And in today’s story, Arundhati reports on the most unlikely of candidates to do just that in India: HDFC Bank. 
 
HDFC is India’s largest private bank and its largest issuer of credit cards. It's used to being treated well in any relationship. But it got a serious jolt when earlier this year, Amazon and Flipkart, India’s largest e-commerce platforms, chose to partner with its rivals ICICI Bank and Axis Bank to issue co-branded credit cards. HDFC’s bid did not even meet the criteria laid out by Amazon and Flipkart. And that’s when it realised its power equation with the e-commerce giants was wobblier than it thought.
 
So, during this year’s festive Diwali season, HDFC Bank decided to stay off Amazon and Flipkart and instead build its own festive shopping platform. If I tell you what HDFC decided to call it, you’ll think it was just some smalltime experiment done by a marketing executive inside a large, conservative bank. Try, “Festive Treats”?
 
But as Arundhati reports, this is HDFC’s first big step to recreate the magic of a direct-to-customer relationship, something it (and other banks) have been losing rapidly to e-commerce and fintech companies. "Banks are now saying I want to be important as an Amazon in the customer’s life,” said a senior banker to her.

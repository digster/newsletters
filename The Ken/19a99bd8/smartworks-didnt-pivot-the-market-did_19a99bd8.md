Long and Short
Wed, 19 Nov 25
The story behind the numbers and motivations driving India's stock market.
Good Morning Ishan,
Walk around Bengaluru’s techy neighbourhoods long enough, and you would think there are only two kinds of offices—tall, gleaming skyscrapers that look imposing enough to shape the new world order, and quirky coworking spaces where a new startup seems to appear (and disappear) from a desk every day.
And then you walk into a Smartworks in Bellandur.
Punching bags and boardrooms, cricket pitches and CFOs. The space feels like coworking—until the tenant list makes your left eyebrow twitch. Google, Accenture, Persistent Systems, EY, Tech Mahindra, Kotak Mahindra Bank, and even Starbucks–from where most employees in the building pick up their coffee.
This is not a startup playground. This is enterprise real estate wearing athleisure.
In Q2 FY26, Smartworks brought in about Rs 425–441 crore in revenue and shrank its quarterly loss from Rs 15.8 crore to just Rs 3.1 crore. A sharp difference from Indiqube–newly listed, still in the red–and Awfis, which is growing, but with a considerable dip in profits.
Over the last three months, Smartworks, which has a market cap of Rs 6,581 crore, has had its stock climb roughly 25%, while Awfis has slipped about 4%, and Indiqube is barely above water at around 1–2%.
| Source: Trendlyne |
It could be unfair to compare the three because Smartworks isn’t competing in the same sport. While its peers chase startups and SMEs—segments getting hammered by the tech slowdown—Smartworks built for enterprises and GCCs from day one. Its model is basically office-as-a-service for companies with money, headcount, and zero patience for playing landlord.
And nearly ten years after inception, the long bet is finally cashing its first real cheque. Today, the company serves over 760 corporate and enterprise clients, with around 90% of its rental revenue coming from large enterprises.
In the world of office spaces, Real Estate Investment Trusts (REITs) are companies that own income-generating commercial real estate. They don’t design cafés or manage operations. Think: office parks, warehouses, hotels–anything that pays rent.
Coworking spaces, on the other hand, are the retail stores of office space. Operators take a huge floor (or floors), slice them into sections, and rent out the space—a desk here, a meeting room there. They throw in cafés, pool tables, maybe even kombucha taps for the vibe.
Smartworks is the rare in-betweener in Indian real estate—too operationally heavy to be a REIT, too enterprise-loaded to be coworking–but perfectly aligned with the moment when GCCs have stopped experimenting and started expanding. GCCs don’t want beanbags; they want 1,000 seats, compliance, cafeterias, and a partner who won’t panic when they say “we need another floor by March.” Smartworks didn’t pivot into that market—it happened to be positioned there before the wave arrived.
And that’s exactly where its rivals are getting squeezed.
Awfis was built for a different era—2015 to 2021—when startups were hoovering up seats, SMEs were expanding faster than their balance sheets, and hot desks were the future of work. That engine has stalled. SME expansion is cautious at best. And a model built on many small tenants and high churn starts to shake the moment the market does. The numbers tell the story: revenue up, but profits are down nearly 59%.
And Indiqube is more enterprise-focused than Awfis, but still tied to the same multi-tenant, mid-market churn economy. Its revenue rose, but it still ended the quarter Rs 30 crore in the red. Indiqube wants to climb upmarket, but most of its portfolio wasn’t designed for whole-floor GCC play.
While the tech hiring freeze hurt everyone else, it didn’t touch Smartworks. Because while IT services barely added headcount this year, GCCs went the other direction—expanding across cities, locking in multi-year deals, and demanding scale.
Just this Monday, Smartworks leased around 1.66 lakh sq ft of office space in Pune to Netherlands-headquartered Saas company Wolters Kluwer.
In its [investor call](https://www.bseindia.com/xml-data/corpfiling/AttachHis/93b008d1-be35-42b0-9c8b-9632aaa792b4.pdf) after the second-quarter results, the company said that GCCs now drive over 15% of its rental revenue, and it expects the sector to grow even more. Also, clients taking 1,000 seats or more already contribute one-third of rentals—up from 12% three years ago.
And then comes the part nobody in coworking can copy: [Smartvantage](https://www.constructionweekonline.in/business/smartworks-smartvantage#:~:text=Smartworks%20launches%20SmartVantage,-SmartVantage%20provides%20GCCs&text=Smartworks%20Coworking%20Spaces%20has%20launched,the%20unique%20identity%20of%20GCCs.).
Until now, Smartworks sold offices. Now it sells operations.
Launched in September this year, Smartvantage wraps compliance, onboarding, legal, taxation, regulatory workflows—the entire messy underbelly of setting up and scaling a GCC—into a single, enterprise-grade service layer. A client that plugs in wouldn’t just be leasing space; they would be offloading a chunk of their India operations to Smartworks.
This isn’t a full-stack GCC building, like ANSR. But it is Smartworks stepping into a space that coworking operators can’t reach and REITs won’t go near. Coworking players are built to handle the deep operational work GCCs need. REITs, by design, don’t offer anything beyond real estate.
Smartworks–with over 760 clients and 46 centres in 14 cities–is planting itself right in that middle ground. This makes the “in-betweener” label even more accurate: not coworking, not a landlord, but something more hands-on and more useful to enterprises.
With tech hiring in a long, moody winter, Smartworks has already begun hedging its bets. Over the last few years, the company has rewritten its demand mix: “Non-IT/ITES now accounts for more than 60% of our demand, largely from consumer, healthcare, and manufacturing,” management told investors.
Earlier this [month](https://www.moneycontrol.com/news/business/smartworks-signs-deal-with-hiranandani-group-for-india-s-largest-flexible-office-campus-in-mumbai-13650087.html), Smartworks inked a lease for 8.15 lakh sq ft at Eastbridge in Vikhroli (West)—a Hiranandani project that will become India’s largest flexible office campus when it goes live in 2026. At roughly Rs 120–121 per sq ft, it’s a Rs 9.4–9.5 crore monthly rental commitment—the kind of cheque no coworking operator built on hot desks would dare to sign.
As one Smartworks insider put it to me, “If you want BFSI, you go to Mumbai. That’s where the big boys are.” Clearly, BFSI is the next obvious pillar.
All of this is happening against the backdrop of a flex-office market that’s booming on paper and tightening in practice. [Cushman & Wakefield’s](https://www.cushmanwakefield.com/en/india/news/2025/09/india-emerges-as-a-global-leader-in-flexible-office-market-maturity) 2025 maturity index ranks India as the most advanced flex-office market in the world, with stock expected to cross 100 million sq ft next year. [Knight Frank](https://aprea.asia/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/Strong_Demand_keep_Occupancy_in_Flex_Spaces_over_80_across_markets-_Knight_Frank_India_Report.pdf) reports that flex occupancy is already above 80% in major cities—94% in Mumbai, 92% in NCR.
But dig deeper and the picture gets more nuanced: demand is shifting away from “coworking” and towards managed offices built for enterprises, with ESG compliance, tech integration, and long contracts baked in. In other words, the market is growing, but the room for traditional coworking is shrinking. Enterprise-led, campus-style operators are where the momentum is—and Smartworks is simply better positioned for that pivot than most of its peers.
The flex business has always rewarded whoever bets correctly on the next tenant wave. Indiqube bet on the mid-market. Awfis bet on the startup boom. Smartworks bet on enterprises and GCCs.
And that’s why this quarter lands differently.

So we’re doing one of our quick roundups today.
Google joins the brick-and-mortar game
Reuters [reported last week](https://www.reuters.com/technology/google-close-picking-sites-india-first-retail-stores-outside-us-sources-say-2025-02-20/) that India could become the first country outside the US to host physical Google retail stores, where the tech giant will sell its range of smartphones and other electronic devices.
Rival Apple’s success in plumbing the Indian market has set off some FOMO, perhaps, because Google has a grand total of just five such outlets right now. Quoting from Reuters:
Google is in advanced stages of finalising locations in or around the capital of New Delhi, and the financial capital Mumbai, said three sources, who declined to be named as the process is confidential.
Google declined to comment on the matter.
The first source said the stores are likely to be around 15,000 square feet, and will take at least another six months to open, though the timeline could change.
The IT hub of Bengaluru in south India was also considered, but New Delhi and Mumbai are the frontrunners, the person added.
“The idea is to compete with Apple… especially to target the luxury segment,” said the person, adding more stores will be considered if the initial ones do well.
To put that into some context, Apple, despite its relatively high price tags for the price-sensitive Indian market, became one of the country’s top five smartphone sellers for the first time last quarter. And its CEO Tim Cook is doubling down, especially on its brick-and-mortar plans: “India set a December-quarter record during the quarter, and we’re opening more stores there… we’re going to open four new stores there.”
Clearly Google, whose own line of Pixel smartphones have hardly dented Apple’s hold on the global handhelds segment, thinks it can’t let India’s premium market go unchallenged.
Nestle mulls taking Starbucks to supermarket shelves
Nestle, which acquired the rights to market Starbucks’ packaged coffee and food products worldwide in 2018, is exploring bringing the brand to Indian supermarkets, [reports](https://www.thehindubusinessline.com/companies/nestle-mulling-extending-global-starbucks-coffee-partnership-to-india/article69176849.ece) The Hindu BusinessLine. The arrangement allows Nestle to sell Starbucks instant coffee ranges, capsules for coffee machines, K-Cup pods, etc., outside Starbucks outlets, but hasn’t yet been expanded to cover the Indian market.
Nestle India is also rolling out its premium coffee machine line.
Axel Touzet, Head of Nestlé’s Coffee Strategic Business Unit said, “We see tremendous potential for growth of coffee in India. The more we make progress in terms of household penetration and more consumers acquire the coffee habit, the bigger the coffee business will become in the country.”
[…]
Meanwhile, Nestle India is introducing the Nespresso portfolio including coffee capsules, machines and accessories. It is also set to soon open the first Nespresso boutique in New Delhi and is planning to expand it into other major cities. “India is one of the fastest growing coffee markets for us. We have seen that affluent consumers have the appetite for the Nespresso portfolio. There is definitely a market for high-end coffee propositions in India and that is why we have decided to introduce the brand in India,” Touzet explained.
The coffee market in India is predicted to reach around $1.23 billion by 2032 fuelled by younger consumers who consider coffee drinking aspirational.
Another company betting on the premium end of India’s consumer market to work its recent magic?
What else is new?
DTDC expands into rapid commerce
The company, one of the older names in the courier and B2B logistics segment, entered the rapid delivery space earlier this month by rolling out same-day and 2–4-hour deliveries. And it intends to follow this up by offering B2B rapid delivery services for products such as spare parts.
To quote [a Livemint story](https://www.livemint.com/companies/news/dtdc-quick-delivery-aftermarket-spare-auto-components-after-quick-delivery-launch-delhivery-xpressbees-ecommerce-11739849438721.html):
“Within the B2B segment, there is a lot of importance in spaces like aftermarket and spare parts that could sometimes take days to get delivered. Most of our customers in this segment were facing this challenge because of which their real sales got impacted,” said Abhishek Chakraborty, chief executive, DTDC Express told Mint.
“We spent the last 3-6 months to help them restructure their supply chain and now, some of our customers are exploring rapid commerce mechanism to help deliver those essential parts to their service engineers very quickly.”
[…]
“Different brands, depending on their products and customer base, will need different solutions, and we now see that rapid delivery has reached a point where it is more than just ordering eggs or delivering daily groceries,” Chakraborty said.
“It is more of a fundamental change in consumer behavioural patterns, and with that, we see a certain kind of scale in the possibilities that emerge from this shift.”
I guess nothing is escaping the quick-commerce bug in India.
Not even spare parts.

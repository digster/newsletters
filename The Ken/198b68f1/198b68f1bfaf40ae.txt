“They say, a coconut tree never harms anyone,” said my botanist uncle to me as we stood under one laden with ripe coconuts at the entrance to his house in Kerala. Just a day earlier, another family member had mentioned the exact saying when I pointed out a tree that was leaning majorly to one side, right at the edge of the Vembanad lake.

There are few trees that are so interwoven into the culture and milieu of a place as the coconut tree is in Kerala.

For instance, how many parts of a tree—any tree—can you name? Not in English, but the language of your parents and grandparents?

I realised I knew the Malayalam words for so many of the coconut tree’s parts by virtue of how embedded they were into life in Kerala, a state I spent all my childhood summer vacations in. To the extent that I did not know the English names for virtually 80% of them.

Do you know what the coconut flower’s spathe is? Or its spadix?

Do you know what the young coconut fruit is called when you can fit two–three of them in your palm?

What is the word for each stalk of its giant leaves? And what do you call the spine that runs through each leaf, the dried versions of which are used to make broomsticks?

The reason I realised I know the Malayalam names for all of these, and more, is because most parts of the coconut tree end up being used in some form of the other.

Its dried wood, leaves, branches, and even flower parts were used as firewood in my grandmother’s traditional kitchen stove. It was the highlight of my day when my grandma would entrust me with the responsibility of fetching an assortment from the shed.

Its dried husk was thrown into cordoned off enclosures in the backwaters so they could soften up, and then beaten and woven into coir ropes by the women https://www.cppr.in/articles/coir-kerala-ylf of the neighbourhood working in groups.

Its leaves are woven into rooftops, privacy partitions and even traditional fishing traps https://keralamarinelife.in/Journals/Vol1-12/07%20Shaji%20C%20P.pdf.

We used its individual leaflets to catch prawns, dried spathes as play boats, leaflet spines to hook and carry the fish we caught, and baby coconuts that had fallen off trees to make throwaway slingshots.

I now understand why Keralites believe a coconut tree never harms anyone.

There is a vocabulary we develop through education and books. And then there is one we acquire by just being part of wondrous experiences, even if the significance of them only becomes clear to us decades afterwards.

Isn’t language such a wonderful thing?

1. Putting my First Principles money where my mouth is

This newsletter is called “First Principles”. Each Sunday, we talk about reflecting, rethinking, and reimagining how to live our professional and personal lives.

Today, I want to talk about how I spent most of Friday—India’s 79th Independence Day—thinking from First Principles on how best to find a set of smart, curious, and ambitious colleagues to reimagine The Ken into its second decade.

Next year, we turn 10. In 2016, when I was starting The Ken with my co-founders, I had zero thoughts about what our first ten years would be like. “Can this be a thing?” was my predominant thought as we launched a narrowly focused, premium, and dramatically limited subscription product.

Well, it turns out it could be. We survived. This October we will turn 10.

And while existential doubt and rear-view mirror paranoia is still a constant companion for me, I am incredibly hopeful about the next 10 years for The Ken.

Because, thanks to you—our subscribers—we’ve managed to build a resilient business model that is also backed by a trusted journalism brand. And thanks to my ambitious and talented colleagues, we’ve also built several products and features that we’re immensely proud of.

But that’s the last decade. What about the next?

In the words of Marshall Goldsmith, “What got us here won’t take us there.”

So, we’re refactoring The Ken.

We’re changing our org structure. We’re creating new functions. We’re reimagining our products. We’re rearchitecting our infrastructure. We’re expanding our business model. We’re creating newer products.

Now that we’ve almost “survived” our first decade, we have a longer arc for our ambition.

What will journalism-based products look like in a post-AI age? What differentiated value will our subscribers expect from us when they’re surrounded by super-capable and super-eager chatbots backed by tech giants? How will traditional formats like longform features or infographics or podcasts compete for subscriber attention? How will live events help us address the biggest questions you have? What will our product user experiences be like? How will we enable our subscribers to learn from each other, through a community?

We cannot do this all by our current selves. Which is why we’re looking for some equally smart, ambitious, and curious First Principles thinker-doers to join us.

Remember I told you I spent the better part of Friday trying to figure this out? Well, that’s because I used it to reimagine what our thought process for such an ambitious hiring project should be. This is it.

2. Are readers writers? 

Hello Ishan, this is Tanim, the newest member of The Ken’s newsletters desk and the one taking the baton from Hari to curate your weekly dives into the wonderful world of books!  

I know all of you read (a lot!), but do you also write? 

Of course, all writers are readers. But how many of you actually put down those precious thoughts of yours on paper or word document? 

Rohin has already written in First Principles about how writing helps us think better, and why it is so crucial to not outsource it to AI. 

How did we get to a stage where we’re giving up our ability to think to tools like these?

Answering that is beyond the scope of this edition and even this newsletter, but one reason is because we’ve all been told that the more we rely on AI tools, the better we get.

Yes, in a small set of cases, that is true. But only because those relying on the AI tools are already experienced and amongst the best in their field, whether programmers or doctors.

But for most others, AI inhibits their own critical thinking and growth. Especially when we outsource our writing to it.

Is writing, thinking? https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/is-writing-thinking/, First Principles by The Ken

So, keeping that in mind, I have an ambitious proposition for all of you. We’re going to change the script for this section a little. 

Instead of just sharing recommendations and short notes, I want members of the community to actually write for us about the books you’ve read. 

We consume so much in our day-to-day life—ideas, memories, experiences—but we only truly begin digesting them when we reflect, discuss, and write. This could be your go-to space for thinking out loud to the community. 

Don’t think of it as a book review—not all the time, at least. Don’t summarise it; argue with it. Wrestle with its ideas. Write down why it excites you, infuriates you, or makes you think differently—even if you haven’t finished it yet. Did a passage stop you in your tracks? Tell us all why. Do you disagree with the author? Tell them how. Fiction, non-fiction, poetry, everything is welcome. 

We want to give you this space to create, not just consume. 

You can still keep recommending books as usual, but if you are feeling creative, get on a blank page and start talking back. I’ve found that our best thoughts often hide in the margins. It’s time to pull them out. You can send your drafts to us at fp@the-ken.com mailto:fp@the-ken.com.

3. Wild things

Hello and welcome to another issue of First Principles Silent Sunday, Ishan. It's Jonathan here. 

Few things in life have left as deep an impression on me as my admittedly limited run-ins with wildlife. To be honest, I don’t know why that is. Perhaps it’s just the allure of the exotic, the rare. Or maybe it’s more profound than that—a subconscious throwback to the freedoms and dangers human beings actually evolved to live with, and the connections and feeling of belonging that come with it. Maybe it’s a bit of both. 

But I remember that herd of elephants breaking trail in the Nilgiris, seen ten years ago and from nearly half a kilometre away, far more vividly and emotionally than most recent things. Ditto for that Malabar Giant Squirrel in Munnar. And that Gaur hiding just behind a bend on that Coonoor trail I had to hastily back away from. High-definition memories, all of them. 

Pictures may not have as weighty an impact, but they do still bring some of the same wonder to otherwise mundane life. Which is why I’m very glad to present two lovely examples of wildlife photography from our community today. They are my top picks for this edition.

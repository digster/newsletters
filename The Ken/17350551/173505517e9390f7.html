"Of all the categories in all the retail sectors in all the world, he had to walk into mine.” 
 
Now, neither Radhakishan Damani, the reclusive billionaire who owns India’s second-largest grocery chain, nor Mukesh Ambani, the world’s sixth-richest person whose company owns the largest grocery chain, said these words. But they just as well might have, because grocery is not where beautiful friendships are made. 
 
Instead, it's where fortunes are ruined.
 
Consider Kishore Biyani, till last year the billionaire founder of India's third-largest grocery retail company. Today, he’s no longer a billionaire and is rumoured to be on the verge of selling his highly indebted group to Reliance, which would give it a market share of over 50% in India.
 
Then, there were billionaire family groups like the Godrej Group and the Aditya Birla Group, both of which have had disastrous luck with grocery retail and ended up selling out.
 
Because grocery economics are brutal. Razor-thin profit margins, which can evaporate in an instant if you’re not paying attention, are married to expensive real-estate, cutting-edge supply chains and fickle, bargain-hungry customers.
 
It's so hard to make money on fresh produce—fruits and vegetables—that retailers often consider them “loss leaders” designed to just bring customers into stores, in the hope that they may buy other products. A single box of, say, a Kellogg’s breakfast cereal, occupies as much volume during transportation as four t-shirts, yet costs just a fraction. 
 
Which is why, after all these years, organised grocers have managed to just capture 4% of India’s market share.
 
The two billionaires who pilot DMart and Reliance Retail clearly see that glass 96% empty, waiting to be filled with their market shares. But both of them have diametrically opposite ways to get there. One keeps its costs ultra-low, supply chain super lean, and store network concentrated in a small number of states. The other is everywhere.

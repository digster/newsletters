If you want a simple illustration of Goodhart’s Law—“when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure”—at scale, look no further than the Indian government’s desire to midwife tech ventures.

Since 2016, over 3,500 startups have passed through around 70 Atal Incubation Centres. The government is so impressed that it has more than quintupled the initial budget of Rs 500 crore to keep the “innovation mission” going.

States are enamoured too. Karnataka has a Rs 1,000 crore fund for deeptech and AI startups.

But, as an official with the Atul Innovation Mission told my colleague Mrunmayee, quality doesn’t matter when nobody’s measuring success. Only 5% of the 3,500 startups have raised external capital, while a third of the companies that emerge from Y Combinator or Peak XV’s Surge manage to do so.

For bureaucrats obsessed with targets they can throw money at, this is a problem for another day. Mrunmayee pulls no punches in her story.

On another note, about 10 days ago, RRP Semiconductor asked the BSE to correct its stock price from Rs 11,666 to just Rs 20 per share.

Now, with only a 1% downmove allowed by the BSE each week, the stock would take more than 12 years to fall to that Rs 20 apiece. Should the exchange and the regulator make exceptions for such egregious cases? A dozen years is a long time to wait for a correction.

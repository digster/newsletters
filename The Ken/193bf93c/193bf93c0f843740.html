The power of persuasion. AI fed with population-level data is creating personalised narratives that are extremely persuasive, but it comes at the risk of losing audience trust

Before we begin, I just want to share that this is a rather proud moment for me. After three years and dozens of narrative thinking workshops under our belt, The Ken Learning is expanding our team. If you love storytelling and believe, like me, that narratives are the language of tomorrow, please consider applying for the role of Marketing Manager. 

And since we are on the subject of milestones, December saw one such breakthrough in the realm of AI storytelling—OpenAI’s demo of its new video generation tool, Sora. 

The tech works the same way as generative AI solutions for text and images. You feed in a prompt and it spits out a video clip. And according to The Guardian, the video generated during the demo was “uncannily realistic”. 

The piece also drove in how widespread adoption is already, and the risks associated with this development.

Video is AI’s new frontier, with OpenAI finally rolling out Sora in the US after first teasing it in February, and Meta announcing its own text-to-video tool, Movie Gen, in October. Google made its Veo video generator available to some customers this month. Are we ready for a world in which it is impossible to discern which of the moving images we see are real? In the past couple of years, we’ve witnessed the proliferation of generative AI text and image generators, but video feels even more high-stakes.

Historically, moving pictures have been more difficult to falsify than still ones, but generative AI is about to change all that. There are many potential abuses of such technology. Scammers are already using AI to impersonate people’s friends or family members’ voices, in order to trick them out of money. Disinformation pedlars use deepfakes to support their political agendas. Extortionists and abusers make fake sexual images or videos of their victims. We are living in a world where some security researchers now suggest that families adopt a secret codeword, so they can prove they really are who they say they are if they have to call for help.

Well, if AI-generated content is getting increasingly more believable, that also means it’s becoming increasingly more persuasive. And I don’t think I need to spell out both the dangers and the potential associated with granting the power of persuasion to machines. 

For instance, Paul Fisher, programme director of the Oxford Senior Executive Leadership Programme, delivered a webinar recently on Persuasion and Influence, and he laid out three important ways in which persuasion can be used to great effect:

1. Driving change and inspiring action

2. Navigating complex relationships with confidence and ease

3. Influencing decisions at the highest levels of leadership

The reasons why handing such capability to AI is risky are the same reasons why leaders need to invest in building this skill. Persuasion can be incredibly effective in driving favourable outcomes, but also in camouflaging the truth and misleading audiences if executed with malintent.

What if the truth was delivered gently?

The popular collocation “brutally honest” represents a commonly held belief that if the truth is told in the plainest terms, it may actually end up hurting someone’s feelings or offending them. 

However, the truth is often not simple. Take Greek philosopher Aristotle’s famous definition of truth: “To say of what is that it is, and of what is not that it is not, is true.”  

I’ll leave you to parse that at your leisure, but Aristotle also believed that people could be persuaded by the truth if it appealed to their sense of logic, emotions, and established a sufficient level of credibility. To persuade an audience to come around to your way of thinking, he believed, a speaker (or storyteller) must appeal to all parts of this triad: logos (logic), pathos (emotion), and ethos (authority and trustworthiness). 

Centuries later, Robert Cialdini’s seminal book—Influence: The Psychology of Persuasion—showed that business professionals, too, use the same principles to build trust with their customers.

As part of his research, Cialdini had decided to spend time studying people he defined as “compliance professionals” – those in sales, marketing, recruitment and fundraising whose livelihoods depend on changing others’ opinions.

After three years of comparing these experiences with the published research, he identified six overarching principles that appeared to underline any persuasive campaign. They were:

Scarcity

Authority

Social proof

Liking

Reciprocation

Commitment and consistency

Fisher, in the webinar I mentioned earlier, argued that all these principles are crucial for leaders if they wanted to bring about any change in their organisations. Because it’s the best way to counter the most common obstacles to change.

And making your message simple and clear, articulating a strong vision, and connecting context with personal and collective stories are the best ways to enhance how persuasive your argument is, Fisher added.

Aristotle, Fisher, Ganz, and Ciadani are all essentially saying the same thing: leaders need to be persuasive if they want to connect with audiences. And they need more than just information or facts to be persuasive, because people usually tend to act due to their emotions, or their subconscious mind, or sometimes even their neurological makeup.

Harvard Business Review defines neuromarketing as “the measurement of physiological and neural signals to gain insight into customers’ motivations, preferences, and decisions, which can help inform creative advertising, product development, pricing, and other marketing areas.” This approach, which blends neuroscience and marketing, is not without controversy, but the practice has become increasingly popular and utilized by major brands like Frito-Lay and IKEA. Insights gleaned from neuromarketing can be used to predict customers’ motivations and preferences when making purchases, as well as assist with brand loyalty and positive brand recall.

And by testing its AI product’s powers of persuasion, OpenAI is assessing its ability to sway people’s behaviour in a whole new way. 

It is already established that the use of AI trained on population-level personalised data can be a powerful tool to change behaviour. In fact, earlier this year, a study published in Scientific Reports concluded that personalised messages crafted by ChatGPT were significantly more influential than non-personalised messages. 

However, remember how I mentioned that there are risks when persuasion is pushed beyond a certain point? It can also end up eroding trust, as another study published this year shows:

For businesses, the study suggests a balanced approach: combining transparency with persuasive incentives can be a highly effective strategy. However, firms must consider potential reputational risks, as pushing persuasion too far could erode trust or attract scrutiny by regulators. For consumers, the takeaway is simple: stay informed and be cautious when sharing personal data. The lure of incentives can make people overlook the long-term risks.

While the regulation promotes transparency, the widespread use of persuasive strategies suggests that firms can still operate in a gray area, complying with the letter of the law while exploiting its flexibility.

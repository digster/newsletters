The mass migration that the pandemic triggered a few months ago has shattered some long-held Great Expectations. Not just for the millions of workers who fled the metros, but for many who believe that western-style urbanisation holds the (permanent) key to economic prosperity in India.
The virus has bared the Dickensian discontents of urbanisation.
Metros and megacities across the world are reporting an urban flight. Maybe that’s temporary. But for working professional India, incidentally, the virus has accelerated a unique trend that has been underway for some time. And now, as India slowly unlocks, data suggests younger metros and smaller cities, such as Jaipur, Lucknow, Bengaluru, and Hyderabad are more resilient, having suffered a smaller contraction in jobs.
There are no ready-to-use tools to gauge how Indian cities are faring post-lockdowns.
So for her analysis today, Ritika Mankar, an economist by profession, created her own tool—a City Keqiang Index (CKI), as it were.
What inspired the CKI? According to Wikileaks, the Chinese politician Li Keqiang did not trust official growth numbers for a province he used to manage. And so, he decided to use some clever proxies of growth instead. He used rail cargo volume, electricity consumption, and bank credit to track growth in his province.
Ritika’s CKI uses air passenger traffic, consumer price inflation for industrial workers, and mobility data. Between the sagging ‘senior’ metros and the recharging ‘junior’ metros, the index tells an urban landscape remapping story that few can ignore. After all, how we navigate our urban spaces will determine how we get back on a sustained economic recovery path.

This 2020 reform has a 1991 ring to it.
If three decades ago India made bold economic reforms to tide over its balance of payments crisis, the agricultural reforms of early June look no less consequential for the country's 146 million cultivators.
One country, one market, one national regulation that the farmers—caught between restrictive laws and monopolistic traders—have dreamt of, finally looks achievable. They will now be able to sell their produce outside the regulated markets, or mandis, designated for their villages. To any buyer with a permanent account number (PAN) anywhere in the country.
Many of the 36 states in the past have tweaked their APMC Act to reduce friction between farmers and buyers, yet both parties have been hobbled. Even when a state, like Bihar, abolished the APMCs, it didn’t bring about the desired results. Prices became volatile, private investment remained elusive. A 2016 attempt to create a national agriculture online platform of APMC markets remained half-used.
With the new reform, private agri operators believe, the stunted sector provides scope for a rapid scale-up. In the heydays of risk capital, say, between 2016-2019, only around $585 million came into agri-tech startups. Contrast that with Udaan, a B2B marketplace, which raised a similar amount in a single round late last year.
Agri aggregators are flexing their muscles now. DeHaat, which handles 400 tonnes of produce from four states in a day , says it can replicate its model in three more states far more quickly.
For the government, replacing markets with organised aggregators can’t be an end in itself. If farmers are left to fend for themselves, if traders are replaced by unfettered aggregators, or if the latter are served a poorly stitched policy for procurement, private companies can only plan but never see breakout moments. At a time when agri-tech the world over is seen as ripe for a ‘Tesla-moment’.

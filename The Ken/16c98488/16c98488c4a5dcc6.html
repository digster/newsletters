"Multiplicity of agencies involved in beekeeping development in India has led to lack of convergence and duplicity of efforts. Hence the EAC-PM of GoI decided to streamline the beekeeping activities in the nation."
 
That's the government... criticising itself. There isn't much more to say, is there? I mean, the government says there are too many government agencies. When was the last time you heard that?
 
This critique, in a report sent by the Beekeeping Development Committee to the PM in June, shows that India is only just waking up to the potential of honey and the importance of bees.
 
Honey production has rocketed 200% in the past 12 years, utilising less than 10% of the country's potential. Yet, beekeeping isn't recognised as an agricultural input, nor is a beekeeper considered a farmer.
 
And that's the least of the problems. About 60% of the industry is unorganised. The states with the maximum government incentives are not necessarily the best suited to beekeeping, ecologically speaking. Honey is among the most adulterated foods in the world, but India has a grand total of zero state-authorised testing labs.
 
Then there are the bees. Extremely sensitive to ecological changes and very vulnerable to pesticides—both of which are only on the rise. Plus the fear of bee stings has dissuaded many a farmer, despite the promise of a profitable business.
 
There is another major reason bees, not so much honey, is critical. Pollination. They can boost crop yield by 20-80% in most cultivated crops, the BDC says. Fewer bees = lower crop productivity.
 
No wonder then it is issuing a clarion call for a revolution, a "Sweet/Golden Revolution." And it has made a range of recommendations.
 
But will it remain a plan on paper? Is it too little, too late? Will the farmers bite? That's our story today, from our contributor Vasudevan.

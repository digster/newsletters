Good Morning Ishan,
 
There has been considerable anger against the ride-hailing duopoly of Ola and Uber, especially in cities such as Bengaluru. Drivers accuse them of imposing huge commissions, and commuters say they charge exorbitant fares. Matters had come to such a pass that the Karnataka government ordered a 5% cap on commissions, a decision that was stayed by the Karnataka high court earlier this year. 
 
Amid the tussle, a new auto-rickshaw-hailing app called Namma Yatri appeared in Bengaluru. Developed by a Softbank-backed fintech, it was to be a panacea for many ills. It charged nothing from drivers as commissions, abstained from surge pricing, and above all, promised a platform that would be owned by drivers themselves. Recently, it got onboarded on the central government-promoted Open Network for Digital Commerce (ONDC).
 
But several months after its launch in Bengaluru, the Namma Yatri story is rather bittersweet. While it has made good progress in getting drivers to sign up and generating more rides, it has still not managed to pose a real threat to Ola and Uber. 
 
Why has Namma Yatri been unable to capitalise on the dissent against existing players? Is its zero-commission policy hogwash? Does it really offer more affordable rides than Ola and Uber? Is it a technologically better platform? Will ONDC’s backing improve its fortunes? 
 
In today’s story, Shaswata hits the ground looking for answers. He finds that, despite certain drawbacks, Namma Yatri could be on the cusp of making a splash soon.

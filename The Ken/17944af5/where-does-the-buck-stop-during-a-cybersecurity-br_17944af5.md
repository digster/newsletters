If there’s any silver lining to the pandemic surge of the last few weeks, it’s the apparent pause in the cybersecurity breaches in India. In the last few months, news of breaches has hit us like weekly movie releases, each with its own cast of characters and plot.
Be it e-commerce platforms, payment gateways, power grids, the Kudankulam nuclear power plant, or even the space agency ISRO, India's response to a cyber breach is predictable. Denial; reluctant admission; and then some delayed but inadequate action.
In the case of Mumbai’s power outage in September 2020, the Centre’s admission came only when The New York Times broke the news in late February. It took Indian intelligence agencies 17 days to block the IPs belonging to a suspected command and control centre of a Chinese hacker group that US-based private cyber intelligence firm Recorded Future had handed over to them.
Even then, the Centre’s only admission was that there was malware found in the systems of targeted utilities. It denied that the malware had any connection to both China and the power outage in Mumbai.
Well, if the teaser so far is perplexing, you must read the full story that Pratap has put together from sources in the sundry agencies purportedly guarding India’s cybersecurity.
Turns out, India simply doesn’t have the necessary infrastructure to monitor threats and respond to incidents at the state and sectoral levels. Alerts about attacks and perpetrators often come from external agencies and researchers outside India—from South Korea, US, Russia, and every other corner on the planet.
In fact, the Mumbai outage shook the power ministry so much that it gave just 45 days to the Central Electricity Authority—until May 10—for a comprehensive set of regulations for the sector. But with the chain of command itself broken in this space, regulations can do zilch.
India’s national cybersecurity coordinator Lt Gen Rajesh Pant has a plan and he’s seeking around $2.7 billion (Rs 20,000 crore) to streamline the scattered bureaucracy under a step-up organisation. Is that enough?

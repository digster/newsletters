Mankind Pharma is finally adulting. After three decades of focus on the domestic market, the company is now working on its relationship with regulated markets
By Seema Singh
5 min read

It’s been more than a year since Mankind Pharma listed on the stock market, but analysts tracking it can’t stop gushing about the company. They pepper reports with phrases like “one of a kind” (rhyming with Mankind), “disruptor with a dose of care” (whatever that means), and so on.

Being the newest India-focused pharma company on the stock market, Mankind, perhaps, reminds investors of Alkem Laboratories—a similarly domestic market-reliant company which has rewarded investors since it listed nine years ago.

But there’s a noticeable difference.

By the time it went public, Alkem had boosted its international business so much that it contributed nearly 25% to its total revenue then. Mankind’s exports, meanwhile, made up a modest 8% of its top line in FY24, up from 3% in the previous year.

In the latest September quarter, the company beat estimates and posted a 30% jump in profits on the back of growth in exports, among other things. Its quarterly revenue grew to nearly Rs 3,100 crore (US$367 million), up over 13% from a year ago.

If you read the fine print, the export spurt came from a “one-off opportunity in the US market” apart from “selective launches in the base business”.

Mankind said it expects the export contribution to remain “around 8% going forward”. The company is banking on its latest acquisition of Bharat Serums and Vaccines (BSV), one of the largest such deals in India, to keep its exports flowing. Mankind paid Rs 13,630 crore to acquire 100% stake in the biopharmaceutical firm from its private-equity owner Advent International.

Bharat Serums has a well-oiled exports business—nearly 50% of its Rs 1,723 crore revenue in FY24 came from that segment.

Selling biology-led products like vaccines and serums requires a lot more quality control and manufacturing oversight than chemistry-based products that constitute regular pharmaceuticals. (That’s why even the largest Indian pharma companies have struggled for years to build their bio-pharmaceutical portfolios.)

So, integrating BSV into itself, managing its exports, and then building export muscle for its own products will be one hell of a learning curve for the 30-year-old Mankind. It’s also a step change for a company that has built its business serving tier-2 and -3 towns and then worked its way up to big cities.

Now, from big cities to international markets is a whole other game—one ridden with high risks and high rewards. Still, that’s a rite of passage for any pharma company with ambition. Most of India’s top pharmaceutical companies by sales earn more than half of their revenue from exports.

As the chart above shows, the India business for most large pharma companies grew in single digits in FY24, unlike their international business. The global pharma market may itself not grow in double digits but for its size, US$1.6 trillion in 2023, a projected steady 5–8% growth over the next four years is as big an opportunity as it can ever be.

For now, Mankind is happy talking about its place in the Indian market. In FY24, it clocked Rs 10,335 crore in revenue, growing 18% from the previous year.

“Despite being one of the youngest companies in the country, we proudly stand as the fourth largest company by value in [the] Indian Pharmaceutical Market (IPM). Our success comes from spotting, seizing unique opportunities, and not just competing with peers. With over 90% of our revenue from [the] domestic market, our business is outpacing IPM growth, supported by one of the largest field forces in India.”

August 2023 AGM, Mankind

The India opportunity is not lost on peers. After focusing on the global market for a while, some of the largest Indian pharma companies are turning their attention back to India. Sun Pharma, for instance, said in its latest annual report that it has increased its India field force by 10% and “expanded its business in tier-2 and tier-3 towns.”

Mankind will hope to build out its export business even as it continues to look for new growth avenues in India, which, from time to time, reveals its limitations. For instance, in October, the market expanded in value due to price increases, but the volume shrunk in almost all therapeutic areas. November is likely to remain the same.

As quality lapses remain a feature of the Indian market, consumers should be happy about Mankind’s, or any pharma company’s, strategy to grow its exports. Because the high-quality bar that’s set for them by the regulated markets lifts the manufacturing standards back home.

Let me add a caveat. Veteran Indian exporters run different manufacturing setups for regulated and domestic markets as the latter don’t have as stringent rules as the former.

But I believe good companies will eventually realise that running different manufacturing standards for different markets—it mostly boils down to the level of impurities in the products—is not just a management headache but can prove counterproductive.

Clayton M Christensen, the Harvard Business School professor, once said, “It’s easier to hold your principles 100% of the time than it is to hold them 98% of the time.”

Serious manufacturing companies would see the business logic in this principle. Mankind is no different.

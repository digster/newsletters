Private equity has earned itself various names, depending on what lens you use: ‘shops’, ‘vultures’, ‘masters of the universe’, ‘leveraged buyout’… but at the end of the day, it’s just capitalism at work.
 
That’s one way to look at what is happening at the multi-asset manager IDFC Alternatives.
 
India’s oldest home-grown infrastructure private equity fund has been sold off to a foreign fund with huge knock-on effects. Soon, all of IDFC Alternatives will have to be disposed of. Rather, is being disposed of. Apart from the sale of its funds, IDFC Alternatives has recently concluded a secondary deal with one of its existing UK investor. But, as the story shows, this discounted deal-making will only go so far.
 
To be fair, infrastructure PEs have had a rough time in India. Most foreign funds have given up on India’s property sector. Some shut shop, some sold out, and some went into hibernation.
 
The stars faded on Alternatives and the first signs of turbulence appeared last year. It had $1.8 billion in deployment then. The sale processes, we have learned, were initiated when IDFC decided to refocus its energies on banking. But the reason isn’t IDFC Bank alone. Then what is? Rapidly pivoting investment strategy, buying from anything to everything? Or a lack of liquidity or exits? Did they get squeezed at the bottom by new funds?
 
Pooja Sarkar has a deeply reported story on why and how the 16-year-old PE is running out of Alternatives. (Sorry, the pun is irresistible.)
 
The fund started with a bang but somewhere along the way it lost its way. After all, being a 16-year-old has never been easy, right?

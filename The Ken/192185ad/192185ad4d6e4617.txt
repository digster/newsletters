Good morning! What a wonderful few weeks of beautiful days we’ve had in Bengaluru, and I’m sure in other cities too. Did you notice how clear the skies were, how crisp the sunlight?

Because they were building up to today!

That’s right. Today marks the official end of summer and the beginning of autumn. It’s the Autumnal Equinox. It’s one of two days in the year when day and night are almost the same length. (I am speaking for most of us in the Northern Hemisphere. For those in the Southern Hemisphere, the seasons are reversed.)

All of us are aware of the change of seasons. We notice it around us. But it’s only been six years since I consciously started to feel the onset of a change in season based on the way sunlight changes.

It was a costly lesson. I can even point you to the exact time and day.

5.36 pm, 20 June, 2018.

That’s when I took this nondescript-looking photo.

What the photo doesn’t truly capture was how beautiful the evening was. The sunlight, the fresh leaves of the Pongamia trees outside our office, the clear blue skies.

After taking the photo, I got into my car and left for home.

After a few minutes, the car sounded an alarm, “Key not in range!”

I ignored it, as I was driving. I mean, it must have been a bug. How could the key not be in range if I was driving the car?

A few minutes later, it sounded again. And then again.

Finally, a few kilometers from the office, I pulled over to the side of the road and checked. The keys were truly nowhere to be found.

And then, in slow motion and in detective-movie-style flashback, I realized what had happened.

I was about to enter the car when I got distracted by the beautiful evening and stopped to take a photo of it. I left my car key on the roof, as I took the photo, only to forget about it. Then, I got into the car and pressed the start button.

The car normally refuses to start if it doesn’t detect the key as being “inside”. But my theory is that it got tricked, because the key was on the roof, but “inside” the boundaries of the doors.

So, the car started with the key on the roof. But as I drove, the key obviously slipped and fell off somewhere. And that’s when the car told me it couldn’t detect the key within range.

I tried retracing my path to find the dropped keys but without luck. I had to then order two new keys at considerable expense since, apparently, they came in pairs and needed to be coded to the vehicle by the manufacturer.

The reason the skies were so beautiful that evening of 20 June was because the next day was Summer Solstice.

Since that expensive lesson triggered by a beautiful sunset, I’ve been more conscious about solstices and equinoxes https://www.britannica.com/story/whats-the-difference-between-a-solstice-and-an-equinox.

1. “Attracting feedback”

Corporate life and career advice is littered with books, advice, and processes to help people get better at “giving feedback”. Sadly, it leads to a place of frustration, ennui, and inevitably anger for many of us. Because no one tells us the truth.

Nobody wants to give you feedback.

This is a starting assumption I trained myself to believe many years ago. And over time, it has become my default.

Think about the number of times you’ve asked your colleagues or friends for feedback on something that was important to you? What was your success rate?

Now think about the number of times a colleague or a friend has asked a group, including you, for feedback? How often did you respond? How eager were you?

Truth is, we’re all too busy, too disinterested, or too distracted to give feedback to others. And if we’re honest about it, why should we? What’s in it for us?

This frustrates me. Because this sends many young professionals down the path where they get disillusioned with the process of feedback. Over time, this reflects in the culture of teams and workplaces, where a “culture of feedback” must then be mandated or pushed from the top down. Where leaders and managers take it upon themselves to exhibit how best to give feedback. Where performative signaling, processes, and rules become more important. (Example, “praise in public, criticize in private”).

1. Never, ever, EVER ask for “feedback”

Feedback is a process. Stop using it as a word when you want people’s time and attention and expertise.

I’ll explain.

Do you go to colleagues and say, “Can you collaborate with me?” We (normally) don’t, because collaboration is a process. We engage in it.

Instead, make your request interesting enough for someone to want to respond.

I hate it when most people ask for “feedback”. Because they use the word as a catch-all.

“Here is my plan, can you give me some feedback?”

“This is my resume, can you give me some feedback?”

Feedback on specifically what? Requiring how much time? Also, why should I?

2. Remember, you are asking someone to invest their limited and valuable time and energy in you

The way we do “feedback” transfers the burden to the recipient of the ask. Is it any surprise we don’t respond? I for one ignore almost 90–95% of requests which end with, “…can you give me some feedback?”

The way I see it, if you cannot be bothered about making it easier and more interesting for me to respond, why would I? After all, it’s your need, right?

Imagine if you knew this was my starting point. That I was inclined to ignore your vague request for “feedback” because I simply didn’t have the time or interest.

What if you were asking someone to invest their money with you? How then would you shape your ask to get me interested? What would your story look like?

3. Soliciting feedback is like fishing

My mental model of feedback is akin to a fisherman casting a line. He casts it, and then waits for fish to be attracted. (Please don’t extend the analogy in the literal direction!)

If the fish don’t bite, the fisherman casts a better lure. Or moves to a better spot. Or casts the line again.

The point being, getting feedback isn’t guaranteed, but you must still cast your lines. Eventually, you’ll get better, and you will start “attracting feedback”. The fisherman who blames the fish isn’t going to be a fisherman for long.

4. Become better at casting lines, even if you don’t catch fish

If you’re fishing to “attract feedback”, then you better learn to love the act of casting lines. If not, you’re going to get disillusioned very quickly.

The best “feedback getters” I’ve seen are constantly casting lines. They’re doing it in conversations, on social media, in meetings and over emails. They’re telling stories about their work that make people curious and excited and ambitious. They’re aware of human emotions and desires. They’re constantly tweaking their lines based on how the fish bite.

This means they’re almost always in love with casting lines—telling stories about their projects and ambitions. Others responding to them and wanting to help them is just a bonus.

Such people have multiple lines cast at any time. They’re not dejected by their inability to attract feedback on a few lines.

This brings me to the biggest realisation I’ve made in my career about “feedback”. It was never about how to give feedback or receive feedback. We had it backwards.

Instead, the winners are those who are best at soliciting feedback. It is a very powerful skill that takes time to develop and shape. But it is an absolute superpower.

P.S: What if feedback is overrated? What if no one wants your feedback as well?

Lie #5: People need feedback

“You’ll be taught phrases such as, “Is now a good time for me to give you some feedback?” And, “Would you care for some feedback?” And the slightly more assertive, “I have some feedback for you. Are you sitting down?” Having learned how to give feedback, you’ll also learn how to receive it through techniques such as mirroring (“Did I hear you say that I need to work on my ‘organizational savvy and politics’?”) and active listening (“Can you clarify what you mean by ‘hopelessly naive’ and give me a couple of recent examples?”).

And of course, should you reject the feedback you receive from someone else because it feels odd, or confusing, or just plain wrong, you’ll be helped to understand that this feeling is just a natural reaction to threat, and that to grow as a person and as a leader you will need to “let go of your ego,” to “embrace your failures,” and to always maintain a “growth mindset.” If you can reframe all this feedback as valuable input to help you grow, then—you’ll be told—you’ll soon find yourself addicted to it. ”

[…]

“These biases lead us to believe that your performance (whether good or bad) is due to who you are—your drive, or style, or effort, say—which in turn leads us to the conclusion that if we want to get you to improve your performance we must give you feedback on who you are, so that you can increase your drive, refine your style, or redouble your efforts. To fix a performance problem we instinctively turn to giving you personal feedback, rather than looking at the external situation you were facing and addressing that.”

2. Lost in translation

Hello, dear readers, this is Hari with the First Principles Books section. I hope everyone’s having a great time winding down this Sunday. 

Last week, I showed you a part of my Antilibrary https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/leaning-into-procrastination/. This week, one book is coming off that embarrassingly large shelf of unread books.

I’ve picked up Geetanjali Shree’s Ret Samadhi https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/51012791-ret-samadhi?ref=nav_sb_ss_1_8 (it’s a Hindi book, and Tomb of Sand is the name of the English translation). I came across it on one of the online book clubs I religiously follow these days. And for me, the biggest draw was the language itself. In all these years, I have never gotten around to reading a novel either in my native Malayalam, or in the language of my childhood, Hindi.

The reason I’m picking it up now is because a few weeks ago, a friend and I were browsing through a bookstore in Koramangala, Bengaluru, and he showed me a collection of Vinod Kumar Shukla’s poems in Hindi called Treasurer of Piggy Banks https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/203695928-treasurer-of-piggy-banks?ac=1&from_search=true&qid=3CXdzh7EmW&rank=1. And this book came with an English translation of the poems by Arvind Krishna Merhotra. 

The poem my friend showed me was deceivingly simple. There was a plainness to it that just stuck, and on the next page was the English translation. It was translated well, but it didn’t carry the same joy I felt in the original.

Since then, I’ve had this thought stuck in my head: just how much meaning and essence of the original is lost in the translation? 

So I thought I’d run an experiment with Geetanjali Shree’s International Booker Prize winner and find out. After I’m done with the original, I’m heading off to get the English translation. I’ll be sure to get back to you all with what I think. 

In the meantime, if you have experienced reading a book both in its original language and a translation, do let me know what you thought of it. Was the essence of the original captured in the translation?

Chitrangda wrote: 

“Last year, at ISB, I not only found friendship but also a newfound love for nature. We made roughly 3 trips and thanks to my new phone, I clicked some great photos (or so I’d like to think).”

It immediately brought back flashes from my own college trips, which to this day remain some of my most vivid and fondest memories. I ended up scrolling through dozens and dozens of photographs from those trips—most terribly unoriginal, but still, with great memories attached to each. Pictures that held me the longest were those of friends… around a bonfire, having lunch on a houseboat, just sitting on a wall chatting. 

After a while though, I noticed something. 

My absence. 

Not just from the pictures (though that was mildly disappointing too), but from the moments I’d captured in those frames. 

And scrolling through all those pictures made me realise just how many of those moments I’d missed. All voluntarily. And now, I wonder how many times I would have made the same choice given the benefit of hindsight. Perhaps if I’d put away the camera more often, I’d have been part of someone else’s picture of that scene. 

Missing the moment you are choosing to record is an inescapable reality of photography. It is a conscious choice, even if, like in the case of my college-age self, it was not a mindful choice. I do not know how many, if any, of you can relate to this. Me? I am no longer constantly behind the camera. It comes out more rarely now, and only for things I find truly striking. Some of my coterie of DSLR-totting fanatics from college have given up photography altogether. Some have become well-known and highly capable professional photographers. 

Behind the lens, in front of it, waltzing in and out… where you choose to place yourself, and where you are most comfortable and content, is something each photographer needs to figure out on their own.

It’s true, a photographer walks and balances on a thin line between the world we inhabit and the world we photograph. We’re usually not the active participants doing the things that people in our photographs are doing; and we’re not the passive audience watching them. It’s a unique space between the two worlds that I know well and I’m comfortable occupying. To do my thing I don’t need to be in the actual moment but need to concentrate and be in the “photographic moment”. It’s often a lonely pursuit.

Missing The Moment And Capturing It At The Same Time, by Steve Simon in The Passionate Photographer https://thepassionatephotographer.com/opinion-missing-moment-capturing-time-no-regrets/

It’s a balance each of us needs to find. If you have a relatable insight or anecdote, do write fp@the-ken.com, I’d love to read your thoughts on the matter.

5. Interesting reads

Are we bearing witness to the end of the Internet as we knew it? To its degradation from a system of oceans and rivers to sludge and runoff?

Prospect: Is anyone out there? (James Ball) https://www.prospectmagazine.co.uk/ideas/technology/internet/67864/dead-internet-theory-ai

Facebook feeds that used to be full of real information and real stories about people from our real lives are now full of low-quality and freakish engagement bait. It is no surprise that many of us, as a result, are looking elsewhere. Google results keep getting worse, social media feeds are full of dreck, and it is impossible to know what to trust.

None of the internet giants seem to even see the problem, let alone a way to fix it. Instead of trying to rebuild internet services to their former glory, they are packing in more AI and automation, and, inevitably even more slop. But an internet built for the bots is doomed to fail: in the end the economy is made up of the collective efforts of humans, not anything else.

If the multi-billion-dollar companies running the internet don’t make it fit for humans, someone else will. However much it might feel that way, the internet is no emptier than the streets of London. We’re all still there, just out of sight.

Word frequency tool ‘wordfreq’ stops updates, overwhelmed by AI spam https://pivot-to-ai.com/2024/09/19/word-frequency-tool-wordfreq-stops-updates-overwhelmed-by-ai-spam/

Forbes Marketplace: The Parasite SEO Company Trying to Devour Its Host (Lars Lofgren) https://larslofgren.com/forbes-marketplace/

I know a lot of folks in the SEO industry. Not one person thinks this is normal or okay. I even heard from a source that I deeply trust that Google employees were complaining about Forbes internally. That was two years ago.

Since then? Not a goddamn thing has changed.

Instead Google continues to unleash their HCU algorithm to nuke niche and small publishers.

The worst part about this? I suspect that all this insanity isn’t even a deliberate decision by Google. I believe the search ranking has gotten away from them. That Google isn’t in control of their own rankings or algorithms anymore. Too many subpar leaders in positions with way too much power, way too much complexity in the SERPs, and AI as a massive distraction for the entire search team.

It’s a perfect recipe for shipping shit.

And now we all have to wade through that shit every day.

AI: Markets for Lemons, and the Great Logging Off (Lars Doucet) https://www.fortressofdoors.com/ai-markets-for-lemons-and-the-great-logging-off/

What happens when anyone can spin up a thousand social media accounts at the click of a button, where each account picks a consistent persona and sticks to it – happily posting away about one of their hobbies like knitting or trout fishing or whatever, while simultaneously building up a credible and inobtrusive post history in another plausible side hobby that all these accounts happen to share – geopolitics, let’s say – all until it’s time for the sock puppet master to light the bat signal and manufacture some consensus?

What happens when every online open lobby multiplayer game is choked with cheaters who all play at superhuman levels in increasingly undetectable ways?

What happens when, from the perspective of the average guy, “every girl” on every dating app is a fiction driven by an AI who strings him along (including sending original and persona-consistent pictures) until it’s time to scam money out of him?

What happens when, from the perspective of the average girl, “every guy” on the internet has become weirdly dismissive and hostile, because he’s been conditioned to think that any girl that seems interested in him must be fake and trying to scam money out of him?

What happens when comments sections on every forum gets filled with implausibly large consensus-building hordes who are able to adapt in real time and carefully slip their brigading just below the moderator’s rules?

I mean, to various degrees all this stuff is already happening. But what happens when it cranks up by an order of magnitude, seemingly overnight?

What happens when most “people” you interact with on the internet are fake?

I think people start logging off.

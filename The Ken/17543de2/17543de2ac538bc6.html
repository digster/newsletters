Normally, our morning emails are about explaining the importance of that day’s story, and quoting important bits from it. Today, for a change, allow me to do something different—explain and quote from a story we did 1.5 years ago.
 
Last April, we wrote a story on Airtel, once India’s undisputed #1 telco, which has since been pushed to #2 by Reliance Jio. We explained how Airtel had tried to turn Jio’s disruptive ambitions against it with some judo moves.
 
It began in October 2018 when Airtel did the unthinkable, booting out 49M subscribers from its network because they weren’t adding meaningful revenue as customers. That was 15% of its entire customer base.
 
A month later, it made its calling plans stricter, forcing every subscriber on its network to pay money to recharge at least once every 15 days. Those who didn’t would no longer receive even incoming calls. For good measure, it refined its definition of who an active customer was, only counting those from whom it earned money in the last 30 days. 
 
Rival Jio defined an active customer as one from whom it earned money in the last 90 days.
 
By doing all of this, Airtel hoped to trap Jio into chasing marginal low-value users who would clog its 4G network without the ability to contribute commensurate revenues.
 
This brings me to Pratap’s story today. In some ways, the sequel to our April 2019 Airtel story.
 
Jio has over 400M users today compared to Airtel’s 320M. But Jio's active users are only 313M, compared to Airtel’s 310M. And as Jio struggles to add its next 100M users in order to justify its future expectations and valuation, it faces stagnant ARPUs and deteriorating network quality. 
 
The tricks Jio used as a disruptor—dirt cheap prices, fast 4G, and massive data caps—cannot be the ones it uses as the leader and incumbent.

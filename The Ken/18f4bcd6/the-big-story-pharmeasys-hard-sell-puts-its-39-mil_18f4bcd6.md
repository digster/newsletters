Good Morning Ishan,
In its corporate filings for the financial year ended March 2023, Pharmaeasy said it had “3.9 million annual transacting users with fulfilled orders”. Today’s story is as much for its 3.9 million online-medicine-and-diagnostic-services buyers as it is for all those who rely on and believe in online pharmacies.
Once IPO-bound and valued at over US$5 billion, Pharmeasy, the self-proclaimed integrated healthtech company, is now a story of desperate and dubious business practices.
At the centre of it all lies the prescription-generation system. I always found this—running a call centre for doling out prescriptions—a head-scratching model. In her meticulously reported story, Shivani busts it.
Pharmeasy’s “Docstat—India’s largest remote-consultation platform” is anything but a consultation play.
It generates prescriptions with half-baked information; pushes down private labels or generic medicines to customers; the so-called doctors, who call in customers to generate their prescriptions, have targets to sell in-house herbals or supplements; non-over-the-counter drugs are sold and prescribed with utter disregard for adverse health consequences…
In 2021, The Ken had written how Pharmeasy had a precarious balancing act in front of it, thanks to its mindless acquisitions. Today, its business practices have become precarious for its customers. Check out Shivani’s [must-read story](https://the-ken.com/story/pharmeasys-hard-sell-puts-its-3-9-million-customers-health-at-risk/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter).

May is not exactly the midpoint of the year but a bunch of reports over the weekend pointed to the sharp drop in VC funding for AI startups in India. A near 70% drop from 2022, which is unlikely to improve in 2024.
We can mope about this but it’s not surprising. Even globally, the nearly 250% increase in AI funding in the same period owes to the fat cheques that Big Tech wrote to have a foot in the revolving GenAI door.
If you take that amount out of the equation, then funding for AI startups in 2023 was much smaller; just about the same as in 2021.
Technology-wise, AI is not like the mobile or social wave. Apart from a tight liquidity market, which constrains investors from backing consumer companies (who typically throw cash to acquire customers), running AI startups is expensive. Both the cost and the need to compute is very high.
So the old model of acquiring millions of customers first and figuring out monetisation later won’t work this time. That leaves the enterprise segment. But that has always been harder to crack in India and is more so now when enterprises themselves are in the wait-watch-learn mode.

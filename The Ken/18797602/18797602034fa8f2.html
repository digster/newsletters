Good Morning Ishan,
 
A few months ago, I asked my 12-year-old niece what career she fancied for herself. "I want to be an Influencer," she said, without missing a beat.
 
The rise of homegrown short-video apps such as Josh, Moj, and MX Takatak, in addition to giants like Instagram, normalised this career option for thousands of people. These platforms, in turn, rode the daily-active-user (DAU) wave thanks to the viral content made by these creators.
 
It was not just about racking up precious DAUs. Those DAU numbers also drew advertisers who saw these apps as an experimental way to reach tier-2-and-beyond audiences. That interest was enough for these platforms to make money.
 
But the downturn upended those carefully laid plans.
 
The plummeting advertiser interest created a vicious cycle. Little advertiser interest meant less money for creators, and badly paid creators meant fewer views for these platforms.
 
Soumyajit maps the stories of a few such creators. With their incomes now roughly halved, creators—and the short-video apps they are tied to—are forced to contend with a new reality.

Good Morning Ishan,

As I impatiently waited for my cab in Delhi earlier this week, I couldn’t help but notice at least 10 electric vehicles (EVs) pass by: two sleek cars, four nifty three-wheelers, and four trendy two-wheelers. As a resident of Kolkata for the past three years, this was a rare sight for me. Here’s the kicker—even in Delhi, just a couple of years ago, I could have been stuck in a time warp trying to spot a couple of EVs at the same place.

The first apparent thought was: what’s the sudden buzz? After all, the EV ecosystem was battling battery fires, charging infra, and whatnot. A hero and victim of its own fate.

As destiny would dictate, on returning from a holiday, I stumbled upon Anushka’s well-researched and number-driven story.

Here’s a glimpse of what she uncovered. In the decade since India set EV-adoption targets—the country wants to electrify 30% of its fleet by 2030—not all states have fully embraced the ride. And even with many subsidies and PLI schemes (read: FAME), the situation has barely improved.

That’s where states’ EV policies come into the picture. And differing priorities have yielded different results.

Delhi, for instance, saw a 142% rise in registered EVs since the implementation of its EV policy in 2020. Interestingly, being late to the EV party worked in its favour. Many bigger regions—Karnataka, Andhra Pradesh, and Tamil Nadu—started laying the ground much earlier: rolling out EV policies to attract investments, create jobs, and boost tax revenues.

So, what’s behind these glaring disparities? It all boils down to the focus and incentives embedded within these policies. Do they prioritise manufacturers, or do they emphasise end-users like you and me? And where does public transport come in all of this?

The answer, dear readers, is not as simple as a one-size-fits-all solution.

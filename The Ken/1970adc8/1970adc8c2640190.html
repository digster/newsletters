Even anodyne statistics can lead to fantastical conclusions in India. 

In FY25, the country gave over 29 million women a permanent account number (PAN), or a quarter more than men, financial daily Mint reported last week. The gap between the genders had been similar in the previous financial year. 

While the 10-digit alphanumeric code is closely associated with income tax, it is necessary for something as basic as opening most kinds of bank accounts. (India’s flagship financial-inclusion scheme is an exception.) What doesn’t hurt is that one can get the identifier in a few days. Having a PAN is not a weighty indicator of upward mobility. 

Even so, the uptick in PAN allotment is proof that “women are striding into economic activity”, according to the newspaper. It also correlated the data with women’s rising share in the workforce, which has given Prime Minister Narendra Modi bragging rights. 

His government has talked up the “rising tide of female participation” in India’s “vibrant” labour market. And just two days ago, he called for reforms aimed at bringing more women into the workforce. 

Between FY18 and FY24, the share of women aged 15 and above who were employed rose sharply—from 22% to over 40%, according to an official labour survey. What’s even more striking is the near doubling of this number for women in rural areas, to 47%. (Roughly 75% of the surveyed men in both urban and rural areas were employed.)

But looking closer muddies the waters.

In FY18, roughly every second man and woman in villages was self-employed. Six years later, men were not much keener on striking out on their own. But significantly more women—three in four—were self-employed, which includes unpaid work on the family farm. The latest annual economic survey, published in January, called this a “rise in family-oriented economic activities”. 

Hardly very lucrative ones, though, because both self-employed men and women reported a fall in their wages, after adjusting for inflation, not to mention a steeper erosion in earnings for women than men.

In an earlier edition of this newsletter, I made a case for why agriculture, in which India has made big strides, could be the economy’s biggest trade weapon. But I also alluded to farming’s falling share in India’s economic output over the past decade, which was also true between FY18 and FY24. The gross domestic product per worker in manufacturing is 3.2X that of agriculture, and productivity in services is 5.5X higher, according to the Foundation for Economic Development, a think-tank. 

So, having more of India’s workers in its fields is grossly inefficient. And this is not the first time that data the government celebrates has been a head-scratcher for economists. 

Whoever said numbers don’t lie certainly didn’t get a chance to look at India’s official statistics. In the four years to FY22, India added 80 million jobs, the ministry of labour said last year. A figure few were quick to embrace, as Reuters reported.

“What is clear is that there is a large increase coming from agriculture and from self-employment, which includes own account work or unpaid family work,” said Amit Basole, head of the Centre for Sustainable Employment at the Azim Premji University.

The jump in employment cannot be equated to the creation of formal jobs with regular wages, Basole said.

The problem isn’t limited to who is producing our goods and services. There isn’t much agreement on how much is being produced, either. 

In 2019, a year after he stepped down as the country’s chief economic advisor, Arvind Subramanian argued in a study published by Harvard University that India’s economic-output growth in the preceding years had been overstated thanks to a change in how it was calculated. 

Raghuram Rajan, a former central-bank governor, echoed Subramanian’s scepticism last year, saying the Indian economy’s impressive expansion had a “little bit of fluff”.

As in the past, economists will find a way to work with suspect data. But the challenge of finding jobs for millions of Indians seems increasingly intractable. As I pointed out in the inaugural edition of the newsletter, India’s high-octane manufacturing push has not resulted in as many jobs as the government would like. Between FY14 and FY23, the last year for which sector-wise numbers are available, manufacturing’s share in employment has slid, with its share at a little over one in ten jobs.

There’s much to dislike about gig work offered by internet companies, but it pays well, all things considered. Zomato’s food-delivery workers, for instance, earn more than India’s per-capita income. But delivering food or groceries or riding a cab is still the preserve of men. Urban Company, a home-services marketplace, is the one large exception, with roughly 40% of its gig workers being women. 

What’s deeply worrisome is that women have far fewer jobs to choose from than men, especially when they are freer than ever from the demands of motherhood. In 1995, an Indian woman would have 3.6 kids on average in her lifetime. Now, she is likely to have two, and by 2050, only 1.3.

If the government had its way, she would thrive in “independent work and entrepreneurship”.

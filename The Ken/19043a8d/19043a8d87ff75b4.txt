In the event there are still a handful of you who aren’t fans of Calvin and Hobbes, and don’t know what “Calvinball” is (I’m judging you, of course), here’s an explanation:

Calvinball is a game invented by Calvin and Hobbes. There is only one rule: it can’t be played the same way twice.

Rules cannot be used twice (except for the rule that rules cannot be used twice), and any plays made in one game may not be made again in any future games. The game may involve wickets, mallets, volleyballs, and additional sports-related equipment, However a Mask is required.

There is only one permanent rule in Calvinball: players cannot play it the same way twice.

The Calvin and Hobbes Wiki https://calvinandhobbes.fandom.com/wiki/Calvinball

I never thought we’d be using Calvin and Hobbes to explain our professional lives, but here we are.

The “player” analogy is clear to most of us. We’re players in various games. At work, at home, in life. We want to become better players. We want to be part of teams that win games. We want to win games.

But something that few of us spend time thinking about is, what is our “game”? Did we choose it? Did we just get put on one? Who made the rules we’re following? Are we destined to keep playing it? Even if we don’t enjoy it?

Welcome to this Sunday’s edition of the First Principles newsletter, an unplanned and serendipitous group walk through a meadow of ideas and thoughts and pictures for the mind. You never know who you might meet or what you might learn.

1. Killing your darlings and playing Calvinball

“Entrepreneurship is as much about knowing what not to do. Kudos on the courage to make the right call. Cue the First Law of Thermodynamics. :)”

That was Srikanth Rajagopalan, CEO of Perfios Account Aggregation, subscriber, fellow alumnus, and longtime friend. He was responding on Linkedin to my sudden decision to kill the First Principles podcast https://the-ken.com/podcasts/first-principles/last-principles/, in many ways the “parent” of this newsletter.

As a more experienced and accomplished professional, Srikanth was able to innately understand two things and express it succinctly.

(a) Knowing what not to do is as important as knowing what to do.

(b) Every time you decide to “not do” something, you free up energy to do something.

Killing the First Principles podcast wasn’t an easy decision for me. It afforded me the privilege to meet some of India’s best founders and CEOs and learn from them by asking them questions. The recording of those conversations that we aired as a podcast was only a byproduct for me. I am serious.

But after nearly two years and 41 founders and CEOs later, it was becoming harder for me and my colleagues to find, convince, and schedule conversations with founders who might enjoy them as much as I or our listeners. I didn’t want the podcast to gradually plateau and stagnate into a safe PR opportunity. I didn’t want us to dumb down conversations and aim for viral success formulas.

I wouldn’t enjoy that, and I was sure, neither would most listeners.

But, at some point, that game stopped being fun. As a business journalist, I felt that the times we’re living in call for more current, more investigative, and more connecting-the-dots kind of storytelling. Even in audio form.

This, in spite of the podcast doing quite well on the charts (we ranked between #3 and #5 on the Business charts most weeks on Apple Podcasts), in spite of us working on an upcoming book based on the conversations, and in spite of warm feedback from listeners each week.

“Kill your darlings” https://nybookeditors.com/2015/12/kill-your-darlings-how-to-approach-your-writing-without-sentimentality/ is an advice given to most writers at some stage of their careers. Sometimes you have to “kill” what you thought were the best parts in your story—characters, scenes, plot points—in order to ensure that what remains is better.

But, who throws away a successful podcast which took years to build? One that was still loved?

Cue Srikant’s observation about the First Law of Thermodynamics, loosely explained as “the total amount of energy is the universe is constant; it cannot be created or destroyed; but it can be transformed from one form to another”.

In our professional lives it simply means that stopping something we spend time on “releases” energy that we can then put to use elsewhere.

I decided to develop a new podcast from scratch. Something that doesn’t exist today. Something that hews more closely to my interests as both a journalist and entrepreneur. Something that The Ken would be better placed to offer.

As much as I love longform interviews with founders, I feel that there are more pressing podcast formats that are needed right now. Venture capital investments have reduced to a trickle. Since the overall pie has stopped growing, companies are fighting each other for every incremental Rupee from a customer’s wallet, a Zero-Sum Game. Few founders are doing genuinely disruptive things, instead focusing on survival and profitability. The tentacles of regulations and regulators are everywhere. AI hype and FOMO is hard to ignore. Companies are hiring fewer people at the entry level and retaining fewer at the experienced level. Workplaces and careers are being upended because when even uber-profitable giants like Google, Amazon and Meta do wave after wave of layoffs, everyone else assumes we must be in the new normal.

We want answers, but no one wants to stick their neck out and speak.

That’s what excites me and the rest of my colleagues at The Ken. It’s why we’re journalists.

Last Principles https://the-ken.com/podcasts/first-principles/last-principles/

To use the game analogy, we were doing quite well. If we kept at it and improved month after month, we could climb the charts.

But, as a player, I was no longer interested in that game. There are already a lot of interview podcasts out there. We’ve done our bit by adding a solid body of work to it.

“I’m a big, big, big believer in ‘where to play’. ‘Where to play’ is 80% of the answer, compared to ‘how to play’.

You choose the battle where you have the greatest chance of winning. And here, it was very simple for me. I always felt e-commerce was here to stay because physical retail in India is under-penetrated. But what was interesting for me was why fashion was the right play.”

Ananth Narayanan, founder, Mensa Brands, in Episode 5 of the First Principles podcast https://the-ken.com/podcasts/first-principles/mensa-ananth-narayanan/

Our new upcoming podcast will seek to play and win in a podcast space I believe does not exist. It is risky and we might fail. As a player I might lose.

But that’s okay because we’d be playing the game we love. And if we lose, we’ll just move to a new game with rules that work better for our strengths, my strengths.

At The Ken we’re grateful that our subscribers and business model allow us the leeway to walk away from games we don’t enjoy (and by extension, won’t be able to make readers or listeners enjoy) and find new ones that we do. Ones where competing with us is harder because the rules play to our strengths and because we’re having fun playing.

Isn’t that what Calvinball is about?

P.S: A good friend, longtime subscriber, and investor in The Ken, Siddharth Bhammer, forwarded me this post from Morgan Housel about humble exits earlier this week in response to the news of the First Principles podcast being shut down. I am obviously not saying there’s anything remotely comparable, but I do love the insight about quitting when you want to, and not because you need to.

Thanks Sid!

Quitting – or adjusting, or changing paths – on top is so hard, because no one knows where the top is. But if there’s any reliable way to sense when it’s approaching, it’s when your success pushes you away from doing things that had been fundamental to that success. This is so obvious. But it’s elusive because success has a tendency of blinding you to both what caused it and how those causes have changed over time.

One of the neat things about Seinfeld is that it was one of the most successful shows of all time, run by people who understood how fragile success can be. Every season, from the first to the last, brought a conversation of, “Are we sure we can do this again? Do we still have what it takes? Is the recipe for success still valid?” One day they woke up and said, “No.” That humility solidified their legacy.

Humble Exits https://collabfund.com/blog/humble-exits/, by Morgan Housel

P.P.S: Coming full circle, Srikanth Rajagoplan had also triggered an earlier edition of the First Principles newsletter. From September 2023, when we were approaching the Equinox instead of the Solstice; the edition was called “Subtract. Delete. Forget.” https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/subtract-delete-forget/:

Subtraction is not just about removing day-to-day distractions. It’s also about taking decisions to kill off projects and products that are going nowhere, and to focus efforts on the most important bits of the business. Peter Drucker, the doyen of management theorists, was an advocate of “planned abandonment”, so that resources that are tied up in marginal activities are freed for more profitable use. Executives should, he advised, routinely ask the same question of every aspect of the business: “If we did not do this already, would we go into it now knowing what we now know?” [The Economist https://www.economist.com/business/2023/08/31/the-best-bosses-know-how-to-subtract-work]

[…]

I’ve been guilty of pushing myself and my colleagues to do more. It’s only in the last few years that I’ve developed the ability to not just accept subtraction, but to also suggest it proactively. I’ve been practicing telling my colleagues that when I (inevitably) go to them with a new idea for a product, story, or process, they should ask me, “So, what do you want me to drop from what I’m currently doing?”

First Principles is Dead. Long live First Principles

2. An FP Books request

Hi reader, and happy Sunday. This is Anushka. I’m usually here with the weekly books section for the First Principles newsletter, but we’re giving it a skip today. Aside from the weekly callout for books that you think the First Principles community would enjoy (please submit them here https://theken.typeform.com/FPBooks!) I have one more request for you this week. 

We get a lot of great recommendations, and we usually run them with an image of the book’s cover, but frankly, given how many books have multiple covers, I often make an arbitrary choice on which one to run. I usually try to pick the cover that I think resonates with the theme of the book the most—and also the one which promises to be the most aesthetically pleasing. And yet… there’s something about flat, two-dimensional cover images that isn’t always the most appealing, right? 

So, here’s what I want to ask you: when you’re submitting your book recommendations, could you also please send in a photo of your physical copy of the book, if you have one? You’ll find a place to upload it in our form https://theken.typeform.com/FPBooks. No matter if they’re old, torn, or worn, we’d love to feature them along with your recommendations.

Markets are lovin’ it

Seema

Like the novel coronavirus, public markets have run riot in the last few months. It’s baffling. Why are they bullish with all-round falling revenues, rising unemployment, contracting GDPs, and sagging morale? 

Citigroup’s investment banking co-head, Manolo Falco, warned clients this week that “financial markets are way ahead of reality with tougher times to come”.

“As the second quarter comes along and we start seeing the pain, and the collateral effects of that, we think this is going to be much tougher than it looks.”

His comments came at the end of a week when stock markets largely rallied even as millions of businesses around the world remained shut and economies lurched towards their worst recessions in memory.

“Markets are pricing a V [shaped recovery], everyone’s coming back to work, and this is going to be fine,” Mr Falco said. “I don’t think it’s going to be that easy quite frankly.”

Citi warns markets are out of step with grim reality, The Financial Times https://www.ft.com/content/d84dc366-cd2f-422a-b804-3efa24262275

Traditionally, uncertainty is bad for stocks. In the past, markets rallied whenever the fog of uncertainty thinned: a majority government comes to power, a border standoff eases… when the US regulator lifts its sanctions on drug companies or gives them a clean chit. Straightforward stuff. 

Not this time. Uncertainty seems to be the tailwind. Also for pharma companies, especially those who have anything at all to do with Covid-19. As Reuters reported yesterday (emphasis added): 

On April 17, Gilead’s shares jumped nearly 10% following a media report detailing encouraging partial data from trials of the U.S. company’s experimental drug remdesivir in severe COVID-19 patients.

Those gains were dwarfed by a price jump in bullish options which traded in unusually high volume just hours before the report and which more than doubled in value overnight.

Gilead trades that made millions raise eyebrows, Reuters https://www.reuters.com/article/us-health-coronavirus-gilead-sciences-op/gilead-trades-that-made-millions-on-covid-19-drug-news-raise-eyebrows-idUSKBN23828D

Moderna, the US biotech that is a frontrunner for a new vaccine, saw its share prices close 21% higher on 18 May, when it released interim trial data. Dr Anthony Fauci, director of the National Institute for Allergy and Infectious Diseases and the American face of the medical response to the pandemic, minced no words in this 1 June interview:

“I didn’t like that. What we would have preferred to do, quite frankly, is to wait until we had the data from the entire Phase 1 — which I hear is quite similar to the data that they showed — and publish it in a reputable journal and show all the data. But the company, when they looked at the data, as all companies do, they said, wow, this is exciting. Let’s put out a press release.”

Anthony Fauci on Covid-19 reopenings, vaccines, and moving at ‘warp speed, Statnews https://www.statnews.com/2020/06/01/anthony-fauci-on-covid-19-reopenings-vaccines-and-moving-at-warp-speed/

The markets continue to be ahead of reality. Also on 1 June, CNN reported: 

Even as critics accused Moderna of overhyping the results released on May 18, a series of transactions were executed before its share price fizzled over the next week. The timing of those deals, former SEC officials said, appear to be "highly problematic" and should be investigated for potential illegal market manipulation.

Just hours after revealing the promising vaccine results, Moderna (MRNA) sold 17.6 million shares to the public. That share sale, unveiled after the closing bell on May 18, was priced at $76; Moderna traded at just $48 as recently as May 6. The deal instantly raised $1.3 billion.

Moderna's coronavirus vaccine announcement set off a frenzy on Wall Street. Now some are calling for an investigation, CNN Business​ https://edition.cnn.com/2020/06/01/business/moderna-vaccine-stock-sales-invs/index.html

After a year-long battering, Indian big pharma stocks have had a bull run since the pandemic struck. On 30 April, Glenmark’s shares went up https://in.reuters.com/article/us-glenmark-pharms-stocks/glenmark-to-conduct-trials-in-india-for-potential-covid-19-drug-idINKBN22C0IZ 9% after the company said it was starting clinical trials of antiviral drug favipiravir, seen as a potential treatment for Covid-19. 

Shares of Cipla and Jubilant Life Sciences—companies that have taken license from Gilead to manufacture its intravenous drug remdesivir—have also soared. Dr Reddy’s, the first Indian company to say it was working on a remdesivir clone, has since dropped out without even licensing the drug from Gilead. But the rising tide has lifted its boat as well.

While some of these drugs have advanced in clinical studies, the uncertainty overhang hasn’t dissipated. As we wrote https://the-ken.com/story/indian-pharmas-great-covid-gamble/, remdesivir is not the preferred choice for the Indian government, which is looking at two other options, driven by cost and ease of delivery. 

Additionally, the Trump administration has vowed to bring generics drug manufacturing to local shores, and the two recent contracts worth US$500 million that it has awarded indicate it’s not empty rhetoric this time. If this continues, it bodes an existential threat https://www.statnews.com/2020/05/26/panic-pharma-buy-american-manufacturing-us/ to Indian generic makers. 

But as Bloomberg columnist Matt Levine noted yesterday, “Stocks are trying to forget 2020 https://www.bloomberg.com/opinion/articles/2020-06-01/stocks-are-trying-to-forget-2020.” They are already into 2021.

So much for the short-termism that CEOs often blame the markets for to avoid long-term innovation bets. 

Truth to power

Rohin

The powerful rarely speak truth to power. Because they have too much at stake. But pandemics challenge our assumptions and upset existing orders. 

Two very important people, and both former insiders, have spoken out on significant matters about India’s Covid-19 response.

The first was SC Garg. Less than a year ago, he was one of the most important officials in the Indian government—its finance secretary. His power and arrogance were legion. No institution was too big for him to take on, including India’s Reserve Bank. Ultimately, he was shunted out by the government into a “lesser” ministry, Power. He promptly resigned https://www.thequint.com/voices/opinion/was-government-fed-up-of-finance-secretary-subhash-chandra-garg. 

A senior bureaucrat scorned is a dangerous adversary. Because Garg published a withering takedown of both India’s response to Covid-19 and its economic prospects, estimating its GDP would contract by as much as 10-12% https://subhashchandragarg.blogspot.com/2020/06/lockdown-stimulus-and-indias-gdp-2020-21.html.

India’s lockdown strategy was faulty and reflected more a panic than any careful consideration of facts, international experience and its economic and humanitarian consequences.

The lockdown was imposed under a naive belief that India would be able to eliminate Covid-19 from the face of India in three weeks’ time. There were two assumptions on which Indian lockdown decision was based. First, there were only 500 odd Covid-19 positive persons and if they were placed in hospitals and everyone else was confined to their homes, the infected and uninfected would not come into contact with each other and the chain of infection would break. Second, the asymptomatic persons would display the symptoms in maximum three weeks and such persons would get identified in this three weeks’ period.

In the heat of moment, India decided to use the brahmastra- total economic and human lockdown- on the entire country when only a tiny part was infected. No other country in the world went for such a sledgehammer approach. One lockdown followed another lockdown. We have had four versions of lockdown so far. While some opening up commenced from lockdown 3, India begins formal unlocking only from June 1.

It's a long read. But understand that it is from someone who is versed with the minutiae of the very details he is now criticising. 

The second person was Nandan Nilekani, the co-founder and Chairman of IT giant Infosys, and the most powerful man when it comes to India’s increasingly ambitious and labyrinthine technology initiatives. Nilekani is also known as the father of Aadhaar, India’s massive unique ID project.

In an interview with ET Now yesterday, Nilekani warned against https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/tech/ites/weve-to-guard-against-covid-apps-increasing-state-power/articleshow/76143519.cms runaway state power if contact tracing apps are allowed to continue even after the pandemic has been controlled. For Nilekani to say this on the record is a big deal.

This (Covid-19) has really done something which is unimaginable, I have to know the health of people I am meeting. Health was supposed to be private. This puts a whole new angle on privacy and surveillance. Obviously, for proper containment we will have to understand who is where and if they are following quarantine. It does run the risk of increasing state power and we have to guard against that and treat many of these things as a temporary step that needs to be rolled back once things get back to normal.

Two powerful people. Two former insiders. Both speaking truth to power.

Malaysians warm up to house calls

Kay

Although Malaysia has reopened many economic sectors since 4 May, several non-essential services such as hair salons, reflexology centres, and beauty services are still shut. The government has yet https://www.malaysiakini.com/news/528189 to devise a set of standard operating procedures (SOPs) for a reopen. 

Some of them have found a workaround to cushion the financial hit: house calls. Hairdressers https://www.malaysiakini.com/news/525205, especially, are among the first to capitalise as restrictions on movement are loosened.

Are they Covid-proofed? Well, some barbers are flaunting a free certification https://barbicide.com/certification/ from US barber product supplier BlueCo Brands (also known as King Research Inc). The certification only requires taking a 12-question quiz on how to prevent the spread of the pandemic.

It’s a bit trickier in the reflexology business since client contact is much higher than for barbers. But customers are more than happy to get a massage at home. 

For instance, Snooze, an on-demand wellness platform that offers massages in Singapore and Malaysia, noted a “significant surge” of requests for in-home massage in the last few weeks. But it opted to https://www.snooze.my/updates/making-tough-decisions-in-tough-time/ stay shut for safety reasons. Sentuh, a Malaysian wellness startup, is pivoting https://www.theedgemarkets.com/article/pivoting-necessary-extend-business-runway-says-ondemand-wellness-platform its core business to on-demand physiotherapy—a government-approved essential service. 

Clearly Malaysians are starting to warm up to the concept of enjoying lifestyle services from the comfort of their own homes. Previously, this was limited to home and cleaning services. But with Covid-19, the list is now expanding. Yet another opportunity for super apps? 

Complications of a cancelled Hajj

Nadine

The Hajj, or pilgrimage to Mecca, is the world’s largest regularly recurring mass-movement of people. More than 2 million participate in the trip annually, and it occurs shortly after the fasting month of Ramadan.

Hajj tours are highly organised and commoditised. There’s a quota system: Each country is allocated a specific number of slots per year. Indonesia, home to the world’s largest Muslim population, has the highest quota. It can send over 200,000 travellers to Mecca for Hajj.

Saudi Arabia’s holy sites were already closed earlier this year due to the coronavirus, which meant smaller pilgrimages were cancelled. 

The writing was on the wall that this years’ Hajj would also be called off, but Indonesia’s Ministry of Religious Affairs waited until 2 June to make it official https://www.thejakartapost.com/news/2020/06/02/indonesia-cancels-haj-pilgrimage-over-coronavirus-concerns.html. The first cluster of Indonesian pilgrims was meant to depart on 26 June.

The first hit is to Saudi Arabia. Religious pilgrimages, of which Hajj is the most significant, contribute US$12 billion https://timesofindia.indiatimes.com/world/middle-east/why-hajj-will-soon-be-the-new-oil-for-saudi-arabia/articleshow/65469627.cms to the country, which equals to 20% of its non-oil GDP and 7% of total GDP.

It also spells trouble for Indonesians, who have booked and saved up for the trip months, if not years, in advance. It costs about US$2,500 to book a Hajj tour from Indonesia. If tens of thousands of travellers claimed full or partial refunds on deposits, that’s a problem. The government is implementing a refund scheme which only allows partial refunds, with the rest kept for next year. That means those who had intended to go this year will be first in line for the 2021 season.

This creates another problem though, because Indonesia’s quota won’t simply double. It will be a drawn-out process to re-organise the pilgrimage plans. It’s not unheard of though. The Hajj has been cancelled https://theconversation.com/hajj-cancellation-wouldnt-be-the-first-plague-war-and-politics-disrupted-pilgrimages-long-before-coronavirus-135900 a number of times, but this is the first full cancellation since the founding of the Saudi Kingdom in 1932.

Trick or tease

Seetharaman

India’s auto industry was in a funk well before the pandemic struck. Last year saw the biggest fall https://timesofindia.indiatimes.com/business/india-business/car-sales-dip-most-in-2-decades-in-2019/articleshow/73063244.cms in car sales in two decades. The lockdown only added to the sector’s woes. Now, as restrictions are lifted, carmakers and lenders are turning to an old lending tool to lure buyers: teaser loans https://in.reuters.com/article/health-coronavirus-india-autoloans/indian-carmakers-offer-teaser-loans-as-banking-sources-say-rbi-softens-stance-idINKBN2390S2. 

Teaser loans offer borrowers a lower interest rate initially but become more expensive later as they become market-linked. Which means monthly repayments go up after a specified period. Teaser rates were first used by the State Bank of India (SBI), the country’s largest bank, in 2009 for home loans. Coming in the wake of the US subprime crisis, these loans did not please the Reserve Bank of India, which raised worries about the borrower’s ability to repay when interest rates increased. 

Subsequently, dual-rate loans declined https://www.businesstoday.in/money/banking/sbi-says-enough-with-teaser-loans/story/18505.html, but not before they were extended to car financing https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/wealth/personal-finance-news/sbi-brings-teaser-rates-to-car-loan-market/articleshow/6021841.cms?from=mdr. SBI tried unsuccessfully to bring back teaser rates in 2015 https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/industry/banking/finance/banking/teaser-loans-again-state-bank-of-india-asks-rbi-to-allow-sub-base-rate-loans/articleshow/48554684.cms?from=mdr and 2019 https://www.livemint.com/industry/banking/rbi-unlikely-to-allow-teaser-loans-back-into-the-system-1568816073728.html. They’re back now, but are a tricky proposition with jobs and salaries uncertain.

A former SBI executive, who spoke on conditions of anonymity, said that offering staggered payments on car loans makes little sense as the tenure is much shorter. This could result in customers being burdened with higher repayments at a time when the long-term impact of Covid-19 and the national lockdown is not yet clear, the former SBI official said.

ICICI Bank-Maruti offer ‘teaser’ car loans amid Covid slump…, BloombergQuint​ https://www.bloombergquint.com/business/icici-bank-maruti-offer-teaser-car-loans-amid-covid-19-slump

There’s no doubt that consumers are going to need a lot of coaxing to buy homes or cars. And even incentives. But teaser loans may not be the best option, especially if banks want to avoid loans turning bad. And the name of one of the three schemes offered by ICICI and Maruti—Balloon EMI—hardly helps matters.

Australia’s foreign student corridor

Nadine

Australian universities desperately need to bring back foreign students—a third of whom are from China and the rest from India, Malaysia, Indonesia and other mostly Asian countries.

“International students bring about A$39 billion (S$36.7 billion) a year into Australia and account for about 26 per cent of total university revenue. The sudden loss of many students from abroad amid the ban on arrivals of foreign nationals due to the pandemic has already led to staff cuts at universities and could leave some facing financial collapse if it continues into next year.”

Coronavirus: Australian universities propose 'safe corridors' to allow foreign students to return, The Straits Times https://www.straitstimes.com/asia/australianz/coronavirus-australian-universities-propose-safe-corridors-to-allow-foreign

Eight of the country’s top universities have reportedly asked the government to sign off on a “safe corridor” plan that would allow students from countries that have the outbreak “under control” to enter Australia.

This is yet another variation of the travel bubble https://www.smithsonianmag.com/travel/five-things-know-about-travel-bubbles-180974983/—countries striking deals to allow people entry under specific conditions for specific purposes. Bubbles have also been proposed to manage entire sports events https://www.espn.com/nba/story/_/id/29120877/the-nba-bubble-blueprint-how-league-finish-2019-20-season.

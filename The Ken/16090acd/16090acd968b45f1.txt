Good Morning Ishan,

Coffee and conversation is always a good idea. Well. Almost always.

The warmth of a steaming cup has served exactly three goals since the Indian health ministry installed a coffee machine in a dusty, pan-stained corner last winter. It provides for the after-lunch caffeine hit, keeps hands warm, and most importantly, entertains new guests in the ministry. Those who have a lot to offer to the government.

One such offer is that of unused hospital beds https://the-ken.com/big-hospitals-lean/?utm_campaign=The%20Ken&utm_source=Email&utm_medium=Newsletter in private hospitals that are otherwise inaccessible to most because the ones who really need them cannot afford them. These beds make about 80% of all the beds in all the hospitals in the country, against the meagre 20% in overcrowded government-funded hospitals. Everyone can see that they are two pieces of a health jigsaw puzzle waiting to be put together, but few did so this year.

In 2017, the Aam Aadmi Party took long strides https://the-ken.com/aap-universal-healthcare/?utm_campaign=The%20Ken&utm_source=Email&utm_medium=Newsletter?utm_campaign=The%20Ken&utm_source=Email&utm_medium=Newsletter towards making free and universal healthcare an achievable goal. It also proved that one service that the state government can offer to gain popularity is healthcare. How? By partnering with privately-funded profit-motivated hospitals. Meanwhile, these hospitals turned against the state as West Bengal government hit them where it hurts the most by capping prices https://the-ken.com/private-healthcare-prices-capped/?utm_campaign=The%20Ken&utm_source=Email&utm_medium=Newsletter of medical procedures.

The negotiations between private companies and state governments will continue in 2018 as the former has losses https://the-ken.com/big-hospitals-lean/to?utm_campaign=The%20Ken&utm_source=Email&utm_medium=Newsletter cover, and the latter is responsible for three-quarters http://rchiips.org/NFHS/pdf/NFHS4/India.pdf?utm_campaign=The%20Ken&utm_source=Email&utm_medium=Newsletter of the Indian population that is still not covered by any health scheme or insurance.

Both in talks behind closed doors and at press conferences, one question became the bone of contention this year—who will pay the actual cost of healthcare? The central government insisted that large corporates should bear the cross. They were not the only ones who pushed the boundaries to new frontiers, pharma companies tried and succeeded in the fields of biopharma and speciality drugs. Large Indian drug manufacturer Sun Pharma partnered https://the-ken.com/biopharma-indian-pharma-achilles-heel/?utm_campaign=The%20Ken&utm_source=Email&utm_medium=Newsletter?utm_campaign=The%20Ken&utm_source=Email&utm_medium=Newsletter with a Korean contact manufacturer to develop a novel biologic, and Biocon succeeded https://the-ken.com/behind-biocons-payday/?utm_campaign=The%20Ken&utm_source=Email&utm_medium=Newsletter by getting a seal of quality on its biosimilar.

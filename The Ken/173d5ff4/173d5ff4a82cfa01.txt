Gold works in mysterious ways

Nithin

During India’s monetary policy committee meeting last week, the Reserve Bank of India (RBI), the country’s central bank, decided to make sure borrowers get the most buck out of their gold. It said borrowers could get close to Rs 90 (US$1.2) for a collateral worth Rs 100 (US$1.3), at a time when gold prices have been soaring. 

The last time the RBI did this, gold loan companies such as Muthoot Finance and Manappuram Finance paid a heavy price. 

From 2011 to 2012, gold prices rallied by close to 50%. You would expect gold loan companies’ shares to have done well, too. However, as these companies were lending at a loan to value (LTV) of ~85%, share prices fell by up to 38%. This is because of the increased risk that a fall in gold prices could have. Let me explain: if a lender loaned Rs 85 (US$1.1) against a collateral of Rs 100, a fall in prices would reduce the value of the collateral. The lender could then lose money on the loan. 

Turns out, you can either have too much LTV, or too little LTV. 

In March 2012, when the RBI capped the LTV to 60%, the share prices of gold loan companies fell by 30-60%. While it meant better risk mitigation measures, since banks did not have these same LTV https://www.livemint.com/Money/PmAoD5ebdNZiU1XVwhJgrO/DYK-Gold-Loan-LTV.html caps, they could lend more. So it lowered business prospects for the gold loan companies as people would rather borrow from the bank by pledging gold. 

In January 2013, an RBI committee proposed to raise the LTV ratio of finance companies lending money against gold to 75% from 60%. The shares of these companies rose by ~20% https://www.livemint.com/Companies/1xGZ3OQ8KY1uoTDRLxBvQK/Muthoot-Manappuram-shares-touch-new-highs-on-draft-rules.html in a single day, as it meant a more level playing field and better days ahead.

The Indian economy was going through a slowdown then too, and its GDP print was below 5% https://www.hindustantimes.com/india-news/why-the-2019-slowdown-is-different-from-2012-13/story-XuRgL94L1g82EaLGqasmAJ.html leading into 2013. It made sense for the RBI to tap into the gold reserves held by Indian families, increase the loan value they could get, and improve consumption.

Then, gold tumbled by over 20% in the following quarter, and the shares of these gold lenders fell by over 60% again. Ouch.

History is likely repeating itself now. 

As gold prices rallied by 50% in the past year to hit giddying highs, share prices of Muthoot Finance and Manappuram Finance have risen too. What makes it different this time?

Unlike the 85% LTV of 2011, the prevailing LTV of 75% gives some cushion to the lenders if gold prices drop. And with demand for gold loans increasing during the pandemic, for investors, these businesses offer some visibility during the uncertainty. 

What’s not different is that last week, because of the RBI’s move similar to 2011, a rising gold price and a slowing economy has led to a relaxation in the lending guidelines.

During this economic slowdown, people need money. And increasing the cash value they can borrow against rising gold prices can protect people’s immediate cash requirements. And hopefully spur growth too.  

However, there’s a sense of déjà vu about this whole thing. While the action to increase LTV may be necessary to help tide over income uncertainties in the short-term, it could have an adverse effect on the gold loan companies. 

The ball is in the court of the gold finance companies now. One can hope that they’ll be more prudent this time with the LTV and lending, because the systemic risk is higher.

Have we learnt lessons from the past? 

A bigger crisis than coronavirus

Jum

The Philippines has been dealing with a huge public health crisis. No, I’m not talking about Covid-19, although it sure is a problem made worse by the virus.

I’m talking about hunger.

48% of child deaths in the Philippines are linked to malnutrition https://www.savethechildren.org.ph/__resources/webdata/file/downloadables/40_Lives-Cut-Short.pdf, as per the latest study by humanitarian organisation Save the Children. That’s higher than the 45% global average.

As the Philippines imposed strict lockdown measures to contain the spread of Covid-19, resulting in business closures and job cuts, more Filipino families reported involuntary hunger https://newsinfo.inquirer.net/1310045/1-of-5-pinoys-hungry-as-sws-records-highest-hunger-rate-since-2014—at 20.9% of the total as of July, higher than the 16.7% in May and more than double the 8.8% in December 2019. It doesn’t end there. With Covid-19 plunging the country into its worst recession https://rappler.com/voices/thought-leaders/analysis-end-growth-how-pandemic-ruined-philippine-economy since World War II, hunger incidence among local families is only expected to grow.

Widespread hunger and malnutrition, especially among children, have serious implications on the economy.

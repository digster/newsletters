I’m sure you’ve heard of Munshi Premchand, the poignant Hindi and Urdu author of the early 1900s. In 1924, he published a book named Rangbhoomi, which translates to amphitheatre. Here are its first couple of lines:

“The city is where the rich live and do their business. The land outside the city is their place of entertainment and humour. In the central part of the outskirts lie their children’s schools…”

Alifiya’s story today immediately reminded me of these lines. For the longest time, India’s elite defaulted to traditional boarding schools like The Doon School in Dehradun and Mayo College in Ajmer—with sprawling campuses away from the city. 

Apart from limiting exposure to technology, focussing on structured study, and sports, these schools provided an intangible benefit. Studying in Mayo or Doon meant access to a powerful network of alumni—think union ministers, top executives, and professionals—a social currency that acted as a launchpad for students.

And thanks to high admission fees and a choosy selection process, these schools remained reserved for the rich. But then came international schools like Ecole Mondiale World School and Dhirubhai Ambani International School in Mumbai and Vasant Valley School in New Delhi. Suddenly they became the top choice for those with deep pockets.

Now, writes Alifiya, a second wave has begun with centuries-old international boarding schools establishing their campuses in India—from Harrow School in Bengaluru to Shrewsbury in Bhopal.

So instead of continuing to take their pre-eminence for granted, India’s legacy boarding schools are doing all they can to remain relevant. Some of these moves are also displeasing its elite alumni.

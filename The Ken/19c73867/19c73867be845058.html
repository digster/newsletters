Rajan Bajaj, the founder of the fintech company Slice, became the MD and CEO of Slice Small Finance Bank on Tuesday. That makes him the lone Indian fintech founder to take on RBI’s hard regulatory knocks, buy a bank and then lead it. 

Some tried but couldn’t make it to the hot seat. In 2024, Paytm’s Vijay Shekhar Sharma* stepped down as chairman of the board of Paytm Payments Bank after the bank was hauled up for its close relationship with the fintech, while Sachin Bansal’s lending fintech Navi failed to meet the RBI’s “fit and proper” condition to obtain a universal banking license. 

Even Bajaj’s appointment might not have come easily. After all, bank CEO appointments by the RBI are contentious decisions. And he may not have been the only name considered by the regulator. 

In its 2020 guidelines, the RBI said proposals for the appointment of a new MD and CEO should invariably contain a panel of at least two names in order of preference. In the past, it has also not allowed executives who have not been bankers to lead a payment bank. 

But Bajaj, to his credit, did not rush to be appointed as the bank’s MD and CEO. He, instead, made his way up gradually, and this likely helped his case.

Slice first bought a 5% stake in the North East Small Finance Bank for $3.4 million in 2022, inching its way to owning the bank. Its merger with the bank was approved in 2024, with Satish Kumar Kalra as the CEO, and Bajaj stayed on as the director. 

For a fintech founder to also be a bank’s CEO is an important step. Bajaj can now chart the bank’s course in a way that keeps Slice competitive, taking full advantage of its banking license. 

Founded in 2016, the Tiger Global-backed fintech spent its first seven years building what it called a “credit card challenger”. It issued prepaid cards loaded with a credit line that worked a lot like a credit card. But its business model collapsed in 2022 when the RBI barred the loading of prepaid instruments with credit lines.

Slice then turned to what seems to be the only way for fintechs to get banking licenses: by making a play to acquire troubled assets. North East Small Finance Bank was badly in need of funds—it had bad loans of 18%—and the regulator wanted the bank to have better prospects. This is when Slice decided to step in and merge with the Guwahati-based bank.

Bharatpe, along with Centrum Financial, too had found its way similarly into owning roughly half of Unity Small Finance Bank in 2021, by taking on the assets and bad loans of the scam-ridden Punjab and Maharashtra Cooperative (PMC) Bank. And though RBI willed this into being to keep the bank alive, a Centrum executive was chosen to lead it, and not anyone from Bharatpe.

Now, green shoots are in sight at both Slice and Unity’s small finance banks. At Unity, the ratio of bad loans has decreased from 45% to 6% in FY25. Meanwhile, at Slice, bad loans have fallen to 6.25% in FY25 from 18% at the time of the 2023 merger.

As per Crisil, the credit rating agency, during H1 FY26, Slice’s bank has seen its total income marginally increase to Rs 632 crore from Rs 604 crore in FY25. The bank’s deposit base rose 61% to Rs 3,900 crore, and its assets under management also went up 27% to Rs 3,800 crore by September 30, 2025. 

By becoming a bank, players like Slice can continue to stay in the fintech fight. But for Bajaj, the real test begins now. He will have to keep his fintech grounding and instincts in check, making sure it doesn’t come in the way of running a bank, even as he strikes the delicate balance between RBI’s demands and his investors’ expectations.

This makes Bajaj the fintech founder to watch in the coming year. 

*Paytm founder Vijay Shekhar Sharma is an investor in The Ken.

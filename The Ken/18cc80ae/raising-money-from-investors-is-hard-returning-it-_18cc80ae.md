Good Morning Ishan,
Welcome to a brand new year of reading and reflection, of podcasts and conversations. A very happy new year to you, from all of us at The Ken.
We are kicking off 2024 with a story that marks the maturity as well as a turning point in India’s startup ecosystem. 2023 will go down as a year when founders and investors stopped throwing good money and time after bad.
After the funding frenzy of 2021 and the credit squeeze of 2023, this may sound unsurprising. But trust our reporting, it’s never happened at this scale or with this level-headedness.
Most startups don’t make it to the finish line; ~90% is the industry statistic. Yet founders rarely think of this number when starting up. And I am sure nearly 35,000 startups that shut shop (or were on the brink of doing so) in 2023—almost double the previous year’s number—didn’t start out thinking they’d fall in the ~90% category.
Surely, some might have been craven also-rans riding the funding wave. But, essentially, striking out on one’s own is a credo, and when on that path, one hits a fork where returning money to the investor is better than plodding along. It’s worth paying attention to.
Arundhati’s story today, where she spoke to five founders who chose to return money to their investors, is not about the act itself but how they went about it. You could say it was expected that obscene valuations and inflated founder competencies would yield many duds. But just because something is expected doesn’t mean it’s easy, she writes.
As one founder put it, “Returning money is harder than raising [it].”
Do investors agree?

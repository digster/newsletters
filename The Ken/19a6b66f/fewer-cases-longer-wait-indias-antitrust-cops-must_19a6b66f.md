Make India Competitive Again
Mon, 10 Nov 25
What India is doing, will do, and should do—to not just survive but thrive in the chaos unleashed by Trump.
Good Morning Ishan,
After nearly five years of suspense, India finally has a verdict on Whatsapp’s data-sharing practices. And it’s not quite what the country’s antitrust regulator hoped for.
In early November, the National Company Law Appellate Tribunal—a body that hears appeals against the orders of, among others, the Competition Commission of India (CCI)—[dismissed](https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/industry/telecom/nclat-sets-aside-cci-ban-on-whatsapp-sharing-user-data-with-meta/articleshow/125103674.cms?from=mdr) the ban on the messaging platform sharing user data with its parent Meta. The accompanying snub to CCI, which had introduced the ban, was that it had exceeded its powers and overstepped its jurisdiction. The upholding of its Rs 200 crore fine on Whatsapp was, at best, a small consolation.
And that’s [the story](https://the-ken.com/story/big-ai-shows-up-in-india-the-antitrust-regulator-hasnt-got-the-memo/) of the CCI right now. India’s digital markets are turning into a dense, algorithmic jungle of data, platforms, and network effects. And the antitrust watchdog is somewhere in there, barking at the trees.
The numbers don’t lie. Investigations opened by the body have halved to seven in 2024, from 13 the year before. This is the lowest they’ve ever been. The time it takes to bring cases to a close, meanwhile, has more than doubled to six to eight months, from just three months earlier, a competition lawyer told The Ken.
Though the CCI claims it has resolved 90% of the cases it’s taken up to date, it’s a classic case of selection bias, said another competition lawyer who works closely with CCI. After all, if the commission took up just seven of the 35 complaints it received in 2024—dismissing 80% of total complaints—that’s hardly a glittering track record.
Jurisdictional limits are another point of contention. Nearly half of CCI’s orders end up being challenged at the appellate tribunal, and stew there for years. Umar Javed vs. Google has been ongoing for seven years. Federation of Hotel and Restaurant Associations of India vs. Makemytrip has been ongoing for six years. Meanwhile, in Europe, most competition cases wrap up in 18 months.
Similarly, the Whatsapp case was taken up by CCI in 2021, when the messaging platform’s privacy policy made it mandatory for users to share their data with other Meta platforms. CCI dubbed this “anti-competitive”; Whatsapp called it a “misunderstanding”, going on to say that CCI was unequipped to understand the technical underpinnings.
The implication was clear: the competition regulator’s plodding pace was no match for the digital economy’s incessant strides.
The number of cases CCI picks up each year is minuscule—and has been dropping over the years. Two years ago, it dismissed 60% of complaints [received](https://carnegieendowment.org/research/2025/02/assessing-the-need-for-a-new-draft-digital-competition-bill-in-the-indian-context?lang=en). That’s now gone up to 80%, according to a back-of-the envelope calculation by The Ken.
Suo moto cases, or those CCI takes up on its own without an external complainant, have fared the worst. The antitrust watchdog has taken up exactly zero suo moto cases over the last two years, and the year before that, it was a mere 1% of the total cases.

At this point, it’s hard to tell whether the watchdog is losing its bite or just tired of barking.
Part of the problem is that the regulator seems permanently buried in paperwork—drafting rules and penalty guidelines, and doing routine admin tasks, according to seven competition lawyers who spoke with The Ken. Worse yet, it’s not operating at full capacity.
An August 2025 report by the parliamentary standing committee on finance highlighted that only 113 of CCI’s 195 sanctioned posts are filled. “In the government, the remuneration patterns we offer to permanent cadres are a tad lower than what they are in the [private] sector,” [said](https://sansad.in/getFile/lsscommittee/Finance/18_Finance_25.pdf?source=loksabhadocs) Ministry of Corporate Affairs secretary and committee member Deepti Gaur Mukerjee—as a possible reason for the unfilled posts.
The increasing complexity of cases in today’s digital world is also flummoxing the regulator, added one of the lawyers.
All while Big Tech is bringing in armies to fight its battles.
CCI can’t exactly bank on legislative help, either. For instance, the Digital Competition Bill meant to ensure timely redressal of anti-competitive practices by digital enterprises has been in the works since 2023. But for the last year or so, it’s been stuck in the draft stage. If the bill gets passed, it will still place the appellate tribunal above the CCI, just like in the earlier Competition Act 2002.
“The industry lobby, particularly Big Tech, doesn’t feel that we’ve reached a stage where we need to have this bill,” said a former chairman of CCI. “This at a time when the rise of a few AI platforms is already showing early signs of consolidation,” said Akshat Agarwal, founder and counsel at law firm AASA Chambers.
The lack of intention gets clearer. “The committee on the bill ended up spending very little time discussing whether we need the law. It’s like they had made up their minds,” said a lawyer who was part of the committee.
In the national capital, it’s common enough to see big announcements eclipse actual implementation. Another victim of this: the Digital Personal Data Protection (DPDP) rules, a detailed, prescriptive follow-up to the DPDP Act passed in 2023. Though IT and electronics minister Ashwini Vaishnav had [announced](https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/tech/technology/dpdp-rules-to-be-released-by-28-september-vaishnaw/articleshow/123982777.cms?from=mdr) that the rules would be out on 27 September, they’re still pending.
If you thought CCI is slow, India’s legislature is slower.
The dangers of a regulator on the back foot are increasing anti-competitive practices in the market, compounded in the age of AI.
“When algorithms start talking to each other without human intervention, competition challenges become more complex than before,” said an AI startup founder. For instance, consider an AI tool that helps sellers on Amazon set prices. Now, the tool knows better than to start a price war. Instead, it can shortlist three key suppliers, and align their prices with each other.
The result: the collusion is invisible, but the prices are locked. All at the expense of consumers.
“This is where the law should come in to say that your AI agents can talk, but they can’t carry one company’s data straight into another’s system,” said the AI founder.
Such AI-induced collusion can occur in pretty much any sector—whether that’s e-commerce platforms, airlines, or even traditional industries such as cement or pharma, where algorithms, not humans, may start setting prices, according to a representative from the office of Abir Roy, founder of law firm Sarvada Legal. “Many of these cases will also likely be closed quickly, since there may not be sufficient evidence of collusion—AI being a black box,” said another lawyer.
CCI’s monitoring of anti-competitive mergers and acquisitions is also hobbled by a rather impractical deal-value threshold.
In 2023, an amendment to the Competition Act stated that all mergers and acquisitions over Rs 2,000 crore would need CCI’s stamp of approval. Earlier, there was no threshold, just an understanding that CCI looked at none but the largest M&As.
But the revised threshold is still too high, leaving several M&As out of CCI’s jurisdiction. Telecom company Jio’s acquisition of conversational-AI platform Haptik in 2019 (for Rs 230 crore); software company Adobe’s acquisition of text-to-video AI startup Rephrase.ai in 2023; and generative-AI platform Typeface’s acquisition of multimedia AI content-creation company Tensortour in 2024 all fell outside CCI’s scope.
Sure, none of the acquired companies were all that big, but now they never will be. Early consolidation kills competition before it turns into a worthy rival.
That said, in the 14 years since it started overseeing M&As, CCI has not blocked a single deal. Clearly, the regulator, which cut its teeth checking mergers of steel mills and prices of onions, is having a hard time adapting to the modern marketplace.
Meanwhile, for every unchecked deal and delayed case, the market has conveniently appointed its own winners.

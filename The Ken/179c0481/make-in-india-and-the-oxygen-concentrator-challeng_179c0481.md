Good Morning Ishan,
The air we normally breathe contains roughly 21% Oxygen and nearly 78% Nitrogen. This factoid is something most of us learned in school, and then usually forgot about.
It took a pandemic to teach us the importance of oxygen concentration in our air. As the Covid-19 virus and its progressively virulent variants attack our lungs, the infection they cause severely reduces the capacity of lungs to suck in enough oxygen from the air we breathe. The only way to help patients during this time is to provide them with air that has a much higher oxygen concentration.
Enter, oxygen concentrators.
These magical machines force atmospheric air through materials known as zeolites, which trap nitrogen and allow oxygen through. They’ve been around for nearly four decades.
And yet, India is facing both a very public and very silent crisis around these machines.
The public crisis is well-documented. There just aren’t enough concentrators to help those in need. As the second wave hit India, prices of existing models tripled and quadrupled. They became luxury items, affordable to only a few. As things became worse, even money couldn’t get you a decent concentrator.
The silent crisis is what Olina reports about in her story today. The desperation caused by the second wave has created a free-for-all concentrator market in the country. There are no quality controls. There are no standards. Cheap imports (marked up heftily by importers in many cases) where values of oxygen output are “hard-coded” into the display readouts have been found. Meaning, a concentrator that “shows” between 93-96% delivered oxygen concentration even while it's only getting 35% through.
That is definitely criminal and potentially fatal.
In this chaos, a bunch of Indian scientists and companies are straining to build indigenous concentrators. It’s not easy. The raw materials are either expensive or unavailable. Quality standards are absent. Support from the government is piecemeal.
“It’s like digging a well when we’re already on fire,” says Amitabha Bandyopadhyay, a professor at the Indian Institute of Technology Kanpur (IIT-K). He’s part of a team that’s working against time to design and manufacture an Indian oxygen concentrator.
Most people assume that the market for concentrators is very transient. And that it will gradually shrink as India’s second wave ebbs.
But what if Covid-19 becomes an endemic disease? One that never really goes away but keeps coming back in larger and smaller waves each year. Concentrators, then, could become a staple feature, like air purifiers.

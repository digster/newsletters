Indian publishing claims to have a magic number—10,000.

If a book sells beyond 10K, more magical things happen: multiple reprints, a push for a film deal, talks of a sequel...

But the catch is, average sales of a book hover around 3000-5000. As for the magic number 10K, it strictly adheres to the number of physical copies sold. Publishers still look at books as either physical editions or as e-book editions, says Amish Raj Mulmi, our new contributor and writer of today’s story.

As an 18-month-old published author, I can relate to it. Even with an advance for 10,000 copies, the magic is suspended. No film deal (understandable, mine is a business non-fiction), no talks of a sequel (the book doesn’t merit one), and no reprints (there’s still time). I had new book deals coming along, but frankly speaking, book writing doesn’t make economic sense. I hope to swallow my words in the not-too-distant future, though. 

Self-publishing platforms like Juggernaut, Matrubharti, Pratilipi are changing the rules of the game. Juggernaut has about a million users across their web and app platforms. Matrubharti on its website reports 2.5 million ebook downloads, with nearly 4,000 authors. Pratilipi has about 16,500 writers in eight languages. 

Making money may still be a while away, but China is an inspiration. In its massive $1.1 billion IPO last month, China Literature disclosed that the average income of the top 10 online literature writers was almost twice that of offline writers.

As Indian platforms toy with the idea of subscription, the books-to-films trend in the interim is a like fresh breath of air. Chiki Sarkar of Juggernaut says, “We’ve sold six books in the last six months with an average offer price of Rs 20 lakhs (i.e. if the film gets made, the option amount is much less). While this sum may not seem huge, it’s far bigger than the advances and earnings these authors would have made.”

Indian self-publishing is yet to have its breakout moment, like, say, 50 Shades of Grey, but it’s certainly breaking out of the old mould.

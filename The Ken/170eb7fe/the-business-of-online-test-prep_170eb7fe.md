"Coaching for an exam on coaching for an exam."
This Inception-level thinking might elicit a chuckle in some. But it'll likely hit home harder if you are an engineer or a doctor. For it's against the laws of nature for students to crack exams for engineering or medical courses without coaching classes and a gazillion prep tests. And in India, you are nothing if not an aspiring doctor or an engineer.
It is this certainty—of lakhs and lakhs of students lining up every year for such coaching—that Unacademy is betting on for its new phase of growth. The seven-year-old online test-prep platform started as the Uber of edtech; collect the best teachers to make bite-sized exam prep videos and load them on YouTube. For free.
Then a year ago, it launched a subscription model and also embraced a structured, Netflix-like offering, with categories for engineering, medical, and other tests. And it took off. At least by the numbers: nearing 100,000 active subscribers, revenue poised to jump 10x to Rs 120 crore ($16.2 million) for the year ending March 2020.
But another critical number ballooned—losses, which nearly quadrupled to Rs 90 crore ($12 million). And that was as of March 2019, i.e. before the expansion went full swing.
Moreover, Olina, the author of today's story, argues that Unacademy's business model itself is in flux. It's wedged between a streamlined test-prep product and a Netflix-like platform—a beast with two opposing DNA strains, she says.
Besides, "who wants to binge-watch economic classes?" asks the founder of a rival edtech company.
Unacademy galloped to the first Rs 100 crore. Olina's story examines if the path for the next Rs 100 crore will be just as easy.

Good Morning Ishan,
With double-digit growth in quarterly revenue and more than 5 million customers, things have been going great for Paisabazaar, PB Fintech’s credit marketplace and financial services arm.
Paisabazaar earns the bulk of its revenue through unsecured credit, meaning loans that aren’t guaranteed by any asset. Examples of such lending products include personal loans and credit cards. The segment accounts for nearly 95% of Paisabazaar’s income.
But for more than a year, the Reserve Bank of India has been prompting banks and non-bank financial companies to scale down collateral-free, small-ticket loans, throwing a wrench into Paisabazaar’s well-oiled machine.
Switching gears to offer secured lending would mean turning Paisabazaar’s customer acquisition on its head by shifting to offline leads. The company has hired senior executives with experience in secured lending to make the transition, but that has led to a conflict between Paisabazaar’s own sales personnel and those of its lending partners.
For today’s story, Rounak spoke with individuals within Paisabazaar to find out more about how the company is trying to adapt to the changing regulatory climate. Last week, I spent a couple of days talking to founders, developers, investors, and dreamers in the AI space. They congregated in Singapore for SuperAI, a two-day conference. Conversations meandered from the practical (“How much money will this save me?”) to the purely philosophical (“What even is AI?”).
There were plenty of interesting questions asked by people who sought to build or work with AI-powered tools in different ways, but answers were scant. When an exchange reached its inevitable dead end, the least offensive way to tie up the conversation was for someone to say, “We’re still early, who knows what it’ll be like in a year or two!”
That’s the polite way to think about it, but it’s fair to be just a little doubtful. This isn’t the first time for a torrent of capital mingling with exorbitant optimism to back new technology that isn’t quite ready for the limelight, yet is under public scrutiny every day.
Large language models, in particular, were like pieces of amorphous, abstract art—described in different ways by anyone who attempted. Watching the models emerge was like “witnessing the birth of a child” (Edward Snowden), or you could “think of LLMs as a graduate—you still have to train them for the job” (Emad Mostaque). These characterisations and everything in between all sounded right in the moment, but feel liminal once you try to think about the bigger picture.
The enthusiasm was palpable, and the products that did work indeed seemed useful, like someone with an extra decade of experience was sharing their intuition with me. But overall, it was one of those rare cases where I left a conference with more question marks over my head than when I first walked in.

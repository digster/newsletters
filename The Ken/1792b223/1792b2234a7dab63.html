Good Morning Ishan,
 
I’ve not had someone close to me die. Not just during the pandemic but in all my thirty-five years.

So death is an abstraction I find hard to dwell on. While I’ve never been told I lack empathy, it’s almost impossible for me to picture the magnitude of pain that results from losing a person you hold dear.

That has changed over the past couple of weeks.

With the unrelenting assault of news about colleagues and friends having to bid farewell to their loved ones, death suddenly seems real in a way it never has. Particularly so when you hear of people in their 30s and 40s succumbing to the coronavirus, and in quite a few cases, due to inadequate medical care.

These are extraordinary times when anxiety and despair reign supreme. Four of my colleagues were down with Covid. So we decided to take a break midweek and not publish stories or our newsletter Beyond the First Order (BFO) on Wednesday. And for the rest of the week, we pulled together to make sure it was business as usual, with some great reads.

Oxygen for Covid patients, or the lack thereof, has been on top of everyone's mind the past few weeks. Maitri's free story on Friday meticulously deconstructed the problems in India’s oxygen supply chain.

The following day, Praveen took a systems thinking approach in his weekly newsletter, The Nutgraf, to explain the bottlenecks in making enough vaccines and oxygen available.

On Tuesday, Anand dwelled on another grave crisis that India is grappling with. By 2050, one in five Indians will be over 60 years old, but most of them either have no retirement plan or are betting on their kids to see them through the final phase of their life—hardly the ideal.

On Monday, we had Seema asking a very important question about one of the most promising sectors. With the number of deep-tech startups growing faster than the funding ecosystem, will it take an investor like Tiger Global to shake things up in the space?

On Thursday, Jon looked at famed startup accelerator YCombinator's increasing influence on Southeast Asia startups, even as alternatives like VC firm Sequoia's Surge are more relevant and offer better terms.  

This week in BFO, Pranav looked at two-wheeler maker Hero MotoCorp's new bet on electric vehicles—it formed a partnership with Taiwanese battery swap station operator Gogoro; Anand wrote about PE giant Blackstone's decision to sell part of its stake in IT firm mPhasis from one of its funds to another; and Sanjana pulled back the curtain on credit card payments company CRED's much-talked-about "donate CRED coins for oxygen" initiative.

That's it from us this week. If you have found ways of soldiering on despite the gloom all around, do write to us. Stay in and stay safe!

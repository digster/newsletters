A bank run and the subsequent fall of Silicon Valley Bank (SVB) in mid-March brought the spotlight back on India’s offshore financial hub, GIFT City.
 
Indian startups that availed SVB’s banking services sought other options, and many pitched GIFT City as an alternative, citing its numerous advantages. The special economic zone (SEZ) lets businesses carry out transactions in globally accepted currencies such as the US dollar, providing special tax incentives for setting up offices. Not even Goods and Services Tax is levied on services provided to and received from GIFT-registered units. Plus, there is also a 10-year income tax holiday.
 
Rounak, the author of today’s story, travelled to Gujarat to take stock of how this hub—about 30 km north of the Ahmedabad airport—modelled after Singapore and Hong Kong fares on the ground.
 
Even though GIFT City’s construction began in 2012, it kept running into regulatory hurdles, and the Gujarat government eventually took over the development in 2018. And the turning point in its journey came when the International Financial Services Centres Authority (IFSCA), the offshore zone’s unified regulator, took charge in 2020.
 
Since then, GIFT-registered firms have risen from 129 to 450 in February 2023 with more than $240 million in investments committed, and over 20,000 jobs generated, thanks to big corporations—such as MUFG Bank, Standard Chartered Bank, insurance firm ICICI Lombard, and fund manager HDFC AMC—and various aircraft- and ship-leasing companies, all setting up shop inside the SEZ.
 
But a host of factors are holding businesses back from making the most of the government’s gift of incentives, and that’s reflecting on the ground.

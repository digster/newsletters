Trade Tricks
Tue, 29 Apr 25
A weekly newsletter about the biggest changes in commerce—focusing on shifts that matter to you..
Good Morning Ishan,
It was very late in my life—my 20s, to be specific—that I ran into the love of my life: Kolkata biryani. A college mate introduced me to it and I quickly fell for the aromatic flavours of chicken served with a little surprise of aloo (potatoes) and anda (eggs).
But that was in 2017. Once I graduated from college, I lost my best supplier. No longer would Zarine, my college batchmate, ferry a handi (pot) full of biryani on her plane rides back from her Kolkata home.
Last year though, my long-suppressed craving for aloo-strewn biryani reached a peak, and I caved.
I searched for Kolkata biryani on Zomato.
That was my first encounter with Biryani By Kilo, an internet-first biryani brand that serves some of the most popular types of biryanis in India: Hyderabadi, Lucknowi, Kolkata. It didn’t take long for me to become a major contributor to the Rs 300 crore total sales the company recorded in FY24.
Fast forward to now, and Sky Gate Hospitality—the company that houses Biryani By Kilo, and four other fast-food brands—is being acquired for a little over Rs 500 crore by one of India’s largest fast-food majors, Devyani International. Devyani, which recorded sales of Rs 3,100 crore in FY24, intends to disinvest two Sky Gate brands, but will keep Biryani By Kilo, Goila Butter Chicken, and The Bhojan, according to its [investor presentation](https://www.bseindia.com/xml-data/corpfiling/AttachHis/eb26ca87-aab8-454d-9d2a-27d46739ab13.pdf).
Not only is the deal one of the first where a legacy quick-service restaurant (QSR) operator has acquired a homegrown fast-food brand, it is also one of the largest in the space.
What gives?
Why does a fast-food giant that has cut its teeth with pizzas, burgers, and fried chicken think biryani should be its next big pan-India bet?
No one who is familiar with India’s food scene has any doubts that biryani is one of the biggest—if not the biggest—dishes on the market. But it has always been an extremely fragmented segment, dominated by local chains and unorganised players.
“The biryani market commands a pretty large TAM of about $3–4 billion, but it’s still a difficult market considering that taste buds for biryani differ across different regions,” said Devanshu Bansul, a research analyst at Emkay Global.
In fact, pizza is the largest category in India’s organised fast-food market, even if it is Indian takes on pizza that are the best-sellers. Peppy Paneer, for instance, is the top selling item for Domino’s, according to Sameer Khetarpal, CEO of Domino’s franchisee Jubilant Foodworks.
According to Biryani By Kilo co-founder and co-CEO Vishal Jindal, though, biryani and pizza are the two most “[delivery-friendly](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4qHrMGh8KV0)” dishes in the world.
Which is probably what caught Devyani’s attention. “The company (also) acquired the brand at an attractive valuation, that too in an equity-swap deal, so investors are happy with it. It’s a favourable gamble,” noted Bansul.
For Devyani, 40% of its revenues comes from delivery, while for India’s #1 QSR operator—Jubilant Foodworks—this figure is 70%.
Mohammad Malik, partner at Vector Consulting, says it makes a lot of sense for such QSR giants with a heavy portfolio of western brands to “Indianise” their menus even more. For instance, if a customer is ordering a pizza or a taco 1.5X a month, adding biryani to your offering could increase that frequency to 1.7–2X a month, according to a venture capital investor who did not want to be named.
But this is hardly the first time that a large QSR operator has attempted to build a pan-India biryani chain. Jubilant Foodworks once incubated a brand called Ekdum Biryani to tap into India’s Rs 15,000 crore biryani market, but was unable to scale it.
As per Jindal, Jubilant was probably not able to crack it because they did not really understand biryani as a product. In an [interview](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4qHrMGh8KV0) to Raj Shamani, an influencer/entrepreneur, Jindal said that Jubilant had got the playbook for pizzas from Domino’s and were successful in replicating that, but they had nothing of the sort for biryani.
After all, for any QSR business, standardisation is the name of the game.
But how do you standardise a dish whose recipes change shape every few hundred kilometres?
Venture capital-backed internet-first brands like Biryani By Kilo have spent millions of dollars on exactly this. For instance, the company has managed to reduce the kitchen preparation time (KPT) for biryanis from 1.5–2 hours to just 10–30 mins, according to a founder of a food brand who didn’t want to comment publicly about a rival.
Usually, cloud kitchens have one centralised hub where they prepare everything from the curry to the rice, freeze it all, transport it to the decentralised kitchen, de-freeze the dish, and then re-heat it before delivery. Biryani By Kilo claims it has a longer-than-average KPT of 25 minutes for Biryani because they cook “fresh”. Basically, even though they source defrosted curry from their central kitchens, the rice and curry are layered and cooked together in local cloud kitchens instead of the entire dish being re-heated.
Now, food enthusiasts may argue that real biryanis take about two hours to cook from scratch. But the venture-capital investor quoted above begs to differ. Biryani, they claim, can be prepared even quicker than pizzas, just like how many local biryani restaurants have done all these years—cook everything in advance and then reheat portions when you need to sell them.
But will such standardised recipes manage to sate India’s fractured tastes?
The proof is in the… biryani.

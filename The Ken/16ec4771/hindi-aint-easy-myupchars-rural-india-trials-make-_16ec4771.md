For more than 40% of India's population—the Hindi-speaking population—up until late 2016, there was no credible content online to answer basic questions on health. Until myUpchar came along.
A simple health content app that would take on questions about health and answer in medical terms - without telling them to eat a fruit, shave their heads or something just as absurd. The bar was pretty low.
This year, with 12 million pageviews a month, myUpchar has grown its portfolio to chat-consultation with doctors, drug delivery, and home collection of diagnostic samples. Spaces that established 'English content' platforms such as 1mg and Pharmeasy already occupy. At least in metros.
And these players, who already have 50X more orders than myUpchar, have begun translating their existing content into Hindi.
Tricky.
myUpchar has an ace up its sleeve though. A dedicated Hindi readership that it can tap into to market its other offerings. It has already run trials in parts of north India, and failed due to broken infrastructure and the cost of serving these parts. It's now trying again, with its own suite of services and centres.
That myUpchar has had the room to stumble and get back up isn't something others can afford. Especially with time running out to reach the 600 million new internet users—mostly in regional languages—brought in thanks to the advent of low telecom tariffs. The Reliance Jio revolution.
Happy Monday. And nope, I'm not translating that.

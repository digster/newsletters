It's not very often that a company in the Indian food-tech space (Swiggy and Zomato aside) makes headlines in the pink pages.
 
Rebel Foods did just that in the past few months. Not because it raised $125 million in its latest funding round, but because of the names pumping money into the cloud kitchen company.
 
Besides the regular VC and PE names, there was the Indonesian ride-hailing company Gojek. Interesting! Then a couple of weeks back, it emerged that Travis Kalanick, Uber's mercurial founder and former CEO, had also invested.
 
Kalanick, in his post-Uber entrepreneur avatar, runs a real-estate company that has a unit (CloudKitchens) which rents out fully-functional kitchen spaces to restaurants. The plot thickens!
 
But wait. Why does India's biggest cloud kitchen company need such strategic investors? After all, Rebel Foods, or Faasos as it was called previously, sits at the top of the cloud kitchen mountain in India - it has 11 brands, 260 cloud kitchens in 17 cities and processes 2 million orders a month. The competition is hardly a threat.
 
Ironically, its scale could turn out to be its limiter, the hurdle in its path to profitability. "I don't see how scale helps cloud kitchens in the Indian context. I'm not sure there's a path to profitability with scale," says one investment banker.
 
It was Rebel Foods' fourth pivot, back in 2016, that made it into a full-fledged could kitchen. A pivot that co-founder and CEO Jaydeep Barman had hoped would lead to profits in 2018. That didn't happen.
 
But the aim is still there. Rebel Foods should be profitable in "a few years," says one investor.

If you travel across the countryside, in Indonesia or elsewhere in Southeast Asia (SEA), it is likely you will come across emerald-green rice fields on your journey. But did you know flooding these paddy fields causes fermentation and releases methane gas into the atmosphere? 
 
Wavemaker Impact (WI), a new type of venture builder in the region's climate-tech sector, is trying to build an enterprise with an aim to help rice farmers shift to a sustainable cultivation method.
 
Another venture builder trying to expand the pool of such startups is Ecoxyztem. It joins an enterprise like a co-founder to help accelerate the firm’s growth. In addition to capital, Ecoxyztem provides hands-on guidance to entrepreneurs. 
 
WI and Ecoxyztem are among the few venture builders in the sector. They have mushroomed because of rising investor interest, with giants like the Singaporean state-backed fund Temasek and SEA-focussed venture-capital (VC) firms such as East Ventures and AC Ventures keen to back climate-tech startups.
 
But these venture builders are discovering there are only a few viable opportunities in the region. And most of the funding at the moment is directed towards two sub-sectors: renewable solutions and e-mobility.
 
Going against the tide, WI and Ecoxyztem want to diversify climate-tech startups beyond the two verticals, making them more attractive to early-stage investors. In that regard, both have made a promising start. 
 
In her deep-dive story, Nadine points out that despite the promise, these venture builders need to align incentives among the founders, VCs, and their venture-building programme—before their own businesses can take off.

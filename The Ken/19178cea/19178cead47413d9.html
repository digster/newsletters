P2P lending has a split personality. And the RBI wants to cure this
By Arundhati Ramanathan
6 min read
View in browser

Good Morning Ishan,

The banking regulator is once again back with another episode of Curb Your Enthusiasm: Fintech Edition (whaddup Larry David fans). And this time, the RBI’s eyes are on peer-to-peer (P2P) lending. 

In India, P2P lending has been suffering from a case of multiple personality disorder. Is it a loan? Is it an investment? Is it both? Can it be both?

On the face of it, it seems to be a loan product. P2P companies like Faircent, Lendbox, Liquiloans are, after all, marketplaces of lenders and borrowers. They are meant to provide lenders with information about borrowers, after which they carry out the instructions of the lender: which borrower gets how much and at what interest, coordinating repayments, etc.. In return, the platform pockets a fee.

But when P2P lending was sold like this, it barely saw any growth. For a long time, these were marginal fintechs, sitting on the sidelines of the fintech explosion. No unicorns, no fancy investors dropping a big cheque, no large scale to boast of.

But during Covid, things took a turn. P2P companies led by those like Liquiloans decided to change things up. They started working with fintechs like Bharatpe and Cred, and they managed to switch around the narrative. 

P2P was no longer seen as a lending product but an investment.

The Lender-Investor

Take Cred Mint, for instance, which says its “investors” can get “returns” “up to 9%”. Most P2P platforms today do something similar. 

The apps of these marketplaces are found in the investment category of app stores. Not the loan category. 

And even the language in which these companies refer to the product is investment-centric. Lenders are called investors; they refer to risk, liquidity, guaranteed return. They don’t talk about it the way one would for a lending product, where you’d be hearing things like Cibil scores, principal, interest, or days past due. 

The P2P loans were even sold mostly by mutual fund distributors.

“We relied on mutual fund distributors because that was the best way to reach investors. It was a complicated product and the investors had trusted the distributor’s word.”

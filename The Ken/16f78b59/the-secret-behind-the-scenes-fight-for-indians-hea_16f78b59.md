“I know a few doctors from India who go to Africa for cataract surgeries [as part of camps] and collect data on their devices. You see a shift to the new currency—data,” says the founder of a medical device company towards the end of today’s story. The doctors are harvesting data from African patients because no clear laws prevent them from doing so.
The same is true in their home country, too. But unfortunately for them, the scale of that data harvesting game has become too big to play in. India’s leading hospitals and healthcare chains like Aravind Eye Hospital, Apollo Hospitals, LV Prasad Eye Institute, Sankara Nethralaya and Narayana Nethralaya are all working with US Big Tech firms like Google and Microsoft to share various kinds of patient data in return for a sip from the holy grail - AI.
Forus Health, a 9-year-old Bengaluru startup, makes high-resolution retinal cameras. Some of them send back photos of patient retinas to Microsoft AI, which then screens them for a particular eye disease called diabetic retinopathy.
In August, Microsoft touted having screened over 200,000 Indians for cardiovascular diseases via its “AI-powered API across Apollo Hospitals”. In all probability, this was Microsoft’s cloud AI scanning Apollo Hospitals’ “dark data” residing in unstructured PDFs to find symptoms. In December, Google published a deep learning paper referring to 600,000 chest X-rays it studied via Apollo Hospitals.
200,000 and 600,000 are rounding errors in the Indian context. The potential market is 1.3 billion. With the US and Europe being highly regulated around healthcare data and China being off-limits for American firms, India is where the large opportunity lies. Indian hospitals and healthcare chains are becoming conduits for Big Tech to access large amounts of high-value patient data sans any sort of legal obligations or protection. Their pitch to most Indian chains is “Come for the cloud computing, stay for the AI.”
But who owns this data? Because privacy is not the only issue. The value of the UK’s National Health Service (NHS) data repository (its primary care records go back decades) on 55 million people is estimated to be £9.6 billion ($12.67 billion) annually, according to the audit firm Ernst & Young. What might India’s be?
Microsoft refused to talk. Google refused to talk. Forus refused to talk. Apollo Hospitals refused to talk.

An abridged, narrative version of the latest episode of Two by Two, The Ken’s premium weekly business podcast.
Good Morning Ishan,
“Don’t think of India as a one- or two-year trade. This is an investment you should be making for your grandkids.”
Rohit Chopra said this so casually that for a second I forgot he was talking about a country and not a retirement plan.
I was born in the early 2000s. For as long as I have been conscious of the news playing in the background of my house, India’s moment has been coming. It was coming in 2005 and then it was coming in 2015. It was also definitely coming by 2025.
I do not have billions of dollars to deploy across global markets. I have small savings sitting in a bank account that earn just enough interest to feel like something is happening when, in reality, nothing is. But this is not just an investing question for me anymore. It is a career question and a life question. About which industries will matter in ten years, which companies will exist and what skills will be worth having when AI becomes infrastructure.
So when Rohin Dharmakumar and Rahel Philipose sat down with Rohit Chopra for this AMA-style episode of Two by Two, I was listening less like a producer and more like someone who has accidentally found herself at a coffee shop table with a man whose actual job is to decide if India deserves his clients’ money.
[Rohit Chopra](https://www.linkedin.com/in/rohit-chopra-2b7270/) is a portfolio manager and analyst at Lazard Asset Management, which manages somewhere between $260 and $270 billion globally. His clients are pension funds, sovereign wealth funds, and endowments. Indian by origin and American by profession, he has spent 26 years investing across global emerging markets. Which means he is comparing today’s India not to its past, but to China in 2005, South Korea in the 90s, Brazil in the 2010s, and asking where in that arc we really are.
Rohin asked him what he is actually looking for when he picks companies across markets, and the framework Rohit uses is worth understanding even if you are nowhere near his scale. He thinks in two buckets.
The first is what he calls “Compounders.” These are businesses with durable competitive advantages, companies that the market underestimates will keep growing for much longer than expected. The second is “Mispricings,” the companies where the market is punishing the stock harder than the actual business deserves.
On that second category, he was surprisingly direct: “The market may be over-discounting the magnitude of disruption. There are opportunities where the risk-reward is quite interesting.” He was talking about a sector that has had a brutal few months—one that a lot of people my age are watching nervously for personal reasons, not just financial ones. I will let you figure out which one when you listen.
We spend a lot of time in this episode talking about what disruption actually looks like when you are managing money for people whose retirement depends on you. AI, of course comes up briefly, but more interestingly, so does the idea that the foundations built over the last decade are finally being used in ways nobody predicted.
And then, towards the end, Rohit mentions one industry he is quietly and specifically bullish on. It is not manufacturing, nor fintech. It’s an industry you have probably already spent money on this year without thinking of it as an investment theme.
What I noticed by the end was this. Rahel, who is turning 29, was asking the same questions I had walking in: which sectors are real and what does it mean for someone trying to build a career here. Rohin, who has been covering Indian business for decades, was pushing just as hard on the FII (foreign institutional investor) outflows, the China comparison, and whether Indian companies are actually investing enough in the future.
If you have ever wondered how global emerging markets look when you zoom in on India with a slightly colder lens, this AMA is a pretty good place to start.

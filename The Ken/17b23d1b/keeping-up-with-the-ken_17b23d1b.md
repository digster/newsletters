Have you tried adding black salt to your green tea?
Of late, I have developed a habit of drinking a store-bought, local green tea that contains, among several herbs, black salt. It helps me resist that urge to add a little sugar to my otherwise light-on-calorie tea.
It is a welcome change in my routine. Usually, I am stubbornly opposed to change. It often overwhelms me, pushes me out of my painstakingly designed comfort zone.
Do you often, like me, feel that the world is changing so fast that you cannot keep up with it? Perhaps news can offer you some respite. It’ll tell you—look here, this is what is happening. This is what is likely to happen.
The past week’s stories at The Ken have played their roles as messengers of change, rather beautifully.
The Life Insurance Corporation of India (LIC), the company from which you perhaps purchased your first term insurance policy, is going public. Seetharaman and Anand’s analysis, published on Monday, write about why LIC’s promoters—the Indian government—need to
[avoid the mistakes](https://the-ken.com/story/why-india-shouldnt-repeat-irctcs-valuation-misstep-with-lics-ipo/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter) they made with the listing of IRCTC. After all, LIC could end up being the third-most valuable listed company in India after Reliance Industries and Tata Consultancy Services.
On Tuesday,
[Bhumika wrote a toy story](https://the-ken.com/story/indias-made-in-china-toy-story-gets-local-makeover-but-plot-holes-abound/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter). No kidding. Did you know that the world’s largest toy shop—Hamleys, is thinking of experimenting with a “made-in-India only store”? The country has a rich tradition of wooden toys but imports 80% of its toys from China. And the Indian government wants to change all that.
What has really changed or has come full circle is the market value of an Indian software engineer. Sumanth's lucid story on Wednesday answers a question that has been on our minds for some time now—why are startups
[luring software engineers with BMW superbikes](https://the-ken.com/story/indias-tech-crunch-the-battle-for-tech-talent/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter)?
At the same time, are API (active pharmaceutical ingredient) companies the golden geese of the pharma sector on the stock market now? With Glenmark Life Sciences listing on the stock market on 6 August, Seema writes how the
[tide has lifted all the API boats](https://the-ken.com/story/glenmark-laurus-labs-head-to-the-china1-finish-line-on-different-tracks/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter), in our Thursday story.
Closing out our week was Pranav and his deep dive into
[Reliance’s push to become the Next smartphone king](https://the-ken.com/story/reliances-offline-push-to-dethrone-the-online-smartphone-kings/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter). It not only wants to leverage its 8,000+ offline stores, but it also wants to onboard mom-and-pop mobile stores nationwide to its new online category—JioMart Digital.

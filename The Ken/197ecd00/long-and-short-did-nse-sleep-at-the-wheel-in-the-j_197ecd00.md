Long and Short
Wed, 09 Jul 25
The story behind the numbers and motivations driving India's stock market.
Good Morning Ishan,
“This (Jane Street index manipulation case) is essentially a surveillance issue,” said Tuhin Kanta Pandey, chairman of stock-market regulator Sebi on the watershed dominating business headlines since last Friday. “Surveillance mechanisms will be upgraded further to strengthen oversight,” Pandey added.
It’s not clear which body Pandey is referring to here. Whether to the National Stock Exchange (NSE), India’s premier stock exchange where Jane Street would have conducted the majority of its trades. Or to Sebi itself, where Pandey took charge in March. Reading between the lines of Sebi’s 105-page order on Jane Street though, it is likely the former. NSE may have much to answer for, even as it seems to be trying, quite hard, to project itself in a positive, proactive light.
In the meticulous interim order by whole-time member Ananth Narayan G, Sebi laid out in great detail how the US-based hedge-fund giant carried out elaborate, massive manipulation in India’s stock market and derivatives (options) trades since 2023. The regulator has now banned the Jane Street group from the Indian securities markets and ordered it to disgorge the over Rs 4,800 crore it made in unlawful gains—the largest such penalty ever imposed by Sebi. More could follow with the probe continuing.
The Ken had broken the story in January 2025 about a mystery fund playing God and wreaking havoc on India’s stock market. We followed this up with a story in June 2025 pointing to Jane Street as the all-powerful hand in India’s stock market. “Sebi’s action is vindication,” said Mayank Bansal, the Dubai-based options trader who had alerted The Ken about the rampant, large-scale manipulation.
Welcome as Sebi’s action is, the timeline of events and other details in the report raise uncomfortable questions about NSE’s role as a first-line regulator that’s supposed to highlight irregularities in the stock market.
Here’s the timeline in the Sebi report after it initiated its preliminary examination in April 2024 into Jane Street’s trading activity to ascertain any market abuse.
It’s striking that NSE had to be prodded into action by Sebi.
For instance, in July 2024, NSE was asked to examine the trading activity of the JS Group to ascertain if there was any market abuse involved. It then took NSE nearly four months—until November—to submit its report on the JS Group’s trading activities. Then, in February 2025, on Sebi’s instructions, NSE as a first line regulator issued a caution letter to Jane Street companies.
There’s also something not mentioned in the Sebi report that raises eyebrows. In April end, NSE is reported to have closed its probe into Jane Street trades flagged for unusual activity.
“The NSE told Jane Street’s local trading partner Nuvama Wealth Management Ltd. in a letter dated April 30 that the investigation has been closed following a response to an initial notice sent early this year, a copy of the letter reviewed by Bloomberg showed.”
Bloomberg report dated May 14, 2025 |
According to the Bloomberg report, the transactions in question involved rapid reversals of trades, sometimes at prices far above or below prevailing market rates.
Just over two months later, such transactions formed part of Sebi’s damning report on Jane Street.
Then, there’s also the question of why NSE, as the first-line regulator, did not alert Sebi to the big positions being taken by Jane Street as early as 2023. Sebi’s report analyses trades and illegal gains made by Jane Street on 21 options expiry days. Starting from August 2023, the list includes dates in October 2023 by when NSE had started disseminating delta-based open interest positions.
So, NSE would have or at least should have known about Jane Street’s big positions at least from the end 2023. These would have stuck out by their sheer size. “The manipulation should ideally have been spotted by the stock exchanges as first-level regulators in India. The biggest blame lies with the exchanges,” said a senior executive from the capital market. NSE is the behemoth exchange in India, with BSE only making inroads over the past couple of years.
Why was this not flagged way back then?
“There is conflict of interest,” said the senior executive quoted above. “On one side, you are regulating and on the other side, you are making money on the business. That is potentially the reason exchanges ignored this.” He added that if entities are in a conflicted position, many will do what is right for their business. Jane Street’s trades would have contributed massive volumes for NSE, and giving this up would have been difficult.
Especially when NSE has been trying hard to go public.
Stock exchanges should not have regulatory powers, said the senior executive, and if they function as first-line regulators, the idea of listing them is bad. “It’s like Sebi or the RBI going for a listing.” Profit should not be a motive for regulators, the senior executive said, adding that even allowing the BSE to list was not a good idea.
On its part, Sebi seems to have erred in waiting too long for the NSE to play its regulatory role effectively. When that didn’t happen, it had to step in itself. And once it did, Sebi had to tread carefully and come up with a watertight report and verdict—one that doesn’t run the risk of being quashed at the Securities Appellate Tribunal (SAT) where Jane Street is likely to appeal. There have been many instances in the past where Sebi’s rulings have fallen flat at SAT’s doors. The Jane Street case is too consequential for Sebi to risk that fate. A thorough, even if delayed, order is better than a quick one that doesn’t cover all bases, it seems to have thought.
Meanwhile, Sebi’s many curbs on options trading from last November— following its studies that more than 9 out 10 retail traders suffer losses—seems to have been born out of the urgency to stanch the bleeding until it was ready to go for a full-fledged operation. Tough choices, indeed.

Good Morning Ishan,
Since 31st March, we’ve been waiting for India’s largest insurer to make the announcement. LIC chief signalled on the 18th that it’d make its entry into health insurance official by the month-end. The stock jumped 7%, clawing back some of the ground it had lost over recent months.
Soon after, the United States Trade Representative (USTR) published its annual National Trade Estimate report, and among the litany of issues was LIC enjoying an “unfair competitive advantage” due to its sovereign backing. While the report—published right before Donald Trump’s reciprocal tariff move—wasn’t about the deal per se, the timing showed the behemoth’s moves are watched by the world.
As many as 860 million Indians don’t have any health insurance. It’s believed LIC could do to health insurance what Reliance Jio did to telecom in 2016.
Like Reliance, LIC has deep pockets to shake things up and make pricing irrelevant. In FY2024, it clocked Rs 8 lakh crore in revenue and a fiscal surplus of over Rs 90,600 crore. That’s a lot of money for actuarial courage. Also enough to subsidise premiums, which today range from Rs 5,000 to Rs 20,000 a year for individual plans and Rs 10,000 to Rs 50,000 for family floaters.
Above all, the insurer has a 1.4 million-strong agent network, which brings 95% of its business. That makes LIC’s entry into health insurance particularly interesting—because the same agents are already playing in that space.
So, can LIC pull off a Jio? The plumbing is done, but healthcare is not telecom. Sudeshna has [a fine read today](https://the-ken.com/story/lic-is-coming-for-health-insurance-reliance-jio-style/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter).
Trump’s latest announcement has wreaked havoc on products across the spectrum, including his outlandish 26% tariff on imports of Indian medical devices. Even by his own logic, the tariff doesn’t make sense: India imports way more medical devices from the US than it exports. Apart from hitting Indian medtech companies, this tariff will affect American companies like Abbott Laboratories and Thermo Fisher too—who are expanding manufacturing in India and ramping up exports. But the whole thing might just have a silver lining—if India lowers its own tariffs on medical devices, it’ll force Indian companies to invest in research and development of what could be high-end, globally competitive products.
In this week’s Long and Short, Seema writes how [Abbott India, Thermo Fisher, and Meril hit all-time highs, and then the medtech madness struck](https://the-ken.com/long_and_short/abbott-india-thermo-fisher-and-meril-hit-all-time-highs-then-medtech-madness-struck/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter).

Good Morning Ishan,
 
It’s a provocative question, isn’t it? 
 
Let’s look at the evidence. 
 
There are three levels involved. 
 
At the lowest level, Android is the most prevalent operating system in India, with a market share of over 98%. 
 
At the middle level, over 97% of all downloads happen through Google’s Play Store in India. 
 
At the highest level, Google owns 9 out of the top 30 recommended apps on the Play Store. 
 
In other words, Google dominates the entire stack in India at three crucial levels - discovery, distribution and destination. You could search the length and breadth of India’s 300 million mobile users, and you probably wouldn’t find a single person who hasn’t used or interacted with a Google product in their lifetime. 
 
It’s hard to explain Net Neutrality without using some imperfect metaphors involving highways, pipes, utilities or electricity. However, this is why we have the department of telecom - which states that ISPs cannot engage in practices such as “blocking, degrading, slowing down or granting preferential speeds or treatment to any content”.
 
As Arundhati notes in her story today, “if the Android-Play Store is to be looked at as an ISP, then Google violates all conditions of Net Neutrality. It blocks, it degrades, and it's placed in a position to favour content”. She makes her arguments across the three levels and poses a question to several key stakeholders in India: Should Google be subject to net-neutrality style regulations in India? 
 
Some say no. Some scoff. Others waffle. 
 
But one person thinks yes. Someone important who understands net-neutrality very well. He says:
 
“While we have ensured the pipes carrying data remain neutral, the problem is there are layers like operating systems and applications that are essentially faucets to the pipe. And they are important part of the network. So these faucets, too, end up becoming gatekeepers. If faucets are treating things differently, the basic philosophy of net neutrality, which is that all data should be treated equally, is compromised.”
 
Who said that?

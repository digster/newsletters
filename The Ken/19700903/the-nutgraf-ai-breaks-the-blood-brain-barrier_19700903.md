A couple of days ago, the messaging app Signal announced a big update.
If you’re using Signal Desktop on a Windows laptop and try to take a screenshot, here’s what will appear.
Don’t adjust your Wifi connection. It’s a black screen.
This is because Signal just added a “Screen Security” setting that blocks all attempts to take screenshots on the app.
If you’re like me, you’re probably thinking, “Yeah, I get it. Some chats are private, and Signal just doesn’t want people screenshotting without the other person knowing.” In fact, Signal is probably late to the game. Other messaging apps like Snapchat and Instagram send a notification to the other person if you try to take a screenshot of a private conversation. And these are chill and laid-back messaging apps. Signal is serious stuff—it’s used by people in the highest levels of government. You can never be sure [who is in your group chat](https://www.theatlantic.com/politics/archive/2025/03/trump-administration-accidentally-texted-me-its-war-plans/682151/) where you discuss war plans. This feature feels like it’s there to protect you from people you trust, but who might not actually have the best intentions.
By default, Signal has turned this block screenshot feature on for all users.

Later in the post, there’s a punchy metaphor that has really stuck with me. You can tell it wasn’t written by ChatGPT (emphasis mine).
“Take a screenshot every few seconds” legitimately sounds like a suggestion from a low-parameter LLM that was given a prompt like “How do I add an arbitrary AI feature to my operating system as quickly as possible in order to make investors happy?” — but more sophisticated threats are on the horizon.
The integration of AI agents with pervasive permissions, questionable security hygiene, and an insatiable hunger for data has the potential to break the blood-brain barrier between applications and operating systems. This poses a significant threat to Signal, and to every privacy-preserving application in general.
The blood-brain barrier is one of the human body’s most important ‘features’. At the most basic level, it’s a set of tightly packed cells that line the blood vessels in the brain and spinal cord. These cells form a barrier that controls what can pass from the blood into the brain. It protects the brain from harmful substances like viruses and toxins, and lets in important stuff like glucose and oxygen. So the human blood can get infected, and the brain can still function. Separation of church and state. Chinese walls. You get the analogy.
A similar barrier existed in the tech world.
Fundamentally speaking, the internet was built on the principle that if you’re a user, the websites and apps were destinations and the operating system and the browser were the means to get to them. However, over time, the companies who built the stuff in the middle started to get a bit restless. Some of them asked, “What if we could encroach a little bit more into the space between users and their destinations? Wouldn’t that be amazing?”
At first, the encroachment took the form of some harmless trespassing—specifically with discovery. Most of the people in the middle said, “The internet has a lot of things. We’ll help you find out what you like and then you can head over to the destinations.” Google built the best way for you to search for what you wanted. Then it built a browser. Facebook (now Meta) figured out how to build feeds that showed you what your friends were reading and sharing, so you could just click on them and go to Buzzfeed or whatever website you wanted to read it on. All ways to help you find out what you wanted to read, watch, or listen.
After some time, all of these companies got bored with becoming discovery platforms, so they went further and asked a question—could they become destinations themselves
The destination era is what we’re in right now. Facebook has stopped becoming a place where you went and discovered cool stuff to read or watch elsewhere—it’s become a place where you read and and watch stuff on Facebook. Creators upload their videos on Instagram. You watch it on Instagram. You never leave the platform. Same with Youtube. Twitter’s algorithms punish you if you add links. And when was the last time you found something interesting on, say, Linkedin and then followed a link to go somewhere else to read it? Discovery platforms became destinations. And this quiet invasion took years. A few years ago, if you wanted to learn about, say, the blood-brain barrier, you’d go to Google and search ‘blood-brain barrier wikipedia’, and simply click on the first link. Now, you just have to search blood-brain barrier and Google search pulls up stuff and displays it on their page—and you never leave Google.
When platforms became destinations, they starved websites and apps of what they’d promised they’d always deliver—traffic and users.
Now, with the rise of agentic AI, platforms are looking to take things a step further.
They want to devour the internet.
If you think about it, this is the natural final stage. Like the story of the camel in the tent, companies such as Google, Microsoft, and others first helped you find information, then encouraged you to stay within their platforms, and now they’re taking everything the internet offers, chewing it, and spitting it back to you.
The eating of the internet began with stuff like reading everything that was there on the ‘open’ internet. Soon, they hit limits. So they started engulfing stuff that wasn’t that open, and wasn’t theirs to take. Books. Research papers. Last year, The New York Times [reported](https://www.nytimes.com/2024/04/06/technology/tech-giants-harvest-data-artificial-intelligence.html) the extent to which AI companies went to achieve their goals. OpenAI surreptitiously stole Youtube videos and transcribed them and fed that data to its machines. Google changed its privacy policy so it could read Google documents that users created using its platform. Meta went after all your social posts. Everything was fair game. Everything was eaten.
And now, the platforms want more.
The next step isn’t content—it’s e-commerce.
Last week, Google demoed a new ‘Shop with AI’ [mode](https://techcrunch.com/2025/05/20/google-adds-ai-powered-shopping-features-for-discovery-and-easy-check-out/) which would help you, a user, to buy any product on the internet without having to visit the website that sold the product. The AI agent would put the product in your cart, keep an eye out for price changes, and then finally checkout and buy it on your behalf.
We’re now entering a world where apps and websites no longer have much of a reason to exist. They have fewer competitive advantages. Better UX? Doesn’t matter. Better shopping experience? Nobody will see it. Good-looking models (not the AI kind)? Invisible to machines.
In a world where machines are hitting websites and apps to collect data and take actions to do things that human users used to do, why should anyone build a destination anymore? Why bother trying to do smart upsell strategies on your app? And why bother with a homepage? Or with any kind of curation? All of these are things that humans need. Machines don’t care.
How much longer before you can ask agents to pay your credit card bills on CRED, and you will never see the app with your own eyes? Or to buy something on Flipkart, without you ever visiting their website and never seeing their deals, offers, or promotions? We are getting closer to this future.
AI broke the blood-brain barrier.
Over the next few months, I predict that apps and websites will invest an extraordinary amount of time and resources into technologies that help them discriminate between users who are humans and machines who are acting at the behest of humans.
The walls are going to be built.
And it is not going to be easy.
Signal built this wall to protect itself from the machine by using one of the oldest technologies that existed on the interest—Digital Rights Management. Simply put, this was something that Hollywood companies fought for to protect themselves from piracy, because they didn’t want people simply taking a screen recording of something they were streaming. It was built by platforms to protect apps and websites from mischievous humans. So if Netflix streamed a movie, it would tell browsers that this was content that was protected under copyright and users couldn’t screengrab them.
And last week, Signal deployed this old weapon to protect itself not from humans, but from machines.
But for how long?

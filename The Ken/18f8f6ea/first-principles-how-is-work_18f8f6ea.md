Your Sunday newsletter on mental models and leadership, seasoned with insights from some of India’s most disruptive thinkers.
What’s your first instinct when someone asks you “How’s work?” or “How’s business?”.
Take a minute to think about it.
I don’t know your answer, but I’ve always had this reflex tendency to respond with “Great!”. It’s what’s expected as an answer, right? I mean, we have to be doing great. Why are we even in the game if we’re not… winning?
Imagine, for a minute, someone who answers “Not so great” or “Sort of at a standstill”. Who says stuff like that??
But then, if you think about it, we do go through stages ever so frequently which are best described as “not so great”, right?
A few days ago a good friend messaged me after a few years. As you can guess, he asked me, “How’s work?”
And I caught myself pre-typing “Great!” in my mind, but thankfully decided to not actually type it out.
Instead, I wrote back: “Okay. In the midst of one of those cycles where we’re building/adding another layer of “foundations”. In-app subscriptions. More podcasts. Redesign. New plans. I think wiring all of them together will lead to the next wave of growth and innovation. I’ve learnt to see things like a garden/farm. Gotta go through annual cycles of tilling, sowing, watering, growth, harvesting, etc. 😊”
I felt strangely satisfied at having articulated myself in this manner. And in becoming more consciously aware of the fact that one of the best ways to look at our professional lives—whether as employees, entrepreneurs, or leaders—is farming.

“Work is not work, work is a mission.”
This line was on the first page of the research note that my colleague Hari Krishna had prepared for me as background for my[ First Principles podcast](https://the-ken.com/podcasts/first-principles) conversation with Manav Garg, founder and former CEO of Eka Software. I say former CEO because Manav moved out from Eka recently after having sold his company following twenty years at its helm.
We had a wonderful and wide-ranging conversation, because it’s not often you find someone who started a company, ran it for 20 years, sold it, and jumped right back into starting… a[ venture fund](https://together.fund/). But let’s save that for later.
Instead, I want to call your attention to something Manav said a few years ago. That “work is not work, work is a mission”.
I could not help noticing how similar it is to a much-used quote that describes farming.
“Farming is not just a job, it is a way of life.”
I immediately connected that to my own response to the question, “How’s work?”
There are hundreds, possibly thousands of articles and social media posts about the similarities between entrepreneurship and farming. Because farmers tend to plant, nurture, harvest, and well, work hard, it’s easy to romanticise (“entrepreneurise”?) the things they do.
- They prepare the ground, which is like creating the right conditions for growth and innovation.
- They plant seeds, which is like seeding and cross-pollinating ideas or hiring people
- They irrigate and fertilise, which is like developing your people and organisation
- They remove weeds, which is like removing obstacles
You get the idea. Beyond a point, the analogies seem, well, glib and social media influencer-ish.
But it’s not what the farmer does that speaks to me. Instead, it’s what the farmer doesn’t. Or rather, can’t.
Seasons.
That’s right. All farming depends on the weather and seasons. Regardless of whether a crop is being planted for the summer (“Rabi” in India), winter (“Kharif”), or in-between (“Zaid”), they are fully dependent on weather conditions.
Where am I going with this, you must be frustratedly wondering?
Imagine asking a farmer who’s just planted his crop, “How’s the crop?” Or asking one who’s just finished harvesting his last crop a month ago and is currently letting his field lie[ “fallow](https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/the-three-stages-of-fallow/)”, “How’s the field?”
Do you think you’ll get “Great!” as an answer from either? Of course not. They know that their fields spend most of their time in a state which to the ignorant eye can seem doubtful and risky.
So?
So, it means that we need to recognise that growth isn’t a perennial reality. It follows stages where we, figuratively, tilled the soil, planted seeds, watered the fields, waited for the rain, added fertiliser, removed the weeds, etc.
Every business and every career has these stages too. We can’t always be growing all the time. We need to till, plant, water, tend, fertilise, etc., too.
During those times, we aren’t reaping. But that’s okay, because we know we’re getting ready for it eventually.
At The Ken, too, we’ve learnt to observe and (grudgingly) appreciate the “seasons” and stages of growth.
Conversely, if you’re attached to just reaping the rewards but haven’t made peace yet with the groundwork, then you’ll struggle like I did for years when people asked me, “How is business/work?”

Aman Resorts, One&Only, and Six Senses are all luxury hotel chains that have two things in common. First, all of them were founder-led. Second, all three founders lost control of what they had built.
The reason? They over-leveraged themselves financially, says Kapil Chopra, founder of Postcard Hotels. Kapil has ambitious plans for the hotel company—he wants to build a strong founder-led chain, and he intends to do it fast. Postcard already has nine locations functioning right now and another 23 soon to open.
Naturally, you’re bound to think that financing all of these expensive hotels—most Postcard Hotels are built from scratch—wouldn’t be cheap. Couldn’t they also end up over-leveraging themselves? But Kapil says Postcard is structured in a way that ensures the founder would never lose control.

Lazy endings
Anurag Gupta wrote in with this spot-on feedback about the way I host the First Principles podcasts.
A quick feedback. I am an absolute FAN of yours and the First Principles Podcasts. I thought of giving you this feedback many times in the last few months…but kept dragging it, and finally today I thought I must do.
First of all, the way you interview the startup founders is just incredibly good. You begin the Podcast well, sort of “invite” the guest (on the Podcast) very very warmly, full of passion & energy etc. BUT the way you end your Podcast is extremely sudden…just a line, that too said very perfunctorily….sort of in low-energy, low passion mode. Just listen to the ending of a few of your podcasts…take Part 2 of Jaydeep Burman’s episode as an example…and you will understand EXACTLY what I mean. And I am PRETTY SURE you are NOT at the fag end of your battery (read energy) levels…it is perhaps just one of those things you may not have realised!!
He’s absolutely right. Endings are usually the Achilles’ Heel of writers and journalists. We spend so much time thinking about introductions, chapters, transitions, callbacks, etc., that by the time we’re at the end, we sort of just slip-slide into it.
Thanks Anurag for this frank assessment. I’ll look to fix this. 🙂

“Aandhis” and helping strangers
Regular reader and contributor Hareesha wrote in with words, photos, and reflections.
“While the newsletter has become a compulsive weekend habit, sharing a response often takes longer as reading the linked articles and satisfying my curiosity on the various subjects takes another 2 – 3 days, often extending beyond. Goes without saying that the journey from reading the newsletter, and just opening the linked articles in various tabs, to reading up has become an enriching experience, often making me think beyond.
Delhi so far, has had a surprisingly kind summer, with an occasional shower and aandhi helping to rein in the heat. But the bigger joy is the trees which continue to be in full bloom. What used to dry and wither away by the end of March/early April is now continuing to adorn the streets of Delhi even in May. It might well be a butterfly effect of global warming, but atleast this one is a treat for sore eyes.
Showing up, consistently, is not just a habit but an art, especially if one is mentally involved in the situation and not just showing up for the heck of it. But dogged persistence of showing up, is often what tips the balances, and convinces the others about your conviction to a cause. But to me, the more impactful action (and it always has to be an action, since we could be thinking endlessly without acting on anything), which is perfectly elaborated in [Stay in the Game](https://www.albertbridgecapital.com/post/stay-in-the-game) is Helping Strangers. While this shouldn’t be at the cost of ignoring your own loved ones, helping strangers is one of the most rewarding actions. It not only improves once self worth, it often builds relationships in most unexpected situations and places.
While Drew Dickson wrote the piece to explain how showing up consistently often changes the course of your life, getting help from strangers in unexpected shapes and forms was my take away from the piece. It is also true that we find it easier to accept help from strangers than from our own family and friends, maybe because we don’t perceive the act as an outcome of pity.”

Good Morning Ishan,
As I read my colleague Suprita’s story today, I recalled a conversation with a senior executive at one of India’s largest mutual funds around August 2024, when Ola Electric had started trading on India’s stock exchanges.
The way he talked about Bhavish Aggarwal, it seemed he certainly didn’t want to get a beer with the chief executive of Ola Electric. But he admitted that Aggarwal’s vertically integrated vision for electric vehicles was “brilliant”. Even if he found Ola’s scooters inferior to Ather Energy’s.
He said Ather was the Royal Enfield to Ola’s Hero Motocorp. (Ironically, Hero is Ather’s largest shareholder.)
Ather is, in more ways than one, Ola’s antithesis. So, if investors have soured on Ola since the listing, there’s reason for them to be enamoured by Ather. Except they aren’t.
Their response to Ather’s nearly Rs 3,000 crore IPO was lukewarm at best. The stock ended Tuesday, its first trading day, 6% lower than its issue price.
In a timely analysis, Suprita breaks down investors’ disenchantment and what the next year holds for Ather. [Give it a read](https://the-ken.com/ather-is-everything-ola-isnt-so-why-arent-investors-lapping-it-up/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter).
Oyo Hotels has reportedly postponed its IPO for the third time as its largest investor, Softbank, was hesitant about the timing. The company will now look to list by March 2026 at a reduced valuation of about $7 billion. But the problem is not when to take it public; it’s what exactly will be taken public, I write in this week’s Long and Short.

The old leader was smart, confident, and strong. The new one also has to be authentic. We’re seeing a polar shift in what people expect from leaders 
 By Ruhi Kandhari https://the-ken.com/writers/ruhikandharigmail-com/

Last year saw a radical redefining of what “executive presence” actually means. 

‘The new rules of Executive Presence https://hbr.org/2024/01/the-new-rules-of-executive-presence’ was one of the 2024’s most read https://hbr.org/2024/08/hbrs-most-read-articles-of-2024-so-far papers on the Harvard Business Review, and it talks about how people’s expectations from leaders have rapidly changed. The paper is quite extensive and covers a lot of ground, but for today’s edition of Inciting Incident, I’m going to ignore aspects like gravitas and appearance and zoom in on just one thing: communication traits.

Authenticity, which didn’t register with survey respondents 10 years ago, is newly prized. Nowadays, to be seen as leadership material, executives are expected to reveal who they fundamentally are—not mimic some dated, idealized model.

[…]

To show confidence and decisiveness but also inclusiveness and respect, to balance powerful speaking and a commanding presence with listening and learning, and to project both polish and authenticity is a heavy lift. But leaders who manage it can inspire their employees to greater achievements and help their organizations truly flourish.

The replacement of ‘forcefulness’ with ‘listen to learn’ and ‘joking and bantering’ with ‘authenticity’ is a polar shift in what executive presence means, and redefines the parameters of communicating as a leader. And this shift has come at a time when the need for leadership training remains deeply felt in India. 

In 2023, The Economic Times reported https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/jobs/mid-career/future-of-leadership-training-in-india/articleshow/103387274.cms that India’s corporate sector invests less than 2% in leadership education and development compared to up to 15% in the developed world, and that nearly 89% of executives prioritise improving organisational leadership. 

But in my experience leading The Ken Learning, where we conduct narrative thinking workshops https://the-ken.com/learning/narrative-thinking-for-leadership-transformation/ for executives across organisations, hundreds of leaders have expressed a keen desire to acquire skills such as story listening and storytelling in order to infuse their organisations with more authentic expression https://the-ken.com/learning/case-study/journalistic-storytelling-is-a-vital-skill-for-the-leaders-of-tomorrow-they-just-dont-know-it-yet/. A clear sign that there’s a mismatch between what leaders are delivering and what is expected of them now. 

However, making this shift is neither simple nor easy for leaders. 

In another article published nearly two decades ago, Harvard Business Review noted https://hbr.org/2005/12/managing-authenticity-the-paradox-of-great-leadership how there is a paradox at the centre of this dynamic. How can a leader be who they are when they are also expected to be confident, decisive, and strong?

Leadership demands the expression of an authentic self. Try to lead like someone else—say, Jack Welch, Richard Branson, or Michael Dell—and you will fail. Employees will not follow a CEO who invests little of himself in his leadership behaviors. People want to be led by someone “real.” This is partly a reaction to the turbulent times we live in. It is also a response to the public’s widespread disenchantment with politicians and businesspeople. We all suspect that we’re being duped.

Authenticity in communication, presence, and behaviour is what sets apart great leaders, because ‘realness’ inspires trust. Their image is not sleek and airbrushed to perfection. Instead great leaders show sincerity, honesty, and integrity. 

But how do you do this while also making sure you don’t come across as weak, indecisive, or too direct? How can you be authentic in expressing your personhood and building trust while also maintaining your gravitas, another crucial trait, in order to influence and inspire?

Harvard Business Review’s paper uses five years of research to conclude that great leaders overcome this paradox by knowing which personality traits to reveal, and to whom and when. Authentic leaders, it states, know where they are going, but also where they came from. They empathise with those they want to influence while retaining their distinctiveness as individuals.

They are like chameleons, capable of adapting to the demands of the situations they face and the people they lead, yet they do not lose their identities in the process.

The paper also lists four pointers that can help leaders do this: manage perception by practicing what you preach; know yourself and others; use where you come from; and conform to social and organisational norms to a certain degree. It is this elusive cocktail of traits that marries authenticity to leadership. 

Another way of looking at it is defined in Kellogg Insight’s 2021 podcast, ‘Executive Presence—Do You Have It? https://insight.kellogg.northwestern.edu/article/podcast-do-you-have-executive-presence’. In the interview, Brooke Vuckovic, a clinical professor of leadership at Kellogg School, offers a simple formula: executive presence is equal to credibility plus ease, all divided by ego. 

To quote the podcast’s host Jessica Love:

Authenticity is about being yourself and normalizing that for others. It’s why executive presence will look different from person to person. It’s why you probably know people who have very different personalities, voices and leadership styles…but who are effective and believable in their own unique ways. Now, there IS another side to this. If you tend to let your anger dictate how you respond to people…that you WILL want to change. We’re not going to include that in the list of things that makes you authentically you. Because ease…is also about your ability to adaptively control your emotions when things get sticky. And that’s important because your demeanor can tell people whether you’re someone they can depend on in a pinch.

In short, a leader cannot express every personality trait and emotion in every situation; they need to be discerning. But they also need to make sure they remain true to themselves.

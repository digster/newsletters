Today's story reminds me of case interviews we had at B-school.
 
If you don't know how it works, it's simple. The interviewer poses a hypothetical situation of a company. For instance, he may ask, 'A company's profits have fallen by 10%'. Your job is to work out why. Profits are made up of two constituents—revenues and expenses. You ask questions about revenue channels. Eliminate all possibilities. Then you go into cost heads, one by one. Until you finally nail down the problem. It's an exercise done by top consulting companies to test your structured thinking, analytical skills and business acumen before they eventually decide to hire the person who has sucked up to them the hardest. That's what their clients reward too. It's a special skill. 
 
Let's do the same thing with Ola Fleet, the cab-leasing arm of Ola Cabs in India. Here's what the numbers of Ola Fleet look like in 2017, as compared to 2016. 
 
Revenue is up 17X. That's good. Excellent, even. So what's the problem?
 
Expenses are up 7X. Oh. Okay. Not too bad. Company must be profitable?
 
No. In fact, losses have increased 5X. 
 
What?
 
Exactly. This is why you should read Salman's story today. He goes into the problems that Ola Fleet is facing as they struggle to hold on to drivers while they flee the platform. Two-sided networks are fickle things, and Ola is witnessing what happens when the fickle turns into a frenzy. He breaks down the delicate balance between drivers and riders which Ola has been maintaining all this time. Today's story is about incentives on the driver side of Ola, and how parties behave when these incentives change. Most importantly, it's a story about impact and who gets affected. Fleet owners. Cab drivers. Ola. Uber. Meru. Salman talks to them all, and stitches it into a narrative to answer a crucial question that prospective consultants scratch their head over: What causes the supply side of a two-sided network to get crunched? What happens when it does?

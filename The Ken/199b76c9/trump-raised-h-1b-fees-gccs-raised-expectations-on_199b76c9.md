Good Morning Ishan,
When US President Donald Trump made the startling announcement of a $100,000 fee on fresh H-1B visas, Whatsapp groups lit up. Anxious parents refreshed immigration blogs, return flights to the US sold out within hours, and it was chaos all through.
Even as the fate of Indian employees working in American companies was being discussed threadbare, another set of employees working in the same American corporations suddenly seemed to have their moment.
Employees at Global Capability Centres or GCCs—the offshore units set up by multinational companies in India—have waited to climb the corporate ladder. Now, with the H-1B visa squeeze, if fewer Indian engineers could move onsite, logic says US companies like Walmart, JP Morgan, Target, and Lowe’s may lean harder on their massive India centres.
India has over 1,700 GCCs—most in Bengaluru. More than two-thirds of Fortune Global 30, and 174 Fortune 500 firms operate GCCs in India, according to GCC-solutions provider ANSR.
Originally meant as a back office to cut down on expenditure, GCCs, over the last four decades, have moved far beyond cost arbitrage. They’ve focused on product engineering, capability building, and even innovation, and with it, the executives also now have fancy titles.
But what about real decision-making?
Mrunmayee, in her [story](https://the-ken.com/story/trump-raised-h-1b-fees-gccs-raised-expectations-only-one-stuck/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter) today, lays out just how well India’s GCCs are placed to make the most of the H1B tightening.

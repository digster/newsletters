Good Morning Ishan,
 
I remember this kid. Tears rolling down his eyes, sobbing incessantly, clutching the yellow-coloured iron grille on the balcony of the third floor of the building, the topmost floor, staring at the book issued from the school library, now carelessly flung into the neighbours' narrow alley, strewn with trash and sewage, in a neighbourhood where making a living took priority over hygiene. 
 
Dad didn’t like story books. That’s what he called them: story book. 
 
He had found me with them, once, twice, a third time, and then more, till one day in a fit of rage—consumed by the thought that his child reads story books all day and does not study, and he will turn out to be the garbage that is young children in this neighbourhood, despite all the personal sacrifices and compromises he has made—he slapped me, took the book from my hands and flung it through the grille, from the balcony.
 
I remember the hurt and guilt simmering in me for days. How will I retrieve the book? The boundary wall is so tall, and I can’t go and tell the neighbours what happened. What will the librarian say? What if he looks inside and finds that the pages are all trashed? Why did dad do this, how can he do this?
 
This is one incident from my life which I can distinctly recall as increasing my resolve to read more story books. I found many things in me I had never known. Loss. Shame. Perseverance. Grit. The ability to fight back. I now see it clearly as a significant event of learning. 
 
In many ways, I believe our series The Simplicity Paradigm comes close. 
 
Over the last six months, the authors Saurabh Mukherjea and Anupam Gupta have covered a range of subjects. From the bias of thinking that you know too much by buying too many books and not reading any to several other prejudices that colour human thought. I remember first learning the word Tsundoku in their piece and then reading more on it. In the last six pieces, Saurabh and Anupam have examined how we live life through the lens of original thinking, spirituality and others like the idea of decluttering. 
 
I don’t think I will ever read as many books as the authors have or even come close to how they have gone about thinking through their daily lives and decisions. For that, I am grateful for their effort in making me think better.
 
Today’s piece is the final instalment of The Simplicity Paradigm.
 
The authors sum it well: “We believe that our ideas are works-in-progress that should be revisited every once in a while, with new ideas and beliefs. As writers, we believe in these ideas and have shared deeply personal details from our lives in the hope that our experiences are useful to the readers. In this final column, we wrap up the series and provide some final thoughts on the way forward.”

Good Morning Ishan,
The world’s most populous country with 1.4 billion people has—believe it or not—a baby problem.
India’s fertility rate is now below its replacement level—the rate at which a population replaces itself from one generation to the next.
Now, this is not a problem that’s unique to India. One in six people worldwide is facing infertility, and the problem is such that the World Health Organization (WHO) declared it a public health emergency last year.
But irony and crisis also spark opportunities and drive solutions. The demand for fertility treatments in the country is bouncing up and away, spawning in its wake a multi-billion dollar industry. Last October, The Ken wrote about [Indira IVF](https://the-ken.com/story/the-cost-of-building-a-billion-dollar-ivf-chain-in-india/), India’s largest fertility clinic chain, bagging the world’s second-largest investment deal in this space. Indeed, a big boom.
But trouble’s booming too. Private fertility clinics charge big bucks—running into lakhs of rupees—for these sophisticated treatments, putting them out of reach for most of India’s millions. To make matters worse, these long-drawn procedures with uncertain outcomes are not covered by medical insurance for the most part.
That’s causing worry among many powers-that-be. A slowing population plus unaffordable treatment can make for a deadly combination, including at the polls. The solution? “Free” fertility treatment at government hospitals. Some states, such as Maharashtra, have taken baby steps to roll this out, and others like Goa and Tamil Nadu plan to do so. Is it any surprise that “free” IVF treatment has also found its way into the election manifestos of some political parties?
But then, there is the million-dollar question that Shivani, the author of today’s deeply reported story, poses: “IVF treatments can break the bank. So, how are states offering it for free?” Is this all for real, or just a gimmick? Do government hospitals really have what it takes to pull off these complicated procedures? Is there really such a thing as a free lunch or a free IVF treatment? Are there exceptions that prove the rule?

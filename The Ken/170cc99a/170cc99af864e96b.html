An Amazon on every corner.

That’s a frightening—if maybe convenient—snapshot of a future that Jeff Bezos is gunning for in India. But Amazon isn’t about to open scores of brick and mortar shops. No. It’s reaching India’s nooks and crannies through kirana stores, the 12 million-plus mom and pop stores that account for the vast majority of the country’s retail market.

Amazon isn’t the only one to covet these local stores.

Hyperlocal companies Swiggy, Dunzo and conglomerates like Reliance—now also in e-commerce—are building partnerships. Point-of-sale companies and even electric bike startups, too, seek to modernise neighbourhood retail.

So what’s in it for your local store keeper exactly?

It’s too early to say. Stores are fragmented, and since their sizes vary, it is tough to standardise. Then the financial incentives also need to work.

E-commerce companies offer small amounts to store and deliver packages—it isn’t enough for some.

Grocery companies want to bring in more customers—but they appear to be developing their own ‘dark stores’ that could threaten business.

Payment companies want to digitise operations—but they are sucking in precious customer data, which rightly concerns shop owners.

The great digitisation of local stores is far from as simple as Bezos, or others, may imagine, and today’s story from Abinaya’s get right into why that is: https://the-ken.com/story/keys-to-the-kirana-store/ (10-minute read)

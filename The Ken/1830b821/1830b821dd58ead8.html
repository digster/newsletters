Good Morning Ishan,
 
Fintech startups in India have a running joke that the banking regulator, the Reserve Bank of India (RBI), can’t go a week without issuing a new circular for them. It comes as a surprise if it does.
 
The growing number of regulatory-compliance requirements in the country has sent the fintechs scurrying to Deloitte, EY, PwC, and KPMG—the Big Four of the auditing business. 
 
If the secret to getting rich in a gold rush is selling shovels, then the Big Four are selling advice to help these fintechs dig themselves out of multiple regulatory holes. 
 
Besides auditing and consulting, regulatory advice has become a new money-spinning vertical for the Big Four. EY, in particular, has been leading the charge. The overall advisory business in India has grown bigger than its auditing business. With a 23% year-on-year growth since 2019, advising clients and ensuring they comply with current policies is an attractive future path for the firm.
 
With the announcement of every new regulation, the consultancies send a note to their clients explaining its significance and impact. Depending on the services they are hired for, the consultancies then look at the client’s business and recommend changes to be fully compliant.
 
The opportunity is unique because they have the edge over both legal and top management consulting firms—McKinsey & Co, Bain & Co, and Boston Consulting Group (commonly referred to as MBB). This type of work is too resource-intensive for legal firms and too small a ticket size for MBB. 
 
In today’s story, Shashwat takes a deep dive into the emergence of this new vertical, which is built on the business of fear: https://the-ken.com/story/indian-fintechs-regulatory-anxiety-is-the-big-fours-next-lucrative-bet/ (8-minute read)

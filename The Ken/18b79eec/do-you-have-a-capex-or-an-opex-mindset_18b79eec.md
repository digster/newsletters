Good Morning Ishan,
I first met Kunal Shah, the founder and CEO of fintech CRED, in March this year. Though we’d communicated a few times earlier over email and text, we’d never met in person. We were meeting at a studio in Bengaluru’s Indiranagar to record an episode of First Principles.
Kunal rode up on a fairly ordinary scooter. He said it didn’t make any sense to drive a car just to get around Indiranagar (the CRED office is just a kilometre or two away). My first thought then: Kunal, in real life, came across as much more relaxed, equivocal, and organic than his online persona. I also noticed he liked using “boss” a lot in his conversations.
Earlier this week, I spoke to Kunal again for today’s edition. He addressed me as “boss” a few times during our chat. It felt like a continuation of our conversation from March.
I asked Kunal about the mental models he most often finds himself relying on when faced with important decisions at CRED.
He has two, he said.
First, “Don’t build something we wouldn’t offer to our friends and family—even if it made sense financially,” he said.
On the face of it, this was a principle I’ve seen numerous businesses profess. For instance, IDFC FIRST Bank applies something called a [“Near and Dear” Test](https://www.idfcfirstbank.com/about-us/our-bank) for its products “so that the employees of the Bank serve only such products they'd want for their own loved ones”.
How did this play out at CRED, I asked Kunal. Why was this so important to him?
“Because we want to build an enduring business that outlasts all of us. You’ll always figure out other ways to make money if you know how to make products and market them,” he said.
I sought from him a decision he had made guided by this principle.
“We’ve stayed away from offering trading of derivatives to CRED users, at least in the current form. Because nearly 90% of derivatives traders lose money, if you see SEBI [Securities and Exchange Board of India] data. It would be profitable for us but very hard to justify as something that is right for the customer,” Kunal replied. “I’m not saying people should not do those businesses,” he added.
And the second mental model?
“Avoid talent that would not give you operating leverage,” he replied instantly. “In everything we do, every business we enter, our focus is to improve revenue per employee. You can’t be a tech business if your revenue only grows with your headcount. So, we stay away from businesses that are operations-heavy, require lots of headcount, or are not tech-friendly. Our revenue per employee has always gone up, and if it doesn’t, we may have to take some hard calls one day. If you think about WhatsApp, it was built with [just] 30 employees and served over 1 billion users. That was solid operating leverage. ”
He switches to talk about a recent CRED feature that allows members to add their vehicle details to their profiles. Or, as CRED calls it, “park” their cars in “Garage”.
“We built Garage [personal vehicle management platform] in three weeks, and we’ve already got a million vehicles parked. It was built by nine people. In comparison, a full-service automobile firm might have a team of, say, 1000 people. Even in our lending business [Rohin: lending money to its users has become one of the most lucrative monetisation opportunities for CRED], our first principle was to aim for operating leverage by avoiding building a large call centre. We said we’d never call a customer. Instead, we’d get them to use our app,” he continued.
I told Kunal that every business would ideally want to have high operating leverage, if it could. But often, it all came down to the talent the company had. He had started by telling me he would “avoid talent” that didn’t give operating leverage. How did he judge potential employees on this dimension before hiring them?
“Simple. I check if they are opex talent or capex talent,” he replied promptly.
Now, opex (operating expenditures) and capex (capital expenditures) are financial concepts—typically not applied to people or talent. While the former are costs that companies incur to run their day-to-day operations, like rents, wages, fees, taxes, interest, etc., the latter are costs incurred to build or buy things that improve a company’s performance in the future, like computers, factories, IP, land, software, etc.
How was Kunal applying these concepts to people?
“You know [it] when someone has a capex mindset. They create playbooks, systems, tools, and tech that make their jobs more relevant. They are obsessive about getting more out of their time. They ask themselves, ‘Is the value they are delivering per hour going up?’ Most people don’t care about it,” he explained.

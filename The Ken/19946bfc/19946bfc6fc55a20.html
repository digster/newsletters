Dear Ishan, good morning. We’re slowly limping back to the sunny, crisp, and cool days of early winter in Bengaluru. I woke up on Monday morning to the brightest skies in a while. It was almost as if Sunday’s “Blood Moon” marked the end of the grey and rainy spell we’d been subject to for weeks.

It wasn’t just that. It rained so heavily on Sunday night that a giant branch of one of the Flame of the Forest trees right outside our community broke off and fell on the pavement. And since it’s flowering season, Gabru and I were greeted with a lush green canopy and indescribably bright flowers blocking our path early in the morning. It was like one of those scenes in Inception, when the buildings start to shift on their axes and become parallel to you!

And what do you do when life gives you a tree canopy on the pavement?

Well, if you’re Gabru, you promptly make it your own by marking it in three to four different places!

By now, we’ve befriended most of the street indies along our path. It took an email from reader Steve Armatas (which I’m taking the liberty of quoting below) for me to realise that there are many of us who aren’t afraid or wary of dogs, but perhaps have never thought of trying to gradually befriend them with offerings. 🙂

“I spent a month in Bengaluru for work a few years ago. I walked 20–30 minutes to the office every day and I remember all of the dogs that I would pass every day. They weren’t threatening, but not friendly either. I wish I had thought of bringing them treats like you did! I really enjoyed my time there; there was something magical about the city and the people I met there. Reading your article brought those memories back to life for me.”

My favourite treats are the Chip Chops diced chicken treats. They’re just the right mix of size (really small) and taste (irresistible). I carry about 12–15 of them in my pockets each morning, reserving two to three per dog that we meet. (In my head, I “expense” it as a price for my happiness each morning.)

It also took Steve’s email for me to realise that, yes, there is something magical about Bengaluru. We use a lot of words to describe the city—cool, laid back, gridlocked, welcoming, modern—but rarely, “magical”. Thanks Steve!

1. The Ken’s 2025 “Case Build” competition

Case-study competitions are so outdated that most of them feel like modern-day Kabuki dances. The method is dated. The cases are wooden. The contexts and conflicts are safe enough to not offend anyone. And the responses are mostly from LLMs.

When we launched our first-ever Case Study Competition last year, we tried our best to break all the constraints. “If you had a billion dollars to ‘win’ in quick commerce, what would you do? And who would you be?” we asked students.

This year, we’re making things even more fun. Instead of picking a sector, offering a list of companies, or even creating a construct, we’re inverting the paradigm and asking students, who would they disrupt?

Why should students always be at the receiving end of AI? If incumbents aren’t agile enough to spot their own weaknesses, why shouldn’t students armed with tools, drive, and insight be the new insurgents?

You can read more about the competition and register for it here: https://the-ken.com/case-competition-2025/

2. “Psychological Runway”

A very good friend sent me this post earlier this week from Daniel Kimerling, the founder of early-stage venture capital firm Deciens. I’d urge you to read it in full.

Why Great Things Take Decades

“I have long believed that accomplishing big, important things takes time, often decades. Building one of the best venture capital firms (with many successful, long-dated vintages) or creating category-defining companies both require patience and sustained effort. For every flash in the pan, there are many, many more ten-year overnight success stories. 

Nvidia, the first company valued at more than $4T, is a timely example. A cursory look at the history of the company’s stock price suggests that it could be considered a 30-year overnight success. All that matters is that they could survive, compound, and ultimately thrive, letting the market finally come to them. An incredible story of perseverance, fortitude, and leadership.”

The post is written from the point of view of startups and companies, but if you really reflect upon it, it’s equally applicable to us as individuals and professionals.

A few weeks ago, we spoke about the importance of waiting for the right opportunity, even if it takes a long while. Earlier, we’ve talked about 10- to 20-year journeys too.

Kimerling’s post is about the “necessary but not sufficient” condition that is often missed—the discipline required to pace yourself out for the long run.

“If an individual or organization collapses—mentally, physically, financially, spiritually, or otherwise—it cannot have any longevity, let alone generational longevity. All the blood, sweat, and tears, all the hard work poured into the company, project, or organization up to that point, might have been for naught.

As an industry, we seem to have developed sophisticated ways to measure and assess operating performance and financial runway – the fiscal sustainability, or lack thereof, of our portfolio companies. At Deciens, we track this in four distinct ways, and we share a company-by-company summary with you each quarter.

But fiscal sustainability is only part of the picture.

An equally existential risk is teams running out of the time, energy, and emotional capacity needed to continue building their companies. Put simply, we’ve lacked a clear view of the psychological runway of the companies in our care – and the people we entrust to run them. While we screen for grit, fortitude, and resilience – all pieces of the puzzle – even the hardiest people can only be knocked down so many times before they throw in the towel.”

4. A clean slate

Hello Ishan, it’s Tanim with your weekly books section. 

Fantasy is one of my favourite genres. There’s just something about the intentional destruction of real-world constraints and building up a completely different—but consistent—universe in its place that I find particularly invigorating for the imagination. 

Good worldbuilding is an exacting art, but a rewarding one. 

You have the benefit of a blank slate and limitless imagination. If your setting is the real world, you have to operate within inherited assumptions—do certain things a certain way because that’s what the laws of this universe allow. But the clean slate that good fantasy is built on obliterates these constrictions; there is no legacy code, no “this is just how things have to work”. 

When you “worldbuild” on a blank slate, you are no longer just improving on what you have. You are forced to not just abide by already existing fundamentals, principles, and rules, but decide what they are. 

The challenge, of course, is to make your eventual creation real enough to pull in your reader, and consistent enough in its rules for your story to make sense. Otherworldly, yet entirely relatable. It’s a tough balance to strike. 

The reader recommendations we feature today are two great examples of this craft in action. But before we get into them, do you know of any great speculative-fiction books that our community would love to read? Please tell us here, and why. 

For this week, Sonakshi Sharma recommends The Way of Kings by Brandan Sanderson:

“Okay, okay, I know this is high fantasy. Maybe it’s not the classic recommendation for this crowd. But this book—and the entire Stormlight Archive series—is a masterclass in worldbuilding, imagination, and using different worlds to explore contemporary social issues (e.g., class, mental health). Which is, after all, what all good science fiction and high fantasy are about! 

More than anything else, it’s just thousands of pages of fun. I return to this one often when I am struggling to give time to my reading, because its immersive narrative makes you feel like you are in the Rosharan universe. It gives you that little kick of creativity you need to make your boring work report not so boring after all.”

And Ritvik Lukose suggests a book from fantasy genre giant Terry Pratchett. 

The Thief of Time, Lukose says, is “brilliant satire that’s both funny and deep at the same time.”

$4 million.
 
$24 million.
 
$17.6 billion.
 
The first $ figure is the working capital a defence startup needed; the second is the order book it could have secured; the third is India’s sizeable budget allocation for the financial year ending this month.
 
Head-turning stats, right? Especially if you remember that in 2021 when Tonbo Imaging was going through a growth crisis, startups were raising tens of millions of dollars in their seed or series A rounds.
 
What Tonbo went through, despite being funded by some well-known financial investors, is the story of many (if not most) defence startups in India. It’s a country where system integrators—the likes of L&T, Tata Advanced Systems, or state-owned Bharat Electronics and Hindustan Aeronautics—and not OEMs occupy the top position in the hierarchy. They procure parts from smaller companies or R&D-for-hire firms like Tonbo and assemble them under their brand name for sale to the Indian armed forces.
 
This is an old story. But it had a plot twist in 2016. India’s Indigenously Designed Developed and Manufactured (IDDM) programme urged companies to source locally designed and manufactured goods, targeting ~50% of components from Indian firms. Still, those like Tonbo couldn’t move fast.
 
You see, India’s defence tech procurement, designed to ensure more competition than to have the best product, is a problem made in India. (India’s space agency also faced this but managed to address it to an extent.)
 
One way for defence startups to bypass DRDO’s technology gatekeeping and other procurement hurdles is to raise private capital and reach the end customer directly. But let’s just say VCs don’t have the nerve to fund defence startups.
 
Tonbo, which also considers Israel’s Mossad and the French army as its customers, had to actually make for the world to make in India. How did it do it? That’s Arundhati’s rewarding read today: https://the-ken.com/story/defence-startup-tonbo-relies-on-its-outsourcing-vendor/ (8-minute read)

Good Morning Ishan,

When Swedish furniture retailer Ikea was looking for a new growth driver for its business a few years ago, The Philippines should have been an unlikely choice for the seller of everything from flat pack furniture to meatballs; a quarter of the country’s population lives below the poverty line, and the average living space barely crosses 300 sq. ft. 

What’s more, Ikea’s mass-produced products are in sharp contrast to a country dubbed 'the Milan of Asia' for its numerous award-winning designers specialising in crafting elaborate pieces out of wood. The Philippine furniture makers create some 20 billion pesos (US$362 million) worth of furniture each year, and Filipinos' love for the furnishings reflects in the way they cram their houses with these designer pieces. 

Despite operating in the shadow of such master crafters, Ikea’s business in the Philippines has blossomed. It belatedly opened the world’s largest store in the country's capital Manila, spread across 700,000 sq. ft. Since then, Filipinos can’t seem to get enough of Ikea–over 100,000 people stroll through its store daily–the company aims to touch 7-million-visitors mark by August 2023. In the first nine months of operation, Ikea Philippines accounted for a third of the country’s furniture market, and it earned over US$117 million in revenues. 

In today’s story, Isa writes about how Ikea Philippines has cracked what appears, from the outside, to be a market unsuitable for a seller of homogenous furniture and furnishings. While the love of loitering in the malls (I didn’t know ‘mall days’ were a thing) helps draw in consumers, there are four things Ikea has accomplished to make its ready-to-assemble products fly off the shelves in the Philippines.

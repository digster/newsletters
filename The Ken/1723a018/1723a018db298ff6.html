Joy, fear, bureaucracy

Rohin

I. The joyless economy

The role of emotion in keeping modern, capitalistic economies running is neither estimated nor understood well. Of course, behavioural economists study and marketers hack our brains into making one decision over another, but rarely do we study the impact of a whole population just shying away from making decisions altogether. Because the joy has gone out of everything.

Going to the 1 Utama shopping mall in Petaling Jaya, Malaysia means this.
Upon entering 1 Utama, a person’s temperature will be taken by a security guard.
They will then have to scan a QR code placed near the mall’s entrances using a phone with a QR code reader as well as complete and submit a self-declaration form that includes providing their phone number and answering some health-related questions.

Those who receive a green QR code can proceed into the mall.

Shoppers are also advised to save a copy of the green QR code, which is valid for an entire day, as they will have to show the code each time they want to enter any shop in 1 Utama.

“If a person receives a red QR code, the security guard will check their temperature again. Those who get the red status twice will be advised to seek medical attention,” said [Bandar Utama City Centre Sdn Bhd operations general manager Samantha] Lee.
Entry pass to shop with peace of mind at mall, The Star
Unlike the ennui that exists in developed countries towards malls, they’re big leisure destinations for friends and families to spend a few hours over the weekend to unwind. If entering a mall feels like checking into a maximum-security prison, how many will still want to do it?

How about undertaking an airplane journey in India?
  • Passengers will only be allowed to obtain boarding passes through web check-in. They are also required to download the baggage tag for their check-in luggage, which is restricted to a maximum of one bag and a handbag.
     
  • The airlines shall not provide meal services on board. Passengers would not be permitted to consume any eatables inside the aircraft during the flight.
     
  • Passengers should travel in an authorized taxi/personal vehicle following the norms specified by MHA.
     
  • Passengers are expected to certify the status of their health through the Aarogya Setu app or a self-declaration form.
     
  • Passengers are advised to minimize use of lavatory and to avoid any non-essential movement in the aisles.
     
  • If a passenger who is not permitted to fly, undertakes an air journey he/she shall be liable for penal action.
Planning to take a flight soon? Read these essential do's and don'ts issued by aviation ministry, Livemint
Why would you fly unless you had absolutely no choice?

As joy drains out of our lives, choices and decisions, human contact will go from something we craved to something we fear.

“The oldest and strongest emotion of mankind is fear, and the oldest and strongest kind of fear is fear of the unknown.” - H. P. Lovecraft
II. The fear economy

“Fear is economic cancer,” says entrepreneur and author Bill Roth.
Soon we will have evidence of the next supply shock impact: a fall in demand driven by fear. In the case of coronavirus, fear is causing people and businesses to avoid purchasing services like travel or dining – which could have a huge impact on the most vulnerable in our society. Fear is driving people and businesses to avoid our workplaces. Combined, this will generate a fall in employment.

If sustained over a long enough time period, this current crisis will create a recession.
Taking on the Behavioral Economics of Fear, Triple Pundit
In a fear-driven economy, consumers reset their choices. Not just for cheaper alternatives, but also because it gives them a sense of power and control over a world they cannot comprehend. Among their choices are cheap indulgences like “sweets, lipstick and chocolate.” 

Also, a preference for familiarity, safety, trust, nostalgia, and nationalism. From a 2016 article with a decidedly marketing outlook:
When people fear the future they tend to move closer together. Expect a switch in spending from out-of-home/on-premise to in-home, including home entertaining and entertainment, home improvements and spending even more time online.
The fear economy – how will you adapt to survive?, Marketing
But fear is a great opportunity for some. “It is when power is wedded to chronic fear that it becomes formidable,” said the late American author and philosopher Eric Hoffer.
III. The bureaucratic economy

“Blessed are the bureaucrats, for they shall inherit the earth,” said no one ever.

Naushad Forbes is a widely respected Indian business leader who is the co-chairman of Forbes Marshall, an engineering and energy conservation company. He warned India of a return to bureaucratic stasis, aka “License Raj”, in an opinion column in Business Standard yesterday.
A dynamic economy demands that no official has the discretion to stop legal activity. In our zeal to control, we have rediscovered what Abhijit Banerjee and Esther Duflo call “the kind of total government chokehold on private enterprise India had in the 1970s”. The net effect is a stalled economy that refuses to restart even when ordered to do so.

[…]

Consider a recent example — liquor shops. For some reason — perhaps the high morals of the Union government official concerned — liquor shops were closed for 40 days. When the Punjab government permitted them to open, the Union government issued them with a notice and forced them to close. Less than a week later, they were permitted to reopen everywhere in the country outside containment areas. But only 20 per cent of shops were allowed to reopen at a time, for limited hours, and with the threat they might be made to close again. The long queues and chaos that resulted would have come as a surprise only to the bureaucrat who formulated the policy.
Let's not slide back to Licence Raj, we must not stifle the economy, Business Standard
Forbes refers to a 1970’s cartoon by India’s best known editorial cartoonist, the late R.K Laxman.
India in the 1970s was a miserable place. My favourite commentator on the times, the genius cartoonist R K Laxman, captured matters best. In one cartoon, an industrialist is showing a visitor round his large unit: “Typing department for licences there, copying in triplicate here, department to collect C-forms there. And [pointing to a small engine operating in a corner] this, by the way, is our factory”.

[…]

Let’s not go back to the Licence Raj. Only Laxman’s successors will benefit.

Turbulence before take-off

Seetharaman

Indian airlines, whose flights have been earthbound for two months now, have been given the go-ahead to operate a third of their flights from 25 May. 
 
But they can’t rejoice yet. Because they don’t get to decide how much to charge fliers. The government will instead do it for them for three months. For instance, it has said that a Mumbai-Delhi ticket can be sold for between Rs 3,500 and Rs 10,000 ($46-132). That’s not the half of it. 
"Tickets in this fare band are split into different buckets. We are putting a second rider. 40% of the seats have to be sold at a fare that is lower than the midpoint of the maximum fare. We are ensuring that the fare does not go out of hand and at the same time, it is viable for airlines also. It is only for the period of scarcity," explained civil aviation secretary Pradeep Kharola.
Covid-19: Cap on airfares to stay for 3 months…, Livemint
The government has divided flights by duration into seven bands, with an upper and lower fare limit. 
 
This is a temporary move and not the first such attempt. During the Kerala floods of 2018, the aviation ministry advised airlines to keep airfares in check. 

Even so, let’s wind the clock back to early 2018. In response to a Parliamentary panel’s recommendation to cap airfares, the government put up a strong defence of airlines’ prerogative on setting fares.
“Pricing deregulation has allowed competition to bring down prices dramatically in India, making it one of the lowest-fare markets in the world,” as per a late-evening statement from the Ministry of Civil Aviation.

According to the government, instances of predatory pricing were far and few between.

“Please note that only between one per cent and two per cent of tickets are transacted at the highest fare basket. A capping of fares could raise prices for the 98-99 per cent of the passengers,” the statement added.
Capping airfares may increase air travel costs: Govt, The Hindu Business Line
The government has done an about-turn ostensibly to help passengers. But what about the airlines themselves? This is what an airline executive thinks could be a consequence of the move:
When demand is muted and minimum fare is higher than what it would otherwise have been, the weakest player suffers the most as it cannot use discounting as a method to steal share from competitors as well as buses and trains, or to stimulate demand. Then the demand goes to the stronger players offering that fare. 
Here’s why a fare cap may not help…, Moneycontrol
A counter-argument to that could be that the smaller players, hurting more than the biggies, may anyway not be in a position to offer discounted fares. But then, that’s their decision to make. 
 
Indian airlines are expected to lose revenues to the tune of Rs 17,000 crore ($2.2 billion) this financial year. They asked the government for a bailout, and in return, they had something taken from them. 
Hanging by a popcorn kernel

Durga

This whole spat between Bollywood filmmakers and Indian multiplexes is a masala flick in itself.

Let’s back up a little.

Indian multiplexes and single-screen theatres have been shut since the nationwide lockdown that started at the end of March. Now, filmmakers looking to release their films turned to the obvious option—OTT platforms.

For instance, Amazon Prime Video clinched the premiere rights for a host of films including two big Bollywood summer releases, Gulabo Sitabo and Shakuntala Devi, as well as Tamil film Ponmagal Vandhal.  

Film-lovers and filmmakers are obviously okay with this. Theatre owners, though, seem quite cross. Multiplex chain INOX went so far as to call filmmakers “fair-weather friends". Ouch.

Rival PVR Ltd steered clear of name calling, but did moan about the fall in occupancy rates due to social distancing. And “new techniques to maximise footfall besides upping the game on consumer experience.”
For instance, PVR Ltd plans to adopt a cashless transaction strategy wherein consumers will pay for the ticket via their cards or e-wallets. Moreover, the company has decided to limit its food and beverage to items such as popcorn, Pepsi and nachos. As per Bijli, these three items alone contribute to about 75%-80% of the company’s revenue.
Coronavirus Impact: As movie theatres look to open shop, all hopes pinned on big-budget films, Financial Express
Notice something here. 75%-80% of PVR’s F&B revenue comes from popcorn, Pepsi and nachos.

Cool, cool.

And guess how much % F&B makes for in its total revenue?

Here’s a cute pie chart from Tijori.
Almost 30%. INOX is much the same.
 
Filmmakers are happily pointing out this huge moneymaker. Here’s Kunal Kohli on Twitter:
Now, don’t get distracted by this popcorn-worthy filmmakers vs multiplexes battle. (I was tempted, too.)
 
The main takeaway here is that there’s a popcorn economy that multiplexes can survive off.

No, it’s legit. Just look at the kind of money popcorn—and I imagine nachos and Coke/Pepsi—brings in.
Source: Tijori
In fact, F&B is the fastest growing business for multiplexes.
The Indian multiplex industry has been growing at a compounded annual rate of 14% over the last three years, but revenue from ancillary segments like F&B and advertisement have grown faster at 21%, according to Inox.
Inox, PVR make every fourth rupee selling food, drinks, Livemint
Multiplexes will ride this one out. No thanks to the peanut gallery.

Do as I say, not as I do 

Arundhati

Marc Andreessen, celebrity investor and cofounder of venture capital firm Andreessen Horowitz, wrote a part soul searching, part scathing note to Silicon Valley last month. It was titled Time to Build. 
Part of the problem is clearly foresight, a failure of imagination. But the other part of the problem is what we didn’t *do* in advance, and what we’re failing to do now. And that is a failure of action, and specifically our widespread inability to *build*.
Marc Andreessen
The note resonated widely and became a warcry for the venture capital and founder ecosystem because of prose like this:
Every step of the way, to everyone around us, we should be asking the question, what are you building? What are you building directly, or helping other people to build, or teaching other people to build, or taking care of people who are building?
Marc Andreessen
So what is Andreessen helping build? 
 
An invite-only social networking app called Clubhouse.
Clubhouse is a social media app where venture capitalists have gathered to mingle with one another while they are quarantined in their homes. The app is, for now, invite-only, and buzzy. 
 
Andreessen’s venture firm, Andreessen Horowitz, won the deal to invest in Clubhouse. Andreessen Horowitz agreed to put in $10 million, plus pay $2 million to buy shares from Clubhouse’s existing shareholders, said a person with knowledge of the funding, who declined to be named because the details were confidential. 

[....]

The financing valued Clubhouse, which started this year and has two employees, at nearly $100 million.

Savio

Researchers are working at breakneck speed to find a cure and a vaccine for Covid-19. Almost everyone in the pharmaceutical industry is either trying to make something from scratch or repurposing existing treatments. And then there are some…let’s just say unique participants.
 
The world’s No.2 cigarette company is waiting for permission to start human trials for a potential vaccine made using proteins from tobacco leaves. 

But how do you know if you already have the disease? Man’s best friend to the rescue, again. *sniff*

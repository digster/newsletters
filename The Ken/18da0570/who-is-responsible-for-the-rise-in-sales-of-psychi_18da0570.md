Good Morning Ishan,
Nothing succeeds like success, and nothing glitters like gold (ask India—the world’s second-largest consumer of the yellow metal after China).
Both of these should manifest in India this week. I wouldn’t at all be surprised if the latest issue of sovereign gold bonds (SGBs)—that opened yesterday—bags record subscriptions again. The last two issues, in December and September, saw Indians lap up over 10 tonnes each of gold, pouring in a massive Rs 7,000 crore (US$843 million) or so in each tranche.
These first-time-ever numbers could be bested by Friday when the latest issue closes. Why?
Simply because money chases returns. Gold’s dream run since 2019 has seen the earliest tranches of SGBs—issued about eight years ago—deliver 12–13% returns a year on average to investors. Add to this the kid-glove tax treatment on SGBs.
But I think investors may be better off not going overboard. Gold, considered a hedge against inflation, tends to go up when the price rise in economies is high. Now, after much wrestling, central bankers, including in India, seem to be finally gaining the upper hand over inflation. Besides, history is instructive—gold has a habit of stagnating for years after strong shows.
Old-timers will remember the multi-year price consolidation until 2002. More recently, 2012 to 2019 was nothing to write home about. I hope I am wrong, though—I, too, have invested in SGBs.
Long story short, play if you have to, but play it measured.
A cautious approach is also what Shivani calls for in today’s important and sensitive [story](https://the-ken.com/story/who-is-responsible-for-the-rise-in-sales-of-psychiatric-pills-not-psychiatrists/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter).
Doctors in India are seeing a spike in mental-health ailments. The role of psychiatrists in managing and treating these cannot be overemphasised. But there is a disconcerting trend underway. Sales of psychiatric drugs are zooming in the country—without psychiatrists being involved in many cases.
Despite low psychiatrist visits, certain combination-drug sales have soared 1,000% in 2023 compared with the year-ago levels, according to a market-research firm.

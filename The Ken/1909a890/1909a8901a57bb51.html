Good Morning Ishan,

They say the quickest way to become a millionaire is to start as a billionaire and launch an airline. Now, mergers may lack the legendary notoriety of airlines, but they are no pushovers when it comes to wealth destruction. The megamerger of global media giants Time Warner and America Online at the turn of the century blew up billions and remains the poster boy of botched mergers. From culture clashes to expected synergies not showing up, a lot can and does go wrong when businesses come together.

So, it’s a whiff of fresh air when mergers do deliver the goods they were supposed to. Ask Incred Finance. In 2022, the young non-bank took a big, risky bet and merged with the struggling Indian non-bank arm of global private-equity giant KKR. It was a marriage of convenience, all right. KKR India Financial Services, backed by marquee investors, was desperate for a bailout—struggling as it was with its past sins made much worse by the pandemic’s blows.

Incred, on the other hand, saw in the merger an opportunity to grow big and fast by getting something it coveted. Calling the shots, Incred’s smart deal structuring resulted in it taking only what it wanted and on its own terms. The result? The merger has helped Incred grow its loan book and profits big and fast. Next stop? An IPO. Did KKR also win? Well, yes, no, maybe—it depends on how you look at it.

Anjali, the author of today’s story, digs deep and decodes the deal that gives mergers a good rep—well, almost—and finds out what it now means for everyone involved.

From across the ocean, Shanies also writes about the coming together of businesses—two giant ones in this case—and the unintended consequences it is unleashing on unsuspecting stakeholders.

In January, Chinese technology company Bytedance, which owns the wildly popular short-video app Tiktok, bought a 75% stake in Indonesian e-commerce major Tokopedia for US$1.5 billion. This one, too, was a marriage of convenience. For Tiktok, the deal was the way to salvage its e-commerce ambitions in Indonesia after the government pulled the plug on one of its key businesses. For Goto, the parent company of Tokopedia, the deal was meant to slash its operational losses by as much as a quarter. So far, so good.

But the devil, as always, lay in the details. And it has come to bite the employees of Tokopedia. In its most brutal round of layoffs so far, Tokopedia sacked hundreds of its employees last month. Those who were spared the cull remain on edge, with big work culture changes, restructuring, potential leadership changes, and integration challenges. Every question and interaction is likely a silent and critical evaluation, Shanies reports.

Then, there are the business challenges to deal with. Mergers and acquisitions have a well-earned rep for not having a happy ending, at least not for everyone. Read this deeply reported story to know how this high-profile acquisition may be a win-win for Tiktok and Tokopedia but clearly not for the latter’s rank and file.

Picture this: a swanky five-storeyed, apartment-style building standing in the backdrop of a vast, quaint white cottage with a red-hat roof, all nestled in greenery.

Seems like a resort, or even a beachy vacation spot, right? But that, in fact, is a factory set up by Kaynes Technology, a 35-year-old electronics-manufacturing-services player.

Don’t believe it? See for yourself.

This facility near Mysuru is nothing like a typical manufacturing plant, which is often, a large, drab grey building with a glass facade.

There are more ways than just one in which the company—that counts India Japan Lighting, Siemens, and Hitachi among its clients—stands out.

For one, when Dixon Technologies, its largest rival, saw a margin of under 4% in FY24, Kaynes’ stood at 14%—the highest in the space.

It’s been on an expansion spree since going public a couple of years ago and has parallelly raised its headcount to nearly 4,000—a fourth of which was onboarded in FY24 alone. Yet, the company’s employee cost, as a percentage of its revenue, has more than halved to 6% in four years to March 2024.

But how does this paradox of “expanding with fewer new hires” even work? Especially when it’s the polar opposite of what government think tank Niti Aayog forecasts: the US$155 billion electronics-manufacturing-services industry will add nearly 6 million jobs as it trebles in size by 2030.

Read Shristi’s deep-dive into Kaynes’ “shortcut” to success in a sector where it rivals national and international names like Tata Electronics, Pegatron, and Foxconn.

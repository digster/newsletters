Unless you’re Reliance Jio or funded by Softbank, it's hard to offer significantly better prices or sustain significantly higher profit margins over the long term. Because, in a free market, as soon as your competitors and your customers come to know about these, they all want a piece of it. That’s why Amazon’s Jeff Bezos said it best as a warning to his competitors, “Your margin is my opportunity.”
 
Something similar is happening to a young cohort of banks in India - small finance banks. Born alongside their non-identical twin, payments banks, small finance banks too were intended by their progenitor to help further its goal of financial inclusion. I’m referring to India’s central bank and banking regulator, the RBI.
 
And while the twin appeared to be terminally ill right from birth, small finance banks looked like a success. Just the top three - AU Finance, Equitas and Ujjivan - raised deposits worth $2 billion in the last two years. While lending out over $3.4 billion.
 
In a country with dozens of banks and about 1.57 billion bank accounts, that was an amazing feat. One made possible by these banks lighting their candles from both ends. At one end, they’d give out risky microfinance loans to low-income households, charging up to 24% interest per annum. At the other, they used the interest profits from those loans to offer savings interest rates that were 2-3 percentage points higher than large banks.
 
Yes, candles lit at both ends do tend to burn brighter for a while. For a while.
 
As Arundhati’s story today explains, small finance banks are now faced with the prospect of both flames approaching their fingers. On the one hand, their microfinance loan exposure is coming down, and with it, their high interest margins. And on the other, they will increasingly be unable to sustain doling out high interest rates to savers.
 
What options do they have? Well, that’s the story: https://the-ken.com/story/when-mfis-stop-being-startup-banks-golden-goose/

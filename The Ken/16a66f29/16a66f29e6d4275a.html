Good Morning Ishan,
 
We have a sporty start this week. The first time I visited a Decathlon store in south Bengaluru, it was on a colleague’s borrowed membership card. It was early 2013, the value-priced sports retailer was just making the transition from wholesaler to retailer.
 
All I'd heard about the experiential centre was true no doubt, but what was most striking was the well-spoken, sports-loving staff who doled out fitness tips to customers. “Don’t sit under a fan or a cooler immediately after a workout,” said one blithe spirit to a 10-year-old, engaging in discussion. Old strategy to hook young customers and keep them as they grow older, right?
 
Many stores now have a carnival look & feel as families hang around before and after shopping. Not surprising. Cities run out of open spaces; Decathlon continues to make its location picks strategically.
 
The French retailer took a decade to turn a profit in India, to beat sports brands like Nike, Puma, Adidas, and to expand to 68 stores and over 5,000 products. It intends to have at least 800 stores in India by 2040. Steadfastness of a marathoner? (Wish some of that long-termism rubbed off our politicians.)
 
While much has been written about why exactly the company is successful—scale, vertical integration, no middlemen or distributors, etc—there’s still very little known about its playbook in India. People don’t even know which executive runs its India operations. It’s pretty low key in a market where sports merchandising, or athleisure, is largely celebrity or high-marketing-cost driven.
 
Harveen has pieced together a story that gives a peek into that playbook, one which allowed Decathlon to double its revenues each year between 2012 and 2016. Like the much-talked about new San Francisco suburb store, where associates run its omnichannel platform on an iPhone, Decathlon is also testing mobile checkout stations at a Bengaluru store. Without much ado, let’s dive in: https://the-ken.com/story/decathlons-playbook/ (15-minute read)

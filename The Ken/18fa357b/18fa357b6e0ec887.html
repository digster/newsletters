Good Morning Ishan,

Six out of 10 Indians diagnosed with cancer will succumb to it.

Crucial to improving this number is early detection. If pancreatic or lung cancer is not detected at stage one or two, the outcome doesn’t change much.

This is where companies such as Qure.ai and Niramai Health Analytix intend to make a huge difference. Qure’s AI algorithm, the first from India to get all the global regulatory approvals, screens for lung cancer using chest X-rays. And Niramai’s thermal imaging-based AI tool screens for breast cancer and is devoid of radiation or touch.

But hospital chains, despite their overzealous announcements on AI adoption, haven’t actually warmed to AI interventions. Among their primary concerns: an algorithm for screening the general population is designed not to leave out any cancer undetected and, hence, results in more false positives. But a hospital’s objective is to spot more symptomatic patients and spare the healthy from follow-up tests.

Seema’s exhaustive story covers the many fault lines in the imminent rise of AI in cancer care, including the datasets these technologies are trained on and who ends up paying for them. Do not miss it.

Our second long-form feature today, by Jeff, is also on AI. But it has nothing to do with India or healthcare. It’s about how Southeast Asia has become the new battleground in the US-China fight for AI supremacy.

The entire engineering team of Dana, one of Indonesia’s largest fintech companies, uses Microsoft’s Github Copilot to write 20,000 lines of code each month and frees up 2,000 hours of labour.

In December, Alibaba’s research arm released a conversational version of its large language model compatible with languages such as Vietnamese, Thai, and Indonesian.

It’s game on for tech giants in a region home to 685 million people.

Sebi is done taking “rumours” lightly

India’s capital-market regulator on Tuesday issued new guidelines to manage the impact of “rumours” on stock prices. Starting 1 June, India’s top 100 listed companies will have to confirm or deny any news that results in a material price change, and they have to do so within 24 hours.

It’s quite common for a company to tell the stock exchanges, in response to an article on a potential acquisition the company plans to make, that it doesn’t comment on market rumours or that it explores such deals in the normal course of business. That won’t wash any longer.

But Sebi also wants to make sure that speculative reports, deliberate or inadvertent, don’t throw a wrench into an ongoing acquisition.

Great as this is, I’d like to see how companies reconcile these new guidelines with their penchant for boilerplate.

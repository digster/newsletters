Dear Ishan, good morning! Do you know about the Japanese concept of “Shinrin-yoku”? I inadvertently indulged in it a few Sundays ago.

It was the Sunday before the Deepavali weekend. I was sitting in our east-facing balcony with the newspaper. I could see the clear, piercing blue skies so characteristic of Bengaluru winters. Yellow, brown, and tiger-striped butterflies were fluttering around the bright foliage, backlit by the sun’s rays filtering through the dappled branches of the water apple tree in our backyard. For some reason, the white butterflies were the only ones dive-bombing. They’d first ride up effortlessly with the currents, then tailspin down at high speeds, spiralling like the paper propellers we used to make as kids in school. They’d pull back a few feet before hitting the ground, and then soar up again. There were lots of dragonflies, too. Overhead, a kite soared lazily, perhaps searching for a meal or maybe just admiring the luridly red flowers of a Flame of the Forest like I was.

I could feel a gentle breeze on my face and skin. I could hear birds chirping all around (the three most common ones I’ve seen are the Cuckoo, Oriental Magpie Robin, and Bulbul). I could smell the freshly laundered set of sheets drying on a nearby stand. It was a multisensorial experience. A “nature bath”.

Which is what “Shinrin-yoku” https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shinrin-yoku is. It was magical. The same things that have always been around me, just connected together differently—almost like a different reality.

And that is what I want to talk about today.

1. Reframing your reality

How many times a month, or even a week, do you find yourself on the defensive inside your own head, against your own thoughts? “Too many to keep count” is my answer. Here are just a few from recently:

– “Your inbox has unread emails from three weeks ago. When are you going to get to them?”

– “The new project at work should have started last month. When will you get to it?”

– “There are so many unread books on your table. When are you going to read them?”

– “The pipeline for your podcasts is virtually non-existent. Are you planning to not have one at all?

– “Your injury is taking too long to heal. Are you sure you want to continue working out and risk making it worse?”

– “By now, you should have interviewed and hired all the critical people for the next year. Will you even be able to do it this year?”

What do they all have in common?

They’re all mental models. That’s right. https://thedecisionlab.com/reference-guide/design/mental-models

“Mental models refer to the internal representations of external reality that individuals use to understand, interpret, and navigate the world around them. They consist of a set of beliefs, generalizations, and assumptions that make up our worldview. And because our worldview affects how we interpret experiences, these mental models also influence our thoughts and behaviours.”

Each of the examples above isn’t objective reality, but my own mind’s subjective interpretation of it.

This is a newsletter about mental models (among many things), so perhaps you’re a bit taken aback. Aren’t mental models supposed to be ones we consciously use to become better?

Well, no.

A mental model is just a mental model. Some are subconscious, some are negative, some are vague, some are powerful.

Let’s take my first example. So what if I have unanswered emails from three weeks ago? Perhaps they’re not that important to me. Perhaps I’ve been busy doing other things that were more important. Perhaps it isn’t that great a virtue to be the hyper-productive professional who answers all their emails within 24 hours? Perhaps even the concept of “unanswered emails in your inbox” is a pointless thing to be worried about in this day and age (and yet, I strangely am?).

The wonderful thing about our brains is that we can reprogram them to view the same reality differently. The term for it, if you’d like one, is  “Cognitive Reframing.” https://www.ebsco.com/research-starters/psychology/cognitive-reframing

“Cognitive reframing is a method used to help people view situations or events in a neutral or positive way rather than negatively. Reframing changes the “frame” through which a person perceives a particular event or situation. Cognitive reframing can include thinking about possible positive outcomes or benefits that the person might not have previously considered. Reframing has numerous benefits, one of the most important being that people’s actions will often change if their understanding of a situation changes. Therefore, a goal of cognitive reframing is to cause a person’s actions to become more positive.”

For instance, here’s an example https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/JhWGF5ktuGALMxazk/cognitive-reframing-how-to-overcome-negative-thought by Declan Molony from the collective blog Less Wrong.

“Another frequent and annoying experience is babies crying on planes. While I’m powerless to stop the noise, I do have control of my reaction.

I solved this problem by cognitively reframing the situation to imagine that the source of the noise was actually that plant-thing from Harry Potter. After practicing this dozens of times over the years, nowadays my automatic reaction to babies crying is snickering to myself.”

In my case, because I’ve been there and felt that as a parent, I always reframe the situation by wondering how stressed and worried the parents of the crying child must be feeling.

While researching for this piece (what, you thought I always winged it, huh?), I came across this nice primer https://www.mskcc.org/sites/default/files/node/218479/documents/cbt-c-manual_session-3_040121.pdf on how to engage in conscious reframing.

Reframing isn’t just for personal self-improvement, of course. It is very much a key skill for leaders.

A classic trap for many managers and executives is the “solution mode” trap. The moment they hear someone, anyone, mention a problem, they immediately start thinking of solutions.

From my colleagues and friends to my wife and son, they all know I have a terrible case of this affliction.

Reframing can help https://hbr.org/2024/01/to-solve-a-tough-problem-reframe-it.

“When we work with organizations and teams, we encourage them to spend more time up front on problem-framing, a process for understanding and defining a problem. Exploring frames is like looking at a scene through various camera lenses while adjusting your angle, aperture, and focus. A wide-angle lens will give you a very different photo from that taken with a telephoto lens, and shifting your angle and depth of focus yields distinct images. Effective problem-framing is similar: Looking at a problem from a variety of perspectives lets you uncover new insights and generate fresh ideas.” 

To Solve a Tough Problem, Reframe It—Julia Binder and Michael D. Watkins, Harvard Business Review 

I loved this example from the US on how the universal problem of dog abandonment was reframed—also from the Harvard Business Review https://hbr.org/2017/01/are-you-solving-the-right-problems (emphasis mine).

“Dogs are very popular in America: Industry statistics suggest that more than 40% of U.S. households have one. But this fondness for dogs has a downside: According to estimates by the ASPCA, one of the largest animal-welfare groups in the United States, more than 3 million dogs enter a shelter each year and are put up for adoption.

Shelters and other animal-welfare organizations work hard to raise awareness of this issue. A typical ad or poster will show a neglected, sad-looking dog, carefully chosen to evoke compassion, along with a line such as “Save a life—adopt a dog” or perhaps a request to donate to the cause. Through this and other initiatives, this notoriously underfunded system manages to get about 1.4 million dogs adopted each year. But that leaves more than a million unadopted dogs—and doesn’t account for the many cats and other pets in the same situation. There is just a limited amount of compassion to go around. So despite the impressive efforts of shelters and rescue groups, the shortage of pet adopters has persisted for decades.

Lori Weise, the founder of Downtown Dog Rescue in Los Angeles, has demonstrated that adoption is not the only way to frame the problem. Weise is one of the pioneers of an approach that is currently spreading within the industry—the shelter intervention program. Rather than seek to get more dogs adopted, Weise tries to keep them with their original families so that they never enter shelters in the first place. It turns out that about 30% of the dogs that enter a shelter are “owner surrenders,” deliberately relinquished by their owners. In a volunteer-driven community united by a deep love of animals, those people have often been heavily criticized for heartlessly discarding their pets as if they were just another consumer good. To prevent dogs from ending up with such “bad” owners, many shelters, despite their chronic overpopulation, require potential adopters to undergo laborious background checks.

Weise has a different take. “Owner surrenders are not a people problem,” she says. “By and large, they are a poverty problem. These families love their dogs as much as we do, but they are also exceptionally poor. We’re talking about people who in some cases aren’t entirely sure how they will feed their kids at the end of the month. So when a new landlord suddenly demands a deposit to house the dog, they simply have no way to get the money. In other cases, the dog needs a $10 rabies shot, but the family has no access to a vet, or may be afraid to approach any kind of authority. Handing over their pet to a shelter is often the last option they believe they have.”

Weise started her program in April 2013, collaborating with a shelter in South Los Angeles. The idea is simple: Whenever a family comes in to hand over a pet, a staff member asks without judgment if the family would prefer to keep the pet. If the answer is yes, the staff member tries to help resolve the problem, drawing on his or her network and knowledge of the system.

Within the first year it was clear that the program was a remarkable success. In prior years Weise’s organization had spent an average of $85 per pet it helped. The new program brought that cost down to about $60 while keeping shelter space free for other animals in need. And, Weise told me, that was just the immediate impact: “The wider effect on the community is the real point. The program helps families learn problem solving, lets them know their rights and responsibilities, and teaches the community that help is available. It also shifted the industry’s perception of the pet owners: We found that when offered assistance, a full 75% of them actually wanted to keep their pets.”

Are You Solving the Right Problems?—Thomas Wedell-Wedellsborg, Harvard Business Review

I’ve been thinking of this problem on my own, too. In the last few months, I’ve seen a few kind folks in our neighbourhood adopt young indie puppies. They are blue-collar workers who might barely be making their ends meet, yet they take care of the puppies, feed them, and love them.

Now, I know they cannot afford to vaccinate or (in some time) neuter/spay them. How can we design a “solution” that lets neighbouring communities or dog lovers sponsor the dogs’ vaccinations at a trusted clinic? I know that Bengaluru’s city administration apparently conducts vaccination and neutering drives every now and then, but they are both unreliable and unpredictable.

If you have ideas or suggestions, do write in to me.

To close our original thread on reframing, here’s some of the newer ones I’ve gotten comfortable adopting. I like starting with the words “I need” because it allows me to reframe things from my perspective.

– “I need weekends and vacations so I can stay creative and energetic.”

– “I need to read fiction so I can experience creativity in different forms.”

– “I need more time for a project than I had estimated because doing it right matters more to me than doing it on time.”

– “I need to avoid social media and messaging apps because I want to use that time for other things that are more important to me.”

2. Pick of the month: Jonathan Coe’s The Proof of My Innocence

Hello Ishan , it’s Tanim with your FP Books pick of the month. 

Before we get to it—a quick note. Next week, it’s your turn. Tell us what you’re reading right here https://theken.typeform.com/FPBooks?typeform-source=the-ken.com. And since November’s theme is mythology, don’t forget to send your favourite myth-inspired recommendations to fp@the-ken.com mailto:fp@the-ken.com or fill out the form https://theken.typeform.com/FPBooks?typeform-source=the-ken.com.

Now, onto my pick: Jonathan Coe’s The Proof of My Innocence https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/207851815-the-proof-of-my-innocence. How can I describe the English novelist’s latest book? In a nutshell, I would say, “Agatha Christie meets George Saunders.” 

Released last year, this 300-page novel is a cake you can eat in two sittings. Maybe four if you take smaller bites. A breezy read, it’s perfect for getting back to books when you haven’t read anything for a while. It is a political murder mystery set in the present day. And when I wasn’t reading, I was googling to sift fact from fiction, for Coe weaves the two so seamlessly, it’s like guessing if an image is AI-generated or real—a tricky game my colleagues know all too well. (We recently had an in-house quiz with some choice “AI-or-real” images; everyone got most wrong.)

I have discovered that Coe consistently writes about contemporary political situations. Here, he’s playing to those strengths while also trying something new with a mixed format, so familiar readers will find fresh ground. There’s a murder mystery, there’s dark academia, and then there’s autofiction, all interspersed to fit into a larger whole. 

?utm_source=ro_hdr&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=View in Browser https://the-ken.com/?post_type=fp_newsletter&p=1648428?utm_source=ro_hdr&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=

The characters are almost all writers: a journalist, a memoirist, an academic, and two novelists. I am always fascinated when writers portray their own vocation. Is it self-referential, a jibe, or an homage? Here’s what one of the characters says about writing fiction:

“Any act of writing must also, by definition, be an act of selection; therefore distortion; and therefore invention. In this book I have skirted the rank dishonesty into which most writers are led by the siren call of ‘fiction’, and which so many needy and credulous readers appear to crave.”

The story follows a left-wing journalist attending a right-wing conference during the brief, chaotic 49-day tenure of Lizz Truss—a tumultuous, revealing period in recent British history. Queen Elizabeth has just died, the economy is having a melt down, and Coe brilliantly captures the era’s chaos from the sidelines.  

He doesn’t explicitly tie Truss to complaints about “wokeness,” showing it instead through her supporters. But tellingly, six months after her prime ministership ended, Truss gave a speech https://www.newstatesman.com/thestaggers/2023/04/absurdity-liz-truss-speech-high-taxes-woke-culture (in real life) blaming the economic meltdown on those who “care about racism and pronouns”. It’s a classic strategy used by governments all over—to blame the culture that doesn’t support them as the one causing all the distress. 

Then there’s Phil, a fresh English graduate working in a sushi restaurant and trying to figure out her life. She is my favourite character. The passivity and inaction that she is trapped in, while being hyperaware of it, felt extremely familiar. The way she is excited about the journalist Christopher’s cause reminds me of how I am excited about Zohran Mamdani’s election in New York, and yet like her, I’m stuck alternately admiring him, and complaining about others, from afar. We even share the same escapism: incessantly playing a sitcom on repeat. And we are both aghast at what people end up saying in political speeches (especially considering some of the excerpts in the book aren’t even fictional). 

Here’s a peek into her world:  

“How is someone like me supposed to survive in a world like this? Everything that defines me is unsuited for it. My passivity. My idealism. My innocence. I just don’t have what it takes.”

For a political novel, it is hardly ever preachy. No omnipresent narrator to break things down for you. The commentary comes through the characters’ actions, which impressed me most. Coe deftly frames them with wildly different viewpoints. If there’s a flaw, it’s that the villains—white supremacists, conveniently murderous—feel a little too clear-cut. Perhaps mystery readers need their monsters obvious. It makes me wonder how I’d react to a mystery where the killer is a socialist! 

All in all, The Proof of My Innocence is a sharp, witty, and perfectly timed satire. It not only presents a puzzle to be solved but also holds up a mirror to our own confusing times. Coe asks us to question the narratives we’re fed, both in fiction and in the news, making for a read that is as thought-provoking as it is entertaining. If you want a clever mystery that offers more than just a “whodunit”, this is your next read. 

On a different note, two FP readers wrote in with thoughtful notes on how to get children to read.

?utm_source=ro_hdr&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=View in Browser https://the-ken.com/?post_type=fp_newsletter&p=1648428?utm_source=ro_hdr&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=

Here’s what Abhed Manocha has to say: 

I have been a curious kid since I was young. I would keep moving from puzzles to games to stories. As I became older, I grew curious about how the world functions and putting structures around it. 

That’s when I started reading books seriously. I had read fiction books when I was younger (class 5–7) but I truly started reading books and understanding them when I wanted to understand the world and the different perspectives around it.

So maybe to get children to read, we need to make them more curious? Because in the end, they will realize that they will find a lot of their answers in them. 

And this is what Daksha Ballal shared with us:

It was such a great read on your perspectives of making a child read. As an Uncle to an 8 year old nephew, my day to day conversations revolve around getting my nephew to read! I knew discipline and banning other forms of entertainment work in the short run only but we still tend to do it due to our own escalation of commitment. As caregivers, we are prone to the bias of doing things efficiently rather than effectively. So here is what I’ve been doing over the past 3 months to inculcate the habit.

1. Change of environment

We are a product of our environment and a stimulating environment can tend to have a positive impact. If you have access to a library or a park and the child sees other kids doing the same, it could instill the habit. This is different from signing him/her up for a reading club cause the child shouldn’t feel it’s an activity to do.

2. Role playing the characters

My nephew is a visual learner and imbibes learning when things are shown to him. So we read a few pages together and then start enacting the characters in the form of a play. Of course, this cannot be implemented for most books and the content and characters must be simple to understand.

3. Tell them why reading matters

A child understands when something is explained to them in simple terms. If we as adults show them that reading matters because it helps them make sense of the world around us, at some point in time, they will fall back on this example and pick up reading. 

Of course, all of the above may not work all the time but as a child evolves, these things have a way of sustaining themselves. As adults, we can only show the way and not force our ways.

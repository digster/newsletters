Good Morning Ishan,
It’s staggeringly low—just 941 registered investment advisors (RIAs) in a country of 1.4 billion people. Sure, not everyone invests. But even considering the number of demat accounts in India now—nearing 200 million—or the count of unique investors (about 130 million), it’s clear as day that India has a woeful shortage of bona fide investment advisors. Worse, the RIA fraternity has been in a steady-decline mode even as the investor count in the country keeps going up.
What gives? Primarily, a stranglehold of rules that squeezed the profession and made the RIA an endangered species. And despite market regulator Sebi trying to ease things last year, it hasn’t helped, at least so far. As it stands, the tiny community doesn’t find it feasible to advise anyone except the rich who can pay for their high costs plus a margin.
But here’s the thing. Most folks—not just the rich—need investing advice even if they don’t know it or claim otherwise. Investing is not child’s play, as many finfluencers make it out to be. The trouble is, many people are now going “direct”, ill-equipped to do so without advice. Many more are going with a steadily growing army of intermediaries whose “incidental” advice comes with a risk of bias—they have an incentive to recommend products where they get a good cut. And yes, the investor pays for this cut with a lower return.
The good fix? Going “direct” with unbiased advice at an affordable fee. Impossible trinity? Well, the solution to the vexed RIA conundrum may lie within, warts and all.

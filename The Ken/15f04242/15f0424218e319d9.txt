Due to the amount of time telecom equipment and communications tech companies spend in courtrooms these days, even simple arguments relating to them sound like legalese, or courtroom repartee.  

Sample this: “They are entitled to do it because it encourages innovation.” “Where is the evidence that they have overcharged? Where is the harm to the industry, the ecosystem?” Where is the evidence of abuse?” and “Ultimately, you have to agree that no harm is being done.”

This is an excerpt from a recent panel discussion in Bengaluru where an academic vociferously argued on behalf of Qualcomm, the chipmaker that owns a host of technologies that go into smartphones and other devices. Even as we speak, Apple and Qualcomm are engaged in a game-changing lawsuit that will decide the royalty fee model of the chipmaker and its business' fate.

In India, Qualcomm is busy building the opinion base. By funding conferences and giving grants to law schools, including ~$1.5 million to one in Haryana. Ericsson, which isn’t big on soft power and has followed the litigation route in India, is upping its ante in hiring legal luminaries for its cases. 

But each time patent holders like Ericsson and Qualcomm flex their muscles, an Indian Original Equipment Manufacturer loses out. With 3G and 4G, it was different. With 5G, and as we move toward a connected world, it’s going to be more painful for Indian OEMs.

“How do we know these companies will not seek royalty, for say, an entire car [if it is connected] or a meter [if it is a smart meter],” asks a lawyer. Having missed out on the earlier opportunities, can India do something about 5G?

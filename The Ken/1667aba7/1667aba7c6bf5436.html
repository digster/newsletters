Sixteen years ago, an article appeared in a business journal called Strategy+Business.
 
A little over fourteen pages, the article had an almost titillating title - 'Fortune at the Bottom of the Pyramid'. The premise was bold and counter-intuitive - by focusing efforts on targeting the most lucrative customers for their products globally, companies were missing out on something else - a much larger, untapped market. The authors argued that the vast numbers of people who could not afford its products weren't a limitation, but an opportunity. 'Inclusive capitalism', was the phrase that C.K Prahalad and Stuart L. Hart used in their article. 
 
There are several companies who did this successfully. Aravind Eye Hospitals. Grameen Bank. Which brings me to today's story. Reliance Jio is trying to add themselves to that list, through their inexpensive, 4G-enabled feature phone - JioPhone. And it's succeeding too. JioPhone is gaining these low-end customers at a rapid clip - nearly 55 million sold so far, and expected to add another 30 million by the end of the year. That's considerably faster than most smartphone players in India. 
 
But where there's fortune to be made, there's a feud in progress. Shashi chronicles this war. Reliance Jio is tussling with the other telcos for these users - most of whom can barely afford to come online. He writes about the incentives being offered by Reliance Jio, their strategy and tactics, and how the incumbents are responding. He calls it a 'low ARPU bet', and that today's low-yield users will become Jio's most valuable ones tomorrow. It's about a war that's going on, and one that we don't see every day, which makes it a story worth reading and sharing. To quote from Prahalad and Hart's article:
 
'Information poverty may be the single biggest roadblock to sustainable development. More than half of humanity has yet to make a single phone call. However, where telephones and Internet connections do exist, for the first time in history, it is possible to imagine a single, interconnected market uniting the world’s rich and poor in the quest for truly sustainable economic development. The process could transform the digital divide into a digital dividend.'

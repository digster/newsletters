Good Morning Ishan,

One of the habits I religiously follow is to always refuel my car to a full tank, and that too once it's nearly out of fuel. For years I did it because it was the only way to track what mileage I was getting in Bangalore’s notorious stop-and-go traffic. (Tracking vehicle mileage is a uniquely Indian obsession.)

I still follow this habit, even though newer cars these days calculate real-time mileage. In Bangalore, the Silicon Valley of India, I’ve been averaging 16-17 km per hour (or, ~10 miles per hour) for the last 18 months. And on average, I spend 24-28 hours inside my car between refuels. 

Suffice it to say, urban traffic in India is a mess. 

But Niti Aayog, the Superman of India’s policy-making bodies, wants to remedy that. Using "Intelligent Transport Systems”, Artificial Intelligence and of course, that hoary chestnut of innovation, “hackathons”. It envisages a glorious future where vehicles equipped with RFID tags seamlessly whiz past sensors that read number plates and deduct appropriate charges via cashless transactions. AI algorithms will analyse traffic patterns and appropriately decide routing and pricing strategies.

But India, in all its glorious diversity and plurality, refuses to play this game.

For instance, the project to give India a common standard for public transport cards is currently going nowhere because Bangalore Metro adopted a different legacy standard. That, in turn, forced Bangalore’s public bus operator, the BMTC, to adopt that too, so that Bangalore could have a common card. Same’s the case with the Delhi Metro and bus network. 

Solving this will not just require hackathons, but money too. Of which there isn’t enough to go around. India’s cities contribute 65% of its GDP but get back only 0.5% for functioning. 

Author Srinivas Kodali delves into the complex and loopy problems facing Niti Aayog. It must standardise and modernise Indian roads and traffic by trying to leapfrog past enmeshed legacies.

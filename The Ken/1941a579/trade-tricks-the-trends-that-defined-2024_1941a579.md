Trade Tricks
Tue, 31 Dec 24
A weekly newsletter about the biggest changes in commerce—focusing on shifts that matter to you..
Good Morning Ishan,
Welcome to the last Trade Tricks edition of 2024.
Given how interesting the year has been for Indian commerce and retail, I thought it would only be right to take a quick look at some of the defining trends and stories of 2024.
[Zomato doesn’t want to make a big deal of its disruption](https://the-ken.com/tradetricks/zomato-doesnt-want-to-make-a-big-deal-of-its-disruption/)
So why is Zomato so sure that Blinkit it’s not hurting kiranas? Maybe it has data that we’re not privy to. But I think it’s not about data at all.
Every few years, there’s a seemingly existential threat to kiranas. First, it was the emergence of supermarket chains like Dmart and Reliance Fresh in the 2000s, then it was the rise of e-tailers such as Amazon and Flipkart. There were several bleeding-heart stories about how the poor little neighbourhood grocer was going to be put out of business by these billion-dollar companies, some of which were funded with foreign capital.
But nothing really happened to the grocer. In fact, two years ago, I [wrote](https://the-ken.com/tradetricks/why-im-more-loyal-to-my-kirana-than-to-bigbasket/) about why I preferred the kirana next to my house over ordering on Bigbasket.
[…]
But do you know who has actually replaced Bhagwati as my go-to grocer now?
Blinkit.
[…]
I’m not quite convinced that Blinkit hasn’t impacted kiranas.
I understand, though, why Zomato would want you to believe it hasn’t. The reason is in all the perception battles that large retailers and e-tailers have had to fight over the years. Given how unequal the fight is, these companies don’t want to be seen crushing mom-and-pop stores.

[The tyranny of choice in online shopping is no longer a given](https://the-ken.com/tradetricks/the-tyranny-of-choice-in-online-shopping-is-no-longer-a-given/)
What Ansari and co-creator Alan Yang tapped into was the “paradox of choice”, a concept popular for two decades now. I’m sure you’re already familiar with it. It’s something we experience often, particularly when we shop online.
A surfeit of options in every product category —> decision fatigue —> dissatisfaction with the eventual choice.
[…]
I was quick to dismiss Blinkit, Zepto, and Instamart’s diversification into non-grocery products. But now, I love that they barely give me any choices in several items and have no reviews, which only reinforced the stasis I found myself in when I used to be a frequent Amazon user.
I have purchased a bunch of things on quick-commerce apps, from a toiletry bag to shorts to tennis balls for my toddler. It’s not necessarily low-value items, though. Next on my list is carry-on luggage, which could cost Rs 8,000–10,000 (US$95–120). Once again, “good enough” is just fine by me. I’m not looking for the most-bought bag or the best-reviewed one.

[Nykaa is doing all it can to protect its turf](https://the-ken.com/tradetricks/nykaa-is-doing-all-it-can-to-protect-its-turf/)
But consumers tend to be more discerning with cosmetics than with, say, socks or sunglasses. They wouldn’t want to trust just about any brand with your skin or hair. And they may be partial to the mascara of one company while being vehemently opposed to using its concealer.
What matters more than the options on quick-commerce apps is the fact that one can lay their hands on the product of their choice in just minutes.
Nykaa knows it can’t take this lightly.
[…]
As of June, one in every four orders placed on Nykaa was delivered the same day or the following day. By this month, the e-tailer expects that number to rise to one in two orders. Not surprisingly, Nykaa’s cost of storing, packing, and shipping beauty products, relative to their price, is the highest it has been in nearly two years.
Nykaa’s response is a no-brainer—beauty brought in over 90% of Nykaa’s revenue in the June quarter. Nykaa’s parent FSN E-Commerce Ventures’ shares are up by a fifth this year, but the S&P BSE Midcap Index has risen by a third. FSN is down almost 50% from the day of its listing in late 2021.
[Dmart is not used to being in a funk for so long](https://the-ken.com/tradetricks/trade-tricks-dmart-is-not-used-to-being-in-a-funk-for-so-long/)
Don’t get me wrong, Dmart is still worth a heck of a lot. With a market capitalisation of almost US$32 billion, it’s among the 25 largest listed companies in India.
But it’s no longer the company that other retailers are benchmarked against. That privilege is now Trent’s.
[…]
There’s one reason for that: [Zudio](https://the-ken.com/story/westside-makes-way-for-a-new-star-in-tatas-retail-universe-zudio/), the value retailer known for its sub-Rs 1,000 (US$12) apparel and shoes. Set up in 2016, almost two decades after Westside, Zudio already has over 400 stores. In a matter of months, there will be two Zudio outlets for every Westside store.
Few expected Zudio’s spectacular rise to harm Dmart. But as I’ve [written](https://the-ken.com/tradetricks/zudio-wanted-dmarts-apparel-shoppers-dmart-is-now-hurting/) before, the kind of shoppers Zudio targets are exactly those that are drawn to Dmart’s cheap t-shirts and sandals.
Zudio is just as inexpensive, and shopping there is not the endurance test that visiting Dmart has become. I’ve never seen a Dmart store that doesn’t look like the outside of Shah Rukh Khan’s house on his birthday.
[…]
Zudio showed that it was possible for a seasoned retailer to pull off something new. This begs the question: why can’t Dmart do the same?
Because there are limits to what a grocer can do.

[Wearables brand Boat’s impressive run was too good to last](https://the-ken.com/tradetricks/wearables-brand-boats-impressive-run-was-too-good-to-last/)
It’s not fair to expect a company to continue holding on to nearly 40% of a growing market. But there’s also trouble elsewhere.
Boat’s a challenger brand in smartwatches, which it launched in 2020. And it’s losing ground there too.
Boat is atop the wearables market as a whole, but Noise is hot on its heels. In the December quarter, Noise’s market share was 13%, compared to Boat’s 19%.
It’s not that any of these brands is vastly superior to Boat in tech or design, even though Noise [raised](https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/tech/funding/noise-valued-at-420-million-in-10-million-funding-from-bose-corporation/articleshow/106269843.cms?from=mdr#) US$10 million from audio giant Bose Corporation in December.
They are all mass-market brands that are almost perfect substitutes for each other, like Micromax and Lava once were. And they all have their moment in the sun because they are able to underprice their rivals and push their products aggressively on online marketplaces such as Amazon and Flipkart.

[PVR Inox is fine with you walking out 15 minutes into a movie](https://the-ken.com/tradetricks/pvr-inox-is-fine-with-you-walking-out-15-minutes-into-a-movie/)
Customers not staying in their seats until the closing credits begin rolling is a nightmare for any cinema operator. One, they leave miffed that they paid a pretty penny for a movie they couldn’t sit through. Two, they kvetch on social media or tell their friends about their experience.
The July–August period wasn’t a bad one for PVR Inox, thanks to films like Stree 2 and Kalki 2898 AD. Occupancy rose sharply, but the growth was noticeable only because the company had had a couple of rough quarters running up to this point.
But what these numbers hide is the fact that even in the best quarter here, the theatre operator couldn’t match the pre-Covid peak occupancy of [36%](https://the-ken.com/story/pvr-inox-stacks-the-odds-against-its-own-subscription-service/). PVR Inox knows how capricious this business is, and has been quite successful at squeezing more out of moviegoers. Food and beverages (F&B), which contribute a third to PVR Inox’s top line, outgrew ticket sales in FY24. I have [written](https://the-ken.com/tradetricks/if-you-think-pvr-inox-is-just-a-multiplex-giant-youre-wrong/) about how its F&B revenue puts it among India’s largest fast-food chains.
PVR Inox—the product of a merger between PVR and Inox Leisure—has even tried to persuade consumers that they are better off thinking about the number of movies they watch rather than how much they enjoy them. But, as we [argued](https://the-ken.com/story/pvr-inox-stacks-the-odds-against-its-own-subscription-service/) after its rollout a year ago, PVR Inox’s subscription plan is a product where “terms and conditions” take on a new meaning.
In other words, the past couple of years has been a story of one hedge after another against the Friday blues that showbiz folks are all too familiar with.
[Raw Pressery’s last-ditch attempt at being relevant is a U-turn on its original promise](https://the-ken.com/tradetricks/raw-presserys-last-ditch-attempt-at-being-relevant-is-a-u-turn-on-its-original-promise/)
When I got on a call with Anju Srivastava, the founder of Wingreens Farms, on a recent Monday morning, I jumped straight to the point.
Why was Raw Pressery—a company Wingreens acquired in 2021—entering a category that was the very opposite of what the brand has stood for all this time? I was talking about Raw’s new energy drink line, for which a formal launch announcement hasn’t been made yet.
Energy drinks, after all, are widely associated with unhealthy levels of sugar and caffeine, and a brand image that screams “machismo”. Raw Pressery, founded by Anuj Rakyan in 2014, is on the very opposite end of the spectrum: a clean-label brand, selling beverages you are more likely to get at your local juice-wala, minus the dust and the flies. Before it was acquired by Wingreens, the company had managed to capture a good-sized chunk of the market, and raise significant funding from marquee investors such as Peak XV Partners (formerly Sequoia) and DSG Consumer Partners.
Srivastava was not defiant. Instead, she asked me why I thought energy drinks were unhealthy. They had high amounts of caffeine and sugar, which people tended to over consume, I answered. Srivastava, an advertising professional-turned-entrepreneur (she launched Wingreens in 2008) had a response to each of these points.

[Reebok needs redemption as badly as Aditya Birla Fashion needs a breakout success](https://the-ken.com/tradetricks/reebok-needs-redemption-as-badly-as-aditya-birla-fashion-needs-a-breakout-success/)
But Reebok’s market leadership turned out to be a smokescreen. In 2012, Adidas disclosed that it had uncovered financial irregularities, including inflated sales, worth Rs 870 crore (US$104 million) at Reebok’s India unit.
As Adidas was left to pick up the pieces, global rivals such as Puma, Asics, and Skechers stepped on the gas in India. In the year ended March 2015, Skechers’ India revenue was a mere Rs 80 crore (US$9.6 million)—or one fourth of Reebok’s top line. Seven years later, Skechers’ sales had shot up to nearly Rs 1,000 crore (US$120 million), which was 2X Reebok’s.
There are several reasons for Skechers’ success in India, but I’d wager that their range of slip-on walking shoes has been key. If you see an elderly person on their morning walk, chances are it’s Skechers on their feet. Skechers eschewed being cool in favour of catering to an underserved demographic. Asics, on the other hand, positioned itself as a brand for serious runners.
But Reebok continued to be rudderless. Then, in 2022, Aditya Birla Fashion and Retail (ABFRL) acquired the rights to distribute and sell it in India.

[Tata uses Westside strategy to save its supermarkets](https://the-ken.com/tradetricks/tata-uses-westside-strategy-to-save-its-supermarkets/)
Trent must have realised years ago that it didn’t have the money or vision to play the scale game against Dmart and Reliance Retail, which makes Dmart look puny by comparison. But it has fared better than them on one front.
When Trent announced its quarterly numbers over the weekend, it revealed that private labels accounted for three-quarters of Star’s revenue. Two years ago, they contributed just half. Analyst estimates suggest that the number is under 10% for Dmart and 15–20% for Reliance.
Every retailer wants to sell as many store brands as possible. Simply because they are a lot more profitable than selling someone else’s products. The strategy has been the most successful one in the apparel industry of India.
And no one does it better than Trent.
Westside is the gold standard when it comes to in-house brands. A decade ago, they brought in 70% of its revenue. Now, Westside sells nothing but its own. Trent then took the same approach with Zudio and decided to price everything under Rs 1,000 (US$12). In short order, it became India’s [hottest retailer](https://www.cnbctv18.com/market/trent-share-price-market-cap-crosses-2-lakh-crore-record-high-tata-group-westside-zudio-returns-19438622.htm).
So why not replicate the same in grocery?

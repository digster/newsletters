Good Morning Ishan,
 
“You're on the desk, right? I want to discuss a story with you.”
 
This was one of the first sentences spoken to me by my colleague, Harveen. It was an hour into my first day at The Ken. I braced myself for a convoluted, jargonistic, flow of consciousness ramble. Nothing against Harveen, of course. I barely knew her. But this is how a lot of story ideas start out. 
 
What she said next though, got my attention.
 
“I want to do a story on Kodak in India,” she said.
 
Kodak. A name I hadn't heard in god knows how long. What was the story, I asked? Kodak's obituary, after all, had been written years prior.
 
“Revival,” she said.
 
Kodak's is an interesting story. Founded in 1888, it was one of the most iconic brands in photography and film. Remember the Kodak Moment? Odds are, you do. For a lot of us, our earliest memories were documented on Kodak cameras. On Kodak film. Preserved in Kodak photo albums.
 
But Kodak fell off at the worst time possible–the dawn of the digital era. Ironically, Kodak ushered in the digital era in photography by inventing the first digital camera. But their head start counted for nothing as they were swept away by the digital wave. It seems almost cruel that in today's world where everyone's a photographer, one of the world's preeminent camera brands is all but invisible.
 
But the company never died. In America, it still does all the things most of us know Kodak for. In India, it pivoted from consumers to businesses. Now, Kodak is turning back towards consumers. And with a whole new line of offerings. This is what today's story is about. It's the culmination of an offhand chat in the office two months ago. That's something I've grown to love about The Ken in my short time here—my colleagues are able to turn curiosity and passion into something tangible and meaningful.

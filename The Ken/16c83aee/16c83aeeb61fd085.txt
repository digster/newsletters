In a few hours today Reliance Industries, India’s largest private conglomerate, is expected to announce ambitious rollout plans for JioGigaFiber, its wired broadband service. Like its wireless cousin, Jio, JioGigaFiber too has been in beta testing before a public launch. But while Jio would go on to have one of the most disruptive launches in global telecom history, JioGigaFiber is likely to have a much more muted debut.

Because rolling out a pan-India fibre network is way tougher than rolling out a wireless one. Even for Reliance. Scratch that, especially for Reliance, whose zero-sum, take-no-prisoners style of competition is legion. 

But like Seema and Pranav lay out in detail in today’s article, fibre may be the one to finally teach Reliance that sometimes, just sometimes, sharing is caring. Already, Jio’s ambitious rollout plans have been dialled down. From covering fewer cities to offering data speeds lower than the promised 100Mbps minimum to abandoning plans for an all-underground fibre network, Jio has had to face reality.

In a sprawling analysis across geographic imperatives, economic rationales and competitive dynamics, Seema and Pranav make the compelling case for the last players standing in Indian telecom—Jio, Bharti Airtel and Vodafone Idea—to bury their hatchets and figure out a way to share their fibre networks. 

“Fibre sharing has to happen. Nobody can stop that,” said a Jio executive. But which fibre is to be shared, that is the question. Because while every wireless tower could be useful to every telco, the same can’t be said of every fibre.

Good Morning Ishan,

Trastuzumab and filgrastim sound like typos. At least that’s the first thought that came to my mind as I read today’s story (one of the perks of being a writer at The Ken is that you get to read stories before they’re published).

But then I read this line: "After 19 years on the market, it (trastuzumab) is still one of the top-selling drugs for Roche, adding more than $30 billion in sales in just last five years.”

Trastuzumab is a drug used to treat breast cancer. And on 1 December, Biocon, in partnership with Mylan, got the approval to manufacture and sell its “biosimilar” for trastuzumab in the US market. Biosimilars are near-identical copies of biologic drugs that are about to fade out of patent protection.

Remember the other tongue twister drug I mentioned, filgrastim? It is a drug that treats extreme blood infections, often caused due to chemotherapy or HIV/AIDS. While Amgen’s Filgrastim was approved for medical use in the US in 1991, it would be 2015 when the first of its competing biosimilars were approved. In just 4 months, rival Sandoz's biosimilar had a 24% share of the market.

That is what Biocon and Mylan will be hoping for with Ogivri (that’s the brand name for their trastuzumab biosimilar). 

But getting FDA approval is just the start of the race because snapping at their heels are drug giants like Amgen-Allergan, Pfizer, Samsung Bioepis and Celltrion developing biosimilars of trastuzumab and preparing to launch. By some elaborate napkin math for trastuzumab, Biocon-Mylan would look at sharing half of the $4.5 billion US-EU market with 3-4 contenders, while being the first mover for some time.

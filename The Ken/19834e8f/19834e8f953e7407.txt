The fact that Reliance Industries is carving out its FMCG arm from its retail company isn’t at all surprising. The retail and FMCG businesses, while linked in their fortunes, are very different beasts.

For one, retail is as much about real estate as it is about the product mix. And FMCG thrives on feet on the street and on finding shelf space at stores large and small.

Plus, Reliance has to take its retail holding company Reliance Retail Ventures, which has a number of private-equity and sovereign-wealth-fund backers, public sooner than later. Having a large FMCG company (Reliance Consumer Products) in the mix makes the story it needs to tell investors somewhat knotty.

But there is something opportunistic about the FMCG spin-off. The timing.

Hindustan Unilever’s chief executives almost never step down before their term ends. In fact, past leaders like Nitin Paranjpe, Harish Manwani, and Manvinder Singh Banga were bumped up to global roles at Unilever.

On the other hand, Sanjiv Mehta, who steered Hindustan Unilever, or HUL, for a decade, retired in 2023. So when his successor, Rohit Jawa, called it quits this month to “pursue the next chapter in his personal and professional journey”, less than halfway into his five-year tenure, investors should have panicked.

But the exact opposite happened.

The stock rose almost 5%. Analysts were almost dizzy with excitement that there was a new sheriff in town at India’s tenth-largest listed company by market value. And not without reason.

HUL has struggled to maintain growth momentum in recent quarters. The company reported 2% volume growth for FY25—the same as the previous year. While its laundry business held steady, categories such as beauty, personal care, and packaged foods saw pressure amid inflation and rising competition.

HUL’s volume growth has not crossed 5% in the past six years.

In its research note, Jefferies said HUL’s performance has been “lacklustre for several quarters”.

This is not just an HUL problem. It’s an FMCG problem.

Suresh Narayanan, managing director of Nestlé India who is stepping down next week, admitted publicly what most CEOs had only been comfortable discussing behind closed doors. In October, after Nestlé’s slowest quarter in eight years, Narayanan spoke of a “shrinking” middle class, which has powered FMCG companies’ rise for years.

The sector is clearly in a deep funk. In the June quarter, FMCG sales by volume in urban India fell 2.6% https://www.livemint.com/industry/indian-fmcg-growth-q1-2025-rural-fmcg-demand-india-urban-fmcg-demand-slowdown-inflation-impact-fmcg-india-edible-oil-11746680267898.html, both from the previous three months and from the same period last year.

Consumers are buying less than before not just because of costly packaged foods, a result of high edible-oil prices. This is what Sanjeev Prasad, MD of broking firm Kotak Institutional Equities and an astute observer of capital markets, had to say:

The challenge still remains that India is simply not creating enough good-quality jobs. It is creating jobs, there’s no doubt about that…

There’s a lot of government data based on the Labour Force Survey. Incrementally, what we are seeing is a lot of new rural female workers are ending up in agriculture. And a lot of urban male workers seem to be ending up in the gig economy, where income levels are not going to be very high anyway.

Reliance has chosen what is definitely a precarious time for its competitors to make its FMCG unit a direct subsidiary. There’s no denying the scale of the venture, which sells kitchen staples, beverages, soaps, and detergents. With sales of Rs 11,500 crore in FY25, it’s bigger than Marico and Wipro Consumer Care & Lighting, and not far behind Dabur India. In the June quarter, Reliance’s FMCG sales doubled year-on-year.

In February, my colleague Aakriti wrote https://the-ken.com/story/pepsis-biggest-bottler-is-pouring-more-cola-to-fight-reliances-campa/ about how Reliance Consumer was shaking up the carbonated-drinks market with Campa Cola, a once-popular brand it bought in 2022.

By pricing Campa Cola at half as much as Pepsi and Coca-Cola, and offering retailers higher margins than its rivals, Reliance Consumer has managed to snag a 10% market share in some states.

Dinesh Taluja, Reliance Retail’s chief financial officer, has said that while the FMCG business benefits from in-house supermarkets, the company has built a “substantial independent distribution system”, with mom-and-pop stores contributing 70% of its sales.

Fortunately for Reliance, its FMCG expansion is playing out at a time of soul-searching for those it looks to challenge. Kotak’s Sanjeev Prasad summed it up best in the aforementioned interview.

It’s across sectors, you are seeing a lot of changes in the market structure, etc, which is resulting in the incumbents starting to lose the dominance which they had in their respective categories or sectors at one point in time. Markets have become more accommodative, products are losing, I would say partly brands are losing relevance, distribution channels are no longer as strong for incumbents as they used to be historically.

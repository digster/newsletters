Deep inside the arrhythmically beating hearts (why they’re all sick, of course) of Indian television, sits a monopoly. A monopoly with a vast, unmatched network of video journalists across the length and breadth of India. A network that sends in dozens, sometimes even hundreds, of videos each day from cities, towns and villages across the country’s 36 states and union territories. In Delhi, the seat of political power, its access to government is unprecedented, often being the only one whose cameras are allowed during major news announcements.
On the top floor of its five-storey HQ in Delhi reside decades worth of video archives, covering epochal Indian events for which there are few other sources. Ten seconds of one of its archival videos costs TV channels roughly Rs 10,000. For some of the clips, though, the prices could be even higher, as there aren't many other sources.
The history of this powerful, and yet strangely secretive organisation (“we don’t speak to the media” was their response to our questions) spans decades. Back to the 1950s. Many have tried to take it on but failed. It’s tightly family controlled but has a significant ownership from Reuters, the world’s 2nd largest news agency. There are rumours of internecine conflict around ownership within the family.
Unless you’re an Indian news or Twitter (politics) junkie, you may not have heard of ANI or Asian News International. It’s the eyes and ears of India. And it’s an enigma.

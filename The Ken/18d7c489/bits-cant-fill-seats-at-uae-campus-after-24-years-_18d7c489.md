Good Morning Ishan,
In international diplomacy, grand roadshows are reserved for special guests. This dignitary last month in Ahmedabad was Mohamed bin Zayed Al Nahyan, President of the United Arab Emirates (UAE). Indian Prime Minister Narendra Modi welcomed Al Nahyan by referring to him as “my brother”, and they walked hand-in-hand.
With a good reason.
Trade—both oil and non-oil—between the two countries has been flourishing. And at about 3.5 million, Indians form the largest expat group in the rich West Asian nation.
It’s no surprise that scores of Indian businesses have set up or are setting up their outposts in the UAE. The business of higher education has also been at it—for years now—with reputed Indian institutions pitching their tents.
Sadly, without much to show for their exertions. India’s storied engineering college, BITS Pilani, for instance, has been in the UAE for over two decades but struggles to fill seats even today. Savitribai Phule Pune University, with 600,000 students in India, was forced to shut operations in the UAE after failing to achieve its target of 60 enrolments.
But hope springs eternal in the human breast. The desire for higher rankings, too. Plus, of course, commercial interests. All this catalysed by a policy push.
So, India’s second-best engineering institute, IIT Delhi, is trying its luck now and will start its first-ever batch in Abu Dhabi this month.
Can it run where many others stumbled? What are the pitfalls? Is there a formula to ace this test?
Let’s just say there’s competition galore. As Alifiya, the author of today’s deeply reported story, puts it nicely, “Cracking the UAE code needs more than just brotherhood.”

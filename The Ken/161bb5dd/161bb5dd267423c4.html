Remember the Idiot Box? You know, the large box that used to be the central feature of the erstwhile drawing room? Well, now we have flat screens and laptops and tablets and even smartphones to show us everything that box ever could, and so much more.
 
With the advent of OTT (over-the-top or on-demand) video services like Netflix, Hotstar, Voot, we're all moving towards paying our cable guys less and subscription platforms more. And the more we watch our sport and sitcoms on these platforms, the greater is the need for them to buffer seamlessly. Enter peering.
 
Peering allows for direct data-sharing instead of through the internet. But like everything that's too-good-to-be-true, it's questionable. Some internet service providers (ISP) have tied up with the likes of Google and Facebook, and, well, let's just say YouTube plays way faster than Dailymotion on their service. And that throws open the net neutrality debate.
 
Sidhartha's piece today aims to get this debate started with regard to peering. "But while the public debate around net neutrality has mostly been about slow and fast lanes, ISPs and big corporations, it is important to bring in the middlemen to the forefront to ensure that we keep the internet an open and level playing field for everyone," he writes.

A few days ago, during a conversation with a founder, we went back and forth about where the profit pools for Indian consumer startups were. The heart of what we were discussing was this question—if you had to launch a fresh consumer-tech startup from scratch with zero funding, which business would you get into knowing that there’s a reasonable chance of making money in the short to medium term?

This is not a straightforward question. That’s because it eliminates three key differentiators that VCs bet on all the time—capital, execution, and proprietary tech. Assuming all startups had access to the same capital, with relatively little difference in their execution skills, and access to specific technology, what kind of startups would almost be guaranteed to make money? 

Over the course of our discussion we came up with three areas—brokerage, gaming, and astrology.

If you take the list of consumer tech startups in India, and look at the ones that are profitable (and I mean profit-after-tax profitable, not any of this adjusted ebitda stuff), that’s a pretty short list. Eliminate the ones that have been founded, before, say, 2010, and that list gets even shorter. Specifically, if you consider startups that have attained a certain scale, all the startups in that list fall in three distinct categories—brokerage companies like Zerodha and Groww; gaming companies like Gameskraft, Dream11, and Gameberry; and finally, astrology companies like Astrotalk. That’s more or less it. 

What does this list tell you about India’s consumer market? 

This is a much harder question to answer. 

The general premise of tech startups is that technology reduces the variable cost to zero, which gives them a big cost advantage, specifically in distribution, that flows straight to their bottom line. It costs a company like, say, Meta almost nothing to “produce” another page on Instagram. But it’s easier to make revenue from it. This is what gives it an advantage over brick-and-mortar businesses. 

This exists along a spectrum. On one hand, there are “pure” tech companies which have no offline operations. Netflix. Meta. Microsoft. Let’s call them non-operational tech startups. Then there are tech companies that have some degree of offline operations, either in creating products or acting as a two-sided network connecting demand and supply. Apple and Tesla are the former. Uber and Amazon are the latter. 

In the Western market, both types of companies make money and are profitable. 

In India, only one of them does. And it’s neither of the above.

The non-operational tech companies in the West succeed either because they have exclusive access to some kind of supply (Netflix), or have network effects (Meta), or proprietary tech (Microsoft). These are all advantages that these companies have accumulated over the years due to a combination of timing, tech, and capital. 

India appears to be different. 

The list of startups that are profitable in India don’t rely specifically on proprietary tech, or capital or network effects. They are companies that picked sectors and placed themselves in the middle of transactions between users and other entities, and took a cut from it. Zerodha and Groww sit in the middle of a user making a transaction to buy stocks and shares. Gameskraft connects users to a game, and wins money. And Astrotalk sits in between a user and an astrologer. 

What’s common to all of these companies? And what does it tell us about how to build profitable tech startups in India? 

I can think of three specific nuances.

One way to think about these companies is that they all operate in sectors with low variable costs and no physical operations. Flipkart, Amazon, Ola, Zomato, Swiggy aren’t profitable (yet) because they all have some kind of physical investments and operations. Maybe they’ll eventually be profitable, but over a much longer time horizon, and with a greater degree of investments. 

Another way to think about it is that all the profitable tech startups have one common promise to users—they help them “make” money instead of spending money to get a service. Maybe this is why a company like, say, Netflix is much less likely to be profitable in India than a Gameskraft. Even though both provide “entertainment”, only one of them lures you with the promise of financial gain. Same for Zerodha and Groww, which help you make investments and trade in a market for making money. Indians seem to be more likely okay with paying money to make money instead of paying for a service. 

The final way, and my favourite way to think about it, is that all these three types of companies are the ones who are somewhat vulnerable to AI. 

My favourite example of this is Astrotalk. Specifically, it’s just a company that connects people to astrologers and takes a cut in the middle. I can’t think of a more Indian company. Last year, it made nearly Rs 100 crore (US$11.5 million) in profit. And it’s been around for just about a couple of years. 

And well, it’s having some uniquely Indian problems.

Originally, the company and the astrologer would have a 50-50 revenue split for each chat or call, an astrologer told Mint. If a customer recharged ₹100, the payment charges would first be deducted, and the remainder would be split equally between the company and the astrologer, with tax deducted from the latter’s payout.

But many astrologers alleged that Astrotalk has been recouping its heavy marketing spend by taking a cut from their pockets. “They initially said that the company’s share would increase to 60% because it would spend 10% on marketing. When it launched other applications, the company said it would reduce the astrologer’s share from 40% to 30%, increasing the company’s share to 70%, because it would be spending 20% on marketing,” said one soothsayer.

Today, astrologers are asked to speak with a customer for more than 30 minutes to avail the original 50-50 payment split—the company takes 70% of the share for the first 30 minutes.

“Often we have to just stretch the conversation to reach the 30-minute point,” said one astrologer, noting that the profiles of those who have converted fewer customers do not appear before new users. Another said he recently received a call from the company offering training on converting users into loyal customers.

A sales job: What Astrotalk’s 41,000 astrologers didn’t see in their future, Mint

And here’s my favourite part.

“Even if you want to, in astrology, you can’t consult somebody for over half an hour. As he is paying by the minute, he also asks pointed questions and expects straightforward answers,” said a fourth astrologer, who left the platform a few months back.

“If you keep on lying and go roundabout with your answers, only then can you convert them into loyal customers,” said another astrologer, adding that customer support told him to suggest remedies to customers and ask them to come back and talk about the outcome. Mint couldn’t verify this as the source did not have a screenshot of the chat.

We’ve been struggling to find a viable use-case for AI for a while now, but it looks like we’ve finally found one. Astrology. I can imagine a future where AI agents can help you trade stocks and shares, or play games and make money on your behalf.

I find the precarity of this quite interesting. On one hand, all profitable Indian tech startups solve similar needs for Indian consumers. On the other hand, they are also the ones who are the most vulnerable to AI. 

Maybe this is what makes India so incredible and special.

Good Morning Ishan,
 
What a difference one generation of technology makes. Yesterday, we saw Reliance Jio announce full-stack 5G telecom capability, not only for its Indian customers but for any eager telco out there.
 
The other reality is that India’s state-owned telco BSNL is scrambling to put together a consortium that can build a 4G network. Still, a gaping chasm exists between the intent and the execution.
 
The geopolitical gust is blowing apart the Chinese hegemony in 4G & 5G networks tech. Earlier this week, the UK banned Huawei’s 5G gear from its network with retrospective effect. BSNL has scrapped its ~$1.2 billion 4G tender, more than 60% of which would have otherwise been built by Chinese vendors.
 
Since then, several rounds of meetings have been held to assess what Indian local vendors have, what they can build, and what they absolutely can’t do in the given time. For the first time, IT services companies have been called to the table to lead the consortium.
 
These are also system integrators for the biggest telcos & equipment makers in the world—AT&T, British Telecom, Ericsson—and yet they are hesitant. There’s a clash of interest—not just with their existing clients but with their investee companies— and there’s ambiguity on whether the government wants an unrealistic 100% localisation or a realistic 30-50%.
 
Can India build an Ericsson or a Nokia overnight? No.
Can it build reasonable capability in 24 months? Yes.
Does it have the will to change its processes and payments to pull off a telecom consortium? Can’t say.
 
In May, we wrote about how BSNL's tender was almost designed to exclude small and Indian companies in favour of the Chinese. Pratap has followed the story. And boy, how it has evolved. He says the government is making a list of sensitive items in telecoms with security implications, and will likely ban their imports from neighbouring countries.

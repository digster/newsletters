Good Morning Ishan,
 
Do you remember this old Coldplay album from the subject line? It went platinum nine times and sold 2.9 million copies. Wikipedia swear.
 
But why do I bring up this dated reference? Because 'A Rush Of Blood To The Head' featured some of the band's best work. I argue that our cache of stories from the last week did the same. In tone, depth, detail and delivery, each nailed the complex set of areas The Ken takes interest in. 
 
There's another reason why I bring up the album. Two weeks in, every day at The Ken has been a rush of blood to the head for me. Get up, get coffee, get moving. These in-depth stories don't just happen. We struggle with sources and pore over reports. More than anything, we agonise over words that convey a sea of meaning, without it actually being a sea of words. Take for example the instructions given to me with the task of writing the weekend round-up. "Use the privilege of writing to tens of thousands of people wisely." Oof.
 
Invoking a music reference is then apt to tell you readers about our story from last Sunday. Our contributor, Karthik Srinivasan, writes about how technology is attacking artificial language 'bubbles' around playlists to make them diverse and multilingual-just like South Indians really are. Do read it, all our weekend stories are currently free.
 
Monday was an absolute thunderbolt. Arundhati and Ashish teamed up to report on all the high jugglery with the PayTM Payments Bank. They report on the chequered history of payments banks in India, and dissect the regulatory cartwheels these banks have had to do to just survive in an 'everything goes' regulatory environment. Has the RBI now put the final nail in the coffin?
 
On Tuesday, Sidhartha wrote on the fortunes of Quick Heal, an anti-virus software company dating back to 1995. In the late 90s, Quick Heal's business model of retailing anti-virus software still worked. In fact, it still captures 34% of the retail anti-virus market. But the threat from freeware services and bundled applications is very real for this old player. Siddartha cracks into their plans to stay relevant in a freemium economy.
 
We were off on Wednesday on account of Independence Day and joined you all in watching patriotic speeches and impeccable flag hoisting ceremonies (ouch). But we did do an update. Vakrangee has been an object of fascination for The Ken for a while. The company's stock has been oscillating between the lower and upper circuits. Also, Sebi has ordered Vakrangee Holdings Private Ltd to make a public offer to shareholders of Vakrangee Ltd. This and more made us update our second story on the company, and in the spirit of I-Day, we made it free.
 
Swiggy 2.0 does alcohol. And milk. No, for real. Salman had this scoop for our Thursday story on the food-delivery behemoth. Not only has it laid big bets by increasing its fleet, its diversification into milk, grocery and booze delivery can only mean its competitors, both big and small, will be sweating it. But will shrinking margins put a stop to Swiggy's sweet, sweet plans?
 
Harveen spent days on Sharechat. And Helo. And a bunch of other home-grown social networking apps, delving not just into their business model but also fencing off 'frandship' requests from a few of the million anonymous users on these platforms. In our second free read for the week, she wrote about how Sharechat, despite its burgeoning size, might have to contend with the dual pinpricks of user retention and regulatory concerns. 
 
I know this week, like any other, was a barrage of good and bad news. I'm tired to the bone. But because it's The Ken, there's hardly time for this luxurious pause. Bring it, Week 3. 
 
As for you readers, hope you have a wonderful weekend. Listen to some Coldplay. Relax. And wait for our weekend offering in your inbox on tomorrow morning.
 
Regards,

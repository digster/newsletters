Long and Short
Wed, 23 Oct 24
The story behind the numbers and motivations driving India's stock market.
Good Morning Ishan,
When it rains, it pours.
And no, I’m not talking about the Bengaluru rains. I’m talking about Tejas Networks’ remarkable rally on the stock market lately. On Monday, the share price hit the 20% upper circuit. And on Tuesday, the stock exchange asked the telecom-equipment maker for [a clarification](https://www.bseindia.com/xml-data/corpfiling/AttachLive/53c44649-541d-494b-96d2-fca8f52cf615.pdf) on the “movement in volume”.
Earlier, on Friday, Tejas announced its second-quarter results. In the first six months of the current financial year, its revenue grew nearly 650% to Rs 4,374 crore (US$520 million) compared to the same period in the previous year. It also posted a profit of Rs 353 crore after tax, reversing from a loss of nearly Rs 40 crore earlier.
It’s hard to believe that just a few years ago, the company’s top leadership would lament their arduous haul at scaling up the business, or bolstering its market cap ever since Tejas listed in 2017. The lack of scale in India hurt its commercial bids in other markets because customers wouldn’t have enough confidence. It also affected its private-market valuation prior to listing, which in turn affected its ability to acquire businesses, either for technology or revenue.
The chicken-and-egg conundrum got resolved when a Tata Sons entity acquired a majority stake in the company in 2021. It was just before another Tata group company, Tata Consultancy Services (TCS), led a consortium to bid for BSNL’s 4G network rollout.
When we last wrote about Tejas [in late 2022](https://the-ken.com/story/the-tata-wind-beneath-tejas-networks-wings-carries-investors-away/), the stock had similarly rallied, and the company was valued at Rs 10,000 crore. We had argued then that the valuations were off the charts with a price-to-earnings multiple of more than 2,000X. One analyst had even called it a “hope” rally.

Well, with a market cap of nearly Rs 22,000 crore today, that “hope” rally seems to have become a “national pride” rally. Within the power corridors of Delhi, it is well understood that the government intends to keep a bleeding state-owned BSNL alive and kicking as the fourth telco in an increasingly duopolistic market. (Reliance Jio and Bharti Airtel combined have more than 81% revenue market share.)
The markets, judging by this week’s surge, evidently anticipate a lot more growth from Tejas’ stock.
For now, it lies at the government’s door. The half-yearly numbers don’t show a breakup, but the numbers for the year ended March 2024 provide the clue—the India-government business contributed 36% to Tejas’ net revenue. The private business in India contributed another 54%, led by business from TCS, the consortium leader and a related party in the Rs 13,000 crore BSNL 4G/5G deal.
What’s interesting to note is that while its India private business grew nearly 170%, its exports grew only 3% in the last financial year. Perhaps Tejas was too busy executing its BSNL project to focus on the international market. So far, Tejas has shipped 4G/5G RAN (radio access network) equipment—which link mobile devices to the core network via cell towers—for at least 58,000 of the 100,000 telecom sites, including 30,000 in the quarter ending September alone.
A significant portion of Tejas’ revenues comes from a small number of large customers, particularly the government. For a company desperate for scale, the BSNL deal is a good springboard, but one cannot ignore that the 4G/5G project will bring in lower margins compared to its traditional, bread-and-butter wireline business. Besides, with government contracts come long payment delays.
In early 2022, when [we wrote that BSNL](https://the-ken.com/story/pmo-stakes-bsnls-future-on-tata-and-an-atma-nirbhar-4g-gamble/) officials were unhappy with the TCS-led consortium’s progress—it had some missed deadlines and underwhelming 4G trials—we were on point. Officials close to the consortium confirmed to The Ken, even as recently as the India Mobile Congress in Delhi last week, that the readiness of the 4G equipment has caused the delay.
What was slated to be fully rolled out by Diwali 2024 has now been pushed to mid-2025.
But Tejas has used this time to shore up its tech offerings. Its acquisition of Saankhya Labs in 2022 added satellite-communications tech to its portfolio. The Ken has learnt that Saankhya kicked out Skylotech India, a satellite communications-based Internet-of-Things service provider, which [had signed](https://pib.gov.in/Pressreleaseshare.aspx?PRID=1679714) a deal with BSNL four years ago.
Now, Saankhya has received a provisional purchase order worth Rs 96.4 crore from the Department of Space for providing and installing indigenous Satcom Xponders—a two-way satcom terminal—on marine fishing vessels. It’s believed to be one of the largest satellite-IoT deployments in the world.
Saankhya is also approved under the semiconductor Design Linked Incentive scheme for the development of a System-on-Chip (SoC) for telecom-infrastructure equipment.
Before their respective acquisitions, both Tejas and Saankhya were stuck in the same boat—starved for funds and struggling to scale.
While a full 5G network for BSNL is still some years away, Tejas is adding capabilities wherever it finds them. Its improved financial situation allows it to act like an anchor OEM. For instance, in December, it licensed a 5G sub-system from a group of telecom researchers, who’d have otherwise struggled to take their technology to commercial application on their own.
The network effect in telecoms that India always lacked is showing green shoots. It’s up to Tejas to use this lifeline not just to build a global business, but also to woo private telcos in India, the world’s largest telecom market by subscriber base.
Relying on a teetering public sector unit like BSNL, even if deemed strategic by the government, can be risky in the medium-to-long term.

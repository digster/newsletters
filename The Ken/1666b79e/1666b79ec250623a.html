Good Morning Ishan,
 
I sincerely hope this finds you well. The wheel turns, seasons change and the weather brings with it a spate of illnesses. And we at The Ken, I'm sorry to say, have not proved immune to it. Adding up all the casualties, about a quarter of our staff have been suffering from one illness or another (including yours truly). But such is life, and we soldier on. Please encourage your friends, family and colleagues to subscribe and fund our mounting medical expenses. 
 
On a more serious note, health has been a theme we explored in one form or another in most of our stories over the past week.
 
We started out with our TKOW piece on Sunday, which explored a dilemma many women face across India (and the world, for that matter): caesarean or natural? My colleague Ruhi dives into the issue, tracing the journeys of two Indian women from pregnancy to childbirth, and the myriad challenges they faced.
 
On Monday, the week began with a bang: Seema set the bar with a deeply-reported story on Practo, a startup that built India's largest online directory of doctors. Unfortunately, it doesn't seem too healthy at the moment—its primary clients (physicians) no longer care about it, and the company's only solution to the growth problem seems to be to throw money at it, Seema reports.
 
We also had another straight-up healthcare story, this one from Gayathri and Ruhi on the recent controversy over 150,000 children being administered a contaminated polio vaccine. The breach was serious and highlights an urgent need to switch from oral vaccines to injectable ones—a shift India is far from ready for, they write. It's our free story for the week, so please do share it.
 
Next on my list is Salman's story on Quikr. Much like Practo, here's another startup in the throes of an existential crisis, with no business model in sight. But you wouldn't get that from the rosy picture the company paints of its realty business, which it has happily built up by, wait for it, throwing money at the problem with acquisition after acquisition. The truth is, Salman says, Quikr is still very much struggling to crack the real estate puzzle.
 
I'm sure you'll recall that news about Samsung setting up a gigantic smartphone factory in India. It was a bit of a feather in the government's cap, bolstering the image of its Make in India initiative, which hasn't made quite the splash the government wanted. The Samsung deal could very well be a watershed moment, but what India needs to boost local manufacturing is a better export policy, writes Vandana.
 
Then we had the last of our weekday stories, Sumanth and Sidhartha's take on the death of Zebpay. The company, which started one of the first cryptocurrency exchanges in India, shut shop in September, blaming the central bank's harsh regulations. But the curious thing is, the company looked quite... healthy. Revenues were up, it had turned a profit, but the founders still decided to call it quits. Why? Well, read on.
 
And before I forget, last weekend we had not one but two stories. Our Saturday offering—also a free read—was a close look at the limits of the human mind, vis-a-vis multitasking, forgetfulness and poor decision-making. It's the second instalment of the Simplicity Paradigm, our monthly series on our mental tics and what we can do to circumvent them, written by contributors Saurabh Mukherjea and Anupam Gupta. 
 
That's all for now, have a nice weekend (and stay healthy).

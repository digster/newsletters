Good Morning Ishan,
If you were to look for when the great telecom war in India truly ignited, you would probably mark this date: 5 September 2016. On that day, the country’s telecom history was rewritten in chapters: Before Jio and Post Jio.
You’ve heard it a thousand times: “Ever since Jio’s entry”. Yawn. I know.
Anyway… Ever since Jio’s entry (apologies!), Bharti Airtel, much like its peers, took a beating on its core front—phone connectivity. It took the telco a good five years to bounce back. Revenues kept going downhill until 2022, before finally scraping back up to Before Jio levels.
This fight, however, hasn’t really played out in Airtel’s enterprise business—yet.
Meet Airtel Business. The side of the telco that caters to connect enterprises, within and without. It’s already a leader in the enterprise connectivity market, with nearly a third of the revenue market share.
Last fiscal, it piled up about Rs 18,600 crore—that’s over $2 billion—a solid fifth of the company’s total revenues. Profits from this division have been strutting around more confidently than the regular consumer business.
But here’s the thing: Airtel Business has to make a call now.
The person who’s been nurturing this division for over twenty years, Ajay Chitkara, has decided it’s time to part ways. The telco’s CEO, Gopal Vittal, now has good reasons to start worrying about this side of things.
For one, industry insiders say a lack of competition in the segment has helped Airtel Business grow so far. But now, Jio is gaining ground—albeit slowly. Automobile bigwigs Skoda and MG Hector and EV makers like Ather Energy have already inked multi-crore deals with the telco.
For another, Airtel Business shares the stage with Tata Communications Ltd (TCL), a heavy-hitter in both connectivity and non-connectivity enterprise play. TCL boasts tie-ups with a slew of India’s top-tier enterprises, and it’s a rival that subscribes to the ethos of doing “more with less”.
Now, it’s up to Vittal to take a leaf out of that playbook. The telco has already merged a couple of teams and created business units with their own leaders—all of whom will answer to Vittal.
What was on the way to becoming a bright spot for the company now needs more than just a shine-up. It needs armour as competition heats up.

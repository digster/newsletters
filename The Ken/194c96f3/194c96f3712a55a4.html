Good Morning Ishan,

You are reading our subscriber-only newsletter Ed Set Go—a weekly commentary on Indian edtech and education

I hope your week is off to a great start! I know what you’re going to say about this week’s edition on Byju’s bankrupt companies: it’s going to be the reader’s equivalent of “are we there yet?”

I feel the same. But it’s important to document what’s the latest in the twisted saga of Byju’s broken edtech empire. If for nothing else but to witness how one man’s ambitions can drastically change his company’s fortunes. And the lives of those around him.

A federal judge in Wilmington, Delaware, has held two people—Vinay Ravindra and Rajendran Vellpalat—in contempt of court and sanctioned a fine of US$25,000 per day for violating the court’s orders. Ravindra is the chief content officer at Byju’s, and was a regular on Byju’s learning videos on Youtube. Vellpalat is the founder of a marketing tech firm called Voizzit Technology Ltd. The relationship between Voizzit and Byju’s is unclear, though media reports on the court case have called him an “ally”.

So, the ally and the content head have been accused of stripping Byju’s US entities EPIC! Creations and Tangible Play (which owns OSMO) of cash, resources, and essential business intelligence. These entities are under the supervision of a court-appointed trustee tasked with selling or raising money against these firms to pay back a US$1.2 billion loan that Byju Raveendran has tried hard to dodge. 

The three US entities, though, have never fallen off Raveendran’s radar. He’s tried multiple times to regain control over these companies. The latest attempt, if true, has to be the most audacious:

A “rogue officer” manipulated Apple Inc. into changing the ownership — and the revenue stream — of two apps that bring in about $1 million a month, according to the trustee, who controls three of Byju’s US units. The apps are used by parents to download education materials for their children, Catherine Steege, a lawyer for the US units said Tuesday during a virtual hearing. The transfer happened shortly after the trustee took control of the bankrupt units: Epic! Creations, Neuron Fuel, and Tangible Play.

Since the trustee, Claudia Springer, was given control of the three units by a federal judge, unknown people directed by Byju’s, which is based in India, have been grabbing cash, moving source code away from cloud systems managed by Apple and Alphabet Inc.’s Google and changing the names of various online accounts, Steege said. “These bad actors began a game of ‘catch me if you can’,” Steege told US Bankruptcy Judge John Dorsey in Wilmington, Delaware. They are “trying to stay one step ahead of the trustee.”

These actions perhaps suggest that someone, on Byju’s behalf, is trying to drain the US entities of any real value or trying to hold on to the IP of these companies, all of which still earn revenue in the US.

Maybe Raveendran’s planning a big comeback with these products in newer markets? A Whitehat Jr 2.0? 

That, of course, will only be possible if Byju’s emerges clean from bankruptcy proceedings and financial probes launched in India. But in the ultimate twist of twists, the trustee appointed by the Indian courts to supervise Byju’s Indian bankruptcy proceedings is now under the scanner too. Pankaj Srivastava had asked the US courts to hand over control for the three US-based entities back to Byju’s, but the US court refused that request. What’s more, the Indian courts have now said Srivastava unfairly kicked the US lenders’ agent, Glas Trust Co., off the creditors committee, and have since made them part of a newly formed committee. Srivastava should be under investigation, according to the Indian courts.

We need to part the muddy legal waters to understand what’s going on. One, of course, these US-based subsidiaries are probably the only thing of value left in the once expansive edtech empire. More pertinent, though, is the ideal combination of unique software and hardware that these companies bring to the table. EPIC! has a catalogue of hundreds of thousands of online books and OSMO has the tablet tech to deliver this content, and more, to individual students. Plus, through Tynker, there’s still a business model to be created around online classes.

If there is an edtech entrepreneur listening and still in a position to sweep up great assets, here’s my idea on a platter.

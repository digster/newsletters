Last week, I tried to pick a fight with a voice bot.

It had called to ask if I wanted a credit card. I interrupted it, prematurely asked about interest rates, changed languages mid-sentence, and generally behaved like the worst possible customer. The bot didn’t flinch. It stayed unfailingly polite and found ways to always nudge me back to its script. The way a veteran call-centre executive would have. By the fifth interruption, the joke was on me. This thing could actually do the job.

Similar interactions are now playing out millions of times a day across India. Call floors are increasingly staffed with digital “employees” like Ananya—the default name on many CRM dashboards—who don’t take breaks and speak whatever language the customer prefers.

What began as cautious pilots have turned into infra. Of course, then, Indian voice-AI startups raised over Rs 280 crore in the last year, betting that banks, hospitals, e-commerce companies, and everyone else will be ready to replace human callers.

Globally, the voice stack is being shaped by companies such as Elevenlabs, OpenAI, Google, and Microsoft. The ones building the speech engines that everyone else runs on. Indian startups such as Gnani.ai, with about Rs 57 crore in revenue, and Uniphore, which claims roughly Rs 1,600 crore in ARR, sit further up the chain, stitching those voices into bank-grade, regulation-heavy workflows. Newer startups even promise 30–40% cost savings by automating outbound calls.

But there’s a limit. Enterprises don’t add voice AI on top of everything else; it has to replace something. IVRs, outsourcing contracts, or people. Budgets don’t expand, and deployments are shaped by regulation and data.

So if the market isn’t infinite, why is the funding rush still on? And when voice AI becomes boring, everyday plumbing, who actually wins? Mrunmayee’s story today explains why India may end up with fewer than 10 meaningful voice-AI players, while everyone else becomes a feature or an acquisition.

Good Morning Ishan,

It took an urgent need for an early-morning blood test and some hand-wringing over the idea of stepping out in Delhi’s 8°C winter weather for me to realise that home visits from diagnostic centres were a thing. Mind you, this was several months deep into the Covid-19 pandemic. All of a sudden, home visits—which were niche and confined to just a handful of cases—became the de rigueur.

And then, companies began pairing up. E-pharma/e-commerce companies and diagnostics companies. Tata bought 1mg. Reliance, with its JioMart ambitions, bought Netmeds. PharmEasy bought Thyrocare. And incumbents—large diagnostics chains with outlets across the country—struggled.

The pharma-diagnostics coupling makes a lot of sense. E-commerce and e-pharma companies had the tech, the logistics, and the customers. It was just a matter of adding one more vertical. And boom—a seamless experience from booking tests online to home collection of samples to digital delivery of reports.

The incumbents are still struggling. Stock prices of listed diagnostic firms, such as Dr Lal PathLabs and Metropolis, have crashed since the beginning of 2022. Their valuations have at least halved from their Covid-induced highs. Metropolis’ trailing price-to-earnings dropped to 42X from 90X.

So, when news broke that it was looking for someone that would buy a minority stake in the company—infusing cash while also steering clear of the everyday running of the company—nobody was surprised. A long list of names circulated as potential buyers, from Flipkart and Amazon to global funds KKR, TPG, Barings, and Blackstone. Even Apollo Hospitals figured in the whisper network once or twice.

In our story today, however, Maitri and Anand bring you confirmation from three separate sources that Flipkart is the lead horse in the race.

The deal makes sense for Flipkart—diagnostics would fill a key piece of the healthcare jigsaw puzzle that the e-commerce major is actively working on. And it’s unlikely to rock the boat by asking for a majority stake right off the bat. That may not be the case with private-equity players, who may not settle for a smaller stake.

But can Flipkart and its fledgling healthtech ambitions do for Metropolis what PharmEasy did for Thyrocare?

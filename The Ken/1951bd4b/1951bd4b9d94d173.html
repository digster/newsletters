Indian sovereign fund NIIF’s biggest exit: Money moves from the left hand to the right hand. The majority owner in Ayana Renewables, NIIF, exited to a joint venture owned by two government companies, one of which already has a listed green business. The optics is telling
By Seema Singh
7 min read
View in browser

Good Morning Ishan,

You are reading our Premium subscriber-only newsletter Long and Short—a weekly commentary on India’s capital markets

In its 10th year, India’s sovereign wealth fund has hit a home run.

The National Investment and Infrastructure Fund (NIIF) exited Ayana Renewables last week, in which it first invested five years ago and held 51% equity. The buyer, ONGPL—a joint venture between oil and gas producer ONGC and power producer NTPC Green Energy—valued Ayana at Rs 19,500 crore.

It’s one of the largest deals in renewable energy in India to date.

Broadly speaking, the three main investors in Ayana—from the UK, India, and India-UK—made a total commitment of $721 million since its founding in 2018. But as of October, the shareholders had “infused Rs 3,750 crore into the platform,” and the remaining committed money was to be used for further expansion from its current 1.6 GW in operation, per a December India Ratings report.

Without drowning you in the alphabet soup of the entities—and there are too many—let’s just say it’s a deal in which the government-backed (49%) NIIF gave an exit to itself and its fellow investors by selling the asset to a government-backed company.

Does it look scripted?

Don’t get me wrong. It’s a good deal; people in the industry tell me the valuation is in the “market range.” In the unlisted space, such businesses are valued at 7-9X EV/Ebitda. The listed market gives a higher multiple, and a non-government company like Adani Green Energy currently trades at 22X EV/Ebitda.

It’s hard to independently arrive at Ayana’s valuation or even evaluate how much better it has done since FY24, when its revenue from operations grew a modest 4% over the previous year, per data from Tracxn.

Renewables is a project-driven business where different projects are “ringfenced” and run as independent companies, each being valued based on its lifecycle stage—in operation, under construction, or to be constructed.

“You won’t be able to understand much from the press release or by looking at the filings. Sometimes companies also sell projects mid-way. Even I find it difficult to sometimes arrive at a valuation of our own business,” chuckled the founder of a renewables power generation company.

The point is, while this has been a circular deal, even ridden with some conflict of interest, it’s worth watching how the eventual owner builds atop this buyout—or whether Ayana, which is largely a sum total of projects bought from a bunch of private companies, including the Renew and ACME Groups, continues to operate satisfactorily in the future.

Big bets // NIIF, India’s quasi-sovereign wealth fund, currently has $4.3 billion of assets under management through a Master Fund, a Fund of Funds, and a Strategic Opportunities Fund

Private equity in public good

Business executives in the industry are happy that “funds are churning” in a sector that needs loads of money to meet India’s energy requirements.

India will have to attract around $400 billion in financing to realise its 500 GW target of renewable energy by 2030. To recap, the government has set itself a huge target—to more than double its clean power generation from 203 GW of renewables in 2024 to 500 GW in just six years.

As a result, ONGC is under pressure and has publicly stated that it would reach net-zero by 2038. Now, ONGC has zero expertise or experience in green energy. So, it bid for Ayana last year and soon after formed a JV with NTPC’s wholly owned subsidiary NTPC Green Energy, just about a week before the latter was to list on the stock market.

Why would a green energy company that is going to the stock market to raise Rs 10,000 crore form another green energy JV with another listed company?

The founder quoted above and two other senior industry executives believe this exit for NIIF—and its fellow investors British International Investment (BII) or former CDC Group and Eversource Capital through its Green Growth Equity Fund—was to make an entry for ONGC into renewables. “Since ONGC has no expertise in handling green power, NTPC will handhold it and eventually hand it over to ONGC because it does have a conflict of interest with NTPC Green being a [newly] listed company,” said the founder.

ONGC is sitting on a lot of cash, and if it were to jumpstart a green business, it would take at least two to three years. So, it made perfect sense for the public sector enterprise to buy a green platform.

It also made sense for NIIF, which had invested a significant portion of its Master Fund for a 51% stake in Ayana. More importantly, NIIF came into Ayana when BII had launched the platform but probably “didn’t have a long-term vision,” said a seasoned industry executive who was privy to the early strategy. As anchor investor, NIIF perhaps needed to steer Ayana as well as facilitate an exit for BII.

Interestingly, JSW Energy was also a bidder for Ayana. But it appears that since it lost out to ONGPL, it acquired another renewables platform, O2 Power, for $1.47 billion in 2024. 

As a pure commercial deal, could Ayana’s sale to JSW Energy have served NIIF better?

Was NIIF under pressure to sell the asset to ONGPL?

We hope not, but if the answer to either of the two questions is yes, then it’s a repeat of 2022 when some of the seasoned backers of India’s sovereign fund were miffed at how they lost a deal to the Adani Group. As The Economic Times reported in September 2022:

Such indifferent performance, plus the passive involvement of the government, with a minority 49% economic interest, along with the future course has peeved several high profile backers who have expressed their misgivings already. Some of these investors from North America and Middle East are key financial allies of the country with significant investments already on ground either as a limited partner to other funds or directly.

A consortium of NIIF-ADIA-PSP Investments, for example, was competing to buy into the Mumbai International Airport but lost out to Adani in a corporate coup. ADIA wrote several letters to the prime minister and the finance minister, which ET also reported, protesting against the way their proposal got rejected. The government did not want to get involved in what they called a commercial dispute between various corporate entities.

“The whole idea was to keep the government capped at 49% to stay out of the CVC audit and get best professional talent but it also turned out to be counter productive,” said an official who worked closely with the fund. “Unlike several other sovereign funds where the government’s funds are at stake, here the government was passive yet it played a key role in the governing council. Two senior finance ministry secretaries were members of the council and there is also board representation.”

NIIF boss Sujoy Bose likely to exit fund after 6 years at helm, The Economic Times 

It’s important that, as a fund, NIIF makes money. Only then can it plough back into future funds through the government. For instance, it recently said it’s going to launch another Master Fund this year, one bigger than the previous fund. But for that, it has to make sure its deals are done at arm’s length. While it gets money from the government, it has to keep India’s taxpayers’ interests at the centre. Or else, it’s just another ‘government’ fund, not really a ‘sovereign’ wealth fund.

“With Ayana, ONGC will get a toehold in the green energy space, but if it is not able to hold on to the team and build on it, then it’s a waste,” said the chief executive of a renewables platform.

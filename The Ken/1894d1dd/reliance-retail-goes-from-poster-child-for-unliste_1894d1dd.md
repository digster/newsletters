Good Morning Ishan,
Thanks to today’s story, I heard about “unlisted shares” for the first time.
More importantly, I learnt not only what the term means but also how such bets went horribly wrong for minority shareholders of Reliance Retail Ltd, an unlisted unit of Reliance Industries and India’s largest operator of supermarkets, apparel outlets, and electronics stores.
As Seetharaman and Anand explain in this insightful story, broadly, there are three types of companies with unlisted shares in the market.
First, there are those preparing to raise funds via an initial public offering (IPO). A company files the offer document with the regulator, and in anticipation of a gold rush after the listing, its shares start trading at a high rate in the grey market. That happened with companies such as Paytm, Policybazaar, and many others ahead of their IPOs.
Then there are companies with no plans of going public but are simply popular. With some astonishment, I found out that one stock that fits this category is the Indian Premier League franchise Chennai Super Kings.
Finally, there are the old guards. These are companies that once traded on bourses but have since delisted. But not all of their shareholders surrendered their stake during the delisting process. So, these shares are still available in the unlisted market.
As I found out today, Reliance Retail falls somewhere between the first and the second categories. There was a lot of hype around its ambitions and some anticipation that the company would eventually go public, making a lot of money for those who held on to its shares.
There was a bit of a twist in the tale.
On Friday, Reliance Retail announced that it would effectively cancel the shares held by its minority investors and buy back their holdings at just about half the rate prevailing for the stock in the unlisted market.
But what could have prompted the company to take this step, and what are the lessons for retail investors?

Good Morning Ishan,
“Content to commerce” seems self-explanatory. But it’d be great if any of you could tell me how it really works or, even better, show me that it does work. It’s been bandied about so often lately that it makes as much sense as “revert” and “revert back” in emails.
One of the most valuable Indian companies often associated with a content-to-commerce strategy is The Good Glamm Group, which sells personal-care products. The US$1.2 billion company––backed by prominent investors such as Warburg Pincus, Prosus, and Amazon––acquired over a dozen brands in the fast-money days after the pandemic.
But, Aakriti says in her well-reported [story](https://the-ken.com/story/good-glam-cant-keep-up-with-its-acquisition-spree/?utm_source=daily&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter) today, Good Glamm’s pitch to be a Thrasio-esque house of beauty brands hasn’t panned out—to put it mildly. The deadline to buy residual stakes worth US$18 million in three of its portfolio brands has passed, The Ken has learnt. And the US$30 million it raised recently may not come in handy here.
What’s more, everyone knows that the D2C party is long over. So what’s left in Good Glamm founder Darpan Sanghvi’s bag of tricks?
One beauty giant zigs, another zags
In late January, when French luxury group LVMH announced its annual results, investors’ attention was on the breakout performance of its cosmetics retailer, Sephora. It had grown the fastest among LVMH’s businesses, making it the group’s third-largest brand, behind Louis Vuitton and Christian Dior.
Sephora’s US$15.4 billion top line was helped by the “lipstick effect”, its chief executive Guillaume Motte told The Financial Times in March. He added that the firm would hit a key revenue target sooner than later.
Less than a month later, Sephora rival Ulta, which is America’s largest standalone beauty retailer, said that after three years of expanding at an impressive clip, growth is moderating. In fact, Ulta’s numbers for the March quarter are worse than it estimated just a couple of months ago. Investors immediately pummelled its shares.
How is it that two companies in the same business have such contrasting takes on what’s in store? Or is it possible that Motte was too sanguine?

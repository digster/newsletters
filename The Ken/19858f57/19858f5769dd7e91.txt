SBI Cards can’t find a way out of its funk. The credit-card issuer’s much-awaited recovery isn’t here yet

A few months ago, the heads of research at some brokerages probably sat their banking analysts down and told them a version of this: “Guys, I know we get things wrong sometimes, but why don’t we do it by design just to have some fun? Let’s publish a report on SBI Cards and say the exact opposite of what will happen.”

I struggle to find a different explanation for how cheery some analysts were about the credit-card issuer owned by India’s largest bank. Even when there was little reason to be so. “The worst seems over,” screamed one report. 

Anything but. It’s impossible to come to a different conclusion looking at SBI Cards and Payment Services’ June-quarter financials. 

At 9.6%, the company’s credit cost—or provisions for bad loans and write-offs as a share of average total loans, after deducting recoveries—rose sharply, both sequentially and year-on-year. In fact, it is at a four-year high while analysts expected it to head down. Spends by its retail cardholders were only up marginally over the previous quarter. Investors sent SBI Cards shares careening https://www.livemint.com/market/stock-market-news/sbi-cards-share-price-crashes-over-6-to-15-week-after-analysts-lower-the-target-price-on-q1-miss-11753682539839.html in response to the results.

The company has consistently ceded ground to rivals in recent years. In the four years to FY25, its market share in credit-card spends plunged from 19.4% to 15.7%. The result was losing its #2 position, behind HDFC Bank, to ICICI Bank.

What particularly worried investors was that even as online spends outpaced card swipes at physical stores for SBI Cards, ICICI managed to pull ahead in digital transactions.

The problem with new customers is different; SBI Cards is simply not signing up enough of them. In the first three months of 2025, it added 1.1 million new accounts. In the following quarter, it only brought in 870,000—a 20% drop. 

At the heart of 27-year-old SBI Cards’ troubles is its changing ownership structure, as my former colleague Rounak wrote in a wonderful story https://the-ken.com/story/sbi-is-why-sbi-cards-ceos-and-execs-cant-stand-each-other/ in March. 

For almost two decades, SBI Cards had a pretty straightforward power-sharing arrangement. The CEO came from SBI, but the real muscle behind the scenes was GE Capital. It was a joint venture—60% SBI, 40% GE—and the board more or less reflected that. Four SBI executives, two from GE. Even the key committees—audit, risk management, nomination—were neatly divided: two SBI, one GE.

SBI handled strategy, product and most of the customer-facing functions, while GE was responsible for operations, customer service, collections, and HR, among others, according to former executives and the company’s annual reports.

That changed in 2017 when GE Capital sold its stake to SBI and private-equity firm Carlyle, which fully exited the company two years after SBI Cards’ IPO in 2020. 

SBI is now the only one at the wheel, and it can’t keep its eyes on the road. 

At SBI Cards, the CEOs are not executives who rise up the ranks to snag the corner office. Rather, they are senior employees of the parent lender, State Bank of India, and they often come with no experience in credit cards. They are, in fact, appointed by the overlords—SBI’s board of directors—for a mere two-year tenure.

SBI Cards hires its other top executives from its private-sector rivals and pays them more than it does its CEO. So the CEO has no love lost for them. The executives, having to report to someone with possibly no experience in credit cards, aren’t thrilled, either. 

SBI Cards, with a market value of over Rs 78,100 crore ($9 billion), is not the only one to have paid the price for the chaos within. Ask its investors. Its shares have risen 13% over the past five years, while the Nifty Financial Services index is up 150%.

Salila Pande has only been CEO of SBI Cards for four months, but that also means she has a mere 20 months left. Perhaps no one else at the helm of a large listed company is weighed down by such a countdown.

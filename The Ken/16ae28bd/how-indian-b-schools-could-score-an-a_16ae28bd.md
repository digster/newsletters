We wrote about how the placement process at B-schools in India was broken, and about how it was reflective of the larger education system, which needs an urgent overhaul.
It’s fair to say that the response to the story was... passionate. On all sides. But none more so than from students and graduates from the top B-schools in India.
Many pointed out they have been saying pretty much the same thing for years. Others said that the story was one-sided and biased. And some wrote in our comments section - okay, fine, we agree, B-schools in India are broken. So what? What is the solution?
And we honestly answered - we don’t know.
Journalists aren’t the people you turn to for solutions. They aren’t management consultants. As someone who has done both jobs, let me say that there are several key differences. For starters, journalists get paid a lot less. Also, journalists routinely get accused of taking money from sketchy organisations to make-up stuff that fits a predefined agenda, and everybody knows that management consultants don’t do that at all.
Basically, a large part of journalism is about reporting that something doesn’t look right here, and this is why, and how. It’s not about prescribing solutions.
But we told ourselves, let’s take a shot anyway.
So we went back to experts and questioned them about possible solutions to the problems—some if not all—that ail the Indian business school setup. The future is changing, the curriculum is not. Skills need to be replaced. Thinking needs to be modified. Are there lessons in what others are doing globally? Can Indian B-schools change? What will it take?
That’s Pradip’s story today. The second part. Possible solutions.
Some look interesting. Some sound impossible. Some look like they just... might work.
Oh, it’s a free story, so you might as well read it, and tell us what you think.

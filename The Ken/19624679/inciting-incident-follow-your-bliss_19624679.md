Inciting Incident is our weekly newsletter about the most powerful tool of our age—stories. Stories told by businesses, leaders, governments.
Good Evening Ishan,
Did you know that the famous quote “follow your bliss” comes from Joseph Campbell? Campbell was the first to decode the storytelling framework that has inspired some of the most famous movies (Star Wars, The Matrix), books (Harry Potter), and even brand stories (Apple and Steve Jobs) in the world. It has also indirectly inspired millions who chose the difficult path: to be an artist or entrepreneur who creates something out of nothing.
The exact passage is:
Follow your bliss.
If you do follow your bliss,
you put yourself on a kind of track
that has been there all the while waiting for you,
and the life you ought to be living
is the one you are living.
When you can see that,
you begin to meet people
who are in the field of your bliss,
and they open the doors to you.
I say, follow your bliss and don’t be afraid,
and doors will open
where you didn’t know they were going to be.
If you follow your bliss,
doors will open for you that wouldn’t have opened for anyone else.
You’ll find much of the sentiment in this poem underlying the Hero’s Journey framework. The many layers are explained in the book, Hero with a Thousand Faces, but a simplified version looks something like this.
Starting its publishing run in October 2021, this newsletter has both zoomed in on the smallest steps in the science and art of crafting stories, and broken down the role of heroes and villains in the world of business storytelling.

But before that, I want to spend some time in today’s edition to shine a light on what serves as the foundation for all great stories. And it’s something that will become even more crucial for brands and businesses in the days and months to come, as Donald Trump seeks to reinvent the global order.
Emotion.
Andy Raskin, who coaches venture-backed CEOs and their leadership teams to align themselves to a high-level story, argues that some of the most compelling sales pitches ([Uber Flip](https://medium.com/the-mission/the-making-of-a-great-sales-narrative-978938b3926), [Drift](https://medium.com/the-mission/the-best-sales-pitch-ive-seen-all-year-7fa92afaa248), [Zuora](https://medium.com/the-mission/the-greatest-sales-deck-ive-ever-seen-4f4ef3391ba0)) [start from](https://medium.com/the-mission/great-pitches-start-with-change-2c7e696b86ea) a very specific point of view.
The (admittedly contrarian) view that motivates my work with CEOs and leadership teams is that great pitches don’t start with your vision, your mission, your product, or a surprising statistic.
Great pitches don’t start with why, if by why you mean a reason you get up in the morning. Great pitches don’t even start with a problem. When you tell people they have a problem, there’s a decent chance they’ll get defensive because (a) they don’t believe they have the problem; (b) they’re in denial; or (c) they worry that if they admit to having the problem, you’ll sell them something. Besides, when you start with a problem, you offer zero context for why solving that problem matters.
Great pitches start by naming a change in the world.
Well, we can all agree after Orange Monday that the world has seen a big, relevant change.
And it is now up to strategic storytellers to craft a narrative that makes use of this to evoke strong emotion among their audiences.
In fact, Marshall Ganz—a senior lecturer in leadership, organising, and civil society at the Kennedy School of Government, Harvard University—says in his book, People Power Change, that effective leaders consciously guide and motivate their audiences by tweaking and tinkering with the dominant emotion of the times.
You can see Trump himself using this lever in his social media instead of trying to offer intellectual arguments for his moves. “BE COOL!” he [posted](https://www.nytimes.com/2025/04/09/us/politics/trump-tariff-pause-be-cool.html) on social media on Wednesday. [Leaders from other countries](https://time.com/7276293/trump-tariffs-reversal-world-leaders-countries-responses-uncertainty-trade-war/), too, are doing the same. Germany has chosen to signal determination; Malaysia, an acceptance of uncertainty.
Germany’s chancellor-in-waiting Friedrich Merz said Trump’s move is a “response to the determination of the Europeans.” The pause came hours after the European Union voted to approve retaliatory tariffs on $23 billion in goods, starting April 15, in response to Trump’s previously announced 25% tariffs on steel and aluminum—which remain in effect. In an interview with broadcaster RTL Direkt, Merz said, “We are determined to defend ourselves,” echoing E.U.
President Ursula von der Leyen’s statement last week. “Unity helps,” Merz added. The E.U. faced a 20% “reciprocal” tariff.

What about India?
Well, my colleagues Rohin Dharmakumar and Praveen Gopal Krishnan had an interesting [talk](https://the-ken.com/podcasts/two-by-two/no-easy-moves-is-india-facing-a-crisis-or-an-opportunity/) with Mohit Satyanand—an entrepreneur, investor, and economy-watcher—earlier this week, and tried to answer whether Trump’s tariffs are a crisis or opportunity for India. And at one point, Satyanand tied his [analysis](https://mohitsatyanand.substack.com/p/get-your-hands-bloody) of the event to one of the three primal emotional responses of all living beings when confronted with danger: fight, flight, or freeze.
His suggestion: “Just don’t freeze, instead get your hands bloody.”

Good Morning Ishan,
When MG Motor launched its electric crossover, Windsor, in September last year, an EV company founder in Bengaluru told me, “This is what India needed, a business model innovation. It’ll change the market.”
He was right on the latter but perhaps spoke too soon on the former.
A year after Windsor hit Indian roads with a battery-as-a-service offering, it almost single-handedly slashed Tata Motors’ electric-car market share by 20%. But its business model success is up for debate. Few customers took that offer, even though the vehicle became the best-selling car in no time. And almost no OEM adopted that “innovation”, even though Suprita, writer of today’s story, has learnt that Tata Motors seriously considered it before dropping the idea.
Despite the global seesaw about the EV industry prospects, more than 35 EV models are estimated to launch in India by 2027.
Windsor certainly gets credit for changing customer perceptions of EVs almost overnight. It brought many first-time EV intenders into showrooms. But will they ride with Windsor or consider it as a pitstop in their EV-ownership journey? Was MG’s Baas a gimmick or a smart price-signalling lever?

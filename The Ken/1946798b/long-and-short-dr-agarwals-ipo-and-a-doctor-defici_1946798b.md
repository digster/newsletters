Long and Short
Wed, 15 Jan 25
The story behind the numbers and motivations driving India's stock market..
Good Morning Ishan,
Private equity momentum in Indian healthcare is on steroids. It’s driving expansion, secondaries, mergers, and acquisitions like there’s no tomorrow.
Dr Agarwal’s Healthcare Ltd, which just got the regulator’s nod to list, is a good example of what growth capital can achieve (or distort) in a short span of time. It’s understood that the company, which already has a listed “material” subsidiary in Dr Agarwal’s Eye Hospital, will debut on the exchanges in the next few weeks. It’s raising Rs 300 crore (US$34.6 million) in a fresh issue and nearly 70 million shares will be on offer for sale from promoters and investors.
(For context, the exercise price in March 2024, when the eye care chain raised Rs 1,000 crore from investment giants TPG and Temasek, was Rs 3,896. The share price may have changed significantly since then.)
Today’s edition is not about the premium at which the shares will list compared to its last fundraise in March 2024. It’s about what its draft red herring prospectus reveals about this business—specifically, that doctors are central to driving revenue and reputation and that doctors’ supply in the market is inelastic, unlike private capital from large investors.
If you speak to any [doctor-entrepreneur](https://the-ken.com/story/cancer-treatment-can-ruin-families-but-one-doctor-thinks-he-has-made-it-affordable-for-one-third-of-india/), or even a professional CEO of a hospital these days, doctors’ skyrocketing salaries weigh on their minds. For instance, cancer chain Healthcare Global founder BS Ajaikumar recently told me that some doctors make Rs 30–40 lakh a month. Retaining or attracting doctors has become core to the business strategy.
While hospital chains like Cloudnine or [Indira IVF](https://the-ken.com/story/the-cost-of-building-a-billion-dollar-ivf-chain-in-india/) offer stock appreciation rights (SARs) to their doctors, Dr Agarwal’s is introducing something new. A Doctors’ Incentive Plan (DIP) that grants “units” to doctor consultants, entitling them to a cash payout equal to the stock appreciation.
As much as 50% of the cash payout will be made within one month of the IPO, with 25% paid after 12 months and the remaining 25% after 24 months.
This is a one-time grant that the hospital made in November 2022; it will not do so again, specifies the IPO prospectus.
In India, while employee stock options (Esops) are common, especially for employees and key managerial personnel, incentive plans specifically for consultants, such as the DIP, are rare, if not entirely unheard of. The DIP provides doctors with a payout linked to the appreciation in the company’s share value as determined by independent valuation.
“Potentially, they would have done this either because many doctors work as consultants and therefore cannot be covered under the Esop plan or the company may be avoiding too much equity dilution,” said Vichitra Malhotra, founder of Veritas Actuaries and Consultants. As consultant doctors are equally critical resources in driving quality of healthcare provided and, by extension, the growth of the hospitals, they can be included in share-based compensation through cash-settled instruments such as SARs, she said. “One can call it DIP or by some other name.”
Since the DIP payment timing will not exceed four years from the date of the grant, per the IPO prospectus, Dr Agarwal’s will have to pay out the entire amount by November 2026 once it goes public.
“I think that’s logical at their end because they may not want to carry on the liability for a very long period into their books and cash it out,” said Malhotra.
Esops in the healthcare delivery industry were uncommon until recently. They were more of a ‘tech sector’ tool to attract and reward talent. But as healthcare grew rapidly on the back of risk capital infusion, demand for talent outpaced its supply and [hospitals took](https://www.moneycontrol.com/news/business/major-hospitals-face-huge-attrition-offer-esops-incentives-to-retain-employees-11276891.html) to granting Esops. Or even SARs.
Technically, on any given day, the benefit to the employee under both Esops and SAR can be matched exactly, said Malhotra. In the case of Esops, the benefit is the share price minus the exercise price, and for SARs, it’s the share price minus the strike price.
So, the benefit to the employee can be the same. Of course, in the case of Esop, there is a longer-term hook for the employee—that they can hold on to the shares of the company because they become shareholders on exercise, she added. “They can hold on for another two to three years and participate in long-term gains of the company. But SAR being a cash-settled instrument, it needs to be settled by the company. So that benefit of holding the shares cannot be passed on.”
At Dr Agarwal’s, which ended the financial year in March 2024 with 667 doctors, 118 doctors joined the company and 113 left in one year. It’s like running faster to remain in the same place. As the chart below shows, the share of consulting charges in its expenses is increasing.
Having consultants on board works in a hospital’s favour, as I have [written earlier](https://the-ken.com/story/how-many-hospitals-can-pe-firms-buy-before-patients-say-enough/). It’s, therefore, obvious why Dr Agarwal’s would take a direct hit on their books by offering an incentive plan to doctors.
Under Esops, the company does not make any cash payout. The employee pays the exercise price, covers the tax, and gets the shares. In a way, they have a market where they can sell their share and the market makes the gain for the employees. But with SARs, being a cash-settled instrument, the money goes out of the companies’ cash reserves. And in this case of DIP, it’s even more time-dependent because the first tranche of 50% payout will happen within 30 days of the listing.
Now, imagine if all doctors encash their DIP incentives at the same time as the IPO.
The company says it has made “some provisions” for this payout, but it could still impact the net income and retained earnings in the financial period following the listing.
Globally, healthcare industries follow various versions of incentive plans. In the West, it is called the physician incentive plan, or PIP. In many countries, this is linked to quality and clinical outcomes, productivity, patient satisfaction, and overall cost reduction. It’s used for system-wide impact or healthcare reforms. For instance, in China such a financial incentive plan was employed to nudge doctors to participate in serving the rural population, and it [proved to be](https://bmchealthservres.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/s12913-024-12080-y) effective.
Financial incentives certainly work in the healthcare industry, it depends on what outcomes they are targeted at.

Good Morning Ishan,
Do you know Shivprasad Swaminathan, Ashish Bharadwaj, and Pritam Baruah? If yes, can you guess what they have in common?
Allow me. Swaminathan spearheads Shiv Nadar University’s School of Law in Chennai. Bharadwaj leads BITS Law School in Kalyan. Baruah is the dean of the School of Law at BML Munjal University. And the three were once colleagues at Jindal Global Law School (JGLS) in Sonipat, Haryana.
But that’s not the only thing they have in common.
Despite leaving Jindal, they continue to carry the law school’s disruptive business model with them. Though with their own variations.
What is this business model, you ask?
Here’s a hint: It helped the Indian School of Business (ISB) stand out among its peers, despite the dominance of IIMs in management studies. It also made Jindal the first of its kind in India.
Now, the trio is using the same model to attract top faculty and make their courses industry-friendly.
Alifiya, the author of today’s[ story](https://the-ken.com/story/jindal-law-school-did-to-law-what-isb-did-to-management-a-new-crop-of-schools-now-want-a-piece-of-the-pie/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter), also found that traditional public law schools like NLSIU in Bengaluru, constrained by rigid curricula and budget limitations, are struggling to keep up. Yet, the true measure of a university’s success often boils down to one important question: who lands the top jobs?
In her story, Alifiya breaks down the answer and shares insights on the famed model.

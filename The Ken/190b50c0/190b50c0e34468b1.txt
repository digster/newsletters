Good Morning Ishan,

We don’t have an Elon Musk in our Indian startup space. No one can really match Musk’s frenetic, disruptive, social media-tuned personality. But we do have founders who like to… cause an upset.

Gaurav Munjal, Unacademy’s mercurial co-founder, is chief among them.

Munjal hasn’t ever been media shy. He’s been rather media-aggressive. While his competitor and contemporary Byju Raveendran (founder of Byju’s) makes, or made, curated and managed media appearances, Munjal wields X (formerly, Twitter) like a megaphone—he belts out opinions, shares pretty important company metrics, and publicly disagrees and throws shade at other founders. There’s a Musk-adjacent quality to all this.

He also likes to keep us on our toes. Immediately after The Morning Context reported https://themorningcontext.com/internet/unacademy-is-up-for-sale-merger-or-bailout that Unacademy may be up for a sale or a merger, Munjal came out swinging against the report.

Notice though, Munjal doesn’t directly deny the “rumour” about a sale or a merger. He says, like he often has in the past, that “Unacademy will have its best year”. In fact, if one trawled through his timeline, this refrain is perhaps his most persistent: it might seem like Unacademy is in trouble right now, but we’re still going to have the best year ever.

Edtech, in India, hasn’t ever been a profitable sector. Some companies went after the same sliver of kids or graduates to sell to, and burnt through their capital quicker than expected. Others, like Byju’s, burnt through their runway by buying out companies who themselves had spent excessively to compete with the likes of Byju’s. It was all a splotchy, circle-y mess until, of course, the bloated effects of Covid ended. And no one ever spoke about profitability ever again.

Except Munjal. 

Munjal has outlasted Raveendran; no one’s heard from Vamsi Krishna of Vedantu for a while. Munjal has bested other competitors by buying them out. And now, he’s facing a whole new set of competitors like Physicswallah and unlikely ones like offline test prep companies Allen and Aakash. 

This makes Munjal the most defiant founder in Indian edtech. It also makes him the loneliest.

Choosing profit over people

Ka-Ching!
Thu, 10 Oct 24
A weekly newsletter about how finance is getting supercharged by tech in India, and how you can make money work for you..
Good Morning Ishan,
This is not how I pictured Ashneer Grover going out. Settling seems so out of character for Grover, who once wrote “anything is possible as long as you negotiate hard” in his hurriedly written autobiography.
In 2022, Bharatpe—the company he had co-founded—filed a case against Grover and his family members seeking over Rs 80 crore (US$9.5 million) in damages. The two parties have been locked in a legal battle ever since, resulting in the Economic Offences Wing (EOW) even filing an FIR against Grover and his family.
All that came to an end last Monday, 30 September, after the two parties entered into a [settlement](https://www.business-standard.com/companies/news/bharatpe-settles-with-ex-co-founder-ashneer-grover-abandons-legal-action-124093000094_1.html). Lawyers from both sides had been locked in a room between 27 and 30 September trying to hash out the terms, said a source aware of the matter, with an agreement being reached at 5.30 am Monday morning—just hours before the EOW could have initiated action that may have lead to the arrest of Grover and his family.
The turning point was Deepak Gupta’s [arrest](https://www.livemint.com/companies/people/ashneer-grovers-relative-deepak-gupta-arrested-by-delhi-police-in-bharatpe-fund-misappropriation-case-11726816600824.html) a few days earlier. Gupta is a relative of Grover, and according to my source, he had “spilled the beans” on 27 September, potentially building a better case for the EOW.
By the morning of 30 September, Grover had given in to Bharatpe’s terms.
This means that:
– Grover can no longer call himself the founder, only a “former co-founder”
– He is no longer on the captable
– He returns the money the company claims he owes, or settles that against his shares
– He stops defaming the company
With these terms, Bharatpe has also managed to claw back Grover’s unvested shares amounting to ~1.4% in the company. Which means Grover has handed over shares worth over Rs 300 crore (US$35.7 million)—four times more than the Rs 8o crore that he was originally asked to return.
He still gets to keep the rest of his shareholding—around 4%—which is still worth close to US$100 million, so it’s not an entirely bad outcome for Grover. But these shares are to be placed in a family trust which will be under board oversight, and will only be released one year after the company’s IPO. The beneficiaries of the shares in this trust will be Grover’s children, and not he or his wife Madhuri Jain. Meanwhile, neither Bharatpe nor Grover can defame each other.
Separately, he also settled with his other co-founder Bhavik Koladiya. In 2023, Koladiya had filed a case alleging that he had transferred some shares to Grover, but that Grover had never paid him for it. With this settlement, Grover has returned those 16,000 shares.
I think developments over the past few days made it increasingly clear to Grover that had he not relented, both he and his family may very well end up being arrested. Or at the very least, locked in a 15–20 year legal battle, which would have also made things very difficult for Bharatpe.
Grover can also thank his stars that no consumers or merchant partners were impacted by the alleged fraud—the one big difference between this case and others such as Theranos’ Elizabeth Holmes or FTX’s Sam Bankman-Fried, both of whom ended up being arrested for fraud.
All it would have taken is one kirana store getting caught in the crossfire, and he could not have had this neat of an ending.
Instead, the aggrieved parties here were the company, and investors such as Peak XV, Ribbit Capital, and Coatue for whom settling was a better option. In the case of investors like Peak XV, many limited partners are already disappointed with the kind of returns they’ve been getting and are pushing for better results. As for Bharatpe, with new management in place, it wants to put the whole thing behind.
It is, of course, possible that things could have ended a lot sooner.
Had Grover chosen to settle before the cases were filed, he could have maybe even retained his earlier 8.4% shareholding, instead of just ~4% now. Out of that 8.4%, he’s now handed over 2.6% to Koladiya, 1.5% to Bharatpe, and the rest to his family trust.
So for a man for whom [dhanda](https://www.hindustantimes.com/business/ashneers-to-startup-founders-dhanda-dhanda-hai-aur-rishtedari-rishtedari-101670757340653.html) (or business) was almost religion, he’s maybe lost out on at least US$100 million of wealth.
Also, for a man who authored a book called Doglapan, meaning double standard (which he wrote as a way of defending himself against the cases brought against him), settling speaks louder than all the words in his book.
Questions sent to Grover went unanswered.

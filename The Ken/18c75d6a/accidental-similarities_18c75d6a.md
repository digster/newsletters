Good Morning Ishan,
A couple of days ago, I got into a small car accident. Nothing too major: I wasn’t hurt, and there was no subsequent legal complaint—the errant truck driver managed to get away quickly, and there wasn’t any law enforcement around.
In the ensuing days, I dropped off the car at the nearest service station, an hour’s drive away, and then spent four hours filling out insurance-related forms.
Details, claims, counterclaims, documentation, evidence, and corroboration.
Luckily for me, I was advised by my folks to take many pictures, note the location of the accident and record any notable details. I made a water-tight case. The claim got approved on Wednesday, and here’s hoping the rest of the repair goes smoothly.
Anyway, the process oddly enough reminded me of the rigors of the process we go through to write our own stories at The Ken. We give more time than most to report stories, corroborate claims across multiple sources, and seek evidence and numbers.
So it’s always worth appreciating the hard work at the end of a week.
We started with Aakriti’s deeply reported [story](https://the-ken.com/story/bse-was-almost-invisible-then-a-new-ceo-challenged-nses-monopoly/?utm_source=recap&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter) on the Bombay Stock Exchange, finally posing a serious threat to its larger rival, the National Stock Exchange. She documents what’s led to this turnaround—a new CEO at the helm. But also notes how any further growth will be elusive, at best.
On Tuesday, we had Shivani’s sharp [story](https://the-ken.com/story/apnas-frantic-search-for-survival-in-the-jobs-portal-maze/?utm_source=recap&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter) on Apna, once a $1.1 billion jobs marketplace, struggling to nail down its product-market fit. Hitherto largely hands-off, its founder Nirmit Parikh recently moved to India for the first time inarguably to steady the ship. A lot had been written about Apna in the days since it reported its financials for the year ended March 2023, but Shivani really managed to separate the wheat from the chaff.
The same day, as part of our international coverage, Isa wrote about an important shift underway in the Philippines. Encumbered by a stifling corporate culture in the capital city of Manilla, several white-collar workers are moving out to tourist cities to hone their passions and entrepreneurial ambitions. Isa’s [story](https://the-ken.com/story_morning_email/philippine-tourism-is-draining-manilas-talent-pool/?utm_source=recap&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter) lays out details of this shift and how employers are now trying to get back on track.
Wednesday carried the definitive story from Nuha on India’s largest fashion etailer, Myntra, losing steam. Growth has now reached the single digits from the 35% it used command earlier, and fewer customer visits convert to sales. In fact, some say Myntra has become an ‘events’ platform with 22 sale days in a month, much higher than rivals like Ajio, which has 14 such days. Amongst all this, it has scaled back on in-house brands. How? Why? And what’s at the heart of this turmoil at Myntra? That’s what Nuha [unpacks](https://the-ken.com/story/how-indias-largest-fashion-e-tailer-is-slowly-going-out-of-style/?utm_source=recap&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter).
On Thursday, Narayanan [wrote](https://the-ken.com/story/rise-in-robotic-surgeries-sees-patients-stuck-in-between-doctors-and-insurers-standoff/?utm_source=recap&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter) about robotic surgeries, which are less invasive than conventional ones. Yet, patients are in a tricky spot when it comes to funding these procedures. Costs for robotic surgeries can be 3–4X that of conventional procedures, and though recovery is easier, the fine print of insurance policies narrows coverage for these procedures.
In our international edition, Prima [wrote](https://the-ken.com/story/why-indonesias-digital-banks-are-shying-away-from-businesses-they-wanted-to-power/?utm_source=recap&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter) about Indonesia’s digital banks, which, although started with an ambition to provide credit to MSMEs, are now withdrawing from them despite the risk of penalties from the country’s central bank.
Finally, on Friday, Rounak [wrote](https://the-ken.com/story/meet-therapists-new-clients-deep-in-debt-and-harassed-borrowers/?utm_source=recap&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter) about the heart-wrenching mental health crisis brewing in India. A number of therapists are taking courses to understand the basics of banking as several clients are coming to them with debt-related woes. While the country doesn’t have a rank of ‘financial therapists’ who combine financial advisory and therapy services like in other nations such as the US, the story makes a powerful case for why perhaps we now should.

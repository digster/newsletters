PM Internship Scheme—another shaky start to another shaky skilling programme. As very low acceptance rates indicate, the scheme is suffering from a fundamental mismatch between who it wants to skill and how much it wants to pay for it

Imagine this. You’re a recent graduate from a tier-3 college in a tier-3 town, maybe even the first graduate in your family. You are, like thousands of your peers, scouring job sites and portals, spending your working hours filling and filing job applications. 

But opportunities are scarce, at least the ones you aspire to—like a job in a large company with acceptable prospects for upward economic and social mobility. You are unsure if your education has equipped you with the skills to build a career in the real world, and now, you are confronted by job posts starting to ask for expertise, or at least experience, in AI. 

To top it all, the most popular option for graduates disappointed with the corporate job market is certainly not available to you. You and your family can’t afford to fund a long and expensive preparation for a civil-service exam. 

The Prime Minister’s Internship Scheme (PMIS), the second round of which is ongoing, is aimed at precisely this increasingly desperate cohort of India’s young. The goal: 10 million internships over five years, in 500 of the country’s top companies—a way to both skill candidates, and expose them to the necessities of corporate life over 12 months. The government boasts partnerships with big names like SBI Life, Coca-Cola, IBM, Reliance, and TCS. 

To be eligible, among other things, the candidate’s household income has to be less than Rs 8 lakh per annum. 

On Saturday, the Business Standard wrote about https://www.business-standard.com/industry/news/pm-s-internship-scheme-what-s-working-what-s-not-what-it-teaches-youth-125080101959_1.html how at least some of the interns who landed positions in round one of the scheme have benefitted. 

But the latest numbers, released in Parliament in July, are far from encouraging. 

Acceptance and eventual joining rates are abysmal. 

In phase 1, over 6 lakh applications were received for 1.27 lakh listings, around 82,000 offers were made, but only 8,700 interns ended up actually joining. The figures for phase 2, which is still ongoing, don’t look that different.

I spoke to a few skilling and recruitment sector executives, all of whom have several criticisms of the scheme. 

But the main one, unsurprisingly, seems to be money. 

To be precise, the lack of it.

Short of cash

Internships under PMIS are paid. A one-time grant of Rs 6,000 as kind of a joining bonus and Rs 5,000 per month as a stipend—Rs 4,500 of which is transferred directly to the intern’s bank account by the government, and the remaining Rs 500 paid by the company from its social responsibility (CSR) fund. Companies can pay more if they choose, but there’s no official data on this and nothing in the scheme encouraging them to do so. 

To be blunt, this is insufficient. 

If your internship is in a tier-1 city, or even some tier-2 locations, achieving an acceptable quality of life on the PMIS stipend can be impossible without family support. And with its Rs 8 lakh per annum household income cap, the scheme is only available to candidates for whom such family support is extremely unlikely. 

I spoke to three prospective candidates who ultimately decided not to apply to the PMIS—and two of those backed out because the stipend was too low. The third managed to secure a much higher-paying apprenticeship via the National Apprenticeship Promotion Scheme (NAPS), with a pay of Rs 20,000 per month. 

“Compared to the options PMIS provides, the youth find they’re better off working in their native towns, even if it is at lesser-known companies in the unstructured sector,” Neelabh Shukla, chief business officer of staffing firm Careernet. 

A standing committee report to Parliament tabled earlier this week raises similar concerns.

“The Committee believes that addressing the challenges faced by candidates from  remote  or  underserved  regions  is  crucial  for  ensuring  equitable  access  to internships. The committee feels without adequate support for living expenses, these candidates may be unable to participate, thereby hindering the program’s inclusivity and its potential to attract a diverse pool of talent.”

Perhaps the PMIS could do with a higher industry contribution. For instance, in the case of the NAPS, the government covers only 25% of stipend, capped at Rs 1,500 per month.

Undercurrents

The money is only the most visible problem. There are others. 

Last year, when the scheme was first announced, we wrote about how it was making Indian companies uneasy. One concern was that a large influx of interns would strain company resources and infrastructure, but as the latest numbers show, that fear has remained largely unrealised. 

The other big point of friction, however, remains. 

Do companies even have enough of the kind of roles the government expects them to provide PMIS interns? If they do, can these interns meet their requirements? And when interns do complete their internships, will they do so having acquired actually valuable skills?

“Continuous engagement with industry doyens has shown that students have theoretical knowledge, but lack experiential knowledge,” Sahasrabudhe said. He’s also the former chairman of the All India Council for Technical Education (AICTE).

Bridging this skill gap requires more than internships alone. A strong academic foundation—where educational programmes align with market demands, especially in fields like AI, data analytics, and cloud computing—is a must, said Vij.

That’s why a majority of interns are hired directly out of top-ranking university campuses, said a software-development engineer working with an e-commerce marketplace.

Shukla says some of this arises from a fundamental mismatch between the desires the government is trying to fulfil—i.e., the aspiration for perceived higher-status jobs (such as in IT)—and the real gap in available talent. “Many desire white-collar jobs, with glass cubicles in high-rises, while the actual workforce requirements are in the factories and fields. Everyone wants to be a software engineer, but no one wants to go to ITIs and get trained into a formalised electrician career,” he says. 

The result is that many of the opportunities under the PMIS scheme are hardly as glamorous as some interns may imagine. Pencil-pushing at one of India’s top 500 companies is still pencil-pushing, after all. “The companies participating are mass-volume employers and are mostly looking for this talent for bottom-of-the-pyramid roles,” said Shukla.

And what about after the completion of the internship? 

AI and uncertain global business sentiment is already starting to provoke layoffs, and while companies may be willing to onboard interns for whom they have to shell out little, turning them into full-time employees is an entirely different question. And other government skilling programmes have run into this problem. 

For instance, in phase three of the Pradhan Mantri Kaushal Vikas Yojana, a flagship youth training scheme of the skill development ministry launched in 2015, over 4 lakh candidates were trained. But only 30,599 were actually placed https://www.business-standard.com/article/current-affairs/placements-in-national-skill-development-programme-abysmally-low-report-122121100106_1.html?utm_source=chatgpt.com. The Deen Dayal Upadhyaya Grameen Kaushalya Yojana, launched in 2014 by the rural development ministry as a skilling programme for rural youth, has seen better results. But the number of candidates placed after training as of March 2025 https://www.pib.gov.in/PressReleasePage.aspx?PRID=2085248#:~:text=by%20PIB%20Delhi-,Under%20Deen%20Dayal%20Upadhyaya%20Grameen%20Kaushalya%20Yojana%20(DDU%2DGKY),as%20under%20along%20with%20placements: is still only 65%. 

In fact, the standing committee report also flags post-internship outcomes as a key focus area.

While the scheme is in its pilot phase, the Committee emphasises that the internship-to-employment conversion rate should be a key success indicator. The  Committee, therefore, recommends establishing a robust system to monitor and track this metric, ensuring the program effectively creates career opportunities and aligns with industry demands.

Like most of the skilling and training schemes India has introduced in the past couple of decades, official statements on PMIS say all the right things. 

That there’s a critical need to “equip India’s youth with the skills they need to thrive in the job market”, and that we need to help them “gain real-world experience while benefiting from financial support”. 

It’s time such platitudes actually started reflecting in its design.

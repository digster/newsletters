Good Morning Ishan,

I love today’s story like how I love you. A lot. Let me explain.

Today’s story has a simple premise; let’s ask the questions, let’s dig deep, let’s try and get better answers because what was, and what is being fed to us, is no good. In journalism parlance, this is good old, ground up reporting. And the subject in question is, when someone asks you, pray dear, how large is India’s e-commerce market, what number comes out of your mouth?

A. $100 billion.

B. $50 billion.

C. $30 billion.

D. Go away. What kind of a question is that? It’s really big. Massive. Huge. Stunning.

Thank god for Trump. Anyway, now will be a good time to introduce the authors of today’s piece. Contributor, Saif Iqbal and my dear colleague, Rohin. The two got together to establish that the right answer to the above question is a shocking $11 billion. That’s it. No more. 

Now, just a few years ago, analysts had predicted a $50 billion market size as early as 2020 (source: UBS India Internet Report, April 2015). Or 7% of India’s $700 billion retail market. As things stand today, e-commerce stands at just 2%. With only a couple of years to go, it is unlikely that we are hitting the 7% mark anytime soon. 

Today’s story also breaks down so many puzzles in the business. Like the crap that’s peddled as gross merchandise value by companies. Check this one out, if a customer merely books (or “checks out”) a smartphone but cancels it right after the order is confirmed, or the order fails because there is a payment failure, the order is still counted as part of GMV. No money exchanged. No goods or services exchanged. But GMV goes up. Welcome to the Gross-to-Net Hotel California. Orders can go in any time they like, but they can never leave.

How are you feeling?
I am not asking because it is polite or because I am someone who works with human resources trying to assess how settled you are in your job; I am not even using it as a conversation starter.
I am asking because I mean it. Also, because I am sad that we are detached from having genuine, vulnerable conversations. So much so that there is a significant amount of literature online that teaches people how to steer past that question politely: just say ‘can’t complain’ or ‘I am good, thanks’. On the other end of the spectrum, there is enough management literature available that encourages managers to ask after their colleagues—in several different ways—and really listen.
As humans, we need that emotional connection. It's a primary need that can be expressed and met organically. In an ideal world, one would imagine that we would naturally bring out our feelings at work—since most of us spend a large amount of time there. But ‘how are you feeling?’ has become a tricky question, a problem to fix. Neither the question nor the answer rolls off the tongue easily.

To conclude the conversation I started at the beginning of this email, I want to say that emotion is not an afterthought or something to be skirted. It is the highest form of expression, even before thought and action. For emotion has the power to really connect people, bring them together, and create prosperity.

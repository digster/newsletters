“Aesthetics were not important,”
[writes](https://scroll.in/article/949350/how-the-hero-cycles-journey-was-started-by-the-munjal-brothers-and-where-the-brand-came-from) Sunil Kant Munjal in
The Making of Hero, his book on the journey of the Hero Group, founded by his father and three uncles in the 1950s. “The Hero cycle had to be a workhorse. The milkman should be able to affix his cans to the carrier, and the farmer his basket of vegetables.”
Over six decades later, that same no-frills approach is central to Hero Electric’s business. That’s how it came to control nearly half of India’s sub-25 km/h, sub-Rs 55,000 ($740) electric two-wheeler market since the launch of its first EV in 2007.
Hero Electric followed the playbook of Hero MotoCorp, India’s largest two-wheeler maker, by targeting the mass market and creating a wide network of showrooms. Hero Electric had over 600 dealers at the end of 2019.
Then came the pandemic, forcing Hero Electric to find a new road to its customers. Hero embarked on a series of online promotions, which saw enquiries jump 50% over the last year. It also decided to give buyers the option of leasing a scooter for 24 months instead of buying it outright—something its competitors such as Ather Energy (which, incidentally, is backed by Hero MotoCorp) and Ampere Vehicles also offer.
As Olina documents in her story today, Covid-19 may have made Hero Electric open to new ways of doing business, and even to new customers like e-commerce and logistics companies. But it’s in no hurry to make changes—like making its scooters “smart”—that come in conflict with its proposition of affordability.

Good Morning Ishan,

Billionaire investor Rakesh Jhunjhunwala doesn’t seem to be having a good run. Star Health and Allied Insurance, one of his portfolio companies,  listed at a 9% discount to its issue price on Friday. And now, footwear retailer Metro Brands—another Jhunjhunwala-backed company—seems set for a tepid listing later this month, with its shares expected to debut just 8% higher. Hardly a premium that would get him excited. 

One reason for this could be muted sentiments due to the Omicron scare and inflation worries. But in the case of Metro Brands, the reason could be the company’s business model itself. And its peer Bata isn’t immune to this affliction either.

Bata, Metro, and the own-store problem

When I mention Bata or Metro, I can guarantee that you will picture their stores before their shoes or sandals. 

That’s not quite the case with Nike or Adidas. Because these are distribution-first brands, in that they are sold mostly through multi-brand outlets (MBOs). Even if such brands are doubling down on their own exclusive stores (and direct-to-consumer websites), MBOs still drive the majority of their business—as much as 60% https://www.statista.com/statistics/888725/nikes-revenue-by-sales-channel-worldwide/ of global revenue, in the case of Nike.

Metro and Bata, on the other hand, are footwear retailers who also own brands.

[Source: Metro Brands https://www.icicisecurities.com/Upload/ArticleAttachments/Metro%20Brands%20Limited%20-%20DRHP.pdf]

Besides its eponymous label, Metro owns other brands including Mochi, Walkway, and Da Vinchi (the ‘h’ thrown in, ostensibly, to make sure we pronounce the Renaissance-era polymath’s name right). It also stocks third-party brands such as Skechers, Clarks, and Crocs. Bata, on the other hand, is known for brands such as Power, North Star, and Hush Puppies (which it has licensed from American footwear maker Wolverine Worldwide). 

There’s no disputing the fact that Metro and Bata have built impressive businesses; the latter is an enduring brand probably found in most middle-class Indian homes. 

But their business model seems to have hit a wall.

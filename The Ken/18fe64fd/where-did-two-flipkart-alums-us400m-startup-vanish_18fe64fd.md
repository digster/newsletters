Good Morning Ishan,
Arzooo was supposed to be a beacon of promise.
It was formed by two former Flipkart executives, Khushnud Khan and Rishi Raj Rathore, in 2018. They were the people who launched the large appliance vertical for Flipkart. And in 2022, Arzooo pulled in Rs 1,117 crore (US$134 million) in revenue. The firm was last valued at US$400 million.
Now, Arzooo’s office is “eerily quiet,” as one employee told Aayush. The company had around 400 people on payroll and 900 in total in 2023, but only fewer than 30 are still around. Three audits—one internal, one led by Arzooo’s investors, and another by a Big Four company—along with tax officials’ visit appear to have been the triggers that led to the company spiralling into its current state.
It took mere months for Arzooo to go from high-flyer to a shadow of its former self. It’s a story of unpaid bills, soured relationships, and the pull of gravity. Aayush dived into the details. Check out his [coverage](https://the-ken.com/story/where-did-two-flipkart-alums-us400m-startup-vanish/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter).
Then, in the Philippines, Isa looked at how the country’s position in the global semiconductor supply chain is changing.
Chip firms in the country have made a killing by handling the last steps of semiconductor production—assembly, packaging, and testing—but they want more. The only meaningful way to accomplish that is to develop the infrastructure, technical know-how, and human capital required to move one rung up in the supply chain and set up wafer fabrication labs.
Philippine companies that are part of the long process of making semiconductors are working on this now. Yet, that puts these companies at odds with their own clients, who are doing the same, but with automation involved.
Isa spoke with several people in the Philippines who are the drivers of change for the local semiconductor sector, from the policy level to companies eager to land new, more lucrative chipmaking contracts. Read Isa’s [story](https://the-ken.com/story/the-philippines-puts-together-20-of-the-worlds-chips-now-it-must-rethink-its-role-in-the-global-supply-chain/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter) to find out more.
Keith Gill, aka Roaring Kitty (on X and YouTube), aka DeepFuckingValue (on Reddit), is at it again.
To recap: Gill turns stocks into memes. My colleague Sumit wrote about him in an email more than two weeks ago, when the finfluencer and Reddit folk hero returned to X and rekindled frenetic activity in his followers’ trading accounts, and the saga continues to unfold. Now, E*Trade (E-Trade Financial Corporation, a subsidiary of Morgan Stanley) may ban him due to potential stock manipulation, triggered by a Reddit post by Gill’s account of a screenshot showing positions in Gamestop plus cash that were valued at over US$289 million after Monday’s close.
The meme stock phenomenon has been dissected in a few ways. Some say it results from stimulus cheques being used for high-risk investments. Others attribute it to Millennial nostalgia fuelling surges and plunges not tethered to tangible factors. But I think the deeper layer is a shared fundamental disappointment, or even distrust, in the conventional financial system: if someone else can move markets in ways that I can’t be a part of, then why bother participating at all?
Gill, on the other hand, wants you to join his brigade.
But figures like him don’t really have staying power. They’re more like seasonal flavours rather than a staple, showing up occasionally to grab your attention. A seasoned hedgie once told me that’s when it’s time to “dumb down” reasonable investment theses and just buy the ticket for the ride.
The problem is the road probably comes to an abrupt end.

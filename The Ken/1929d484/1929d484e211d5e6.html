Ola and Uber are in a “late-stage duopoly”. After spending millions and millions of dollars, they’ve finally secured pole positions in ride-sharing in India. Both these companies control ~70% of the market, and have created network effects that make it much harder for new entrants to enter and compete with them. 

However, this victory is now under threat. And just like how Ola and Uber conquered city after city using a disruptive model and technology, the same thing is threatening to happen to them. Uber and Ola are facing structural disruptions from not one, but multiple fronts in India. 

Single-handedly, none of these disrupting forces are enough to break this duopoly. However, put all of these together, and they can pose a serious, existential challenge to both these players. The threat to Uber and Ola isn’t a single giant killer, but a slow fade into irrelevance from a thousand cuts, each slicing away key, lucrative parts of their businesses from different directions.

In episode 14 of Two by Two, my co-host Rohin Dharmakumar and I have a discussion with three experts, each with strong beliefs about which of these disruptions will prove to be the most impactful for Ola and Uber. 

Joining in are Professor Srinivasan R, who teaches Strategy at IIM Bangalore; Pradeep Puranam, Head of Revenue and Operations at Yulu (ex-Udaan, -Uber); and Nilesh Sangoi, CIO of Fincare Small Finance Bank, previously CEO of Meru Cabs.

You build a taxi. You build a taxi. Everybody builds a taxi

If you already own a taxi fleet, it’s not all that hard today to get it online and compete against Uber and Ola in your own backyard. The Open Network for Digital Commerce (ONDC) allows for exactly that. “If you want to build a business on top of ONDC, you can do it for very cheap,” Prof. Srinivasan R had noted in the very first episode of Two by Two. [See: Will Flipkart become Phonepe before Phonepe becomes Flipkart?]

He’d also observed how effective it was to build and run small experiments on top of ONDC and identify interesting business use cases. We didn’t get too deep into the weeds then, but this time around, we asked him to elaborate a bit more. In fact, we discussed one of ONDC’s most well-known use cases—Namma Yatri.

Prof. Srinivasan: The technology infrastructure for finding a car and a driver is, basically, a three-way matching. The driver, and the rider, and the price discovery. 

A lot of times in closed network mobility, the price was ‘announced’. That is where the surge price, price gouging, all of that happened. In open networks, we can actually do an Indrive kind of thing. Price discovery is open. [Indrive is a US-based international ride-hailing service.]

How do I find a driver? How do I find a rider? How do I find the right price?

Somebody has coded out this entire discovery process, put it out in the open. It is there on Github to download. You run it on your own. It is white-labelled. You brand it. You build a Namma Yatri, you build RK taxis, you build A1 Cabs, you build Red Taxi, you build Blue Taxi, whatever taxi you want. I can build a taxi service for myself.

There are companies that have used ONDC to build their own pickup and drops for employees. They use that algorithm to figure out how many taxis there are in the basement, who can I assign to which employee. 

They are using the same tech.

The paradox of scale in ride-hailing

Understanding the history of how ride-hailing evolved allows us to contextualise how and why Ola and Uber have gotten progressively worse over the years, despite dominating the market with their VC money chest.

Nilesh Sangoi was previously CEO of Meru Cabs, one of India’s first large-scale ride-hailing companies. And he had a thing or two to say about why running a cab business at scale is very hard to execute once you take away all the bells and whistles of VC funding.

Nilesh: Unlike a normal business, or especially the tech business, the cab business is very paradoxical. Generally you scale, and as you scale, your overheads are taken care of and you start breaking even and get profitable.

Here, I’ve seen that profitability is largely with the smaller players. You can remain profitable till you are a certain size because you can micromanage every small part of that game. But the moment you become big, I think the profits are more difficult. Aggregators don’t own cabs. They don’t have repair and maintenance costs, or other such overheads. It’s a completely asset-light model to a large extent. Still, they’ve been posting Rs 1,000 crore, Rs 2000 crore kind of losses.

So the overheads are killing the business, in a way, because ultimately, the whole objective of any business is to generate profit. If you haven’t posted a single rupee of profit for 14 years, then you know… 

Who’s disrupting? Why is it getting disrupted? Why do more players want to get into that industry?

So in India, unfortunately, the mobility business is not profitable till date. Nobody has cracked it.

Ten years ago, this was the hypothesis, that the winner takes all—you kill all the competition, you become monopoly or duopoly, and then you command your premium. 

Today, you know, with good certainty, we can say that that has not happened.

The electric fleet slicer

Obviously, these decentralised solutions built on top of ONDC are promising, but over the years, another entrant has entered the ride-hailing business—EV fleets. The Blusmarts and Shoffrs of the world, which come attached with a premium tag and have quickly stolen away the more premium end of the spectrum from Uber and Ola.

Pradeep Puranam, who is the head of Revenue and Operations at Yulu, has seen both sides of the coin. In an earlier stint, Pradeep was Head of New Mobility at Uber, and he had a sharp take on why EV fleets could be a real threat to Ola and Uber’s dominance.

Pradeep: The biggest threat to Ola and Uber in the next few months, I feel, would be the number of players who are bringing together electric fleets and setting up their own service, trying to take a share of the premium or airport rides which fund profits for the two.

It’s very easy to put together a small fleet and to sort of run it profitably. Prior to the EV revolution, it was very hard to pull diesel vehicles—diesel prices have gone up significantly, and pollution standards have become stricter. It’s hard to scale a diesel fleet beyond a certain point. 

EV fleets, on the other hand, have certain inherent structural advantages in terms of repair and maintenance being cheaper, charging being very inexpensive. The breakeven for a fleet owner is that much easier. I think this is something which is going to disrupt the profit pools of Ola and Uber, and they should definitely watch out for that.

Do the threats facing Ola and Uber come from within?

Okay, so until now, everything I’ve said is a reflection of external forces that will force Ola and Uber to sit up and work out a defensible strategy.

But what if complacency has set in within these organisations?

To this effect, my co-host Rohin made a very valid point quite early in our discussion—that both these companies have grown comfortable with where they are and don’t seem too bothered to protect their own interests.

Rohin: For me, the biggest threat to Ola and Uber is incumbency and complacency.

Ola and Uber, today in India, are not the same companies that they were five years or 10 years ago. There’s an Ola Electric IPO, Bhavish’s attention is all over the place. Suffice to say, it’s not on Ola Cabs.

Uber has done its IPO, Dara has turned around the company, they’ve made a billion dollars in profit, they’re absolutely killing it in the rest of the world, India does not seem to be on priority. So the fire and the zeal that drove them to compete and offer better products, the kind of leadership and people that they had… it’s gone, it’s missing. And I don’t see it coming back anytime any soon.

And that’s really the biggest threat.

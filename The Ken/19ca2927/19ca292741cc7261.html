You know those days when it seems like the whole world is out to get you?

Well, if you are Anthropic, this is one of those times. 

Consider what the company has been going through just in the last week. 

You discover that Chinese AI companies are running industrial-scale campaigns to steal your models, so you publicly admonish them. In India, you’re still making headlines for refusing to lock arms with your biggest rival at some bizarre photo-op that you still can’t make sense of. 

And now, to cap it all off, you’re going to war against your own government.

The US government uses AI models for all kinds of things. Most of them are administrative stuff like managing payroll, HR systems, and insurance. But it also needs models for highly classified missions which are deeply linked to its systems.

Right now, there’s just one model that’s been approved to use for that purpose—Claude, which is owned by Anthropic. 

Anthropic seemed fine with that arrangement, but the first signs of tension emerged after reports said the US military had used Claude in its operation to kidnap Nicolás Maduro from Venezuela. Over the last few days, both sides have made their differences clear…

This week on the Zero Shot podcast

Hi! I am Vidhatri, the producer of Zero Shot. Last week, I was in Delhi for a personal visit, but I could not escape the India AI Impact Summit. During dinner at the iconic Gulati Restaurant on Pandara Road, I was surrounded by delegates from across the world who were clearly in the capital for the event. The friend I was staying with had a networking dinner scheduled for “AI startup” friends from business school. They were in the city for the summit, too. Not to mention the numerous tech and policy friends from Bengaluru who found themselves in Central Delhi that week. 

Everyone was—and still is—talking about the mega gathering that took place. As an AI podcast, we joined the conversation too. But the focus was not the robot dog or the awkward Altman-Amodei moment. Our host, Praveen Gopal Krishnan, had a very simple yet deep question: What actually happened at the summit? 

To help us answer the question, we brought on our Delhi-based reporter, Inderpal Singh, who was at the summit. He gave us a full view of the events, demos, sessions, and politics, as well as the conversations inside and the chatter outside. Praveen and Brady Ng, our other host, asked Inderpal all sorts of curious questions. 

The conversation revealed that the summit was actually two events: a builder expo and a geopolitical convention. Those things don’t typically square. But the Indian government’s choice to make it such reveals a lot about its AI ambitions. This conversation tackles every pressing question on AI today: US-China competition, security, impact, sovereignty, and much more.

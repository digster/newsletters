Good Morning Ishan,
What’s costlier for a business—a debt raise or equity funding?
Debt? Because interest has to be paid, hell or high water? Not quite. The old chestnut—higher the risk, higher the reward—means that equity is more expensive than debt. After all, equity shareholders are last in line if the business goes belly up.
But being cheaper is one reason venture-debt financing is finally finding its place in the Indian startup sun. Unlike its glamorous venture-equity cousin, venture debt doesn’t dilute promoters’ stakes. More importantly, big-ticket equity funding has been hard to get, of late.
Ergo: more startups are now knocking at the doors of venture-debt firms. Over the past few months, companies like milk-and-grocery-delivery startup Country Delight and electric two-wheeler maker Ather Energy have borrowed big sums from the likes of Alteria Capital and Stride Ventures. Another firm, Trifecta Capital, launched its fourth debt fund—which it aims to make its biggest yet—in August.
As much as US$1.2 billion was deployed by the industry last year, 4X from five years earlier—and this should ideally grow more. Investors are lining up to pour money into venture-debt funds. It helps that some part of the venture debt could convert into equity stake for investors.
But—and there’s always a but—not all’s well in this universe. Venture debt remains joined at the hip with venture equity, and the latter’s not doing too well. Also, some bitter past experiences are making these debt providers wary and choosy. This comes with costs and consequences, as Gaurav lays out in [today’s story](https://the-ken.com/story/startups-cant-get-enough-of-venture-debt-but-debt-cant-get-enough-venture-capital/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter).

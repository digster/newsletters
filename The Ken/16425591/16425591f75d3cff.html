Friday is finally here. 
 
I don't know about you, but it's been a long week for us at The Ken. Especially because we are in the middle of some aggressive hiring here. Staff Writers. Graphic Designers. Digital Marketing. Interviews. Assignments. Offer letters. The works. 
 
Personally, the interviews with the digital marketing candidates have been nothing short of illuminating. We were looking for someone who understood what made The Ken tick, was creative and could make sense of abstract data to create sharp, penetrating insights. So, we kept our expectations low. And then the candidates came in—most of them, subscribers—and proceeded to blow our minds. Some of the ideas have been phenomenal. One person suggested a fiendishly simple, but startlingly effective hack to change how we send our daily emails. Another person created a model to show us how we could market The Ken digitally among retired professionals. We even had someone who told us a fantastic concept for a podcast. We thanked everyone and plan to appropriate all these ideas. 
 
That's what unconventional ideas can do; they change the fortunes of a company. Today's story is about a company that survived and thrived because of smart, outlandish ideas. Mobisy had multiple avatars, each time driven by a different strategy, until it finally found its place in the sun with its Bizom platform. Some of the stuff they did from a marketing and adoption standpoint was extraordinary. They even had a name for it—algorithmic jugaad. Lalit Bhise, their CEO, gives an example of this: 

 

Stretched for resources, this new team came up with unique ways to solve problems. For instance, Nilesh Chhajed, a former failed entrepreneur who joined Mobisy to set up its Mumbai operations, came up with the idea of evangelising Bizom’s usage among its subscribers and charging them for it (convincing customers that higher usage by sales staff would lead to higher revenue). 

“My relatively idle customer support team handling inbound calls was now doing many more outbound calls, and we were getting paid for it. We call this 'algorithmic jugaad',” says Bhise.

 
Wait, you ask. What subscribers? What problems were they trying to solve? What is algorithmic jugaad, exactly?
 
All good questions. To know the answers, you will need to read the story. You can find it here: https://the-ken.com/story/mobisy-algorithmic-jugaad-company/
 
Also, today's story is by Rohin, so it's told from the perspective of a CEO, which makes it even more fascinating.

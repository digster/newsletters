Imagine if someone had told you in 1998 that the minimal, fast, and dramatically better search engine that had just launched, the one whose founders despised advertising-funded search engines, was really the plumbing for the most efficient advertising machine ever? I’m talking about Google. Or that the social network that launched in 2004 to help you connect with your classmates and take funny quizzes, was the scaffolding for the most efficient data collection machine ever? I’m talking about Facebook.

By all accounts, even the founders of these giants didn’t know this back then. But such is the nature, speed and scale of tech innovation and evolution that some companies arrive at their “real” business model in a way that looks obvious to the rest of us in hindsight.

But what if, instead of stumbling through interactions and pivots, you actually planned things out? As in, decide upfront what your “eventual” business model was going to be, and then design stepping stones for yourself to get there. 

Let’s take an example. Indonesia has over 60 million micro, small and medium enterprises (MSMEs) that together account for 97% of its workforce and 60% of GDP. Say you wanted to offer them financial services like loans, digital payments, online storefronts and credit financing?

Best of luck. MSMEs are a notoriously hard market to crack, especially if you want visibility into their operations and financials. Then, there’s also the fact that the majority of the 60 million MSMEs are still too nascent and small to be able to afford any new services.

Okay, so then, let’s take a step back. What if we offer them well-designed and simple bookkeeping and accounting software? Software that not only makes it easy to manage your daily business but also send WhatsApp messages to suppliers and customers who owed you money? The best part—accounting software “sees” all the money, products and sales going through a business. 

Great idea, but best of luck convincing MSMEs to pay for it, even though it would be very useful to most of them.

Then, let’s take a step further back. Let’s give it to them for free. That’s right. 

That’s exactly what BukuKas and BukuWarung are doing. Both are just two-year-old companies, but have raised US$60 million and US$70 million in funding, respectively, this year alone. The latter’s valuation is reportedly already US$250 million.

Their “ultimate” business model is the pot of gold that VCs are hopeful about. “Five years from now, we’ll be very different from the early days,” said Krishnan Menon, CEO of BukuKas.

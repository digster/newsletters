What if the world’s largest drone maker decides to give India a pass?
What if it’s also the largest supplier of drones in the country?
What if the so-called local (amateur?) tech advisors muddy the waters instead of charting new courses?
It grinds the nascent but promising drone industry to a halt. And China’s DJI, by far the world leader in this space, is busy lobbying the US which is banning its drones and sitting out India’s puzzling policy-making.
From the time in 2014 when a Mumbai eatery used a drone to deliver a pizza and woke the authorities up to drone regulation, to August 2018, when the new civil aviation requirements rendered all drone operations in the country illegal, it’s a messy tale.
In his story today, Pratap says the system wasn’t always this broken. The Pandora’s box was opened when the tech policy think tank iSpirt and some drone companies joined hands with the Indian government to take drones and aerial imaging into the digital age with their DigitalSky platform. (To refresh memory, iSpirt also contributed to building the Unified Payments Interface, UPI, on which PhonePe, Google and WhatsApp have based their payments offerings in India)
But for DigitalSky, they needed DJI to comply with the Indian requirements, otherwise 80% of Indian drones in use are illegal. Even trips to DJI headquarters were futile. The Chinese company said it’d wait for Indian regulations to settle.
Yesterday, the southern state of Telangana sought an exemption—to airdrop medicines in remote areas. How did we completely break the working system? And if India missed the drone hardware bus, could software be the saviour?

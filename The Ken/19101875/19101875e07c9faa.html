Good Morning Ishan,

This morning’s story is about Razorpay making up for lost time.

The RBI embargo that prevented the payment aggregator from onboarding merchants was lifted seven months ago, and Razorpay has been on a quest to clear its backlog of 50,000 merchants—plus, the many more who joined its waitlist.

Putting the pedal to the metal to work with enterprise customers changed Razorpay as a company, its employees told us, and that wasn’t just in the first moments after the RBI rescinded its restriction.

Salary hikes were capped at 8% for FY23’s appraisals, for instance, a steep fall from the 20–25% range that was the norm before. Even though current and former employees still say Razorpay is a great place to work, the days of plum perquisites appear to be over. Administrative processes have changed, too. “There’s more control from mid-management and a sort of disconnect has formed,” one former staffer told us.

But that structure, along with internal shuffling and skill cultivation when the RBI embargo was in place, is yielding benefits now. Since the beginning of 2024, Razorpay has launched 60 new products, including fraud detection tools and an AI assistant. It plans to roll out another 25–30 by the end of the year.

Razorpay processes US$180 billion in payments annually. Its costs are rising but the company is positioning itself to take revenue numbers higher, too. Cross-border payments, which account for 15% of Razorpay’s net revenue, are seen as the next source of growth for the company.

These changes and the move to shift its domicile from the United States to India are all part of Razorpay’s preparation to go public, which the company expects to take place after FY26. Anjali has the details. Check out her story about the transformations taking place within Razorpay.

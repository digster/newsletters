Zomato rides the irrationality. The cynical view of Zomato is that it’s playing the TAM game, because the public markets are driven by narratives right now

Good Morning Ishan,

Well, so I guess it’s finally official.

Food delivery giant Zomato on Wednesday completed the acquisition of fintech major Paytm’s entertainment ticketing business, Paytm Insider, both the companies said in regulatory filings.

Last week, Zomato announced that it was acquiring Paytm Insider for ~2,048 crore, in a bid to scale up its going-out business and add newer use cases.

Under the deal, Zomato has fully acquired Paytm’s movie ticketing business Orbgen Technologies Pvt Ltd (OTPL) for ~1,264.6 crore and its events ticketing business Wasteland Entertainment Pvt Ltd (WEPL) for ~783.8 crore.

The new business will be spun off into a separate going-out app, District, in the coming few weeks.

District will be Zomato’s third attempt at creating a “super brand” after the success of its food delivery business and quick commerce venture Blinkit. With the new vertical, Zomato is going after the largely unorganised live events and ticketing market.

Zomato completes acquisition of Paytm’s entertainment ticketing biz, Business Standard https://www.business-standard.com/companies/news/zomato-completes-acquisition-of-paytm-s-entertainment-ticketing-biz-124082800685_1.html

Look, there are two ways to write today’s newsletter. On one hand, I can make the argument that Zomato wants to be known as a company for two things:  

Going out.
 Staying in. 

That’s it. That’s the strategy. Welcome to my Linkedin post. 

Zomato started off by solving a problem that all of us encounter—where should we go out for a meal or a drink? It began as a repository of “eating out” places. Restaurants. Pubs. Bars. Over time, it added more and more layers on top of this. You can use Zomato to book a table. Or to pay and get a discount. Or leave a review so you can tell all your friends that you are a “foodie”. Or read reviews. Or check out menus. Bottom line—if you are anywhere in India, and if you want to go out somewhere and you aren’t sure where, you open Zomato. 

But not everyone wants to go out. Some people are lazy and prefer staying in. Some of these people are introverts and would really not want to interact with a human being, and are willing to pay extra just to avoid the hassle of calling restaurants to place a delivery order. If they could just hit some buttons on an app and magically get food delivered to their houses, that would be amazing.

So Zomato decided to help these people out and set up an entire delivery fleet across the length and breadth of India’s major cities, just to deliver food to them. Then it helped some local entrepreneurs set up restaurants that just did food delivery. Then it helped those restaurants procure https://blog.zomato.com/decoding-hyperpure the stuff they needed to make the food. 

After a few years of doing this, Zomato figured out that if it could deliver food, it could also deliver other things, like groceries. In many ways, Zomato didn’t have a choice but to do this. The company has been around for a little more than a decade, and has saturated both the going-out and the ordering-in market for food. Hence, it acquired a company that was known for grocery delivery, and rebranded it to a new app called Blinkit. Investors were sceptical but Zomato didn’t care. In a few quarters, Zomato scaled Blinkit at an astonishing rate. Introverts couldn’t believe their luck that they could get everything from printouts to the latest iPhones, all in 10 minutes, and they didn’t have to leave their homes. Zomato had completely conquered the staying-in market. 

And now Zomato is wondering, well, how can we conquer the going-out market? It looked around to see what else people go out for. Turns out, people don’t just go out to restaurants, they also go out to attend events like stand-up comedy shows, music concerts, or other events. So it did exactly what it did earlier with the staying-in business—it acquired a company that had some experience doing this, rebranded it, and has started making plans to scale it. It’s the same thing, all over again. 

Of course, we could sit and try to understand if it really is the same thing or not. Perhaps we can argue that Zomato expanding its going-out business from food to events is in some way vastly different from it expanding its staying-in business from food to groceries. Sure, sure. Maybe it’s different. Maybe it’s the same. There are good arguments on both sides. 

But one thing is definitely different this time around. 

And it’s that Zomato’s investors aren’t sceptical anymore. If anything, they are probably way more exuberant than they ought to be.

Personally, I think that’s the more interesting story. 

It’s easy to make the business or the strategy case for Zomato acquiring Paytm’s entertainment business and doing what it did with Blinkit, but on the going-out side of its business. 

Today, I am making a different case, i.e., Zomato is selling a narrative. 

A couple of months ago, my colleague Seetharaman made an argument https://the-ken.com/tradetricks/zomato-needs-to-feed-the-beast/in his newsletter that Zomato was doing this acquisition because it needed to “feed the beast”. His point was essentially that Blinkit’s growth was going to tap out at some point, and Zomato wants to get ahead of it by stitching up another ‘growth business’.

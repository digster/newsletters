“I think I am at this stage,” Byju Raveendran says, circling, stressing on the dot.

“So everything has to be exponential. How fast things change. How fast technologies are getting adapted. Even before the regulations are able to catch up; in seven years, everything will change. Before regulators, governments and people catch up, things will change because of the intervention of technology. Like what happened with Uber.”

The above excerpt is from our first story on edtech firm Byju’s six years ago, where, among other things, The Ken called it a “hype machine”.

To his credit, founder Raveendran did make everything “exponential”, and in less than the stated “seven years”. Since that story in April 2017, we’ve written about 17 standalone stories on this edtech, each capturing a turning point in the company’s life.

Today, Alifiya and Vanita write another such story. One that talks about how Byju’s’ bizarre ways of doing business have brought, well, exponential hardships for its employees.

Currently, the job market has over 24,000 active and accessible talent from Byju’s owner, Think and Learn Pvt. Ltd, says Prasadh MS, head of workforce research at staffing firm Xpheno.

The last two weeks alone have seen an addition of 3,000 Byju’s employees to the active-job-seekers count.

Layoffs in the last year or so have been steady and near-universal among many startups as the venture-capital tap ran dry. But Byju’s employees have it worse as they are associated with a company that has a questionable record in corporate governance.

For a founder who boasted that his company was the largest startup employer in the country with a headcount of 55,000, the current state of Byju’s’ laid-off and existing workforce is a story that serves as a reminder that when things go wrong, they can go exponentially wrong. (Pardon my repetitive use of ‘exponential’.)

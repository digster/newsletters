Airtel, Bajaj Finance start a staredown with Jio. The new alliance offers each partner a good shot at their respective goals 
 By Rounak Kumar Gunjan https://the-ken.com/writers/rounakthe-ken-com/

You are reading our subscriber-only newsletter Ka-Ching!—a weekly commentary on fintech and personal finance 

Imagine this:

It’s a dusty battlefield in the middle of nowhere. A strong gust of wind kicks up a cloud of dirt, out of which a lone figure emerges. A seasoned, grizzled veteran clearly ready for another fight. 

The camera slowly pans 180 degrees, past the barren sepia wasteland, to land on his rival. Younger, yes, but confident; someone who has, by now, gotten the better of many an older opponent. 

They slowly stride towards each other, until there’s just a few metres between them. 

The background music intensifies. Hands inch towards holsters, eyes never wavering from the challenger in front. 

The music ramps up another notch, until… bam! Both turn toward the noise, and see another figure walking out of the ruins. In just a few seconds, the seasoned veteran now has a friend standing beside him. 

What was a 1 vs 1 suddenly becomes a 2 vs 1. 

If you are wondering why I’m narrating the climax of a classic spaghetti western to you this morning, it’s because that’s pretty much what happened earlier this week when telecom major Bharti Airtel announced https://www.moneycontrol.com/news/business/airtel-bajaj-finance-partner-to-create-financial-services-digital-platform-12914547.html a partnership with Bajaj Finance, India’s largest non-bank. 

According to the official statement, Airtel will start by offering Bajaj Finance’s products on the Airtel Thanks App and later through its nationwide network of over 2,500 stores. Two Bajaj Finance products are already live on the app, and by the end of the year, the partnership aims to offer ten financial products through the platform, including gold, business, and personal loans, and a co-branded Insta EMI Card. 

It’s a pretty run-of-the-mill integration to facilitate more credit, albeit an ambitious one. 

But it’s also a challenge.

A face off

Both Airtel and Bajaj Finance are veterans of their respective businesses, having been around for over three decades. Both have deep distribution networks across the country—Airtel for its SIM cards and telecom services, Bajaj Finance for its consumer durable loans. And both are at interesting junctures in their journeys. 

Airtel, for instance, is doubling down its bets in the financial services segment through Airtel Payments Bank, one of only three profitable payments banks in India, and Airtel Finance. 

Airtel Payments Bank posted Rs 674 crore (US$78 million) as revenue in the quarter ended September 2024, a 58% year-on-year growth. On average, it adds nearly a million new customers every month. Airtel Finance, on the other hand, is a high-margin software-as-a-service product run by a lean team of nearly 30 data experts stationed in Gurugram. We wrote https://the-ken.com/story/your-sim-card-is-a-new-weapon-in-airtel-and-jios-fintech-war/ about it last year.

Bajaj Finance, for its part, is looking to expand beyond its existing user base and products, according to a senior executive in the company. From being a largely urban, short tenure loan-focussed lender, it wants to expand into an almost bank-like entity that provides long-term, secured loans with a significant presence in the hinterlands.

To that effect, it has started https://the-ken.com/story/bajaj-finance-wants-to-lend-like-a-bank/ dabbling in commercial-vehicle financing, agri-financing, tractor-financing, and even microfinancing. And the diversification makes sense. The Reserve Bank of India has time and again rung the alarm bell on rapidly proliferating small-tenure loans, the mainstay of Bajaj Finance. In 2023, the RBI actually raised the risk weights for consumer credit to 125% from 100%—a move aimed at reducing risk by ensuring lenders have enough capital available before they disburse such loans. 

Obviously, Bajaj Finance needs a new set of users to sell its products to—users who are less risky and have the appetite for bigger-ticket loans. 

Enter Airtel, with nearly 410 million users.

Taking the fight to Jio

Ever since Reliance Jio’s launch nearly a decade ago, Airtel has constantly found itself playing catch-up with its younger rival. According to quarterly industry reports, Airtel’s overall subscriber base is currently about 70 million short of Jio’s, despite having been around for a lot longer. 

Both telecom majors have also had a finger in the financial services pie for a few years now. Just a couple of days ago, news broke https://www.moneycontrol.com/news/business/mutual-funds/jio-financial-services-blackrock-infuse-rs-117-crore-in-mutual-fund-business-12916283.html#google_vignette that Jio and its US-based partner Blackrock have infused Rs 117 crore in their mutual fund business. 

It’s pretty clear Airtel doesn’t want to allow Jio Financial Services, Reliance Jio’s wholly-owned subsidiary, to steal a large lead in the financial services space. 

But why Bajaj Finance for a partner? Why not go it alone? 

“Airtel gains an edge through Bajaj’s expertise and credibility in the financial services space. Bajaj Finance offering products through a telecom app is better than a telco giving out loans,” said a senior Airtel executive. Essentially, it’s easier for Airtel to team up with an existing lender than start a credit business from scratch. 

Jio Financial Services is also largely focussed on the business-to-business loan segment and is engaged in asset-management services, so it makes sense for Airtel to try to break into retail lending. And Bajaj Finance, like I mentioned earlier, is India’s largest non-bank lender in the retail space. 

Bajaj Finance needed new, more creditworthy users. Airtel needed a boost to keep up with Jio in financial services. 

A team-up gives them a good shot at both goals.

Let's start with a thought exercise. How many charging stations does India need for its electric vehicles?
A 1,000? 100,000? 1 million?
Chances are, you don't know. That's ok. Neither does the Indian government. But the government is hell-bent on playing some kind of electric Midas to India's vast public and private transport sectors. All it needed was a shiny, new wand. And charging stations will do just the trick. For now.
The government's doing some simple math in its head. More places to charge = more EVs on the road. That's why the government recently gave out tenders to public sector companies like NTPC, EESL, and REIL to set up 2,600 charging stations across 62 million-plus cities.
Big whoop? Not exactly. Setting up a charging grid in India will excavate uniquely Indian problems. Parking. Uneven power supply and distribution. Tariff control. Even standards for plugs aren't... well... standard.
But the real kicker is that charging operators like the Finnish company Fortum or home-grown ones like Magenta and Vottic, haven't yet figured out a way to make any real money from setting up these charging stations.
Without a vast fleet of electric cars zipping across India's roads, it's hard to predict what this public network of chargers could look like. But my colleague Pranav has dug his heels in and gotten us some answers. They lie in monetising data. In partnering with reluctant automakers. In using spaces like malls, cafés, and public parking spaces creatively.
Fortum and its likes have thrown caution to the winds and jumped into the Indian market. Even though, realistically, public charging might only constitute 30% of the total demand. And there's also battery-swapping lurking in the corner, ready to upset these best-laid plans.

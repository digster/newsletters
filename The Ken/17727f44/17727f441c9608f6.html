Good Morning Ishan,
 
Initially, the issue was about getting vaccines. Could India guarantee enough vaccines for itself in the midst of a mad global scramble for the first available and approved doses?
 
Despite initial uncertainty, we managed.
 
Then came the next hurdle—delivery. Could we make use of the vaccines we had to efficiently vaccinate those who needed them the most? With a population of over a billion and most vaccines being extremely temperature-sensitive, it's a tough ask.
 
The government began conducting dry runs, and the public was assured we would manage. After all, the government had a secret weapon to track vaccine administration—CoWIN. The app is meant to record vaccine beneficiary data and track whether those eligible for the vaccine have got their jab on time.
 
CoWIN, however, has not lived up to its name. The platform's repeated glitches have caused bottlenecks in vaccine drives. None of this has been helped by the fact that the entire vaccine administration process is disjointed and often manual. Systems meant to alert recipients, for instance, aren't integrated with CoWIN, nor are they particularly effective or accurate.
 
As today's piece makes clear, CoWIN's shortcomings indicate a lack of foresight on the part of India's health officials when it comes to piloting and scalability. Only three dry runs of the platform were done—and even those were only in a few districts—before it was expected to do its job nationwide. And like its sibling, India's now-forgotten contract tracing app Aarogya Setu, CoWIN also has the potential to be a minefield of privacy issues.
 
Today's story on CoWIN is about how even the best intentions matter little in the face of shoddy execution. A team effort by Pratap, Olina, and Maitri, it's also our free story for the week. Do share it widely.

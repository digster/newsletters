Good Afternoon Ishan,
Imagine an accident on a highway. Multiple victims, blaring ambulance sirens, and hospitals a fair distance away.
For doctors and paramedics who are first on the scene, one question takes precedence above all others: who can be saved, and who can’t? If they find victims who are too severely injured to be treated with the resources at hand, they leave them be and move on to others.
This is, in fact, the first thing taught to first-response specialists. When resources are scarce—think oxygen during the second Covid-19 wave in India—and too many people need them, it’s time for a very quick calculus.
Assess which of your patients are most likely to survive. Focus your efforts on them. There may be people in worse condition, but you have to accept that you cannot help them. There’s a word for this process: triaging. Figuring out where scarce resources will be most effectively utilised, and washing your hands off the rest.
And in times of economic distress, it happens in companies too. Employers still want to retain some people, but it’s all triaged. Only those most likely to benefit the company are retained. The others? Move on, please.
This is exactly what my colleague Akshaya found out when she began exploring retention policies for this week’s episode of Cost to Company. From what she gathered from her sources, it seems companies are shifting to a new retention system, one that’s hidden from employees.
Companies have now started sorting employees into specific categories. And based on where they get slotted, they receive different retention incentives and perks. AKA, companies are triaging their retention.
How? There are three classifications – superstars (those who have exceeded expectations), finishers (those who have done the bare minimum), and also-rans (those who have failed expectations).

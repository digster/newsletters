Good Morning Ishan,

In its most elemental form, scaling is about how a system responds when its size changes. It’s also about maintaining the building blocks of the system as it grows from small to large. This is familiar in economies, engineering, physical systems—in all evolutionary processes. Sadly, it breaks down in many other spheres.

Take the case of the IIMs. The prestigious Indian Institutes of Management franchise decided about a decade ago to grow rapidly. If the first generation of eight IIMs were built over 40 years, the next 12 were set up between 2011 and 2015. Fast and furious as they drove, the new IIMs attained the ideal batch size in 10 years, which older IIMs took decades to achieve.

If we consider placements as the next building block of the IIM system, newer IIMs have impressive stats there as well. IIM Tiruchirappalli, for instance, had 119 companies recruiting from its campus in 2020, a 26% increase from the previous year. But it’s here that similarities with the legacy IIMs begin to erode.

As if a public power struggle https://the-ken.com/story/leadership-storm-iim-calcutta/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter at IIM Calcutta wasn’t enough, IIM Tiruchirappalli had a mini replay when members of the faculty shot off an anonymous, 16-page letter to the Board of Governors, accusing the current director of financial impropriety, nepotism, and opaque decision-making, among other complaints.

Other IIMs have existential issues of their own. Arising from their location, the need to raise their own funds, and earn brownie points. In a wonderfully reported story today, Olina argues that the most damaging bit is that the newer IIMs are often run like mini placement-oriented industries, devoid of the open and transparent academic culture of the older IIMs.

Talking of culture, female students of IIM Tiruchirappalli had a curfew imposed on them; they needed permission to leave their hostels to even attend placement interviews.

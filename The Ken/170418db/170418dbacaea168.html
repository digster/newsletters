There are many ways - savoury and unsavoury - to divide people in this world. One of my favourites is "Are you a coffee lover or a tea person?" Indians, almost unanimously, are tea, or chai, drinkers. But the cult of coffee has been steadily growing.
 
Coffee connoisseurs talk about the three waves of coffee. The first started in the 1800s and brought pre-ground coffee into homes. The second wave was when cafe chains mushroomed, selling an "experience" and making words like espresso and latte mainstream. That's right, Starbucks is the poster child of this wave. But these waves were criticised for selling "bad" coffee.
 
The third wave, in the early 2000s, was a reaction to this lousy coffee. It takes the focus off cafes and into the cup, bringing an appreciation for coffee as an artisanal or craft beverage.
 
This wave has reached India's shores via niche joints like Blue Tokai, Curious Life, Flying Squirrel. And the eponymous Third Wave Coffee Roasters.
 
But marrying artisanal love or the cafe experience with a rock-solid business - because a cafe chain is a business at the end of the day - is not easy. From Indian chains like Cafe Coffee Day and Barista to foreign companies like Starbucks and Costa, all are in the red, with a dwindling store count.
 
It will be some success story then if Bengaluru-headquartered Third Wave Coffee Roasters succeeds in its plan to move from 15 stores currently to 100 stores in the next year. The expansion plan though is decidedly anti-third wave given it includes food, central kitchens, deliveries, kiosks and also cafes. Moreover, the company has been finding it tough to raise funds.

Good Morning Ishan,

 

Our story today is about the state of driverless cars in India. 
 
By now, I am sure you know what to expect from my emails. Some light-hearted attempts to poke fun at the idea of driverless cars on Indian roads. Maybe one non-controversial joke about cows. Another about traffic jams. One more about pedestrians in Bengaluru walking on roads while bikers use the footpaths. Maybe even repeat what every half-wit with an ambition for stand-up comedy and access to a WhatsApp group says: "Isn’t all driving in India basically driverless? Hahaha."
 
A lot of people say that if you can make autonomous cars work in India, they can work anywhere in the world. India is the true test, they say. It’s chaotic. It’s crazy. It’s unpredictable. 
 
You may agree. 
 
I don’t. 
 
To get driverless cars to work in India conditions is remarkably simple. Driving in India can be boiled down to four rules: 
 
1. Simply follow the car in front of you. 
 
2. Let bikes and autos do whatever they wish. 
 
3. Surrender to the inevitable, supreme, karmic truth that the scumbag who cuts you off in this signal will only find himself stuck underneath the next flyover between two Tempo travellers for the next hour. In Bengaluru, you don’t beat traffic. Traffic beats you. 
 
4. Don’t shoot toll-booth attendants. 
 
That’s it. Really. I don’t know about you, but I think driverless cars will be able to do all the above steps flawlessly, with a single-minded, focused, robotic accuracy. All without forced small talk about your salary, job prospects and marital status. Seems like it’s an obvious win-win. 
 
But apparently not. Sajjad Anwar, a cartographer and programmer who's worked with open-data and mapping projects in India, and our contributor today, has a story which says quite the opposite. His story delves into the things that make India uniquely positioned with respect to autonomous driving. The challenges range from connectivity issues, infrastructural problems, myopic policy thinking and above all, a significant lack of will from decision-makers to address these problems head-on. All while other countries march ahead. He thinks this is a mistake. And one which we will pay a heavy price for.

Indigo, India’s largest airline by a mile, may have enjoyed an early victory against auto major Mahindra & Mahindra over the alleged infringement of its trademark callsign—6E. But things are anything but 6E (sexy for those who haven’t yet figured out the phonetic play) for the low-cost carrier.

A survey last week by a German consumer-rights group ranked Indigo among the worst 10 airlines in the world. That must have been a bolt from the blue, given this was not long after it bagged the ‘2024 Airline of the Year’ award from global market-intelligence firm CAPA and touted its three ‘awards and accolades’ from industry bodies in the September quarter. 

Indigo has, of course, refuted being in the dregs of the aviation world and cast doubts on the credibility of the agency that relegated it thus. It has also pointed to monthly data from aviation regulator DGCA that shows it scoring high on punctuality and low on customer-complaint ratios “for an airline of its size and scale of operations”.

That “size and scale” likely explains the disconnect between what Indigo (along with the myriad industry bodies) thinks about its services and what its fliers feel about it.

Indigo has become a victim of its own success. This winner’s curse is showing not just in customer complaints but also in the airline’s financial performance. Despite flying nearly two out of every three fliers in one of the world’s largest and fastest-growing aviation markets, the airline posted a loss of close to Rs 1,000 crore (US$118 million) in the recent September quarter. What gives? Size hurts.

Indigo has a point. DGCA data shows that in October 2024, the airline’s cancellation rate of 0.78% was much lower than that of Spicejet and Vistara, and only somewhat higher than Air India. Its number of complaints per 10,000 passengers—at 0.2—was also far lower than Air India’s 0.6 and Akasa Air’s 0.5. Besides, Indigo’s on-time performance at four metro airports (about 72%) was better than that of the competition.

But here’s the thing. In absolute terms, a far greater number of fliers were hurt by Indigo’s cancellations and delays. Nearly 24,000 passengers were affected by the airline’s cancellations in October, and close to 100,000 fliers were stranded by flight delays longer than two hours. This is far worse than the travails inflicted on passengers by rivals, including its closest competitors Air India and Vistara. Similar patterns were seen in earlier months, too. 

Alternative flights, refunds, and the like—mandated by the regulator—don’t quite quell the ire of the aggrieved flier. And that’s what is likely showing in Indigo’s poor customer satisfaction scores in the German firm-led survey. More passengers, more complaints, more the chance of becoming a lightning rod. 

Solving this will require more efficient processes and a concerted attempt at improving customer satisfaction, both before boarding and in-flight. 

That’s easier said than done for an airline of Indigo’s size and scale. It flew over 86 lakh domestic passengers in October, or nearly 3 lakh passengers each day. This, plus its fleet of about 400 aircraft and counting, makes it among the largest airlines in the world. Making this elephant dance can be a herculean task.

Still, it might be possible, even imperative. From being a pure-play low-cost carrier, Indigo is now going hybrid with the recent launch of pricier business seats on some routes. This means it will have to keep a tight leash on cancellations, delays, and complaints if it intends to make a dent in Air India’s dominance in this space. 

A more intractable problem seems to be air ticket prices. Here’s what Indigo had to say about its September quarter loss, despite growing revenue 15% year-on-year: 

“Indigo, however, reported a net loss of Rs 9.9 billion driven by higher fuel costs and a peak number of groundings, which have now started to reduce.”

The numbers are telling. 

During the quarter, Indigo’s fuel cost per available seat kilometre (CASK) grew by over 4%, and its total CASK grew about 12% year-on-year. CASK is a unit measure of an airline’s cost. In contrast, the airline’s revenue per available seat kilometre, a unit measure of its revenue, grew less than 5%. And its yield—a unit measure of passenger ticket revenue—grew just over 2%. Simply put, Indigo was not able to raise its ticket prices and revenue to make up for the increase in its costs, leading to a big splash of red on its bottom line. 

Why couldn’t an airline with an outsized market share do that? Precisely because of its outsized market share, of course.

Air fliers are already feeling the pinch from high ticket costs, and any sharp increase will risk a backlash both from passengers and the government. As the de facto price setter in India’s aviation market, Indigo would again become a lightning road. Also, the risk of ceding market share to the Air India + Vistara combo runs high.

Indigo’s problem of grounded aircraft due to Pratt & Whitney engine issues—and the associated costs—is unlikely to go away anytime soon either. While the peak of this trouble may have passed, there were still over 60 such aircraft as of September. The airline’s management expects the number to go down to the mid-40s by FY26. Still, that’s a long way to go. 

The winner is walking on eggshells. 

On the bourses, though, Indigo stock seems to be enjoying the TINA (there is no alternative) premium. Air India is not listed, and has its own share of troubles with the Vistara integration. Spicejet is listed, but one never knows if this cat-with-nine-lives may join its now-shut peers like Kingfisher Airlines, Jet Airways, and Goair. Akasa Air is still small and unlisted.

So, like in the skies, Indigo is a winner on the stock market—despite the odds. Its investors will hopefully be hoping against the winner’s curse.

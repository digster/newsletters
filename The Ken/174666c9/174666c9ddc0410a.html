Three people go to a restaurant. The bill comes to $30, so each of them pay $10 each. Then the manager finds a mistake and realises that the bill was only $25. So, he gives $5 to the waiter to return it to the three guests. Now, the waiter is smart and understands there’s no way three people can divide $5 among themselves. So, he keeps $2 and returns a dollar each to the three guests.
 
So each guest paid $10 and got $1 back—$9 each, effectively. So three guests paid $27 altogether. The waiter kept $2. That’s $29.

But the guests originally paid $30, so where’s the missing $1?

In today’s story, Rohin and Pranav explain that for most delivery platforms in India, the costs per order looks something like this: Rs 45 for delivery. Rs 10 for customer support. Rs 5 for refunds. Rs 5 for discounts. Rs 5 for others. In total, the sum of all costs per order is Rs 70...or $1. To break even, a delivery platform has to find a way to make $1 per order. That’s the goal.

Swiggy, India’s most valuable food-unicorn, is on a quest to find this dollar. Finding this dollar in food delivery was hard enough, but now Swiggy wants to do it in grocery delivery, where margins are way lower. It sees a way to do that with Instamart, a cloud convenience store modelled on the tens of thousands of 7-Eleven convenience stores that dot cities in Japan, US, and Southeast Asia. Or the hundreds of goPuff cloud stores that dot US cities.

Instamart isn’t Swiggy’s first crack at groceries. But it is Swiggy’s most ambitious and strategic attempt to succeed in a market where the pandemic has wrecked food delivery and restaurants. It’s not going to be easy, and above all, it’s going to place Swiggy in a difficult place with its partner stores—putting them in direct competition.

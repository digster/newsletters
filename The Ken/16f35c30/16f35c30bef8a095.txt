Vicco turmeric, nahi cosmetic

Vicco turmeric ayurvedic cream

If you know the rest of the Vicco Turmeric skin cream jingle https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=U6ay1p-OIcs by-heart, you grew up in India. Though, I must say, India's obsession with turmeric—a staple food ingredient—is probably well-placed.

Of course, like everything ancient and Indian—from yoga to chicken tikka to Jaipur Literature Festival—the West is majorly into Indian spices. No surprise then that the best of India's turmeric is exported.

And Indians end up consuming sub-standard, often adulterated turmeric.

Joy.

Now, DiasporaCo is one such American spice company. It sources its turmeric directly from Indian farmers and sells it in the West for almost 60X the price of turmeric sold in India. It can afford to price itself high, at least in the US, for the quality it promises.

Its founder Sana Javeri Kadri, though, wants to bring this quality turmeric back to India. By mid-2020. Would price-sensitive India let the diaspora sell to the public?

Kadri hopes her focus on quality and the company's fair-treatment policy towards farmers would give her a leg up. 

A typical farmer in India makes anywhere between Rs 60-80 for a kilo of dried turmeric. Grinding and powdering, which leads to around 7% to 10% wastage, brings the cost of making one kilo of turmeric powder up to around Rs 100. But DiasporaCo pays its farmers around Rs 325 a kilo. “We don’t look at the market prices for any of our spices,” admits Kadri. “We sit with our farmers, look at their finances, and come up with a completely new number that is right for them.”

Besides, she's willing to bring the prices down to a bracket better-suited to the Indian market. Still, it'll probably be on a Godrej Nature's Basket shelf rather than at your local grocer.

Kadri is now hoping for a yellow brick road to the turmeric Emerald City—India. Can she realistically make it?

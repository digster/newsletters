Every year, Diwali is accompanied by advertisements for massive discount sales screaming from every screen and hoarding. This year, my biggest purchase turned out to be a PlayStation 4.
I’ve never been what you’d call a gamer, having not played anything beyond the occasional Pokemon game. But there’s one role-playing game (RPG) that has been on my mind since it released last year—The Outerworlds.
The Outerworlds is set in a dystopian future where humans have colonised multiple planets across the galaxy. Each colony is owned by the corporation that bids the highest. It doesn’t take long before you see the correlation drawn between an obsession with profits and the destruction of ecosystems and human lives. And the subliminal messages—the propaganda posters, a ‘profit-hungry’ Board, abandoned colonies—all look eerily similar to the news on our screens.
I’m as new to business journalism as I am to gaming. And if the game set out to be a crash course in dysfunction, I’ve realised The Ken’s stories break down such dysfunction. They offer a different perspective of the world, distilling not only how businesses influence everything but also how everything is connected.

[wrote about](https://the-ken.com/story/druva-an-indian-cybersecurity-solution-with-an-india-problem/) Druva, a 12-year-old cloud data protection startup. With India witnessing a rising number of cyberattacks, its cybersecurity market is expected to be worth about $3 billion by 2023. And though it’s an Indian company—it was started in Pune—it’s nearly unknown in the country. However, India’s upcoming data protection law could give it a shot in the arm.
Seema, on Tuesday, reflected on the diagnostics lessons that India should be learning after 118 million Covid tests. With the prices of Covid tests dropping, she writes that India now needs to devise a long-term strategy that encompasses quality of testing kits, preparation for better assays for when vaccines hit the market, and scope beyond Emergency Use Authorisation.

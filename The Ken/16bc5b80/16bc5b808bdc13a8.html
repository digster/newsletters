SoftBank Standstill Obligation 

 

Remember when startups in India would trip over each other to collect SoftBank money? Give board seats. Give equity. Take sweet cash. Repeat.

 

Last week, that changed:

 

The founder and largest minority investors of Oyo Hotels and Homes have put in a clause in the company’s charter that SoftBank cannot increase its shareholding in the company beyond 50% without both its approvals. 

 

This is an unusual development. OYO and SoftBank don’t have a regular investor-portfolio company relationship. SoftBank has been Oyo’s lead investor for the last five rounds, which has taken the company’s valuation from $100 million to over $5 billion. In the process, SoftBank now owns 46% of OYO. 

 

 

So what’s really going on? It’s hard to tell. But it’s likely one of two things: 

 
  1. Ritesh Agarwal, the CEO of OYO, along with other investors, placed this clause to limit SoftBank’s influence and to maintain some degree of freedom. This is almost exactly what Ola did a couple of years earlier. Perhaps OYO is learning from Ola. 
     
  2. SoftBank wants OYO to find another lead investor. SoftBank rarely takes a majority stake in a company, much less a controlling one. Is this a way to place a limit on itself, so that OYO is forced to find another investor?
 

But then again, SoftBank and OYO are locked in something called a Russian Doll Ponzi. What’s that? You should read the story. 

 

The watchdog finally barks...at Google

 

Does Google abuse the dominant position of Android to block rivals in India? 

 

That’s the question that the CCI (Competition Commission of India) has been investigating. For months, the news came in a steady trickle. First, there was a report that the CCI was looking into a complaint. Then, we gathered that it had ordered an investigation into antitrust. 

 

Last week, the CCI’s initial report was published.

 

It’s brief, but it doesn’t look good for Google. 

 

By making pre-installation of Google’s proprietary apps conditional, Google “reduced the ability and incentive of device manufacturers to develop and sell devices operated on alternate versions of Android”, the CCI said in the order. “It amounts to prima facie leveraging of Google’s dominance”. 

 

This has kicked off a larger investigation. It’s supposed to be finished in 150 days, but you know how these things work. It may take longer. Until then, just read three of our stories. One about how Google abuses its position in India. The other about how we need a Net Neutrality-like law to control Android in India. And finally, the lessons that we can draw from three women across the world. 

 

The CCI is one thing, but what happens when other watchdogs are caught napping? Like, say...credit rating agencies in India?

 

Section 36. Two Investigations. One indefinite leave.

 

After a long investigation into lenders and auditors of IL&FS, last week, the focus finally turned to the credit rating agencies.

 

Let’s recap. There’s a company called Infrastructure Leasing & Financial Services (IL&FS)—a massive “shadow lender” to infrastructure projects across India. A few months back, it defaulted on some payments. Everybody lost their minds when this happened. Mostly because nobody saw this coming. 

 

Why not? Because credit rating agencies told us everything was fine. Until it was too late. Why? Because credit rating agencies get paid by the issuer. So, in this case, IL&FS was paying ICRA and CARE to rate their own bonds. Unsurprisingly, they got the highest possible ratings. Three big things happened last week: 

 
  1. The Enforcement Directorate (ED), which investigates money laundering, summoned the top executives of ICRA and CARE—the rating agencies for IL&FS—for questioning.
  2. The Serious Fraud Investigation Office (SFIO), which investigates...serious fraud, found out that IL&FS’s senior management influenced the rating agencies by threatening to withdraw business. 
  3. ICRA has asked its Managing Director and Chief Executive Officer Naresh Takkar to go on indefinite leave on Monday. The shares of the company plunged by 5%.
 

I recommend reading our excellent story. It’s about the conflict of interest that exists between credit rating agencies and IL&FS, and how it got this far, and what reform looks like. 

 

Samsung and Huawei’s terrible week in India.

 

A few months ago, we published a story titled, India, the middle kingdom for 5G, is battleground zero for Huawei and Samsung. In it, we wrote, 

 

“In this fast shifting market, India is uniquely positioned. It is the only large country which hasn’t officially taken a stand against Huawei. Understandably so. The telecoms market in India is dominated by Chinese goods—from networks equipment to phones and other electronics. While India contributed a teeny 1.2% to Huawei’s $109 billion revenues in 2018, it holds great potential as the second largest telecoms user base in the world after China. Its mobile subscriber base is over 1.1 billion and growing. And, crucially, it imports over 90% of its telecom equipment.”

 

Last week, both parties took casualties:

 
  1. Samsung laid off 1000 employees in India to cut costs thanks to competition from Chinese companies. 150 of these are from the telecom division. 
  2. Huawei got not one, but two signals that its future in India may be dire. 
 

First, Union Minister Ravi Shankar Prasad said in Parliament that Indian entities that supply US-origin technology components to Chinese telecom major Huawei may face penal action under American regulations. 

 

Second, principal scientific adviser K Vijay Raghavan went on the record to state that India should “go for trials immediately with all, except for Chinese vendors”.  

 

But why this fear against Huawei and 5G? It’s complicated. Best is to read our stories. We did one on telecom equipment security, and one on 5G standards. Read. Get caught up. There’s a lot going to happen here. 

 

One complete shut down. One partial shut down.

 

Koinex, one of India’s largest cryptocurrency exchange companies, shut down last week. 

 

"After months of uncertainty and disruption, we have regretfully decided to shut down all digital assets exchange services and operations today," wrote Koinex co-founder Rahul Raj in a blog post. 

 

He cites multiple reasons—unclear regulations, disruption in payment and operations, and denials of service. The company couldn’t even pay salaries to employees without getting calls from a bank to inquire what was going on. 

 

They weren’t the only ones who fell to regulation. The other, in a completely different industry, was Shein. The Chinese e-tailer, after a crackdown by the customs department, has started refunding money to customers and has stopped taking new orders. 

 

Chinese e-commerce imports to India operate in a grey zone. Some, like Club Factory, another Chinese e-tailer have derisked themselves by reducing imports and trying a marketplace model.

 

Both of these situations are nuanced. That’s why you should read our stories. One about how RBI’s move to ban crypto is like a second Demonetisation; the other about Club Factory’s position.

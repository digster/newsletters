Last July we broke one of our own rules when we launched The Ken on Weekends.
“With this email we’re breaking two inviolable rules we’ve followed since the day we started—we don’t publish stories on weekends and we don’t email you on Sundays. They served us well, but, hey, rules are meant to be broken.
I’d like to introduce you to The Ken on Weekends, or #TKOW (pronounced "tea-cow”). One original story every weekend that is distinct from our weekday journalism, and yet subtly cut from the same cloth from which The Ken emerged.”
For us, it was an important experiment. We called it a Beta.
“So, we’re launching as a free beta like we did with The Ken. Over the next few weeks and months, you will see a variety of weekend stories from us. Each distinct in its treatment, subject or point of view. With your valuable feedback, we will iterate and fine-tune our themes and focus areas until we have a product that is ready to exist as our first separate product.”
But over the last 12 months, we’ve realized that the distinction between our weekday and weekend features is much smaller than we’d imagined. SoftBank Standstill Obligation
Remember when startups in India would trip over each other to collect SoftBank money? Give board seats. Give equity. Take sweet cash. Repeat.
Last week, [that changed](https://timesofindia.indiatimes.com/business/india-business/softbank-faces-oyo-riders-for-stake-increase/articleshow/70079754.cms):
The founder and largest minority investors of Oyo Hotels and Homes have put in a clause in the company’s charter that SoftBank cannot increase its shareholding in the company beyond 50% without both its approvals.
This is an unusual development. OYO and SoftBank don’t have a regular investor-portfolio company relationship. SoftBank has been Oyo’s lead investor for the last five rounds, which has taken the company’s valuation from $100 million to over $5 billion. In the process, SoftBank now owns 46% of OYO.
So what’s really going on? It’s hard to tell. But it’s likely one of two things:
- Ritesh Agarwal, the CEO of OYO, along with other investors, placed this clause to limit SoftBank’s influence and to maintain some degree of freedom. This is almost exactly what Ola did a couple of years earlier. Perhaps OYO is learning from Ola.
- SoftBank wants OYO to find another lead investor. SoftBank rarely takes a majority stake in a company, much less a controlling one. Is this a way to place a limit on itself, so that OYO is forced to find another investor?
But then again, SoftBank and OYO are locked in something called a Russian Doll Ponzi. What’s that? The watchdog finally barks...at Google
Does Google abuse the dominant position of Android to block rivals in India?
That’s the question that the CCI (Competition Commission of India) has been investigating. For months, the news came in a steady trickle. First, there was a report that the CCI was [looking into a complaint](https://in.reuters.com/article/google-india-antitrust/exclusive-india-orders-anti-trust-probe-of-google-for-alleged-android-abuse-sources-idINKCN1SH05O). Then, we gathered that it had ordered an investigation into antitrust.
Last week, the CCI’s initial report [was published](https://in.reuters.com/article/google-india-antitrust/exclusive-google-appears-to-have-leveraged-android-dominance-india-antitrust-watchdog-idINKCN1TU0H5).
It’s brief, but it doesn’t look good for Google.
By making pre-installation of Google’s proprietary apps conditional, Google “reduced the ability and incentive of device manufacturers to develop and sell devices operated on alternate versions of Android”, the CCI said in the order. “It amounts to prima facie leveraging of Google’s dominance”.
This has kicked off a larger investigation. It’s supposed to be finished in 150 days, but you know how these things work. It may take longer. Until then, just read three of our stories. Section 36. Two Investigations. One indefinite leave.
After a long investigation into lenders and auditors of IL&FS, last week, the focus finally turned to the credit rating agencies.
Let’s recap. There’s a company called Infrastructure Leasing & Financial Services (IL&FS)—a massive “shadow lender” to infrastructure projects across India. A few months back, it defaulted on some payments. Everybody lost their minds when this happened. Mostly because nobody saw this coming.
Why not? Because credit rating agencies told us everything was fine. Until it was too late. Why? Because credit rating agencies get paid by the issuer. So, in this case, IL&FS was paying ICRA and CARE to rate their own bonds. Unsurprisingly, they got the highest possible ratings. Three big things happened last week:
- The Enforcement Directorate (ED), which investigates money laundering,
[summoned](https://www.business-standard.com/article/companies/ed-summons-chief-rating-officers-of-icra-and-care-in-il-fs-probe-119070201463_1.html) the top executives of ICRA and CARE—the rating agencies for IL&FS—for questioning.
- The Serious Fraud Investigation Office (SFIO), which investigates...serious fraud,
[found out](https://www.moneycontrol.com/news/business/sfio-probe-reveals-ex-ilfs-brass-used-pressure-tactics-for-favourable-ratings-4165701.html) that IL&FS’s senior management influenced the rating agencies by threatening to withdraw business.
- ICRA has asked its Managing Director and Chief Executive Officer Naresh Takkar to
[go on indefinite leave](https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/markets/stocks/news/icra-plunges-5-after-md-naresh-takkar-sent-on-leave/articleshow/70035003.cms) on Monday. The shares of the company plunged by 5%.
I recommend reading [our excellent story](http://link.the-ken.com/wf/click?upn=HQSq6eeRV1xAVaGuFOahuNCZVVXPeP-2FJpSqxhvIUDrkv90I7agfYkeWWP3LdRz3Sw8Rtyt9V0RhUesY0rZglSGtgQhd97oQo5tjPi1TTfug-3D_S4jLOsNx-2Fv6Jev2nvow8b7P9pBCWGd8KNZfb200gkZLQNlXQHio0noZzXtsWoloZmPWeyZsh7a4QCV9ufwCUKi00jlBT0uEdvwYDMuVuqcMPuLzhnKc1Jb4kXF3q2zgBPnSRx7OFMI8zHq7N1U-2Bz-2FS0R76-2F84MQsKoQ0mVRnWqsmkKjrV615Y8MLFXITLdvR7CbBuo-2F8SksgVlLtWf6TzZIHoQI4rls0Kcnc2kV6A1NrTy2QBNkSbVw7g4rpwG4h8-2FZ5FFBc5lpeXlUzvikoYVBOjt5HYNxi1u2soc7L3M99LcVHY-2Fh9SPMs0kYiAQeMSwK44yh7EDSO0oPCogCOCOuEXvOH3Ji4w7L0Ypb-2FCVmNijvUqqASFIPBZ1N-2By18HjC6W9KZr2CkoF2Gq9-2FSUjb3O5F7BQIUPblAtzWpiwGaZvXb1ixy1r2oVHnurcQSl). Koinex, one of India’s largest cryptocurrency exchange companies, shut down last week.
"After months of uncertainty and disruption, we have regretfully decided to shut down all digital assets exchange services and operations today," wrote Koinex co-founder Rahul Raj in a blog post.
He cites multiple reasons—unclear regulations, disruption in payment and operations, and denials of service. The company couldn’t even pay salaries to employees without getting calls from a bank to inquire what was going on.
They weren’t the only ones who fell to regulation. The other, in a completely different industry, was Shein. The Chinese e-tailer, after a crackdown by the customs department, has started refunding money to customers and has stopped taking new orders.
Chinese e-commerce imports to India operate in a grey zone. Some, like Club Factory, another Chinese e-tailer have derisked themselves by reducing imports and trying a marketplace model.

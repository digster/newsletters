The National Stock Exchange of India (NSE) released its financials last week. But it didn’t attract much attention. Its figures for the quarter and year ended March 2023 were lost among the results of listed companies.
The NSE’s cash-equity trades—the conventional buying and selling of stocks—fell 20% in the year ended March 2023. But this is not surprising, given its comparison with the post-Covid bull run of the previous year. Surprisingly, though, this didn’t stop the NSE from posting a 40% rise in its revenue and profits.
So what caused this jump? The answer lies in the crux, or the main course, as they say.
In this case, it’s a business, separate from cash equities, that has become the NSE’s top revenue source in recent years. Even as cash market volumes suffered, the volume for this secret sauce surged over 70% last financial year. As a result, this segment now accounts for four-fifths of the NSE’s transaction revenue, in contrast to less than one-third before the pandemic.
But what’s a story without conflict and a dish without a surprise? The NSE has some, too. For one, the process of becoming an NSE shareholder is long and complex. Then, the BSE, long used to playing second fiddle to the NSE, is again trying to chip away at the latter’s dominance.

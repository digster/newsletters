In this week’s episode of Two by Two, my co-host Praveen Gopal Krishnan and I decided to examine the serious new challenges that have emerged for supermarket chain Dmart.
This was hard to imagine once because no retail business—online or offline—could compete with Dmart’s steep discounts. Or the fact that despite them, its profit margins consistently beat its competitors’. Dmart punches above its weight even in the global arena.
To quote our guests:
Seetharaman: …which is why Dmart is incredible. They don’t just make money, they make a lot of it
Govind: Absolutely.
As subscribers to The Ken might already know, Seetharaman G is our deputy editor and our resident expert on all things retail. Over the years, he’s written extensively about Dmart in his weekly newsletter, [Trade Tricks](https://the-ken.com/trade-tricks/stories/). Our other guest, Govind Shrikhande, is a former managing director of Shoppers Stop and has spent over 40 years in the retail sector, having been part of the launches of Denim and Arrow, the relaunch of Vivaldi, and the turnaround of Shoppers Stop itself.
Unsurprisingly, given its numbers and history, the public markets have placed a lot of confidence in Dmart stock.
But that is changing.
For Dmart, there are finally challenges rising over the horizon.
When quick commerce [first burst on to](https://the-ken.com/the-nutgraf/the-surprising-resilience-of-10-min-grocery-deliveries/) the scene, most people expected local kirana stores (small neighbourhood stores) to be hurt the most. But it’s now becoming clear that quick commerce might be coming for Dmart’s prized value-customers too, just not in the same way Dmart won them over.
And quick commerce isn’t the only threat either.

One of the main focuses of our discussion was how Dmart seems to have slowed down its expansion plans. It has supermarkets in 381 locations across India, but its business model calls for owning the land which its super markets are built on—something that’s probably getting tougher to pull off.
Dmart also uses what’s called the cluster approach for expanding into new locations.
Seetharaman: Its first store was in Powai in Mumbai, then it expanded into the rest of Maharashtra, then Gujarat. So these are its two largest states. If you look at its footprint, it’s in the west of the country and the south. They have a few stores in the north, but just in NCR. They don’t have a single store in any of the eastern states, nor do they have anything in Uttar Pradesh or Bihar, two of the largest states in India.
They look at scale economies. They want to keep adding more and more stores, and capture more and more of the demand within a particular region, which also helps with supply chain efficiencies.
So most of Dmart’s stores are located in tier-1 cities. And do you know who else is targeting the same spaces? The 10-minute-delivery companies.
Dmart does have a ‘quick-enough-commerce’ vertical, which it started long before the pandemic. Chances are, most of us have not heard of it, let alone used it. So what does Dmart Ready really offer?
The exact question I posed to everyone in the discussion was:
What is the purpose of Dmart Ready? Is it to defend Dmart’s existing customers? To say, “look, there may be some of our customers who want to consider delivery. Better we offer them delivery rather than risk them being poached”?
Or is it to win new customers?
And without missing a beat, both Seetharaman and Govind replied: “it’s only to defend”.
Govind explained why.
Most legacy retailers have always believed (and even now, it is proven) that online retail is not profitable at all. This is because of the returns, the low ticket sizes, and multiple other issues.
And even today, there is Primark—one of the biggest fashion value brands in the world—which does not do online at all. It is available online only to book a slot in the store.
[…]
So they have proven that without online, you can really do business.
Zudio has proven it in India. They are not online at all, and they don’t offer online stocking. They don’t do online delivery. Nothing at all.
[…]
The problem is, we sometimes believe that, “look, we are not into the valuation game, we are into the customer game.” And then we try and tell ourselves, “look, let’s do the better service.” And that’s where the legacy retailer is falling behind in adopting online and really driving it hard.
That’s part one.
Obviously, it’s a very risky game, because when you are looking online and burning a lot of money, it’s a big risk. So do you want to destroy what you’re earning right now, or are you looking at defending?
And that’s what Dmart Ready is.
This is just a very small part of what we discussed, so do [tune into the podcast](https://the-ken.com/podcasts/two-by-two/dmart-versus-the-challengers-at-the-gate/) if you’re interested in hearing the rest. But as we turned towards our closing arguments, I asked everyone what they thought Dmart’s position would be in the future.
Praveen proposed one hypothesis—that Dmart might believe this is a cycle, so it’s taking a pause to preserve capital and is waiting to rebound once things turn around.
Most of us agreed that Dmart wouldn’t be able to make much headway in the quick-commerce space. But nobody was fully willing to write it off given the kind of business it has built over time, and the kind of customer it serves. The typical Dmart customer is all about the discounts, after all.

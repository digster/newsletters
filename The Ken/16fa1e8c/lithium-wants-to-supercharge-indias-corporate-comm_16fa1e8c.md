Are you reading this on your way to work? If so, are you in a car? Is it your car, or are you car-pooling, or is it a cab? An office cab, by any chance? Is it an electric cab?
If so, chances are you are in a cab run by Lithium.
Lithium Urban Technology is the country's first fleet of all-electric cabs. Some 1,100 of them, all Mahindra and Mahindra cars, and all used to transport employees to and from work.
On the face of it, it seems like a great startup idea.
Market? Check. A $4 billion one of which 95% is unorganised.
Costs? Check. The cost of running an EV car is about 1/4th that of a diesel car; companies save 10-15% on their transport bills.
Predictability? Check. Office timings and pick up and drop points are known.
Scalability? Umm ...
That's where Lithium has struggled since it started in 2015. In five years, it is in only five cities—Bengaluru, Delhi, Pune, Hyderabad and Manipal—and has just over 1,100 cars, a sixth of what it had in mind.
For one, India's much-vaunted drive to boost EV manufacturing and adoption is idling in neutral. And if one manages to buy an EV car, the monthly financing costs, when added to the running costs, suddenly doesn't make EVs look as appealing. Even if a cab is run 24 hours a day!
Then there is the perennial problem of a lack of enough charging points, which only feeds into the "range anxiety" issue. On top of that, Lithium's competitors are also experimenting in the space.
All said and done, Lithium seems to be a company ahead of its time. But can it get ahead now? Can it click into a higher gear (I know, gears and EVs)

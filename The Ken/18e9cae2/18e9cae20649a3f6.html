Good Morning Ishan,

If what doesn’t kill you makes you stronger, India’s 12-million plus kiranas have gone from strength to strength over the years.

These mom-and-pop stores spread across the nooks and crannies of the country have proved wrong the prophets of doom several times. Over the years, they have fended off many seemingly existential threats—from shiny supermarkets such as Big Bazaar to e-commerce giants such as Amazon—and lived to tell the tale. In the recent past though, a feisty, new deep-pocketed challenger has been growing bigger, stronger, and dangerous by the day.

Quick-commerce players such as Zepto, Blinkit, and Swiggy Instamart are delivering what was considered the secret sauce and sole preserve of the kiranas—the convenience of quick delivery at the customer’s doorstep. Besides, many of these players offer something that kiranas do not or rather cannot give—discounts.

Many customers, at least in big cities, are shifting loyalties, and several kiranas are feeling the heat. A recent survey of 900 kiranas showed that many mom-and-pop stores have seen sales dip 10–30%, courtesy of quick commerce.

Much is at stake. India’s US$600 billion grocery market is dominated by kiranas, and they have a lot to lose if the quick commerce juggernaut rolls on.

But the size of the fight in this dog should never be underestimated. Many kiranas are taking the war right into the enemy camp, deploying unique strategies and tactics they have up their sleeves.

“The news of kiranas’ impending death is greatly exaggerated,” quips an industry player. But this game is well and truly on and could turn either way, writes Aakriti in today’s deeply reported story that also saw many of The Ken’s reporters reach out to their neighbourhood kiranas. Read it here.

April fool, cool, tool

Ok, confession time. I almost fell for an April fool’s day prank yesterday. But how I wish it was not a joke.

‘Finally! MRF announces a 10:1 split of shares and declares a special dividend of Rs 776 per share’, a message on a Whatsapp group let me know. My eyes lit up. This long-awaited big move by India’s leading tyre maker would, at last, make the stock affordable for retail investors (like me), I thought—by cutting its price from (believe it or faint) Rs 1,35,000 a piece to Rs 13,500.

It also quickly took me back to the story we did last June—Why MRF, Nestle, and other ‘pricey’ stocks are happy being exceptions—when the MRF stock crossed the Rs 1 lakh mark, a first in India.

Alas, the dream crashed in quick time, and I found myself smiling sheepishly when the hoax was revealed.

Anyway, I do think that affordability for retail investors is something that all listed stocks should give due consideration to. That would be really cool.

It would also be cool for retail investors to get going with their investments at the start of the new fiscal year—from April itself, and not wait until March next year. Old jungle adage: the early bird gets more worms.

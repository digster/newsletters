Does your country idolize a particular profession? Perhaps engineering, law, performing arts, medicine, or teaching?
Here, in the Philippines, we used to idolize nursing. Yes, I used the past tense.
When I was in school, all the adults wanted to become nurses. Their scrubs were a badge of honour; it meant you were educated, charming, and well-off. Hospitals worldwide coveted Philippine nurses, so becoming a nurse was a way to make a living in dollars while being held in high esteem.
Nursing was so attractive that in the 2000s, it led to a culture of ‘volunteerism’ alongside an oversupply of some 400,000 nurses.
Some 15 years later, the Philippines is struggling with a shortage of 106,000 nurses. How did that happen?
Katrina’s story reveals the bleak truth: Philippine nurses are the lowest-paid ones in Southeast Asia. While the standard nurse-to-patient ratio is supposed to be 1:12, most Filipino nurses have to attend to a lot more, sometimes up to 60 patients at a time.
Despite having 935,000 registered nurses, the Philippines cannot keep them from leaving the profession or the country.
How did the Philippines go from exporting nurses to closing its hospital units? Is there even a way forward for the once-esteemed industry?

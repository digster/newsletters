Earlier this year, Amitabh Kant, the uber-czar of policymaking in India, lent his stamp of approval to the “gig economy” when he touted the 2 million-odd jobs created by people becoming Uber and Ola drivers since 2014. There’s also the Swiggy delivery executives, the Dunzo riders, homeowners who’ve listed on Airbnb. And many others on digital platforms like Upwork, TapChief, Fiverr, and Freelancer. A recent PayPal report estimated that 1 in every 4 gig workers globally is Indian.
 
The explosion of smartphones and cheap internet connections have supercharged the gig economy, which is the subject of today’s story. For a change, it’s not written by one of our writers, but by Sampad Swain, the CEO of a company that powers payments for gig workers.
 
Cab drivers and food delivery riders aren’t the only gig workers. HR and soft skills training happen to be some of the most popular gig careers in the country, while jobs like graphic and web design, app development, photography, and content writing follow close behind. This even extends to top-ranking graduates selling education expertise on WhatsApp study groups.

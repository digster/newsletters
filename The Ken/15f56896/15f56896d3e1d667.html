Never have big technology companies wielded so much power; never have they fielded such close public scrutiny. The love-hate relationship of big tech and big government today is unprecedented.
 
A new legislation is under consideration in the US—Honest Ads Act—which will require Google, Facebook, Twitter and other such internet companies to keep public databases of who is paying for political ads on their networks. Yesterday, a lobbying group representing top artificial intelligence companies, including Facebook, Amazon, and Google issued a warning to the American lawmakers: hands off of our algorithms.
 
In such times, one is right in thinking that public policy’s role in a tech company is no less than that of the business head. If it’s Facebook India, then maybe it’s more. That’s what the current view is, with respect to the recent exit and entry of senior officials in the company:
 
“Facebook’s perception in India is that of a policy-first company. Look at this way: policy-first, sales-next and product third.”
 
Which is why it’s believed Facebook’s most prominent person in India is not its managing director but its chief of public policy, Ankhi Das. And she’s just made an important hire in Shivnath Thukral, who’s stepped down as managing director of Carnegie India to take the position. Thukral is a former journalist and corporate affairs and communications professional.
 
All this makes a lot of sense given Facebook’s new-found dedication to work with governments, particularly in countries where elections are due in 2019—India, Italy and Canada.
 
Venkat has earlier written on Facebook’s increasing association with the government. Continuing that thread, today he tells us how Das, with an astute understanding of Indian politics, is positioning Facebook in India.

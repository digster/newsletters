The laws were a windfall for the likes of Origo Commodities, a 10-year-old agri procurement, warehousing, and financing company. But farmers had other plans. For weeks, thousands of them have protested in the national capital, asking for the laws to be scrapped.В 

Origo still wants to reshape how agri commodities are sourced, but without quite upsetting the applecart. The company is pitching securitisation as a cheaper alternative to loans for buyers of produce. Lenders packaging home and vehicle loans as an investment product and selling it to institutions is hardly new; India's securitisation market is worth $27 billion.

But Origo is the first to do this with agri commodities in India. In October, it joined hands with financial services firm Vivriti Capital to securitise $640,000 of maize.В 

As Shreedhar writes in today's story, the potential is undoubtedly huge, but so are the challenges. Securitisation at scale will not be possible without investors like mutual funds.

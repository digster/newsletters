Good Morning Ishan,
 
I've just about gotten rid of a bad habit I picked up during the pandemic: shopping on Instagram.
 
If you use the social media platform, you'd know that Instagram's ad algorithm is incredible! It knows what you're likely to buy and pushes those products and brands—most D2C—on your feed. I spent a significant amount of money buying stuff from D2C brands off Instagram during lockdown times—most of which I could have done without.
 
I don't have this habit anymore because, for some reason, I'm barely on Instagram these days. But when I read Aayush's story this week, I realised that I'm probably a minority.
 
Aayush wrote about how logistics companies like Delhivery, Xpressbees, and Shiprocket are sprinting to enable same- and next-day delivery for D2C brands. Imagine buying a pair of shoes or that pop-culture merchandise that you don't really need and then they deliver it to you within a day. If this was there during the lockdowns, I would probably have exhausted my salary in a week! But instant-ish delivery is a huge business now in the e-commerce logistics space, although not an easy one for the companies to crack.
 
Over in Indonesia, there's a different trend being witnessed in the D2C e-commerce industry. E-commerce enablers or SaaS companies like Shopify and Lummo went crazy during the pandemic onboarding merchants, mostly SMEs, that were desperate to have a digital storefront. But while the e-commerce SaaS platforms were able to help digitise millions of merchants in the region, they could not guarantee growth in sales. And now that the world has all but reopened, it's a big problem.
 
Maitri and Gaurav had a very interesting story about Ultrahuman, a Bengaluru-based fitness wearable startup, and its new continuous glucose monitor (CGM). It was worn by cricketer Shreyas Iyer during the Indian Premier League earlier this year, among other influencers. Such a device is generally used by diabetics to monitor their sugar levels, but it's now finding use in the preventive fitness space. Better-established and deeper-pocketed rivals such as sugar.fit and HealthifyMe have also dipped their toes into the business.
 
Olina wrote about how a patchwork effort by Indian philanthropies wants to make edtech solutions for students affordable, accessible and, most importantly, accountable. But with a funding winter engulfing the sector, and a complex government system to navigate, scaling a public-private partnership is no easy feat.
 
Shashwat dived deep into online travel aggregator EaseMyTrip. Bootstrapped and profitable, it has quickly become the second largest player in the sector—behind only MakeMyTrip, which has a ~52% market share. However, EaseMyTrip has reached here using a mix of organic and not-so-kosher tactics, which have worried competitors.
 
That's all from us this week. Have a great Sunday! And let me know what unhealthy pandemic habit you've managed to drop.

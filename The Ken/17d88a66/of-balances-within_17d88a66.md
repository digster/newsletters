This is the first time I’m writing to you since joining The Ken as a journalist. And believe me or not, eight weeks ago, my total experience in a field that tells soul-stirring stories to curious readers like yourself would have been a big fat zero.
It’s a whole new world. Of brand new information and its share of unknowns. A few minor bumps here and there. With time though, as I have settled, a neat balance has started to emerge in my day.
And now, three stories in, as I write this recap email, I am reminded, through these wonderfully reported stories and newsletters, why this uncharted territory feels like home. It’s been an exciting week at The Ken, and I can’t wait to share it with you.
Our week began and ended with two stories on the same subject, on two conglomerates in the same space, with fingers in one common pie.
On Monday, Seetharaman zoomed in on the Tata Group’s electric vehicle (EV) ambitions from the lens of its charging unit,
[Tata Power](https://the-ken.com/story/tata-power-ev-charging/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter). The conglomerate has a little thing going on when it comes to EV—Tata Motors rolls out the cars, Tata Chemicals makes the lithium-ion cells, and Tata AutoComp assembles the battery packs. Tying all this together is Tata Power, which has grabbed up to a 40-50% market share across the public, fleet, and home charging segments in India. About its unit economics, though, don't ask.
By now, you know it as I do—where there is a Tata, there is a Reliance. On Friday, Shruti and Olina team up to unravel a rather unusual team-up orchestrated by a two-year-old EV ride-hailing startup
[BluSmart](https://the-ken.com/story/tata-jio-bp-supercharge-blusmart/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter). The company has a partnership with Tata Motors for the EVs and one with Reliance Jio and UK-based mobility investment firm BP Ventures to charge them using super-hubs. Surely this can't go on for long, can it?
On Tuesday, Munsif took a peek behind
[Apple India](https://the-ken.com/story/apple-indias-four-year-struggle-to-make-services-work/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter)’s curtains to tell the tale of its struggle to keep its flock together. Apple India’s star-studded senior leadership was tasked with building up its services business in the country. But a blanket global strategy, attrition among leadership, and a focus on revenue over volume hobbled its plans.
On Wednesday, I explored the three-way balancing act at home services startup
[Urban Company](https://the-ken.com/story/urban-cos-three-way-balancing-act-in-search-of-profitability/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter). The company artfully dealt with the protests by its beautician partners. But UC now has a bigger challenge—identify where the balance lies between decent wages for its partners and its bottom line.
While things may have settled down at UC for now, the same can not be said for VC firm Sequoia India, wrote Maitri and Seema on Thursday. The value of their investment in 13-year-old healthtech poster child Practo has halved in the last five years. Sequoia, though, has a plan fraught with risk—it involves its new darling child, surgery expert
[Pristyn Care](https://the-ken.com/story/sequoia-hitches-practos-slowing-cart-to-pristyns-runaway-growth-train/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter), taking over its senior of 10 years. Who would have thought!
The world of business is full of such unusual stories.

Long and Short
Wed, 26 Nov 25
The story behind the numbers and motivations driving India's stock market.
Good Morning Ishan,
A month ago, Keshav and I wrote about the strange story behind RRP Semiconductor’s 900X stock jump and India’s chip fever.
Its summary reveals some of the company’s many ironies.
There’s a lot more intrigue from where that came. A small-volume-big-price-spike mystery. A whodunit. Here’s the full [story](https://the-ken.com/story/the-strange-story-behind-rrps-900x-stock-jump-and-indias-chip-fever/).
Now, more strange things have happened over the past few weeks.
Here’s a list–it would be comic if it weren’t so alarming. The most serious of these are the misses by the BSE, the country’s second-largest stock exchange on which the RRP Semiconductor stock trades.
In its September quarter [results](https://www.bseindia.com/xml-data/corpfiling/AttachHis/116d86bb-1654-426f-a8a6-e6aed2273d6f.pdf), RRP Semiconductor declared something truly rare: negative revenue of Rs 7 crore. The company attributed this to a reversal of revenue it had recognised earlier. This reversal–due to a dispute with a client over solar panel installations (not semiconductor deals)–means that the company moved from marginally positive revenue until the March quarter, to zero revenue in the June quarter, to negative revenue in the September quarter. Yet, the stock commands a market capitalisation of over Rs 15,000 crore.
Speaking of which, the RRP Semiconductor stock continued to surge even after the BSE first flagged the unusual price surge on 20 October and put the stock on enhanced surveillance measures. It rose every single trading day by 2%, climbing from an already astronomical Rs 9,292 to Rs 11,784 on 7 November. On that day, the BSE imposed tighter trading restrictions, in effect allowing the stock to move just 1% every week.
Ideally, the BSE should have stepped in far earlier, as The Ken wrote in October.
Yet this fledgling firm, with erratic earnings and no clear business, commands a market capitalisation of nearly Rs 13,000 crore and a price-to-earnings ratio north of 1,900X. The fact that this failed to raise alarms at BSE or Sebi sooner is remarkable.
That it took more than a fortnight after its own warning to tighten the squeeze is even more remarkable. But then, the stock market and its players are far from ideal.
This raises the question: what is the ideal price for RRP Semiconductor? Well, the company thinks it should be trading at Rs 20. Here’s what it said to the exchange on 14 November.
“The Board would like to (state) that the company is taking appropriate steps and request to regulator and BSE to adjust the trading price of the company to removing the artificial move of the share price around Rs 20/- to Rs 11,666/- and place the trading price of Rs 20/- or such other price as regulator fix and appropriate for the company;”
Now, with a 1% downmove allowed by the BSE each week, the RRP Semiconductor stock will take more than 12 years to reach Rs 20 apiece, even though its run-up to these heights took just a year and a half. Should the exchange and the regulator make exceptions for such egregious cases? A dozen years is a long time.
Interestingly, RRP Semiconductor has seemingly been intimating the BSE about the price spike for a long time and is now asking why the exchange didn’t act sooner.
In its responses to the BSE’s queries on 28 October and 7 November about the stock price movement, the company included the following points:
“…on 3 April, 2025 the company has informed the BSE (surveillance department) about the price movement. Therefore, we are not sure what action the BSE has taken…”
“Our earlier intimation dated 14/08/2025 relating to price movements where in the Stock exchange the trading price was Rs. 3,888/- and despite the said fact what action Stock exchange has taken in this regard to control the price movements.”
The BSE and its surveillance department clearly have much to answer for.
Meanwhile, RRP Semiconductor’s own recent announcements are also head-scratchers.
At the instance of Rajendra Chodankar, the group’s de facto head and guiding hand, RRP Semiconductor has proposed to withdraw the preferential allotment of equity shares to promoters and non-promoters, including Chodankar. It has also proposed to remove “RRP” from its name, stating that it “is being abused by people not connected with the company or business of the same”. If these proposals–on hold for now–go through, RRP Semiconductor will be stripped of its name and money and will effectively wind down.
Not that it has much to show in any case. By its own admission earlier this month, the company has not begun any semiconductor manufacturing activities, applied for any government policy, or partnered with any celebrity (cricketer Sachin Tendulkar).
The facade is unravelling, fast.
Not just the company, the BSE will be left squirming too. And the NSE, the country’s largest stock exchange–on which RRP Semiconductor is not listed–will have to disguise its schadenfreude. For a change, and unlike [before](https://the-ken.com/long_and_short/did-nse-sleep-at-the-wheel-in-the-jane-street-saga/), its bitter rival is at the receiving end this time.

Good Morning Ishan,

Move over techies, finance folks are having a moment.

Even when the last few years brought tremendous growth in business and employee base, CFOs were never a priority at mid-to-large startups. Unicorn Physics Wallah was founded eight years ago but doesn’t have a CFO yet. Upgrad appointed one this month.

Recruitment firms say they are getting nearly 3X more demand for CFOs. One of them, Korn Ferry, has even brought in a partner to exclusively hunt for CFOs.

Fintechs, SaaS, and D2C firms are offering annual compensations in the Rs 2.5–4.5 crore (US$300,900–541,000) range, which is 2.5–5X of what these positions earned until early 2023.

Finance professionals, while enjoying the attention, are also grappling with responsibilities that a CA and a CFA training never prepared them for. “We are the new sales people,” said a finance professional working in the business finance team for Ola Electric.

There’s a lot at play here. Much more than companies realising that a good narrative also needs a strong spreadsheet. Vanita gets to the bottom of this shift where risk-averse CFOs are in the driving seat at risk-loving startups.

Nostalgia is the flavour of the week

I read a headline yesterday: “Nostalgia can sometimes pit you as a fuddy-duddy brand”. This is the CEO of MTR Foods, Sunay Bhasin, talking about sustaining the 100 years old brand. One of the ways it’s catering to GenZ is by, well, catering to their “heightened need for convenience”.

MTR says it’s preserved its taste and quality and innovated on other things.

What role does nostalgia play when a brand is brought from the dead?

Kinetic Engineering has resurrected its moped brand Luna as an electric two-wheeler via a subsidiary called Kinetic Green. Since its launch in February, claims the Firodia Group, 5000 units have been sold and the company is looking to sell 20X more in the coming fiscal.

The group is bringing back e-Luna after a 24-year hiatus. And it may have timed it right for the tier-2/3 markets where people are looking for EVs but for much shorter commutes.

When the 75+ year old Bajaj Auto resurrected its Chetak brand for e-mobility in 2020, the scooter was coming back after a 15-year production break. Many thought it was a gamble because the new-age e-scooters were all the rage. In 2023, Bajaj Chetak tripled its market share to 10%.

Other things remaining current, riding on nostalgia may not be fuddy duddy after all.

Many CAs focus so much only on tax compliance and regulations. For people who need some basic business finance consulting, CAs are very reluctant.

Sometimes, CAs do not want to take steps to implement new rules as they always think that is risky. CAs have all the required knowledge to change them to act as advisors. More than tax, there are so many areas they can advise to a business manager. In fact, they have the power to lift many MSMEs, who really do not have access to financial management expertise. They fail to capitalize on these opportunities. They focus on signing and certifying and getting their fees. The knowledge they could apply is not used for the growth of the organizations they serve.

Rayudu A Commented

This is an interesting read. Very well written by the author, Nuha Bubere. One way I can think for the fashion e-commerce players is to charge an extra fee at the payment page while checking out their order before going to the payment processing page. This can act as a safety net for the seller and also keeps the customer confident about purchase. The additional fee can offer them longer return window and these customers can be prioritized for solving the issues during their product-return journey.

Under a scenario where the customer doesn’t make the return, the e-commerce platforms can return that money back in the form of credit points (like Zomato Credit Points). Infact, Myntra’s SuperCoins can be used to return the amount, that will only incentivize the customers to buy again from the e-commerce.

Second way is that, similar to Swiggy One’s Membership, the fashion e-commerce platforms can charge a monthly/quarterly/annual fees (similar to Amazon in some sense) bundling the return policies into the plans. I am aware that the above suggestions do come with consequences. However, by doing the precise balance of the mix of offerings in the membership plans, it will be a win-win for everyone.

PS: More than happy to listen to the other side of the argument if anyone feels the other way around.

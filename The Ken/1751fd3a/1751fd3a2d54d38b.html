In the Hall of Fame of daft policy announcements made by global internet giants, Google's 29 September announcement took the 2020 Gold Medal. Rival Apple was drawing fire from companies like Facebook and Spotify and Epic Games over its App Store fees. Google could've quietly stayed out of the spotlight and waited for things to cool down.
 
Instead, it announced that all app developers would need to use its in-app payment options no later than September 2021. That meant they would also need to pay Google 30% of all such payments.
 
I don't know how many levels of lawyers and communications/PR/policy employees and advisors this announcement ran through before it was made public, but I'd assume quite a few. In India, this announcement followed closely on the heels of Google's decision to briefly ban digital payment giant Paytm's app because it used scratch cards and cash backs that were akin to "gambling".
 
In sort of a reverse order, Google's fight with Paytm had provided the spark that would later ignite the kindling from its decision to demand 30% from all in-app payments in India. The two would combine to quickly create an anti-Google movement among dozens of Indian startups and founders.
 
"India needs a local app store long term else 30% tax will eat up most businesses, is anyone trying to build one?" said payments unicorn Razorpay's co-founder on Twitter.
 
"30% GateKeeping fees. Startups and consumers have very little choice," said the former head of digital media for India's ruling party, the BJP, adding #DigitalMonopolies to his tweet for good measure.
 
In a very short time, over 150 Indian startups moved past their considerable differences in egos and ambitions to unite against Google.
 
Shreedhar and Bhumika analyse why they did so. And what they want. While most say that 30% is too much for a startup to pay to a "gatekeeper" like Google, they also seem to value the quality and reliability of its Android Play Store. Most don't seem to want a government-run App Store.
 
But what do they want?

Good Morning Ishan,

What do you think coffee snobs discuss when they meet?

  • Why you can’t settle for any gooseneck kettle.
  • How it takes real skill to use a Moka pot.
  • The last time they were forced to drink a Starbucks coffee, and how traumatic that was.

But the ones hating on Starbucks miss the point of the global chain.  Most people who walk into a Starbucks outlet in India don’t make anything other than instant coffee at home, if at all. And to some, it’s a reliable meeting place that just happens to sell coffee.

Small wonder, then, that Starbucks is the first café people think of in most Indian cities. Over a decade after setting up shop in the country, Starbucks has 350 outlets.

After the pandemic, it looked like Starbucks would have no competition. Café Coffee Day, once India’s pre-eminent coffee chain, was debt-ridden and rudderless after the death of its founder. Costa Coffee, on the other hand, was just chugging along, despite entering India years before Starbucks.

And the new-age coffee chains, Third Wave Coffee and Blue Tokai Coffee, were happy serving the discerning drinker.

That has changed lately, though.

A year ago, there was one Third Wave outlet for every four of Starbucks. Now there’s one for every three.

Third Wave is anything but the niche brand it used to be, what with a $35 million fundraise in September. In other words, Third Wave is no longer just a Bengaluru phenomenon.

As Nuha writes in her wonderful story today, Third Wave’s transition is as much to do with keeping a close watch on Starbucks as about making the most of Café Coffee Day’s decline: https://the-ken.com/story/third-wave-coffee-is-bad-news-for-starbucks/ (8-min read)

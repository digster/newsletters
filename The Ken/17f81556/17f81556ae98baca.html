Good Morning Ishan,
 
I don’t know about you, but at some point in the future, I want to start my own business. (Or get my hands on one.)
 
Everything about it sounds challenging, but only one thing makes me absolutely crap-my-pants terrified—the prospect of dealing with investors.
 
And I don’t mean investors as people. I’m sure most of them are wonderful souls (and institutions). But being the source of conditional capital comes with certain expectations, and it’s meeting those expectations I find so daunting.
 
Yes, I know you don’t have to go to investors; you could always bootstrap your startup. But the last time I looked, there were no silver spoons in my kitchen drawers, and building even a reasonably-sized corporate empire usually takes a lot of silver spoons. If not at the beginning, then certainly to scale.
 
Which brings us back to investors, who come in all types and sizes and want and need different things at different times in different stages of a company’s lifetime. And every story in our roster last week, though their primary focus may have been on different aspects, reminds me of just how difficult it is to satisfy those ever-changing needs.
 
Making profits will always get you gold stars. But sometimes, scale can be even better. And having to balance that and a million other things makes for a bit of a wild ride.
 
Take Seema’s story on Apollo Hospitals. In 2019, the healthcare giant demerged its front-end multi-brand pharmacy operations while retaining the far more lucrative back-end business. The move made little economic sense then, but it would help the parent firm access foreign direct investment, which India does not allow in multi-brand retail. Less than three years later, Apollo has brought its front-end, back-end, and digital businesses under the same umbrella in Apollo HealthCo—also because it will help it access more capital. (That doesn’t look like it’ll be easy, by the way, and you’ll find out why when you read the story.)
 
Then, there’s Seetharaman’s piece on Reliance Retail. Attracting investors isn’t really the problem here for the new crown jewel of the Reliance group—it’s already one of the hottest stocks on the unlisted market. But with very high margins in segments that analysts find hard to explain and cherry-picked releases of data, Reliance Retail may have to dance to a very different tune when it faces retail investors on the public market—à la post-IPO Zomato. ICYMI, transparency is also on the ‘keep investors happy’ cheat sheet.
 
On Wednesday, Bhumika delivered an in-depth breakdown of what Swiggy’s acquisition of discovery and reservation business Dineout means. Restaurant reservations form a mammoth $17 billion market, and the acquisition will help Swiggy compete better with Zomato for the all-rounder crown. But, there’s also this little nugget—“While the Dineout acquisition adds meat to Swiggy’s bone for its planned initial public offering, it also muddies its proposition for its investors, bringing it into competition with a whole assortment of players”.
 
Soumyajit’s story is also about an acquisition: social media platform Sharechat taking over Times Group’s short video platform TakaTak. Merging TataTak with its own short video creation Moj will make Sharechat the biggest Indian player in the sector. But unlike the previous case, this is expected to strengthen Sharechat’s pitch to its investors. Sharechat does have to start making money, though, and the path to monetisation is going to be long and hard.
 
And in our final story of the week, Aayush wrote about MyGate. India’s largest security and community management company is wading into uncharted waters in search of revenue (who knew RWAs would be tight-fisted?). The lack of it has been hurting on the investor front, with sources saying the company has been facing difficulties in raising capital. MyGate is now dabbling in everything from real estate listings and brand engagement to community buying and event management, but it’s a big mountain to climb.
 
So there you have it. What all this looks like to me is a constant, never-ending tightrope walk in the middle of a hurricane. And one I know I’m not ready to brave yet. For those of you who are/have, kudos.

Dear Ishan, how are you? I hope you had an interesting January. I certainly did.
I worked out diligently the first three weeks, only to fall sick in the fourth. Half of Bengaluru seems to be down with a sore throat or cough or flu.
Over the years, I think I’ve figured out how to make my flus or viral infections more tolerable. Betadine medicated gargles at the first symptom of a sore throat (most, perhaps all my infections start there) and then lots of warm water and steam inhalation over the next three–four days (if a sore throat is where my infections start, stuffed sinuses is where they end, partly due to assistance from a deviated septum!).
My colleague Ruhi Kandhari, who leads our narrative storytelling efforts, added a new element to this mix a few years ago: Lemsip. It’s basically 1,000 mg of paracetamol and phenylephrine. As someone who hates self-medicating (I don’t think I’ve taken antibiotics in nearly a decade), I’ve made an exception for Lemsip because it just makes the first few days tolerable.
Some of us, perhaps Indians specifically, have this unstated belief that colds and flus are meant to be miserable. That they should be. Thanks, but no.
I also travelled to Mumbai and spent a day with a bunch of wonderful folks, including a few subscribers, talking about narrative storytelling and mental models. I love the warmth and connections in such settings. It makes what we do at The Ken so much more real every now and then.
I’m writing this at a Blue Tokai café in Indiranagar, waiting for my teenager to finish his guitar lessons. En route to his weekend class, we listened to a Guitar Greats playlist in the car. I remember when he was young enough that I’d tell him about the bands and artists and their idiosyncrasies and skills. This time, it was clear he is more knowledgeable. He was telling me which guitar solos “just sound complex and are played to sound cool, but are really easy to play”, or which guitarist between Stevie Ray Vaughn and Eric Clapton influenced the other.
There are few times in life when you feel silently proud about knowing less. This was one of them.
I’m also happy to report that[ Bribo](https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/bribo/) is doing quite well. I mean, as well as he can given how serious the injury to his rear leg was. Some bits of his “personality” have changed. For instance, he is less active, because running is harder. He has assumed a sort of tenuous and benevolent leadership over his siblings. They swarm over any food I make the mistake of putting on the ground (really, they sniff through a full bowl in 10 seconds!) and Bribo just stands back, saying, “I can’t deal with this!”. So, I have to take two bowls these days—one for him, and one for the rapacious puppies.
Summer is nearly here in Bengaluru, and soon in other cities. The days are already quite warm, and soon they will be hot. While most of us think about where animals and birds in the open get their food from, we don’t really think about where they get their water from.
It’s much harder for them to find water than it is food.[ Water for Voiceless](https://waterforvoiceless.org/) is one of the longest running initiatives to address this. Support them, or consider placing bowls near your home and filling them with water daily through the summer.

I’m restarting the First Principles podcast, after[ ending it](https://the-ken.com/podcasts/first-principles/last-principles/) last June. Here’s what I had said back then:
“Hi. I have some news for you which is too important to bury for later. We’re bringing the First Principles podcast to a stop. Sorry, I’m bringing the First Principles podcast to a stop.
No, this is not a stunt. And yes, I mean exactly that. We will not be publishing any more episodes of First Principles.
*Sigh*
That was hard. Harder than I want to let on.
We’re just over a month away from celebrating our second anniversary. I still remember the very first episode that I’d recorded in August 2022, with Kabeer Biswas, the co-founder and CEO of Dunzo.
Like most editorial bets at The Ken, we did not have a 100% fully-formed idea of the podcast when we launched. Broadly, I knew that there was a space for deep, unhurried, authentic and most importantly—candid—conversations with some of India’s best-known founders and leaders.
Conversations, not interviews.
Over time, our format evolved as listeners started to send in feedback and as I started understanding what topics founders were willing to reflect upon on air.
Great conversations can only exist at the intersection of those two—listener and guest interest.
We interviewed a total of 41 founders over the two “seasons” of First Principles. The sectors we covered were diverse. E-commerce, electric mobility, fintech, agritech, edtech, beauty, real estate, hospitality, healthcare, online insurance, online stock broking. That’s not even an exhaustive list.
As the length of our conversations ballooned to two hours, we went from being a fortnightly to a weekly podcast by splitting those conversations into two.
On Apple Podcasts, we’ve consistently been ranked between #3 and #5 on the Business charts.
The First Principles podcast inspired the First Principles Sunday newsletter, which I started writing last May. Over time it “evolved” independently of its “parent” and is today a distinct and eclectic newsletter on its own, with a huge and vibrant global community of readers and contributors. Though the DNA is common—first principles thinking.
And I haven’t told you this yet, but there’s a First Principles book coming out too.
Yes, yes. All of this is great, Rohin. So why on earth are you shutting down the podcast?
Normally, I’d have replied with some safe and anodyne answers. We want to quit while we’re ahead. That two years is a great run for a podcast. That finding successful and candid founders is becoming harder, because everyone is focusing on survival and playing it safe.
Those responses are all accurate. Two years is a great run. We do want to quit while we’re ahead. And yes, finding well-known founders who want to be reflective and candid is hard.
As a result of which more of our time is spent searching for, emailing, and convincing guests who will meet our—your—bar than on crafting conversations.
Which brings me to the real answer. It’s no longer as fun and exciting for me.
Remember I’d told you that great conversations exist at the intersection of listener and guest interest? Well, there’s also host interest.
One of the foundational truths we believe in The Ken’s Podcasts team is that the host is merely a stand-in for the listener. I don’t know about you, but I want my hosts to be unassuming, friendly, and energetic, but also curious, well-researched, and determined.
Because we can’t be in the studio or in front of a mike asking guests questions, we trust that our host will. We hope that the host will be as curious, excited, and determined to get to the heart of a story or conversation as we would.
As much as I love longform interviews with founders, I feel that there are more pressing podcast formats that are needed right now. Venture capital investments have reduced to a trickle. Since the overall pie has stopped growing, companies are fighting each other for every incremental rupee from a customer’s wallet, a Zero-Sum Game. Few founders are doing genuinely disruptive things, instead focusing on survival and profitability. The tentacles of regulations and regulators are everywhere. AI hype and FOMO is hard to ignore. Companies are hiring fewer people at the entry level and retaining fewer at the experienced level. Workplaces and careers are being upended because when even uber-profitable giants like Google, Amazon, and Meta do wave after wave of layoffs, everyone else assumes we must be in the new normal.
We want answers, but no one wants to stick their neck out and speak.
That’s what excites me and the rest of my colleagues at The Ken. It’s why we’re journalists.
So, expect a new podcast from us—from me—very soon. I have a feeling you might like it.
If you’ve stuck around till now, thank you. I’d love to understand from you what else you’d like from The Ken’s Podcasts. Topics, guests, styles… whatever your suggestion or critique, we’re always and all… ears. What do they say? “The link is in the show notes”.
And as for First Principles, well, we’ll always have Paris.”
I still stand by all that. But, short story—after shutting down First Principles, I started the[ Two by Two podcast](https://the-ken.com/podcasts/two-by-two/) with my co-host Praveen Gopal Krishnan and colleagues Hari Krishna as producer (who you also know as your books editor on this newsletter) and Rajiv CN as sound engineer.
We’re enjoying where it’s taking us. And I think, so are our guests and listeners.
But First Principles continues to stay on Apple’s podcast charts. Listeners continue to write in to us, asking if we’d consider restarting it. But those are merely “necessary not sufficient” conditions for us to bring back the podcast.
The real reason lies buried in the words I’ve quoted above from our last podcast: “It’s no longer as fun and exciting for me.”
I still miss those deep conversations. I want to do more of those. I want to make them fun and exciting for myself and our listeners.
I’m changing my mind. I know I ought to feel defensive, but I don’t. For most of my career I’d have stopped myself from backtracking on major decisions. And in the event I had to, tried very hard to sneak the reversal and hope no one notices.
But why though? As one of my favourite quotes of all time goes, “When the facts change, I change my mind—what do you do, sir?” (from the late John Maynard Keynes).
Consistency is overrated over the course of our careers. I’d rather be happy and right, than miserable and consistent.
First, we examined the implications of refusing to change your mind in a real-word context with important outcomes—an entrepreneurial pitch competition. Consistent with prior research, we found that entrepreneurs had a general tendency to dig their heels in: 76% of entrepreneurs refused to change their minds when faced with contradictory evidence. Unfortunately, this tendency turned out to be counterproductive to their interests. Specifically, entrepreneurs who changed their minds during the pitch were almost six times more likely to advance to the final round of the competition.
Next, we took our findings from the pitch competition into the laboratory to find out more about what was driving these outcomes. Participants played the role of investors evaluating entrepreneurs, where half of the entrepreneurs were described as changing their minds while the other half were described as holding their ground on an initial stance in the face of valid contradictory evidence. Similar to what we observed in the actual competition, participants believed that entrepreneurs who changed their minds should advance in the competition, compared to those who dug their heels in. We also found that participants perceived those who changed their minds as lacking confidence, but demonstrating intelligence — the results suggest that in an entrepreneurial context at least, showcasing intelligence is ultimately paramount.
[Research: Changing Your Mind Makes You Seem Intelligent by Martha Jeong, Leslie K. John, Francesca Gino, and Laura Huang](https://hbr.org/2019/09/research-changing-your-mind-makes-you-seem-intelligent), Harvard Business Review
Entrepreneurs and leaders are constantly changing their minds in the face of newer contexts and times. It doesn’t mean they don’t have strong beliefs, but that they’re not bound by them. If you’re clear about why you want to change your mind, lean into it.
I am looking forward to the first episode from “Season 3” of First Principles. It will be hard to host multiple podcasts, but I hope to reduce the frequency of First Principles (Two by Two is weekly) to a number where I am not compelled to “fill the calendar”, but only invite founders and leaders I genuinely want to learn from.

I have, over the past few days, managed to finish a few of the books suggested by the First Principles community. One, recommended by Sateesh Kumar—called [The Paris Review Interviews, 1](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/11473.The_Paris_Review_Interviews_1)—has an introduction by Philip Gourevitch that’s really grown on me.
Sateesh had first sent in his recommendation after [an edition](https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/listen-to-your-scar-tissue/) in which I’d spoken about my affection for books that are just a collection of questions and answers. Interviews, essentially. And Gourevitch’s introduction struck a particular chord with me because a lot of it revolves around things we tried to apply consistently with our guests on the [First Principles](https://the-ken.com/podcasts/first-principles/) podcast.
Now, this book contains interviews of mostly writers, creators of worlds. But their appeal is very similar to what the founders and leaders on our podcast had to offer. Check out this little excerpt from Gourevitch’s introduction:
…The men who started The Paris Review took the dissenting view that instead of pontificating about writing they should simply publish the stuff: fiction and poetry, nonfiction and plays. Who needed a theory, much less a dogma? “The literary magazines seem today on the verge of doing away with literature, not with any philistine bludgeon but by smothering under the weight of learned chatter,” William Styron (at the age of twenty-seven, one of the elders of the original Paris Review circle) declared in a sort of anti manifesto in the inaugural issue. He called for the magazine to devote its pages simply to “the good writers and good poets, the non-drumbeaters” and “non-axe-grinders.”
Excellence, then, would be the magazine’s only editorial requirement, and the interviews were conceived as the best way to discuss writing and the writing life in their own terms–by letting writers speak for themselves about their work.
Replace just a few words and he could be talking about what we’ve tried to do with every episode of First Principles. I think both the book and the podcast are worth your while, and you should check them out if you haven’t already.

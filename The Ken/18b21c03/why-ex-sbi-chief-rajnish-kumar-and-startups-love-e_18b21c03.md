How public servants retire their retirement these days will make for a glorious “rewirement” feature any day. Today, we focus on just one.
“My character is diametrically opposite to what typically you would expect from a senior functionary in SBI,” Rajnish Kumar told The Ken over a morning tea at The Leela Ambience hotel in Gurugram last Thursday.
Glad that the former chairman of the State Bank of India—the country’s largest public sector lender—himself said so. Ever since he retired a year ago, Kumar has been accepting advisory and executive roles at some of the largest startups with a regularity that has left many wondering—why choose troubled startups?
Byju’s, Oyo, and BharatPe—all with unicorn-sized problems on their balance sheets or business models or governance. Does Kumar have a saviour complex?
Maybe. But more than that, he believes one needs to take risks in life. For someone who gets offered a board position almost every other week, Kumar is picky. For instance, it took Bharatpe co-founder Ashneer Grover nearly five months and four attempts to convince Kumar to join Bharatpe.
At Oyo, it was a much longer pursuit for the founder, Ritesh Agarwal. He sat with Kumar on the Board of MDI, the B-school in Gurugram, and persuaded him for over three years to join as a strategic group advisor.
In the case of the beleaguered Byju’s, it was one of its investors who prevailed upon Kumar. Today, despite all the financial mess inside the company, Kumar thinks it’s “not a sinking ship”.
Whether his name on company boards adds gravitas, opens doors, or just lends credibility in difficult times is a moot question; why Kumar chooses to be on startup roller-coaster rides is a more interesting question. One that Rounak tries to answer in this delightful read deftly.

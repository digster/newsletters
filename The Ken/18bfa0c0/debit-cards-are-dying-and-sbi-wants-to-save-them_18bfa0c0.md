Good Morning Ishan,
Being the State Bank of India—the banker to every Indian—cannot be easy.
On the one hand, those cruel folks online can’t stop making memes about its infamous “lunchtime”. On the other hand, the Reserve Bank of India—the banking regulator—leans on it to bail out basket cases like Yes Bank in March 2020.
As if all this isn’t enough, the government, SBI’s majority shareholder and driving force, expects it to post big profits and pay fat dividends. All while making a success of the universal banking agenda by opening millions of bank accounts across the length and breadth of the country.
A side effect of the latter is that SBI has ended up issuing the maximum number of debit cards in the country. A humungous 260 million, or more than a quarter of all the active debit cards in the country, as of September.
And that’s become a problem, to put it mildly.
You see, the debit card has been comprehensively beaten by that ubiquitous upstart—UPI—in the payments game. And it is the credit card that folks turn to when it comes to settling big sums. The humble debit card, it turns out, has fallen between the stools. And SBI is hurting—its debit-card base is shrinking and transaction volumes are falling.
In its current form, the debit card may not exist more than 5–6 years from now, experts say.
Yet, SBI has done something in the past couple of months that has made heads turn. It has gone all guns blazing on promotions and reward points for spending and transactions on its debit cards.
Is SBI trying to resuscitate a loss-making, dying business and a product nearing extinction? Why? Is there more to it than meets the eye?
Narayanan, the newest writer at The Ken, lays out the answers neatly with nifty charts in his debut story today. “It’s simply economics,” he says.

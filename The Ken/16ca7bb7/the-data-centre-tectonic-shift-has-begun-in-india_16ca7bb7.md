Good Morning Ishan,
A $90 billion (largely) energy-to-chemicals conglomerate. A $13 billion mining-to-ports conglomerate. A multi-billion dollar real estate conglomerate. The companies I’m referring to are Reliance, Adani and Hiranandani respectively. In the normal course of things, a majority of the business interests of these three don’t overlap. But something’s changed over the past 6 months, because all three have announced ambitious plans to go head-to-head in one of the most drab businesses: setting up data centres.
Why?
It’s simple really. Because India has 17% of the world’s population and 12% of its Internet users. But just 2.2% of its data centres.
That’s about to change. Aided by a coordinated policy thrust from the central government, various industry regulators and the central bank, the market for data centres located within India is about to explode.
"Data is wealth and Indian wealth must remain in India,” said Reliance’s chairman Mukesh Ambani last week at his company’s annual general meeting.
"Today, data is a real wealth and it is being said that whoever acquires and controls the data will have hegemony in the future,” said India’s Prime Minister Narendra Modi last year at Davos.
The writing is clear. Which is why even though setting up a data centre in India currently requires over 60 different permission from 12-15 different government departments, there is a queue of companies who want to set more up. Heck, the Adani Group wants to invest Rs 70,000 crore (~$10 billion) to set up data centres when the total cloud computing market is only expected to touch $7.2 billion by 2022. What does Adani know that others don’t?
In his first story for The Ken, Pratap analyses the structural shifts that are starting to take place, the competitive advantages and imperatives, and the likely cast of players.

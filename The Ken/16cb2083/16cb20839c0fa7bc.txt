Good Morning Ishan,

You simply could not have missed the barrage of news reports in the past few days on the to and fro between restaurants and foodtech companies, mainly Zomato.

Perhaps restaurants were hoping for poetic justice. After all, why else would they launch their protest against Zomato Gold and other discount programs on 15 August, India's independence day?

It started with 300 restaurants. Three days later it was 2,200, over a third of the 6,500 outlets on Zomato Gold.

Their protests against the discount mania in the foodtech industry were certainly not new. However, this time there was something different.

You could sense it in Zomato CEO Deepinder Goyal's comments—going from reminding restaurants of the notice period to quit Gold, to admitting the company has made mistakes.

You could sense it in the scaling up of the demands by the restaurants—from the removal of deep discounts to a six-point agenda covering delivery and all things foodtech.

You could sense it the calls to convene meetings—where industry body NRAI was earlier the first at the table, now aggregators were requesting discussions.

A flurry of such meetings were held over the past few days. The upshot: "it was decided, that all aggregators will rejig their features which will allow the restaurant-customer ecosystem to detox from the addiction of deep-discounts that has crippled the industry."

The battle is won, but the war is not over. Neither is the victor decided. Still, the past few days have seen a new power dynamic, a shift in strategy, in assembling a beachhead, writes Abinaya in today's story. 

A story that starts a little before November 2017, when Gold was launched, and runs through the frenetic events of the past few days. A story that is still in the making.

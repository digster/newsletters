Good Morning Ishan,

Like most Japanese martial arts, Jujutsu too is immensely fascinating. Its underlying principle is to harness and redirect your opponent’s energy against themselves instead of trying to fight it by expending your own. Ninjas, though, largely relied on their own collection of guerrilla warfare and fighting techniques, Ninjutsu.

Ninjacart, India’s largest fresh produce supply chain platform, deserves a black belt in corporate jujutsu. Even as its peers in India’s startup ecosystem are struggling to stay afloat in the face of calamitous revenue falls and supply chain disruptions, Ninjacart is actually becoming more resilient and promising. 

Founded in 2015, its mission has been to “de-layer this supply chain”. It sources and delivers over 1,000 tonnes of fresh fruits and vegetables from 20,000 farmers to 60,000 retailers, all within 12 hours. But unlike its B2C peers like Bigbasket who service millions of consumers across hundreds of pin codes, Ninjacart serves around 60,000 retailers, most of whom are in big cities like Bengaluru.

In a wonderful story, equal parts analytical and observant, Sumanth unravels the secret behind Ninjacart’s business model, strategic planning and philosophy. How did a company that famously (and allegedly) refused “an offer you cannot refuse” from Reliance Industries, India’s largest conglomerate, in early 2019, get to where it is? How did it figure out how to convert each apartment community into its own food retailer? How did it manage to operate its complex fleet of over 1,000 trucks twice a day during India’s lockdown? How is it offering its supply chain as an “API” to other startups like Meesho?

Good Morning Ishan,
When India’s central bank dealt an existential blow to Paytm Payments Bank* in late January, Razorpay should have been among the first to take advantage of the fallout.
The US$7.5 billion-worth fintech, one of India’s largest payments-service providers, bought Ezetap in 2022 to shed its online-only identity and leverage the point-of-sale (PoS) payments company’s brick-and-mortar presence.
The US$150 million acquisition should have given Razorpay the arsenal it needed to quickly sign up Paytm’s merchants. But, as Gaurav argues in today’s [story](https://the-ken.com/story/what-does-razorpay-have-to-show-for-its-150-million-ezetap-acquisition/?utm_source=daily&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter), there’s a world of difference between the kind of online merchants Razorpay built its business on and the ones that typically buy PoS devices.
And it certainly doesn’t help that the biggies of the banking world—Axis Bank, HDFC Bank, ICICI Bank, and State Bank of India—control two-thirds of the 8.6 million PoS machines in operation.
Such a declaration may seem premature, considering we are just two months into 2024. But the merger of Reliance Industries’ and The Walt Disney Company’s media operations in India is one of those industry-reshaping events.
The combined entity, worth US$8.5 billion, will control 40 channels. But Reliance and Disney investors will be more interested in how the two streaming services, Jiocinema and Disney+ Hotstar, fare.
What’s increasingly true of streaming in the US—sports programming as a way to fix subscriber fatigue—has always been the case in India. The primacy of cricket (read Indian Premier League) is almost impossible to shake off.
Hotstar reportedly lost one-third of its subscribers within months of losing the digital rights for the Twenty20 competition to Jiocinema in 2022. And when Jiocinema decided to make the IPL free, the ad revenue more than doubled the top line of Reliance’s media business.
Without the IPL, even an endless library of movies and shows amounts to little. This means the Reliance-Disney venture absolutely needs the tournament’s popularity to head in just one direction.
*Paytm’s founder Vijay Shekhar Sharma is an investor in The Ken

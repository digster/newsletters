Jakob Nielsen, known as the guru of web page usability, drafted the "Law of Internet User Experience" in 2017.

It states that since users spend most of their time on other websites, they prefer your site to work the same way as others'. This familiarity, Nielsen says, keeps users engaged with your site. No violations permitted.

Two years later, HT Media Ltd. brutalised (violated is too polite a word in this case) that law with a radical overhaul of livemint.com http://livemint.com/, the site of its business publication, Mint.

The refurbished portal, launched in January 2019, was unlike any other media website. Gone was the homepage display with reams of hyper-linked headlines. Gone were drop-down menus to pick from myriad news topics. Gone was the option to keep clicking through and going deeper into the site.

Instead, Livemint's visitors are now greeted with a single-column, infinite-scroll feed of stories, akin to Facebook and Twitter. Clicking on a headline expands the article on the same page, with the scroll feature restarting at the end of the story.

These and other extreme changes violently defied Nielsen's law. And doing that, Nielsen had warned, would make the "back" button very attractive to visitors. 

So, Mint went ahead and committed another cardinal sin of web design—it broke the "back" button.

If it's a head-scratcher so far, it gets even more bizarre. Livemint ended 2019 with 33 million visitors, an 11X jump over two years. Along the way, it dethroned long-reigning rival The Economic Times (ET)*, which ended last year with 24 million visitors.

But was it merely a website overhaul that helped Mint best ET? Was that even Mint's ultimate goal? What about HT Media's other sites?

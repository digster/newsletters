Good Morning Ishan,
This week, I watched Everything Everywhere All at Once. I’m sure it’s on your watchlist too, so not giving you any spoilers. The film was an enjoyable watch, but it was not really what I thought it would be. I walked into the theatre expecting a time-travel, multiverse adventure. Instead, I experienced a heartwarming story about complex family dynamics, generational trauma, and forgiveness.
We never really know what lies at the heart of something until we dig deep. And when we do, the truth might contradict our presumptions completely. This was a pattern I noticed in some of The Ken's stories this week.
In Monday's story, Shashwat wrote about Zomato’s [sustainability narrative](https://the-ken.com/story/green-push-or-greenwash-sustainable-funds-dont-buy-zomatos-esg-narrative/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter). The foodtech giant hopes to attract environmental, social, and governance (ESG) funding through initiatives such as making food deliveries plastic neutral. But in reality, its ESG push has few takers, and a lack of sincere commitment to sustainability efforts makes the exercise futile.
On Tuesday, Gaurav wrote about SoftBank-backed [Atome Financial](https://the-ken.com/story/uncharted-accounts-what-softbank-backed-bnpl-platform-atomes-rosy-financials-hide/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter), one of Southeast Asia’s biggest BNPL companies. When the company filed its 2021 numbers, they seemed mighty impressive—almost too good to be true. But a closer look reveals that all is not good with Atome’s numbers. Around $105 million, i.e. 35% of its total income in 2021, came from an unusual one-off gain.
Anand’s Wednesday story was about the rapid [growth of Jar](https://the-ken.com/story/us300m-jars-golden-run-strikes-a-jarring-valuation-note/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter), a savings and investments app. Its USP (allowing micro-investments in digital gold) has sent its valuation soaring to $300 million during the funding winter. But Sebi is tightening the screws on digital gold, and a shiny new competitor to the product waits in the wings.
On Thursday, Nadine and Aayush wrote about Indian startups’ struggles in Indonesia, Southeast Asia’s largest economy. While some companies like Redbus and Rebel Foods have flourished, Zomato and Meesho have had to shut shop in the country. Read to find out what it takes to build a[ successful business](https://the-ken.com/story/the-indian-founders-guide-to-building-a-successful-business-in-indonesia/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter) in the archipelago.
On Friday, Seema and Maitri wrote about [“eye-commerce”](https://the-ken.com/story/welcome-to-rollup-eye-commerce-what-asgs-bid-for-vasan-tells-us-about-future-of-indian-eye-care/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter). ASG Eye Hospital is buying Vasan Eye Care and other private practices across the country. Retail eye care in India is buzzing with potential, but private equity infusion comes with conditions.
To be honest, I’m not big on business podcasts (I’m a true crime and pop-culture girl). But this week’s lineup of our podcasts changed my outlook toward business podcasts. The Ken’s [Cost to Company](https://the-ken.com/podcasts/cost-to-company/the-product-manager-is-a-politician/) explored the politics behind product management (I will admit I now have a better understanding of what product managers do).
In the latest episode of [First Principles](https://the-ken.com/podcasts/first-principles/inmobi-first-principles/), Naveen Tewari, chief executive and co-founder of InMobi—India’s first unicorn—lets his guard down and talks about failures, course corrections, innovations, and a lot more. You should listen to them both, I’m sure you’ll like them.
Do share your thoughts on Everything Everywhere All at Once. I hope you have a restful Sunday. We’ll be back on Monday morning, as always.

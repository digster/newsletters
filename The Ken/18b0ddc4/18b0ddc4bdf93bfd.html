“There is a tendency for us to either play defense or cut costs during bad times, or coast along in the good times. It is important to do the opposite.”
Sunday, 8 October 2023

 

Dear Ishan, good morning. If every instinct you have is wrong, then the opposite would have to be right. Right?

 

Fans of the greatest sitcom ever made (and perhaps that will ever be) might have recognised my opening line. It’s from one of the episodes of Seinfeld: “The Opposite”. To be honest, when I started the First Principles newsletter, I didn’t foresee leading with a Seinfeld reference. And yet, here we are.

 

It began with a breakfast meeting a few weeks ago. I was meeting Shan Kadavil, the co-founder and CEO of online fresh fish and meat store Freshtohome. I was a full half-hour late due to some unusually heavy Monday-morning traffic, but since the hotel was walking distance from Shan’s office, I caught him relaxedly reading a business newspaper. 

 

Over the spiciest Eggs Akuri and toast I’ve had in my life, we chatted a bit about fish varieties. After a lifetime of “hardcore” meat-loving, I’d turned pescetarian in late July as a six-month experiment. 

 

A bottle of water and a coffee later, as I felt the heat from the Eggs Akuri start to recede, I asked Shan for the mental model or first principle he relied upon most frequently at Freshtohome for making big decisions. 

 

“When things are good, I tell my team to kill things that are working. And when things are bad, I tell them to imagine if everything went right, what would you do? That’s my job as CEO,” he said. 

[Image courtesy: Sardenacarlo, Pixabay]

What was that? When things are good, he told his team to kill things? Why?

 

Because, he said, as CEO, it was his job to break the default habits and dominant mindsets inside his organisation. When times are good (for example, when companies are flush with funding or the economy is booming), teams within organisations tend to coast along. They take good times for granted. “Killing things” during such times forced his organisation to rethink from the ground up, for the better. 

 

And when things were already bad? What did he mean by “imagine if everything went right”?

 

Shan replied that it was a concept he learnt from Mark Pincus when he worked at Zynga (Pincus is the founder of the online social gaming company). When a company or team is in dire straits, a leader can ask, what would people do if things were the opposite? What would they do then?

 

We broke off into a side discussion about how in 2012 Zynga ended up launching its own website for playing games when they had their backs to the wall negotiating with Facebook. 

 

I asked Shan for an example of his “do-the-opposite” credo at Freshtohome. 

 

He told me about how, for years, Freshtohome had struggled to compete with smaller meat and fish shops that managed to process orders at Rs 10-15 (US$0.1-0.2). Freshtohome, in contrast, used to average close to Rs 40 (US$0.5) per order because they did a lot of processing at large factories assigned to individual cities. 

 

“I asked them, how can we be better than them (the smaller, standalone shops) on everything but worse at order processing, even after raising US$250 million and employing so many smart people?” said Shan. 

 

By focusing on “killing” their existing order-processing assumptions (large factories were too expensive in smaller cities without enough scale), Shan said he forced his team to come up with the idea of using part of the retail space in their stores (they don’t just sell online) to process meat and fish. 

 

Similarly, he said Freshtohome ended up investing in retail expansion last year when money was scarce and venture capital hard to raise (they raised US$104 million in March this year).

 

Here’s the Harvard Business Review on doing the opposite:
The test I apply is quite simple. I look at the core strategy choices and ask myself if I could make the opposite choice without looking stupid. For my wealth managers, the opposite of their “where” choice was to target poor individuals who don’t want and aren’t willing to pay for comprehensive wealth management services. The opposite of their “how” is to provide crappy customer service.\
 
The point is this: If the opposite of your core strategy choices looks stupid, then every competitor is going to have more or less the exact same strategy as you. That means that you are likely to be indistinguishable from your competitors and the only way you will make a decent return is if the industry currently happens to be highly attractive structurally.
The First Question to Ask of Any Strategy by Roger L. Martin, Harvard Business Review
Exploring and attempting the opposite has been a key part of The Ken’s own journey too. For instance, we chose a great deal of “opposites” to what was conventional wisdom in 2016.
 
- Published only one story a day

- Went all-in on the subscription model, with no backup plan

- Priced our journalism at a significant premium over anything else that was there

- Put everything behind a paywall
- Decided to not discount our subscriptions frequently or deeply
 
We’re still experimenting, but these have played a big role in getting us here. 
 

“There is a tendency for us to either play defense or cut costs during bad times, or coast along in the good times. It is important to do the opposite. My job as CEO is to do that,” said Shan as we shook hands and decided to do a “proper Mallu” (we’re both Keralites) breakfast the next time.

On to the books section! Let’s start with the community recommendations. 

 

Satyajeet Kelkar is the COO at Nutech Engineering Technologies Ltd. He tells me it is one of India’s oldest designers and manufacturers of precision cooling systems for critical facilities such as atomic plants, refineries (and now for photovoltaic cells and rare-metal processing).

 

Satyajeet recommended Mindset: The New Psychology of Success by Carol S Dweck.

Here’s why he recommended it, in his own words (I’ve lightly edited it).

“Dr Carol Dweck takes her hypothesis across analogies from sports, business and leadership, education and parenting. The book talks about two types of Mindset’s—the one that holds us back and the one that enables us to grow.
 
Breaking mindsets into ‘Fixed’ and ‘Growth’, she points out that a fixed mindset works with a set of pre-ordained assumptions (like inborn talent), associates success to these assumptions and failures either to uncontrollable externalities or inherent weakness. It does not encourage rigorous questioning or learning from failure.
 
The growth mindset, on the other hand, encourages a fundamental questioning of assumptions; “...are these beliefs true and at the same time, question if these assumptions are actually relevant to your present context”—>this is where I find it similar to First Principles thinking.
 
An axiom may be true, but the context in which one applies it might have changed… This makes it necessary to question our assumptions about the context.
Consistently resilient firms, for example, would typically go back to the core assumptions around which the business was built, break them down (product, tech, consumers, environment, capabilities), see if any of these apply today and would stand valid in the future, and disrupt themselves rather than wait to be disrupted.
 
The book re-wired the way I think about abilities—my own, the people I work with, my kids… And instead of siloing people based on a fixed notion of what they can do, it's more on the lines of “given these skills, how much more can we train them to do better?” or “what new skills can we impart?”
 
A significant part of the restructuring that I mentioned in my earlier email is also in part a result of the idea of questioning the prevailing belief about the line of business, confront facts, however brutal they might be, and course correct. We have exited some legacy businesses contributing over 30% of revenue but were a drag on operating cash flow and did not fit into where the firm needs to be over the next five years.”

I ended up reading a few books myself. 

 

The first was Carved in Sand by Cathryn Jakobson Ramin. It’s a book about how our memory starts to play tricks on us as we hit middle age. We blank on names, struggle with crosswords, and miss appointments. I’m reading a bunch of such books as I hit many such middle-age ‘milestones’ myself. Wouldn’t recommend it to anyone else. And even otherwise, consider the book just the author’s own experiences with memory, possible causes, and potential treatments. 

The second book I read was along more conventional lines. Anatomy of a Breakthrough by Adam Alter is a breezy read through various phases or stages of “getting stuck” in life, and what we can learn from those who break through and succeed. Instead of one or two “big” ideas that are overwritten to form a book (as is the common formula for books of this genre), Anatomy of a Breakthrough covers dozens of situations and predicaments and solutions. You may not walk away with a big takeaway, but will definitely have a newfound appreciation for the process behind innovation, creativity, and ambition. 

It’s from this book that I ended up discovering the wonderful “Pixar’s 22 Rules of Storytelling”, written originally as a tweet-thread by Emma Coats, a former Pixar Story Artist. Here are a few of my favourites.
4. Once upon a time there was ___. Every day, ___. One day ___. Because of that, ___. Because of that, ___. Until finally ___.
 
6. What is your character good at, comfortable with? Throw the polar opposite at them. Challenge them. How do they deal?
 
9. When you’re stuck, make a list of what WOULDN’T happen next. Lots of times the material to get you unstuck will show up.
 
11. Putting it on paper lets you start fixing it. If it stays in your head, a perfect idea, you’ll never share it with anyone.

Phew, that’s all for this week. See you next Sunday!

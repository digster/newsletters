Trade Tricks
Tue, 04 Mar 25
A weekly newsletter about the biggest changes in commerce—focusing on shifts that matter to you..
Good Morning Ishan,
When I joined my colleagues Rohin and Praveen on their podcast, Two by Two, in December to discuss “[The death of D2C](https://the-ken.com/podcasts/two-by-two/the-death-of-d2c/)”, there was an unmissable trigger.
Large and “traditional” FMCG companies, which were once acquirers of D2C startups, have sobered up. Their acquisitions haven’t really scaled up well, even as they’ve figured out how to compete with D2Cs. As a result, the acquisition premium for D2C startups has plummeted from the peak during the post-pandemic days. In some cases even a 50% discount from the peak isn’t leading to deals.
After Covid, when e-commerce adoption surged, fast-moving-consumer-goods (FMCG) makers such as Tata Consumer Products, Marico, and ITC rushed to [hoover up](https://the-ken.com/story/marico-emamis-quest-to-catch-the-d2c-lightning-in-a-bottle/) internet-first food or personal-care brands.
But they soon realised that winning over hordes of young consumers with these brands was little more than a mirage. As the pandemic receded into the background, consumers embraced shopping at stores once again, and venture capital-backed brands discovered, much to their dismay, that the cost of signing up new users had once again shot up.
But something shifted soon after we recorded the episode.
Hindustan Unilever (HUL), India’s largest FMCG company by sales and market cap, bought Peak XV Partners-backed skincare startup Minimalist for close Rs 3,000 crore. Just two weeks later, rival ITC [acquired](https://the-ken.com/tradetricks/why-itc-is-buying-prasuma/) Meatigo, a frozen-foods and packaged-meats brand, for one-tenth of that. And reports say Wow Skin Science, a Chrys Capital-funded cosmetics company, is in [search](https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/tech/technology/d2c-beauty-company-wow-seeks-strategic-buyer-at-a-lower-250-million-valuation/articleshow/117030951.cms?from=mdr) of a suitor.
The upstarts’ products are certainly not flying off the shelves. Minimalist, whose sales almost doubled to Rs 350 crore in FY24, is an exception. A pioneer in India, Minimalist set fire to conventional FMCG marketing by putting the active ingredients of its products, and not some fuzzy promise, front and centre on the packaging.
Most digital-native brands, though, have seen their fortunes slide. Wow Skin’s investors reportedly want to sell the company at $250 million, considerably lower than the $400 million it commanded after its last fundraise. Amazon-backed Good Glamm Group is [floundering](https://inc42.com/features/good-glamm-groups-bad-formula/).
So what explains the FMCG giants’ change of heart?
Marico chief executive officer Saugata Gupta had the answer in a recent [interview](https://www.livemint.com/industry/retail/fmcg-competition-india-marico-direct-to-consumer-brands-d2c-vs-legacy-fmcg-brands-quick-commerce-consumption-inflation-11740458334307.html) with Mint.
“Direct-to-consumer companies are looking at strategic exits instead of going public or scaling independently, and I think consolidation is likely.”
The bloodletting in the stock market could delay the 26 initial public offerings, with a combined share sale of [Rs 43,000 crore](https://indianexpress.com/article/business/ipo-pipeline-faces-uncertainty-as-market-sentiment-takes-a-big-hit-9864914/), that the regulator has greenlit. But this is not the primary factor in young FMCG brands’ desire to be acquired.
The company that was supposed to set the stage for a bevy of personal-care and food startups to go public did anything but.
Investors first took Honasa Consumer, the owner of Mamaearth—among the most prominent internet-first cosmetics brands—to new heights, then promptly pummelled the stock.
Within 10 months of its November 2023 listing, Mamaearth’s shares had surged by two-thirds. But they are now trading a third below their issue price.
In a press note, the distributors’ body estimated unsold stock at Rs 300 crore and unresolved credit notes at another Rs 50 crore.
The cracks, though, appeared long before the last quarter. As distributors tell it, the real problem started months ahead of Honasa’s IPO, when the company began flooding the market with stock to boost its sales figures. The pitch was simple: these products will fly off the shelves. They didn’t. By then, even FMCG giants like Hindustan Unilever and Nestlé were feeling the pinch of a consumption slowdown.
Mamaearth sold investors on its FMCG dreams. Consumers had other plans, [The Ken](https://the-ken.com/story/mamaearth-sold-investors-on-its-fmcg-dreams-consumers-had-other-plans/)
The mistake Mamaearth made has been the undoing of other such brands too.
Scaling in FMCG isn’t just about getting on the shelves—it’s about staying there. Marico’s brands, like Parachute and Saffola, have spent decades building trust and customer loyalty. Mamaearth, with its 200,000 retail outlets, isn’t there yet. Meanwhile, HUL, which directly supplies 2.5 million outlets, moves mountains of products because customers trust the brand.
For all its marketing muscle and IPO theatrics, Mamaearth misjudged the leap from a plucky D2C startup to a typical FMCG company.
For FMCG majors, it’s a buyer’s market like never before.

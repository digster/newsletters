If penicillin was one of the marvels of the 20th-century medicine, so was the warning from its inventor. 

“The time may come when penicillin can be bought by anyone in the shops. Then there is the danger that the ignorant man may easily underdose himself, and by exposing his microbes to non-lethal quantities of the drug, make them resistant.” Alexander Fleming had said in his Nobel Prize acceptance speech in 1945.

According to India’s most definitive study, released last month, Indians show resistance as high as 99.9% towards some antibiotics. When an over 10% resistance is almost too high.

We have abused and misused antibiotics like it’s nobody’s business. Starting at the doctors’ doorstep. Every patient in need of surgery requires just one dose of antibiotics pre-surgery, but doctors, as a precautionary measure, end up prescribing three doses a day for 5-7 days. This is an irrational but common practice in India. Some 800,000 pharmacists, who can’t be easily, or possibly, regulated because they also save lives in remotest places and are part of another common practice, where people pop in antibiotics for sore throat or diarrhoea. (Both self-limiting.)

Then you have the massive poultry industry, fed and fattened on colistin, a reserve category antibiotic.

Thankfully, Kerala in south India (no surprise), has shown the way. Bureaucrats have found that by not feeding antibiotics to chicken in poultry farms, not only is the resistance avoided, the weight of the chicken and size of the egg remain the same. Some other states are waking up to the, er, chicken-egg dilemma, but that’s not enough. The World Bank reckons the global economic cost of resistance could rival that of the financial crisis of 2008-09.

Good Morning Ishan,
 
Online learning—like online work—has become the norm over the last year due to the pandemic. But in many ways, it has ushered in the future. 

Like the future of work, online learning makes sense as a hybrid that mixes in-person education rather than online-only.

But there’s a long way to go.

As a parent of two kids, I can tell you that managing online learning during the pandemic was a full-time job—something that most parents simply don’t have time for. In many cases, they also don’t have the materials. Many kids simply lack the devices to go online, while for others in rural areas, finding a decent signal is not easy.

The reality is that millions of kids simply lost a year of school, often at critical times.

That’s the subject of today’s story: the lost year of school in Indonesia, which has the world’s third-largest population of school-age children.

With 62 million pupils attending more than 500,000 schools, Indonesia’s education sector is behind only China and India. The pandemic battered the country harder than anywhere else in Southeast Asia, meaning most schools have been shut for a year.

It’s been tough, but it has advanced some policies of the new education minister, Nadiem Makarim. The former Gojek CEO wants to rebuild the system with a greater focus on digital skills and e-learning.

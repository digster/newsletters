Good Morning Ishan,

Do you play poker?

The pandemic tends to remind you of times when you had fun outside your home. For me, it often reminds me of the time when I would get together with a bunch of friends. We would order food and drinks, and play a few rounds of casual Texas hold ‘em.

But isn’t there always that one annoying friend in the group? The one who, after a few drinks, would just want to bet everything they had. Go all in. Nearly every round.

You’re quite sure they’re bluffing. But what if they’re holding a straight? They could also just be drunk. But it could be both. Annoying, isn’t it?

One thing certainly happens, though. They raise the stakes and the game intensifies.

At The Ken India this week, we published stories of companies and institutions that are betting big. But there’s no free lunch. The stakes are higher than they have ever been.

Here’s a recap.

We kickstarted the week with Swiggy’s latest big bet https://the-ken.com/story/with-instamart-swiggy-wants-to-create-a-7-eleven-in-the-cloud/?utm_source=recap&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter—Instamart. Having once expanded from merely catering to the demand side of food ordering to the supply side with cloud kitchens, Swiggy attempted the same in grocery delivery with their Urban Kirana stores. But the pandemic not only disrupted that plan, it also wrecked the demand for Swiggy’s primary business. The company is now convinced that it can supply everything that a 7-Eleven store offers, do it in the cloud, and create enough margin to break even. Rohin and Pranav try to read Swiggy’s hand.

Next, we wrote about the upcoming merger https://the-ken.com/story/the-unequal-marriage-of-incred-and-kkr/?utm_source=recap&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter between KKR’s non-banking finance arm—KIFS—and the four-year-old InCred Finance. In her debut story for The Ken, Shruti describes this as an unequal marriage, but one that has benefits for both KIFS and InCred. On paper, that is. The two firms have radically different DNA and target offerings. So, whether or not this bet pays off for either company depends highly on how the merged entity will function. The NBFC sector, having faced a series of headwinds recently, will not make matters easy.

We also wrote about robots this week. Shreedhar covered how Milagrow https://the-ken.com/story/milagrow-sweep-robot-market/?utm_source=recap&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter saw a spurt in demand for its floor-cleaning robots during the pandemic. Something was different, though. Milagrow, used to seeing 70% of its revenue come from corporates, suddenly saw 90% of it come from individual customers. It is now going all-in on selling direct-to-consumer. Will Milagrow’s China connection be a cause for concern?

Adding to our extensive coverage https://the-ken.com/narrative/road-to-electric-vehicles/ of electric vehicles in India, Olina explained what comes next after the Indian government’s notification that allowed for the registration of EVs without batteries https://the-ken.com/story/electric-vehicles-batteries-identity-crisis/?utm_source=recap&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter. She argues that while this change may have appeal for consumers, in the absence of a battery standard, it has created a tug of war between key members of the EV value chain—vehicle manufacturers (OEMs), battery makers, and fleet providers. To leverage this new twist in policy, players like Ola Electric and SUN Mobility have to bet on one of two possible options, each of which requires huge upfront investment.

This week, we also wrote about Covid-19 testing https://the-ken.com/story/indias-49-million-covid-tests-hide-more-than-they-reveal/?utm_source=recap&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter in India. Even though India has conducted over 49 million tests, a veil of secrecy shrouds the real picture. Maitri unveiled that a much-larger-than-ideal number of tests are being done using the less accurate Rapid Antigen Testing kits, which at their worst, are about as effective as a coin flip. This is problematic for three reasons: cases missed by antigen tests could go on to spread the virus further, while others are likely to take the pandemic less seriously. It could also skew how Indian authorities calibrate their response to the pandemic.

Interestingly, two days after we published this story, the health ministry urged all states https://twitter.com/dawalelo/status/1303967802286432258/photo/1 to retest all negative cases of RAT through the more accurate RT-PCR tests.

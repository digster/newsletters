Good Morning Ishan,
When EV makers start offering Re 1 exchange offers to clear stock, you know something’s gone off the rails. And that’s exactly where India’s EV market finds itself—caught between big ambitions, policy flip-flops, and a lot of confusion.
On paper, everything looks great: government registration data shows gains in two-wheelers, three-wheelers, and buses. But check the government’s own tracker, and you’ll see a different picture. EV sales and penetration actually fell in 2024. So what gives?
Take Delhi—home to pollution and now endless EV policy extensions. The government has once again reinstated road-tax exemptions, citing apocalyptic air quality. But if tax immunities were the fix, EVs would be the norm by now. Instead, sales continue to sputter. For that reason, Telangana, too, had to reinstate its 100% tax exemption.
Meanwhile, India’s storied Fame scheme, which was supposed to be the engine driving EV adoption, has instead sent manufacturers into chaos. This year, the rules changed overnight, disqualifying vehicles made before April for new incentives. “You take the hit,” sighed a weary EV CEO, as Rs 50,000 to Rs 1 lakh dealer discounts ended up on the manufacturer’s balance sheets.
Then, in November, a three-wheeler subsidy quota got exhausted, leaving manufacturers with unsellable stock.
And passenger EVs, which contribute most to tailpipe emissions, remain excluded from direct subsidies. Sure, two-wheelers and buses show promise; but a global study suggests subsidies will only work in the medium and long run if India gets the framework right.
Seema [breaks down the data](https://the-ken.com/story/dear-ev-subsidy-planners-the-data-has-a-few-questions/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter) and the research, showing why India’s EV subsidies aren’t driving the needle, especially for passenger cars. From confusing consumer preferences to a glaring infra gap, it’s clear India needs more than a policy extension—it needs a roadmap that actually works.

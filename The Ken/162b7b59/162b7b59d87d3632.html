Good Morning Ishan,

The day I joined The Ken, I had a question for Rohin.
 
“Why do we send our daily email at eight in the morning?” I asked him.
 
Rohin explained how the time was chosen very carefully. Our research had indicated that it was when the reader was the most fresh, unencumbered and ready to start the day. Also, for our readers living in Bengaluru, it was when they would have just booked an Ola or an Uber to go to work and would be feeling like Elon Musk—filled with tension, staring at a screen, unsure whether the vehicle was going to arrive or not.
 
Have you noticed how tiresome it is to book an Ola or an Uber these days? It’s impossible to find cabs, and if you do, the driver usually calls you and asks you so many questions. Where is the pickup? Where is the drop? How are you paying? Do you want a home loan from ICICI bank? More than once, I’ve gotten angry and told them please call the Cambridge Analytica head-office in London; they have all these details.  
 
After all this, the driver usually says okay I am coming, and then cancels the ride. Sometimes after two hours. Once I had a ride cancelled after the driver arrived at my gate. It was so last minute that Chennai Super Kings manager called me asking for his details because they wanted him to play for them.  
 
You know what I am doing these days? I have started booking two cabs. One Uber. One Ola. For backup. Riders do this all the time in Bengaluru, I am told. They just have to. Backups are important. They hedge your risk. Are you really going to rely just on Uber?
 
Strangely, Ola is doing exactly what their riders are doing. Just in case Uber cancels on them.
 
Today’s story by Patanjali and Venkat is about Ola’s backup. It’s a backup plan that stretches across three continents, with millions of dollars at stake, while the future of India’s transportation industry hangs in the balance.
 
And it’s fascinating.
 
You should read it here: https://the-ken.com/story/ola-uk/

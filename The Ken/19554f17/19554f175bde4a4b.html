VCs and techies are turning India into a nation of hired hands

The future of work is… whatever you don’t want to do

India’s unemployment crisis is fueling a booming gig economy, where startups—big and small—turn even the smallest chores into business opportunities. Venture capitalists have poured $20 billion into gig-driven startups, betting on an endless supply of underpaid workers to keep their business models afloat. From kneading dough to standing in line, tech apps are transforming mundane tasks into paid gigs—because there’s always someone willing to work for less. As stable jobs vanish, millions of young Indians are turning to odd jobs, while the government’s plan to issue ID cards quietly makes this gig economy official. [Read the full story]

SUVs are taking over India—one accident, one parking spot at a time

With bigger cars flooding the market, everyone else—from real-estate developers to traffic officials and even pedestrians—is struggling to keep up

Indians are driving more SUVs than ever. And it’s got everyone around worried. Pedestrians and cyclists are scared. Real-estate developers and road-construction contractors are also voicing concerns. Traffic Police officials across Indian metros too blame SUVs for the gridlock. So, why are Indians still hell-bent on ditching hatchbacks and sedans? Enter aspiration. [Read the full story]

Curefoods is no longer just Eatfit. It wants to eat Domino’s

It got off the ground as a health-first cloud-kitchen brand—and then embraced pizzas, donuts, and dine-in restaurants to grow

When Curefoods acquired the franchise of Krispy Kreme in December 2024, it might have looked like an unusual move given its flagship brand Eatfit’s focus on healthy food. The company has been on a growth path since it started tapping on Indians’ junk-food cravings than catering to the clean-eating crowd only. Its pizza brand Olio has shown the most promise, being one of the few that’s seen profits in its bucket; Olio does Rs 120–130 crore in annualised sales. Its lofty ambition is to take on the market leader Domino’s at a time when it’s more vulnerable than ever. But that may be easier said than done. [Read the full story]

Pepsi’s biggest bottler is pouring more cola to fight Reliance’s Campa

Pepsi and Coca-Cola had been the undisputed kings in India’s aerated-drinks market. Until Reliance-backed Campa Cola re-entered with its palm-sized bottles at half the price

Varun Beverages, the largest bottler of Pepsi in India, has been one of the best-performing FMCG stocks. But currently, its share price is down 30% from its 52-week peak in August 2024. On top of its decades-old rivalry with Coca-Cola, it’s now got to deal with the stiff new competition from Reliance’s Campa Cola. So, to keep up, the Pepsi bottler is bringing glass bottles back and much more to protect its turf. [Read the full story]

******

India’s Rs 18,000 crore battery plan is stuck at Rs 24 crore

Reliance, Rajesh Exports, and Ola have little to show in terms of production. And India may not have all that it needs for an advanced battery industry

With an outlay of Rs 18,100 crore as subsidies, India wanted to boost local battery manufacturing and reduce its dependence on imports. But three years on, the government has spent only Rs 24 crore. Moreover, the winning companies, too, are yet to start commercial production. China is an aggressive player in the battery and EV space and dominates the world even in the supply chain. It even controls most of the raw-material processing. But all may not be lost yet for the budding Indian market. While Tata Agratas, Exide, and Amara Raja want to produce cells without waiting for subsidies, some component makers are setting up plants outside China to boost the supply chain.

In what feels like another life—though, in truth, everything before March 2020 feels like another life—I spent the better part of two years in a forest in the hills of Tamil Nadu. During my first April there, I woke up to the acrid, suffocating stench of smoke. The hills were ablaze.
 
Turns out, these fires were started by people. Each year, like clockwork, locals would set fire to large swathes of land. In some cases, because the scorched earth was particularly conducive to the growth of a shrub used in the making of brooms. In most cases, though, the primary reason was to encroach on land.
 
It is a peculiarity of land ownership in India that in many cases, especially in rural areas, property boundaries are demarcated in the strangest of ways. A particular tree or a large rock could determine where one plot ended and the next began. Razing a tree to the ground in a fire, or moving a rock under the backdrop of a blaze allowed bad actors to grow their properties overnight. Or at least buy them a legal claim to the now-disputed land.
 
When confronted with realities like this, it's little wonder that two-thirds of civil cases in India are property disputes. The problem is that most states haven't surveyed their land in decades. Those that have are still stuck in the past, largely failing to digitise this information or, indeed, land titles.
 
In 2008, the Union government decided to change this, launching an ambitious plan to map land parcels and digitise India's land records properly. A dozen years later, the programme has only achieved a fraction of what it set out to do.
 
Already, this threatens to torpedo multibillion-dollar government schemes aimed at farmers. After all, how do you aid them if you aren't even sure whether their farms exist, how large they are, or who exactly owns them, asks Seetharaman in today's piece. In it, he delves into the complexities of mapping India's land, digitising the records, and what this means for various stakeholders. It's a free piece, so do share it widely.

One of the branches of science that has exploded in the last few years is the study of ancient DNA. We are getting an entirely new understanding of the nature of human variation. You must have read reports on Harvard geneticist David Reich and his (really large) team’s work in recent months. Especially on who the people of the Indian sub-continent are and how they got here.
 
In his work, Reich makes a riveting point. He says he is discovering “genomic signatures of inequality in human history” in the DNA samples. For instance, in the Indian or African-American ancestry, a lot of genetic variations come from the male side. (More on that in his book.)
 
It’s fascinating how genetics is today also a tool to correct some of the other inequalities in DNA. Take childbirth, for example. It’s a roll of the dice, a genetic gamble. 1 in 166 children born in India has genetic abnormalities. Because we do not screen them prenatally, India is home to the largest number of children born with genetic disorders.
 
Simple, non-invasive screening tests have been available since 2011 but as we know, most new medical tech is expensive. Now, a Bengaluru company is making a case that this test be used in prenatal screening. At a price point that’s cheaper than imported ones, it works just as well on Indian women as it works on women abroad.
 
Mass adoption is what this Sequoia-funded company is gunning for. Will India nail the ethical and affordability issues and embrace the future en masse? Or is NIPT destined to remain a luxury afforded to only the affluent and aware? Which may well lead to a new divide, a genetic divide, among the rural and urban, rich and poor...

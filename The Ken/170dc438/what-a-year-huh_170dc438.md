India celebrated Holi last week. At least, it tried to. Usually, Holi—the festival of colour—marks the end of winter in the Indian north. Inspired in equal parts by an ancient tale of good over evil, and decades of choreographed Bollywood dance numbers, north Indians take to this no-hold-barred festival with particular gusto.
Not this year, though. 2020 will go down in history as the first Holi where people were too afraid to even shake hands, let alone douse each other in coloured water. The only enthusiast around me was my 7-year-old niece, who brought a water gun to the party. Sadly, she found only reluctant victims and amused herself by watering the plants instead.
It was eerie coming home that day. Streets were empty, and not just because it was a holiday. It has been eerie every single day—in autos, in the office, at home—to operate under the pale shadow of a real-life, invisible monster.
In the coming week, The Ken is going enforce its first-ever, team-wide work-from-home policy. We go on as usual, just in our pyjamas. But it's really not usual. For journalists, least of all. I've been taking comfort in what other journalists have been posting about their experiences.
There's another thing I'm clinging to dearly—doing my job.
As our editors told us on Friday, 'focus' is going to be our single biggest strength. To separate hysteria from facts. Stories from noise. Meaning from chaos. Last week's stories show just how much we intend to stick to our mission.

Arundhati spun together a deeply-reported story on how Yes Bank's fall has caused a whole host of second-order effects for fintech firms like PhonePe. Could PhonePe, and other businesses dependent on Yes Bank's API, have escaped the damage?

Whenever people marvel at Amazon's world-dominating ambition and seeming invincibility, they always forget the role luck played in Amazon’s fortunes.
Amazon, as many of you already know, started out in 1994. Founder Jeff Bezos rightly foresaw how the internet would rewire commerce around the world for ever. By 1997, Amazon had gone public. It continued to grow, in what was now clearly (in hindsight) a bubble—the original “dotcom bubble”.
The bubble would peak in March 2000, before the crash started, wiping out an entire generation of Amazon’s peers. And leaving it as one of the only large and listed e-commerce companies still standing. With shareholders who had already been primed by Bezos to not expect any profits, but instead to give Amazon a long rope to grow its top line and market share.
The rest is a cliche called history.
But did you know Amazon *just* managed to raise $672 million in February 2000 by offering its convertible bonds? Just one month before the dotcom crash began. Alongside the long rope from its investors, that cash was the cushion Amazon needed to emerge as one of the only survivors of the dotcom era.
A similar stroke of luck may have favoured 1mg, a 6-year-old online pharmacy that has raised close to $50 million in venture funding. While most of 1mg’s peers were getting attacked on streets, in courts and through laws in other states, 1mg quietly bided its time and scraped by because the regulators in Haryana, the state in which it set up shop, were quite lackadaisical.
That allowed 1mg to survive. And with the Indian government now set to legitimise e-pharmacies via new regulation that cuts across states, 1mg is in pole position to capture the vast opportunity to sell medicines via the internet to Indians. The market for medicines sold via pharmacies is $17.7 billion. Currently e-pharmacies account for less than 1% of it.

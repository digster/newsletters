Good Morning Ishan,
The past two weeks have been rather hectic for me. I’ve been busy shifting homes from Mumbai to Bengaluru. Though I’m concerned for my fellow Mumbaikars, the thought of escaping the city’s deteriorating air quality has curbed my early-onset homesickness to an extent.
My usual morning routine went for a toss amidst all the frantic packing, the last-minute catchups and get-togethers—both personal and professional—and the hustle to make it for my flight on time. But there was one part of my routine I just couldn’t miss.
Yes, you guessed it—reading The Ken’s stories! In case you missed any, here’s a recap:
The shares of 30-year-old electronics-manufacturing services (EMS) company Dixon Technologies plunged 20% on a single day in January, only to surge 80% over the last six months. It is now the world’s [priciest](https://the-ken.com/story/forget-taiwans-foxconn-and-pegatron-indias-dixon-is-the-worlds-hottest-electronics-maker/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter) EMS stock. Despite some rather alarmed investors, Dixon’s chairman, Sunil Vachani, remained unperturbed through this journey, wrote Seetharaman in Monday’s story.
In her debut story for The Ken on Tuesday, Aakriti wrote about 139-year-old fast-moving consumer goods (FMCG) major Dabur India. The Ayurvedic brand finds itself in an identity crisis. While most of Dabur’s products are geared towards consumers who are 40 and above, it wants to appeal to Gen Z. Will its [premiumisation](https://the-ken.com/story/dabur-seeks-gen-z-approval-to-shed-boomer-image/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter) drive win them over?
When the Reserve Bank of India (RBI) launched e-rupee about a year ago, it expected the digital currency to go the Unified Payments Interface (UPI) [way](https://the-ken.com/story/can-indias-digital-currency-still-be-another-upi/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter). RBI set a target of a million daily retail transactions by December 2023, but even 2% of it hasn’t been attained. To understand why, read Wednesday’s guest article by Ateesh Tankha, Ganga Narayan Rath, and Maulik Gawarepatil.
Telcos pay the government a hefty sum for the networks on which they provide their services. Looking to share the burden, they have trained their sights on big tech, including Meta, Google, Netflix, and Amazon. Caught amid this [tussle](https://the-ken.com/story/google-meta-may-have-to-pay-telcos-fair-share-but-not-until-elections/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter) is the government, which is unlikely to decide on the matter at least until next year’s general elections, wrote Pratap in Thursday’s story.
In the week’s last story, Shivani dove into the uneasy relationship between food-and-grocery-delivery giant Swiggy and its [delivery](https://the-ken.com/story/customers-love-convenience-swiggy-wants-profits-what-about-delivery-partners/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter) partners—its biggest asset. The company’s never-ending quest to improve customer convenience falls largely on them. However, Swiggy hasn’t been able to keep them happy. Demanding better pay, its delivery partners recently went on strike in Mumbai. Can Swiggy keep both customers and delivery partners happy while trying to turn profitable?
That’s all, folks! See you next week.

Good Morning Ishan,

In January last year, a company called Liufang Technologies was incorporated in Bengaluru. Its registered address was a nondescript three-storey building in Ejipura, the poorer and more congested suburban neighbour to the richer KoramangalaвҖ”the suburb from where many of the cityвҖҷs best-known tech startups, including Flipkart, began.

Compared to FlipkartвҖҷs first year though, Liufang Technologies was on fire. By the end of the year, it was running a 2,000-person call centre thousands of kilometres away in Gurugram, next to IndiaвҖҷs capital, New Delhi. The call centre agents were responsible for managing the customers of over 350 different apps that Liufang Technologies was running in India.

The apps were instant loan apps, the type you see as an ad on Facebook as youвҖҷre scrolling. Click a link to download from GoogleвҖҷs Play Store; give access to your contacts, messages, location, images; and then boomвҖ”instant transfer of thousands of rupees into your bank account. No questions asked.

There are hundreds, maybe thousands of such apps. And theyвҖҷre killing Indians.

On Friday, a 24-year old graduate from the southern Indian state of Telangana died by suicide after taking instant loans from theses apps.

Another victim was Abhishek Makwana, a young sitcom writer. Makwana died by suicide in December after borrowing a few thousand rupees each from dozens of such apps. His brother discovered 72 on AbhishekвҖҷs smartphone, after his tragic death.

Abhishek's loan amount just kept ballooning in size because these apps charge 800-1,000% interest on an annualised basis. And if you miss a payment, they will call up your contacts and harass them.

Nine out of the 72 apps on AbhishekвҖҷs smartphone were traced back to Liufang Technologies, the company I began this email with.

In her investigation into this massive and worrisome trend, Arundhati connects the dots and maps the holes in IndiaвҖҷs fintech landscape that made this crisis possible.

From Chinese white label companies that provide apps and cloud infrastructure to unethical non-bank lenders who lend these apps their licence to payment gateways who have no way to verify antecedents to the ease of publishing Android apps to the lack of proactive policing by IndiaвҖҷs regulators. ItвҖҷs dominoes all the way.

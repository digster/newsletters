Tata uses Westside strategy to save its supermarkets. Star has gotten quite good at selling its in-house brands

Good Morning Ishan,

If you asked me to name the supermarket chains that mattered the least in the Indian retail landscape, despite having been around for years, I’d go with Sanjiv Goenka-owned Spencer’s, Tata’s Star, and Amazon- and Samara Capital-backed More.

In that exact order.

But I guess it’s time for me to revisit my opinion on Star.

I have in the past touched upon the struggles of Spencer’s https://the-ken.com/story/spencers-retails-bittersweet-turnaround-recipe-for-natures-basket/ and More https://the-ken.com/tradetricks/kumar-birla-takes-yet-another-shot-at-fixing-his-spotty-record-in-retail/. And this https://the-ken.com/story/tata-tescos-retail-identity-crisis-is-now-an-existential-one/ is what I had to say about Star, a joint venture between apparel retailer Trent and British grocer Tesco, in 2020:

Having survived the ravages of the supermarket war thus far, time is not on Star’s side. The space is rapidly consolidating and evolving, and Star can no longer afford to remain an afterthought. The company is giving it the old college try, though. It plans to expand in clusters—opening new stores near existing stores—a strategy for which Dmart is well-known. And it wants to increase the number of high-margin private brands. Again, a strategy not uncommon among its rivals, including Future Retail and Reliance Retail.

It’s time to go big or go home for Star. And Martin Bailie, THPL’s chief executive and managing director since April, will not have the benefit of a honeymoon period. Intentions are one thing, execution another. Star’s struggles with the latter are what has led it to this point.

In the four years since, Star’s store count has gone from 57 to 72. That’s nothing considering Dmart added over 40 stores in the year ended March 2024. By revenue, Dmart is 20X as big as Star.

Trent must have realised years ago that it didn’t have the money or vision to play the scale game against Dmart and Reliance Retail, which makes Dmart look puny by comparison. But it has fared better than them on one front.

When Trent announced its quarterly numbers over the weekend, it revealed that private labels accounted for three-quarters of Star’s revenue. Two years ago, they contributed just half. Analyst estimates suggest that the number is under 10% for Dmart and 15–20% for Reliance.

Every retailer wants to sell as many store brands as possible. Simply because they are a lot more profitable than selling someone else’s products. The strategy has been the most successful one in the apparel industry of India.

And no one does it better than Trent.

Westside is the gold standard when it comes to in-house brands. A decade ago, they brought in 70% of its revenue. Now, Westside sells nothing but its own. Trent then took the same approach with Zudio and decided to price everything under Rs 1,000 (US$12). In short order, it became India’s hottest retailer https://www.cnbctv18.com/market/trent-share-price-market-cap-crosses-2-lakh-crore-record-high-tata-group-westside-zudio-returns-19438622.htm. 

So why not replicate the same in grocery? It’s quite easy to do this in staples, such as pulses. There aren’t too many brands in this category, so it’s a cinch to convince consumers to buy the store’s own alternatives. But where this gets really hard is...

“Why should our reader care about this? So what?”

It’s a question we reporters are often asked when we set out ideating and reporting for a story.

We tend to visualise our reader as a busy Indian woman, in her thirties, with a thriving career, managing work and home, living in any city. And she pays for things with her time first.

Such imagery lets us do a few things when we report and write. We know that the story has to be compelling enough that she should be able drop everything else to read it in the morning. And if we clear that first bar, then she should not once feel like she lacks the context to understand what is happening in the story.

This lets us walk that fine line between overexplaining and assuming prior knowledge.

Though this is what we set out to do with every story, some may hit the mark and some may not. As for the “so what”, it’s a check that lets us bring only the most important and consequential stories of our times to the reader.

With the IPO floodgates open, AI causing real impact by meddling with careers and changing businesses, and business leaders feeling the heat of all this, we had a busy year. And a lot of this struck a deep chord with you, <dear reader>.

These are the stories that resonated with The Ken’s readers the most in 2025, going by what you read and shared the most.

Instead of making consultants’ lives easier, generative AI led to shorter deadlines and vanishing creativity. Abhirami’s story brought up the all important question: what is the future of consulting if AI can do everything a consultant can?

“A consultant’s job boils down to just a few things—interacting with clients, doing research, making Excel models and slides, and coming up with insights,” said a manager at Bain. “Every single one of these can be automated with AI.” Even client interactions are not spared, since it takes AI but a minute to whip up and send a thoughtful email.

How AI is creating a rift at McKinsey, Bain, and BCG

Kalyan Krishnamurthy, chief executive of India’s largest e-commerce platform Flipkart, has been a wartime CEO for a while. Today, he looks more like a general who’s running out of lieutenants on the battlefield. Nuha has an incredible profile on one of the most important new age company CEOs of our time.

Every morning, Kalyan Krishnamurthy makes six phone calls.

… he–and by extension Flipkart—needs someone on the other end who knows where the war is headed.

Inside Kalyan Krishnamurthy’s fight to steady Flipkart as lieutenants flee

Anand, The Ken’s finance editor, over the course of six months, broke the story on US-based hedge fund Jane Street’s manipulative practices. In January, he wrote about a mystery fund playing God and wreaking havoc on India’s stock market. He followed this up with a story in June, pointing to how Jane Street, which is far larger than its peers in the massive options trades in India, was emerging as the all-powerful hand.

The “mystery fund” or the manipulator had been lying low following the media glare after The Ken’s story and statements by Sebi officials about keeping an eye on “any signs of manipulation”.

… the manipulator is back—on tiptoe first and then with a vengeance.

Is Jane Street the all-powerful hidden hand in India’s stock market?

The year 2025 was when foods got all bulked up, thanks to protein in everything. It was also the year when a genuine public-health concern turned into one of India’s fastest-growing food categories. Pranathi, The Ken’s resident chronicler of fads, called it.

..don’t be fooled by the big numbers brands plaster on their products. The whole big packet might contain, say, 40–50g of protein, but a regular serving size? That’ll be much, much less.

Protein water, protein roti, protein kulfi: inside India’s $1.5 billion food fetish

On paper, 360 One Wealth and Asset Management looks flawless: a Rs 42,000 crore market cap, among India’s largest pools of assets under management, and a co-founder CEO with a near-mythical reputation. But beneath that shine, the cracks were showing, as Aakriti found out. Top leadership exits, promoters offloading stock, and even early backers like Bain Capital quietly easing out.

Wealth management, unlike other parts of finance, is not easily automated. It is a relationship business, where trust travels through people. And while 360 One has created over 200 dollar millionaires through its ESOPs, according to Bloomberg, insiders say the bigger friction isn’t money—it’s voice.

360 One wrote the wealth-management playbook. Now it is learning a hard lesson

What does it take to be able to grow a business? Finding just the right number of frustrated people, turns out. Debanjali found out that when the Big Four were ramping up tech consulting, it became the perfect window for veteran partners to walk out of EY India.

…long-term careers are no longer nurtured. Employees aren’t oriented to EY’s values, because they’re hired to be placed at client sites and never integrate into the firm. “On paper, there are plenty of programmes, but if you’re just hiring people to deploy on projects, and then letting them go once the project ends, what’s the point?” a former senior employee said.

How EY became rivals’ feeding ground for tech consultants 

An MBA degree is turning out to be the cricket of education. Every school wants to be a B-school because that’s what the market wants, but when the country’s premier social-sciences institution starts to think like that, it’s an important shift. Atul’s story on Tata Institute of Social Sciences’ transformation was equal parts revelatory and concerning.

In addition to changes in admission procedures, this means less rural immersion and more organisational economics in the syllabus, fewer teachers from civil-society groups and more from the IIMs, and more management majors and minors at the undergraduate level.

TISS is starting to look more like an IIM than TISS

Private-equity firms like Apollo Global are betting big on Indian education, and their playbook mirrors a Western model: optimised for cost control, standardised for scale-up, and centralised for effective management. Valli set about finding what this means for Indian schools which operate in a far more volatile education market.

Private equity may not hold back on spending, but where that money goes—and where it doesn’t—reveals a skewed sense of priorities. For instance, at Sancta Maria, a new pickleball court was installed post-acquisition when a better use of that investment would have been for teacher-training programs, said an executive of the school.

Private equity’s priority for Indian schools: pickleball courts over teacher training

Indigo knows how to bookend a year. Its latest set of troubles, thanks to a shortage of pilots, has earned the airline its passenger’s wrath. But even early this year, it was plain to see just how its cost-cutting ways were putting off passengers, as Rounak found out.

Another unresolved refrain remains the size of cups. “We’ve brought it up several times—replace those tiny water cups with bottles,” said the cabin crew member. “The crew keeps running to refill those cups, adding to everyone’s work, fatigue, and eventually aggression. [But] nobody listens anymore.”

Indigo’s market value is soaring. So is passengers’ fury

On average, over 35 startups shut down every day in India. Employees move on to their next stint, but Suprita, after speaking to 17 founders, found out how most founders find themselves unemployed, thanks to their high pay and a leadership attitude.

“It’s harder to rebuild again and gain the trust of VCs,” a co-founder said. “Corporates do not really know what to do with failed founders. While building [a venture], you are seen as a hero. After failure, you are either invisible or treated like a scammer.”

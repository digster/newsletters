Long and Short
Wed, 22 Oct 25
The story behind the numbers and motivations driving India's stock market.
Good Morning Ishan,
At one point, every fintech in town wanted to be a non-bank and tried its best to get a non-bank license, because conventional wisdom said lending had the best margins.
But some NBFCs have figured out an even better business: becoming anchor investors in IPOs.
Usually, large mutual funds, insurance companies, and even pension funds are the institutions that feature on anchor investor lists. But there is a peculiar name that has shouldered its way into the list of top 25 IPO investors in India, according to Chittorgarh, an IPO data company.
Rajasthan Global Securities now shares this space with large mutual funds such as SBI Mutual Fund and Sundaram Mutual Fund—both highly sought-after anchor investors. So far, Rajasthan Global Securities has invested Rs 482 crore across 152 IPOs, investing an average of Rs 3 crore per issue. While it has mostly participated in SME IPOs, it has also received allotments in 25 mainboard IPOs, including Tata Capital, which was the country’s largest public listing in 2025.
And it’s not alone. The 2025 IPO wave is seeing plenty of non-banks—from Winro to Sachin Bansal’s Navi Finserv—pop up as anchor investors.
Anchor investors play a crucial role in IPOs. Investment bankers woo the top institutions, like mutual funds, to bid for an issue before it opens to retail investors. And it is anchor investors’ interest that becomes a lead indicator for retail investors to take a cue from. So companies filing for IPOs want top anchor investors on their side to showcase demand.
Current regulations are also designed in such a way that these investors stay for the long-term—50% of purchased shares have a 30-day lock-in, while the rest are frozen for 90 days from the time the shares are awarded.
Now, even though market regulator Sebi and the Reserve Bank of India (RBI) allow financial institutions like non-banks to be anchor investors, if you go through the list since 2007 (compiled by Chittorgarh), you’ll find none of the significant non-banks like Bajaj Finance or Cholamandalam Finance in it.
But smaller non-banks are not holding back. All they need is the capacity to invest at least Rs 10 crore in a mainboard IPO, as per Sebi’s rules.
Rajasthan Global has been in business since 1995 and has a non-bank license from the RBI. It uses this license to do some collateral-based lending to real estate developers, but only in the Delhi-NCR region. That too, concentrated among just 18 borrowers.
Even after all these years, though, its loan book was only Rs 424 crore as of December 2024, with no bad loans to boot, according to a credit rating report. But the company reported a profit after tax (PAT) of Rs 535.42 crore for the period. Its income grew 192% to Rs 870 crore in FY24 from a year ago, while its profit after tax grew 200% in the same period to Rs 689 crore.
It has been able to achieve this kind of profitability because of its main business: taking anchor allotments as a Qualified Institutional Buyer (QIB) in several IPOs, for both mainboard and SME listings. In fact, 95% of its gains and income are from the capital markets, according to the credit rating report, and it has been an anchor investor in over 100 SME IPOs and 25 mainboard ones.
Navi Finserv, too, seems to have taken to this money-spinning business, [investing](https://www.chittorgarh.net/reports/anchor-investor/tata-capital-anchor-list.pdf) Rs 20 crore in the Tata Capital IPO as an anchor investor, according to Chittorgarh’s data.
Some other non-banks are even going beyond their means to be a part of the 2025 IPO gold rush. In the recent mega LG Electronics IPO, Winro Commercial, which has a market cap of just around Rs 31 crore, bid for shares worth nearly Rs 749 crore, raising eyebrows among market players, according to a Times of India report.
For FY25, the company had earned a consolidated net profit of Rs 481 crore on a total income of Rs 440 crore. Market players said that such disproportionate bidding when compared to a bidder’s financials should always alert investors as the current IPO frenzy could end in heartbreaks
IPO Frenzy: NBFC with 31 crore mcap bids for Rs 749 crore of LG shares, [Times of India](https://timesofindia.indiatimes.com/business/india-business/ipo-frenzy-nbfc-with-rs-31-crore-mcap-bids-for-rs-749-crore-lg-shares/articleshow/124468281.cms)
While it’s logical that these types of non-banks are part of average IPOs that may find it difficult to find investors, what about the large listings? “The more blue-blooded companies will typically not have such investors as anchors,” explains the aforementioned investment banker.
Also, bankers may be using large IPOs as a reward for these non-banks—as an encouragement to become anchors in the smaller issues. “There may be an arrangement between promoters, bankers, and these NBFCs that if you support me here, I won’t leave you high and dry,” said the veteran banker.
While this is a great opportunity for these non-banks to earn a quick (and good) buck, it speaks volumes about their ability as a lender.

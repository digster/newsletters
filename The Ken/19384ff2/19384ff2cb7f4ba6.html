Good Morning Ishan,

You are reading our subscriber-only newsletter Ed Set Go—a weekly commentary on Indian edtech and education

Imagine, for a second, that you’re an Indian student. You’ve put in years of hard, bone-crushing prep to ace your exams. You’ve scored well, but it’s not enough to get you into a good college in India. The competition has edged you out by a fraction of a grade. But you’re not worried. You were never going to duke it out here anyway. You have plans to study abroad in the US. 

And you know that you aren’t alone—in November, a report released by the US government showed that Indian students now make up the largest share of foreign students in the US. India even knocked China down the brain-drain list.

There’s only one problem, though. The US now has a new president, and guessing his attitude towards foreigners, on any given day, is like trying to predict the weather.

Maybe you were too young in 2017 to register the chaos that ensued when Donald Trump took office for the first time. Proposals to limit H1-B visas and the Optional Practical Training, a programme that allows students to work for a few years in the US after they graduate, scared the Indian community. The number of H1-B petition denials went up from 3.2% to 18%, according to one estimate.

Those fears have now returned, despite Trump’s campaign promise of giving all graduates an automatic route to receiving a green card.

“The Trump administration would likely emphasise an ‘America first’ approach, promoting domestic growth, controlling inflation, and encouraging re-shoring and industrialisation. The election outcome will significantly impact the regulatory landscape for technology companies in critical areas like artificial intelligence (AI), machine learning, data privacy, and antitrust laws,” said Sachin Alug, CEO of talent solutions company NLB Services.

It’s not just students who’re worried about getting in. Universities like MIT and the University of Massachusetts in Amherst have also issued advisories to their foreign students to be in the US by January 19, the day before Trump is officially sworn in. They anticipate that things could change quickly because the new administration has threatened to pass a slew of executive orders on day one. 

Reading all of this commentary made me think back to my conversation with an Indian edtech entrepreneur in DC a few weeks ago. He heads a company that’s created an end-to-end process for Indian students who want to study abroad. We were right in the throes of the election result, but unlike students back home, and universities in the US, he was quite chipper.

You see, the geopolitical tension between Canada and India is taking a toll on all study-abroad businesses. Even if Canadian universities comprised only a small part of your overall business, the tension between the two countries only underscored the uneasy and unpredictable nature of the sector.

So Indian study-abroad businesses are now pressing on the US’ doors for their clients. They think that, despite Trump’s unpredictability, the closed doors to Canada may convince more Indian students to seek opportunities in the neighboring nation—especially one where college attendance rates, in the local population, have been steadily declining. 

“The atmosphere” among lobbying circles in Washington DC is like “the surge of enthusiasm when Bill Clinton was elected,” the entrepreneur told me.

If this US sojourn doesn’t pan out, many of these companies have also made back-up plans. In addition to Indian students, they offer the same services to students in Southeast Asian and African countries. That way, they aren’t completely dependent on any one country’s changing diplomatic ties with nations.

The companies have derisked themselves, at least partially. The students though? They’re in limbo:

“I knew the elections were going to happen in November 2024, so I delayed my plans to go to the US. The visa processing time is enormous right now, so I opted for a gap year. I will now wait until Trump takes over the White House in January and apply in September 2025, depending on how friendly he is towards immigrants in his second term,” said a 23-year-old from Delhi.

Last month, the Indian government released a new app aimed to train school teachers. Called the TeacherApp, it purports to be a one-stop solution for all that a teacher might need to “meet the demands of a 21st century classroom.”

The app, created by the Bharti Airtel Foundation, was launched by the education minister last month and it offers over 76 upskilling courses, expert webinars, and what seems to be an interactive learning interface.

The launch of the app raises two questions for me:

1. This is a great idea for a ground-up edtech startup. Just like numerous doubt-solving apps popped up for students during the pandemic, an online, on-demand guide for teachers seems like just the kind of app that has the potential to go viral.

2. Whatever happened to DIKSHA? The platform was supposed to be the ultimate open resource for student and teacher learning. During the pandemic, it became a go-to for many school teachers. But maybe the massive data breach of DIKSHA’s servers cooled the government’s attitude towards it:

“Files stored on the unsecured server contained the full names, phone numbers, and email addresses of more than 1 million teachers. According to data in the files, verified by WIRED, the teachers worked for hundreds of thousands of schools located in every state in India. Another file contained information about nearly 600,000 students. While the students’ email addresses and phone numbers were partially obscured, the data included the students’ full names and information about where they went to school, when they enrolled in a course through the app, and how much of the course they completed.”

Cheers!

Since numbers speak louder than words, here’s how the late 2019-early 2020 layoffs in the unicorn-soonicorn club of India stack up: Paytm*: 600; Zomato: 540; Ola: 300; Urban Ladder: 246; 1mg: 300; Rivigo: 100…
Most of these layoffs have been at entry- and mid-level positions, and in companies that have a large offline component.
Founders and senior execs of some of these companies admit that hiring had become the solution for anything they wanted to do: “If you want to gain more market share, you hire. If the competitor is about to raise money, you hire more.”
Such indiscriminate expansion often brings growth on paper, but doesn’t materially improve the capabilities of a company. For instance, Paytm, say its former executives, has about 6,000 employees but could easily be operating with half that capacity.
Now that the axe is falling on employees, it’s doubly ironic that it doesn’t do much to achieve the new Holy Grail—profitability. Because the top 50 executives account for more expense than mid-to-bottom 1,000 employees. (It’s true for just one company, and is an overgeneralization here, but it makes the point.)
Churn is par for the course in the VC-funded startup world. Hire for growth, fire to sustain. We saw the cycle not too long ago. Is 2020 different?
Companies like Ola, Oyo, and Zomato, tech companies whose very existence is based on using tech to reduce operational costs, grew by adding people to solve a problem rather than creating tech processes around it. Some are correcting that. Zomato, for instance, laid off 540 people to automate its customer support.
We may lament the gaucherie of this growth model but it’s five years of cumulative mistakes manifesting at the macro level.

On a rainy Thursday evening, I met up with an old friend from India’s edtech scene. He was in the US to find ways to expand his business here, and was generally chuffed at the reception and interest he was getting from American educational institutions.

As we got up to leave, I asked him something that’s been on my mind for months: who’s the “edtech” funder in India? Who still has an “edtech thesis”?

He thought for a while before he ruefully shook his head. “I can’t think of anyone,” he answered. All the VCs who had developed a rationale to invest in edtech during the pandemic have long since wrapped up their bets. The ensuing carnage in the edtech sector hardly recommends it to anyone anymore.

So, I was surprised to see a new-ish investment last week in maths edtech startup Bhanzu, which raised US$16 million in a round led by Epiq, a late-stage growth fund.

At first glance, Bhanzu looks like a platform that helps young kids understand basic mathematical concepts. It seems to have all the hallmarks of an online learning tool—leaderboards, game-based learning, and an AI doubt-solving bot called Bhanzu Buddy.

Bhanzu launched its operations in 2020, and among its earliest backers were Kunal Shah and stand-up comedian Biswa Kalyan Rath. Starting the same year as the pandemic could have given Bhanzu the early lift it needed to get into homes and onto screens.

But what’s the “thesis” behind the second round of funding? Is it the man, the math, or the machine?

Star founder redux

Much of Bhanzu’s allure comes from one of its co-founders, and inarguably the face of the product. Neelakantha Bhanu Prakash, the “fastest human calculator in the world”, wants people to know that he’s a pro at mental maths. He won India its first-ever gold in the Mental Calculation World Championship, and he never lets anyone forget it. On Bhanzu’s website, an endorsement from the BBC— “Bhanu is to mathematics what Usain Bolt is to running”—is front and centre.

The startup seems to be moulded around the founder’s fame, because his personal branding and his face is on every video published by the site. The advertisements for Bhanzu also talk about how quickly Bhanu calculates, how skilled he is at maths, and how he wants to change the anxious relationship that students have with maths. The intention is noble: to help them overcome “maths anxiety” by teaching them a combination of basics and shortcuts to start doing well in the subject.

The investment thesis doesn’t seem to have changed much. The famous founder pulls in the sales leads, which are used by a sales team to convince parents to buy the product. This is the Byju’s or Physics Wallah playbook—the success and the failure of the venture is heavily attached to the integrity of the founder.

Foreign markets and the desire to crack the US

Indian edtechs seem convinced that India’s a saturated market. The overlap is slight between school, traditional tuition, test prep, and the parents that can pay a premium for an expensive online solution.

Bhanzu wants to play in the same field—its products are targeted towards Indian-origin parents in countries like the US and Australia who want their kids to excel in maths.

A one-hour Bhanzu class can cost between US$25 and US$30, and there are three categories of courses to choose from—the beginner level offers 30 sessions while the most advanced level is 150-sessions long. Bhanzu has the potential to earn anything between US$750 to US$4,500 from a student.

The question is, are parents paying?

Customer reviews for Bhanzu are the usual mix https://www.mouthshut.com/review/bhanzu-review-ptuprsrsmnp of “great teacher, bad customer service” type of feedback. One parent, from Sydney, seems to have posted https://www.expat.com/forum/viewtopic.php?id=1036574 their experience across review platforms:

There are just as many glowing testimonials about Bhanzu’s style of teaching, but what seems to emerge is the possibility that Bhanzu is struggling with the gap it’s trying to fill: is it a maths program for students who are already good at the subject but want to be exceptional? Or is it a more guided study programme that tutors students on their day-to-day maths challenges? 

In other words, is Bhanzu trying to create more Bhanus? Or is it trying to help you pass your exams?

In VC-speak, this is a non-trivial problem, and important to solve before Bhanzu overpromises and underdelivers.

Ka-Ching!
Thu, 13 Nov 25
A weekly newsletter about how finance is getting supercharged by tech in India, and how you can make money work for you.
Good Morning Ishan,
During a Youtube “Ask Me Anything” session in October, Zerodha’s* founder and CEO Nithin Kamath announced that the discount broker would start facilitating direct investments in US stocks via Gujarat’s Gift City by early 2026. And with this, it became the latest to join the Gift City bandwagon.
Apart from brokers, mutual funds too are jumping in thick and fast. DSP Mutual Fund made its Gift City debut with the actively managed offshore retail fund DSP Global Equity Fund in June, while Parag Parikh Mutual Fund is set to [launch](https://www.financialexpress.com/business/investing-abroad-ppfas-mutual-fund-to-launch-global-fund-of-funds-and-india-flexi-cap-fund-from-gift-city-4030274/) three new overseas Fund of Funds based out of the financial and technology hub. Even Tata Asset Management has [launched](https://www.business-standard.com/markets/news/tata-amc-india-dynamic-equity-fund-gift-city-ifsca-approval-mutual-fund-125091700598_1.html) a Dynamic Equity Fund and Mirae Asset Mutual Fund is planning to [launch](https://www.outlookmoney.com/news/mirae-tata-asset-gear-up-for-retail-fund-launches-at-gift-city-to-tap-global-themes) multiple retail mutual fund schemes based out of Gift City.
Why the rush?
Investment, like nature, abhors a vacuum. And the easier, more straightforward way to invest abroad—via domestic mutual funds that invest in international stocks—has been shut since 2022, when the Reserve Bank of India’s mutual funds’ overseas investment limit of $7 billion was [breached](https://the-ken.com/story/rbis-7-billion-damper-on-indias-overseas-investments/). Domestic funds offering international schemes stopped accepting fresh lump sums and even overseas exchange-traded funds, with a separate $1 billion limit, [closed](https://the-ken.com/kaching/the-mutual-fund-door-to-foreign-investments-just-got-shut-tighter/) the door.
The Ken had [written](https://the-ken.com/story/rbis-7-billion-damper-on-indias-overseas-investments/) about this back in 2022:
But three years later, the limit is still the same, in spite of India having huge forex reserves to the tune of around [$700 billion](https://www.moneycontrol.com/news/business/markets/mutual-fund-industry-seeks-increase-in-overall-overseas-limit-13166556.html). Fund houses’ patience has run out. “There’s no point waiting. We need to move on,” said a senior mutual fund executive who did not want to be named.
No wonder they’re flocking to Gift City. It’s just the market—supply meeting demand. And there’s big demand for foreign stocks, what with the dream AI-fuelled rally on the US bourses. Just that it’s a rich-only invite, a premium club.
While domestic mutual funds investing abroad have to answer to market regulator Sebi, the Gujarat International Finance-Tec-City is a different animal. One of its key features is the International Financial Services Centre (IFSCA), under which financial transactions are—for all regulatory and tax purposes—treated as if they’re being carried out in foreign territory. So, all mutual funds offering schemes out of Gift City are covered under IFSCA, exempting them from RBI’s $7 billion overseas investment limit.
But there’s a catch: funds based in Gift City tend to cater to the moneyed folk, those who can pony up a minimum $5,000 investment (Rs 4.5 lakh). This high watermark, at least in the Indian context, keeps away the hoi polloi who could earlier invest as little as Rs 500–1,000 through SIPs in domestic mutual funds investing in foreign stocks.
This begs the question, why has the government opened a Gift City window for the rich, when the domestic mutual fund door remains tightly shut for the common man?
Indian residents can park up to $250,000 a year in offshore investments under the Liberalised Remittance Scheme (LRS).
Essentially, the LRS is an RBI-established gateway that allows Indians to send funds outside the country for a variety of purposes—education, medical treatment, investment—without seeking RBI approval for every transaction. All international investments through the Gift City route fall under the LRS.
But here’s the simple, if unfair, math: even if just 28,000 investors put in the maximum of $250,000 each under LRS via the Gift City route, it will equate to the $7 billion limit imposed on mutual funds. And with the minimum $5,000 investment, just 1.4 million investors through the Gift City route would have breached the $7 billion mark. In contrast, India has over 55 million mutual fund investors—the majority of whom are now being deprived of the right to invest in stocks abroad.
How is this fair?
“Most available funds in India are US-oriented, especially NASDAQ-focused,” said Dhilip Krishna, a registered investment advisor. “But now, many of them are closed or have fluctuating limits, making life difficult for investors.”
The result: if you’re a domestic mutual fund investor, getting in on a company like Nvidia—whose stock has been on a rocket over the past few years—is now close to impossible. Using international investing as a hedge against the depreciation of the Indian rupee is out too.
Instead, the only ones who can buy into the action are those who can afford an investment of $5,000 (Rs 4.5 lakh)—the minimum ticket size for DSP’s Global Equity Fund. Even Mirae Asset Mutual Fund’s upcoming Gift-City scheme is likely to have a minimum ticket size of either $5,000 or an even higher $10,000.
Sure, the small investor can also use the LRS route to invest directly in foreign stocks and funds through brokers and service providers who enable this. But direct investing is riskier compared with investing through a professional fund manager. “With mutual funds, one gets the benefit of investing with experienced professional fund managers, so the experience of investing beyond our borders should be better compared to people doing this themselves,” a mutual fund CEO had [said](https://the-ken.com/kaching/the-mutual-fund-door-to-foreign-investments-just-got-shut-tighter/) in an earlier edition of Ka-Ching!
For Gujarat’s smart city, once home to select non-retail products such as hedge funds and alternative investment vehicles, this is undoubtedly a win. For the country’s aam aadmi investor, not so much.
“The LRS route is far tougher and is basically for people who are at a much higher income level,” said Suresh Sadagopan, founder of Ladder7 Wealth Planners, a financial-planning platform. “The government is, in a sense, restricting who can invest in these kinds of offshore products.”
The powers-that-be seem bent on consolidating overseas investment activity under Gift City. No quarrels with promoting India’s own offshore investment hub. But the real gripe is that it comes at the expense of the easier, more widely accessible route. Common investors are bumping up against a closed border, while the rich can globetrot freely.

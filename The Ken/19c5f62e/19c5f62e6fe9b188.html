Gen Z’s hunt for the elusive ‘cheap’ home of their own

Faced with rising prices and uncertain job prospects in the age of AI, young Indians feel renting is wiser. But they aren’t giving up on the home-ownership dream yet

India’s housing market is flush with premium and luxury units, with steep ticket prices becoming the norm. For Gen Z, who are keen to buy, the unavailability of homes within their preferred budget is a problem. While developers claim a demand slowdown, Gen Zs believe the market, as it is, doesn’t cater to them. Crashes reveal the hidden costs of India’s private-jet boom

Maharashtra Deputy CM Ajit Pawar’s death in the Baramati crash brings fresh attention to a fast-growing sector, where accidents expose risk, but rarely dent demand

Non-scheduled aviation operators or private charters account for the second-highest number of air accidents in India. Yet, politicians and the ultra-rich rely on them, thanks to their advantage of speed and access over commercial flights. But the crash of Learjet 45, a private jet operated by VSR Ventures, which killed a top politician, has laid bare the reality of this industry. In the gap between operators’ race to meet urgency and the regulator’s years-long probes, accountability weakens. [Read the full story]

Uber knocks at a new door as Rapido shuts many others

Fixed-schedule corporate travel requires a different muscle from gigwork-based ride-hailing—unless Uber can cross-pollinate the two

After years of building a strong on-demand ride-hailing DNA, Uber is venturing into the fixed-schedule employee-transportation services (ETS). It’s facing headwinds from competitors, and looking to tap into the fast-growing $6 billion B2B market. Uber is differentiating itself in small but significant ways—allowing drivers to switch to B2C and investing heavily in operations with its deep pockets. But on-ground realities for B2B transport will be a steep learning curve for Uber, before it catches up to veteran players. [Read the full story]

Voice AI has gone from whisper to commotion. Can the market get any louder?

Startups in the sector have raised Rs 280 crore in the last year, but many may not be around in a few years

Voice-AI startups in India have raised over Rs 280 crore in funding by early 2026. Banks, e-commerce, healthtech, and edtech platforms have begun using AI callers in live operations, swapping it in for calling teams and BPO contracts. Most deployments still involve multiple iterations, including pilots, script tuning, and language adaption, before scaling. Founders and investors broadly expect the market to consolidate, with smaller voice-AI startups eventually being absorbed by bigger platforms and enterprises. [Read the full story]

Early-stage deals are falling even as total AI capital rises. From residencies to sharper underwriting thesis, investors are intervening earlier to shape companies before they scale

VCs are being more picky about what a “fundable” AI is in 2026. The funding in India’s AI space continues to narrow, especially for native-AI. Being a wrapper alone isn’t enough anymore; to be successful, a startup has to have more than just an AI integration. Some VCs are deciding to step in from day one, running residency programs and offering mentorship to founders. [Read the full story]

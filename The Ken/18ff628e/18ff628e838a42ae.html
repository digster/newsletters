Good Morning Ishan,

So, here’s a story about something that happened last week.

E-commerce giant Amazon reportedly has appointed one of the Big Four accounting firms to perform the due diligence procedure to buy MX Player. The process might take 30-40 days to get completed, sources told Mint.

We earlier reported that Amazon was in advanced discussions with Times Internet to acquire its video streaming platform MX Player.

After the due diligence report, if everything goes well, Amazon would acquire MX Player for about INR 600-INR 900 Cr in the next two months.

“Earlier, Times Internet was asking for over $100 Mn for MX Player, while Amazon’s internal team valued it at around INR 500 Cr ($60 Mn). The deal is likely to be in the range of INR 600-900 Cr,” a source told the publication.

Amazon Starts Due Diligence To Buy MX Player For About INR 900 Cr, Inc42

Okay, let me confess. I lied. 

That news report isn’t from last week, but from March 2023. 

Here’s the actual report from last week.

Amazon has agreed to acquire MX Player as part of its strategy to expand its portfolio in India’s thriving market for video streaming services. MX Player will add to Amazon’s existing video services like Prime Video and MiniTV. The US technology giant is expected to retain the MX Player brand post the acquisition.

“We are always looking for ways to introduce new products and services that help improve customers’ lives. We’re excited to continue to entertain India with the great local originals and exclusive content available across our Prime Video and MiniTV services in India,” said a spokesperson for Amazon India.

The deal concluded on Wednesday, people in the know said, valuing MX Player at $50-100 million.

Amazon acquires video streaming platform MX Player, ET Tech

There’s a lot going on here that’s quite striking. 

And I know what you are thinking: did Amazon take 15 months to finish something they initially expected would take “30-40 days”? Of course not—this isn’t a flyover at Koramangala in Bengaluru. Amazon wanted to acquire MX Player early last year but by July 2023, talks had broken down and according to some reports, everybody walked away. The exact reasons for the failed negotiations are unclear, but more on this later.

The more interesting part here is MX Player’s valuation itself, which appears to be unchanged from last year. Note that the valuation is presented as a pretty wide range both in 2023 (Rs 600–900 crore, or US$72–108 million) and in 2024 (US$50–100 million). If you do the conversion, it broadly looks like MX Player’s valuation has remained the same over the last year or so. Also, the fact that nobody seems to want to tell us the exact valuation is a sign that it’s probably on the lower end. In fact, at least one publication has characterised the deal as a “distress sale” and suggested that Amazon has valued MX Player at US$50 million. 

It really looks like Amazon got the better end of the deal? 

One year ago, Amazon valued MX Player at US$60 million. Times Internet probably said, “No, this is much more valuable”. Amazon said, “Give us a final price”. Times Internet named a number. Amazon said, “I’m walking”. Times Internet probably thought, “This is a bluff. They’ll be back.” They didn’t. And well, 15 months later, it looks like Amazon finally got what they wanted at the price they originally wanted. 

The reason why I find all of this interesting is because clearly Amazon wanted MX Player last year, but they didn’t want it that badly. They were prepared to wait it out, postponing whatever plans they had for MX Player, likely because of the difference in valuation. Surely just the opportunity cost of waiting 15 months would have cost Amazon much more than the difference itself? Was it truly worth it? It’s not like Amazon couldn’t afford to pay for MX Player last year. This is Amazon. They probably have a spare US$50 million lying around on the footer of their website or something.

This is all the more interesting if you consider how much India’s streaming world changed in the period between Amazon walking away from MX Player last year and last week:

  • First, Jio acquired Disney+Hotstar and became India’s largest and most powerful OTT company. I’ve had some fairly controversial views about Hotstar over the last couple of years, and just a few months ago, I wrote that we’re all in Jio’s world now. The streaming wars are over, and Jio has won. 
  • Second, Netflix finally rediscovered its mojo in India. I’ve also been fairly critical about Netflix in the past, but last year, they finally figured it out. I wrote about how they laddered down creatively with the right product and price combination that unlocked distribution for them. They appear to have learnt from Hotstar’s mistakes and seem to be on an upward trajectory in India. 
  • And finally, around May 2023, the Times Group’s promoters finalised a settlement to split the media conglomerate’s empire between them. This is crucial, because the Times Group owns Times Internet, which owns MX Player. It’s also possible that this may have been a reason why Amazon’s deal for MX Player was stuck in cold storage for so long.

Simply put, a lot happened in the last fifteen months. I’m not even getting into regulatory things like the Telecom Act, which came into effect, and has implications for OTT platforms in India.

And yet, somehow, Amazon stuck to what it was doing, with seemingly little urgency, playing it cool with MX Player. Anyone else would have panicked and second-guessed their actions, and tried to do something… anything. I can easily imagine Amazon going back to Times Internet and shelling out some extra cash to close the deal quickly to get their hands on MX Player, but they didn’t. Not while Jio was acquiring Disney+Hotstar. Not while Netflix found itself. And not when the Times Group was being split. 

Which essentially brings me to the question—why? 

I’m not sure, but my best guess is that Amazon really didn’t want MX Player that badly. It wasn’t a negotiating tactic. It was just a reality for them. One reason might be because they had already figured out what they wanted to do and decided to stick to it, i.e., mini TV. 

So a quick word about mini TV. Simply put, this is Amazon’s ‘free’ OTT product that’s completely supported by advertising. And it’s not a separate platform—it’s embedded within the Amazon shopping app. You can check it out for yourself. If you open it, you’ll find shows, series, and everything else in there, with non-skippable ads. Oh, and occasionally, Amazon will show you products you can buy on the app. You’re watching a show, and right in the middle, a video ad shows up for, say, a ceiling fan. The product page for the fan is displayed right below the player, and you can make a purchase right there on Amazon.

So the reason why this is interesting is because Amazon appears to have figured out something a ton of people have tried and failed—commerce advertising.

In many ways, commerce ads are the holy grail of advertising. 

Normally, there are two types of digital ads—performance and brand. Performance ads ask users to do something, like download an app. Brand advertising shows you something with the hope that you remember it and have a positive association. Both have their strengths and weaknesses. Performance ads have a clear and measurable ROI—but advertisers usually struggle to understand if the app has been downloaded by the “right” users. Maybe users download an app because it costs them nothing, but then they do nothing afterwards because they don’t have any money to spend. In brand advertising, it’s the other way around—you can show an ad to the exact target audience you have in mind—say, rich, affluent people, but you can’t tell if or when they take action. 

Commerce advertising solves both of these problems. You can show something to someone, usually a product. And the action you’re expecting from them is to buy it—right there without leaving the app. If they do it, you can take a cut at the point where the money is exchanged. If they don’t, you can change who you show it to, and learn from it. In many ways, commerce advertising is the best of both worlds. 

It’s also incredibly difficult to achieve. For years and years, multiple ad-tech companies have taken a shot at commerce advertising, and nearly all of them have failed. Facebook launched it first in 2014—calling it the ‘buy button’. It didn’t work. Twitter tried it out too, and eventually wound it down by 2017. 

Commerce advertising isn’t just abstract for me—it’s personal too. When I worked as a Product Manager at Inmobi, my first product was to build out commerce advertising. Just like Facebook and Twitter. We took multiple attempts at it and tried all kinds of things, but it simply didn’t work. 

The reasons why commerce advertising doesn’t work is a much longer topic, but the short version is that users simply don’t work like that—they don’t watch things and then see something and then decide to buy it then and there. Plus, the harder problem is the integration itself—the notion that an ad-network, like Facebook, Twitter, or Inmobi, could seamlessly integrate the catalogues of multiple merchants with a dedicated cart and payment system within their app itself was somewhat fanciful. 

Of course, that’s what we all believed. 

Until one company came along and showed us how it’s done.

Instagram. 

Essentially, Instagram proved that if you want to create commerce advertising, you need to begin as a ‘content’ app. And if you did that, then the brands would come and create content alongside other users. And finally, you need to make the ads ‘native’, i.e., they should look and feel like regular content, which users would see and follow to discover products. And then, they should be able to buy these products without having to leave the app itself. Facebook, Twitter, Youtube, and others like Inmobi did all of these to varying degrees, but nobody got all the ingredients down perfectly like Instagram did.

And now, it looks like Amazon might be next. 

In some ways, Amazon is coming at it from the other direction. Instagram started off as a content app that integrated commerce into it. Amazon is a commerce app that’s integrating content. I see no reason why one approach is better than the other, but the one thing that Amazon needs to make it work isn’t products—it’s addictive content, something that Instagram has in spades.

And I believe that’s where MX Player comes in. 

The one difference between stories that are reporting Amazon’s acquisition of MX Player this time around versus last year is that this time, everyone is careful to say that Amazon has acquired “some assets of MX Player” and not all of it. I think this distinction is important, because it suggests that Amazon is more interested in specific parts of the app. Sounds like all the content to me. Not the users. 

I’ll end with a quote from Amazon’s spokesperson when they were asked about the deal. 

“We are always looking for ways to introduce new products and services that help improve customers’ lives. We’re excited to continue to entertain India with the great local originals and exclusive content available across our Prime Video and miniTV services in India.”

It’s still early days, but Amazon probably knows something about how to make commerce advertising work for them.

And MX Player is going to make that a little bit stronger.

My first proper weekend of 2026 was spent dusting off the bookshelf. And as timing would have it, I landed on a book about a company that seems to manufacture executives who become CEOs—at companies ranging from Godrej Consumer to Chanel. 

For those who couldn’t guess, the book is Sudhir Sitapati’s CEO Factory. And the company, Hindustan Unilever. 

Incidentally, it offered new lessons for me that explain the changes afoot at India’s largest, but currently wounded, value retailer, Dmart. 

This weekend, on 10 January, Dmart published its financials. But along with the numbers, the company also snuck in an announcement of a change in management—one expected, and one not. 

Last year, we covered how Navil Noronha was going to step down, leaving behind a sterling legacy. And now, exactly a year later, the CEO-designate Anshul Asawa is taking his place, with 1 February as his start date. He comes after having spent three decades at Unilever—the same place Noronha came from in 2004, before becoming Dmart’s CEO in 2007.

While this was expected, the surprise lies in the changes Asawa is bringing to the senior management. A press release said that new positions have been created under “key managerial personnel”, essentially the company’s top leadership that reports directly to the CEO:

Sachin Jaolekar, now the VP of FMCG, comes in from his earlier designation of VP of buying and merchandising. 

His role within the retailer would now become wider, and he would now be taking ownership of the entire “P&L of the FMCG vertical”, explained Akshay D’Souza, an independent FMCG consultant with over 24 years of experience. 

From staples to shampoos, fruits and vegetables to detergents, Jaolekar, in the new role, will be responsible for handling the sourcing, product pricing, and even how they are displayed at over 440 Dmart stores.

So from an organisational standpoint, the role would not just be about looking at sales but the entire category, with the goal of driving profitability, D’Souza added.

Similarly, Dastgir Shaikh, who was earlier the category head of general merchandising, will now be promoted to vice president of this category.

This structure is different from that of a typical retailer in India, where you have C-level executives like CEO, CFO, and COO on top, followed by Chief Business Officers (CBO) for different categories like FMCG, apparel, and general merchandising (the top three core categories for a typical retailer). Just below the C-suite executives are the Vice President (VP) roles. 

Take the example of Vishal Mega Mart, another value retailer, though smaller in size than Dmart. They have a CEO, CFO, and COO, followed by CBOs for different verticals like apparel and FMCG.

Dmart, however, has now departed from its earlier hierarchy, which had a “layered management” with multiple regional heads serving as COOs, to a “leaner leadership”, according to Livemint’s reporting. Where typically CBOs would be responsible for their vertical’s P&L, Dmart has now brought in VPs reporting directly to Asawa. Although the designation is technically lower, the responsibility is much, much higher.

This instantly reminded me of a quote by Hindustan Unilever’s former CEO, Sanjeev Mehta, who is mentioned in Sitapati’s book: “Pay six people the salary of eight people, and get them to do the job of ten people.”

Is that what Asawa, also a product of HUL, wants to replicate?

After all, the common thread between the new Dmart CEO and his two VPs is also their past experience with the Unilever group.

And D’Souza affirms that it seems like Dmart is trying to replicate the famous “HUL playbook” of giving “greater responsibility and accountability” to leaders. Essentially, to be the CEOs of their own vertical. And, with the heads of different categories directly reporting to the CEO, it makes room for quicker decision-making. 

This is especially important at a time when Dmart is facing a double whammy, with pressure building from quick commerce on one side and rising competition from other retailers like Reliance Retail and Vishal Mega Mart on the other. 

The wobbly giant

In top metros, the pandemic changed consumer behaviour, with doorstep grocery delivery displacing affordable value purchases from say their nearby Dmart.

“But the strongest criticism of Noronha’s stint is that he was perhaps too sanguine about Dmart’s resistance to the e-commerce threat.

…

In the initial years after Avenue’s listing, Noronha managed to parry analysts’ questions about the company’s lukewarm approach to e-commerce. Sure, online-grocery shopping was growing, but no one was close to making money. And Dmart was quite good at constantly becoming a better version of its frugal, highly profitable self. 

So investors thought Dmart knew something they didn’t. But quick commerce’s emergence against all odds has made them rethink that assumption. Dmart, too, knows that it needs to act. 

Navil Noronha’s sterling legacy at Dmart hangs in the balance, The Ken

In the last 10 years, while Dmart’s sales grew at a compound rate of 25%, in the last five, it has slowed down to 19%, according to data from the financial data platform Screener. 

And with Vishal Mega Mart recently listing on the market, and Reliance Retail looking to go public in 2028, while expanding on quick commerce through Jiomart, the ever-present competition among big-box retailers has turned aggressive.

In the latest financial report, the third-quarter results showed a fifth consecutive decline in quarterly like-for-like (LFL) growth. The metric measures sales growth in the same store compared to the previous year. LFL sales growth in Q3FY2026 stood at 5.6%, lower than last year’s LFL of 8.3% in the same quarter. 

To put it simply, this is Dmart’s attempt at readying itself for the next leg of growth. As the baton of India’s largest value retailer gets passed on from one HUL veteran (Noronha) to another HUL veteran (Asawa), one will have to see how the retailer grows in this increasingly challenging environment.

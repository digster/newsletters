Good Morning Ishan,
Most forms of public infrastructure have clear benefits for broader society. Renewable energy reduces carbon emissions. Advancements in communication networks give us stabler internet connections. E-service portals turn long waits and cumbersome procedures in government offices into online forms that can be completed at our own pace.
When it comes to facial-recognition technology (FRT), however, few people champion its implementation, yet it’s a fact of life now. Stroll around any major city, and it’s easy to lose count of how many times your visage is captured in a day. That number is only going up.
That means FRT providers and system integrators are making bank. They fulfil large government tenders worth up to Rs 500 crore, which function as endorsements to lure in private-sector clients willing to shell out 4X compared to their public counterparts.
Consider India’s Smart Cities Mission. The decade-long initiative has funnelled more than Rs 1.64 lakh crore toward companies that supply infrastructural hardware and software, including FRT systems. Contracts like these have given FRT providers the visibility and credibility they need to woo enterprise customers, where larger profits can be gleaned.
That means the FRT companies that slogged it out to satisfy the needs of entities like Indian Railways are set to reap major benefits as they engage the private sector.
In today’s story, Abhirami captures how the FRT business works and how companies in this space will evolve in the coming years. Check out her [coverage](https://the-ken.com/story/how-facial-recognition-firms-turn-public-deals-into-private-gold/).
In this week’s Long and Short, Seetharaman analyses why State Bank of India, the country’s largest lender, wants to drive home the narrative of accumulating deposits through its latest scheme, Har Ghar Lakhpati.
In the 11 years to March 2023, the share of deposits in household assets has fallen prey to investments in the stock market and funds. And why wouldn’t it? After all, while a term deposit may offer up to 7.5% returns annually, Nifty 50 has given more than double that in the past year.
Can SBI woo customers to put their money into deposits, or is it just a hopeless attempt at repairing something that’s already broken?

India is an unpredictable country. In some areas, it completely “skips” trends that developed countries have gone through. Fixed telephones (winner: Airtel). Wired broadband (winner: Reliance Jio). Big box retail (winner: Flipkart+Walmart?). 
 
But in others, it follows developed countries in a fairly predictable manner. Cable and Satellite TV. Junk food. Yoghurt.
 
That’s right. Yoghurt. India is going through the early stages of a yoghurt revolution. The same revolution that allowed an upstart Turkish immigrant to start selling Greek-style yoghurt in the US in 2007, and by 2017, to grow sales to over $1.5 billion. Yup, Chobani.
 
As Indians get richer and more urbanised, they’re shifting from making curd at home (two thirds still do) to buying yoghurt from stores. The market for Greek yoghurt, though still small, is growing at 250% annually.
 
The increasing awareness and purchasing power of the middle class have translated into a rush for protein, nutrition and digestive aids. Apart from being tasty, it also aids in digestion, making it an ideal snack, especially in summers. As the world’s largest vegetarian population clamours for protein, India’s dietary predisposition to dahi—and by extension, yoghurt—has primed the yoghurt industry for takeoff. 
 
In our fridge at home, you’ll find greek yoghurt, probiotic yoghurt, lactose-free “artisanal” yoghurt (!) and home-made curd.
 
Which brings me to Ruhi’s story today. It’s about the acquisition of a roughly football field-sized dairy facility on the outskirts of Delhi by the homegrown dairy company Parag Foods. But the seller was Danone, the €25 billion French packaged foods giant Danone.
 
Why did Danone sell its only facility in India? Why did Parag buy it? And what does that mean for food startups like Epigamia and Raw Pressery.

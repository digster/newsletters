After the revenue collapse, a cost reset

Rohin

India has been locked down for nearly two months now. What that has delivered is an unprecedented collapse of revenue for most businesses. With India’s GDP forecasted to decline by as much as 5% this year, most entrepreneurs have given up on the growth estimates. Survival is the new black.

If revenue collapse was a lead indicator of Covid-19, a cost reset is a lag indicator. Line item by line item, India’s tech startups are going after their costs.

For most founders, the top three cost items are employee salaries, office rentals, and software and hosting expenses. In most cases, both the absolute and relative amounts spent on these heads were “givens” from what seemed like a different era. Not the price of a full-stack developer or a 10,000 square-feet office in a buzzing suburb or a software subscription, but their worth. Hint: they’re all worth what someone is willing to pay.

If the Covid-19 revenue collapse started with customer’s unwillingness to pay, it has to transmit to a business’ inability to pay. A revenue collapse has to end up as a cost reset for most businesses. It’s math.

The layoffs from ambitious, storied, and well-capitalised companies like WeWork, Swiggy, Zomato, and Cure.fit are just the beginning. Tellingly, the Indian government has withdrawn its meaningless order mandating full salaries to employees even during the lockdown. 

Companies of all sizes are renegotiating their rentals. Since most of them have just spent the last two months working remotely, they are perfectly capable of walking away from their existing offices if landlords refuse to budge. Landlords will have no option but to blink first.
Co-working companies know that their landlords will be worse off without them—the demand for office space in India is set to decline 15-20% in 2020 thanks to the impact of Covid, according to JLL.

“If you throw me out, you are not going to find anyone for a while. And then if you do find someone, you have to give them three months free and also pay two months’ brokerage,” says the CEO of a large co-working company who does not want to be identified as he is negotiating rental terms with his landlords.
Quarantine capitalism 

Arundhati
 
To know how businesses that were dealt a body blow will react when presented with a sliver of opportunity to make money, Karnataka has a show-and-tell in progress now. Hotels and transport companies that were hit by coronavirus travel restrictions are now buzzing with activity. 
 
Karnataka has mandated that anyone coming into the state needs to be quarantined for 14 days. But in a hotel and on their own dime.

Businesses that have been starved for customers are now doing what capitalists do. Never miss an opportunity. And, in a way, quarantiners are perfect customers for businesses. The choice is limited and they are in need.

With that, here is an amazing story of businesses trying to recoup the losses.
The tale begins with faulty temperature checks on arrival in Bengaluru. Then, it moves on to quarantiners being charged Rs 100 for a one-kilometre ride on a bus run by the government. That even government services like transport, which are usually subsidised, got on to the recouping-losses wagon shows services’ appetite to get back in business.
Hotels earmarked for quarantine not only charged double the price they advertised on arrival, but also demanded that the quarantiners pay up for the full 14-day stay in advance. The second-order effect of businesses that have been held back by 57 days of lockdown reopening and getting a whiff of customers.
Soon, from quarantine capitalism, businesses could shift gears to lockdown capitalism—for when customers emerge from lockdown and buy products and services. Unlike companies that are not funded by venture capitalists who could finance discounts, real businesses that are trying to get back up will need to find a way to make up for lost money. That will come at a cost. 
Philippines’ mass testing: Catch-22 for business

Jum

Filipino businesses are having a WTF moment. After being forced to shut their shops during the strict Enhanced Community Quarantine (ECQ) in the past two months, they were greeted on Monday with news that the government had no programme for Covid-19 mass testing yet. The government expected the responsibility to be taken up by the private sector.

The announcement drew backlash from many business owners, who are dreading shouldering additional costs after seeing zero to minimal sales during lockdown.

Wasn’t the whole point of the restrictions to buy time for the government to come up with a comprehensive testing plan?

This tweet sums up their sentiment:
During ECQ, only one person per household could leave home for basic services. And only essential services could continue running in Luzon—the Philippines’ main island group, where most businesses are situated—between mid-March and 30 April. After that, the government started easing up restrictions, depending on whether an area is considered high-risk for Covid-19 or not. 

In all the weeks of trying to “flatten the curve”, the country of 105 million people ramped up testing capacity to only 11,000 tests per day.

Following the backlash, the government clarified that it had no plans to pursue mass testing in the first place. It wants to aim for “targeted testing” of the most vulnerable, like individuals showing symptoms or those who had exposure to other infected persons, equivalent to just 1.5-2% of the population.

With limited tests to be expected from government-run initiatives, the private sector will have to come up with ways to create safe environments for their workers. Or risk becoming the epicentre of new waves of infection, which is not good for business. 

While large companies may have the resources to foot the bill for the tests, it’s a totally different story for micro, small and medium enterprises (MSMEs). They account for 99.5% of established businesses in the Philippines, and 63.2% of its workforce.

Cash-strapped MSMEs may opt to remain shut altogether because of the added cost, say business groups.

The cost of the tests is not the biggest problem. Where do you even get enough of them? And which ones are reliable?
Disincentivising a return to normalcy

Rohin

Poor OPPO. The Chinese smartphone maker is big on India. It even has its own factory in Noida, in the northern state of Uttar Pradesh. Now, it faces penal action. Why will any company risk reopening its offices and factories if it’s going to be penalised for something that isn’t in its control?
The manufacturing plant of Oppo in Greater Noida was sealed Monday, only a few days after it resumed operations, when at least nine employees tested positive for Covid-19. All the employees in the plant are being tested now, and depending on how many test positive, the district authorities could initiate penal action against the smartphone maker.

This was discussed with manufacturers during preparatory meetings before permission was granted for opening factories," a senior bureaucrat said.

Humans: 1; AI: 0

Savio

The chaos and uncertainty during the pandemic have claimed an unlikely victim: machine learning systems that make sense of our online behaviour. These algorithms are trained on normal human behaviour and can adapt to changes by design, but the pandemic-related behavioural changes are too significant for them to keep up, MIT Technology Review reports. 

In one instance, a company that detects credit card fraud needed to tweak its algorithm to account for a surge in demand for power tools and gardening equipment. A firm that uses AI for investment recommendations based on the sentiment of news stories was confused by the gloomier-than-usual news. A large streaming firm’s viewer recommendation system was thrown out of whack by a sudden influx of content-hungry subscribers.

Even Amazon was affected. But in this case it tweaked its algorithms so that sellers that handle their own deliveries were likely to show up as top searches, near the coveted “Add to Basket” button. It did this to ease pressure on its warehouse and logistics.

Rajeev Sharma, global vice president at technology services firm Pactera EDGE, told MIT it’s time for AI to be upskilled.
Sharma thinks more AIs should be trained not just on the ups and downs of the last few years, but also on freak events like the Great Depression of the 1930s, the Black Monday stock market crash in 1987, and the 2007-2008 financial crisis. “A pandemic like this is a perfect trigger to build better machine-learning models,” he says.
 
Our weird behavior during the pandemic is messing with AI models, MIT Technology Review

Return-less, trial-less apparel sales?

Rohin

Free returns have been to online apparel sales what free trials have been to offline stores. Take them away, and few customers will want to plonk down money to buy clothes they don’t know will fit them. Vogue Business has a solid piece on a ticking bomb.
Shoppers return 10 per cent of what they buy in stores, but as much as 40 per cent of what they buy online, with returns rates on the higher end for fashion and apparel. And online returns are costly for brands, costing as much as $10 per return, says Forrester retail analyst Sucharita Kodali.

It sets up a perfect storm: as companies increase online sales with out-of-character markdowns, free shipping and exorbitant returns windows, they invite an unprecedented volume of returns four to six weeks later. In other words, while more online sales are what brands want, an influx of returns might be an unwelcome byproduct.
The inconvenient challenge of online returns, Vogue Business
But what will this look like? Canada has some early answers, and they aren’t pleasant.
Initially, the province wanted to eliminate change rooms, but settled on retailers making every other one vacant and disinfecting those that are used after each customer is finished. Returns were also set to be banned, but now must be quarantined for 72 hours before they’re put back on display. The same might apply to clothing that’s been tried on.

It’s clear the Saskatchewan government wants to eliminate as much of the tactile connection in stores as possible. It suggests that customers shouldn’t touch any items they don’t intend to buy and signs should be posted around the store to remind them. Any table or shelf displaying items in bulk will need to be disinfected after each customer touches it or be staffed by an employee who can hand items out.

This kind of near-touchless experience may end up resembling a showroom, said Matthew Teeple, who covers Canadian apparel and footwear for market researcher NPD Group. For example, instead of customers digging through clothing racks, which often attract high traffic, they may need to ask a salesperson to do so.
Sorry, ma'am, that dress is in quarantine: How clothing stores are prepping for a strange new reality, Financial Post
IPOutta here

Savio

The United States and China are already at loggerheads over technology, trade, the WHO, and the spread of the coronavirus. There is likely to be a new battleground.
Nasdaq Inc is set to unveil new restrictions on initial public offerings (IPOs), a move that will make it harder for some Chinese companies to debut on its stock exchange, people familiar with the matter said on Monday.
 
While Nasdaq will not cite Chinese companies specifically in the changes, the move is being driven largely by concerns about some of the Chinese IPO hopefuls’ lack of accounting transparency and close ties to powerful insiders, the sources said.
Exclusive: Nasdaq to tighten listing rules, restricting Chinese IPOs - sources, Reuters
On Tuesday, Nasdaq informed Chinese coffee company Luckin Coffee that it plans to delist it. This comes a month after Luckin disclosed that its COO Jian Liu and other staff had fabricated more than $300 million in sales last year.

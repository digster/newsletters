Working with established financial institutions is a holy grail for fintech companies. The reputational boost aside, it offers near-guaranteed steady work and access to plenty of end consumers.
When Lentra, a four-year-old firm that built an API to process loans, started partnering with banks and major financial-service providers in its home market India, you could argue that it had made it.
But take a closer look, and the idea begins to dissolve. While Lentra’s tech stack does get integrated by major firms, it’s sometimes limited to a small part of loan-processing potential—roughly 2% in one case.
Moreover, the introduction of the account-aggregator ecosystem—a government-led initiative in India that makes it possible for banks and other financial institutions to collate and share information about their customer bases—lowered a drawbridge over what Lentra previously saw as its moat.
That’s why the company sees Southeast Asia as its next frontier—or a Hail Mary. It has already begun building teams in Indonesia, the Philippines, and Vietnam, forging connections with financial-service providers in these geographies.
Maybe Lentra’s own framing as a partner to conventional stakeholders in banking and finance, rather than a disruptor, gives its image softer edges—making partnerships more palatable.
In today's story, Rahul unpacks the whys and hows behind Lentra’s move into Southeast Asia, starting with an acquisition in Singapore.

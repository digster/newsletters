Sometimes contrast does it best. In painting, sculpture, photography, drama, or other creative art forms. And in business writing.

Stacked squarely between 11,000 NBFCs in India—most of whom are irrelevant, with loan books of less than Rs 1 crore (~$1,400)—and modern fintechs, Five Star Finance is unconventionally traditional. (Okay, it’s an oxymoron, but Arundhati would have you read the story first and debate later.)

For the first 20 years of its existence, Five Star had a loan book of less than Rs 1 crore. Then it exploded. It grew 20X in the last 7 years and will disburse Rs 2100 crore ($298M) by March end. It expects to grow 100% for the next two years. Now, why is that sexy?

Because unlike the lending fintechs, Five Star lends in an (unglamorous) niche, takes collateral for its loans, and lends at handsome rates. Because it’s the first midsize NBFC that PE giant TPG has bet on (Rs 425 crore). Because with growth rates comparable to fintechs like, say, Capital Float and Lendingkart, Five Star is able to earn more than them.

While its founder describes the fintechs’ unsecured lending, politely, of course, as “less work to do”, India’s NBFC meltdown and China’s peer-to-peer lending crisis show that easy deployment of money and algorithm-assisted repayment do not a lending business make. Newer fintechs may even be launching an ‘instant business loan’ feature on their apps, but there’s little doubt a bubble is building in the lending business with low-risk-high-availability capital.  

“There is no difference in repayment rates between secured and unsecured lending when the times are good. But there is a stark difference in bad times.” There goes the Five Star CEO with decades of cyclical experience.

Arundhati’s story shows how Five Star is able to match fintechs, and even outperform them. Despite spurning every rule that most fintechs find critical for growth.

Good Morning Ishan,
More often than you think, valuations of businesses don’t make a lot of sense even when companies have thousands of shareholders, as is true of listed ones.
And when firms have just a handful of investors, which is the case with most tech startups, their valuations are basically Aramaic. But even among these, some stand out.
They secure impressive price tags before they have a business. OpenAI rival Mistral, for instance, was valued at $260 million within a month of its founding last year.
But that’s nothing next to what India’s largest tractor maker, Mahindra & Mahindra, pulled off in 2022.
It managed to convince British International Investment to put roughly $250 million into an electric-vehicle subsidiary at a valuation of $9.1 billion. And that unit was yet to be set up.
Last August, Temasek followed suit, raising Mahindra Electric’s valuation to nearly $10 billion.
Not much had changed in the interim, though. Its FY23 revenue was a grand total of zero. Even now, M&M’s market share in electric cars is a mere 7%, next to Tata Motors’ 70%.
Something’s got to give, you must be thinking, especially with investors tamping down their excitement over EV companies lately. Well, it has.
Anand takes a close look at a crucial development that went largely unnoticed last month: a fund-raise by Mahindra Electric, though not from another big-name investor but from its parent.
The money raised was insignificant, but the signal it sent wasn’t: Mahindra Electric’s lofty valuation may finally have met reality.

Have you, like me, found yourself looking for your glasses without your glasses? The family may find your blurry search funny, even as you desperately stumble from table to teapoy before triumphantly finding success. Or they may find your glasses and hand them to you with a smile, smirk, or silence. Maybe it’s time for a device tracker.

Thankfully, the search is helped by my long association with my glasses; the touch speaks. Unlike with humans, with glasses, familiarity does not breed contempt, it gives comfort.

Comfort that’s been eluding many buyers of Lenskart’s glasses, as Pranathi finds in her story today. From rashes to crashes, many frustrated users of the high-profile IPO-bound company’s spectacles have been looking askance at their ‘value-buys’. Buys they are having to churn in quick succession for something new.

The price is low, just about Rs 1,000 a pair in the “1+1” offer that Lenskart pioneered, which is now being aped by many in the industry. But the cost is high, as many frustrated customers are finding out. What gives?

Material matters, as Pranathi writes. Material that cut costs for Lenskart and pegged up its margins and valuation to some crazy levels, but left users in material discomfort. Quality matters too, no? When medical necessity becomes fashion accessory, it’s a sight to behold. Unseemly though it may be.

It is a big irony when seen from the customer’s perspective. Customers buy insurance for peace of mind, but at the time of need, hospitals often show them the mirror — that the insurance contract paper is not truly worthy.

On one side, insurance companies have been losing money for decades due to weak underwriting. On the other, hospitals are consistently increasing their EBITDA and overall margins. This imbalance highlights a systemic problem. Unless government bodies intervene, insurance in India will continue to remain underpenetrated. We may keep talking about awareness, but penetration will not improve.

If we look closely, health insurance premiums have gone up by almost 50% post-COVID. The so-called “growth” in the sector is not from adding new customers, but rather from increasing premiums. Meanwhile, hospitals are run like corporates, with full focus on ROI. Here, patients come second, and returns come first.

The entire process ultimately goes against insurers, who continue to operate at a loss. A serious government intervention is needed — especially to cap certain costs — otherwise, this cycle will only worsen.

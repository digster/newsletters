For long, ITC has wanted to shake off its cigarette-selling image, trying everything in the book from foods to agri products. Including, well, books. ‘Others’—a residual nondescript corner in its books—houses ITC’s stationery business. It’s a tiny side-show, relative to cigarettes and the behemoth’s other categories. But such is ITC’s size that this tiny category has been India’s largest stationery business for years.

But that pole position is now in imminent risk of being breached. A feisty competitor, a fraction of ITC’s size, is closing in rapidly. In just two years, Doms has gone from being half to two-thirds of ITC’s stationery sales. At this pace, this family-run company will race past the professional-run ITC in a year or two. The market has taken note, and the Doms stock, listed less than two years back, is up 3X since and worth nearly $2 billion.

What makes Doms tick—in business and with school kids, the primary users of its pencils, pens, and paper? Doms is also going deeper, broader, and backward with interesting moves and strategic buys. Not just to secure its flanks but also to catch its customers young. Really young. What about ITC? Let’s just say, it’s not twiddling its thumbs. The size of the fight in the dog will decide the fate of this David versus Goliath duel. Read Nuha’s story today to know more.

On another note, Birla Opus’ disruptive entry into paints has left Asian Paints with a bad aftertaste. Now, with Birla (via Ultratech Cement) and Adani entering the wires-and-cables business, Polycab India has two new players chasing it down. Yet the market loves Polycab, as the company’s stock hit its highest price on 10 October.

In today’s Long and Short, Seetharaman G decodes how and why Polycab has defied the initial panic and fueled a massive rally.

When most of the market is outside India, it doesn’t make sense for “decision makers” to be in India.This article seems to be focusing too much on senior leadership who usually resort to “empire building” in India.

The question to ask is what matters for a individual contributor in a GCC because they’re most of the head count. The low level IC just cares to be treated on par with their global counterparts in terms of the work they do. For a software development organization for example, you get to work on the same software that their global counterparts do.

This only works well when the India team is well intergrated into the global org. Frankly, as titles grow in India work, the managers build empires and work goes to shit. Indian managers go begging for work in the US and low quality work gets pushed down “freeing” up global teams to do more important work.

GCCs which are very selective in hiring, integrate well with global teams, pay well and discourage empire building do well with or without H1B woes. Alternatively, GCCs with India or the region as a significant and growing market also do well provided the market is really significant and requires strong local leadership.

Good Morning Ishan,

Startup founders are often slightly mad. Over the many late nights, weekends, months, and years that they devote to their startups, they often swing between being delusional, obsessive, delirious, grandiose or paranoid. Because not only do they see a future that others don't, they must also convince and motivate others to help create it. Even when, at times, their own belief wavers in the face of failure.
 
Aneesh Reddy, the founder and CEO of Capillary Technologies, is one such person. Since starting his company in August 2008, he has gone on to build an organisation that employs over a thousand people and generates an annualised revenue of nearly $50 million. The company virtually built the customer loyalty management category among India's retailers; used an insurgent model to break into new stores before Slack showed the world how to; started a SaaS-based pricing model much before trend-chasing VCs discovered it; still raised over $100 million in venture funding.
 
Yet, Capillary has grown through both India's retail boom and bust times. Its earliest customers (Future Group, India's second-largest retail group till last week) and investors (Café Coffee Day, India's largest coffee chain) have fallen by the wayside. Naturally, Capillary's journey is a window to India's tumultuous last decade.
 
Capillary, too, has cheated death many times. It went from zero stores that ran its software (which allowed brands to target loyal customers with offers) in February 2009 to 1,000 by the end of the year, and then 6,000 in 2011. It expanded from India to the Middle East and Southeast Asia, and then to the US and China.
 
Its US operations nearly got it killed before it wisely chose to retreat. It became cash-flow profitable in the first quarter of 2020, only to see a global pandemic lay waste to retailers.
 
Reddy, in an intensely candid and self-reflective interview with Sumanth, talks about Capillary's choices, decisions, successes, and failures.
 
"I keep joking sometimes in the company that we have the curse of being successful in Asia," he says.

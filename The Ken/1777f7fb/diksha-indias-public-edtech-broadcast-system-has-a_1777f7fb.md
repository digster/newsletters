One nation, one ration card.
One nation, one unique ID platform.
One nation, one digital platform.
One nation, one health ID/platform.
What’s there to not like about such unifying ideas. Federalism, local utility, flexibility, accountability, outcomes, etc., can always be fixed later.
DIKSHA, or Digital Infrastructure for Knowledge Sharing, has emerged as another platform ‘marvel’ in India, a country that could easily do with a raft of educational reforms.
From the outside, DIKSHA’s a super-platform. It can carry content. It can train. It can even generate useful data for states to use for policy making. DIKSHA also has a plug-and-play feature called “energised textbooks”, which links a scannable QR code, found in physical textbooks, to content available online.
A platform for hundreds of millions of students and teachers, DIKSHA was developed by a 40-member team at Ekstep Foundation in Bengaluru, a philanthropic initiative of Rohini and Nandan Nilekani, and Shankar Maruwada. The latter two are also credited with creating and scaling public digital goods like Aadhaar and eKYC.
During the pandemic and due to frequent nudges to state governments, DIKSHA’s usage has skyrocketed. It now boasts impressive, even if opaque, statistics—15.9 billion learning minutes and 1.8 billion learning sessions completed.
It was therefore not surprising to people in the tech community when DIKSHA was recently used as a “soft power tool” by the government, which presented it to around a dozen African nations as the “world’s largest, most diverse school education
platform”.
DIKSHA may be a government-owned platform, but it’s powered by Ekstep’s in-house tech Sunbird, an open source platform which, incidentally, has resulted in DIKSHA becoming a locked black box, says Olina in her story today.
To quote a Maharashtra state education professional: “The challenge is that when everyone has a say, no one really has a say. To get any new feature on DIKSHA, states will have to band together and lobby for it. It’s difficult to get your voice heard otherwise.”

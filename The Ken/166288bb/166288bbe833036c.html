Good Morning Ishan,
 
Thank god for Sunday. 
 
Phew! I’ve had some week. Have been on the road, a good part of last week. Meeting people. Drinking a lot of coffee. Thinking. Deciding.  
 
But now I’m glad to be home. Back to my tiny study room. My work table. Back to the grind. Back to you.
 
I think we can start this beautiful Sunday morning with a few minutes of silence for inspiration. Because the first time we spotted this, we were like, nah, this is just a one-off. But we are nosy, curious little things. So, my colleague Pranav went out and started buying other items. One from Baba Ramdev’s Patanjali. And one from the competition. I only want to say this for emphasis and context, but Baba Ramdev is a billionaire who started life as a Yoga guru. Preaching healthy living. It is another matter altogether that he now helms one of the fastest-growing, most successful FMCG companies in India. For the last few years, Patanjali has been giving its peers a run for their money. Of course, the jury is out if this is what Baba Ramdev will finally be remembered for. 
 
But back to Pranav and his shopping spree. 
 
The man bought chocolates. Biscuits. Some soya sticks (not featured in the story)...just a few in a long list of daily grocery items. 
 
It is uncanny how Patanjali’s products mirror those of the competition. In the way they are packaged. In the way they look. The colours. The feel. You’d really need to try to tell them apart. 
 
On the face of it, the strategy looks deliberate. Crafty. But the more I think about it, I think it is brazen. Something that blurs the line between inspiration and let’s just get this damn thing to look like competition because, hell, we know that already works, so why bother reinventing the wheel. Why bother spending money, building something ground up? 
 
Needless to say, companies have noticed. In March 2016, rival consumer goods company Emami took Patanjali to court claiming that Patanjali’s Kesh Kanti hair oil was very similar to its own Kesh King brand. Both in name and also in the font and presentation of the brand. And that Patanjali had also copied the “unique, aesthetic and innovative shape” of the bottle the hair oil comes in. The Calcutta High Court found merit in Emami’s claims and issued a preliminary injunction against Patanjali. But in May, both sides settled out of court. Patanjali still has a similar looking Kesh Kanti hair oil bottle listed on its website.

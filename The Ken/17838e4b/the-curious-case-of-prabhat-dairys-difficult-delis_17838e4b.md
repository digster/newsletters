A thriving dairy business. A tough stock market listing. Doubling of its share price in a little over two years, and then a crash. Extreme share price volatility and a buy-out by a French suitor for Rs 1700 crore ($234 million). To top it all, a rosy promise to its 49.9% minority shareholders in 2019 to share a substantial portion of the proceeds of the sale.
What has since happened to Prabhat Dairy is no less than a mystery. But as any seasoned writer will tell you, there are a limited number of plots to go around. Mystery novels tend to follow a formula and Prabhat Dairy’s is this: The road to minority shareholders’ haven is paved with false promises and inadequate punitive action.
Except in this case, the market regulator Sebi and the Securities Appellate Tribunal (SAT) are also playing ping-pong with their rulings.
Meanwhile, there’s been a significant mark-down in the net proceeds for distribution—from a “substantial portion of Rs 1700 crore” to “Rs 1,000–1,200 crore” to now a whittled-down Rs 872 crore.
Ideally, the delisting process should have been done and dusted a few months after the deal closed in April 2019. What followed instead were regulatory interventions, suspicions of fraud, and a forensic audit by Grant Thornton. Since mid-2020 when Sebi first linked the delisting with the audit, SAT has quashed it in its 4 March ruling where it effectively delinked the audit and the delisting process.
Will this end the ugly and protracted battle with investors?
Just before Prabhat Dairy, there was a visible case of Lloyd Electric selling its majority business to fellow competitor Havells in 2018. The road to shareholder value (erosion) was once again paved with “accounting jugglery and fund diversion”.

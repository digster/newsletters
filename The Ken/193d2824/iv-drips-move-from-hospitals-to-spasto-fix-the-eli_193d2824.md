Good Morning Ishan,
New Year’s Eve is approaching. You find yourself in one swanky spa clinic, where plush loungers, soft music, and Pinterest-perfect interiors promise rejuvenation. A nurse slides a needle into your arm, sending a cocktail of glutathione, vitamin C, and something called NAC or NAD+ coursing through your bloodstream. An hour later, you’re (supposedly) glowing and energised… and (definitely) Rs 50,000 poorer.
If you can imagine this, you’ve entered the new world of India’s intravenous (IV) drip economy—a wellness trend worth millions of dollars that blends equal parts science, psychology, and Instagrammable aesthetics. What once was a lifesaving medical tool is now a lifestyle flex.
Need a revival after a late-night party? There’s a drip for that. Want glowing skin before an office event? There’s a drip for that, too. So, clinics like Kosmoderma, Reviv, and Elixir Wellness are cashing in, with offerings like the Glow drip—packed with “skin-brightening liquid”—and the Fat Burn drip, promising effortless weight loss (just add Rs 4,000 and stir). Prices can go up to Rs 80,000 per session. Besides, IV bars are making cameos at destination weddings, catering to grooms, bridesmaids, and their collective hangovers.
Of course, there are a few tiny catches. For one, the medical community isn’t exactly thrilled. Doctors warn that these treatments are largely unregulated, with the potential for severe health risks. Allergic reactions, infections, or worse. Then there’s the ethical dilemma, too: while India’s affluent are hooked to NAD+ cocktails, a large part of the nation still struggles to access basic healthcare.
So, why is this booming? Blame the pandemic for a start. Toss in some celebrity sparkle (Arjun Kapoor and his hydration drip in Austria, anyone?) and a generous dose of social media-induced FOMO.
But here’s the question: are these treatments truly life-changing, or are they just glorified placebos dressed up as luxury experiences?

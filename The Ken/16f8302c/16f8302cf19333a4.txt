When I read Olina's story, the first acronym that popped into my head was "WTF". But, because that sort of French is not entirely appropriate, let me tell you about the other acronym that came to mind.

NIMBY!

Or "Not in my backyard." A NIMBY is someone who acts in self-interest by opposing the development of a technology that would benefit them only because it is too close to home for comfort. Often, the opposition is fiercest from those nearest the proposed location.

For example, I, who otherwise supports renewable energy, would staunchly object to the construction of a wind turbine if it obstructed the view from my balcony (who cares if it could cut my electricity bill in half?).

Another example is Hero Electric—India's largest manufacturer of electric two-wheelers by sales volume—vociferously objecting to the Indian government's 2018 EV policy just because the subsidies on offer exclude a majority of the vehicles it makes.

The policy, called FAME-II, subsidises only two-wheelers above a specific top speed. That excludes almost 95% of India's EV two-wheelers, including at least eight of Hero's 12 scooter variants.

Paradoxically, the two-wheeler EVs that qualify for the subsidy, like those by Ather, are too costly for mass adoption. The net result was two-wheeler EV sales crashed 94% in Q1 2019, even as FAME-II kicked in.

Yet, in another twist in this EV tale, Hero objected to a recent report claiming it had all but halted its Rs 700 crore ($97.4 million) investment to ramp up production capacity. 

In today's story, Olina examines who exactly benefits from India's EV policy and if there is more than one NIMBY among Indian EV makers.

Vishal Mega Mart is going public today.

The retailer is known nationwide for its product variety and reasonable prices. Any average household can buy everything it needs with one stop at any of Vishal’s packed stores, where products are crammed into every nook and cranny, and items overflow to line the sides of staircases.

Groceries, personal care items, infant supplies, clothing and footwear, appliances, kitchenware… the list goes on. In all, more than 70% of these are private-label products, according to Vishal’s DRHP, so the chain can keep costs low and margins high.

A mix of planned, habitual purchases and impulse buys keeps Vishal relevant for many Indian families, and the numbers speak for themselves: in FY24, the company’s sales hit Rs 8,900 crore, one-fifth of Dmart’s figure. For context, the number two player in this category, Trent, reported Rs 12,000 crore worth of sales in the same period.

Vishal’s Rs 8,000 crore (US$943 million) offering has drawn US$19 billion in bids, and the anchor investors that have been allocated shares include the funds of JP Morgan, HSBC, and the Singapore government. The company describes itself as one of the top offline-first diversified retailers in India and has convinced some analysts and investors to mention it in the same breath as Dmart, Trent subsidiary Zudio, and Reliance Retail, even though Vishal’s core market isn’t quite the same as those firms’.

But that comparison gives Vishal a compelling story to tell about itself—one that is palatable to public-market investors and gives juicy returns to Samayat Services, which holds a 99% stake in the chain.

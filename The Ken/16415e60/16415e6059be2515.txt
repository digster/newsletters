In the name of digital records, this is where we are all headed. 

*A newborn weighs 26 kgs.

*800 people get admitted into a hospital with 200 beds.

*Average length of stay of a patient: Two years.

Outrageous, you’d say. Sometimes a decimal goes missing, or an additional zero comes in. These are typical teething issues that come with a government choosing to collect health records electronically. The northern state of Haryana got the American giant UnitedHealth Group (UHG) to build E-Upchaar in 2014.

More than three years later, as Modicare is ready to roll, all eyes are on Haryana as it plays guinea pig in the implementation of the e-health system, or EHR. Though transformative and cost-saving, the hamster wheel is the same everywhere; it comes without warning signs. In a country like the US, which has been seriously trying to implement EHRs for years, the sailing is far from smooth. Even at the US Department of Defense, a $4.3 billion EHR programme is being flayed https://spectrum.ieee.org/riskfactor/computing/software/how-much-trouble-is-the-new-us-defense-department-electronic-health-records-program-in because?utm_campaign=The%20Ken&utm_source=Email&utm_medium=Newsletter it’s neither “operationally effective nor suitable”. (Please note, it is meant to replace a 2004 EHR system which wasn’t effective either. Long live EHRs...)

Back home, it has taken Haryana Rs 90 cr (~15M), four years and multiple instances of reprimanding UHG to get this far, where it has about 10 million people under EHR. Both the state govt and UHG seem to be unhappy but unlikely to walk away from the deal.

In a country where the A, B, C, D—an easy way of calling out the disjointed appointment, billing, clinical and discharge—of healthcare are broken, the centre and states are only going to call for more EHR or EHR-like solutions. Durham-based IQVIA began on one such project, a data repository, as of yesterday.

Ruhi spent some time at Haryana hospitals trying to understand what is going on in the name of EHR.

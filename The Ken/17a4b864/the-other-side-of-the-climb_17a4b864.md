Good Morning Ishan,
This is the first email I’ve written for The Ken and not to The Ken.
I joined the team as a Subscriber Community Specialist recently. If you’ve written to us in the last month, chances are it was probably me who wrote back to you. My job at The Ken is to listen to you, build better experiences for you, and maybe throw in a few bad jokes and tell some good stories every now and then (the last two, I’ve voluntarily taken it upon myself to do).
This week at The Ken, we wrote some rather good stories too. Reading them reminded me about the time my friends and I stumbled on a 10-foot wall in the middle of a trek in Mussoorie last week. We couldn’t change directions or turn back; climbing over it was the only option.
Surprisingly, minus a few bruises, we turned out just fine. And the trek turned into a story worth telling and a memory worth cherishing. The protagonists in our stories this week have a couple of metaphorical walls to scale as well. How many bruises will they suffer?
Many, as it turns out in the case of the Tata Group, one of India’s oldest conglomerates. Seetharaman, on Monday,
[wrote about](https://the-ken.com/story/1mg-curefit-and-tatas-desperate-search-for-a-new-avatar/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter) its super app ambitions, which recently got a shot in the arm with the acquisitions of BigBasket and 1mg, and a minority stake in CureFit. However, tying together Tata’s various businesses and acquisitions and investments is no child’s play.
Meanwhile, in another part of the Tata offices, the company was having a tryst with a digital transformation. The conglomerate is infusing money into Tata Teleservices, a company that the group was once forced to write off. Now, wrote Pratap and Anand on Friday,
[it’s hoping](https://the-ken.com/story/tata-tele-goes-from-joker-to-ace-in-tata-groups-digital-reshuffle/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter) that Tata Tele will act as a segue into India’s 75 million-strong SME goldmine.
In our
[free story](https://the-ken.com/story/covid-home-testing-gold-mine-missing-efficacy-ores?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter) this week, Maitri delved into the world of Covid home-testing kits. They’re as convenient as a pregnancy test and quickly becoming a significant part of India’s $9 billion diagnostics industry. However, only one out of three companies that have received approval to sell their kits has revealed accuracy data. Without accounting for how accurate these tests are, many Covid cases will slip under the radar.
Sumanth, on Tuesday,
[analysed SoftBank’s role](https://the-ken.com/story/the-return-of-the-dragon-softbanks-role-in-a-new-india/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter) in a new India. Three years after exiting Flipkart, SoftBank returned to India with a bang. However, its return is no easy feat. The Indian ecosystem has evolved, there’s more competition for SoftBank and startups are spoilt for choice.

[wrote about](https://the-ken.com/story/bukuwarung-bukukas-and-the-monetisation-conundrum-for-indonesias-digital-bookkeepers/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter) digital bookkeepers in Indonesia, BukuKas and BukuWarung, modelled after red-hot Indian startup Khatabook. Focussed on digitising Indonesia’s 60 million-plus MSMEs with free business services, these startups are peaking in their current valuations, but who’s going to solve the revenue puzzle?

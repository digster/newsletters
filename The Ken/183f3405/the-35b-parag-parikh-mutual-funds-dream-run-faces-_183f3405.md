PPFAS Mutual Fund, otherwise known as Parag Parikh Mutual Fund, is hardly in the top tier of its industry. It ranks 20th by assets under management (AUM) among India's 43 mutual-fund houses. And its AUM is a fraction of market leader SBI Mutual Fund's.
But before you conclude that the nine-year-old firm is an also-ran, consider this: since January 2020, which saw the onset of the pandemic, PPFAS' AUM has shot up 9X to $3.5 billion.
Most of that is tied up in its flagship, the Parag Parikh Flexi Cap Fund, which pioneered the concept of investing in domestic and global equities.
It has been a consistent outperformer, with its annualised returns considerably higher than those of the equity index it's benchmarked against.
But 2022 has been an annus horribilis for the fund relative to past years. The flexi-cap fund has slid almost 6%, compared to a 0.2% decline in the benchmark. This is thanks to two reasons:
- In February, the capital-markets regulator restricted mutual funds from investing in overseas stocks to keep them in line with the $7 billion cap the Reserve Bank of India (RBI) has set for this purpose.
- The rout on global bourses, especially in tech stocks, in 2022. Meta is down 60%, and Microsoft, Amazon, and Alphabet are down by half as much. (The fund owns all these stocks.) And the fund has not been able to buy the dip because of the foreign-investment ceiling.
The fund's value-investing approach and contrarian bets in India, including cigarette and FMCG major ITC, have paid off. But crucial to its appeal for investors is that they get to indirectly own a piece of companies listed outside India.
With its USP under attack now, how does PPFAS continue to stand apart from its peers?

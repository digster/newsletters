The last nail in the coffin for Kathpalia’s Indusind Bank stint. There is one quality a banker needs above all else, and that’s trustability
By Rounak Kumar Gunjan
6 min read
View in browser

Good Morning Ishan,

You are reading our subscriber-only newsletter Ka-Ching!—a weekly commentary on fintech and personal finance

It’s impossible to get by this week without talking about Indusind Bank. 

On Friday, the Reserve Bank of India approved only a year’s extension to the bank’s managing director and CEO Sumant Kathpalia. This, despite the bank’s board wanting him to remain at the corner office for three more years.

Then, on Monday, the lender disclosed a Rs 1,580 crore discrepancy in its derivatives portfolio, potentially impacting 2.35% of its net worth. The issue had been first identified internally in September 2024, and an external agency has been hired for a review, Indusind said. 

The bank’s stock has tanked. Despite a mild recovery on Wednesday, Indusind’s share prices are down 24% since the disclosure—a market cap erosion of close to Rs 17,000 crore. Several mutual funds with high exposure to the bank have taken a hit. 

All of this has been on the frontpage of newspapers, so I’m sure you’re caught up on the basics. What I really want to talk about, though, is the analyst call the bank held on Monday to clear doubts and apprehensions about the large discrepancy. I wasn’t invited, so I listened to the call recording.

During the meeting, Kathpalia was asked a question about RBI’s decision to slash his tenure as CEO. He said this:

“I think… a… a… of course this [the discrepancy] would have had a bearing, because they [RBI] were aware of this issue. 

We actually… there was some inkling towards the issue.

I think… we couldn’t… we can’t… I can’t be… 

I don’t know what is the rationale for them to give me one year, but I think they’re uncomfortable with the way—my leadership skills—of running the bank is. And we have to respect that. 

[…]

I remain committed for one year to the bank. And I think, let’s see how the process goes. The board will evaluate external as well as internal candidates for the CEO position.

I don’t know whether they [would] evaluate me or evaluate somebody else. That’s for them to decide.”

Hardly the sound of confidence. But I had a couple of questions about how things went down, and Kathpalia’s future at the bank, so I spoke to a senior Indusind executive and called up a retired banker, once the chairman of a large bank. 

Given what they say, Kathpalia’s time as the bank’s chief executive is almost certainly drawing to a close.

The most worrying deficit

The analyst call lasted 28 minutes, and all through it, Kathpalia and his team couldn’t stop mentioning how the mismatch was first spotted internally. “An internal check. We have done it through our internal process”—the bank’s leadership team repeated this point eight times during the call. An average of once every three-and-a-half minutes.

I can understand why bankers would want to stress something like that; after all, trustworthiness is a non-negotiable quality in a banker. And so the bank rolled out a version of: “yes, alright, I messed up. But at least I have the integrity to report it to you myself”. 

In this case, though, the discrepancies span five to eight years, Kathpalia confirmed during the call. It is beyond me, banking experts, and brokerages how they went unnoticed during every internal and external audit for as long as they did. 

Brokerages such as Kotak Institutional Equities, Motilal Oswal, and Morgan Stanley have all stated that the episode has impacted the bank’s credibility. Nuvama Institutional Equities wrote: “The time line is discomforting: the CFO resigned just before the Q3 earnings, the CEO recently got a one-year extension instead of three, and now a derivatives-induced dislocation.”

According to the senior Indusind Bank executive, the analysts call for clarifications on Monday was a way to shore up some of this rapidly decaying trust.

“It was important for the bank to establish that we spotted it and reported it to the regulator. And not the other way round.”

How and when these disclosures came about isn’t very flattering to the bank, though. Indusind Bank announced its results for the quarter-ended December 2024 on 31 January, and as is customary, there was a detailed analyst concall following the results. One that went on for a good 56 minutes. 

But the bank decided to not breathe a word about this discrepancy at that time. When an analyst asked them on Monday why they had remained quiet, both Kathpalia and Arun Khurana, the company’s executive director, deputy CEO and CFO, rushed to speak over each other. The gist—‘we didn’t have enough to talk about it then.’

Given Kathpalia says the issue was identified internally as early as September 2024, I’ll leave you to make of that what you will. 

The former chairman of the large bank I spoke to believes the bank announcing the discrepancy after the RBI’s notification on Kathpalia’s shortened tenure is “optics”. An attempt to treat the two events separately to avoid a hit on their credibility.

“It’s good perception engineering,” the Indusind Bank executive laughed. 

Of course, if that was the objective, it doesn’t help that Kathpalia admitted in the analyst call that the RBI “was aware” and had “an inkling” about the discrepancy when they made their decision to offer him only a year’s extension. While both bankers I spoke to say that the RBI would have had the complete details and not just an inkling, I haven’t been able to confirm this independently. 

Either way, a one-year extension is usually a pretty strong signal that the incumbent CEO must pack his bags, find a suitable successor, hand over the reins, and leave. To that effect, the regulator has already asked the bank’s board for two probable candidates for the chief executive role, according to the senior Indusind Bank executive.

These last few years have hardly been smooth sailing for Kathpalia, as we wrote in a 2024 story: Indusind CEO’s 400-day test—win RBI over or retire. Clearly, recent events have made it all but impossible for Kathpalia to do that. 

It is but natural for the regulator to choose to get the bank back in order over giving another long chance to its CEO.

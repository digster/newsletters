Good Morning Ishan,
I remember visiting an international school in Mumbai with my partner and our toddler last year. The executive in charge of admissions, as she made a pitch on why the school deserved its gasp-inducing fee, asked us if we’d like some coffee.
In the same breath and tone, sensing our daughter’s growing impatience, she asked us if we’d like a nanny.
Before I could fully process that question, the executive took us to the school’s pickleball court and said no other school in the neighbourhood had one, gleaming with pride. At this point, I was too bewildered to be impressed.
But Global Schools Foundation (GSF) and International Schools Partnership (ISP) know that appearances matter. Backed, respectively, by private-equity firms Apollo Global Management and Partners Group, these two multi-country school operators have bought nine K-12 chains in India since 2021, and intend to spend millions to expand their footprint.
“Standardised visual upgrades that photograph well took priority over less visible investments, such as training or transportation,” Valli writes in her deeply reported take on the impact on the schools that were hoovered up. But these upgrades don’t fund themselves, as parents and teachers will be quick to point out.
Regardless of what they buy, PE firms largely stick to this uncomplicated sequence: trim the fat, grow the top line, juice profits, and find a buyer who’s willing to pay top dollar.
There’s nothing wrong with this playbook, except sometimes it breaks more than it builds.

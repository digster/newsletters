The a2 Milk Company partnered with the show around the same time the three new Masterchef judges were announced in 2019. Set up in 2000 by scientist and entrepreneur Corran McLachlan in New Zealand, it’s now in markets like Australia, China, and North America. Though the company isn't present in markets like India, A2 milk as a concept has captured the imagination of the Indian milk consumer.
A1 milk, or the milk that comes from cows of Western origin like Holstein Friesian, Jersey, etc, is suddenly being considered inferior to A2 milk—which is obtained from indigenous cows. In India, that would be Gir, Sahiwal, etc. And A1 is available in larger quantities than A2.
Less supply and increasing demand have made A2 quite the opportunity.
According to market research by beverage brand Hugg Beverages, Indians are ready to pay anywhere from 2-4X the price of regular milk for A2 milk. A litre of cow’s milk from the Mother Dairy brand, for instance, sells for Rs 50 (US$0.6). But Ghaziabad-based Hetha Organics prices its A2 milk at Rs 180 (US$2.3) per litre. Hetha’s ghee (clarified butter), derived from A2 milk, is sold at Rs 5,000 (US$65) per litre, up to 10X the cost of regular ghee.
Even the prices of Indian cows providing sellable A2 milk are shooting up.
Sure enough, over the last seven years, some 50 startups and small-sized dairy companies have cropped up in India claiming to sell "certified" A2 milk. They claim A2 milk to be pretty much the elixir of life—better for people with diabetes, heart disease, and even lactose intolerance.
That's great, right? Not really. Only nine of these companies publicly upload DNA test certification documents to support their claim of selling A2 milk.

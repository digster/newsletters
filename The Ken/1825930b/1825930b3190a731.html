Hello Ishan,
 

For over a year now, everyone has been obsessed with The Great Resignation. 

 

All over the world, employees have been voluntarily (and of late, involuntarily) quitting their jobs to do other things. There has been a lot of talk about why this is happening, what happens next, and how long this trend will last. 

 

But few conversations are about how these people are quitting. 

 

I’m talking about resignation letters. 

 

A beautiful resignation letter is a balancing act. You need to use all the right words. Carefully thank those you probably dislike. Be sufficiently vague about your future plans. Slide in palliative phrases like ‘cheering from the sidelines’. Then, there’s the timing of it. Should you send it before meeting your manager? Or immediately after? And what on earth should the subject line be? Who should be on the cc? And more devilishly, who is on the Bcc? Even Google searches aren’t particularly helpful. 

 

Many people still don’t understand the value of a good resignation letter, or the harm that a badly written one can do. In the world of tech, which is increasingly connected, and layoffs, which are increasingly common, it probably pays to part with organisations on good terms instead of burning bridges behind you. 

 

And the resignation letter is usually the final official communication that an employee has with the organisation. Which means it stays on the records as a permanent snapshot of their experience.

 

So yes, many important questions.

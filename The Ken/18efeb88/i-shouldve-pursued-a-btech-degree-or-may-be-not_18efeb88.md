Good Morning Ishan,
It’s one of Kolkata’s sultry evenings in June 2011.
I’m trying to have a conversation with my mother. I’m angry. I don’t want to express it. So I’m pleading.
“Why are you not understanding? I don’t want to become an engineer.”
She’s repeatedly wiping my tears with one end of her saree. But she’s not giving in.
Cut to Thursday, 11 April 2024.
I’m on a call with a technologist for a story.
“Domain name changes are very easy. It’s the package name that’s problematic. You can’t even deploy APIs to change these strings,” they say.
“Hmm,” I reply.
“A new tech stack means a complete overhaul of the ecosystem…” they nonchalantly continue.
The conversation goes on for almost two hours. My takeaway: I should’ve quietly obeyed my parents and pursued a B.Tech degree.
Over the next couple of days, I seek help from more techies. During these long chats, I ask questions. A lot of them.
Nevertheless, I file the story. Anticipating a barrage of questions.
Though against my will, time continues to pass by. And it’s Monday, 15 April.
My colleague, Aakriti, kicks off the week with an excellent [report](https://the-ken.com/story/good-glam-cant-keep-up-with-its-acquisition-spree/?utm_source=weekly_email&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=weekly_recap_card) on Good Glamm, a popular e-commerce beauty and personal care aggregator. She finds that despite the firm’s recent fundraising, investors are worried about profitability amid declining demand for D2C products. Also, Good Glamm isn’t able to keep up with its acquisition spree.
My mind: “Is this a signal? Have I also bitten off more than I can chew?”
Eventually, it’s Tuesday: the D-day
I’m in front of my laptop at 7 am—an hour before the story lands in subscribers’ mailboxes. I’m blankly staring at the screen. An eternity later, it’s 8. The story about Digiyatra—the contactless, biometric entry system at airports—finally gets published. We [explain](https://the-ken.com/story/you-need-to-download-digiyatra-again-but-its-less-about-a-tech-upgrade-and-more-about-a-scam/?utm_source=weekly_email&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=weekly_recap_card) why a week-long outage and a sudden app launch are not because of Digiyatra’s expansion plans—it’s more about a scam.
My mind: “This is it. My arguments need to withstand the scrutiny of all of The Ken‘s tech-proficient subscribers.”
The fear spikes every time there’s an X (formerly Twitter) notification or a message on the office group. But thankfully, the day passes peacefully.
Then comes Wednesday morning.
I wake up far more relaxed. But by now, it seems, the universe, or at least The Ken, has sworn that not a day shall pass by without making Rounak realise he’s a non-techie.
Shanies [writes](https://the-ken.com/story/why-taiwan-poaching-tech-talent-isnt-bad-news-for-indonesia/?utm_source=weekly_email&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=weekly_recap_card) about how Taiwan’s high-tech industry became an opportunity for Indonesians to build their careers. And which are these industries, you ask? Primarily, the semiconductor and computer engineering industries.
Do you acknowledge the conspiracy? It doesn’t stop here.
My colleague, Abhirami, publishes a [story](https://the-ken.com/story/nvidias-ai-chips-are-about-to-flood-the-indian-market-but-who-needs-them/?utm_source=weekly_email&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=weekly_recap_card) about Yotta Data Centre, backed by Hiranandani Group. It recently received 4,000 H100 GPUs to empower budding AI startups. Thousands more are on the horizon. But the fundamental question she asks is, does the future of AI demand this magnitude of chips?
The lucidity of these stories makes it a breeze to understand tech concepts.
Thursday arrives and I’m expecting yet another tech story. But I’m wrong.
Contributors Ateesh Tankha and Ganga Narayan Rath write about bank lockers. And it again triggers an engineering memory.
It was a time when I was trying to convince my parents against a B.Tech degree. I stood true to myself and I said, “I’m an average student. I won’t be able to crack the IITs or NITs. And do you know about the fees of private engineering colleges?”
My father had an answer ready. “You don’t worry about money. We have valuables safely kept in the bank locker. I’ll sell them.”
Tankha and Rath, in their story, [argue](https://the-ken.com/story/why-banks-are-no-longer-ideal-to-deposit-your-valuables/?utm_source=weekly_email&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=weekly_recap_card) that banks are no longer ideal to deposit your valuables. In fact, new rules meant to enhance banks’ locker services for clients are having the opposite effect. They’re unlocking a window of opportunity for private players.
Only if my father knew.
Friday is here and the engineering streak is finally broken.
My colleague, Shivani, [writes](https://the-ken.com/story/mankind-pharmas-canny-marketing-worked-wonders-until-it-began-selling-supplements/?utm_source=recap&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter) about Mankind Pharma, a US$11 billion company that wants to extend its dominance from pregnancy-test kits and contraceptives to multivitamins. But the segment is proving to be a tough pill to swallow.
Hang on, we do much more than just publish stories.

In retrospect, I’m happy that my mother eventually gave in that evening and asked how one becomes a journalist. I can be an engineer on Monday, a banker on Tuesday and a product strategist on Wednesday, and still remain a journalist throughout the week.
It’s a happy choice… on most days.

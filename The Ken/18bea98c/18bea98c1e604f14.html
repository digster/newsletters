For the most part of 2023, the ministry of heavy industries and electric two-wheeler makers in India have played a very public cat-and-mouse game.

The government seeks to recover Rs 469 crore ($56 million) from seven companies that violated the localisation norms under the FAME-II subsidy scheme.

Surprisingly, the ministry has still not learnt its lessons.

The Ken has accessed documents that show a similar story unfolded in the charging infra space. A few charger manufacturers did not meet the localisation norms. Yet, they were awarded the tenders. By none other than giant oil marketing companies.

Indian Oil Corporation Ltd gave Okaya Power a Rs 125 crore ($15 million) contract for setting up 2,550 chargers across the country. Servotec Power Systems bagged a Rs 46 crore ($5.5 million) deal with Bharat Petroleum Corporation Ltd (BPCL) for 800 chargers.

We’ve earlier written how nearly 60% of BPCL’s fast-charging infra wasn’t functional as of 31 May.

On 7 November, the ministry circulated an updated localisation criteria that dilute the level of domestic sourcing. The rush is to disburse the remaining Rs 240 crore ($29 million) out of the total Rs 1,000 crore ($120 million) earmarked under FAME-II for chargers before March 2024, or else the subsidy will lapse.

Once again, charger manufacturers are “confused” about what constitutes “localisation”. The new norms also leave more scope for future foul play, over and above the escape hatch that FAME already provides to companies.

In an excellently reported story today, Nathan argues why fixing FAME-II’s charging loophole is key to building consumer confidence and a truly local infrastructure.

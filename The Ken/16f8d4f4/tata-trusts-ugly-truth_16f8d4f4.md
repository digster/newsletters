Remember that kid in school who was always the teacher's pet for saying the right things? Or maybe that politically-correct boss' favourite at your workplace?
And you'd just keep hoping that one day, there will be a moment of truth. One day, everyone will know what they've really been up to all along?
I don't know if that moment came for the person you're thinking of, but it did for India’s largest and oldest charity, Tata Trusts. As recently as October 2019, this large philanthropic body was given a reality check—India’s income tax (I-T) department
[cancelled](https://www.thehindubusinessline.com/companies/how-the-tax-department-cancelled-tata-trusts-registration/article29871106.ece) the tax-exempt charitable trust registrations of the six trusts it houses. For being more business than charity.
What makes this development even more interesting is that Tata Trusts claims it "voluntarily surrendered" its tax-exempt registration under the I-T Act in 2015. But it hasn't paid taxes even once in the last four years.
Not disconcerting at all, right?
Well, Tata Trusts could now face a
[tax liability](https://www.business-standard.com/article/companies/rs-12-000-cr-tax-blow-i-t-dept-cancels-registration-of-6-tata-trusts-119110200056_1.html) of up to Rs 12,000 crore, over 10X the grants it disbursed in the year ended March 2018. And all this while, guess what it's been working on?
Cancer care.
Not the sort of place you'd want them taking their baby steps in philanthrocapitalism. Especially in the midst of their tax troubles. Over the last few years, Tata Trusts has set up two cancer care hospitals in Uttar Pradesh and one in Andhra Pradesh. It also signed an MoU to build 19 cancer care facilities—the largest number of charitable cancer care hospitals in India—with the north-eastern state of Assam. Its Assam plans, however, are in turmoil. Allocations slashed; the number of centres more than halved.
The uncomfortable facts don't stop here.
According to the Public Accounts Committee (PAC) of Indian Parliament, Tata Trusts spent its money in a way that not only violated the I-T Act but also the trust deed. By donating to things that weren't really charitable—A 150,000 sq foot building bearing the Tata name at Harvard Business School, for instance.

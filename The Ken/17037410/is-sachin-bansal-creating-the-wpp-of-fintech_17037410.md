The year was 1985.
Wire & Plastic Products, a 14-year old British company that manufactured, well, wire baskets, was quietly bought out by a diminutive Harvard MBA who then installed himself as its new CEO. The new CEO wasn’t interested in any sort of baskets, though. He viewed WPP merely as a vessel to contain his outsized ambitions that would span decades—build a global marketing services giant.
Too impatient to actually build out his vision, the young CEO started acquiring companies he wanted. He began with ad agencies, acquiring 11 in the very next year. Soon he acquired dozens of companies—PR firms, ad agencies, media buying companies, marketing research firms, consultants.
Today WPP is the world’s largest communication services firm, earning over £50 billion annually. Chances are, almost every other ad agency or PR firm you can think of belongs to WPP.
The CEO was Martin Sorrell.
In 2018, Sorrell resigned from the company he built over allegations of personal misconduct and use of company money. His net worth today is estimated to be close to $500 million.
That very same year Flipkart, India’s largest e-commerce company, was acquired for $16 billion by Walmart. And in spite of his desire to stay on and build the acquired company into a “$100 billion” giant, Sachin Bansal, its co-founder, was forced to leave the company he built because his co-founder and lead investor didn’t stand by him.
The only thing more interesting than a tech billionaire is a tech billionaire with a chip on his shoulder. Bansal has unfinished business. Like Sorrell, he too wants to build an all-powerful empire by cobbling together various companies, products and strategies. In fintech.
There are many similarities between the Sorrell of 1985 and the Bansal of 2020. Both had outsized visions and unrealistic expectations of winning in competitive markets. Both used holding companies to accelerate their pace of growth, through acquisitions.
There is one key difference, though. Sachin Bansal is starting a billionaire. And he wants to go all-in with everything he’s got.
Bansal’s holding company, Navi Technologies, is rapidly acquiring companies. These include non-banking lender Chaitanya India Fin Credit; Mavenhive, a tech consulting company; Essel Mutual Fund; and DHFL General Insurance.
HDFC is India’s most valuable private bank. Navi’s goal is to become India’s next HDFC. Not by opening branches, but by acquiring companies.

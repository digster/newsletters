It’s only in the last couple of years that I’ve come to an understanding about a particularly insidious weakness of mine.
I confuse talking about how to solve a problem with making actual progress solving it. Get a few enthusiastic people together and spitball ideas for an hour? Love it—so many good ideas; so many opportunities I never saw before. Catch a friend during a chai break for an impromptu brainstorming session? Yes please. And let’s extend that to two chais and an egg puff.
Of course, as days and weeks of this incredibly fertile-seeming debate carry on, and the wonderful possibilities you’ve collected pile up, the problem just sits there. Unsolved. And more often than not, getting harder to solve with each passing moment.
It took me an embarrassingly long while to realise that while all those conversations were in fact productive in seeding inspiration, they were entirely useless (if not actually destructive) without structure and focus.
Don’t get me wrong. Groups, communities can be incredibly powerful tools for innovation. The more diverse the better. But not all questions benefit from group debate, and it is all too easy to use them the wrong way. Group bias, polarisation, the common knowledge effect… all rear their heads. There are a couple of excellent articles from Harvard Business Review that break down these problems at a gratifyingly deep level, so check them out when you have time: [Why Groups Struggle to Solve Problems Together](https://hbr.org/2019/11/why-groups-struggle-to-solve-problems-together), and [Some Questions Benefit from Group Discussion. Others Don’t](https://hbr.org/2021/09/some-questions-benefit-from-group-discussion-others-dont).
But once you are cognizant of the pitfalls and structure your discussions to avoid them, what groups genuinely interested in innovation can do when pulling together is amazing. Especially when they are large and diverse, not just in terms of gender, age, and economic conditions, but also cognition—aka, how individuals think about and engage with new, uncertain, and complex situations.
From a HBR study:
The three teams that completed the challenge in a good time (teams A, B, and C) all had diversity of both knowledge processes and perspective, as indicated by a larger standard deviation. The three that took longer or failed to complete (D, E, and F) all had less diversity, as indicated by a lower standard deviation.
Intuitively, this makes sense. Tackling new challenges requires a balance between applying what we know and discovering what we don’t know that might be useful. It also requires individual application of specialized expertise and the ability to step back and look at the bigger picture.
A high degree of cognitive diversity could generate accelerated learning and performance in the face of new, uncertain, and complex situations.
[Teams Solve Problems Faster When They’re More Cognitively Diverse, Harvard Business Review](https://hbr.org/2017/03/teams-solve-problems-faster-when-theyre-more-cognitively-diverse)
Bringing all the moving pieces together can be a challenge, but once in place and pulling towards a common goal, the results can be impressive.

Hello Ishan, it’s Hari here. This week, for a change, I thought we’d strike out from our usual slate of recommendations to shine some of the spotlight on a recent award-winning book that I think is a matter of pride for the nation. One that, ever since reader Yashika Jain wrote in with her recommendation last week, I’ve been itching to pick up.
Yashika: “Any words I might say will fall short of the praise it has received, especially after winning The International Booker Prize last week. It discusses, with precision and care, the everyday lives of women in southern India. There are a lot of things to love about it, but the one that stays are the characters. For Banu Mushtaq is, above all, an observer of humans.”
The book is an achievement nearly 33 years in the [making](https://www.vogue.in/content/banu-mushtaq-wrote-heart-lamp-for-33-years-nominated-for-international-booker-prize), and Banu delivered a beautiful [speech](https://indianprinterpublisher.com/blog/2025/05/single-sky-banu-mushtaq/) after the announcement. I’m sure some of you have already heard or read the translation. The best thing she said, for me, was this: ‘This book is my love letter to the idea that no story is local.”
[Heart Lamp](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/205544315-heart-lamp) is a short story collection. The last book to bring home the International Booker Prize was Geetanjali Shree’s novel Ret Samadhi (or [Tomb of Sand](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/58703758-tomb-of-sand)) which we had [featured](https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/attracting-feedback/) back in September. One of the nicer things I have heard about the English translation of Heart Lamp (it is a Kannada book) is that translator Deepa Bhasthi has kept the essence of the language and culture intact, and has not shifted its tone to appeal to Western readers. If you’ve already read the book, do write in and tell me how you liked it.
You can, as always, send in your own recommendations [using this link](https://theken.typeform.com/FPBooks).
Our second recommendation this week is from my colleague Tanim, which also happens to be a translation: The Vegetarian by Han Kang. Translated by Deborah Smith.
Tanim: “The setting in the book is as real as any relationship, whether it’s portraying married couples or sisters. But the events that unfold force you to deal with uncomfortable truths even if the events themselves seem out of this world. The writing is very imagist so everything feels very vivid. Pick it up if you want a short, powerful read.”

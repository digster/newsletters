"If you had to go for dinner—a fancy dinner—and you had to pick out an outfit, you'd go shopping, try out a varied assortment of clothes and then settle on your final attire for the evening. Correct?"

This is the analogy a senior Tata executive likes to give every time he's met with a question about Tata Industries Limited's digital health division Tata Health and its long testing phase. A four-year-long testing phase.

"If you had just bought the tie, would it make for the whole outfit? Does it make sense to focus on the tie alone?" he asks again.

The answer to the first question is no. As for the second, well, Tata Health is indeed a special tie—one that has left its mark. Even during its testing phase.

Tata Health, looking at a possible launch in the first half of 2020, made for 11.31% of Tata Industries Limited's (TIL) turnover in the year ended March 2018. That's a feat for a not-yet-a company.

Given this predicament and with one of India's biggest (and oldest) conglomerates backing it, Tata Health stands as a veritable contender in the digital health race. While it's been trying and testing, potential competitors Portea, Practo, Lybrate and many others have grown at breakneck speeds (with VC money), burned money, pivoted, and basically seen far too many changes. Tata Health has quietly watched from the sidelines.

With a goal to become a “major provider of preventive, personalised primary care” in India, according to TIL's executive director, KRS Jamwal, Tata Health now has its work cut out. It doesn't help that its many tests have left various partners miffed.

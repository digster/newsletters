I told you so.
That's how most fund managers must be reacting to the $121 billion clobbering that investors have handed Adani Group stocks in nearly two weeks.
The companies' absurd valuations were despite mutual funds largely staying away from investing in them. But there were exceptions, as always.
Like NJ Mutual Fund. As of December 2022, roughly 7% of its assets under management (AUM) of Rs 4,500 crore (~$545 million) was tied up in Adani stocks. Not quite the situation NJ would have liked to find itself in after raising Rs 5,200 crore ($630 million) for its maiden fund in October 2021.
That's the most any fund-house has raised for its first scheme.
What's more, NJ is owned by India's largest mutual-fund distributor (by income), which used its extensive network to hard-sell the scheme.
NJ is already bigger than much older rivals such as Bank of India Mutual Fund and IDBI Mutual Fund.
But pedigree and distribution chops can only go so far. Despite the mutual-fund industry reaching new heights, NJ's AUM has been on the decline since August 2022.
It may still be the early days for NJ. But its reliance on quantitative analysis and mathematical models—instead of assessing a company's fundamentals—has flattered to deceive so far.

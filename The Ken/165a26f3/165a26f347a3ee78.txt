Streaming services are on steroids. Even if you are not the kind to browse the infinite collection of things to watch on a streaming app, you can’t miss the even broader fare that is going to be on offer. Thanks to the infinite deals that are being stitched.

For now, they have found a saviour in telcos. Just for now. From international production houses like Eros to the local ALT Balaji, a significant part of customer growth and revenues are coming from telcos. As much as 60-70% in the case of the latter.

Telcos know that streaming is the future. Just this past month, Airtel announced two content partnerships—one with broadcaster Zee, and another with Netflix. A couple of days later, Vodafone sent text messages to its postpaid customers saying they’d be eligible for a free yearly subscription to Amazon Prime Video as well as a two-month subscription to Netflix.

It’s raining freebies because telcos think it’s too early to push customers to pay for streaming content, but they are careful to link it to telco behaviour—bundle subscriptions to data packs. Multi-year partnerships are helping telcos reduce subscriber churn, even as they’ve found a way to control their funnel by implementing an app-in-app experience for these partnerships. Conversely, streaming services are relying on telcos to help with promotion and marketing for their international expansion.

But how long will this relationship last? Content owners believe they are in a better position than during the VAS (value-added services) days. Still, one can’t help asking if this would lead to a situation where a content creator has to pay to be on a telco network, a new type of carriage which we saw with television.

Shashidhar’s story today examines how long this honeymoon period could last and what is likely to emerge after that.

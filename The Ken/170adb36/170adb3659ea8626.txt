Today’s email (and story) is about the source code hosting phenomenon that is GitHub but allow me to tell you about a very special cat. I promise it’s related.

OctoCat is, as the name suggests, equal parts cat and octopus. The head of a cat mounted on five tentacles. It’s also the mascot of GitHub. As the story goes, GitHub’s founders discovered OctoCat on a stock image site, fell in love with it, and bought the exclusive rights to it. Its creator, incidentally, is Simon Oxley, the man who created the original white bird logo for Twitter.

The logic behind GitHub choosing OctoCat is fascinating. Git, the software on which the platform operates, has a concept known as an “octopus merge”—the combination of three or more branches of development. On the other hand, cats rule the internet. OctoCat, therefore, was the perfect manifestation of the nerd culture that reigns supreme on GitHub.

OctoCat’s tentacles also symbolise multi-tasking and collaboration, the very DNA of the largely open source platform. As Shreedhar writes in today’s piece, “Hundreds of developers across teams can work on a project simultaneously while hosting the common codebase on GitHub.” There are also close to 150 OctoCat variants—from Octobi Wan Catobi to Octdrey Catburn—representing the sheer diversity of the platform’s 40 million developers.

In 2020, OctoCat’s tentacle finally reached India. The country was already its third-largest developer base, but GitHub has announced it will now open two offices and hire 200 employees, too.

Why the focus on India? Because Microsoft—which bought GitHub in 2018—believes enterprise subscriptions and not open source projects are the way forward. How does India fit into this? That’s what Shreedhar decodes in today’s story, which is also our free story of the week. Do share widely!

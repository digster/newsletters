Rise and shine, good-looking. And allow me to take you back a few years. 

I remember meeting Tulsi Tanti, the chairman & managing director of Suzlon, in October of 2011. The meeting, which had finally materialised after at least two years of requests for one, was in Pune. At Suzlon’s global headquarters called One Earth. Back then, it cost a total of Rs 430 crore to build. If any man could be a symbol for die-hard optimism in the face of the most daunting challenges, Tanti would come out tops. I still remember this bit from our four-hour-long conversation: “By the end of the year, Suzlon will become the most profitable wind energy company in the world.”

That was then. Now with the benefit of hindsight, almost six and a half years later, I can say that Tanti’s statement couldn’t be further from reality. 

Couldn’t be further from the delights of optimism that fill the hearts of humans, especially the kind known as entrepreneurs.

I mean, Suzlon has been a turnaround story in the making for the last decade. Corporate debt restructuring. Sign of a turnaround. Default on Foreign Currency Convertible Debentures (FCCBs). Turnaround. Investment from pharmaceutical billionaire Dilip Shanghvi. Turnaround. Layoffs. Turnaround. Plans to raise additional Rs 900 crore by non-convertible debentures. Turnaround. Despite all of that, the company isn’t doing so well. Suzlon reported a consolidated net loss of Rs 470 crore in the fourth quarter of FY18 compared to a net profit of Rs 588 crore in the same quarter of the previous financial year. The total income from operations fell from Rs 4,999 crore in the fourth quarter of FY17 to Rs 2,244 crore. The other metrics don’t look good either.

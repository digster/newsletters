The third generation doesn’t want grease under their fingernails. It isn’t ecstatic about settling labour disputes or spending decades in sunset industries, either. Instead, it wants overseas equities, quarterly portfolio reviews, and maybe a rooftop coffee in Dubai while the money compounds.

That’s India’s latest thrill for the ultra-rich: turning family offices into the actual family business.

For a growing number of heirs, the family office is turning into the main act. Why run a textile mill in Surat when you can run a portfolio from your laptop? Why haggle with suppliers when you can “diversify” into luxury stocks in Paris?

Take Ankit Minocha, who inherited a commercial leasing business in Delhi. A nice, steady cash flow. He went on to launch Adezi Ventures, a family office that now makes up more of the family’s income than the original business. Others are following. Pharma heirs. Textile heirs. Even election commissioners’ grandchildren. Factories are being sold; investment committees are being formed.

It’s fun. It’s also… maybe not sustainable. The Nifty has given investors precisely nothing this past year. “Animal spirits”, as Uday Kotak likes to remind everyone, don’t live inside a spreadsheet. And those 20–30% returns from family businesses are hard to replicate with ETFs.

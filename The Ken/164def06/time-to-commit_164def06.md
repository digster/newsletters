I see you. Sitting there, enjoying the succour of the weekend. We intrigue you, why else would you be reading this on a day when most people simply want to switch off. Maybe you’re on the brink of purchasing a subscription, you’re just looking for something to push you towards it.
Hopefully, this mail will be that push. But regardless, I get your hesitation. I do. The moment before you actually commit to something is possibly the most nerve-wracking time of all. The actual doing, not so much. That’s a formality. But being poised on the edge of possibility not knowing what’s next, that’s hard. Sometimes, though, it pays to just take that leap. Our stories this week encapsulate this sentiment in different ways.

Blume isn’t in the best position. Its Fund I is nearing an end, with exits proving difficult and returns still a way away from what investors were promised. Its founder Karthik Reddy, though, remains undeterred. Now, even as the firm seems stuck in VC limbo, Blume is backing itself to redeem its Fund I, while going all out to raise a new fund.

Daga, the man behind brands like Wild Stone and Secret Temptation, knows the importance of committing all too well. Starting off with a tiny Kirana store, Daga put all his eggs in one basket when he decided to start a talcum powder company. That business venture has today culminated in McNROE Consumer Products, a Rs 400-plus crore behemoth.

MedGenome is betting big on NIPT, the gold standard of prenatal screening. However, with both government and insurers refusing to foot the bill for the expensive procedure, it’s down to MedGenome to find a way to drive the procedure’s popularity. Ruhi’s piece looks at how they intend to.

Commitment as a result of inevitability. Sometimes, choice is just an illusion when faced with the unstoppable march of progress. 5G is on its way, and incumbent holders of spectrum will have to make room for the new tech on the block. It was the week’s free story, do check it out.

With a glut of regulators all vying for control of the space, even as the Centre currently mulls its own legislation, businesses are left to navigate the increasingly tricky regulatory terrain.

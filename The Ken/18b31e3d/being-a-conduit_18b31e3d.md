Good Morning Ishan,
It’s been a week of nearly incessant downpours in the evenings and nights in Bengaluru, which I largely missed because I was in Mumbai most of the week.
But it did make me think about how we process the rain differently as adults. For many years now, the first thoughts heavy rains trigger in me are, “Great. I hope it flows into the lakes and rivers and dams and fills them up till the next monsoons.”
I can’t help it. Because water shortages are now a perennial occurrence for most of us every year, I’ve started “seeing” even the beauty of the rain through a utilitarian lens. That’s our Earth’s reality today.
You must have heard some version of the quote, “We don’t inherit the earth, but merely look after it for our children.”
Looking after something that doesn’t truly belong to us is a concept that exists in business too. It’s called “fiduciary responsibility”.
A fiduciary responsibility implies you’re looking after someone’s else’s assets, whether it be money or a company. And that you are bound, legally and ethically, to put their interests ahead of your own through your actions, not your own.
We’re not legally bound to safeguard the earth for our children and future generations, but I would say we can’t escape our ethical responsibilities.
Fiduciary responsibility is a phrase that gets tossed around a lot in corporate and legal circles, but rarely with deep thought.
It popped up in my conversation with Ruchi Kalra, the co-founder and CEO of business lending platform Oxyzo and also the co-founder of B2B procurement platform OfBusiness. Both are unicorns.
Ruchi didn’t use the word fiduciary responsibility when I asked her about the mental model she turned to most often when faced with major and tough decisions. But she didn’t need to either.
She said the first thought that comes to her mind when someone comes to her with a tough problem or decision is, “How do I explain it to a shareholder?”
“If I have to take a decision, how will I play it back to the stakeholders on whose behalf I am taking that decision? I am responsible on behalf of shareholders and stakeholders. Thus, can I convince them? Is it good for the company? If yes, then I have internalised that (the tough decision).”
I’m not fully convinced yet, because I cannot imagine how this works at an emotional and conceptual level when you’re in the thick of things. Ruchi’s answer seemed too theoretical to me.
So, I asked her again.
“Can I be the conduit to answer that question on behalf of shareholders?”
Then it made sense. Ruchi placed herself as not just a guardian of the company’s present and future on behalf of its shareholders and stakeholders, but also as a conduit.

I could definitely visualise the concept of being a conduit for decision-making. But my subsequent research led me to [an interesting article](https://fromthegreennotebook.com/2018/04/26/how-good-leaders-communicate-conduits-vs-reservoirs/) that expanded Ruchi’s concept further into broader aspects of leadership.
|
A common trait in all great leaders is being a conduit of knowledge – disseminating and sharing what they know to those who could benefit from their knowledge. This is palpable in any organization: observe the way information flows and you can tell if a leader serves as a conduit of information or as a reservoir, requiring subordinates to pull information from them.
Reservoir leaders typically are not aware of their own shortcoming. Reservoirs believe they are effective because subordinates constantly seek them out for information, input, or opinions. This creates a perception of positive leadership; the leader issues initial guidance, the operator returns for feedback, and all appears to be well. However, if the reservoir is removed, no additional information will be forthcoming and all momentum stops. This is the fallacy: subordinates working for a reservoir cannot operate independently. Subordinate elements cannot function in the leader’s absence.
By contrast, a conduit shares all available information with action elements, enabling continuity of effective operations even when the source is removed. Through effective dissemination, subordinate and adjacent elements can determine the best courses of action without circling back for detailed guidance. The choice to operate as a conduit rather than a reservoir of information can be difficult, because it removes the outward perception of positive feedback.
|
|
How Good Leaders Communicate: Conduits vs Reservoirs, Ryan C. Boileau

Ruchi Kalra
I am paranoid about…that’s a difficult one.
I don’t know. I actually don’t know. But I think I am very paranoid about when I see that there’s been a system which is built. And if I get a sense that someone is trying to bypass the system, then that person has had it. You tell me that you’re doing it.
You are facing this issue. That’s fine. Then we figure out a way together. But if you try to do it sheepishly and you try to figure out a way to just cross-circuit it, that’s something that I…

Because I think in the growth phase, it’s equally important to set the right processes, set the right systems. And the orientation, whether you are in sales or operations, etc., should be towards making the system better.
I’m absolutely game for the fact that you’ll need exceptions. Absolutely, this is a space.

This week was also when we released episode 27 of the First Principles podcast, with Lalit Keshre, the co-founder and CEO of Groww. Here are a few snippets, in case you haven’t listened to it yet.
He talks about why customer experience is the most important business goal at Groww, not revenue.
|
At the business level, at a company level, we don’t set financial targets for anybody. There is a small team, which works on monetisation and everything.
As I said, we are very over-indexed on customer experience. So you go and talk to anybody in the company, and their primary focus will be how to provide the best customer experience. The entire company is… that’s like the North Star for the entire company. That is the culture of the company, everywhere people would be talking about providing the best customer experience.
[…]
At Groww, our hypothesis was that we will build a D2C brand—direct to consumer, will not have… will talk to customers directly, will understand customers directly, and we will be kind of close to them. And to build that, the biggest requirement is that you need to be a pull brand.
You can’t be a push brand.

And what kind of culture exists within Groww.
|
Rohin Dharmakumar
What kind of people succeed at Groww? And what kind of people don’t?
Lalit Keshre
Yeah, very good question. A lot of candidates also ask me this question.
Number one is if you don’t have customer obsession, it’s very difficult to… aisay bolte hai naa… Groww ke andar sau galtiyan maaf (they say at Groww a hundred mistakes can be forgiven), but this is not acceptable if you take the customer for granted.
Number two, ownership.
So Groww is also a very non-hierarchical and non-bureaucratic company. Even at a very junior level, I interact directly. When you talk, it is not like you’re talking to the VP, or you’re talking to an engineer or associate programme manager or product manager and so on.
So, ownership is very important. Ki aap le raho ho kuch woh aap kar rahe ho (you do what you have taken on). You can’t make excuses ki usne nahi diya (that person did not give). You can’t blame others, which is not accepted.
These are the two major things.
And the third which helps is curiosity.
Rohin Dharmakumar
How do you judge curiosity when you’re having a conversation with someone?
Lalit Keshre
See, there are a lot of things. There are a lot of ways.
But one simple way is… let’s say I ask you, what have you done in the past? And then you tell, and then I ask. So I’m also in general, curious, and you ask another thing. So how many levels deeper can you go? Can you go 10 levels deeper?
Can I ask 10 questions on one particular topic? If I can’t go beyond two, three, it’s very, very tough to say that you’re curious. But if I can go deeper, so in general, people say that if a candidate is being interviewed with me for one hour, that’s a very good thing.

Good Morning Ishan,
 
History and tradition are closely intertwined, often spoken in the same breath, often mistaken for each other. But they’re a bit of an odd pair.
 
Both have the weight of years behind them, but only one has the advantage of hindsight. History enables you to look back and learn what to do and what not to, whereas tradition wraps you up in the comfortable arms of the “established”. It leads you down a path of familiarity that you eventually loathe deviating from.
 
Tradition is both the angel and the devil on your shoulders; one offers you guidance and comfort, the other binds you tightly.
 
We published just four stories at The Ken this week due to Holi, but all of them straddled the above themes of history, tradition, and modernity. They explored change, made a case for breaking out of moulds, and talked about why too much of anything—even if it comes from millennia of history and tradition—isn’t a good thing.
 
On Wednesday, in our free story of the week, Maitri wrote about the dangers of herbal drugs in the context of the Covid-19 pandemic. Everyone wants to shore up their immunity, and as a consequence, herbal “immunity boosters” have become as commonplace, popular, and easy to stock up as instant noodles. The result? A long string of avoidable liver injuries.
 
On Thursday, Anand wrote about how the National Stock Exchange—originally envisioned as a solution to the monopoly of the century-old Bombay Stock Exchange—has itself turned into a problem child. There’ve been glitches, scandals, everything that the NSE hoped to avoid, but to no avail. Do India’s stock exchanges need an overhaul?
 
On Friday, we had Sumanth writing about San Francisco-based Superhuman.com, a premium email client that lets you shave seconds off every email operation. While this doesn’t add up to much, Sumanth argues that Superhuman is special because it has rethought all aspects of the business—product, market, marketing, and customers. India had a similar premium email app called Newton, which was cheaper and arguably better than Superhuman, but it failed. What can Indian startups learn from this?
 
On Tuesday, Pranav tackled a story about the bane of existence of much of India’s road-bound passengers—the age-old auto rickshaws. The mode of transport is tightly regulated, its drivers are unionised, and most of all, it couldn’t hold a candle to the air-conditioned comfort of cabs. Until now. The pandemic has now kicked off a three-way three-wheeler race, and ironically, two of the three in the race are companies that traditionally shunned autos for the very reasons quoted above.
 
History is usually a favourite topic of Praveen’s in our Saturday newsletter, The Nutgraf, where he draws parallels from centuries-ago battles and backgrounds to drive home what’s happening in India today. This week, he talks about the history of Monopoly and which form of the tedious board game the Reserve Bank of India is playing.
 
This week, we also celebrated one year of launching our daily newsletter Beyond the First Order. For our anniversary edition, we had Nithin writing about what is happening to the Great Indian Middle class. Olina looked at the consequence of not having a Covid-friendly exam solution and as a result, exam fever has taken on a new meaning at IIM Indore. And Arundhati wrote about what impact Mobikwik’s alleged data leak could have on RBI’s rules around recurring payments.
 
That’s it from us this week. I hope you have a fantastic Sunday. Stay safe (and wash your hands).

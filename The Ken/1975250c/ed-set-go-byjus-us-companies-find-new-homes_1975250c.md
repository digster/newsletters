When does an international company stop being truly international? When it doesn’t own any foreign assets anymore, or when it loses its international audience?
The founders of Byju’s—once the largest edtech company in the world—may be asking themselves this question. This week, [two of the three](https://marketbrief.edweek.org/strategy-operations/coding-provider-tynker-sold-for-2-1m-as-byjus-bankruptcy-plays-out/2025/06) companies Byju’s had acquired in its brief but eventful US stint were sold off by the group of lenders who’d sued it for repayment of a billion-dollar loan.
The two entities—Epic! and Tynker—were well-established names in the US education landscape. Epic! was Byju’s pipeline into schools, as nearly every school in the US had a subscription for its books. Tynker, a large coding platform for kids, was a more new-age acquisition, and was Byju’s way to enter the D2C market for online classes. Once Whitehat Jr failed to expand its business to the US, Byju’s would’ve relied on Tynker to take over the mantle.
As it panned out, despite the inherent strengths and popularity of these companies, Byju’s never had the chance to fully leverage them. Epic! came with an eye-popping price tag of $500 million, while Tynker was acquired at $200 million.
In a bid to settle accounts, these companies have now been sold at a massive haircut. Tal Education, the Chinese edtech megafirm, has bought Epic! for $95 million, while Tynker went for just $2.2 million to a competing coding-for-schools firm called Codehs.
This leaves only one US-based company, Osmo, for the lenders to sell off—but they’re still quite a few million dollars short of recouping their loan amount.
In the meantime, though, these sales mean that Byju Raveendran’s efforts to revive his company, and the edtech dream, will likely be confined to Indian shores or in the UAE, where he now lives with his family.

As we wrote in 2023, Byju’s paid a cool $950 million for its US acquisitions, but the outside world never quite knew how the entities would work together, or if they’d fold into the larger Byju’s brand. Byju’s announcements in the US were always about celebrity endorsements and fundraises—there was little, if any, talk of strategy. At an investor’s conference in March 2023, Divya Gokulnath took centre stage to discuss Byju’s “AI plans” and demoed a compilation of static screens that was supposed to be Byju’s next big tech move.
None of that materialised. And while Epic! did add Byju’s branding to its masthead briefly, there was no real integration of product or strategy.
Tynker, on the other hand, had an Indian cofounder Krishna Vedati, who was appointed as the strategic head of Byju’s US operations shortly after the acquisition. Vedati even toured conferences on Byju’s behalf, after a string of India-based leaders had exited the company.
Raveendran carved out a [big role](https://www.afaqs.com/people-spotting/byjus-appoints-krishna-vedati-as-president-of-global-growth-and-strategic-initiatives) for Vedati:
In the newly-created role of president of global growth and strategic initiatives for BYJU’S, Vedati will serve as a senior executive for the Americas helping drive topline revenue and user growth, partnerships with major brands, and identifying strategic acquisitions. Vedati will also expand the company’s international offering by overseeing new integrated products and services from the BYJU’S learning portfolio to students and teachers in Canada, the U.S., and Latin America.
At conferences, Vedati would go on to push back against early rumours of trouble within the company, and defend the layoffs that Byju’s had started in India. He was determined to be a good soldier in the cause of Byju’s global expansion. It’s a pity he didn’t have much time before the company imploded.
In a much-publicised interview with ANI, Raveendran claimed that he was confident of regaining control of his company again. “Mistakes were made” was a common refrain in the interview, though he dodged actual culpability by portraying himself as a victim of his own ambition.
It seems hard, though, given that the company is literally being sold for parts in the US and is operationally defunct in India. Raveendran’s response to that is: “I’ll just start a new one.”
Will it have the same global ambition? Will it be based on the same ruthless sales tactics the company previously espoused? Will its founder still sell out entire stadiums for CAT prep classes? Raveendran, at least, says yes.

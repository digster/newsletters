It is only fair that more than a decade should have gone by. In the year 2008, when I was young and ambitious, I was chasing a story on battery-operated vehicles and the Ministry of New & Renewable Energy (MNRE). Back then, MNRE had come up with an electric vehicle (EV) funding programme. Rs 12 crore in support for Research & Development projects on advanced high energy density batteries, ultracapacitors, control systems and other components for battery-operated electric and hybrid electric vehicles for surface transportation.
 
I was very excited. Whoa, I thought. Progress.
 
In 2009, as part of an ambitious editorial project, I studied EVs—from the Nissan Leaf (Leading, Environment Friendly, Affordable, Family Cars) to the Chevrolet Volt to the EV1 conspiracy, the Tata Indica doing pilot projects in Norway—on calls with senior executives from these companies, and chatting with experts and authors.   
 
I was very excited. Whoa, I thought. Progress.
 
In 2012, I sat opposite Carlos Ghosn, former chairman of one of the world’s largest car combine, Nissan & Renault, discussing if EVs are closer to the mass market. He said they are, and that they make total sense for a country like India. He added: emerging market leaders will be part of this transformation. 
 
I was very excited. Whoa, I thought. Progress.
 
Which is why reading my colleague Olina’s story today gives me goosebumps. 
 
She says 2019 is the year of the electric vehicle. When it all comes together. The policy called the Faster Adoption of (Hybrid &) Electric Manufacturing scheme, or FAME-II, was passed last week. Carmakers of all classes, budget to luxury, and everything in between, say they will launch an EV soon. Maybe as early as this year itself. But, and there is always a but, this storm could also leave a disaster in its wake. India’s capacity to engineer and produce EVs is as middling as it is scarce.
 
There are few truly qualified engineers to build EVs. Talent is scarce, like, well, talent. No seriously, it is as bad as it sounds. Today’s piece is a must read, and it's free. Here: https://the-ken.com/story/electric-vehicles-talent-gap-future/

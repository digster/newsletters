I’ll confess—I haven’t had an anti-virus software on my laptop for years now. In fact, none of us at The Ken do. 
 
Okay that was a trick opening. We all use Apple MacBooks, which don’t really need an anti-virus.
 
But in an age of daily security breaches, crypto extortion, and sophisticated viruses that can steal your data, your money or your CPU power, anti-virus software is the first line of defence for most people.
 
There’s just one problem—few people seem to want to pay for it.
 
That’s the unfortunate problem Quick Heal Technologies, India’s most popular anti-virus software company, is facing. It has a 34% market share, sure. But its revenue growth rate over the last two years is an anaemic 2.65%. When virtually every software or internet company is growing rapidly thanks to the explosion of India’s Internet population (from 8.5% in 2010 to 36.5% in 2017).
 
The likes of Avast globally and Qihoo 360 in China have shown that the freemium model is better suited to anti-virus software. Or that the best way to reach hundreds of millions of consumers was online.
 
Quickheal instead relies on an army of thousands of channel partners to push its product. A model that’s under threat, too.
 

What options does it have to escape being commoditised and disrupted? Read Sidhartha’s story to find out: https://the-ken.com/story/no-easy-cure-for-quick-heal/

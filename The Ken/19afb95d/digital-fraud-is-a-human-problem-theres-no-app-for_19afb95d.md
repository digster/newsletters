Make India Competitive Again
Mon, 08 Dec 25
What India is doing, will do, and should do—to not just survive but thrive in the chaos unleashed by Trump.
Good Morning Ishan,
The Indian government changed its mind last Wednesday.
The telecom ministry withdrew its order requiring phonemakers to pre-install Sanchar Saathi, a state-run cybersecurity app, on every new mobile device sold in India. The plan lasted barely a week. It’s surveillance-by-default, said the Opposition; civil-liberties groups said it bulldozed consent, and handset makers—who would be the ones actually forced to do the bulldozing—looked like they would rather not.
So the Centre stepped back.
But the entire episode has proved instructive because it sits at the intersection of two India-scale truths.
First, India has digitised at a breathtaking pace over the past decade through digital infrastructure, including connectivity, identity, payments, and data sharing. Policy and regulatory support, domestic tech talent, and a vast consumer base have led to lasting economic, commercial, and social benefits, including greater inclusivity, transparency, and affordability of formal financial services, particularly banking and payments.
The most visible symbols of this success are the ubiquitous QR codes, waved at you by rickshaw drivers, flower sellers, delivery agents, and so on. Removing friction from everyday life is a good thing.
Second, the government would very much like this infrastructure to be safe. After all, it had allocated Rs 782 crore in Union Budget 2025–26 for cybersecurity.
But the problem is that the moment it tries to bolt a security measure onto millions of devices by force, everyone remembers that they like privacy, too.
Reuters reported last week that India is [also weighing](https://www.reuters.com/sustainability/boards-policy-regulation/india-weighs-greater-phone-location-surveillance-apple-google-samsung-protest-2025-12-05/) deeper phone-location tracking powers—proposals Apple, Google, and Samsung have already opposed. In other words, Sanchar Saathi wasn’t an isolated misstep; it was an early skirmish in a larger battle.
And the backdrop is unavoidable.
One particularly Indian innovation is the social-engineering fraud called “digital arrest”.
For those of you outside India, this involves authentic-looking fraudsters impersonating law-enforcement officials entrapping gullible victims over video calls, and extracting vast sums of money to escape penalties for fictitious offences “detected in their name”.
If you pare it down, it works on a few basics:
Conditioning
Crime-based television shows—Crime Patrol being the category-defining example—have enjoyed massive viewership for over a decade. Parrot Analytics data [shows](https://tv.parrotanalytics.com/IN/crime-patrol-sony-entertainment-television-india) Crime Patrol Dial at 12X the demand of an average Indian series in the past month, indicating extraordinary sustained interest. Even on traditional TV, the franchise continues to command steady linear audiences.
They have, inadvertently, normalised the visual shorthand for authority: a stern face, a khaki uniform, and a badge. For millions of middle-class linear-TV viewers, this is enough to trigger instinctive compliance. The System 1 brain sees “cop,” not “actor”.
Creativity
Smartphone cameras and editing apps have made it trivial to produce professional-looking videos. Backgrounds can mimic police stations or government offices; scripts can be lifted straight from the crime shows above. In a world where deepfake tools are getting cheaper by the month, this will only intensify.
Data hygiene
We routinely shout out our mobile numbers at supermarket cash counters for loyalty points of dubious value. Bank branches still need customers to verbally confirm account details in public spaces. And the digital economy’s weak points—data leaks, cloud misconfigurations, lax access controls—have multiplied.
Take one example: in August, a cloud misconfiguration at Indian fintech called Nupay exposed a publicly accessible Amazon Web Services server containing 273,000 PDF documents of bank transfers across institutions, as [discovered](https://techcrunch.com/2025/09/26/thousands-of-indian-bank-transfer-records-found-online/) by Upguard.
Cybersecurity incidents reported in India doubled from 1 million in 2022 to 2.2 million in 2024, per government data. Officials told Parliament that citizens lost more than Rs 22,800 crore in 2024 to cybercriminals

Fear of authority:
All authority is instinctively feared. School headmasters, cops, taxmen, RTO officials… the list is long, but the attitude is the same. While their job is to protect and facilitate, the common perception is that they wield power to harm. Except for the powerful few who can buy influence, the common man fears authority.
Put all of this together, and you have the perfect recipe for fraud: credulous consumers, creative fraudsters, an innate fear of authority, and a frictionless digital ecosystem that makes moving money easy. And what is today a cottage industry will only get worse as AI tools become commonplace and affordable. Imagine scambots operating social engineering frauds at internet scale!
The typical response from the government has been to come up with digital tools to contain this menace. Initiatives like SIM-binding and fraud-tracking apps are well-intentioned but misplaced.
DoT’s SIM-binding directive—which forces messaging apps, such as Whatsapp, Telegram, and others, to tie accounts to active SIM cards—remains in force. Authorities claim to have blocked 0.9 million SIM cards and 0.26 million IMEIs linked to cyber fraud in a year
Social problems need human solutions; digital tools can only help.
These suggestions are directional, not exhaustively researched. But each borrows from what has worked elsewhere.
- Use the same crime shows as communication platforms. I don’t watch TV, but I’m guessing that most episodes show popular stuff like killings and physical violence. Why can’t we create specific shows or episodes that recreate
[digital-arrest frauds](https://www.bloomberg.com/opinion/features/2025-10-12/digital-arrests-inside-india-s-scariest-scam-and-how-to-stop-it)and show people how to be more sceptical and careful? - Introduce good friction. Nudge account holders to set transaction limits for value and volume through different channels: online and offline. Rework digital workflows to hold transactions until an additional human verification is done for out-of-pattern transactions, even if initiated by account holders.
- Train banking staff to be more watchful. Update their SOPs to identify suspicious transactions. There’s a
[heartwarming story](https://timesofindia.indiatimes.com/city/gurgaon/alert-axis-bank-manager-foils-digital-arrest-bid-saves-rs-64-lakh-of-senior-citizen-in-gurgaon/articleshow/125493344.cms)about an alert and empathetic bank manager who prevented a fraudulent transaction. Make this the norm rather than the exception. - Create a blocklist of accounts that were used to receive fraudulent transactions. Go deep into how they were set up, what KYC processes were weak, and what additional steps we need to weed out such accounts. Re-validate accounts that have been dormant for over X months, though this may be a nuisance for some genuine users. Hurry up,
[mule](https://the-ken.com/story/a-rs-2500-cr-scam-powered-by-mule-accounts-an-illustrated-guide/)-detection[solutions](https://rbihub.in/projects/mulehunter)that are still in the the pilot stage. - Track the IP addresses that fraudsters use while initiating video calls. Get the platforms to start recording sessions initiated by these IP addresses automatically. Automatically record sessions that last beyond, say, two hours. Let all participants know that the session is being recorded and transcribed. Perhaps this will scare off fraudsters from hanging up.
- Design digital-insurance products for the masses, like companies that have cyber-insurance policies. For a nominal monthly outgo, create a sizeable cover to protect for extreme losses. And target these products at the right segments as described here.
- Family members and neighbours often spot behavioural changes before institutions do. Public campaigns should empower them to intervene early.
A frictionless digital economy is hugely net positive. Ironically, the victims of digital fraud are usually the digitally unsavvy and the most vulnerable. Protecting them will take a whole-of-society approach, a human touch, and coordinated government intervention.
India has done this before. During the pandemic, the country administered over 2.2 billion vaccine doses across tens of thousands of vaccination sites in under two years, powered by the Cowin stack but executed by millions of frontline workers. It was a masterclass in mission-mode governance.
Around 106,000 police officers have now been trained in cybercrime investigation, showing the scale of human capacity the government is mobilising
We will need a similar mission-mode effort now.

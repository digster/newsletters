Ka-Ching!
Thu, 30 Oct 25
A weekly newsletter about how finance is getting supercharged by tech in India, and how you can make money work for you.
Good Morning Ishan,
The other day, over a pint at The Bier Library in Bengaluru’s Koramangala—because not every “coffee chat” means Starbucks—a soonicorn founder said something that stuck.
“I took a personal loan using the Cred app,” he told me, “because the bank wanted me to apply first with a long list of details and then do a video KYC. I wasn’t comfortable with all of that.”
He wasn’t complaining about interest rates or repayment terms. He was complaining about friction. “It’s the whole process. The conversations with the bank. The endless requests for documents. It’s all just… too much,” he sighed.
Traditionally, one would assume digital-lending apps and non-banks (NBFCs) are for people with patchy credit scores. Not the upper crust of startup founders or mid-level IT professionals with solid Cibil scores and high incomes. Apparently, that assumption is outdated.
After that conversation, I checked around. Half a dozen founders admitted they, too, would rather borrow from apps like Cred or Navi than from a traditional bank. And they’re not outliers. The salaried middle class, the ones banks have spent decades wooing with glossy personal-loan ads, are [increasingly choosing](https://www.loansjagat.com/blog/why-most-borrowers-are-choosing-nbfcs-over-banks) digital non-banks instead.
The math isn’t even that different. “Banks offer ‘instant credit’ at 10–16%; sometimes even more. Cred, Navi, KreditBee… they start at around 10–12% and sometimes go up to 25%. At times, the deal is better than what banks offer,” another borrower told me. “Sure, the rates can vary, but it’s the quick approval that matters.”
Digital non-banks approve loans within 24–48 hours. Banks, despite all the “digital transformation” talk, still take 3–7 business days. And unlike many banks, apps like Moneytap or Cred keep a pre-approved credit line ready.
“These platforms offer better services as well,” claimed the founder of a D2C company. “You could close the loan whenever you want. If it’s by a bank, it’s not easy. Their app or website does not offer that facility. You may have to visit their offices.” And time, for these individuals, is costlier than the interest difference.
To be clear, this is not about those “Chinese” lending apps the Enforcement Directorate [keeps probing](https://www.thehindu.com/news/national/ed-attaches-1939-crore-in-assets-linked-to-operators-of-instant-loan-apps/article68555003.ece). These are digital NBFCs registered with the central bank.
And they have been busy. The Reserve Bank of India’s [Financial Stability Report](https://rbidocs.rbi.org.in/rdocs/PublicationReport/Pdfs/0FSRJUNE20253006258AE798B4484642AD861CC35BC2CB3D8E.PDF) for the first half of FY2025 states NBFCs and fintechs now account for 84% of personal loans under Rs 50,000.
A self-regulatory organisation for fintechs recently [put numbers](https://faceofindia.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/10/Digital-Personal-Loans-Jun-2025.pdf) to that dominance by analysing personal loans disbursed by over 110 digital NBFCs between April 2022 and June 2025. Also, in the first quarter of this fiscal alone, over 30 million of the 38 million personal loans sanctioned came from digital NBFCs, according to credit bureau CRIF.
Sure, the ticket sizes are smaller—about Rs 14,000 versus Rs 4 lakh for banks, per the self-regulator’s report—but that’s the nature of these unsecured, quick-turnaround loans. Think small, fast, and algorithmically approved.
In terms of market share, digital NBFCs are eating the banks’ lunch:
- Their share of total personal loan value rose from ~29% in Q3 FY23 to ~37% in Q3 FY25.
- Their share of loan volume (number of loans) jumped from 85% to nearly 93% over the same period.
That’s despite an overall decline in personal-loan originations. So yes, people are borrowing less overall. But when they do, many prefer fintechs.

Two clear shifts are driving this, per the industry report: Gen Z and rural India.
Over 60% of digital-loan customers are under 35. Nearly 44% have less than five years of credit history. And the share of borrowers from tier-3 and rural regions rose to 36–40% for various digital non-banks in Q2 2025.
| Fintech Association for Consumer Empowerment |
Personal loans were growing steadily until late 2024, then dipped in early 2025, thanks to the RBI’s crackdown on unsecured lending and [higher risk weights](https://www.rbi.org.in/Scripts/NotificationUser.aspx?Id=12567&Mode=0&utm_source=chatgpt.com) for consumer credit. [Rising delinquencies](https://www.moneylife.in/article/unsecured-loans-see-rising-delinquencies-amid-economic-uncertainty-report/76511.html) didn’t help either.
Still, the data tells a story. Only 8% of borrowers went to banks for loans, but those few accounted for 60% of the total loan value. Meanwhile, digital lenders issued smaller, faster, riskier loans to the masses.
Which raises a question: are banks becoming mere license partners for fintechs? The rails, not the engine?
| Fintech Association for Consumer Empowerment |
Just this week, Flipkart’s Prakash Sikaria announced SuperAccount—powered, of course, by Kotak Mahindra Bank.
Fintechs may look like the future, but they still need a bank’s license and deposit infrastructure to exist. Banks, meanwhile, need fintechs’ user interfaces to stay relevant. It’s a weird symbiosis. The digital lenders get regulatory cover, and the banks get to feel cool again.
To be fair, banks haven’t been lazy. Over the past decade, they have [pivoted](https://x.com/kaul_vivek/status/1983041129231614108) aggressively from corporate lending to retail loans. That shift, along with [other factors](https://www.pib.gov.in/PressReleasePage.aspx?PRID=2146819), helped slash bad loans from 11.2% in 2018 to 2.3% now.
But that same focus has starved small and medium enterprises of credit. And the corporate-bond market hasn’t quite filled the gap, as economic commentator Vivek Kaul recently [noted](https://x.com/kaul_vivek/status/1983041129231614108). So while retail is booming, the broader credit ecosystem looks increasingly lopsided.
UPI changed how Indians spend. Gen Z now expects everything—payments, credit, even wealth management—to exist in a tap-friendly digital format. Banks are still adjusting, trying to decide how much risk is worth taking before the RBI shows up with a tough circular.
This is what economists Peter Howitt and Philippe Aghion—of “creative destruction” and the [Nobel Prize in Economics](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/economic-sciences/2025/press-release/) fame—were talking about. Innovation keeps creating new markets, and the incumbents always pay the price.

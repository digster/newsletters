Delhivery’s Sahil Barua is bang on about quick commerce. And also wrong. It isn't just kiranas that are under threat

By Seetharaman G
4 min read
View in browser

Good Morning Ishan,

All those railing against the deleterious effects of quick commerce got some serious ammunition last week. A research firm said rapid-delivery apps would take away US$1.3 billion of kiranas’ sales this year. (Kiranas are small neighbourhood stores.) What’s more, a national body of traders published a “white paper” outlining how the likes of Blinkit and Instamart allegedly violate foreign-invesment and competition norms.

When Sahil Barua, co-founder and chief executive of logistics firm Delhivery, joined the debate by saying on an earnings call that quick commerce is disrupting kiranas more than conventional e-commerce (read Amazon and Flipkart), it came as no surprise. But to be fair to Barua, he had said as much in August, when his counterpart at Blinkit-parent Zomato, Deepinder Goyal, argued the opposite in an interview with The Economic Times.

“Blinkit is not really eating into the kiranas at all. It is not even affecting companies like Dmart. I think we’re more or less eating into the Amazons and the Flipkarts, the ecommerce sector, as well as modern retail in the larger cities.”

As early as this April, my colleague Aakriti Bhalla marshalled data to make the case that kiranas do need to be worried. But why would Goyal not accept what is increasingly obvious?

I’m not quite convinced that Blinkit hasn’t impacted kiranas. 

I understand, though, why Zomato would want you to believe it hasn’t. The reason is in all the perception battles that large retailers and e-tailers have had to fight over the years. Given how unequal the fight is, these companies don’t want to be seen crushing mom-and-pop stores.

Zomato doesn’t want to make a big deal of its disruption, The Ken

So Barua is right and Goyal isn’t. But only about the consequences for kiranas. 

They swap places on quick commerce’s impact on e-commerce.  This was Barua on an earnings call in August:

The reality of e-commerce is that a lot of the inventory is not fast-moving inventory. So, to give you an example, Meesho as a marketplace platform doesn’t necessarily lend itself to a quick commerce format. And when you add that up across categories, marketplaces, the long tail that’s available online, our view is that the actual volume that’s at risk on e-commerce actually is likely to be pretty small. And so, I don’t think 30-minute or one-hour sort of delivery timelines are going to massively disrupt the broader swath of e-commerce. I think there are a set of narrow categories, and even within that, a set of narrow SKUs within those categories, where the model makes sense.

Yes, fast-moving inventory—or, in other words, grocery—is the lifeblood of quick commerce. And it’s impossible to deliver hundreds of thousands of products in 10 minutes. But instant-delivery apps don’t have to because much of Amazon and Flipkart’s long tail is meaningless.

I’m not sure how far the likes of Blinkit and Instamart will go in their quest to take on Amazon and Flipkart. They are constrained, in a good way, by the size of their warehouses and their rapid-delivery promise. They will never be “everything stores”, but it’s this inability that allows them to convince consumers to be okay with limited choices.

The tyranny of choice in online shopping is no longer a given, The Ken

Blinkit, Zepto, and Instamart have also been incredibly nimble in tapping very specific use cases—selling gold on Akshaya Tritya and Deepavali gift hampers. Are these frequent purchases? Of course not. But this is part of the companies’ attempt to persuade consumers to think of them as more than just destinations for impulse buying. 

Selling electronics and appliances serve the same purpose. Bigbasket is happy to deliver a Rs 7,500 (US$90) LG microwave oven or Rs 42,000 Bose speakers from Croma in 15 minutes. 

I understand why Barua might want to be sanguine about the resilience of e-commerce as we know it. He has a dog in the fight. Ferrying shipments for online marketplaces and direct-to-consumer brands helps bring in 60% of Delhivery’s revenue. It’s also its most profitable vertical. 

Delhivery is also building dark stores, or small warehouses, to be shared by brands that cannot afford to have their own.  The company believes it can group orders and optimise routes to deliver in 2–4 hours without losing money. In other words, what it has been doing in all these years, only with a shorter timeline. 

Ten minutes or two hours—the definition of “instant” delivery varies depending on the product. Having a refrigerator delivered and installed in a couple of hours is as instant as it can get. 

The point is, the business Delhivery cut its teeth in—next-day or same-day delivery—is without a doubt under attack.

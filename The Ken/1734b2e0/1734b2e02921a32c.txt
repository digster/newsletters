Let's start with a pop quiz. If it takes one minister one day to come up with an acronym, how many days, and how many people does it take to build out the acronym?

The acronym in question is 'Swayam', which is Hindi for self. And it stands for a befuddling 'Study Webs of Active Learning for Young Aspiring Minds'.

But what's in a name? Swayam's purpose was noble—to be a one-stop national melting pot of quality online courses from grade nine physics to university-level robotics, accessible to students from all walks of life.

"Tailor-made for pandemic times," I hear you say?

Yes and no. Meaning that while most online courses shot out of the blocks in the lockdown-enforced edtech race, Swayam was still lacing its boots and fixing its gear.

You see, like its name, most of Swayam was retrofitted. Teachers were stood in front of green screens, only to dish out the same sage-on-stage lectures. Potential tech partners had to first build the application software and then customise it to Swayam's needs. And when finally built, Swayam was for laptop screens in a smartphone world. And let's not forget quality control.

Swayam's struggles were not due to lack of funds: its 2016-17 budget of Rs 75 crore ($9.9 million) had ballooned to Rs 117 crore ($15.5 million) by 2019. Nor for the lack of expertise, unless self-inflicted: a Rs 28 crore ($3.7 million) government audit deemed Swayam's predecessor—the IIT-run NPTEL—unsuitable for scaling.

Yet, NPTEL was given the operational reins of Swayam 2.0 in 2018. And still, Swayam is grappling with fundamental problems like teacher recruitment, writes Olina in her story today. It's as much a "what could have been" story as it is a "what can be" story.

Make India Competitive Again
Mon, 08 Sep 25
What India is doing, will do, and should do—to not just survive but thrive in the chaos unleashed by Trump.
Good Morning Ishan,
About a week ago, on 30 August, Indian Prime Minister Narendra Modi and his Japanese counterpart, Shigeru Ishiba, took the Hayabusa (Japan’s fastest bullet train) to whizz towards Miyagi prefecture at 320 kilometres per hour. Their [destination](https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/industry/cons-products/electronics/pm-modi-visits-semiconductor-plant-with-japans-ishiba-reaffirms-indo-japan-tech-partnership/articleshow/123599140.cms?from=mdr): semiconductor equipment giant Tokyo Electron Limited’s (TEL) facility in Sendai. Their objective: to spotlight heightened Indo-Japanese cooperation in the semiconductor sector.
During the same two-day visit, Japan [announced](https://indianexpress.com/article/india/behind-japan-68-billion-investment-in-india-a-message-to-us-president-trump-10220946/) a $68 billion private investment target for India over the next decade, some of which is bound to land up in India’s semiconductor sector. Tokyo Electron, for its part, [announced](https://www.moneycontrol.com/news/business/tokyo-electron-announces-new-development-hub-in-bengaluru-boosting-india-s-semiconductor-drive-13523443.html) just a few days later that it would build a new development hub in Bengaluru—one focused on software development, equipment design, and deeper R&D collaborations with the local ecosystem.
This isn’t just optics. In the inescapably international machine that is the global semiconductor industry, Japan is a crucial player; it [produces](https://www.csis.org/analysis/mapping-semiconductor-supply-chain-critical-role-indo-pacific-region) over half of the world’s semiconductor materials and one third of chip equipment. (TEL alone holds a 21% global market share in the latter.)
And Japan isn’t the only such heavyweight in the semiconductor value chain.
A typical integrated circuit chip can involve over 500 distinct production stages and cross as many as 70 international boundaries before it reaches the consumer. Across this complicated global supply chain, the scale of interdependencies is vast: China has a monopoly over certain critical minerals, the US and UK have a combined monopoly over chip design tools, and so on. Moreover, there are dozens of hyper-specialised suppliers spread across the world who hold virtual monopolies over critical process technologies needed to manufacture chips. They can act as technological ‘choke points’.
[In pursuit of a semiconductor industry: What India can learn from South Korea and Singapore, CSDR](https://csdronline.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/06/CSDR_In-Pursuit-of-a-Semi-Con-Industry_Jun-2025.pdf)
Smooth and disruption-free operations and linkages across such chokepoints is a prerequisite for any functional semiconductor ecosystem, especially one as ambitious as India is envisioning. After all, to quote PM Modi in his [address](https://www.pmindia.gov.in/en/news_updates/pms-speech-during-semicon-india-2025-at-yashobhoomi-delhi/) at the Semicon India event last week, India aims to develop no less than a “full-stack” semiconductor ecosystem.
Another such chokepoint, and an oft-quoted one in this context, is Taiwan. “An earthquake hits Hsinchu and we [the global chip industry] are finished,” Shashwat TR, CEO of semiconductor design firm Mindgrove Technologies told me on a call last week. Hsinchu, of course, is the location of the Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company’s (TSMC) headquarters, and critical for the [production](https://www.theguardian.com/world/article/2024/jul/19/taiwan-semiconductor-industry-booming) of over 90% of the world’s advanced chips (including Mindgrove’s own microcontrollers and Vision SoC camera chips).
| Source: McKinsey and Company, 2025 |
For an ecosystem as nascent as India’s, how well such risks are managed is directly linked to success. An earthquake that destroys all of TSMC may not be a very likely worry, but with US President Donald Trump’s foe-now-friend-now style of diplomacy, others aren’t so improbable. As both the US and China have shown in the past year, dominance in supply chains can and have been weaponised.
“People probably didn’t pay any attention to supply chain safety when they globalised. Now that geopolitics has entered into it, they realise what a problem it is,” noted GS Madhusudan, CEO of fabless semiconductor firm Incore Semiconductors.
With $10 billion now “fully committed” to 10 semiconductor projects, and phase two of its Semiconductor Mission soon to be [unveiled](https://telecom.economictimes.indiatimes.com/news/policy/india-semiconductor-mission-secures-10-billion-commitment-phase-two-announcement-imminent/123651135), there is much India can’t afford to ignore.
Of course, Madhusudan was quick to note that weaponised supply chains weren’t an immediate or overpowering risk. “Reconfiguration of supply chains take a long time, and nobody will weaponise beyond a point, because if you overleverage it, people will wise up and set up alternative supply chains.”
But some tariff and non-tariff barriers (such as investment restrictions) originally erected to reduce trade imbalances have already worked against India’s ambitions in the semiconductor space. Take, for example, India’s six-year-old decision to [withdraw](https://government.economictimes.indiatimes.com/blog/why-did-india-exit-rcep-largest-trading-bloc-in-history-with-a-combined-gdp-of-38-trillion/113008013#:~:text=India's%20decision%20to%20withdraw%20from,years%20validates%20India's%20major%20concern.) from the world’s largest multilateral free trade agreement—the Regional Comprehensive Economic Partnership (RCEP)—which includes China, Japan, South Korea, Australia, New Zealand, and all 10 ASEAN countries.
For some time now, World Bank data has [shown](https://documents1.worldbank.org/curated/en/099812010312311610/pdf/IDU0938e50fe0608704ef70b7d005cda58b5af0d.pdf) that the [main beneficiaries](https://www.business-standard.com/external-affairs-defence-security/news/india-s-success-in-capturing-china-plus-one-strategy-limited-niti-report-124120400682_1.html) of the China-plus-one strategy adopted by companies looking to reduce dependence on China (by setting up facilities in other countries) are actually countries with deep trade and investment ties to China’s ecosystem, such as Vietnam and Malaysia.
To quote a senior executive in a Malaysian semicon test-equipment company (itself a subsidiary of a parent company based in China):
Thanks to Trump, and the US’ threat of punitive tariffs, there are now indications that India may [rethink](https://www.outlookbusiness.com/economy-and-policy/india-may-consider-re-joining-rcep-whats-pushing-indias-comeback-to-the-trade-bloc-after-5-years) its RCEP decision.
Modi’s and Chinese President Xi’s recent bonhomie earlier this week, where they [declared](https://www.reuters.com/world/china/india-china-are-partners-not-rivals-modi-xi-say-2025-08-31/) that their two countries were “development partners” and not “rivals” may make such integrations easier. Reports indicate that the GoI may even [relax](https://www.fortuneindia.com/economy/explainer-why-india-may-revisit-press-note-3-curbs-on-chinese-investments/126235) investment restrictions placed on China under ‘Press Note. 3’, which at least for India’s semiconductor ecosystem, would be welcome news. In fact, India’s own [Economic Survey](https://www.indiabudget.gov.in/budget2024-25/economicsurvey/index.php) made the case last financial year that India should allow FDI inflows from China as a means to strengthen its presence in global value chains.
But there is also the matter of technology, both direct transfers through deals and experience gained through joint ventures.
At the Semicon India event held last week, Tata Electronics [signed](https://www.manufacturingtodayindia.com/merck-and-tata-electronics-sign-mou) an MoU with German giant Merck to jointly develop capabilities in semiconductor materials, fabrication infrastructure, and advanced gas and chemical delivery systems for its $11 billion fabrication unit in Dholera (which is India’s first commercial fab).
But the founder and CEO of a well-known semiconductor solutions firm says building a good technological base will “take time”. And that this is an area where India will need to focus on building a strong home team, because technology transfers, even through joint ventures, could be hard to swing.
The technological capabilities of local vendors in the ecosystem will be critical for the economic security of the ecosystem India is aiming to establish. And to be able to eventually flex its own muscle in the space. “I was thrilled and pleasantly surprised to see two Indian companies [at Semicon India]—one that’s making silicon ingots, which means they’re making wafers, albeit in low quantities, out of Hosur of all places, and another one in Pune that’s making machine tools. We may be far from making lithography tools, but at least we’re headed there one step at a time,” this founder told me.
Support for such ancillary industries and suppliers is likely going to be a key part of the second phase of the Indian Semiconductor Mission. At Semicon India 2025, the Union electronics and IT minister [said](https://www.business-standard.com/industry/news/ism-2-0-to-cover-semiconductor-ecosystem-partners-as-well-vaishnaw-125090201193_1.html) that a “significant portion” of the second round of incentives will be earmarked for such ecosystem partners.
Some of this has already started, with India [relaxing](https://www.pib.gov.in/PressReleasePage.aspx?PRID=2135116) SEZ rules earlier this year, and [announcing](https://www.pib.gov.in/PressReleasePage.aspx?PRID=2116172) a $4 billion PLI scheme for electronic components. The goal is to onshore supply chains and foster “backward linkages” that can help reduce costs for Indian manufacturers. (And perhaps also the geopolitical risks littered across the supply chain.)
Of course, getting all this in place may take decades, which is why India’s ability to generate domestic demand for its young ecosystem will play as important a part in nurturing it as securing supply-chain resilience. “In this day and age, you can only build a sustainable domestic semiconductor ecosystem based on local consumption, not based on exports, because there’s an overcapacity problem in the world,” pointed out Madhusudan. McKinsey, for instance, [estimates](https://www.mckinsey.com/industries/semiconductors/our-insights/semiconductors-have-a-big-opportunity-but-barriers-to-scale-remain) that around $1 trillion will be invested by global companies in new fab facilities over the next five years.
But there are perhaps opportunities India can start exploiting first. Fabless design- and product-focused exports are certainly one such area (the government has a new design-linked incentive scheme in the works), but also, to quote Madhusudan:
To be a reliable global producer of even such chips, though, and thus attract the kind of future investments the ecosystem requires, India will need rock solid supply-chain linkages for critical gear and materials.
And global relationships that aren’t easily shaken.

Ed Set Go
Mon, 26 May 25
The one stop to understand the biggest shifts in education, written by the best edtech journalist in the country..
Good Morning Ishan,
It’s been a week.
When news broke that the Trump administration had revoked Harvard’s ability to enrol foreign students, my thoughts immediately went to Indian friends and colleagues who are studying there. To be stranded in a foreign country—midway through an expensive degree, with no certain legal status—is alarming.
It’s also a great segue for Indian news outlets to pivot the conversation to how India should become a knowledge hub, instead of just a source of global talent. Five more foreign universities, vetted and approved by the University Grants Commission, will open campuses in India, [according](https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/industry/services/education/india-moves-ahead-with-global-education-push-as-five-foreign-universities-get-campus-approval/articleshow/121390728.cms?from=mdr) to the Economic Times. Six more proposals, the report says, are in the works.
Of course, the big question is quality. What will Indian universities offer that encourages students to stay back? More importantly, what will the job market offer?
When it comes to the potential of the market, some investors seem to be thinking like the universities that have set up base here. The chief at Prosus, Fabricio Bloisi—the South African investment firm that had to write off its entire investment in Byju’s—recently put out a [statement](https://yourstory.com/2025/05/ai-opportunities-disruption-edtech-prosus-ceo-fabricio-bloisi-byjus) about this:
Bloisi noted that the firm’s thesis of investing millions of dollars in Indian edtech highlighted its push for supporting a segment that can use technology to make the country run better and to create a competitive advantage.
[…]
However, Bloisi holds a very optimistic view of the sector, despite the challenges. “It’s part of life. We’re not going to be less bold or to take less risks because of that. We just learned that sometimes technology is not ready for the future we predicted.”
This is an interesting turn of phrase: “technology is not ready for the future we predicted”. It sounds like something you’d nod to during a TED Talk, but it’s also just obtuse enough to leave you wondering what it actually means.
Does Bloisi think that tech was the ultimate problem with Prosus’ Byju’s investment? Or did he truly think that online recorded content was an idea ahead of its time? I’d like to ask what his prediction was.
Because now, it does seem like tech has the power to manipulate the future away from any one person’s prediction. With AI, edtech companies are going to have little to no monopoly over content and delivery. Whatever we thought about future models of edtech, AI has made sure we eat humble pie.
Quick backstory: Chegg is an American edtech company that invented a Netflix-type subscription model for academics. Almost two decades old, Chegg was a go-to for textbooks, exam prep, and on-the-spot answers. It [leapt ](https://www.inc.com/bruce-crumley/chegg-once-a-hot-education-tech-startup-now-battles-ai-chatbots-to-survive/91018987)in popularity during the pandemic, as subscribers to its premium service grew from 2.3 million in 2019 to 5.3 million in 2022.
But the reversal in fortunes was just as dramatic—Chegg didn’t see AI bots coming for its core business.
Since AI bots can provide summaries, answers, and even entire essays, Chegg’s business model doesn’t seem very relevant or unique. Much like the news outlets that have sued Google and its ilk for their AI summaries derived from their own news articles, Chegg has also blamed big, bad tech for using its source material without giving due credit.
In response, Chegg has had to streamline. Two weeks ago, it laid off 22% of its workforce, announced plans to shutter its US and Canada offices, and is putting more weight behind its own in-house AI tool, which will reduce the company’s dependence on humans and make generating answers a more efficient process.
Several edtechs, to different degrees, have faced a reversal in fortunes once the pandemic ended. But not all have had such open exposure to AI copying their business model. There’s been massive progress with chatbots that can now tutor students and personalise their educational journey, but as quickly as these tools are growing, there are still logistical problems to solve vis-a-vis adoption and comfort levels of talking to an artificial being as a teacher.
The point is: edtechs have to future-proof their business from the threat of AI. If AI is able to, at scale, tutor students, or for a small price, be a pocket-sized guide on mathematics problems and English essays, then there’s a threat to how much edtechs can innovate beyond that. Or, companies could take the Khan Academy route and partner with firms like OpenAI to preempt getting scooped by tools like ChatGPT.
Either way, it seems like edtechs may have to think up new business and revenue-share models with AI companies that will inevitably home in on their core business. Who’s ready to take the plunge in India?

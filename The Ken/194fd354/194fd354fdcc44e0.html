Every IPO these days is expensive. At least that’s what securities analysts say. They told me this last week when I wrote about Dr. Agarwal’s Healthcare listing. And today they tell Anand, who’s writing about Hexaware’s public issue that’s underway.

Maybe things will change this year even though 2025 is described as the “red hot” year for IPOs. At the IVCA event in Mumbai on Tuesday, Kedaara Capital’s Manish Kejriwal said the second half of this year “will be a buyer’s market much more than a seller’s market”.

Let’s not dwell on the state of the grey-market premium or the first-day subscription. Even long-termists in the IT sector who believe Hexaware is a “sound business run by solid management” are staying away from the IPO.

The current seller, Carlyle, and the previous seller, Baring, have made good returns on Hexaware. The latter made a cool 62% return in one year when it bought stakes, took Hexaware private, and sold it to Carlyle in late 2021.

Between then and now, Carlyle has nearly doubled Hexaware’s valuation to Rs 43,000 crore. In the current IPO, the PE is selling over 20% stake to public shareholders and will make 84% in less than four years. It also pocketed most of Rs 1,700 crore in dividends that Hexaware paid out in this period, with the company’s dividend payout ratio surpassing that of its peers.

That’s par for the course; PEs’ job is to make money. But by pricing the IPOs too high, they are probably accelerating everyone else’s learnings.

As Anand writes, “India’s largest IT-services IPO yet may be headed the way of India’s largest IPO yet—Hyundai Motors India IPO last year struggled to cross the finish line and still trades below its issue price.”

Let’s get to his arguments.

Have you made a trip abroad to attend a live event in 2023 or 2024?

Did you have the option of attending the same (or equivalent) event in India?

Why did you choose not to attend the same event in India?

These were the three main questions we posed to listeners of Two by Two in a recent survey to understand the biggest problems with hosting events—big or small—in India.

In episode 29, released this week, hosts Rohin Dharmakumar and Praveen Gopal Krishnan sit down with Shreyas Srinivasan, former Chief Product Officer at Paytm* and founder of Paytm Insider, which has now been acquired by Zomato and rebranded as District, and Sudhir Syal, who founded and built Bookmyshow Southeast Asia and the Middle East, to understand where India falls short.

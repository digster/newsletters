Good Morning Ishan,
India’s telecom story is a highly distorted one.
Where else will you find users paying as low as ~US$0.15 per GB of 4G data while consuming the world’s highest average of 10GB per month?
This type of distortion is becoming apparent with India’s 5G consumption, too.
5G was originally sold as a panacea to all consumers’ and businesses’ data and speed problems—from doubling up as an enhanced broadband for retail customers to playing a role in mission-critical data exchanges in autonomous driving, medical robotics, etc.
But the telcos are yet to see any meaningful gains with this technology.
Seema, the author of today’s story, connects the dots to show that what’s unfolding with 5G is a lot like what happened with 3G.
3G was meant to be a data network, but the high demand for voice services meant the 2G network was stretched thin. So, 3G ended up only enhancing the voice service.
A similar story is playing out with 5G. It was meant to improve the data capacity of strained 4G networks at Jio and Airtel. But both telcos—having spent a little over US$15 billion on buying spectrum alone—have not been able to make any money from 5G.
Jio and Airtel, which together have 81.5% of the revenue market share, ought to be feeling the pinch of this 5G push. But only one of them is really admitting to the foolhardiness of pursuing 5G.
Now, as telcos ready themselves for dropping another US$11.5 billion in the spectrum auction in May, telcos have some tough choices to make that will directly affect the way you and I consume 5G.
Is history about to repeat? Seema argues that [5G is heading for the same fate as 3G](https://the-ken.com/story/your-5g-phone-is-bleeding-airtel-and-jio-dry-is-3g-history-repeating-itself/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter).
Electronic suppliers Foxtron, Pegatron, Salcomp, and Tata Electronics are all competing for a greater share of Apple’s iPhone manufacturing business.
Apple, for its part, is building 78,000 units of industrial-housing facilities along with its suppliers to house factory workers to bring more efficiency to the process. Foxconn, its largest supplier, alone gets 35,000 housing units from this plan.
Now, Tata Electronics has also made its intent clear.
Reuters reported that Taiwanese supplier Pegatron is in advanced talks to hand over control of its only iPhone manufacturing facility in Chennai to the Tata Group. This, after the conglomerate already took over Wistron’s Bengaluru facility.
The fight between Apple suppliers just got more intense.

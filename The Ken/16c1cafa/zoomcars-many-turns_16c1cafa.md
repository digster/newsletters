For all their own Kool-Aid that founders feed on, few begin a media blitzkrieg barely a month into the business. In March 2013, one such founder wrote to me for coverage in Forbes India. Already featured on half a dozen business publications and TV channels, he insisted we write about him. After a few email exchanges, where I couldn’t help saying he had more than his fair share of media coverage, he wrote: “I think you would be very hard-pressed to find a more compelling startup in India to cover.”
Two non-Indian founders getting into India’s first car rental business—sure, the idea was compelling, but what more was there to write about a 5-week-old company? Greg Moran, co-founder of Zoomcar was persuasive, to the extent that, unlike anything I had done before, I told him to hunker down and build his business first. Stories would come later.
Well, six years later, Olina brings you a nuanced story. Zoomcar is a leader of the market it helped create, blazing a trail for those like Drivezy, Revv, and Myles to follow in the car rental space. But in an age where car ownership patterns are changing as fast as new models drive out the showroom, Zoomcar wanted a fatter slice of the mobility pie and tried everything that it could to get it. In the process, the simple subscription model has morphed into a Frankenstein's monster. Too complex for users, too clunky to run a smooth operation.
Moran didn’t anticipate this. He wanted tech to simplify operations but a lack of capital, regulatory workarounds, a whole bunch of tinkering with the offerings, and, ironically, an expat founder’s discomfort with the Indian business environment have truly put Zoomcar through its paces. Perhaps OEM backers, Mahinda & Mahindra and Ford have more faith in its business model than traditional investors. Is Zoomcar at a fork in the road, again?

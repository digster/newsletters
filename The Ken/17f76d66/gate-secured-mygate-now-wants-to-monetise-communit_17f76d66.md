When I first began using MyGate after moving to my current home in October 2019, it was exactly what it described itself as—a security and community management app. It was used to allow or disallow visitors—guests, delivery agents, cabs, household help—and communicate with the resident welfare association (RWA) and other residents.
Today, MyGate is a completely different animal. In the last year or so, I have used it to book cabs to the airport and schedule at-home RT-PCR tests. It also offers a host of other services that I haven't tried yet, like AC service and repair, bathroom cleaning, pest control, salon, and relocation. There's also a peer-to-peer marketplace, where you can buy and sell anything within your society and nearby societies. Recently, it launched a property search marketplace called MyGate Homes.
Founded in 2016, MyGate is almost unrecognisable from two years ago. Apart from these new services, it has also changed its pricing model—pivoting from a pure subscription-based play to a freemium model. A lot of this was done, as Aayush reports today, not just because of the pandemic but also to fend off a new, well-funded rival. When it launched in 2019, NoBrokerHood offered its services for free to societies using MyGate. The new entrant scaled up to 12,000-plus societies in no time.
While MyGate still has an enviable customer base of 25,000 housing societies across 27-plus cities in India, its decision to expand stems from the difficulties it faced with getting customers to pay for its services. RWAs are notoriously tight-fisted, running on small budgets.
However, in its search for more revenue streams, it appears MyGate is just throwing stuff at the wall to see what sticks. And in doing so, it's competing with standalone apps that specialise in these services, like Urban Company and NoBroker, the parent of NoBrokerHood. In its quest to become a one-stop-shop for all residents’ requirements, has MyGate bitten off more than it can chew?

Hello from Melbourne, where I am attending the Boxing Day Test.

Attending the Boxing Day Test match has been a childhood dream of mine, and I’m really happy I managed to get here. As I’m writing this, Team India isn’t in a great position, but I’m hoping someone can turn things around today. 

Anyway, today’s edition is a special one—because everything here has been entirely contributed by subscribers of The Nutgraf. Last week, I asked you for your biggest, boldest predictions for 2025. Several of you sent me some amazing, contrarian predictions. 

Here are some of the best ones: 

Quick-commerce’s growth will finally hit a wall 

This was by far the most popular prediction. But the most interesting part was how people justified and argued their positions. 

For instance, subscribers like Zahid Hussain made an incredible case for it using anecdotal evidence. In his words:

“You know when something is hitting a limit when some internal A/B tests are pitched as product choices to a customer on app home screens. Why do I say so? 

Two interesting things I noticed on a Sunday Grocery Run in Mumbai. 

The Bigbasket app discontinued the standard slotted grocery service, Super Saver, and started offering only 10-minute deliveries for all orders. 

The Zepto app on the other hand has introduced a ‘SuperSaver’ service to increase its order value and extend its arm to the value-shopper. Right on the app homepage. 

The shift reminds me of a timeless Telugu proverb, ‘ఓడలు బళ్ళవుతాయి, బళ్ళు ఓడలువుతాయి’. Translates to: ‘The ships will turn into carts and the carts will turn into ships.’ A reference to old world trade. Carts handled small-time local trade, while ships carried valuable international trade. When tides changed, so did their fortunes. Notice how both the product names (Super Saver) are also so similar. 

You choose between Zepto and Zepto Super Saver at the beginning. Both deliver in the same time (under 10 minutes) at two different cart values.

You can only choose between BBNow and Big Basket Super Saver at the checkout stage. Both deliver your goods at the same price but the latter allows you flexibility to schedule a delivery.

These are sure signs of the end of the innovation cycle—an attempt to copy one another’s playbook. 

The second hint is that a deep pocketed player (Amazon) has decided to enter the category, because now there is sufficient clarity on the playbook. No more requirement to burn cash to build a playbook. The innovation has ended and with it the new user acquisition. 

The third hint is the increasing debates around all the dark patterns and hidden charges these platforms levy. We are a couple of influencers away from a large-scale boycott call. We don’t like to be cheated at the cost of convenience. Akin to how everyday surge pricing showed the limits of ride hailing apps. 

The fourth hint is counter intuitive. At some stage, delivering the world in 10 minutes will become such a complex supply chain problem that it will impact user experience. Already, people are using multiple quick-commerce apps even for a single category. Falling user experience will find it hard to recruit new users. 

The fifth and last one should come from the limits of language. With the likes of Myntra (M-Now), Tata Neu (Flash), and the many white labelled quick-commerce services for brands expanding, it should exhaust all plausible nomenclature options for such a service. This might sound in jest, but stranger things have happened before.”

Quite persuasive. 

Another way to see it is from a numbers standpoint. And well, Arjun Puri, another subscriber, made an exceptional demand-side quantitative case about why quick-commerce growth is going to taper off. These are his arguments:

  1. Post Covid, all B2C industries saw a period of very high growth, driven by pent-up demand and revenge consumption

  2. However, from mid-2023 onwards, mainstream B2C companies have seen a marked slowdown in sales, driven by high inflation, high household debt, and a weak job market

  3. Premium categories and players saw their growth continue longer—Nestle growth peaked after HUL and remained above HUL’s for a number of quarters. McDonald’s grew ahead of Domino’s for a while, before falling behind it—and Starbucks grew ahead of both till Q4’23

  4. Quick commerce has recruited new users who are shifting their consumption from GT and Modern Trade, helping quick commerce to grow

  5. However, other internet B2C firms—like Zomato (food delivery) and Nykaa (all verticals) have seen GMV growth plateau over the last four quarters or so

  6. At present, Q-Com players are in land grab mode, as The Ken had reported—everyone is rushing to open new dark stores, enter new cities, and promos on Q-Com apps have increased (speaking from personal experience)

  7. However, by mid-2025, I expect to see quick-commerce MTUs plateau, as these players hit their natural limits on users and cities (given that neither offline nor online consumers are showing strong underlying consumption growth trends at present)

  8. As of 2023, there were 25M eGrocery MTUs, and at present, Blinkit is at 9M, and Instamart at 6M. While there would be an overlap between the two, given the smaller geographical reach, I don’t see total quick-commerce MTUs going beyond a certain proportion of eGrocery MTUs in the near term

Arjun even sent in some incredible charts!

Here are some other interesting predictions from subscribers: 

The AI bubble will be over by the end of 2025 (via Yash Jahagirdar).

“The hyperbole valuations do not justify the value created by AI. Most of the systems are still very much dependent on legacy software and though AI can write standalone programs/code, it cannot solve intersystem issues consisting of a mishmash of legacy and new software applications. The VCs will start looking at the value created rather than going with the hype bandwagon.”

“The crypto-bubble will pop, once again. Overseas governments will get bored with propping this up, and do a full U-turn. Leading to a second wave of freefalls” (via Anirudh Gupta).

“Founders will prioritise bootstrapping with customer revenue, or doing only one seed round and building to massive ARRs. Software businesses, AI-enabled, will prioritise keeping teams under 10 members, optimising for the smallest possible teams. Revenue per employee will become the top metric that companies track” (via Anirudh Gupta).

“America’s gambling addiction is going to find its way into India’s ecosystem. Beyond stocks, crypto, and sports into other use cases such as elections, events, etc. This will be an absolute gray area, but will be fun to watch with popcorn” (via Anirudh Gupta).

Influencers will become less influential (via Vaibhav Jain):

“There has been constant evolution in the influencer business and it’s now getting concentrated to a few. The gatekeeping is visible and it will just grow. The sources of revenue for influencers will shrink and brands will now concentrate more on the popular faces. We recently saw a post go viral from someone who tried everything for three years but her Youtube career just failed. The algorithm is evolving faster than ever and only strong capital can cope, so the brands that were feeding this economy will get safer with their experimentation and will work with known names rather than new influencers.”

Finally, some of my favourite predictions came from Pranav Surya:

“Delhi will have lower population in 2025 than in 2024 marking a reversal for the first time since records began post-Independence. People will vote by foot for the air quality they have to endure. This will mark a decline that’s hard to reverse, and needs severe political will. Delhi is no London, because there are alternative cities such as Mumbai, Bengaluru where people can still move for economic prosperity. 

Horizontal e-commerce growth will slow even more. Both have their hands full tackling quick commerce and will lose sight of traditional e-commerce. Amazon will run out of patience sometime soon with its India division and Flipkart will need to IPO soon (need to stop funding discounts as much as they can). The ones greatly affected will be Delhivery, Shadowfax (private), and Ecom express (to IPO) whose valuations will fall by a third. They will have to move into B2B (FTL/LCL) or cold chain logistics to capture new growth or to grow into current valuations.

Foreign travel will outgrow domestic travel by 2035, 2025 will mark a sharp turn in this trend. As seen in policies around foreign travel taxation, this is an area in which the government can raise tax revenues, but it’s evidently a booming sector. This will show up in passport power and visa-free opening up of lanes in subsequent years. Akasa opened as an international carrier from birth and airlines will reap big rewards on riding this wave. 

Oyo will go public in 2025. It’ll be 90% OFS and not much new equity will be doled out. It has a weird funky balance sheet with a couple of personal- and company-level debts, but its opening will be at least 10x oversubscribed. Expect it to list at a US$6–7 billion valuation (from US$4–4.5 billion currently).”

Several other subscribers sent me many more predictions, and I wish I could have carried them all. 

Thank you so much for writing in, and for making 2024’s last edition of The Nutgraf incredibly special. I hope to do this more often in 2025 and beyond. 

Take care, and happy new year from me and the rest of us at The Ken. Wish you a wonderful, happy, and exceptional 2025.

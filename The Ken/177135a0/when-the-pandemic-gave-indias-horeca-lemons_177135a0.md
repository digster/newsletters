India’s 1.2 million mom-and-pop kirana stores are talked about a lot. From the government to consumer product companies to e-commerce giants to telecom operators, everybody is interested in kiranas and their well-being.
Meanwhile, India has 1.3 million HoReCas, or hotels, restaurants, and caterers. And they’re hurting.
Take Bakers Circle, a nearly two-decade-old B2B baker and confectioner. The company supplies its products to most of India’s largest and best-known restaurant chains like KFC, Domino’s, and Subway. As the pandemic struck, Bakers Circle’s orders from its largest customers fell 95%. It still hasn’t recovered fully.
Or Tulip Institutional Services, a 25-year-old company that catered over 30,000 meals each day to companies and offices. That number has come down to 3,000 today.
Much has been written about the consumer-facing side of food—largely through the lens of Swiggy and Zomato. But the pandemic hit business-to-business (B2B) companies like Bakers Circle and Tulip even harder. Worse, they were virtually invisible to most of us.
In today’s story, Bhumika reports extensively on how B2B companies rapidly rejigged themselves into B2C ones to survive, and perhaps even thrive. From setting up consumer-facing brands to attempting to deepen the market for frozen foods to refashioning their massive kitchens into delivery-focussed cloud kitchens. They’re throwing everything and the kitchen sink at the problem.
But can B2B companies truly master B2C? Can tens of thousands of fickle consumers and their fast-changing preferences replace a few dozen large customers with bulk orders? Can the lemonade from the pandemic lemons become a sustainable business even after things start returning to normal?

Courtrooms are far from being machines of efficiency.

There are still some courts where handwritten documents recording testimonies are passed from one judge to another, at times years after the moment when pen touched paper. In many cases, illegible handwriting makes these statements difficult to parse, and their original meaning is lost to time.

These are common occurrences across India. When judges issue verdicts, there’s an air of uncertainty.

Accurately documented depositions are part of the solution, and tools made by organisations such as Adalat AI and Nyaay AI are being adopted by India’s judiciary. The Kerala High Court, for instance, now mandates the use of AI-assisted live transcription.

There are other use cases for artificial intelligence in the legal system. We’re now in a moment of real, fundamental transformation in this space. “Think of how UPI changed payments, or before that, how passport issuance time reduced from years to weeks,” said Utkarsh Saxena, co-founder of Adalat AI, to The Ken.

However, that doesn’t mean courtrooms around India will become ultra efficient at the snap of a finger. There are plenty of hurdles to overcome. AI developers need to train models to handle every language in the country, and that will take time.

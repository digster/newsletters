Good Morning Ishan,
Jio Financial Services is making a US$4.3 billion bet on telecom gear. It is buying equipment from Reliance Retail and leasing it to Reliance Jio Infocomm’s customers. Regular Reliance family affair, right?
But here’s the thing: Jio Financial stands to gain the least from the deal.
The company is banking on the device-as-a-service model to boost its revenue, which has, so far, relied on non-operating income. Despite the listed firm’s hefty valuation, its current business size doesn’t quite justify the hype.
But will people go for rentals over ownership? That’s the billion-dollar question.
Reliance Retail gets to sell a ton of devices, and Jio Infocomm could see a surge in its broadband and 5G services. Both companies are gearing up for big public market debuts, and this deal might just boost their valuations. But if the leasing idea flops, Jio Financial could end up with a mountain of unsold gear.
It’s a high-stakes gamble with potential for big payoffs. Or big losses.
Will Jio Financial’s move hit the jackpot? Dive into Anand’s [sharp breakdown of this latest jig from Jio Financial](https://the-ken.com/story/who-stands-to-gain-the-most-from-jio-financials-us4-3b-lease-deal-hint-not-jio-financial/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter), which one analyst calls “a shell company of sorts”.
Over in Southeast Asia, China’s EV brands are making life difficult for its local rivals. Coby writes that BYD, SAIC Motor, and Chery—currently locked in a price war at home—are expanding aggressively into the region and they’re bringing the same cutthroat competition with them.
With the US and EU markets increasingly wary of Chinese EVs, these brands are looking for new growth opportunities. And they’ve already captured 75% of the sales in the world’s leading emerging EV market.
But making further inroads isn’t going to be easy. Vietnam, for instance, is Vinfast’s home turf. It’s not going to roll out the red carpet for Chinese competitors.
At the same time, some analysts suggest that Southeast Asia could now become a new arena for infighting among dominant Chinese brands. And as investment gates swing open, there’s a risk that [homegrown EV ecosystems could get swallowed up](https://the-ken.com/story/byd-vinfast-are-in-a-race-who-will-win-the-southeast-asian-circuit/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter).
Beyond mere competition
Yesterday’s buzz revolved around Zomato’s US$36 million injection into Blinkit, its quick-commerce wing. But let’s be real, isn’t the quick-commerce story getting a bit stale?
What truly piqued my interest was the subtle mention of a US$12 million infusion into Zomato Entertainment, signalling the company’s dive into live events. Why? I mean, have you seen the “Zomato Live” feature popping up in the app lately? It apparently lists thousands of exclusive gigs, concerts, stand-up comedy shows, and whatnot across 12 cities.
But hang on a second. Isn’t this turf already claimed by the behemoth, Bookmyshow (BMS)? So why is Zomato crashing this party late?
It’s likely about the increasingly attractive market of intellectual property events. Take BMS’s prized possession, the globally renowned Lollapalooza music festival. (Last year, Aayush [discussed](https://the-ken.com/story/the-pandemic-couldnt-kill-bookmyshow-but-it-definitely-changed-its-face/) how BMS, despite its focus on movie ticketing, sees live events as the new gold rush).
Now, Zomato has Zomaland, and Swiggy has Swiggy Carnival. The strategy is clear: host lavish events, invite A-listers, and strike lucrative deals. But they’re also leveraging these events to strengthen ties with partner restaurants (with their participation) and secure that elusive thing called customer loyalty, which is becoming more fleeting in food delivery.

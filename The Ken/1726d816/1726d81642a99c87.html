Firms face dipping productivity, learning gaps explode, but IPOs still go on
Edition #45. Monday, 1 June 2020
Someone sent you this? Sign up here
Good morning,

If you’re in India, welcome to Lockdown 5.0. Or are you calling it Opening Up 1.0? Firms might face dipping productivity levels, while restaurants and coffee shops in SEA may never be anything but cloud kitchens again. Learning gaps will explode, but IPOs may go on like before, as markets continue to confound us. 

Let’s dive into a new week on the first day of June. (Did you think we’d make it this far?)
The reinforcing feedback loop of wage cost

Arundhati
 
In a previous edition of BFO, Rohin wrote about the big cost test for companies. With revenues falling, one big cost companies are shaving off is salaries, so they are laying off people en masse. The Indian Express reported just five companies—Uber India and South Asia, Ola, Zomato, Swiggy and Cure.fit—have announced 4,441 job cuts in the last month.
 
Consumer Finance company Home Credit alone laid off 1,800 people last week. 
 
So, even though companies are sending people back home to cut costs, they have created an artificial supply constraint. 
Also, the lockdown inflicted sudden shrinking of income flows and negative expectation that the future will continue to be gloomy, weakened demand as well. Uncertainty about the future, which Keynes referred to as “state of confidence” impacts both autonomous and induced investment demand. On the other hand, the lockdown keeps workers away from the workplace (even though there are enough willing workers and employers in the economy), thus reducing the flow of production. This can be looked upon as an unboundedly high transaction cost incurred by firms to bring in workers to the production site. The cost is so high that it acts as a capa­city constraint. Put differently, the lockdown creates a supply-related “artificial” capacity constraint for production, which is significantly below the pre-lockdown supply constraint
Paradox of a Supply Constrained Keynesian Equilibrium, Economic and Political Weekly
If traditional economics has its way, these supply constraints are bound to drive wages and production costs up. Productivity, though, is a whole other matter.

During normal times—whatever that even means, anymore—layoffs have a 20% impact on the job performance of those remaining, according to this 2018 HBR article. The layoffs this time around are actually accompanied by longer shifts for those remaining. The Karnataka government, for instance, has allowed factories to increase working hours from 8 hours a day to 10 hours a day. 

Fewer people doing more work is bound to have an impact on productivity. Decreased productivity despite longer hours will take the companies straight down the path of an overworked and burnt-out workforce, resulting in even worse productivity. And that makes way for… well, more layoffs.  
An academic disaster comes to roost

Olina
 
For systems with endemic challenges, the Covid injection is likely to cause ruptures that will now take even longer to heal. India’s migrant crisis, in part, is caused by historically weak labour laws, which have totally crumbled under the economic pressure of the pandemic.
 
The other complex, multi-year impact is on what’s described loosely as “learning levels”. In India, on average, a grade 5 student can’t read or do math sums from a grade 2 textbook. Sure, this learning gap is a function of poor teaching and bad schools, but a vast array of reasons exist outside the school and in homes. Poorer students suffer more, says the data, because they often have less academic support at home. On Friday, we wrote about how the digital divide is impacting Filipino schools.
 
The correlation between parents’ education levels and computer literacy is significant here:
In Jharkhand for instance, only 34 percent of Class 5 rural children could meet the bar for reading - the lowest in the country. Other parameters were also low - only 43 percent of mothers and 67 percent of fathers had been to school, just 11 percent of the families had a computer-literate member, and only 4 percent were found to have some reading material at home during the survey.
Lockdown has put 321 million Indian children away from school, widened learning gaps, Mint
Online classes—unfortunately—are the only means to stay connected with students during a pandemic. Hit by Covid-19, this gap between the digital haves and have-nots will exacerbate. A gap—like the summer break—already causes some dips in learning, especially for more disadvantaged students. A 3-4 month gap is academically fatal. Schools in states where infection rates are climbing may not want to open up at all.

In the US, the “achievement gap” between richer kids and those from minority or disadvantaged backgrounds is worsening.
Increased reliance on homework, for example, widens achievement gaps. Children whose parents can more effectively help with homework gain more than children whose parents can do so less well.

We also know that the educational gap is wider when children return after summer vacation than it was in the spring, because middle-class children frequently have summer enrichment that reinforces knowledge and experience. The larger gap shows up in test scores, but also in less easily quantifiable areas that are particularly valued in higher education, professional workplaces, and civic life, such as cooperative skills in group activities, possibly due to enrichment from things like summer camp and family travel.”
Why covid-19 will ‘explode’ existing academic achievement gaps, Washington Post
The system needs realignment and a more equitable distribution of resources. But how do you fix the engine while you’re driving the car? The car is stalled now, though. Maybe now is the time. 
An Internet pill for a migration-induced headache

Arundhati
 
If there is one thing that the lockdown has shown, it is that most jobs can be done remotely through the internet. In 2019, India’s southern state of Kerala just went a step further—it declared the internet a fundamental right of every citizen. Now, it’s trying to build on that declaration.
Kerala is the first state to declare the internet as a fundamental right of the citizen. As part of this, the K-FON project was launched to provide quality internet free of cost to the poor and at affordable rates to others. No other state in India has implemented such a scheme. Despite the delay due to the lockdown, M V Gautam, CMD of consortium leader BEL, has promised to complete the project by December this year," Vijayan said.
Kerala to provide free high-speed internet connections to the poor soon, Livemint
That's not just a new feather to stick in its cap, it's something Kerala desperately needs because the state is broke.
 
"If we include what the Centre would give, we will be able to touch about Rs 2,000 crore in revenues. The rest is borrowings. For the payment of salaries alone, we require Rs 2,500 crore," said the state’s finance minister Thomas Isaac on 25 April.
 
The state, while topping the charts in indicators like health and education, has not made a mark for itself in terms of GDP contribution. It ranked eleventh in Gross State Domestic Product.
Source: Ministry of Statistics and Programme Implementation
What helped the state lead in health and education indicators was the US$1 trillion of remittances flowing in from Keralites settled in West Asia.
Every fifth house in Kerala is officially estimated to have an expat in the Gulf. Likewise, every fourth Indian in the Gulf region is estimated to be a Keralite. They send home money like clockwork: more than ₹1 trillion arrived last year, accounting for almost one-third of the state’s total income.​
Paradise lost: The exodus from Gulf, Livemint
Now that the state is facing a high reverse migration problem—nearly 400,000 people have registered to return to Kerala—the state needs to find a way to make up for that income deficit.
What the state is currently doing is to understand the extent of migration, what kind of skills they have and what can we gain from them (once they return)," said R. Ramakumar, a member of Kerala’s planning board and an economist at the Tata Institute of Social Sciences.
Paradise lost: The exodus from Gulf, Livemint
While Kerala may struggle to find jobs for displaced blue-collar workers, if anything opens up possibilities for Kerala’s citizens, it has got to be the internet. 
Taking the jolly out of Jollibee

Jum
 
As the pandemic threatens its future, Asia’s largest restaurant chain Jollibee is making changes to meet the moment. The Philippines’ homegrown brand has set aside US$138 million to build cloud kitchens and strengthen its delivery service globally.
 
That’s a major departure for fast food chains, whose operations tend to be optimised around physical stores with integrated kitchens to attract walk-in customers.
 
Cloud kitchens aren’t the only structural change for Jollibee. In the Philippines alone, where the restaurant empire has trounced the likes of McDonald’s, it has piloted chatbot ordering systems and digital payment options like e-wallets for all its restaurant brands on Facebook Messenger. It’s also closing a number of its weaker outlets.
 
The company incurred a US$41 million loss for the first quarter of 2020, a reversal of last year’s US$27 million in profit. Three of its biggest markets—home base Philippines, the US, and China—have been severely affected by pandemic lockdowns. Analysts are looking beyond 2020 for when Jollibee may finally return to a more jolly state.
Awful time to go public? These companies don’t agree
 
Seetharaman
 
It’s understandable if companies are not keen on initial public offerings (IPOs) now. Across the globe, new listings in March mopped up 20% less money than in the same month last year, as markets reeled from the impact of Covid-19. Several IPOs have been put on hold, including that of Burger King India.
 
Companies may not get the valuation they want and investors are unsure of what these companies will look like a year from now. And that’s if they survive. 
 
But the owner of California-headquartered coffee retailer and café chain Peet’s Coffee is not one of those. Though its plan to go public predated the coronavirus, JDE Peet’s decided to stay the course. But haven’t cafés been badly hit by the pandemic? Yes, but 80% of Peet’s revenue is from coffee sold at retail stores, a segment that has done particularly well thanks to people stocking up on coffee during the lockdown. 
 
Visiting cafes may not be an appealing idea to many in the near future. And periodic lockdowns may be inevitable till we have a vaccine. Both of these point to more cups of coffee at home. So last week, JDE Peet’s went public in Europe’s biggest IPO—and the world’s second-largest—this year. It raised US$2.5 billion at a valuation of US$17.3 billion. 
 
Another sector whose listing plans haven’t been waylaid by Covid-19 is biotech. And the reason is quite interesting:
Unlike technology companies, biotech startups usually have little or no revenue and typically burn through investor dollars for years while they test new medications.
Consequently, investors usually aren’t worried about falling sales. 
Biotech IPOs Sail Through Coronavirus Storm, The Wall Street Journal 
Also favoured by investors are companies that go public for the express purpose of making acquisitions. Called special-purpose acquisition companies, these entities accounted for 85% of the money raised through IPOs in the US in April. 
The jump shows investors are betting there will be good deals to scoop up when the coronavirus subsides, but remain hesitant to put cash into companies going public soon. 
The Hot IPO Trend of 2020…, The Wall Street Journal
Then there are industries whose immediate future may not be very bright but stand to gain in the long run. Music is one of them. Despite a 25% drop in revenues in 2020, Goldman Sachs is bullish about the industry’s prospects this decade and believes its revenues could nearly double to US$142 billion by 2030. That could be a key reason for the US$1.8 billion sale of secondary shares in Warner Music Group by its owner. 
 
These IPOs are also being helped by a market rebound. The S&P 500 index in the US, for instance, is back at its early March levels. Clearly, it isn’t all gloom and doom for some companies. 
Fighting for a future

Olina
 
Social distancing norms are bound to go for a toss in a protest. Also, the central tool for protests—sloganeering, chanting and raising a collective voice—is potentially fatal as virus-wrapped aerosol can make a meal of the tightly-bound protesters. 
 
This last week, protests erupted in two corners of the world—the US and Hong Kong. Both, broadly, about the unjust trampling of human and constitutional rights. To the chagrin of epidemiologists and authorities everywhere, the protestors couldn’t really socially distance themselves from each other.
There’s a reason. The central visual of a protest is walking shoulder-to-shoulder in solidarity. But when a disease threatens to wipe out humanity, are there alternative ways to fight for the future?
 
The American Civil Liberties Union has released a useful guide on protests in a pandemic. Some remedies include drive-by protests, while others confined to their homes can paste signs in their windows.
 
Potentially, the safest and most permanent way to effect change would be to “vote by mail” in the US, which will extend franchise not only to those stuck at home, but also to those who are being denied the right to vote remotely. The US President, though, is less keen on that option.
Pandemic-led DIY 
 
Arundhati
 
India has never been much of a DIY economy. This has led to the rise of businesses like Urban Company, which helps provide various service professionals like plumbers, carpenters, and electricians.

And after two months of lockdown, as appliances have been put through the wringer, households need repair professionals more than ever. But the pandemic also has people terrified of allowing strangers into their homes.
 
Welcome to what The New York Times calls the telemedicine for home repairs. 
HomeServe and Hippo have set up free virtual house calls available to anyone, not just current customers. In March, two Harvard Business School students started Dwelling, a website where homeowners can get a free diagnosis of their issue by uploading photographs and descriptions of the problem for a technician to review. And Streem, a virtual platform that connects contractors with homeowners remotely, has experienced “exponential growth” since stay-at-home orders were enacted, according to Ryan Fink, the president of Streem, which is owned by the home-protection plan provider Frontdoor.
Telemedicine for Home Repairs, The New York Times
The next unicorn, perhaps?

Good Morning Ishan,
 
A few weeks ago, I was privileged to be seated in a journalism workshop with some of the finest minds in the business. There were Pulitzer winners, regional heads of large media organisations, men and women who'd covered every major event from the Haiti earthquake to the Ferguson riots. And me.
 
My imposter syndrome was peaking.
 
Then came a question that reassured me I belonged. Why did you get into journalism? I heard a little bit of myself in every response. "Speak truth to power", "Be a voice for the voiceless", "Hold power to account", the answers rang out. 
 
The one that truly hit home, however, was simpler still—"Bear witness".
 
I must confess, with protests against the government's Citizenship Amendment Act and the National Register of Citizens spreading like wildfire, police crackdowns resulting in deaths, and the looming fear of disenfranchisement for many, I feel a certain helplessness. 
 
As a business journalist who deals with billion-dollar valuations and large, faceless corporations, I have to ask myself, do I still bear witness? And despite the self-doubt that creeps in, I know for a fact that I do.
 
Each week I get to edit the work of a newsroom full of phenomenally talented journalists who bear witness. Who shine a light on companies and sectors in a way no one else does. With objectivity and integrity. Last week we saw more evidence of that.
 
On Monday, we had Shreedhar and Sharath take a long, hard look at the mid-level cull playing out in India's IT sector. It is now a race for upskilling—mid-level managers need to either skill up or step out.
 
Tuesday saw Jum writing about HonestBee, an on-demand grocery player in Southeast Asia. Once one of the hottest startups on the scene, today, HonestBee is sweating on investment from Philippines-based JG Summit for survival.
 
We then had Ruhi writing about medical devices e-commerce marketplace MedikaBazaar. The platform is attempting to do something even Amazon has struggled with—bringing the full spectrum of India's medical devices space online. It won't be easy.
 
Pratap was Thursday's author, writing on the Indian cybersecurity space. More specifically, on how Indian cybersecurity firms are all but blocked out of the tender process for public sector projects. It was our free story for the week, so do share it widely. 
 
We closed out the week by looking at India's increasingly organised used car market. Even as auto sales slow, the likes of CarDekho and Cars24 are driving forward, writes Vandana.
 
Of course, there's also our newsletter—The Nutgraf. This one takes a look at the craziness that has come to define 2019.
 
That's about it from my end. If you're joining the protests, be safe, stay peaceful. If you're against the protests, support the right to dissent in a democracy. We're all in this together; be kind to one another.
 
Merry Christmas in advance. I hope 2020 brings you everything you want.
 
Regards,

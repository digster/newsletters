Good Morning Ishan,
Universities are built over decades. So by that count, Sonipat-based Ashoka University completing 10 years and graduating 5,000 students is both a blip on the higher-education radar and a major milestone.
Five years ago when we [wrote](https://the-ken.com/story/100-million-ashoka-university-higher-education/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter) about why and how it was founded, the campus was thrumming with liberal arts approaches in its curriculum. Today, a new discipline is taking over students’ imagination—entrepreneurship. In May, 1,500 students completed that course; the current semester has enrolled 1,000 students.
For a campus where a student wanting to be a “PM” could mean anything from “prime minister to a product manager”, the rise of entrepreneurship—in student popularity and management focus—reflects the zeitgeist.
Nathan, an alum of Ashoka and the writer of today’s story, learnt that at a recent roadshow for parents and potential students, the Q&A section was all about placement outcomes and little about the curiosity-driven learning approach. He’s also learnt that since the entrepreneurship courses are a hit, the university plans to offer it as a major subject soon.
For the longest time, even though the cost of liberal arts education was high, universities did not prioritise economic returns. But Ashoka is a university primarily backed by entrepreneurial energy and donations—it has raised nearly Rs 3,400 crore in private and public commitments—and economic returns are baked into its DNA.
As this most ambitious private-education experiment in India enters its second decade, Nathan gets up close with the dilemma the university faces—to respond to parental pressure and behave like a B-school or march on with promoting intellectual curiosity. It’s a [wonderful read](https://the-ken.com/story/10-years-in-liberal-arts-pioneer-ashoka-university-puts-its-b-school-boots-on/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter).

Good Morning Ishan,
If a rival’s rival is an ally, then India and the US should ideally be best pals—bound together by their common wariness of China. But in a world turned topsy-turvy by Trump’s tantrums, there was this recent spectacle of the Chinese foreign minister calling for “cooperative pas de deux”—a ballet dance—between the dragon and the elephant. It is “the only right choice for both sides,” the minister noted.
Choices, as we know, can be complicated.
Not just India, even some US firms with operations in India are having to make the “right choices”. Case in point: Ohmium, the protagonist of today’s story. This green-hydrogen player—with a sprawling plant on the outskirts of Bengaluru, one of its five facilities in India—was counting on a big opportunity when the US batted for clean energy not so long ago. For India, too, it meant a good fighting chance in the renewable-energy space after having lost much ground to China in the solar race. And then, Trump came along again and upset the applecart with “drill, baby, drill”, his kill pill for clean energy.
But countries and companies have a knack for adapting to complex situations with complex choices. On its part, Ohmium—which has raised $250 million so far—is looking for green shores elsewhere, including in India. And it’s betting on a technology that’s costlier than the more popular choice, especially among Chinese green-hydrogen makers. But popular, as we know, need not always be better. Or at least, that’s what Ohmium thinks.
Will it be proven right? What does Ohmium’s big bet and counterintuitive choice mean for India? Will it give the country a chance that makes neighbours turn, well, green with envy?

Hindustan Aeronautics Ltd (HAL) was already struggling to keep up—but Donald Trump just made things worse here as well. His return to the White House has sent global defense spending into overdrive, forcing countries to build their own arsenals instead of relying on the US. India got the memo. For the first time, private companies can now manufacture fighter jets, breaking HAL’s decades-long monopoly. The problem: HAL’s delivery delays make it an easy target. With billions pouring into defense worldwide, private competition isn’t just local—it’s global.

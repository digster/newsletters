Good Morning Ishan,

A decade ago, Facebook and Instagram entered my world. I was still in school and had just got my hands on a smartphone.

Relationship statuses, parental conflicts, and detailed whereabouts—suddenly I was exposed to the ins-and-out of someone else’s life. Looking back, these were simpler times when people weren’t expressing themselves in content templates yet and oversharing wasn’t a significant phenomenon.

Sometimes, the information on these platforms felt revelatory (think, a teenager’s guide to love and lovemaking) or irrelevant (the intricate dynamics between friends who didn’t even know I existed).

All of this became part of my feed. And the people who produced these became part of my chosen social network.

All in all, it was a great deal of nothing. Just harmless fun, right? Things, however, have changed since then.

Harm has now infiltrated every social platform. It occasionally even jumps out of the digital territory to the ‘real world’.

Last week, my colleague Arundhati reported on one such jump on a widely-used but little-recognised social network, Unified Payments Interface (UPI).

You could now be under the radar of law enforcement authorities and have your bank account blocked if you’ve (even unknowingly) transacted with scammers or someone who may have made or received payments from fraudsters.

On the social network of UPI, you are the person you transact with. Find out here how the payment system that unites friends and strangers can put you in the crosshairs of cops and banks.

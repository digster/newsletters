Good Morning Ishan,
 

As you might have guessed from the subject, our story today is about the rather ephemeral concept of original thinking. Wait, don’t delete this email just yet. Give me a minute.
 
Google the words “original thinking” and you’ll end up with “Five Tips for Original Thinking”, “4 Steps to Original Thinking”, “Three things you didn’t know about original thinking” and more. Much more. From articles on reasonably respectable sites such as Forbes and Inc to “business thinking” and “life hacks” blogs.
 
And I have a bit of a confession to make. You see, I’ve written a few of those blogs and newsletters. On topics like “Five hacks to get your mind ready for success” and “Business lessons from Diwali for managers”. I'm not making this up. And don’t be too quick to judge, I was young, and the money wasn’t great but it was money earned by Googling and cobbling together a totally unoriginal 400 or 500 words. It worked.
 
It was, however, distinctly different from what we have for you today. Because it’s by two guys who have really lived by what they preach. (As opposed to mass-produced “content” from 20-somethings looking to make a buck ghostwriting.) Yes, it’s time for our monthly piece by Saurabh Mukherjea and Anupam Gupta on their Simplicity Paradigm. Neither of whom is afraid of going against the grain.
 
Saurabh, for one, has gone from newspaper boy to bathroom cleaner to cashier in a betting shop, to economist with the UK’s financial regulator and management consultant. All by the tender age of 25. Today, he’s highly respected for what the finance folks call “contrarian” views.
 
Anupam, on the other hand, is a pedigreed analyst. From CLSA to HDFC Securities to Barclays. Now, he’s a consultant with Saurabh's firm. But his real passion is his (rather popular) podcast on personal finance—he wants to reach out to the world, he’s not afraid to say.
 
In today’s piece, they break down what original thinking means to them—a concept that encompasses everything from radical thinkers to those who take ideas and constructs that already exist and improve them.
 
They break down their own experiences of “thinking differently”. Which they stress is not an exhaustive account of creativity or innovation, just what works for them. Clearly, it’s worked pretty well.

Good Morning Ishan,
As part of its broader Digital India mission, the Indian government’s penchant for open networks is well-known and appreciated. There is the hugely successful Unified Payments Interface (UPI) that has become ubiquitous for seamless digital payments. The Open Network for Digital Commerce (ONDC), too, is making steady progress.
But there is one crucial laggard in this quest—Unified Health Interface (UHI). This was to be a UPI of sorts for the health sector. According to the consultation paper on UHI, some of the key uses of this platform would be “enabling a large segment of Indian users who do not have physical access to doctors to be able to connect with them digitally; and share health information with their health-service providers and receive prescriptions, lab reports and doctor notes digitally on their devices.”
For hospitals, the key takeaway was the ability to gauge demand for a healthcare service on UHI and then sell it to the patient. Doctors and hospitals would also be able to access patient health records digitally with the click of a button.
But as things stand, UHI is making little progress, and hospitals are unwilling to come on board. And these reasons have contributed to the ongoing pull and push between a think tank named iSpirt (Indian Software Product Industry Round Table), the brains behind UHI, and the National Health Authority, the nodal agency for implementing the programme. iSpirt is the invisible force that spearheaded the advent of the national biometric ID programme Aadhar as well as UPI in India.
In today’s story, Anushka goes behind the scenes and looks at what is stalling the progress of this ambitious programme.

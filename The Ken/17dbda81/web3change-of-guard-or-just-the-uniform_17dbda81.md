In its broadest sense, Web3 refers to a version of the internet where we’ve gotten rid of the middlemen and replaced them with applications governed by users. The middlemen here are usually characterised as Big Tech (à la Meta, Twitter, Google). And the applications replacing them as being governed by users via the ownership of specific crypto tokens, operated using blockchains.
If you think that sounds like a moonshot, I agree. It is one.
One problem springs to mind straight away, for instance. How many people really understand how blockchains work? And will people adopt such systems if they don’t understand how it works?
Well, Olga Mack, entrepreneur and blockchain lecturer at the University of California, Berkeley, has [this](https://www.npr.org/2021/11/21/1056988346/web3-internet-jargon-or-future-vision) to say: “To the average person, it does sound like voodoo. But when you press a button to switch on lights, do you understand how the electricity is made? You don't have to know how electricity works to understand the benefits. Same is true of the blockchain."
Now, the massive surge in crypto prices over the last two years has undoubtedly contributed to the buzz around decentralised internet applications. And some of the excitement may evaporate once prices cool down. But close to 8,500 such apps are operational currently, according to data provider DappRadar.

You ask me to name one Indian company whose influence is wildly disproportionate to its size, and I'll immediately say Dunzo.
Its revenue in the year ended March 2022 was a piddly $7 million. Zomato's, for context, was almost $515 million.
And, as problematic as private-market valuations are, India has had over a hundred startups—including some real head-scratchers—valued at $1 billion or more. Dunzo is not one of them.
But none of this mattered when it came to how Dunzo was perceived. Here was a trailblazer that made it possible to quickly courier the earbuds your friend left at your place or to get a can of Diet Coke delivered in minutes.
In the process, Dunzo created the kind of consumer brand any tech company would kill for.
Then, the tide turned for once-celebrated quick-commerce startups, and Dunzo had to rethink its identity and lend its logistics chops to other brands.
Being a delivery partner to the likes of McDonald's and Licious brought in just 10% of Dunzo's revenue last year. Now, it's 30–35%. At the centre of this shift is Jiomart—the e-commerce service of Dunzo's largest investor, Reliance Industries—which is Dunzo's largest enterprise customer.
This, some would say, is a boring business. And Dunzo was never meant to be boring.

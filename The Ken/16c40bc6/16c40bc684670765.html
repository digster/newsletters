Good Morning Ishan,
 
India’s healthcare system may be broken in too many places to count, but the progressive and methodical elimination of many deadly and debilitating diseases is one of its indisputable victories. And no disease struck more fear in the mind of Indians, especially parents, than polio. Poliomyelitis infected children on a large scale in the 1900s, beginning with the common symptoms of a flu but ending in permanent paralysis or death for a small subset of those infected. But a globally coordinated program of vaccinations and surveillance rid practically most of the world of this dreaded disease in the 1990s. And the WHO declared India Polio-free in 2014.

But nature doesn’t stand still, and neither do viruses and diseases. So even though polio’s gone, a plethora of newer names have taken its place. Names like AFP (Acute Flaccid Paralysis), a broad basket of symptoms with causes other than polio, but which affected 36,338 children in India last year. Names like EV71 and EVD-68, microbes belonging to the genus enterovirus (to which polio belongs), which have been dubbed “the next polio.”  Names like West Nile virus and Epstein Barr virus, both of which are also known to trigger paralysis even though they’re not not enteroviruses. Names like Guillain-Barre Syndrome, an autoimmune illness that can result from even an influenza vaccine.

But, argues contributor Priyanka Pulla in her deeply reported and worrisome story, India hasn’t changed its approach to surveillance in a post-polio world. Even as a rash of newer viruses and diseases spread through diverse states like Uttar Pradesh, Maharashtra and Karnataka, it is ill-equipped to investigate non-polio AFP cases. 

“This is very bad news,” says VK Saxena, who heads the Enterovirus Research Centre, referring to a particularly virulent variety of EV71 among AFP-afflicted children in the North-eastern state of Mizoram. “If the virus spreads to the rest of India, we will be in deep trouble because we are not prepared for anything like that.”

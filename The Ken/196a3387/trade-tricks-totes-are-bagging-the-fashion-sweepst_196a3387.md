Trade Tricks
Tue, 06 May 25
A weekly newsletter about the biggest changes in commerce—focusing on shifts that matter to you..
Good Morning Ishan,
My relationship with tote bags goes all the way back to my childhood. Of course, we just called them jholas then.
I remember my mother stitching them out of older garments, especially worn-out skirts and jeans. The first one she made for me, for instance, was fashioned out of an old denim skirt, with a baby pink strap and a Barbie inscribed on the front.
They weren’t fashion statements. To me, and most people around me, they were just everyday bags to carry our meagre spoils in—the “fancy” ones came with zips and a small compartment or two. Lugged around by everyone from the postman to the farm worker to the archetypal, bespectacled journalist; mostly made of cotton or khadi and with zero decoration.
Put that humble jhola and some specimens of the newest rage in handbags next to each other, and you might be forgiven for thinking they’re basically the same thing. Maybe some difference in proportions. A change or two in patterns and texture.
But nothing much about the tote bag right now can be called humble.
I had the chance to speak to several people in the fashion and accessories space over the past few days, and they all agree that totes are taking centrestage as the accessory of choice in an increasing number of wardrobes. Especially among the younger crowd, and across wealth strata. Think broke college students and the fancy kid holding a Subko coffee.
And as they’ve transitioned from humble tools to high-fashion statements, they’ve attracted a stream of luxury and premium brands looking to bag a jackpot or two.
In fact, according to Mohit Jain, founder of luxury handbags and accessories brand Miraggio, totes might constitute 40% of the entire handbag market right now. They have become a staple, adds Laksheeta Govil, founder and CEO of Fizzy Goblet, a premium designer footwear and accessories brand.
To be clear, totes have been on this path for a while. Even decades ago, some high fashion brands were tapping into the simple appeal of the concept and giving it a luxury spin. Variety started appearing in texture and material. Leather took over from trademark canvas. They started taking over space in fashion catalogues and designer collections.
The 1984 Hermeś Birkin bag, for instance, was launched to be a practical yet stylish carry-all, much like a tote. Yet, it cost as much as the downpayment for a flat in Mumbai.
If anything, things have only ramped up since. To quote Vogue Business, totes are now “big business”.
In 2023, the hashtag for the Marc Jacobs Tote bag garnered 86.5 million views on TikTok alone, with Google searches for the bag surging by 1,850 per cent compared to 2022. Dior’s Book Tote was also highly sought after, averaging 40,000 monthly searches throughout 2020 to 2023. Lyst named The Row’s Margaux bag the hottest product of last quarter after searches spiked by 63 per cent, thanks to celebrity fans like Kendall Jenner, Zoë Kravitz and Jennifer Lawrence.
“We are witnessing a shift in the perceived value and how much people are willing to spend on a luxury tote,” says Marta Indeka, senior foresight analyst at strategic foresight consultancy The Future Laboratory. “Until a couple of years ago, Goyard used to be the ultimate luxury tote, but now that consumers started seeing casual everyday bags as investment pieces, many brands have successfully launched designs that reached It-bag status.”

But setting aside the glamour of high-street fashion, it is really the casual everyday segment that is [estimated](https://www.databridgemarketresearch.com/reports/global-tote-bags-market#:~:text=The%20casual%20everyday%20segment%20is,among%20young%20and%20urban%20consumers.) to have the highest share (and highest potential) in the tote market, driven by young and urban customers’ desire for stylish and multipurpose bags.
And India is experiencing a design boom in this space to feed the growing demand, say people in the segment.
Search for “tote bag” on any popular fashion retailer and you’ll see scores of brands and hundreds of options. For those who prefer a minimalistic feel with more emphasis on colours, there’s Dailyobjects. And Zouk caters to the design-heavy connoisseur, who maybe wants a motif or a mandala-themed edition.
One reason why brands are so keen on the category, according to Saxena, is because they are highly unisex, with even typical men’s backpacks now regularly being swapped for cloth totes. That expands the market far beyond anything for a “regular handbag”.
Totes have even accrued such heft that they are now popular cogs in marketing and community campaigns. To quote Gautam Sinha, of artisanal leather brand Nappa Dori, the tote has almost [become](https://m.economictimes.com/industry/cons-products/fashion-/-cosmetics-/-jewellery/bag-to-differ-how-jholas-and-bazaar-baskets-are-getting-stylish-makeovers-as-perfect-casual-bags/amp_articleshow/120023021.cms) something of a “calling card”:
And no one really expects the fad to fade away anytime soon.
Though, apparently, if Indian tote users had a say, there is one way they could be better.
“We [Indians] really want a zipper on the tote bag,” Govil told me. “Not just for like one compartment, but the entire thing needs to have a zipper and should feel like it’s secure.” Which is why, according to her, Fizzy Goblet’s first tote bag iteration featured a zipper for the central compartment that is now a brand staple.
I guess my mother really was ahead of the curve with the zippers.

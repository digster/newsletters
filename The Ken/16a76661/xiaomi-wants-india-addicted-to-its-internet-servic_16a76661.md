The story was headlined, “In China, an Empire built by Aping Apple”.
Xiaomi’s affinity to build Apple-inspired products is hardly new. Its playbook has been to copy what Apple did and deliver it at a lower price. Get market share. Win. That’s what they did, more or less. And it worked. In fact, the company did other things to play up their Apple association. Their CEO, Lei Jun, gives an annual presentation wearing black turtlenecks and blue jeans.
Right from the beginning, that’s how Xiaomi was defined. First by itself. And then by others. Apple lite. Affordable Apple. Apple of Asia.
Then Xiaomi entered India.
In a short time, Xiaomi made its mark. Soon, it became the leader at 29% of the market and accounts for 57% of smartphone sales online. Xiaomi got here through a combination of great products, aggressive pricing, and smart marketing. But things are starting to slip. It always does in this segment.
So what does Xiaomi do when it’s stuck in a market with razor-thin hardware margins, aggressive competitors and no differentiation? Unsurprisingly, exactly what Apple did. Services. Music. Storage. Content. Apple Music. iCloud. iTunes. Same stuff. Different names.
But it also did things Apple never did. Like showing ads on its phones.
Why?
But Xiaomi is in a bit of a chicken and egg situation. It can choose to monetise using advertising, or it can monetise using services. To do the former, it needs to differentiate by continuing to build compelling and inexpensive devices so that it keeps market share. To do the latter, it needs to build great applications that its users are willing to pay for which drives up recurring revenue.
And Xiaomi’s users don’t seem like the type who will pay for stuff. Turns out there are some things that aren’t that easy to replicate.
So what are Xiaomi’s plans in India around services?

Let’s say you walk into your favourite coffee shop and order a flat white. You have a crisp banknote (lucky you), digital currency issued by the government, a balance in your e-wallet, and stablecoins accessible on your phone. The cashier accepts all four. How would you like to pay?

It depends on where this vendor is. If you’re in Shenzhen, China, for instance, there’s a chance you’d be turned away if you only had a hundred-yuan note, while the barista apologises for not being able to rustle up coins or bills for change. But across India, UPI and cash are routine.

The number of UPI transactions keeps going up month by month, aside from a slight dip in June. While that form of digital transactions is becoming more prevalent, the surprising development is that the amount of cash available in India is also growing.

The Reserve Bank of India’s research says there was Rs 37 lakh crore of cash in circulation in FY25, a bump of Rs 2 lakh crore from the previous year.

This is all to say that Indians aren’t about to quit cash anytime soon, as much as the RBI wants the e-rupee to proliferate and says the usage of cash impedes progress. Ateesh explores all of this and more in today’s story.

We often say, “ignore the degree, hire for talent or passion”—and that sounds great in theory. But in practice, it’s extremely hard to templatise. For an HR manager, this becomes a minefield—opening up risks of bias, favouritism, and inconsistency.

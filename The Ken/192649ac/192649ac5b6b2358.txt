Three things to know about Indian edtech right now. The largest funding rounds from the last financial year have little to do with actual teaching

Good Morning Ishan,

We’ve been on this journey for a solid part of three years now. It’s taken us through the absolute best and worst times in Indian edtech. Right now, I’d say we’re in some sort of holding pattern. Edtech companies and investors are too shaken by the big purge of 2021 to make any large bets. Yet, in fresh funding rounds, investors are breaking out of old patterns.

Physicswallah’s fundraise, from a few weeks ago, might’ve seemed like a revival of the old test-prep guard, but funding trends over the last two years show a steep decline for the traditional business models of edtech.

1. Education loans are a legit edtech business model now

Traxcn, a business intelligence firm, released a comprehensive report https://cdn.tracxn.com/marketing-campaigns/EdTech_-_India_-_Tracxn_Feed_Report_VJnHLXb3jDkXglSZSYrCg.pdf last month about the state of edtech funding in India. The most funded business models, over 2023 and the first quarter of 2024, are businesses that extend education loans to students and edtech shops that help students prepare for college. A large subset of the latter are study-abroad platforms. Avanse, an education loan provider, has raised over US$200 million in the last two years. 

The other popular sectors in the funding mix are loans for school education, course-creation engines, and college-discovery platforms. Noticeably missing are test-prep and school-level course providers.

In fact, companies that fall into the category of “admission support for students” or more generally, college prep, seem to be the only ones attracting funder interest, with the recent exception of PW.

One can argue that extending loans, or advice, isn’t really education, and that there should be boundaries drawn around what edtech signifies. The question is, who decides that? The needs of the market? Or funders looking for their next big exit?

2. Both companies and investors are now wary of the seed stage

Have we run out of good ideas to test in edtech? It would seem that no new technology, or groundbreaking business model, has naturally occurred in Indian edtech. This tells us that the widespread application of AI is maybe a few funding cycles away.

In the meantime, the number of seed-stage rounds have dwindled over the last half-decade.

Seed-stage funding has fallen from US$76 million in 2019 to just over US$8 million in 2024. In 2020, this number had jumped to US$91 million, but contrast that with the jump in total funding for edtech in general—US$2.3 billion—most of which was scooped up by Byju’s. Money continued to pour into the sector in 2021, which was peak time for edtech funding, but finally cooled once students returned to school. That would’ve been the time to innovate on business models that weren’t completely reliant on marketing dollars spent on attracting new students.

But it has been the seed stage that’s been most impacted by the downturn in funding, as evidenced by the second graph to the right. The number of rounds fell from a high of 278 in 2019 to a mere 26 in the first half of 2024. This means that five years from now, there are going to be fewer early-to-mid stage bets for investors to take, and even fewer bets that make it to the later stages.

3. The demographics of the founder pool haven’t budged

This graph isn’t entirely surprising. 

The preference for IIT-bred founders is an open secret among those of us who’ve covered Indian edtech for a while. IIT Bombay tops the chart—its alumni have scooped up almost a billion in investor dollars in the last few years. Notably, the biggest fish in the sea, Byju Raveendran, isn’t an IIT graduate. And neither is Alakh Pandey, an engineering school dropout. 

These founders have pulled in funding based partly on their larger-than-life personalities. For the rest though, a degree from an elite engineering or business school has had an outsized benefit in attracting funds. It may also tell us something about who represents these fund houses: like funds like.

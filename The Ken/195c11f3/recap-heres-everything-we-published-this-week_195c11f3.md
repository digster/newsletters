[Phonepe spent millions to rival Policybazaar. Its users couldn’t care less](https://the-ken.com/story/phonepe-spent-millions-to-rival-policybazaar-its-users-couldnt-care-less/?utm_source=weekly_email&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=weekly_recap_card)
The Walmart-owned fintech’s failure to sell insurance to its UPI users means the payments company’s desire for a new business with scale remains just that
Since its inception in 2020, Phonepe has invested over Rs 1,000 crore in its insurance business, spending nearly Rs 700 crore on advertising and promotions alone. Yet, the vertical could generate just about Rs 150 crore in revenue and sell nearly 15 million policies; the financial-services arm contributes just about 4% to Phonepe’s revenue. Being over-reliant on payments could hurt Phonepe given that the government is now considering cutting down UPI-related subsidies. With an IPO around the corner, that could leave Phonepe gasping for breath if it doesn’t diversify its revenue streams sooner. He ran Karnataka’s water department. Now, he’s winning its biggest contracts
Denta Water and Infra listed on the bourses on 29 January, just weeks before Bengaluru’s water crisis began rearing its head. One of the key promoters of this engineering, procurement and construction (EPC) company is former secretary of Karnataka’s minor irrigation department C Mruthyunjaya Swamy. The government’s inability to solve the water crisis has resulted in a mushrooming of private water-infrastructure companies like Denta. In the meanwhile, existing water departments are broken, courtesy of inadequate staff and bureaucratic inefficiency. The trick is to combine the advantages of legacy with the agility of a new-age brand
Indian family businesses contribute more than two-thirds to India’s GDP, with younger generations almost always expected to run these businesses. However, brands built by family businesses, like Baidyanath’s syrups or Medimix’s soaps, are now being bracketed as “parent’s brands”. The result? Second- and third-generation founders are starting their own brands like Dr Vaidya’s, Sadhev, Kitchen Treasures, and Kapiva. Though legacy opens doors for them, the struggle of charting a new course, while getting both parents and employees onboard, is real.

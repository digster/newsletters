Phonepe spent millions to rival Policybazaar. Its users couldn’t care less 

The Walmart-owned fintech’s failure to sell insurance to its UPI users means the payments company’s desire for a new business with scale remains just that

Since its inception in 2020, Phonepe has invested over Rs 1,000 crore in its insurance business, spending nearly Rs 700 crore on advertising and promotions alone. Yet, the vertical could generate just about Rs 150 crore in revenue and sell nearly 15 million policies; the financial-services arm contributes just about 4% to Phonepe’s revenue. Being over-reliant on payments could hurt Phonepe given that the government is now considering cutting down UPI-related subsidies. With an IPO around the corner, that could leave Phonepe gasping for breath if it doesn’t diversify its revenue streams sooner. [Read the full story]

Deep dives

This ex-bureaucrat was part of Bengaluru’s water problem. Now, he is selling the solution

He ran Karnataka’s water department. Now, he’s winning its biggest contracts

Denta Water and Infra listed on the bourses on 29 January, just weeks before Bengaluru’s water crisis began rearing its head. One of the key promoters of this engineering, procurement and construction (EPC) company is former secretary of Karnataka’s minor irrigation department C Mruthyunjaya Swamy. The government’s inability to solve the water crisis has resulted in a mushrooming of private water-infrastructure companies like Denta. In the meanwhile, existing water departments are broken, courtesy of inadequate staff and bureaucratic inefficiency. [Read the full story]

Your guide to India’s Rs 10,000-cr AI mission that’s running on FOMO

Taxpayers’ money is spent on looking for ‘one’ breakthrough moment, aka Deepseek. Building competitive advantage and solving large problems could be surprise by-products

Money-wise, India’s $1.2 billion for its AI programme is not just modest, but meagre. Impact-wise, Meity can define clear problems and put the government’s weight behind solving them. Approach-wise, what we have today is spray-and-pray. Opportunity-wise, there’s a real chance to create a few large companies which solve population-scale problems. [Read the full story]

Google, Facebook, and… Blinkit? The new battle for India’s ad money

Advertisers are cashing in on quick commerce—by pitching products you can’t even buy there

Quick commerce is burning cash, but ads are bringing in Rs 3,000–3,500 crore a year. Non-endemic ads—credit cards, loans, stock-trading apps—are flooding these platforms, turning waiting screens into prime ad space. And advertisers are loving the affluent, high-spending users—and the data that comes with this. But marketers are still figuring out what works, with no clear playbook for a successful quick-commerce campaign. [Read the full story]

*****

Inheritor turns founder: Go Zero, Kapiva defy business-family convention

The trick is to combine the advantages of legacy with the agility of a new-age brand

Indian family businesses contribute more than two-thirds to India’s GDP, with younger generations almost always expected to run these businesses. However, brands built by family businesses, like Baidyanath’s syrups or Medimix’s soaps, are now being bracketed as “parent’s brands”. The result? Second- and third-generation founders are starting their own brands like Dr Vaidya’s, Sadhev, Kitchen Treasures, and Kapiva. Though legacy opens doors for them, the struggle of charting a new course, while getting both parents and employees onboard, is real. [Read the full story]

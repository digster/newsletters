Good Morning Ishan,
 
A conversation I had with a friend recently about Russia’s invasion of Ukraine ended with this conclusion: we’re living in a simulation and whatever powers are in charge of it are unable to progress to the next level.
 
I mean, the early decades of the 20th century had a pandemic, a recession, and multiple world wars. The early decades of the 21st century have had, so far, multiple recessions, a pandemic that shows no signs of slowing, and, well, Russia just invaded Ukraine (the Middle East has been locked in conflict for decades now, but we all know it doesn’t count).
 
Another friend suggested that the next thing on the cards could possibly be alien contact. Sir, Enrico Fermi would like to have a word with you.
 
But pardon my dark humour. There are few other ways to react when you see the Taliban call for peace in Europe.
 
It’s inexcusable that history keeps repeating itself, but at the same time, it also feels inescapable. It feels like we’re doomed to be stuck in the same cycle over and over again, like some particularly nasty episode of Groundhog Day.  
 
This week at The Ken, we featured companies that are looking to learn from their own histories, trying to see what they can, or should, change. Or in the case of some of India’s lending fintechs, it’s their customers.
 
In a stellar piece of reporting—also our free story of the week—Arundhati hunted down the ghost loans haunting some of India’s fintechs after a number of customers discovered loans they’d never taken reflected on their credit histories. At fault are growth-hungry lenders, ignoring established safeguards and willing to take shortcuts in their quest to build out their loan books.
 
Soumyajit’s story on YouTube analysed why the video streaming platform decided to kill its Originals division, its content production arm, but keep Premium, its subscription business, alive. It has to do with YouTube’s own history of hosting user-generated content.
 
Aayush wrote about how 25-year-old Indiamart, India’s largest B2B platform—and also possibly its oldest—needs to put away its history and begin thinking like a growing company again. The company has to both compete with newer and more advanced startups in the space and also open up new business avenues after the pandemic.
 
The subject of Arpit’s story this week, LEAD School, is doing something few Indian edtechs want to do—digitise hundreds of thousands of India’s affordable private schools. However, the newly-minted edtech unicorn has more than a few speed bumps in its way.
 
Bhumika’s story delved into direct-to-customer (D2C) marketplaces. Or rather how D2C brands have evolved from depending on large behemoths like Amazon and Flipkart to opening their own e-commerce portals to listing in small, niche, curated marketplaces.
 
On the newsletters front, we have a bunch of must-reads this week: Praveen’s deep dive into what the war in Ukraine means for India, from an economic perspective; Jaideep’s analysis of the whole Wriddhiman Saha-anonymous cricket journalist saga and why it stems from the toxic nexus between Indian cricketers and the media; and Seema’s piece on why chemistry, while not as glamorous as other subjects, is just as important. After all, chemistry is called the “central” science.
 
That’s all from me. I’m going to keep an eye on what’s unfolding in Europe and hope for the best. If there’s anything that history has taught us, it’s that—as trite as it sounds—this too shall pass.
 
Have a restful Sunday. We’ll see you tomorrow.
 
Best,

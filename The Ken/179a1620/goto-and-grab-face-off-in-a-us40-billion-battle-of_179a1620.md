Go regional, or stay local? That's been the number one question on entrepreneurs' minds ever since startups became a thing in Southeast Asia.
The principles are simple: stick to what you know and do it well and deep, or be bold, go broad and spread yourself as wide as you can.
It’s an existential question for most companies, but for the first time, we’re about to get some answers in the form of two would-be US$40 billion public market companies.
In the regional camp sits Grab. The Singapore-based ride-hailing firm put its flag in eight countries in Southeast Asia, saw off Uber’s local business, and is shooting to list in the US through a SPAC merger deal that values it at US$40 billion. (We
[wrote](https://the-ken.com/story/the-ripple-effects-of-grabs-us40-bn-spac-listing?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter) about its chances recently.)
In the local corner is GoTo, the pride of Indonesia and the subject of today’s story.
The name may be new, but GoTo combines the proven might of ride-hailing firm Gojek and e-commerce company Tokopedia. They push deep with nearly 12 billion registered merchants in Indonesia and 1.8 billion claimed transactions per year, just shy of Grab’s regional 1.9 billion.
But, while Grab spans Southeast Asia, more than 90% of Gojek’s business is in Indonesia, despite efforts overseas. Tokopedia has no business outside of home.
With very different approaches, can GoTo and Grab both command US$40 billion valuations?
When Gojek and Tokopedia merged, my gut feeling was that GoTo really needs a regional story to sell to investors. But, as Nadine reports today, its depth in Indonesia—the world’s fourth most populous country—stands out.

Good Morning Ishan,
In agritech, the back-breaking road to real money is paved with good intentions. Most companies start with farmer-facing solutions only to realise that even with millions of farmers on their platform, they can’t make money.
It’s the farmers’ share of wallet. So, most agritechs are faced with a “trilemma” sooner rather than later. As Pranathi’s story argues today, it’s like a triangle—with technology, scale, and impact making its three sides. For VC-funded startups, which by design are hungry for scale, tech and scale are the preferred sides. Impact takes a backseat, but risk capital would’ve nothing of that in their regular business cycle.
Viewed with this lens, all the pivots in Indian agritech in the last few years begin to make sense. Including those of the early movers like Ninjacart, Dehaat, and, most recently, Waycool’s.
In this context, four-year-old Vegrow’s narrow focus on trading in just fruits, with bananas and pomegranates forming the bulk of its trading basket, makes for an interesting story. It’s a company working under the radar—the founders initially refused to engage with Pranathi, saying, “We are media shy”.
But others in the industry were not shy about decoding its business model.
At the outset, Vegrow operates a straightforward model: it aggregates supplies from farmers, farm gate partners, importers, and farm aggregators, then sells through various channels, including mandis, exports, wholesalers, and modern retail.
Unlike the input focus of other platforms, it focuses only on the output side of the business.
Without being everything to everyone, Vegrow is growing rapidly. Is that a blueprint for future agritech? Let’s dive into [the story](https://the-ken.com/vegrow-takes-on-ninjacart-and-dehaat-with-two-fruits-just-two/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter).

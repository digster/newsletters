Two years ago, Indian restaurants decided that they'd had enough of food delivery apps Zomato and Swiggy. The two platforms charged heavy commissions—as high as 30%—normalised deep discounting, and refused to share customer data with the restaurants. The breaking point was Zomato's loyalty programme, Gold, which forced restaurants to foot the cost of freebies while paying Zomato for the privilege.

For the restaurants to have any hope of countering the food delivery behemoths, however, they had to band together. Individually, they may as well have been negotiating with the wind. Rallying under various banners, they managed to rein Zomato and Swiggy in, even if just slightly. Gold was even halted and then relaunched under a new banner, and with a more restaurant-friendly proposition.

But just as restaurateurs felt they'd changed the power dynamic, Covid struck. With dine-in effectively decimated, restaurants were more dependent on Zomato and Swiggy than ever.

The fact that the combined might of India's restaurant industry could barely budge Zomato and Swiggy makes what Domino's Pizza has done in India all the more impressive. The brand has managed to make its app a genuine alternative to the two food delivery giants. In the quarter ended December 2020, it accounted for half the revenue of Jubilant FoodWorks, which runs the Domino's franchise in India. Zomato and Swiggy together didn't contribute even half that in the same quarter.

Domino's also pays far lower commissions for orders placed through the food delivery duo compared with other restaurants. It even has a unique data-sharing agreement in place. It's a win-win for Jubilant.

Now, Jubilant must recreate this magic for its newer brands in the Chinese cuisine and biryani space. Unlike its runaway success with Domino's, however, this will not prove as easy. How Domino's scored a sweetheart deal with Zomato and Swiggy and why Jubilant is unlikely to manage an encore is the subject of today's story by Seetharaman.

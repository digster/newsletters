Long and Short
Wed, 29 Oct 25
The story behind the numbers and motivations driving India's stock market.
Good Morning Ishan,
If you grew up in Chennai in the nineties, you can’t have missed going to an Arun Icecreams outlet for that rare treat. Arun Icecreams is to Chennai what Corner House is to Bengaluru or Nirula’s is to Delhi. My personal Arun Icecreams favourite used to be the Cassata. What six-year-old can resist something that looks like a rainbow?
Behind this beloved brand is Hatsun Agro Product, India’s largest private sector dairy company, with a market cap of Rs 25,000 crore. It has a strong presence in Tamil Nadu, also owns other brands like Ibaco, its premium range of ice-creams, and Arokya branded milk, paneer, and curd.
But the 39-year-old company has had a rather lacklustre performance on the stock exchanges, even lagging the BSE FMCG index for most of the year.
That is, until 27 October.

On that day, its stock saw a sharp vertical climb, rising by almost 20% to Rs 1,080 a share after its second-quarter results, hitting the upper circuit limit.
This rally was fuelled by its profit after tax, which jumped 70% to Rs 110 crore from a year ago—among the highest it has seen in 10 quarters, as revenue rose 17% to about Rs 2,430 crore.
The Rs 223 crore [acquisition](https://www.thehindubusinessline.com/economy/agri-business/hatsun-agro-net-profit-up-by-70-in-q2-on-acquisition-synergy/article70207803.ece) of Odisha-based dairy company Milk Mantra (known as Milky Moo) played a part in this increase. And now, it’s on its way to becoming the Rs 10,000 crore company it hopes to be by the end of the year. In the first half of the 2026 financial year, its revenue stood at over Rs 5,000 crore, with a profit of Rs 245 crore, which the company claims makes it the first private dairy company to see such numbers.
Most dairy companies live and die by milk procurement prices, but Hatsun has shown this quarter that it can take the edge off that impact.
The proof lies in its margins.
The company’s operating margins in the September quarter grew to 13% from 12% a year ago. This came at a time when milk procurement costs rose that quarter.
This pressure is evident at its smaller rival, Heritage Foods, where margins fell by 100 basis points from a year ago to just 7% in the latest quarter.
But Hatsun dodged that bullet, thanks to two reasons.
One is its capacity utilisation. ICICI analysts say Hatsun is better at managing average production, reducing wastage, and ultimately needing to invest less in capacity overall.
The Chennai-based company processes over 40 lakh litres of milk and milk products daily across its 20 processing facilities in Tamil Nadu, Andhra Pradesh, Karnataka, Telangana, and Maharashtra. It owns over 13,000 milk banks, covering around 10,000 villages with chilling and dairy units across its key markets. This enables it to get access to over 500,000 farmers. (For context, a co-operative like Amul sources milk from 3.6 million farmers.)
The other reason for the company’s better margins is the contribution from its ice-cream segment, the most lucrative product in dairy.
Hatsun was onto value-added products even before it became a trend with dairies—it began with Arun Icecreams and only later took to selling milk. Similarly Milky Mist, another Tamil Nadu entrenched dairy which is looking to go public at a valuation similar to Hatsun’s, has also built its business entirely around selling milk products—curd, paneer, etc.—with milk constituting only 5% of its topline, as The Ken [wrote](https://the-ken.com/story/milky-mist-is-anti-milk-its-still-a-rs-20000-cr-dairy-giant/) earlier.
ICICI analysts, in a December 2024 note, said they expect the revenue contribution of the ice-cream segment to expand to ~14% in FY26E from ~12% in FY24. The company does not give this breakdown.
As much as ice-cream is central to Hatsun’s story, the segment remains particularly challenging. The likes of HUL have [struggled](https://the-ken.com/story/how-hindustan-unilevers-ice-cream-demerger-is-not-about-ice-creams-alone/) with their ice-cream brands because of the high costs involved. Moreover, there are over 200 brands in the country, but only a few have become national players; among them, Amul itself has a 40% share in the organised market.
Though Hatsun has grown its market share—thanks to greater acceptance of its brand in Maharashtra and its recent acquisition of the Milky Moo brand in Odisha—it will have to contend with the likes of Milky Mist in its home base and upstarts like Hocco and Go Zero elsewhere.
While the second quarter seems like a comeback, the company has its work cut out. Its profit in the September quarter was still sequentially lower than in the preceding June quarter.
Moreover, it’s still valued at nearly 70 times its earnings. Its nearest competitor, Heritage, which is just about half its size revenue-wise, is valued at only about 26 times earnings.
Sure, this valuation is lower than the 135 times it commanded in 2023, but investors will expect the company to grow into its current valuation. Ice-cream or not.

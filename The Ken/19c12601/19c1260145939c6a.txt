Open a browser and go to office.com. You’d expect to see details about Office, Microsoft’s hugely popular and profitable office productivity suite. I’m writing this on Word, which is part of Office.

Sorry, I should say was.

Because office.com now welcomes you to the “Microsoft 365 Copilot app”. There is not one mention of the word “office” on the site. 

Copilot, which started out as Microsoft’s coding assistant, has metastasised into something that encompasses everything from code to documents to spreadsheets and presentations? 

I saw this somewhat sad offer of a free trial a few days ago on one of Microsoft’s own, well, Office support sites.

noop:void(0)

On Thursday, Microsoft’s stock fell by 10%, wiping $360 billion off its market capitalisation. Because investors got spooked at both the scale of its data centre spending (66% year-on-year) and how dependent it was on OpenAI (45% of its committed cloud contracts are from OpenAI). 

Guess whose else stock has been taking a hit?

Adobe. 

Its market cap has fallen https://www.tradingview.com/news/invezz:2c96cc73e094b:0-here-s-why-adobe-stock-price-has-crashed-and-why-it-may-rebound/ from a peak of $340 billion to $129 billion. 

Again, it’s AI that’s the culprit. 

Adobe, too, is using free offers https://www.deccanherald.com/technology/after-perplexity-pro-airtel-subscribers-get-free-access-to-adobe-express-premium-3879814 to drum up interest in its offerings. 

I think it’s too late…

This week on the Zero Shot podcast

Hi, it’s Vidhatri. I have made a habit in the last year or so: checking AI-related news twice a day. What started out as curiosity (and anxiety because of all the ‘AI will make your job redundant’ conversation) is now helping me as the producer of Zero Shot, so I am thankful. The one thing that constantly caught my eye was headlines about Apple falling behind in the AI race, bogged down by delays in launching notable AI features or products. We got a major update this month: Siri will now be powered by Google’s Gemini. I thought it was a big win for Google, but did not think much about the larger implications of what it means for the AI race.

And then we recorded the latest Zero Shot episode https://the-ken.com/podcasts/zero-shot/googles-on-the-drivers-seat-in-the-ai-race-who-could-trip-it/, which helped me frame this deal. Rohin Dharmakumar, who was the lead on this episode, started the chat with a strong proposition: Google is miles ahead of all other AI companies because it is able to control “all aspects of AI from consumer services to enterprises to consumer hardware to data centres to even its own chips”. This top-to-bottom hold, Rohin argues, firmly puts it in the driver’s seat of the AI industry. He backed it up with numbers on Google’s leading market share across products (Gmail, Youtube) and markets, rewarding the company.

But does that mean it’s invincible or at the top in the AI battle? Brady Ng countered that it is not necessarily the case. In his point of view, infrastructure efficiency is a better parameter than distribution. He says Microsoft is a contender because the company is thinking about the actual details of AI economics and what makes models better.

What about OpenAI? Praveen Gopal Krishnan, one of our co-hosts, has a take. And it involves ads.

It is a must-listen conversation that takes off from the Apple-Google deal and covers a lot of ground.

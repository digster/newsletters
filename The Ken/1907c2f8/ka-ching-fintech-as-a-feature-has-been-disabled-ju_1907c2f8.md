Thu, 04 Jul 24
A weekly newsletter about how finance is getting supercharged by tech in India, and how you can make money work for you..
Good Morning Ishan,
This week, Zomato’s Board of Directors decided to officially withdraw its application for a non-banking finance company (NBFC) licence. In May, it had [voluntarily surrendered](https://www.livemint.com/companies/news/zomato-to-surrender-rbi-licence-to-operate-as-online-payment-aggregator-11715616638963.html) its payment aggregator licence.
Huh.
Do you remember how not very long ago, every company wanted to be a fintech?
And why wouldn’t they? Indian fintechs [received](https://w.tracxn.com/report-releases/india-fintech-annual-report-2023) a cumulative US$8.4 billion in funding in calendar year 2021—one of the hottest sectors in the country. It got to the point where venture capitalists were joking about how founders could draw a cheque just by mentioning “financial services” in their pitch deck.
But that was in 2021. In 2022, fintechs could raise only around US$5.4 billion. In 2023, this fell to US$2 billion, and the writing on the wall was clear: pureplay fintech was no longer as lucrative a bet as it once was.
But payments and transactions were still going strong. They had, by now, become an inseparable function for most companies—be they in food aggregation, ride hailing, or e-commerce. So many of them decided to go for the natural extension and enabled lending and insurance services too. What were earlier standalone businesses would now exist as mere features on their apps.
Embedded finance, research reports and sectoral analysts called it. Cool folks came up with fintech-as-a-feature.
Clever.
Anyway, whatever they chose to call it, companies loved it. Take Ola, for instance. It has a wallet service, Ola Money; it sells co-branded credit cards; it offers postpaid services. It’s the same with e-commerce giants Amazon and Flipkart.
The hypothesis is straightforward: the hero service, be it ride-hailing or e-commerce, has already acquired a bunch of customers who keep coming back to the app. So why not sell them financial services too?
Neat. Logical.
Only, if it is so simple, what made Zomato back out? And it’s not just Zomato, even non-banks are going the same way. Nine of them have [surrendered](https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/industry/banking/finance/ujjivan-financial-services-fino-finance-other-nbfcs-surrender-licenses/articleshow/111436122.cms?from=mdr) their licences in the recent past—while two ventured technical reasons, the remaining have decided to exit the business altogether.
Fintech-as-a-feature is being toggled off.
And I think there are a couple of major reasons why…

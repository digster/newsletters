Good Morning Ishan,

I don’t know about you, but I still feel a tad uncomfortable shelling out that tidy health-insurance premium every year. Despite knowing fully well its importance and even after years of paying up diligently.

It’s “protection money”, but in a desirable way. Not being covered could burn a big hole in the pocket if and when a health emergency strikes.

Yet, random thoughts arise when the time comes to pay. Why pay to protect from risks that may not really materialise? Is it not money wasted if nothing happens during the year? The rational mind then wrestles down the irrational one, and the credit card gets duly charged. Life goes on.

This year, though, the duel was much tougher. Thanks to my health-insurance provider, which raised the premium amount so steeply that I did a double take. I asked around, and many of my friends and colleagues have also had to part with much bigger sums than the previous year—25%, 30%, or even 50% more. I think many of you might also have experienced this. Did it leave you upset and perhaps looking for options and answers?

What’s really happening? Did anything change drastically for the premiums to go up so sharply?

Abhirami went digging and came out with intriguing findings in her story today. Health insurers, it turns out, are blaming hospitals and the pandemic hangover, among a host of other factors, for the premium spike. Hospitals are pushing back. Easing up of regulations is also playing its part.

In all this, customers are left saddled with bigger, much bigger bills. And it is likely to get worse. What? Why?

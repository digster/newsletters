We are the stories that we tell ourselves

 

In my final year of college, I flunked a core paper. And failing a paper in your final year meant being ineligible for placements and higher studies. But what hurt me the most was that I had known the professor who taught me that subject before I joined the institute. So, the loss was a bit more personal; I felt I had disappointed someone whose approval mattered to me.

 

Now, my ego perceived this loss funnily. I did not sulk. Instead, I daydreamed of filmy scenarios wherein one day—a decade or two later—I would accidentally bump into the same professor at an airport lounge. After polite salutations, I would ask him which flight he was taking. He would mumble the plane’s name and then ask me what I was doing in life. And with a dramatic pause and in full baritone, I would say, “Well, I happen to own the airline you are flying.”

 

Ba-dum, tis!!

 

He would be surprised; I would add, “Flunking the paper was the best thing that happened to me.” (What!? It’s my story. So my rules!)

 

However, fate played spoilsport with my ambitious dream sequence as I bumped into the same professor at the movie theatre last week. And in my mind, only one thought ran: “Oh-no! It’s too early. This wasn’t supposed to happen for another ten years at least.”

 

Well, I assume you, dear reader, are giggling at my unfortunate encounter with my professor. But think about it this way, aren’t most of our plans based on the stories we tell ourselves? And it’s not just us; organisations and companies, policymakers and politicians, preachers and teachers are all also telling stories. Then, we, as investors, clients, and voters, choose to buy or reject the story sold/told to us. And now, suddenly, the whole world appears to be a tale.

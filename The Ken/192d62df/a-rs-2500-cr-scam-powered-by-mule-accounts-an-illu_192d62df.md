Good Morning Ishan,
You might think mules are beasts of burden meant for mountainous terrain, that L1 is an entry-level position for software developers, or that MaaS is some new tech acronym meant to make your life easier. Well, today’s story will have you rethink all of that.
Introducing “Mule-as-a-Service”, or MaaS. It’s the newest scam wave sweeping through India’s financial underbelly, powered by mule service providers. These providers don’t directly participate in the fraud; instead, they deploy a small army of “mules”—people who lend their bank accounts to cybercriminals to launder dirty money through the country’s vast and growing digital-payments network.
The entry-level mules, or “L1s”, are at the base of this pyramid, moving money in and out in just 15 minutes—just quick enough to avoid detection. All in exchange for a cut.
But not all mules, who usually come from low-income backgrounds, participate willingly. Some do it unwittingly.
MaaS is massively scaling up, and these mule accounts fuel a range of scams—phishing, sextortion, and more. So much so that India could lose Rs 1.2 lakh crore to cyber fraud next year, with mule accounts draining 0.7% of the country’s GDP.
The impact is such that even RBI has begun cracking down on this menace. But fraudsters, as always, have found ways to stay ahead.
My colleague Rounak went surfing illegal betting sites and sneaking into Telegram groups to chat with people who operate as pawns in this network. Our design team worked with him to map out the entire MaaS ecosystem, from cradle to grave.

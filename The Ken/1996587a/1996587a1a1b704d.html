Here’s what Praveen has to say in our debut edition.

Last week, OpenAI put up some striking new billboard ads across India.

All of them highlighted ways to use ChatGPT, like asking questions about nutrition.

Or seeking help with schoolwork.

Or… er… whatever this is.

This ad takeover wasn’t limited to major cities like Bengaluru, Delhi, or Mumbai. It even appeared in cities that aren’t typically seen as obvious target markets for AI products. Someone who spotted a ChatGPT billboard in Jaipur wrote, “Open AI is not waiting for India to come to AI, they’re taking AI to every corner.”

Cut to 2015.

At the time, Facebook (now Meta), realised it was running out of new users to acquire, so it set its sights on India—the company’s last growth market. When The Guardian eventually chronicled what happened next, it noted somewhat ominously, “Facebook was not about to sit around waiting for them to get online on their own.”

Sound familiar?

One of the steps Facebook took to hasten this transition in India was launching a product called Internet.org (later rebranded as Free Basics), which offered millions of Indians free access to a limited version of the internet.

The way Silicon Valley’s biggest AI companies are attempting to capture India’s users in 2025 is an upgraded model of what Facebook tried in 2015: offer an experience of the new technology for free and distribute it via partnerships with India’s telcos. Back then, Facebook tied up with Reliance Communications to give away Free Basics. Today, Perplexity is working with Airtel to do the same. In both cases, the motivation is to lock early users in walled gardens after dangling an irresistible freebie. It’s just the audience that has shifted a bit—Facebook targeted lower-income people who had never been online, while OpenAI is going after young students.

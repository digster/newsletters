It’s hard to keep track of the number of companies that edtech decacorn Byju's has acquired over the last few years. From 33-year-old test prep company Aakash Educational Services, which it bought for $1 billion, to a little-known double-solving platform, HashLearn. From kindergarten and primary school to test prep and beyond, Byju’s acquisitions have given it a foothold in almost every corner of the edtech market, both vertically and horizontally.

Except for the public sector.

The Indian education system is a big unwieldy stack of chaos—multiple schooling systems, syllabi that change from state to state, and millions of students who fall through the cracks. And while the more privileged students can sidestep these pitfalls through private schooling, the vast majority of students have to make do with India’s crumbling public schooling system. The pandemic, which only exacerbated already evident inequities, made things worse.

Byju’s is trying to help with its ‘Education for All’ programme, offering its study materials free of charge to underprivileged children. Recently, it signed an agreement with government think tank NITI Aayog to share its copyrighted “scholastic content”—a variation of its supplementary, at-home learning product—to students in classes six to 12, and provide NEET and JEE test prep coaching to 3,000 meritorious students from grades 11 and 12. All this across 112 of India’s most developmentally challenged districts—termed ‘Aspirational Districts’ by NITI Aayog—to begin with.

For Byju’s, this lets it bypass the frustrating, bureaucratic process of catering to each district separately. NITI Aayog, meanwhile, gets to fulfil its mandate of ensuring that Aspirational Districts improve their economic indicators

Ambition sometimes wilts in front of ground reality, however. Neither Byju’s nor NITI Aayog accounted for one of the biggest pieces of the edtech puzzle—implementation. The project seemingly lacks buy-in from states, a clear methodology or any way to measure its success.

It’s only the third time I’m writing the Zero Shot column, but boy, I do feel like Captain Haddock from that viral meme! So much has happened since—*checks my calendar*—15 October that I feel I’m already pre-overwhelmed about the things I have to catch you up on.

AI’s “flood the zone” strategy means we get saturated and overwhelmed before we have time to process developments. I’m going to work hard to consciously avoid that trap in the next edition by focusing on a few things and going deeper.

This week, I want to talk about drudgery.

I picked this very human condition to categorise some of the developments I’ve been tracking for the past few weeks.

Drudgery

Much of corporate life is filled with drudgery that must be performed and endured: writing meeting reports that no one reads, logging timesheet entries that no one cares for, creating project plans that are unrealistic, updating dashboards that no one checks, doing reviews that are perfunctory, attending training programmes that are boring, writing performance reviews, and submitting bills for reimbursement.

In our latest episode of the Zero Shot podcast, Brady pointed out how the biggest value of AI lies in eliminating such inefficiencies at workplaces, not improving productivities. I agreed with him.

Firstly, there are way more inefficiencies in most companies—Brady called it corporate debt—than there are efficiencies.

Secondly, the passable quality of AI required to fix inefficiencies is much lower than that required to maximise existing efficiencies. By that, I mean organisations and workers are more likely to tolerate a passable AI that helps them avoid drudgery than they will an AI that makes them better at what they are already pretty good at.

In my case, for instance, the bar for an AI that can help me write is impossibly high. But for one that lets me transcribe a Youtube video so I can read it instead of sitting through it, the bar is much lower.

For me, watching a long Youtube video is drudgery. Writing is anything but.

But the thing about corporate drudgery is that it’s woven deeply into the fabric of companies. Pull one thread, and the entire tapestry comes unravelling.

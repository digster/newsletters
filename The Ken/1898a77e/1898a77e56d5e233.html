At first glance, building a rocket launch program in Southeast Asia seems far-fetched, but conditions for it are now ripening

Good Morning Ishan,

Many kids around the world dream about strapping into a rocket and heading into the unknown as space explorers, floating in zero-gravity. Few are inspired to build careers out of that fantasy decades later.

But that wasn’t the case for Simon Gwozdz. The founder of Singapore-based Equatorial Space Systems says watching the Hollywood film Apollo 13 when he was five years old set him on the course that eventually led him to establishing a company to develop hybrid-engine rockets.

Fast forward to six years ago. Gwozdz met Praveen G Perumal at a space carrier conference. The two hit it off, with Perumal joining Gwozdz for the project that very weekend.

Not long after, Equatorial’s CTO, Jamie Anderson, signed on with the company after devoting much of his entire life to rocket development. When he was growing up in a small town in Australia, Anderson saw rockets as a vessel for escapism and figured out ways to sate his curiosity:
I used to fund myself by working paper routes and odd jobs like lawn maintenance. Then, I saved all the money to pay for travelling to a university, where I could make photocopies of any journal paper or book about rockets. I essentially taught myself, and I started experimenting with rocket propellants and solid fuels when I was around 15 years old. Fast forward to 1986, and the Challenger accident happened [with the space shuttle exploding after liftoff]. That was a big moment for me. I was watching live, so I saw it happen on live TV. The shock set in. The next morning, the first thing in my head was that there had to be a safer and better way to do this.

Two years later, Anderson began communicating with the American Rocket Company, which was based in California and developed hybrid-propellant rockets. It wasn’t long before the firm signed an MoU (Memorandum of Understanding) to examine the licensed production of its rockets in Australia—Anderson’s first experience with launch commercialisation was more than 33 years ago.

Now, Anderson leads the development of Equatorial’s hybrid-engine rockets.

Singapore as a spaceport?

In the public consciousness, rocket launches are associated with a few locations—Florida’s Merritt Island, Baikonur in the Kazakh Steppe, and Sriharikota in Chennai come to mind. Singapore doesn’t immediately make the list, but Equatorial sees the city-state as a place where existing industries give it certain advantages. Perumal puts it this way:
There are logistical and industrial bases here in Singapore, with industries such as semiconductors, oil, and gas. And since Singapore is well known for its shipping industry, there is a natural progression to use rockets. Since we’re trying to build vehicles, a lot of what we’re doing is to reduce costs and price points by taking learnings and solutions that exist in other industries and using them to build a space industry in Singapore.

Additionally, being near the equator means rockets launched from Singapore or nearby can take full advantage of the planet’s rotation speed, since they will already be moving at roughly 1,650 km per hour relative to Earth’s centre.

Test-Fires

When The Ken spoke with Gwozdz, Perumal, and Anderson in July, they were preparing for a test the next day. It was one in a series of many steps that would take the firm from Technology Readiness Level 5 (breadboard technology that needs rigorous testing) to Level 9, or “flight-proven” via a fully completed mission. Here’s how Anderson described Equatorial’s push forward:
For a propulsion system’s TRL, ground testing will only get you to around the fourth or fifth level. We had a subscale test—a small demonstration flight—but the next step is to scale that demonstration up, including the size of the propulsion system and the flight vehicle. The rocket that we launched in Malaysia three years ago had a diameter of just 10 cm, and it was 2 metres long. Our next one has a 30 cm diameter, and is more than 6 metres long, so this step is already a considerable scaleup.

When it comes to companies that make instruments meant to barrel at high speeds into the sky and break through Earth’s atmosphere, it’s easy to get lost in the poetics of space travel. But hearing Gwozdz, Perumal, and Anderson speak about their work sparks more earthly questions: what kind of permits are needed? What sort of authorisation processes are in place for companies that are building the space trucks of the future?

Given that the fuel composition of Equatorial’s hybrid rocket motor doesn’t fall under rules for restricted chemicals, matters are a bit more simplified. Perumal explains it succinctly:
That’s the neat part—we don’t need it. It really depends on the jurisdiction. In many places, there is no such thing as a license for a rocket engine test on the ground, for instance.

But other licences may be required for ground tests or rocket tests, depending on the jurisdiction. Gwozdz adds:
With that said, you may still need to deal with the environmental protection authorities because you’re emitting gas that may or may not contain anything that’s damaging to the local ecosystem. In our case, we’re in the clear when it comes to environmental impact. The noise that is generated is another important element in the impact assessment before we can proceed, especially at a larger scale.

Since Malaysia doesn’t have a system to issue rocket launch licences, Equatorial seeks clearance from local police and landowners to ensure their bases are covered.

Trucking to travel

When it comes to launch vehicles, the propulsion system comprises more than 90% of the rocket, says Anderson. Building a new system from the ground up could have serious ramifications for the rocket’s architectural design, which means a system that is easy to manufacture and test could make a world of difference.

Equatorial’s team says they’re building the rockets that will be used for space trucking—a way to offer relatively affordable access to lower Earth orbit and beyond. There’s a heavy focus on logistics. The long-term goal is to build such a network across the solar system.

Space tourism remains a possible business direction for the firm, but that is a much more ambitious goal than servicing satellite companies. To reach this point, Equatorial would have to demonstrate very high reliability and an impeccable flight record. Having a propulsion architecture that is inert is a good starting point.

The rest is just aiming for the stars.

Good Morning Ishan,
Many kids around the world dream about strapping into a rocket and heading into the unknown as space explorers, floating in zero-gravity. Few are inspired to build careers out of that fantasy decades later.
But that wasn’t the case for Simon Gwozdz. The founder of Singapore-based Equatorial Space Systems says watching the Hollywood film Apollo 13 when he was five years old set him on the course that eventually led him to establishing a company to develop hybrid-engine rockets.
Fast forward to six years ago. Gwozdz met Praveen G Perumal at a space carrier conference. The two hit it off, with Perumal joining Gwozdz for the project that very weekend.

Two years later, Anderson began communicating with the American Rocket Company, which was based in California and developed hybrid-propellant rockets. Trucking to travel
When it comes to launch vehicles, the propulsion system comprises more than 90% of the rocket, says Anderson. Building a new system from the ground up could have serious ramifications for the rocket’s architectural design, which means a system that is easy to manufacture and test could make a world of difference.
Equatorial’s team says they’re building the rockets that will be used for space trucking—a way to offer relatively affordable access to lower Earth orbit and beyond. There’s a heavy focus on logistics. The long-term goal is to build such a network across the solar system.
Space tourism remains a possible business direction for the firm, but that is a much more ambitious goal than servicing satellite companies. To reach this point, Equatorial would have to demonstrate very high reliability and an impeccable flight record. Having a propulsion architecture that is inert is a good starting point.
The rest is just aiming for the stars.

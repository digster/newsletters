The McDonald’s drive-through’s improbable rise in India. A concept once alien to the country is about to flourish
By Seetharaman G
4 min read
View in browser

Good Morning Ishan,

India is unforgiving to the idea of driving up to a fast-food restaurant and ordering a burger, fries, and Coke—all without stepping out of the car. 

Drive-through restaurants are built on three non-negotiables: a space large enough to park multiple cars; wide roads for the person behind the wheel to zip around and focus on tucking into the burger or taking it home quickly for dinner; and free-flowing traffic that doesn’t force the driver to choose between screwing the Coke lid on and avoiding hitting the pedestrian making an unexpected dash across the road. 

India sucks in all three. 

So it’s commonsensical that if we don’t want to make the effort to get to a restaurant, we let Zomato or Swiggy bring the food to us. 

But McDonald’s seems to think drive-throughs can be big business in India. In the December quarter, Westlife Foodworld, the McDonald’s franchisee for India’s western and southern states, opened 15 outlets. Half of those were drive-throughs. 

These numbers may be puny, but over a fifth of the 421 McDonald’s stores in these two regions are already drive-throughs. And here’s the kicker: every third McDonald’s launched in the future will welcome eaters on wheels. (For context, in the US, 95% of McDonald’s locations have a drive-up section.)

What has made India so friendly to this fast-food channel all of a sudden? 

Ample highways that run counter to India’s mostly creaky public infrastructure are the primary reason. National highways with four lanes or more have expanded two-and-a-half times in length in the decade to 2024. High-speed road corridors totalled a mere 93km in 2014. They had crossed 2,100km by 2024. 

Standalone restaurants or food courts have been the norm along highways. But wider roads generally come with more room on either side. Take the Mumbai-Pune Expressway. When I drove through it this weekend, I spotted a food court with a McDonald’s drive-through. Unlike a few years ago, the food mall is not merely on the stretch. Getting there means taking a detour off the road. 

A Youtube video of a McDonald’s on the Mumbai-Delhi Expressway shows a drive-through as vast as those found in the US. Here, customers often enjoy their meal in the air-conditioned confines of their cars, as is the case with one in four fast-food orders in the US. 

If drive-throughs have found a new lease of life between cities, they have the chance to flourish on their peripheries too. 

Mumbai residents travelling to Navi Mumbai tend to marvel at the relative abundance of the one thing they don’t have: space. In its FY24 annual report, Westlife identified city suburbs and national highways as pockets of opportunity for drive-throughs. Incidentally, the first such McDonald’s outlet in the country, launched around the turn of the century, was in Navi Mumbai.  

Fast-food chains’ yearning for drive-throughs is understandable given the higher volume of orders they can process compared to a dine-in restaurant. The pandemic-influenced shift away from eating at restaurants in the US is evident in the big bets on servicing customers in cars.

Decades after the drive-through revolutionized restaurants, fast-food chains are betting more than ever on customers who never set foot in their dining rooms. McDonald’s is testing a new space-themed concept called CosMc’s, centered on drive-through service. Chipotle Mexican Grill is building hundreds of drive-up lanes for picking up digital orders. Shake Shack, long focused on dine-in service, has been outfitting dozens of locations with drive-throughs.

In India, food delivery dominates instead, thanks to the popularity of Zomato and Swiggy and the availability of an inexpensive workforce to ferry the grub. That certainly won’t change anytime soon. 

But drive-throughs will begin to matter in a way they never have. KFC may not be as keen on motorists as McDonald’s, but every tenth outlet run by one of KFC’s two franchisees is already a drive-through. Indians tempted by the utility and thrills of high-speed highways are a large enough market.

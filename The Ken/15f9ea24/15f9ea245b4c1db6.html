Good Morning Ishan,
 
Travel dreams are what dreams are made of. Well, that’s how it goes for me. 
 
So I’m not surprised that Oyo wants to go places. You know what else isn’t surprising? The way the stars are aligned now. There’s some cash on the table, some semblance of an international strategy, and desire to execute. I mean, not long back, Oyo entered and withdrew from Kuala Lumpur, within just twelve months. And now, it's back. 
 
...On to Thailand, Indonesia and Vietnam.
 
There’s something that I particularly like about today’s piece. How Patanjali has weaved Zomato’s experience venturing into Southeast Asia into the copy. So Zomato went to SE Asia around 2013. In the last four years, it has exited Malaysia, and some bare-bones app is still operational.
 
But in larger countries like Indonesia, the company hasn’t been able to make much headway. Just take Indonesia, for example. The market is dominated by Go-Jek’s Go-Food and Grab’s GrabFood. A Nikkei Asian Review story says, “Go-Food lists some 100,000 stores, from local satay stalls to fast food chains to pricey sushi restaurants.” And more: “Go-Jek said that Go-Food had 15 million orders in its first year [2015]. This is already more than any other food delivery service in Southeast Asia and India, but is a fraction of the market in China, where the top players take millions of orders every day.”
 
Which brings me to Oyo. And I’m not surprised that Maninder Gulati of Oyo says that he is aware of the challenges of the Southeast Asian market. And there, his strategy will be aggressive marketing. “None of these guys can build what Oyo is. If you haven’t built fundamental capabilities, all you can do is OTA handle management. And that isn’t difficult. That anyone can do,” he says.
 
To which Nathan Boublil, CEO of Zen Rooms, Oyo’s competitor says: “Each of these countries speaks not just a different language but has a different pain point.”
 
Point.

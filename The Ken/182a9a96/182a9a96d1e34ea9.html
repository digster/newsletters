Good Morning Ishan,
 
Let’s play connect the dots.
 
One: Every year, nearly 80,000 students enrol themselves into India’s 1,200 law schools.
 
Two: Not all partners at law firms retire.
 
Three: India has a vibrant and growing corporate sector that needs legal services.
 
India’s top law firms don’t like the picture that’s emerging. And rightly so.
 
As of April 2022, one in five lateral law hires were those moving from professional law firms to in-house legal teams at giant corporations. At the end of 2021, that number was one in 20.
 
In-house legal teams are in. Where once a firm might have had a small team of lawyers on its payroll, in-house legal teams these days are the size of small startups themselves. Accenture India, for instance, has a 200-plus strong legal team. Flipkart and Adani Group both have legal teams numbering in the 90s.
 
And it’s not just the corporate segment. Young lawyers can look for opportunities at fund management houses. Or public policy—companies like Ola, Twitter, and Meta have their own public policy teams. Lawyers within these teams get to work at the intersection of law, business, and public policy.
 
At the root of all this is a very simple sentiment: “Young lawyers have no patience for something they do not align with.” By the way, that’s straight from a senior lawyer who moved to a large e-commerce company after having spent nearly a decade in a leading law firm in the country.
 
India’s law firms are now scrambling, handing out partnerships and promotions like candy. One leading New Delhi-based law firm even promoted 15% of its workforce en masse in June 2022.

Good Morning Ishan,
Even without applying the “six handshakes rule”, I know enough people with migraine who’ve been told by their doctors that there’s really “no treatment or cure” for their condition.
As many as 217 million people in India—about 15% of the country’s population—suffer from migraine.
So, when Dr Reddy’s launched a digital therapeutic, a band to be worn on the arm for neuromodulation, more than a year ago, it was worth watching. At least four such products, one as long ago as a decade, have entered the Indian market. But none could crack it.
Dr Reddy’s, which has an exclusive licence with an Israeli device maker, is putting its might behind neurology. (As well as other diseases.) The early results are encouraging, not just for patients but also for Indian pharma, which sorely needs differentiation in the brutally competitive generics industry.
Lupin is dialling up the stakes even further. It launched a cardiac-care-management app last year and has lined up a bunch of insurance companies as its clients. If digital therapeutics for patients means going off pills, for insurers, it’s a shot at shrinking the number of claims they receive.
Indian pharma should look to Apple for inspiration. There’s a growing body of research that shows the Apple Watch is being used informally in medical care even though disease-specific devices have been approved. Interestingly, published work on new applications for the Watch isn’t coming from the tech giant itself.
About a decade ago, Apple had one part-time doctor on its staff; today, it has an army. If pharma companies can assemble and empower a digital team, they can unleash off-label uses for their products.

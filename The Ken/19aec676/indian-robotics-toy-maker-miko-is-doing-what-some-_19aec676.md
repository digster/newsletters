Good Morning Ishan,
As much as I try not to expose my 16-month-old to screens, it’s inevitable. She peeks into my phone as I shoot out a message or even tries to swipe a blank screen.
Founders of the 10-year-old robotic-toys company, Miko, believe they have a fix for this fixation–“If you can’t eliminate screens, why not make screen time educational?”
That’s how they came up with the Miko robot, a small tablet strapped to a pair of wheels with googly, cartoon eyes that obeys a five-year-old’s every command.
The made-in-India robot can do math, make kids dance, teach them exercises, play hide-and-seek, and get them to bed by 10 p.m. Backed by the likes of Stride Ventures and Ivycap Ventures, Miko is currently valued at $550 million.
Starting at $199, it is cheaper than its US-based competitors, such as Moxie, Jibo, Kuri, and Anki’s Cozmo, which are all priced upwards of $500 and are now no longer functional.
In the $8 billion social-robots market, these US-based companies were haemorrhaging money.
So, what is keeping the Indian Miko alive, and will it dodge the robotic-toys curse that its US counterparts seem to have faced?

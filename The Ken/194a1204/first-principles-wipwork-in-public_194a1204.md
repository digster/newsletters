“If it is not on a public Slack channel, then does it even exist?”
This is an inside joke at The Ken. A joke on me.
It’s because I am quite militant when it comes to my colleagues preferring to discuss matters related to their work via private channels or direct messages. Our thriving, open, fun, collaborative culture took many steps back during the two years of the pandemic when we were largely remote. “DM culture”—colleagues preferring to communicate almost always through 1-1 private messages—took root
The thing with cultural changes is that it’s hard to observe the effects in real time, good or bad. They play out over years.
It was during a conversation with Harshil Mathur, the co-founder and CEO of Razorpay, for my First Principles podcast that I heard a strong articulation against it.
“One of the core tenets of our culture is transparency. That we focus a lot on creating a transparent environment where people inside the company have a very clear view of what’s happening and where we’re going, and it comes out in various ways. Most of our discussions will happen on slack public channels and not on private channels. We have a strong discouragement of DM’s policy. Don’t do direct messages, use public channels as much as possible, to something as simple as that there’ll be no cabins in the office.
So there are no cabins in the office, even more interesting there are no curtains in the office so like if you look at Razorpay office, no room in Razorpay has frosting, like, all glasses are transparent and stuff like that.
These are small things but it adds up together to create a culture of transparency that and the reason we have transparency as a core focus value is that we believed that a lot of innovation happens from the ground. Like, innovation happens bottom up and that innovation cannot happen till you have information asymmetry. If people don’t have visibility, if my sales guy doesn’t have visibility on how my product is developed, he can’t come up with an idea on “Okay, like, can we build a product this way and it will sell better,” right?
Information symmetry is important to have ground-up innovation and that is the reason we created a lot of focus on having a transparent culture so that is one aspect of the culture, there are a lot more but one of the things that we were very focused on is that we were very deliberate with our culture, we still are.”
Razorpay CEO Harshil Mathur talks about deliberate culture, building to a need, and the principles of product development, [Episode 6 of First Principles](https://the-ken.com/podcasts/first-principles/harshil-mathur-razorpay/)
One of the most common ethical conundrums that leaders, especially CEOs and founders, grapple with is how much to disincentivise certain kinds of behaviour. For instance, do you tell your colleagues and hope they listen? Do you call out instances? Do you wait for enough to come round? Or do you actively throttle it?
From Harshil, I learnt how and why Razorpay disincentivised private Slack communications. And I replicated it at The Ken over a few iterations. Anyone is free to create and use private channels or DMs, but we won’t save the messages beyond a few days. Conversely, all messages on public channels are saved for posterity, meaning you can always search and find things you’re looking for.
Harshil talked about transparency and information asymmetry as strong reasons to nudge people at Razorpay away from private communications. And I agree with him.
I have another reason: WIP. Yes, Work in Progress. But also, Work in Public.
Do you remember how teachers graded your exam responses as a kid? If you’re of a particular age or above, you probably remember some sort of binary grading. Where you either got the answer right, or you didn’t. Nothing else mattered.
Thankfully, many schools and teachers today grade children based on the intermediate steps they take in their pursuit of the solution to a question. If they’ve gotten most of the steps right but perhaps made a “silly mistake” near the end, the teacher is unlikely to award them a zero. Some teachers even grade the steps partially along the way, so children can realize what they got right, and where they went wrong.
This is an inherently better way for children to learn how to think and solve, and not merely to be right or wrong.
The “rough work” and the steps and the “margins” matter. Because it’s how you get to the solution.
“Puzzle questions” that often feature during college or job interviews have the same approach. No one really expects candidates to accurately guess[ “the weight of a plane](https://www.indeed.com/career-advice/interviewing/how-do-you-weigh-plane-without-scales)”. Instead, they want to know how candidates break down and solve the problem.
Why, even large language models understand the importance of “[chain of thought](https://openai.com/index/learning-to-reason-with-llms/)”.
Chain of Thought
Similar to how a human may think for a long time before responding to a difficult question, o1 uses a chain of thought when attempting to solve a problem. Through reinforcement learning, o1 learns to hone its chain of thought and refine the strategies it uses. It learns to recognize and correct its mistakes. It learns to break down tricky steps into simpler ones. It learns to try a different approach when the current one isn’t working. This process dramatically improves the model’s ability to reason. And yet, smart professionals in many organisations subconsciously retreat into the trap of binary outcomes. They work on ideas or plans in private, hoping that the final product will be successful. They don’t see value in exposing their “chain of thought” publicly, because what would be the value in that?
This is, sadly, a trap.
Working in public—sharing your chains of thoughts with your colleagues or managers or CEOs—strengthens your own ability to reason, iterate, and argue. It allows you to get feedback on the “steps”, allowing you to fine tune your work to improve your chances of success.
When you work in binary mode, you inhibit your own ability to grow. You atrophy your WIP muscles.
Conversely, when you talk, others listen. And when others listen, you find the confidence to talk better. It spurs your own evolution.

Just how fast should food be delivered? How fast can it be delivered?
If you ask India’s quick-commerce players, they’ll tell you—“very fast”. In 10 minutes. Maybe even less.
Both Swiggy (Snacc) and Zomato (Bistro) have launched dedicated 10-minute food-delivery apps over just the past few days. Zepto’s 10-minute food delivery service has been up and running for a couple of years now. It’s clear quick-commerce players see an opportunity in expanding into food.
Restaurants, though, are fuming. The NRAI has even brought up the bogie of taking the issue to India’s competition watchdog. Their peeve is that the present avatar of 10-minute food delivery is largely banking on cloud kitchens and dark stores. Why are aggregators competing with their restaurant partners? Is it even fair when aggregators have access to restaurants’ pricing and order data?
This week’s episode of Two by Two is a deep dive into the incentives driving 10-minute food deliveries, and the many implications for restaurants, aggregators, and customers.

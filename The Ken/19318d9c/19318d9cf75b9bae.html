Here in the US, where I live now, no matter what side of the political rift you’re on, there’s no denying that the 5 November Presidential election has been a momentous one. It’s been a tough week, and starting next week, Americans will have to contend with what’s next.

Donald Trump’s campaign, though largely quiet on issues of education, has made its preference for a freer market and more school choice options apparent. In a nutshell, this means giving families the financial option to send their children to private schools or be homeschooled—on the taxpayer’s dime. This is a death knell for the US’s vast public school system, which already labours under an acute lack of funds and resources, like full-time teachers.

American parents choose to homeschool or put their children in smaller private schools for a bunch of reasons: they may want to get their kids out of “woke” environments that promote gender and racial equality, or maybe their kids are being severely bullied and are having a hard time adjusting to a school. 

The US isn’t the only place with growing online school options. Even a quick Google search for such places in India will yield several results. There’s even an online shopping-like user interface where parents can customise what they like and at their preferred price. This is a screen grab from what looks like an online school marketplace:

The direction of this shift has always made me think about how edtech might play a role here. What can it specifically offer to the proponents of homeschooling that helps plug the gaps in home education? Turns out, the VC-industry has already sniffed out an opportunity.

In a January 2024 interview with Forbes, VC firm GSV’s co-founder and CEO Micheal Moore noted that edtech would very much be part of the movement away from public schools:

“Personally, I think the most exciting opportunity is in microschools, but homeschool, charter schools, low-cost private schools and virtual charter schools are all positioned well for this coming boom,” he says.

It could be a boom time for edtech

Homeschooling rates went drastically up during the pandemic because in some states in the US, school closures went on for longer than people expected. Now, schooling is mostly back to normal, but the idea that kids can learn at home, with the right tools, has taken a solid grip among some parents here. A recent analysis by the Washington Post indicated that homeschooling has surged 51% since 2018.

In India and China, the two other bellwether economies for edtech, a lot of the switch has been driven by after-school tuition support for students. If teachers can’t get it done in school, let an online tutor coach you instead. And the unending number of entrance exams fuel this need—no one school is going to prep you enough to get into an IIT. You’re going to need outside help.

In the US though, the after-school tuition market can’t pull the kind of numbers that it does in India and China. Schools in the country are largely supposed to provide all kinds of support—from extra coaching to mental health.

So it would make sense that edtech options for homeschooling are designed to meet the needs of a full day of learning. Companies like Outschool and Kaipod Learning have created a buffet of different options that parents can choose from to constitute this whole day. Kaipod, specifically, aims at turning independent educators into small business owners with a set of online tools:

Each pod is typically made up of 12 to 14 students and one educator. The pod covers a range of school subjects—from math to reading to science—with each student following their own self-paced and family-selected curriculum. Tuition prices vary by pod. 

[…]

If parents have not already settled on a homeschooling curriculum, KaiPod helps them construct one. “So they may choose an a la carte approach of curriculum—Khan Academy for Math or Lexia for reading or something else for science,’’ Kumar says. “And then they come to the Kai pod three, four or five days a week to do all the other things and have a safe place to work.”

Tech has increasingly become a core part of the homeschooling and podschooling culture, with little oversight on the quality of these learning options. In some ways, this is the ultimate triumph of the free market—parents can choose or reject online learning options depending on their quality. Let their wallets speak for themselves.

But there are problems with this plan—the most obvious one being regulation. These schools can be run with little to no actual teachers involved. Homeschools can be as simple as a few computers, a few tablets, and one minder without any specialised degrees. Good homeschool tech options should personalise the learning experience; bad ones will provide low-quality learning with little oversight. 

For edtech companies, the lack of regulation could initially lead to growth. There’s no need to hire qualified teachers for real-time instruction. They could stitch together existing material and provide a comprehensive-looking package to parents. But in the long run, there’s going to be some sorting out of students when they have to cross a barrier, like a standardised test or a college application. In those cases, the quality of the homeschool curriculum is going to matter.

So the shift towards school choice can be an opportunity for edtech companies to innovate, improve, and find ways to differentiate themselves in the market. Before there is talk of regulatory fetters, these companies have the runway to scale up. They should make the most of it—hopefully with the goal of students learning as their north star.

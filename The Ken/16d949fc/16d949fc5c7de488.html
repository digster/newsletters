Growing up in a reasonably religious family, I was told that if you pray for something, God would give it to you. An amazing proposition to a 5-year-old with a propensity for excess. Over time and multiple sessions of fervent prayer, it dawned on me that this system didn't have the 100% success rate that had been advertised. 
 
Outraged, I approached the purveyors of this fake news demanding answers. Their response was bullet-proof: "God works in mysterious ways".

Since then, I've stopped putting my faith in mysterious, omnipotent forces, choosing instead to look for meaning in the tangible. And, I must say, especially when it comes to the government, it often makes as little sense.

"The government works in mysterious ways" is my new mantra to explain the sometimes baffling developments we report on.

Today's story by Suraksha deals with just that.

In 2008, three PSUs which contributed the bulk of vaccines to the government's Universal Immunisation Programme (UIP) were shuttered for quality reasons. Private companies stepped in to fill the void. So far, so good.

But in 2012, the government decided it wanted to be self-sufficient. So it announced it would set up an Integrated Vaccine Complex (IVC) which would handle vaccine research, manufacturing, and supply three-quarters of the UIP's requirements—some 585 million doses of vaccines. And it would be ready in three years.

And to execute such ambitious plans, it outsourced the task to a PSU that specialised in making condoms. You can't make this up. To be fair, the PSU did have a vaccine unit that was tasked with this. Except, its only real claim to fame in the vaccine space was in procuring vaccines. In the late 90s.

Seven years and Rs 600 crore later, not one vaccine has been produced. It will be years before the IVC is fully operational. In the meantime, the project has seen cost overruns even as the private sector has continued to shoulder India's vaccine burden.

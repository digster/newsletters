Two Budget benefits buried under the buzz. And at least one of them should be of help to many
By Anand Kalyanaraman
6 min read
View in browser

Good Morning Ishan,

You are reading our subscriber-only newsletter Ka-Ching!—a weekly commentary on fintech and personal finance

Fair to say that Trump’s tantrums stole the thunder from Sitharaman’s schemes. Little else can explain the stock market’s behaviour despite the crowd-pleasing Budget speech last weekend. 

India’s equity-market bellwether, the Sensex, ended flat on Saturday and took a deep dive on Monday. It was as if the market brushed aside the Rs 1 lakh crore largesse handed out by the finance minister to middle-class India. And then, on Tuesday, the index took off like a rocket. Only to fall again on Wednesday.

This roller coaster was the outcome of the new US President’s words and deeds, and the tariff wars thus unleashed and almost immediately paused. Shock, relief, shock… A wild four years lie ahead. 

In any case, what Donald Trump did to Nirmala Sitharaman, the exempt-till-Rs-12-lakh change in the new tax regime did to everything else in the Budget. It completely overshadowed them. The big tax break was splashed all over the media, and with good reason—the middle-class has finally got some real relief after a long time.

But I won’t bore you with more on that. I would rather talk about two other changes that got buried under the headlines.

One should help many. The other will be redundant to most.

Home in, without strings

Taxpayers who own two houses now have it much, much easier. They can claim both houses as ‘self-occupied’ without the onerous strings that came attached earlier. 

‘Self-occupied’ for houses is exalted status in tax law. On such properties, the annual value—the assessable rental value under tax law—is considered nil. Ergo: there’s no tax to be paid. 

For the longest time, only one property could be claimed as self-occupied. Other houses, even if they weren’t let out, were deemed as let out and taxed accordingly. That hurt many. 

So, in 2019, the taxman extended the benefit to cover up to two houses. But claiming this break was easier said than done, thanks to tough terms and conditions. 

The Budget speech on Saturday acknowledged the knots and untied them.

“​​Presently tax-payers can claim the annual value of self-occupied properties as nil only on the fulfilment of certain conditions. Considering the difficulties faced by taxpayers, it is proposed to allow the benefit of two such self-occupied properties without any condition.”

The part in bold will be a huge relief for many.

Earlier, one of two conditions had to be met to claim ‘self-occupied’ status.

Either the house was occupied by the taxpayer as their residence. Or the owner was unable to occupy the house because they were living in a different city for employment, business, or profession. Not just that, in the other city, the taxpayer had to be living in a house not personally owned by them.

If you think this is convoluted, you are right. Tax laws are like that.

Thankfully, this year’s Budget has simplified this. Now, up to two properties can be claimed as self-occupied as long as they’re either occupied as the taxpayer’s residence, or the taxpayer can’t occupy it due to any reason. This opens the door for many to save a tidy amount of tax.

But note that a property can be claimed as self-occupied only if it is not rented out. If you rent out the house, you will need to pay tax. No escape hatch there.

Say you own a house in Mumbai which you occupy and another house in Bengaluru that’s vacant. You can claim both houses as self-occupied. But if the house in Bengaluru is let out, you can claim only the house in Mumbai as self-occupied. The rules are similar even if you have two houses in the same city.

To make things even easier, this benefit is available in both the new tax regime (with lower tax rates and nearly no deductions) and the old tax regime (with higher tax rates but many deductions). So no head-scratching or number-crunching needed on this. It helps folks all around.

The same cannot be said about the next change.

NPS Vatsalya tax-break

Last September, the government launched NPS Vatsalya, similar to the retirement-focused National Pension System (NPS) that’s been around for years. But Vatsalya is for parents who want to start investing early for their child’s retirement savings. That’s right—for their child’s retirement. The NPS Vatsalya account becomes a normal NPS account and is transferred to the child when they turn 18. 

It’s a fine product, considering the huge amounts people actually need at retirement. Starting early, equity investing for the really long term, a well-regulated low-cost product—the NPS Vatsalya ticks many boxes. Big compounding benefits lie on the horizon.

But it did not have a sweetener that many Indians look for—a tax break. 

NPS offers an exemption on contributions up to Rs 50,000 under Section 80CCD (1B). So the Budget last Saturday extended the benefit to NPS Vatsalya too.  

But to be frank, this won’t mean much to most income-tax payers and seems more like a box-ticking exercise. 

Deductions like Section 80CCD (1B) are allowed only in the old tax regime and not in the new one. And about three-fourth of taxpayers have already shifted to the new tax regime. This exodus is certain to pick up speed after the recent Budget, because the new tax regime now means lower tax than the old one for most folks. 

Simply put, this tax break is no longer relevant for most people who might want to put money in NPS Vatsalya. 

But products like the NPS and NPS Vatsalya, and other long-term investment products like PPF, continue to make sense and remain relevant for taxpayers looking to build a healthy nest egg. As do portfolio essentials like health insurance which don’t get tax breaks in the new tax regime either.

Investments and protections should be independent of tax breaks.

Hopefully, the new income-tax bill which is to be tabled this week will drive home this point again, loud and clear.

Good Morning Ishan,
The BDS movement—that’s “boycott, divestment, and sanctions”—has caught fire in some parts of Indonesia. Lists of companies are circulated on social media to convince consumers to avoid products from firms perceived to be affiliated with the Israeli state.
This has transformed some households’ shopping habits. And some major global brands, like Unilever, are in the crosshairs of pro-Palestine activists.
As it turns out, Unilever’s troubles in Indonesia aren’t limited to consumers switching to other products since October. In fact, its operating profit has been sliding for years. Three executives left their roles in the past weeks, including the president director.
People within Unilever Indonesia told us that their product strategy is broken, and the way decisions are made isn’t swift enough to keep up with larger trends and consumer preferences.
In today’s story by Michelle, we get a view of the way Unilever’s Indonesia arm is on the decline, with boycotts of its products being only the latest challenge for the company: [https://the-ken.com/story/unilever-took-a-blow-in-profits-an-israel-linked-boycott-threatens-knockout/](https://the-ken.com/story/unilever-took-a-blow-in-profits-an-israel-linked-boycott-threatens-knockout/?utm_source=daily&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter) (12-minute read)

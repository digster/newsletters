The year has just kicked off, and I’m sure many of you are taking the weekend to cast away the mental haze that descends on so many of us during the transition from December to January. Maybe you’re setting resolutions, maybe you’re doing some soul-searching. A version of that is unfolding right now among China’s AI developers. Some startups are rethinking how they’re positioning themselves because of a deal that closed in the last days of 2025.

Two billion dollars. Ten days, or thereabouts. Uncountable geopolitical and compliance tripwires. Those are the contours of Meta’s acquisition of Manus, a developer of a general-purpose AI agent that is based in Singapore and has Chinese roots. (More precisely, Meta acquired Manus’ parent company, Butterfly Effect.) Much of the commentary around this event frames the transaction as confirmation that AI developers in China are after Western (mostly US) capital and backing, but chatter within the country has a completely different tone.

For years, Chinese startups have had to make an important decision early on: stay at home and focus solely on the domestic market, or head overseas and abandon China altogether. Most of the time, a startup can’t do both, unless it develops a split personality like Bytedance, which can lead to all manner of headaches https://techcrunch.com/2025/12/30/heres-whats-you-should-know-about-the-us-tiktok-deal/ later on.

Think of it like this: moving out of China isn’t just about selecting certain markets over others. It’s an active choice to live on a different part of the internet, one that feels like hostile territory, at least at first…

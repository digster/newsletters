Good Morning Ishan,
There’s a serious exercise afoot at the national level to bring “equivalence” between all school boards—state and the CBSE—by 2026.
One Nation, One School Board at play here? Maybe.
But what is happening currently at the school boards otherwise is fascinating. With a surge in demand for international boards, legacy schools are picking up the IB tag in large numbers. India has 216 IB schools, a 31% increase since 2019.
In June, the IB board said it would approve 100 more schools in the country.
Despite a steep fee structure, parents can’t seem to have enough of these tiered programmes—pre-school and primary, middle years, and diploma/career related.
Since a full IB education can be prohibitively expensive, parents are choosing to mix and match boards. Schools are playing along with a ‘blended approach’ to save costs.
For instance, two years ago, a Pune-based student shifted from an ICSE school to an IB school in grade 6. Her fees soared to Rs 4.3 lakh (US$5,200) from Rs 1.2 lakh (US$1,400), says Alifiya in her widely reported story today.
Some parents see the IB education as a status symbol; others consider it a gateway to foreign universities. Some are just confused and end up with wrong expectations in a marks-obsessed society. School administrators admit that not all parents do it for the right reasons.
On its part, the IB board is trying to shed its high-profile image by tying up with state governments—from Delhi to Haryana to Andhra Pradesh.
And in Mumbai, one of the schools run by the Brihanmumbai Municipal Corporation now offers an IB curriculum. It has only 26 seats.
Schooling does interfere with one’s education; renowned American writer and humorist Mark Twain was perhaps an exception.

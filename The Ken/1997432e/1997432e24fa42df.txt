Zomato’s District doesn’t want to pay for the sins of others. What it takes to ease event attendees’ woes—at least at the venue

Of all the businesses Eternal is in, what’s most fascinating to me right now is its restaurant-reservation-and-ticketing app, District by Zomato. 

I don’t think any big surprises are in store in food delivery, quick commerce, or even its restaurant-supplies venture. The first is profitable and the other two aren’t. That isn’t changing anytime soon. Rising competition, to varying degrees, is a fact. But I don’t see much hand-wringing among investors over that. 

District, birthed after Eternal bought Paytm Insider a year ago, is the smallest and youngest of the lot. In the June quarter, District brought in roughly Rs 200 crore in revenue, which was not even a tenth of each of the other businesses. 

Unlike quick commerce, which Eternal took a shot at before it became a phenomenon, entertainment ticketing is a segment that has had an incumbent for years—Bookmyshow. It’s not that Bookmyshow has a huge top line to show for its dominance. In FY24, its revenue was a little over Rs 1,400 crore. Eternal’s was above Rs 12,000 crore. So why would the latter even bother with this business? Can ticketing ever be more than a sideshow to grocery and food delivery? 

I don’t have the answer to that, but how District intends to navigate the limitations of live experiences in India is slowly coming into view.

While District cannot solve problems like traffic and lack of parking that deter people from an evening out, [District CEO Rahul] Ganjoo says he wants the app to remove other kinds of “friction” and encourage people to go out. Google Maps may help a user plan their route to the nearest bar, gig, or movie theatre, but District should be their go-to app to see if there’s something worth going out for in the first place, he said.

District by Zomato is focused on acquiring new users, pilots in the ‘going out’ business, Mint https://www.livemint.com/companies/district-by-zomato-eternal-district-app-online-event-ticketing-india-quick-commerce-growth-11758203072988.html

“Friction” is code for all the ways attending an event in India can make your life miserable. Two years ago, comedian Trevor Noah’s fans miraculously got past Bengaluru’s gridlock to reach the venue, only to be greeted with a cancelled show because of poor acoustics. 

In January, my colleague Praveen wrote https://the-ken.com/the-nutgraf/going-out-of-india-is-easier-than-going-out-in-india/ about the awful state of live experiences in India after his visit to Melbourne to watch a Test match. 

If there’s one thing that the Boxing Day Test showed me, it’s how painfully behind India is in terms of stadium experiences, infrastructure, and simply… niceness. Toilets. Water. Gates. Ticketing. Security. Food. The list is endless. 

And no amount of money and beautifully designed apps can fix this. 

Live events are one of those things that force everyone, irrespective of class and privilege, to confront India’s cities with all of its glory. It requires people to step out of their gated communities and travel on India’s roads. It requires you to breathe the air outside. It requires you to go through the experience of booking tickets and using bathroom facilities. None of these can be bought. None of these can be escaped from. Nobody can get a live event delivered to your doorstep in 10 minutes using an app. 

Eternal, which runs India’s largest 10-minute-delivery company, knows this. It obviously witnessed the ire directed at Bookmyshow, which organised and sold tickets to the Noah show. “The auditorium is a glorified Amazon warehouse! One large hall with no elevation, no stadium seating,” said https://www.thenewsminute.com/karnataka/trevor-noah-show-bookmyshow-slammed-for-shoddy-arrangements-to-refund-tickets an attendee. 

As Ganjoo told Mint, there’s a lot about being part of a live event that District can do nothing about. And much of that has to do with what you have to endure before you get to the venue. But if the Noah debacle is any indication, District can surely ease your woes after you get there. 

But that isn’t possible if District is merely an organiser and ticketing platform. It needs to be more, as this story last week made clear. 

‘Going-out’ app District by Zomato has taken over the operations of Bengaluru venue Terraform. The 16-acre, 17,000-capacity arena was built and launched by Go-Golf Entertainment in November 2024.

Now known as the District Arena@Terraform, the al fresco venue was designed to provide event organisers a plug-and-play experience and boasts a “modular main stage equipped with a roof truss and IMAG extensions”—those would be the video projections that magnify the proceedings for those at the back—as well as “a dedicated artist village and backstage area”.

District by Zomato takes over Bengaluru venue Terraform, Music Ally https://musically.com/news/

In 2010, Live Nation, the world’s largest concert organiser, merged with Ticketmaster, a behemoth in ticketing, to create an ecosystem of one. 

Across the world, Live Nation had exclusive booking rights for or has an equity interest in 394 venues and 137 festivals in 2024, according to company filings. It promoted more than 54,000 live music and other events involving 11,000 artists. 

In North America, the DoJ says Live Nation controls more than 265 concert venues, including more than 60 of the top 100 US amphitheatres, and directly manages more than 400 musical artists. 

How Live Nation calls the tune for the live music industry, Financial Times https://www.ft.com/content/fdf2ba90-fc50-4c1f-a2c8-8efcec140980

The US Justice Department sued Live Nation last year, accusing it of being an illegal monopoly. Last week, the country’s antitrust body and seven states sued it for allowing scalpers to buy tickets they then resold at higher prices.  

Live Nation’s unsavoury practices aside, its playbook has lessons for anyone in the business of events in any large country. 

While live music is known for being a low-margin business, Live Nation has found ways to boost revenue by monetizing nearly every square inch of a venue.

The company says its highest-margin division is sponsorships and advertising, which creates branded experiences like an exclusive viewing platform for Verizon https://www.wsj.com/market-data/quotes/VZ customers.

It also has seen ancillary revenue rise to more than $40 a person in 2024. That includes spending on add-ons like VIP upgrades, portable phone chargers and beverages like Liquid Death https://www.wsj.com/video/series/the-economics-of/how-liquid-death-turned-plain-water-into-a-14-billion-beverage-brand/BE9CE03E-578D-4145-B62B-269B2916CCE1?mod=article_inline, which Live Nation invested in and made the exclusive water provider at many of its venues.

Live Nation dominates live music. Here are the ways it cashes in on concerts, The Wall Street Journal https://www.wsj.com/business/media/live-nation-business-model-music-industry-657d1ca6

India is nowhere close to a scenario where it has to worry about a Live Nation-like monopoly in India. If anything, ownership of venues is too scattered, and the standard operating procedure for large events is often laughably inadequate. Just ask the aforementioned attendee https://www.thenewsminute.com/karnataka/trevor-noah-show-bookmyshow-slammed-for-shoddy-arrangements-to-refund-tickets at the Noah show in Bengaluru. 

“Entry to the venue needed a QR code scan—all high-tech. One small issue—there was no mobile data access at the venue. No internet, no QR code.” 

Small wonder, then, that this is among District’s top priorities for Terraform. 

In a continued effort to innovate, the arena will soon feature technological enhancements such as high-speed internet connectivity and seamless mobile pre-order and top-up functions.

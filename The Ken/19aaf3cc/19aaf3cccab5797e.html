Dear Ishan, good morning! How alive are you feeling this Sunday morning? Me, I’m writing this on a Saturday as usual, with the smell of petrichor in the air. It rained briefly just after noon in Bengaluru. Many years ago, it was then that I first noticed, hey, November Rain!

I was listening to this French artist called Serge Gainsbourg (the album is Histoire de Melody Nelson), about whom I had no idea till an hour ago. I discovered him by scrolling to the very end of Apple Music’s “70’s Essential Albums” list. I think I’ve told you before that I like to “rebel” against algorithms every now and then. Currently, I’m doing my best to listen only to albums instead of playlists and compilations.

Tell me, wouldn’t you want to accidentally discover an artist with a bio as interesting as this?

“Serge Gainsbourg made his recording debut in 1958, but it was in the late ’60s that his brief affair with actress Brigitte Bardot parlayed Gainsbourg’s jazz-inflected music to lushly arranged and erotic sound. Afterward, he became involved with Jane Birkin and they recorded the 1969 duet “Je T’Aime…Moi Non Plus,” a song complete with steamy lyrics and explicit heavy breathing. Although banned in many countries, it reached the top of the charts throughout Europe. Gainsbourg returned in 1971 with Histoire de Melody Nelson, a dark song cycle which signalled his increasing alienation from modern culture: drugs, disease, suicide, and misanthropy became thematic fixtures of his work.” ~ Jason Ankeny (Apple Music)

I’m planning to discover more such artists and their music by cutting through the forest undergrowth that is algorithmic recommendations. Seriously, have you tried to avoid them altogether?

Come to think of it, it’s been a while since we created a community playlist. So, let’s create a playlist composed of albums!

What I mean is that you have to suggest one album that you really love, but also your favourite song from that album. That way, we can create a playlist of songs that is also a starting point to jump off into great albums.

One more point–while we impose absolutely no guidelines (much less restrictions) on languages, genres, or decades, we will look for album suggestions that aren’t too popular or cliched. So, please, no The Life of a Showgirl.

We're putting together a collection of great albums. No compilations. No essentials. No best-ofs. No playlists.

1. Homeostasis

Does a startup feel anything? Does it know if it is alive? Flourishing? Or dying?

Of course it doesn’t. This is the very problem that one of the most important essays on the topic, by Y Combinator co-founder Paul Graham, seeks to highlight (we’ve briefly linked to it in the past).

Why do so few founders know whether they’re default alive or default dead? Mainly, I think, because they’re not used to asking that. It’s not a question that makes sense to ask early on, any more than it makes sense to ask a 3 year old how he plans to support himself. But as the company grows older, the question switches from meaningless to critical. That kind of switch often takes people by surprise.

Default Alive or Default Dead–Paul Graham (October 2015)

Since a company has no consciousness, it falls upon its stewards–founders, CEOs, boards–to safeguard and prolong its life.

Who does it for us humans? Homeostasis–the dynamic and self-regulating process that allows us to maintain a stable internal state even while our external environments may change.

While the force of homeostasis is pushing us toward routine, the force of change is constantly trying to upend it. As a result of these forces dancing around each other, our problems tend to walk the awkward gait of punctuated equilibrium: an effective solution appears to have settled down, then after a while, a change unmoors it and the understanding process repeats. The punctuated equilibrium pattern appears practically everywhere, indicating that this might be another general pattern that falls out of the underlying processes of mental modeling.

…

Every revolution of the process of understanding adds to our model, making us more capable of facing the next round of change. Sometimes this process is just refining the model. Sometimes it’s a transformational reorganization of it. This is how we learn.

Moreover, this might be how we are. This story of learning is such a part of our being that it is deeply embedded into culture and even has a name: the hero’s journey. The call to the adventure, the reluctance, the tribulations, and facing the demons to finally reveal the boon and bring it back to my people is a deeply emotional description of the process of understanding. And often, it has the wishful “happily ever after” bookend — because this would be the last change ever, right? It’s another paradox. It seems that we know full well that change is ever-present, yet we yearn for stability.

Change–by Dimitru Glazkov (April 2022)

Change–by Dimitru Glazkov (April 2022)

View in Browser

I’m currently watching Pluribus, the brilliantly creative new TV series from Vince Gilligan, the creator of Breaking Bad. Without giving away too much (which is given away in the very first episode anyway), it gives us glimpses of what humanity-wide homeostasis might look like.

In contrast, machines have no concept of homeostasis. That includes LLMs, which have been given “chat” and “personality” skins to make us think they are human-like.

First, LLMs are “free energy” thinkers. The force of gravity that pushes the ball in human thinking is the force of homeostasis: a resolute solicitor that drives us all to conserve energy and even find a surplus of it to ensure our own flourishing. We humans are “default-dead”: unless we abide by this force, we perish. Unlike us, LLMs have no such compulsion. In their realm, the energy is free. Thinking happens as a matter of “being run”. Put differently, their “thinking ball” is not rolling down the slope. Instead, it’s driven by an unknowable abundant force through what seems like the same sloped terrain as in human thinking, but is actually something  radically different.

Machine Thinking–by Dimitru Glazkov (January 2025)

Do the large language models running on data centres the size of Manhattan, consuming more power than entire countries, and sucking up rivers-worth of water understand that they are severely out of balance? No, of course they don’t.

But we do. Because we’re human. Because we’re educated.

Yes, because we’re educated.

Education is closely linked to our need for homeostasis, says Antonio Damasio, the neurobiologist who is known for positing that our consciousness itself arises from homeostasis.

Education provides, instils, and imparts specific knowledge. The next question is: what is education for? And this is the crux of the argument I will present here. From the perspective of biology, education provides the individual with behavioural strategies and with knowledge that allow the individual to harmonize with others; in other words, harmonize with society and with its structures, rules, and operations.

Invented by humans, the ultimate practical goals of education are very simple: reduce harm to the individual, enhance the individual’s welfare and, at the same time, reduce the destructive forces that threaten others and the environment—most of all, the social environment—and, overall, increase the wealth of society and its prosperity. So, what I am saying is that there is a necessary process of harmonization that has to do with the operation of those individuals that are in a specific universe, but the ultimate goals of any educative process are, in the end, the reduction of harm to the individual and the enhancement of the individual’s welfare and, of course, once that operates for the individual, we end up producing a result that extends to others in their surrounds.

The critical issues should be obvious. Whether in primary or secondary school, or at university level, and, needless to say, at home prior to school and after school, education is about allowing human beings to reach their highest potential for personal happiness and the promotion of the happiness of others. From a biological point of view, there could not be anything else but this. It is about the promotion of personal happiness and the promotion of the happiness of others. Of course, we are using the term ‘happiness’ in a rather general way, because happiness, in this sense, really translates into better function, better operativity of the individual. On the other side of the equation, it should be clear that this entails also reducing harm to both self and others, and protecting the social fabric and the physical environment.

The new science of consciousness–by  Antonio Damasio (April 2025)

Damasio’s explanation of education as a process through which we help harmonise young adults with society, so that both can become better in the future, is a great one.

We’re in an age where one set of people questions the value of education, while another set wants to use LLMs and AI to reimagine it. Education and college, which we once took for granted, are now seen as either use-cases for AI or impediments.

Is it any surprise that AI and tech companies are at the forefront of either dismantling education or seeing it through the narrow lens of training future workers?

“College is broken,” one Palantir post said. “Admissions are based on flawed criteria. Meritocracy and excellence are no longer the pursuits of educational institutions,” it said. The fellowship offered a path for high-school students to work full time at the company.

After deciding to apply, Zanini found out he got the fellowship at around the same time he learned of his admission to Brown University. Brown wouldn’t allow him to defer and he had also landed a full-ride scholarship through the Department of Defense.

“No one said to do the fellowship,” said Zanini, who turned 18 in September. “All of my friends, my teachers, my college counselor, it was a unanimous no.” His parents left the decision to him, and he decided to go with Palantir.

Zanini is one of more than 500 high-school graduates who applied for Palantir’s “Meritocracy Fellowship”—an experiment launched under Palantir CEO Alex Karp’s thesis that existing American universities are no longer reliable or necessary for training good workers.

…

The inaugural class of 22 Palantir fellows wraps up in November. If they’ve done well in the four-month program, they’ll have the chance to work full time at Palantir, sans college degree.

Palantir Thinks College Might Be a Waste. So It’s Hiring High-School Grads–by Angel Au-Yeung (WSJ)

The word “Fellow” used to mean a person who was elected to become a member of a professional organisation because of their work or achievements. It was a mark of honour. Of education.

What does it say when Palantir (and Peter Thiel in 2010) question and discard the need for education, but cynically retain the appearance of it through the word “Fellow”?

2. On rereading

Hello Ishan, it’s Tanim with your books section for this week. 

What’s the last book you reread that you would like to recommend to the community? You can share it here or write to us at fp@the-ken.com. 

For me, Harry Potter is probably my most-reread series. Back in school, I’d revisit it out of necessity–we could only borrow three books for the summer. But after demonetisation, my bookshelf was born anew. I will leave the reasons for that to your imagination.

The excitement of rereading differs from that of reading for the first time. It stems from a process of accretion: knowledge of what is to come changes speculation about outcomes to speculation about meanings—a deeper form of excitement.

As Patricia Meyer Spacks elaborates in her book, On Rereading (quoted above), foreknowledge truly changes the way you experience a book. You might discover a subtle trail of breadcrumbs the author left behind, or it could just be confirmation bias. 

When I was young, my rereading was rushed, focused solely on plot. For example, I would actively try to avoid Sirius’ death every time I reread Order of the Phoenix because it hurt my little heart.  Now I have graduated from being plot-oriented to focusing on descriptions–the very parts I used to skip in high school. 

That is, if I reread a book now, I focus on how the characters are framed, how they are dressed, and how their environment is designed. It is much like contextualisation; which often gets sidelined as mere background. But without the supporting cast, there’s no glamour and action. In fact, in Greek drama, action itself was born from choral singing, which primarily provides context. 

The last books I reread were Pride and Prejudice, Oroonoko, and The God of Small Things. I am not sure which was the very last. 

But I do remember that I had a particularly hard time with The God of Small Things on my first attempt (when I was 12 years old), so much so that I sold my copy–a decision I now deeply regret, as it was an old edition. Imagine my disappointment when I realised it was part of my undergraduate syllabus. Fortunately, this forced me to give it another chance, and I was surprised I had ever disliked it enough to let it go. The wonders of passing time, huh? 

Incidentally, these three books, written in three different timelines, trace a fascinating arc in English writing. Oroonoko (published in 1688) is packed with action verbs, as Aphra Behn was experimenting with her style. Pride and Prejudice (published in 1813) is full of drama and colour as novels became a thing. And The God of Small Things (published in 1997) transcends all traditions of novel-writing, as we consume the book like a river of words flowing through our senses while we try to stitch together the experiences of the two siblings. 

Action is so decentred in Roy’s novel that I have no clear memory of the plot sequence, unlike the first two books. When I think of it, images come to mind: pickles, Kathakali dancers, an old bungalow, the boat. On my first complete read, these were primary colours with rough edges. On the second, the edges softened, the colours became complex, and it became one beautiful, sad palette. 

Understanding the big picture is just one of the many perks of rereading. But beyond that, as we grow older, it creates a space for our thinking to evolve, as Victor Brombert puts it:

Rereading is subject to fortuitous circumstances, and remains a strictly personal affair. But the act of rereading, especially of books that have had a transformative effect, illustrates a wider common experience: the continuous shuttle, or to-­and-­fro movement, between art forms and lived life. It is a creative weaving, a process by which we are ceaselessly shaped. 

When I first thought about rereading, it never occurred to me to think beyond books but this observation led me to another thought.

The divide between art and lived life is blurrier than ever. We are constantly performing in our daily lives; we are “social actors”, as Erving Goffman put forth in his book, The Presentation of Self in Everyday Life. 

So, just as we reread books to understand them a little better, sometimes we should slow down and reread our own experiences, allowing ourselves the space to grow.

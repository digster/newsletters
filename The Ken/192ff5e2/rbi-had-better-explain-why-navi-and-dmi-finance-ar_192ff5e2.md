Good Morning Ishan,
Do regulatory guidelines—especially those set by the Reserve Bank of India—need to be specific, equitable, and reasonable to ensure clarity, acceptance, and compliance?
The answer to that question is central to the argument Ateesh Tankha and Ganga Narayan Rath lay out in their piece today.
Last month, the apex bank barred four non-banks—Navi Finserv, DMI Finance, Asirvad Micro Finance, and Arohan Financial Services—from handing out new loans, thanks to “material supervisory concerns”. Shaktikanta Das, governor of the central bank, added for good measure that these four and other shadow banks charge “usurious” interest rates and offer sales incentives that are “purely target-driven”.
But is it really usury if you’re lending to a section of the population with poor or no credit history by levying an interest rate that is high enough to sustain your business? Commercial banks would do the same.
The frenetic growth of non-banks in the past couple of years has pushed up bad loans. Even so, if over a tenth of the entire micro-finance loan book were to be written off, it would still be less than the relief provided to the bankrupt telco Reliance Communications.

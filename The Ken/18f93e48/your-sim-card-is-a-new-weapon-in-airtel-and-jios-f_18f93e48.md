Good Morning Ishan,
For Bharti Airtel’s fintech arms, business is humming. Airtel Payments Bank is already profitable and most of Airtel Finance’s revenue goes straight to the bottom line.
These two providers have to hit high bars. There’s limited space for growth in Airtel’s bread-and-butter telecommunications services, especially at a time when rival Jio has managed to one-up the company for two years in a row. So, financial services are now a new front for the duopoly.
Airtel has cracked the code. The members of its existing SIM card distribution network—kirana owners and other retailers—took on double duty to function as business correspondents across India. Customers’ mobile numbers became bank account numbers. People who paid Bharti Airtel to stay connected with each other ended up becoming clients who paid Airtel Payments Bank to transact with each other, too.
Similarly, SIM cards are an essential part of Airtel Finance’s success. As a telco, Airtel naturally has detailed knowledge of its customers’ habits and behaviours. Even though RBI has clear rules about how that information needs to be siloed, it’s still “difficult to compete with a telco’s data,” said one Axis Bank executive to Rounak, who dived deep into how Airtel managed to build successful fintech businesses.
Check out Rounak’s story [here](https://the-ken.com/story/your-sim-card-is-a-new-weapon-in-airtel-and-jios-fintech-war/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter).
A woman in ripped jeans steps through debris that looks like it was left behind by a tornado. She picks up what still passes for a guitar—there are holes punched through its body, and layers of wood are peeling off. She takes a seat. A tablet rests on an easel, showing sheet music. She tunes the instrument; one string has snapped. Humming, she strums chords. A tagline appears on the screen: Creativity cannot be [crushed](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=uvGX9zqoMSw).
That is Samsung’s latest ad. The company is ribbing Apple’s iPad Pro ad that ran in early May, where musical instruments, art supplies, camera gear, an arcade machine, emoji stress balls, and other objects and materials associated with creative expression were crushed under the unstoppable force of a hydraulic press, set to Sonny and Cher’s All I Ever Need is You.
This manifestation of the Apple-Samsung matchup is fun. Altogether, the two ads last just under two minutes. But there’s enough cheek packed into the riposte that there’s a cultural conversation even for anyone who isn’t in the market to buy yet another device.
Sure, we’re under no illusion that both brands benefit from this type of jousting (or posturing). But it’s also nice to have an entertaining moment articulated in charming visual language, a playfully smart idea executed at the right time to grab our attention. Sometimes, tech companies shape our collective cultural experience too.

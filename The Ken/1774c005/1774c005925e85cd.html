Good Morning Ishan,
 
Sell shovels in a gold rush, goes the old adage. That's the fortunate position pharma companies found themselves in over the past year, as a once-in-a-generation pandemic swept the world, killing over two million.

Even as other industries suffered from the shock of the pandemic, with countries going into lockdowns of varying severity, pharma companies were primed to profit. After all, when an infamous, now-deposed tangerine could suggest bleach as a potential treatment, how hard could it be to sell actual pharmaceutical drugs to a desperate public and a medical fraternity struggling to combat an enigmatic virus?

And sell drugs they did. Over the past year, pharma companies proffered all manner of drugs as potential treatments for Covid-19. From the anti-malarial drug hydroxychloroquine, to Remdesivir, a failed treatment for Ebola which was resurrected in the hopes that it had finally found a virus it could beat. Neither, it should be noted, proved particularly effective against the virus, just like the vast majority of drugs that were dusted off and repurposed for the fight against Covid.

Even if they didn't help patients, they did help line the pockets of their manufacturers. But did they help pharma companies soar the way one would imagine? Not quite. While the pharma sector has enjoyed a bull run over the past year, Covid-related drugs, as it turns out, only accounted for a small share of their overall sales. And their overall sales? Put simply, they weren't so hot due to a fall in non-Covid drug sales.

Take Gujarat-based Zydus Cadila, for instance. One of India's largest pharma companies, it saw its sales growth plummet from 12.67% in 2019, to just shy of 4% in 2020. This, despite selling hundreds of crores worth of Remdesivir.

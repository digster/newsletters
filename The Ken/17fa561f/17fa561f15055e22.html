Good Morning Ishan,
 
I recently finished reading Daniel Kahneman’s Thinking, Fast and Slow, which talks about the dichotomy between two modes of thought all of us have inside us. It was a great read. The thing that stayed with me the most, though, came from the very first page, where Kahneman mentions a setting in which his book may benefit the reader most—the “proverbial office water-cooler”.
 
Thanks to the pandemic, we’ve had precious little of that in the last two years. Working remotely has meant the reduction of our work conversations to a baser, transactional version of themselves.
 
I’ve had moments where I came across information that looked like it was important, but I couldn’t exactly put my finger on the why or the how. In meeting people at the office over the last few weeks, talking about new ideas, listening to their experiences, things clicked. As Kahneman says in his book: “Questioning what we believe and want is difficult at the best of times, and especially difficult when we most need to do it, but we can benefit from the informed opinions of others”.
 
This is also what we aim to achieve at The Ken—bringing you in-depth, deeply reported stories that will not only enrich your next conversation but also inform your next big decision.
 
If you are going back to the office (or not) and looking to connect with new (or even old) colleagues again, this week's stories are a great place to start.
 
On Monday, Arundhati wrote about the wave of employee stock option plan (ESOP) buybacks that has taken over the Indian startup ecosystem lately. Previously a privilege reserved by profitable companies for their employees, ESOP buybacks are now all the rage. This has helped change the perception of ESOPs being just paper money, but the populist approach might also come with its fair share of pitfalls.
 
On Tuesday, in our free story of the week, it was yours truly looking at India’s apex body on technical education, AICTE. The body was expected to promote the quality of colleges across the country, but tight control and excessive red tape have stunted their growth. The government is working on a draft bill for a new regulatory body, but for technical institutions, it’ll just mean one invigilator in place of another.
 
Jaspreet had the perfect read to get over the hump day—a deeply-reported story about seven-year-old banking tech firm Zeta. The company landed a digital transformation contract with India’s biggest private bank HDFC Bank. The project has opened many doors for Zeta, including that of investors’. Success could be the proof-of-concept for the company’s ambitions, but failure could hobble its flight.
 
Pratap closed the holiday week with a story on Reliance’s startup accelerator JioGenNext. Over 170 startups passed through their gates, but less than 20% ended up working with the conglomerate. The hit-and-miss track record is now raising questions about the programme’s future.
 
On the newsletters front this week, Jaideep wrote about why Indian football fans have been seeing actor Ranveer Singh all over their Twitter timeline lately; Olina wrote why UGC’s “expert” moves have left PhDs baffled; Seetha wrote about what it really means to be a D2C brand.
 
That’s where I leave you today, with some fantastic stories that hopefully lead you to even better conversations. As for me, my next book is calling me.
 
Happy reading!

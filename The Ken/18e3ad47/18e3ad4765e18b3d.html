Does it really matter to you if your credit card is on Visa or Mastercard or any of the other card networks?

The Reserve Bank of India thinks it does. Going by its 6 March circular, it ostensibly wants to give credit-card holders the right to choose their card network.

But the idea here is to level the playing field for Rupay, the homegrown challenger to Visa and Mastercard, which control 90% of the market. Rupay doesn’t have international acceptance and has a high transaction-failure rate. Naturally, banks don’t seem to have much use for Rupay.

But, as Rounak argues in his story today, the RBI’s solution strikes at the heart of banks’ relationship with card networks.

Roughly 15% of the credit cards in circulation are co-branded cards. These cards, issued by e-commerce platforms, airlines, and others in partnership with lenders, are exclusive to one card network. That will no longer be true. With a lot of the benefits these cards offer being a function of exclusivity, the networks will now have to rework their pitch.

Our second story of the day, by Taro, is about a country that has always punched above its weight. Singapore, despite being a tiny city-state, has played a crucial role in the rise of Southeast Asia’s startup ecosystem.

But its shallow talent pool and high costs are forcing its young entrepreneurs to relocate to Vietnam, Indonesia, Thailand, and the Philippines. In 2021, eleven startups in Singapore managed to get valued at US$1 billion or more. In 2023, none did.

What makes this trend interesting is that Singapore seems to be engineering it, at least partially, in its search for seasoned entrepreneurs and investors. And some high-profile names, including Bytedance founder Zhang Yiming and Neil Shen, founding and managing partner of venture-capital firm Hongshan (formerly Sequoia China), have relocated to Singapore.

Nuclear energy is once again desirable, thanks partly to AI

Pricey, prone to delays, and leaves toxic waste—there’s no shortage of reasons for most countries to shun nuclear-power plants.

But many are changing their minds. Or, instead, they are being forced to. It’s becoming increasingly clear that relying on just solar and wind energy is not going to be enough to meet net-zero targets. India wants to triple its nuclear-power capacity by 2031-32. And it’s not just renewables that are eligible for billions of dollars in tax credits in the US—even nuclear power, a carbon-free source, is expected to get a leg up.

It certainly helps that there are new technologies such as cheaper and less risky small modular reactors, which have made atomic energy appealing once again.

The other big factor in its imminent resurgence is artificial intelligence (AI). More specifically, data centres built to handle AI workloads. An AI data centre uses 2.5X the electricity as a regular one. By one estimate, AI data centres will consume as much power as the Netherlands in three years.

Unlike solar or wind energy, the supply of nuclear power can be uninterrupted, which is a must for data centres. So it’s hardly surprising that Amazon Web Services, the largest cloud provider, is reportedly paying US$650 million for a data-centre campus next to a nuclear-power facility in Pennsylvania. Microsoft, Amazon’s closest rival, posted a job opening last year for a nuclear-energy expert to work on an energy strategy for its data centres.

By now, we’re primed for a steady stream of commentary on how the rise of AI will decimate some industry or the other. But nuclear power’s prospects go against the grain.

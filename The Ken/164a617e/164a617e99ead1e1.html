Looking at Indian telecom, one would think employing the Complexity Theory would explain some of the weirder things that happen in the sector, from time to time. To no avail, though. Far from evolving into a natural state between chaos and order, the complexity is mostly chaos.
 
Our story today looks at one component of this complex system, one which was meant to expand the market and help telcos make additional revenue by reselling. Even give them some competition in niche services, like serving a hospital or a large corporation. That’s how Mobile Virtual Network Operators  (MVNOs) have worked in some of the developed markets.
 
Since April 2017, the telecom regulator has been issuing MVNO licenses after India became open to consumer-facing MVNO businesses for the first time. It was “seen as a natural progression towards enhancing free market principles and contributing to the efficient use of existing telecommunication infrastructure..."
 
But was it a case of too late, too much?
 
As many as 61 licenses have been issued; only one company made a sincere start, and it tanked. None of the major telcos is keen to enter into an MVNO agreement. Yet the regulator is doling out more licenses. Does India even need MVNOs now? In the hyper-competitive telecom business, can MVNOs build a viable model?
 
Perhaps the fresh lease of life that VoIP got in June this year could infuse some life in MVNOs. In his debut story, Salman SH, our newest writer, tries to answer some of these questions. Can you see an order emerging out of chaos? Read his story and figure; it's free: https://the-ken.com/story/mvno-india-trai/

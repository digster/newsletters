Good Morning Ishan,
 
As of May, Indians had around 1 billion credit and debit cards. Using those, they made nearly 200 million online transactions each month. And over half of those were “guest checkouts”, meaning the customer has neither registered nor stored their card details with a merchant while making a purchase. They just enter it as part of the purchase flow. We do it all the time.
 
But, if India’s central bank had its wishes, all of those transactions might’ve fallen off a cliff. Because the RBI has been insisting that no merchant has the right to store a user’s credit card details on their systems. Instead, they have to “tokenise” the card details with a user’s consent and only store the encrypted results.
 
But here’s the thing—the reason most users rely on a guest checkout is because they don’t want to store their card details with many merchants, tokenised or not.
 
So if the RBI had not granted a last-minute reprieve to everyone by deferring the deadline for mandatory tokenisation from 1 July to 1 October, guest transactions would have plunged into a black hole. In case of refunds or disputes, merchants and banks would not know who to send back money to because they don’t have the details.
 
Guest checkouts are just the giant tip of the payments iceberg. No one knows how monthly instalments and recurring transactions would perform under a tokenised scenario. And in case you forgot, Indian businesses are still reeling from last October’s RBI mandate around recurring card-based payments. As recently as last month, such transactions were experiencing failure rates of anywhere between 30% to 75%.
 
Amidst all of this, a senior banker made these two observations to Jaspreet Kalra, the writer of our story today.
 
- “Customers don’t get why this change is happening so it hasn’t been a priority item for them”
- “If customers face refund issues, they won’t return to the same merchant”
 
A hugely disruptive regulation being introduced to help customers will thus, in the short run, ensure that the brunt is borne by many hapless online merchants. Because after many years of regulatory laissez-faire—letting society and markets evolve with minimal interference—India’s financial services and payments space has now firmly swung towards the other extreme. Anything that moves will be considered suspect, and will be aggressively regulated.
 
I think we need to ask our regulators—what are the principles through which they guide their regulation? How many waves of disruption can a business, merchant or economy take before it starts eating away at trust built over decades? While asking myself these questions, I came upon a few global regulators who attempt to define this.
 
Principles of good regulation—UK’s Financial Conduct Authority (https://www.fca.org.uk/about/handbook/principles-good-regulation)
 
Proportionality: We must ensure that any burden or restriction that we impose on a person, firm or activity is proportionate to the benefits we expect as a result. To judge this, we take into account the costs to firms and consumers.
 
Consumer responsibility: Consumers should take responsibility for their decisions.

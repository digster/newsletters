Thus spake India to the telecom standards body. Not once, not twice, not ten times. But for years.

Let us in, it said. We’re the world’s second largest telecom market. Our geography, population and industry structure is nothing like any other country. We have some ideas to improve how mobile networks are run!

Gosh no, exclaimed members of the club. This is the big boys league. Have you ever gotten a single standard or patent approved with us earlier?

Well, no, replied India.

We thought so, said the big vendors. Each of which sold billions of dollars of equipment to India. (Customers wanting to contribute standards? Gasp! What was the world coming to?!)

But there’s 5G coming up, replied India, determined to not give up. We’ve an idea on how to enhance the signal transmission range of a base station from 1.7 km to nearly 12 km. That will also help improve battery lives of smartphones. 

In February 2018, with the 5G standards window closing in, India finally cracked its entry. 

This is important. Because unlike most technologies where research happens first and standards are developed later, in telecoms it's the other way around. Research gets encoded into standards first, products and solutions are developed later. Put simply, telecoms standards define what direction future technologies will take. Participating in and influencing 5G standards, therefore, has become a massive techno-political exercise.

The traditional telecoms patent holders—Qualcomm, Ericsson, and Nokia—expect to earn nearly $20 billion in annual licensing revenue from 5G handsets alone. 

Which is why India’s one small step on standards is also its one giant leap. Read Seema’s story to understand the backstory, details and impact of this.

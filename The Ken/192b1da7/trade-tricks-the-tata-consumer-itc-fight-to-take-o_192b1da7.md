Trade Tricks
Tue, 22 Oct 24
A weekly newsletter about the biggest changes in commerce—focusing on shifts that matter to you..
Good Morning Ishan,
Over the weekend, I was on Blinkit ordering groceries, and when I searched for oil, the one brand I didn’t expect to pop up was Tata Consumer Products’ Simply Better.
To be sure, Tata Consumer added cold-pressed oils to its FMCG lineup over a year ago, and months before its acquisition of Organic India. But I hadn’t seen the products online or at supermarkets until a couple of days ago. This simple discovery led me to wonder how much of my pantry Tata Consumer has cornered in recent months, even if its investors are not exactly in the mood to be so reflective—the company’s stock fell by 7% on Monday after its September-quarter results.
The company behind the iconic Tata Salt and Tata Tea is not alone, though, in its desire to greet you from every shelf in your kitchen. There was a time when your biscuits were Britannia, spices were Everest or MDH, ghee was Amul, and pulses were unbranded.
Now, I wouldn’t be surprised if every one of those items came with the ITC label—not to mention wheat flour (which ITC dominates with its Aashirvaad brand), instant noodles, and chips.
Tata Consumer, born out of the merger of Tata Chemicals’ foods unit and Tata Global Beverages, is even more enthusiastic in widening its product range. Besides salt, tea, and coffee, it sells bottled water, honey, cereal (thanks to the purchase of Soulfull), and Indian-Chinese noodles, soups, and condiments (the result of another acquisition, Ching’s Secret). What’s more, Tata Consumer’s rivalry with ITC cuts across pulses, spices, and ready-to-eat foods.
This is a much bigger deal than it seems at first glance. Historically, FMCG companies settled for being top of mind in a handful of food categories—think tea and ice cream for Hindustan Unilever. It’s not that they didn’t sell other products; they just couldn’t expect to be a force to be reckoned with across the board.
This wasn’t timidity, the companies were merely being practical in a fractured market. But that has begun to change in recent years.
One possible explanation for the FMCG majors’ delayed interest in masala brands is that the latter were largely region-specific, which didn’t quite align with their pan-India approach. [Dabur CEO Mohit] Malhotra went so far as to say the spice business needed to be grown “state by state”.
Every FMCG product sells more in some parts of the country than the rest, but the general thinking is that it should have countrywide appeal.
But even while being restricted to certain pockets of the country, these spice brands’ size is anything but insignificant. The country’s six largest spice makers, with Everest Food Products topping the list, had combined sales of over[ US$1 billion](https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/industry/cons-products/fmcg/six-companies-add-1-billion-of-masala-to-topline-in-fy22/articleshow/97016310.cms?from=mdr) in FY22.
So it has become a no-brainer for large FMCG companies to gobble them up.
First ITC and Dabur, now Tata and Wipro want in on your masala box, [The Ken](https://the-ken.com/tradetricks/first-itc-and-dabur-now-tata-and-wipro-want-in-on-your-masala-box/)
Grains and pulses were among the only unbranded items in an urban consumer’s kitchen until recently. Even now, most people are just fine buying them without bothering about provenance—just [2%](https://images.assettype.com/bloombergquint/2022-06/778da6e1-5198-4c9c-b07f-469deb59a195/Motilal_Oswal_Tata_Consumer_Products_Annual_Report_Update.pdf) of the market for pulses is branded. But a growing number, with a high disposable income, not only wants a brand they are familiar with, they are even willing to pay a tidy premium for products made by shunning modern farming or manufacturing methods.
Tata Consumer’s organic split chickpeas, for instance, are priced 30% more than the variant grown with chemical fertilisers or pesticides. Its cold-pressed groundnut oil costs twice as much as rivals’ refined alternatives.
But unlike in the past, when FMCG companies would make perfunctory category extensions, Tata Consumer and ITC have wagered large sums on their “whole-pantry” strategy. ITC gobbled up spice-maker Sunrise Foods for Rs 2,150 crore (US$256 million) in 2020. At the start of this year, Tata Consumer paid Rs 7,000 crore (US$833 million) for the parent of Ching’s and Organic India. That’s almost half its sales in the year ended March.
If either company is successful, India will get its first truly diversified food behemoth, one whose reputation isn’t tied to only a couple of products. Much like what Hindustan Unilever has pulled off in both home and personal care.

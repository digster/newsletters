Good Morning Ishan,
I’m sure I’m not the only one addicted (that’s right) to watching sprint races in track and field competitions. My Youtube feed is embarrassingly dominated by the likes of Noah Lyles, Yohan Blake, and Sha’Carri Richardson, among others.
But have you noticed how unfazed these sprinters are even when a peer falls during a race? It’s like they’ve really got blinkers on.
Something similar unfolded in the Indian co-working sector. Unaffected by the Wework bust, the Indian counterparts are having a heyday, thanks to corporates ending the work-from-home regime. But that’s not why Gaurav chose to write about Awfis—a nine-year-old co-working company. What caught his attention was its unique business model.
The same model that led Awfis to post back-to-back profitable quarters, with its bottom line doubling in the June 2024 quarter. The very model also enabled Awfis to expand aggressively, with triple the number of centres across more cities than rivals Wework, Tablespace, and Smartworks.
But choosing to take a less obvious route comes at a cost, right? The same business model weighs down Awfis in three distinct ways. One of them is being considered a “second grade” co-working space, unable to attract the likes of American tech giant Microsoft—ones with the deepest pockets.
What is the business model, you ask? For that, you’ll have to read Gaurav’s [sharp story](https://the-ken.com/story/what-does-awfis-know-about-co-working-that-wework-doesnt/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter).

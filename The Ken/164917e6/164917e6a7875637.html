Amazon Web Services, or AWS, is easily the best-hidden technology giant in plain sight. If it were a standalone company, it would rank as the world’s fifth largest software company after Microsoft, IBM, Oracle and SAP. AWS’ topline in 2017 was $17.4 billion with operating profits of $4.3 billion.
 
And because it sits quietly within Amazon, like a nested Russian doll, very few people notice how powerful and dominant it is.
 
In India, AWS’ revenue is estimated to be $500 million out of a total cloud market spend of $1.4 billion. Its next competitor, Microsoft’s Azure, is roughly half that. 
 
But cracks may be starting to appear in AWS’ dominance. Though its overall revenues are still growing, its market share saw a slight decline for the first time in 2017. Led by startups who find its pricing too much to bear, a number of its customers are opting for other cloud providers. 
 
The deserters include unicorns like Ola, Hike and InMobi, to smaller startups like Clovia, Explara, Brown Apron, and Rappier. 
 
Though the revenues impacted may not be big at this stage, Arundhati, in her story, points out that India is strategically important for global cloud providers because about  30% of the world’s 11 million software developers exist here. As do the second largest number of Internet users.
 
Now the Indian cloud market is still growing healthily, which means a shrinking or stagnant market share can go hand-in-hand with growing revenues. But will that console an ambitious take-no-prisoners (sorry, I’m guilty of using that phrase once too often!) company like Amazon?

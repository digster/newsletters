Good Morning Ishan,

When the Indian stock market's 'Believe it or not' is written, the Adani Group stocks will find a place of pride in the list. The jaw-dropping rally in the six Adani listed stocks over the past year has propelled Gautam Adani to the position of the second richest Asian—only behind fellow Indian business magnate Mukesh Ambani.

Prominent among the outsized gainers is the group’s clean energy venture Adani Green Energy (AGEL). The stock has risen more than 10 times from its March 2020 lows, boasts the highest market cap in the Adani stable, and sports a price-to-earnings valuation of—believe it or not—925X. That’s many orders of magnitude of what Tata Power and the world’s largest clean power producer, NextEra Energy, trade at (about 30-35X).

Speaking of size, AGEL is India’s clean energy top dog,  a status bolstered by its recent acquisition of SoftBank’s clean energy unit in the country. Its portfolio of 24.3 GW is double that of its closest competitor Goldman Sachs-backed ReNew Power. The juggernaut rolls on, it would seem.

But, as always, there is a catch—many actually—as Seetharaman lays out in today’s power-packed story. 

Just about a fifth of AGEL’s portfolio is operational, much lower than ReNew’s 55%. Also, as much as 80% of the company’s pipeline capacity is not backed by power purchase agreements. That could come to bite, given regulatory risks and the poor financial position of state-run power discoms. 

More importantly, uncertainty brews around AGEL’s largest project—an 8 GW solar tender the company won last year at a tariff of Rs 2.92 ($0.04) per kWh. This project alone accounts for over 40% of its pipeline by capacity. Finding takers at that price, considerably higher than newer bids, is going to be a tough task.

And yet, the AGEL stock continues its heady trip. A peculiar shareholding pattern helps here. But can valuations be divorced from fundamentals forever?

Fri, 27 Feb 26
Multiple stories, multiple perspectives, one theme worth your time—every week.
Good Morning Ishan,
Bharti Airtel has decided that selling data is not enough. So now it wants to turn data into credit.
The company is infusing Rs 20,000 crore into its newly RBI-licensed non-banking financial company (NBFC), Airtel Money. It’s a number large enough to signal seriousness and small enough to still look prudent on a telco’s balance sheet. Is it diversification or is it escalation?
For years, telecom operators insisted they were in the connectivity business. Then they realised they were in the data business. Now they believe they are in the underwriting business. In hindsight, this move, which pits it directly against Jio Financial Services, feels almost inevitable.
Telcos like them know when you recharge, how regularly you pay, what device you use, whether you upgrade, where you live, how stable your usage is, and whether your postpaid bill ever bounces. It cannot read your messages, but it does not need to. Behaviour itself is a credit signal.
Until now, Airtel monetised that signal by building a lending service platform, partnering with banks, surfacing curated borrowers, collecting a fee, and letting others take the risk. It was elegant and capital-light.
But elegant, capital-light businesses eventually provoke a question: if you control distribution, data, and customer acquisition, why let someone else earn the interest spread? Rs 20,000 crore is Airtel’s answer. It no longer wants to be the matchmaker between borrowers and lenders.
But this shift did not happen overnight.
Over the past few years, Airtel’s lending service provider platform pushed thousands of crores worth of loans through partner balance sheets. In our story on how your SIM card [became a weapon](https://the-ken.com/story/your-sim-card-is-a-new-weapon-in-airtel-and-jios-fintech-war/?utm_source=newsletter&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=collection) in Airtel and Jio’s fintech war, we saw how telecom data—device type, recharge frequency, EMI behaviour, geography—was used to help lenders identify and service borrowers. The operation was lean and software-like in its margins, with minimal capital at risk.
Airtel is banking on its deep distribution network to do what many fintechs in India would give an arm and leg for
Payments offered a preview of the next step. In our reporting on Airtel Payments Bank’s push to become customers’ “[safe second account](https://the-ken.com/story/why-airtel-payments-bank-sees-its-no-1-future-tied-to-being-your-no-2/?utm_source=newsletter&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=collection),” we saw how the company tried to turn regulatory constraints into a feature. Recurring UPI autopay volumes rose, engagement deepened, and revenue per user improved. But payments banks cannot lend. Also, deposits are capped and margins are thin.
India's largest payments bank broke the payments-bank curse and is making a play to grab a share of UPI transactions from the big banks. But it should be careful what it wishes for
An NBFC licence changes the equation. It allows Airtel to convert behavioural data into interest income, to keep the upside and absorb the downside. That is precisely what makes public-market investors [nervous](https://www.livemint.com/market/stock-market-news/bharti-airtel-share-price-drops-4-on-nbfc-expansion-plans-biggest-intraday-fall-in-3-months-11771913653514.html).
Because lending is not telecom.
Telecom rewards aggression because scale quickly improves operating leverage, and even pricing mistakes, while painful, rarely threaten the survival of the business. Lending operates on a very different rhythm. It rewards caution, punishes overconfidence, and often allows growth to look perfectly healthy right up until the moment it isn’t.
Of course, every telco war has two sides.
When Reliance demerged Jio Financial Services (JFS), it did so with characteristic theatre. In a story, [we unpacked](https://the-ken.com/story/the-secret-to-jio-financials-13x-net-worth-boost-in-the-reliance-demerger/?utm_source=newsletter&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=collection) how treasury shares worth roughly $12 billion were shifted into JFS, boosting its capital base 13X while ring-fencing regulatory risk. One stone, many birds.
Showing off its financial-engineering chops, Reliance shifts a ~$12 billion investment to the fledgling financial-services business while also ring-fencing it from a looming regulatory threat
Jio began financial services not with underwriting muscle, but with financial engineering muscle. Then came the experiments.
In another narrative, we examined its Rs 36,000 crore [device-leasing proposal](https://the-ken.com/story/who-stands-to-gain-the-most-from-jio-financials-us4-3b-lease-deal-hint-not-jio-financial/?utm_source=newsletter&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=collection), a move that floated its telecom and retail siblings while handing Jio Financial operating leverage and inventory risk. Device-as-a-Service. Internal capital choreography.
To kickstart its business, the US$27-billion financial arm of Mukesh Ambani-led Reliance has devised a unique experiment that involves two of its siblings
And then the hiring spree. In a story about how Jio Financial is already the hottest new employer, we [saw a pattern](https://the-ken.com/story/freshly-minted-jio-financial-is-the-hottest-new-employer/?utm_source=newsletter&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=collection): ICICI-heavy leadership, risk-first executives, lending focus. A serious buildout. A second attempt after Jio Money’s stalled ambitions.
Jio started with capital muscle and financial engineering. Airtel is starting with data muscle and distribution discipline. Both are converging on the same conclusion: telecom is an extraordinarily efficient way to acquire financial customers.
The seduction is obvious. India’s formal credit penetration still leaves room to expand, and millions remain underserved or thin-filed. Telcos sit on a steady stream of behavioural data that traditional lenders cannot easily replicate.
But the markets have sensed some tension; the stock dipped after the announcement. Not because investors oppose growth, but because capital deployed into credit behaves very differently from capital deployed into spectrum. Towers depreciate, but loans default.
The question is no longer whether telecom companies can enter finance. They already have. The question is whether they can run disciplined lending operations through a full credit cycle.
With an investment of over US$14 billion, the company is expected to replicate the disruption Reliance brought to the telecom industry. And it's putting together a crack team

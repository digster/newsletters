Good Morning Ishan,

Both began life about a decade and a half ago in a nascent yet glittery market. Both got backing from marquee investors. Both shone and grew to around the same size in the first few years. And then, something changed. One took the elevator—the other continued running up the stairs. Cut to today, the faster one is the poster-boy of its market, while the runner-up is busy playing catch-up.

Aakriti’s fine Friday today spotlights Bluestone, a standout player in India’s fine-jewellery market. It’s doing fine, though not as well as its now much bigger and more famous peer, Caratlane. Playing second fiddle likely doesn’t sit well with IPO-bound Bluestone, even if it’s putting on its I-am-fine mask. (Fine, I’ll stop).

What really happened though? How did Caratlane manage revenue of over Rs 2,000 crore (US$240 million) in the year ended March 2023 with profit of over Rs 100 crore—while Bluestone was far behind with revenue of under Rs 800 crore and a loss of over Rs 150 crore? Like Shashi Kapoor’s iconic line “Mera paas maa hain” (I have mother on my side) in the 1975 Bollywood flick Deewar, Caratlane could well say, “Mere paas Titan hain” (I have Titan on my side). The Tata-group controlled jewellery major’s strategic investments and strategies dramatically changed the fortunes of Caratlane and its founder Mithun Sacheti. Interestingly, Bluestone, its founder Gaurav Singh Kushwaha, and its investors also stand to benefit big from what Titan did for Caratlane. To understand this intriguing dynamic among rivals and why Bluestone needs to run faster and assert itself soon, click here.

Sebi strikes, Sebi doesn’t

A year and a half ago, The Ken wrote about the corporate governance mess at India’s top power trader, PTC India, and its shadow bank subsidiary, PTC India Financial Services (PFS). The denouement in this long-running controversy in the two government-controlled companies finally arrived the day before yesterday. Market regulator Sebi imposed hefty fines and banned Rajib Kumar Mishra, chairman of PTC India, and Pawan Singh, former chief executive of PFS, from holding positions in listed companies. The regulator has struck—and struck hard.

But the same Sebi has dealt with kid gloves in another major corporate governance controversy—ICICI Bank’s proposal to delist ICICI Securities. The Ken wrote about this last year and also last month. Last week, Sebi issued an “administrative warning” to ICICI Bank over its “not appropriate” outreach to shareholders of ICICI Securities regarding the vote, cautioning it to be careful in the future. The regulator’s action seems grossly inadequate. ICICI Bank’s attempts to influence the vote have vitiated the entire voting process, but Sebi has let it off with a mere rap on the knuckles. The regulator should have ordered another vote, no less. Besides, The Ken has been asking why Sebi initially waived a key requirement that would have prevented the delisting process by a share swap. There has been radio silence on this.

Different strokes for different folks?

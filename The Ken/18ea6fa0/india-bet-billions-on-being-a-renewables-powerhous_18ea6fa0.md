Good Morning Ishan,
Let’s just say the massive flip-flops on solar manufacturing that you sometimes notice (or ignore) in the press are undermining India’s gains in the renewables installed capacity.
The twin goal to have hundreds of mega solar farms and thousands of jobs via solar manufacturing is based on an amateur strategy—throw things at the wall and see what sticks.
In the last two years, solar module imports have been rising despite a 40% customs duty. India imported modules and cells worth US$5 billion in 2023—2X the figure in the previous year.
But last week, after months of grumbling by domestic producers, India once again created barriers for solar-module imports. But there already are casualties.
In January, billionaire Sajjan Jindal-owned JSW Energy put its solar-panel plant on hold just when it was about to start building it. With module prices crashing 60% in a year, the management found the “velocity of this turbulence” unfathomable.
The neat US$2.9 billion in production-linked incentives isn’t assuaging the impact of this turbulence. Several things are at play here. Seetharaman connects all the dots and [makes a persuasive case](https://the-ken.com/story/india-bet-billions-on-being-a-renewables-powerhouse-the-world-had-other-plans/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter) that the world’s fifth largest economy can either add GWs of renewables or claim sufficiency (and jobs creation) in solar manufacturing. It can’t have both.
Salary arbitrage or the audit bake-off?
Auditors are feeling the financial pinch. In the last 24 hours, at least two Asian countries reported that audit firms are unhappy with the fees. Or the rate of growth of their fees.
In India, audit fees grew just 28% in the five years since 2018, when the market capitalisation grew 92.75% and profit after tax grew nearly 126% for Nifty 500 companies, reported a business daily.
Over in Japan, the auditing industry is being squeezed by a “decline in hourly fees” as businesses switch to less expensive accounting firms. Public companies have been shifting away from the Big Four—which have been increasing their fees—to small and mid-sized entities that charge lower fees.
Executives from some reputed audit firms argue India is among the lowest audit fee markets. But isn’t that true for most industries? (Except maybe for some well-funded startups, which distorted the market for a few years.)
Telcos argue the average revenue per user (Arpu) and price per GB of data are the lowest in India. Healthcare providers argue for the quality of service delivered, Indian healthcare costs are the lowest in the world. It’s the same for pharmaceutical products. The list is long.
As we [wrote](https://the-ken.com/story/no-one-wants-to-just-be-a-ca-ask-those-enrolled-in-cfa/?searchTerm=Charted+accountant&utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter) recently, a CA certification alone isn’t enough. Businesses want accounting++. Perhaps audit firms should look for their respective ++ instead of comparing audit fees from different markets.

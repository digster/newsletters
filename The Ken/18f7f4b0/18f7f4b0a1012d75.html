Good Morning Ishan,

Nearly 600 children in Bikaner and 300 in Jodhpur left their schools soon after the board exams this year. Dropout rates also spiked in cities like Fatehabad and Ambala in Haryana. These were “Techno schools” run by the coaching giant Sri Chaitanya.

The leadership is unfazed, though. Such blips are baked in the aggressive expansion strategy that the group has followed in northern India of late.

For three decades, Sri Chaitanya and Narayana—coaching-schooling giants from Andhra Pradesh and Telangana—have thrived on India’s fixation with competitive exams. The former’s network of 750 branches earned it a revenue of over Rs 5,000 crore (US$600 million) in the year ended March 2023, while the latter’s 700+ branches helped it rake in Rs 3,500 crore (US$420 million), according to the companies.

The two groups had been testing waters in the north for some years but the pandemic pushed them to go beyond the Vindhyas. Now, the newer markets pose unique challenges, forcing them to make a million tweaks.

One is treading carefully, but the other is playing with fire, write Devanshi and Aayush, in a deeply reported story:

The AI march and a college principal’s cry for a vision for students

How I wish I had a tiny LLM in my brain to process all the AI announcements from OpenAI and Google I/O in the last 48 hours. Google has made Android and its Pixel series sexy again. People watching this space say these new releases will change everything. Now Apple’s AI-powered iOS update next month is eagerly awaited. Smartphones are going to get way smarter.

In the midst of all this, reading St Stephen’s College Principal John Varghese’s Op-ed in The Hindu, “Young people fade away when there is no vision” was sobering.

Varghese is arguing to place students at the centre of all exams, admission rules, and education policies. Since the Common University Entrance Test (CUET) was introduced two years ago, the quality of students entering the undergrad and postgrad courses has deteriorated, he says.

Blaming it on the coaching centres won’t absolve anyone because these centres are businesses, run for profits. They are not in the business of improving students’ critical thinking ability or “social proprieties”. If CUET hasn’t solved the problem that insane cut-off marks (100% in some colleges for Delhi University admissions) created, then what more is left?

Varghese suggests introducing ‘personal interviews’ in the mix which can “help our young people from perishing”. It’s a good idea, but then it’d require training the interviewers, to keep their biases at home and screen students with sensitivity. Tall order, IMO.

I don’t like Pulse candy. There, I said it.
Now I understand that its success has taken the world of FMCG and management case studies by storm, but I don't get it. The strong, sour taste at the very end of the candy life cycle, exactly the part that most people seem to like, I find that part too much. A bit over the top, but only slightly better than the knowledge that Rajnigandha Pan Masala is a mouth freshner.
Over the years, I’ve thought deeply about other products too.
Soy milk: Tasteless white liquid
Green tea: Flavoured water
Lays Magic Masala: Air mixed with salt mixed with fried potato
Parle G biscuit: The taste of humble beginnings
ID filter coffee: Pretentious brown liquid in a packet
Haldirams aloo bhujia: Tiniest crunchy potatoes in the world that are far more addictive than murder
Any other candy from a grocery shop: Taste of compromise because the shop owner does not have change.
My idiosyncratic thoughts aside, food and food science is serious business. Look around you. Every product you consume has been thoroughly researched by companies who want to sell you their wares, and ensure that you are hooked to them. From toothpaste with charcoal to crisps to "ajwain" cookies to almond juice with spring water to "meetha paan"; at the heart of all these products is food science. And doing that are food scientists. People like Shuja Ahmed of Raw Pressery, the flagship brand of Mumbai-based Rakyan Beverages. Or VVS Mani of Bengaluru-based Unibic Foods.
Today’s story from my colleague Roshni is about them. And the science they practice.
Simply put, it is all about taste. “The quest to differentiate can go to naught if you overlook one tenet: the road to a consumer’s heart is not through the stomach,” says Roshni. “It’s through the tongue—even when you’re a brand that prioritises health over mere flavour. People may not want refined sugar, but they also don’t want their tongues invaded by chlorophyll and little else.”
If you’ve ever wondered what goes on behind the scenes in FMCG companies, engineering the food you eat, today’s piece is for you. Think flavours like masala. Or Peri Peri. Or just orange in a world where 20,000 flavours of orange exist; the world of food science is both complex and fascinating.
Do you have a favourite? What does it taste like?

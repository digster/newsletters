Imagine a restaurant telling its patrons: “We know you don’t always finish your food, so how about you only pay for what you eat? We’ll weigh the leftovers on your plate and knock a neat chunk off your bill.”

Sounds odd? 

Well, PVR Inox, India’s largest cinema chain, announced something in the same vein last week.

One can log in to the PVR app and select titles flashing ‘FLEXI’ to be able to leave the show at any point and claim a partial refund. Go outside, scan the ticket or QR code at a touchpoint to record the time of leaving, and claim a 30-60% refund. 

Do you want to leave movie mid-way? You can claim a partial refund at PVR Inox, Mint

Why exactly would PVR Inox do this? 

The explanation attributed to a PVR Inox executive in the news article is classic press-release boilerplate.

“Professional predicaments and family reasons can often lead to viewers having to leave a film incomplete. This will give them control over their movie-going experience and offer greater flexibility, especially to people who may otherwise not have planned to watch a particular film.”

How often have you made for the exit in a movie theatre for the reasons stated above? Let’s be honest, when it does happen, it’s almost always because the film is garbage. 

Customers not staying in their seats until the closing credits begin rolling is a nightmare for any cinema operator. One, they leave miffed that they paid a pretty penny for a movie they couldn’t sit through. Two, they kvetch on social media or tell their friends about their experience.

The July–August period wasn’t a bad one for PVR Inox, thanks to films like Stree 2 and Kalki 2898 AD. Occupancy rose sharply, but the growth was noticeable only because the company had had a couple of rough quarters running up to this point.

But what this chart hides is the fact that even in the best quarter here, the theatre operator couldn’t match the pre-Covid peak occupancy of 36%. PVR Inox knows how capricious this business is, and has been quite successful at squeezing more out of moviegoers. Food and beverages (F&B), which contribute a third to PVR Inox’s top line, outgrew ticket sales in FY24. I have written about how its F&B revenue puts it among India’s largest fast-food chains.

Granted, there’s no F&B without selling movie tickets for PVR Inox. Nobody is going to the cinemas—or even can—just to eat the middling food. But gone are the days when samosas, popcorn, and watery Coke were pretty much the only food choices available to moviegoers. PVR now sells everything from gelatos to bento boxes.

PVR Inox—the product of a merger between PVR and Inox Leisure—has even tried to persuade consumers that they are better off thinking about the number of movies they watch rather than how much they enjoy them. But, as we argued after its rollout a year ago, PVR Inox’s subscription plan is a product where “terms and conditions” take on a new meaning.

For one, subscribers must enrol for a minimum of three months. They can’t use the service on Fridays, Saturdays, and Sundays or book recliner seats. Premium formats such as Imax, Gold, and Insignia are excluded, and Passport isn’t valid for movie tickets priced above Rs 350 ($4.2).

In other words, the past couple of years has been a story of one hedge after another against the Friday blues that showbiz folks are all too familiar with. The opening weekend is usually a coin toss, and very rarely does any film get a second chance. 

This is terrible for a company that is answerable to investors every quarter. So PVR Inox, which has nearly 1,750 screens, is now using older films as a backstop to new ones. In September, the re-release of films such as the Hindi horror flick Tumbbad was responsible for 13% of PVR Inox’s admissions. 

While the tickets for such movies are priced at a 30–35% discount compared to those for new releases, the chance to juice nostalgia at a fraction of the cost of buying a new film is hard to pass up for PVR Inox.

With its pay-for-as-much-as-you-watch offer to customers, the company is offering yet more insurance against the possibility of being let down by the next big flick. But PVR doesn’t have too many arrows left in its quiver.

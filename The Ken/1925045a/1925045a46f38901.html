Sarvam AI is trying to speak your language—but it might still be learning how to talk.

If you’re not knee-deep in India’s generative AI scene, “Sarvam” might not ring a bell. It’s a one-year-old startup doing some interesting things. Interesting because it’s raised US$54 million—the largest haul for an Indian AI startup—has big backers, and yet remains something of a mystery.

Sarvam did generate (ha!) some hype last December with Openhathi, a bilingual model. Then, it went quiet. But last month, it returned with a fresh batch of products, this time for enterprises: translation tools, voice models, and even legal tech. Its voice model now handles speech-to-text and translation, and it’s tinkering with a smaller language model behind closed doors.

Exciting? Sure. But for a company flush with cash and people like Nandan Nilekani—Infosys co-founder and Aadhaar architect—in its corner, you’d expect a sharper pitch. Yet, as Abhirami reports, the industry feedback so far is…not so exciting.

Developers aren’t impressed with the new voice model. They say it’s glitchy, looping instead of answering. After all, it’s trained on just 100 hours of voice data—barely a week of Netflix, for a company trying to conquer Indian languages. Then, there’s the synthetic data. Sarvam’s using it to train its text models—cheaper, easier than real-world data. But experts warn that such models can implode.

Yet, for some reason, Sarvam’s ticking along. It’s even got big clients in Pine Labs and Appsforbharat—but many of its services aren’t even built on its own models yet.

That’s the intrigue: Sarvam has the buzz, the bucks, and the brains, but can its products even walk the talk? Abhirami tries to find the answers.

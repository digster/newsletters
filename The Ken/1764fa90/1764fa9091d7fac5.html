Good Morning Ishan,
 
Credit begets credit.
 
It's harder to borrow if you have never borrowed. Just a couple of weeks ago, I went to a state-owned bank to seek a loan for a piece of land I wanted to buy. And the branch manager wanted to know if my partner or I had any liabilities. We didn’t.
 
"How do you not have a loan? That's not usual for the salaried class," he responded. I did have a loan that I repaid a few years ago, and I have a credit card. He was instantly relieved; he could check my creditworthiness online.
 
However, I'm in a very small minority. Just over 3% of the 900 million Indians who use banking services have a credit card. So what do the other 97% of Indians do when they wants to buy a phone or a pair of shoes online but cannot—or do not want to—pay for it immediately?
 
They can turn to "Buy Now Pay Later" (BNPL) startups such as LazyPay and Simpl which don’t bother with credit scores. They front customers anywhere between Rs 2,000 ($27) and Rs 10,000 ($135), which has to be repaid in one go in a couple of weeks.
 
But there is little money in this for BNPL service providers. So, as Bhumika writes in today's story, they want customers to do two things: repay even small sums in equated monthly instalments (EMIs) and borrow more–up to Rs 1 lakh ($1,300). But they have to fend off growing competition from the likes of Amazon, Flipkart, and Paytm. And deal with questions over whether EMI-based lending is even BNPL.

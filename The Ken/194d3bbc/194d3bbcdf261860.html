ITC Hotels: 0, Indian Hotels: 1. India’s second-largest hotel chain is open for business on the bourses, but investors aren’t checking in

By Anand Kalyanaraman
5 min read
View in browser

Good Morning Ishan,

You are reading our Premium subscriber-only newsletter Long and Short—a weekly commentary on India’s capital markets

Last week, ITC Hotels finally made its stock-market debut, freshly carved out of cigarette-to-FMCG giant ITC Ltd.

With a loud crash. 

It was supposed to be a celebration—a coming-of-age moment for India’s second-largest hotel chain. Instead, the stock promptly faceplanted.

That was odd.

But first, some context: companies born out of a demerger and to be listed are first put through a special price-discovery session. On 6 January, ITC Hotels emerged from this with a “discovered price” of Rs 270 a share. But on 29 January, its big day on the bourses, the stock opened 30% lower at Rs 188. And over the six trading sessions since then, it has only fallen more. It’s down to Rs 164.

So, what happened?

It’s possible that passive funds sold their ITC Hotels holdings in panic for whatever price they got. Their hands might have been forced. Thanks to ITC’s demerger, ITC Hotels temporarily became part of key benchmark indices, but this would end three days after listing. Passive funds, which have to follow those indices, did what they had to do: hit the sell button.

Even so, the fall in ITC Hotels seems like an overreaction. Given what we know about the chain—next only to the Tata-group controlled Indian Hotels. In a nutshell, it’s doing great. 

The good thing about listed companies is that they have to disclose a lot. So, we know that ITC Hotels wants to raise loans of Rs 1,000 crore, likely for expansion. And the report issued by credit-rating agency Crisil on 6 January to facilitate this fund-raise gives it the best ratings and glowing references.

Some extracts.

“ITC Hotels demonstrated a strong performance in fiscal 2024 with revenue of Rs 3034 crore (Rs 2629 crore in fiscal 2023) and EBITDA margin of 33% (31% in fiscal 2023)

“Operating performance remains robust in 2025 with a 15% growth in revenues to Rs 1471 crore recorded during the first half of fiscal 2025”

“With growth in demand expected to outpace supply growth, the company is expected to sustain its healthy operating performance in fiscal 2025.”

“ITC Hotels will have a strong financial risk profile, with expected net worth exceeding Rs 10,000 crore and a debt free balance sheet post demerger.”

“Liquidity will also be supported by the transfer of cash and cash equivalents of Rs 1500 crore from ITC as part of the demerger.” 

“Strong business risk profile driven by established position in the hospitality industry, strong financial risk profile, healthy operating performance post pandemic along with healthy outlook for the industry”

“ITC Hotels remains a strategically important entity for ITC, reflected in 40% shareholding and board representation where strategic inputs will be provided….. ITC will be a strong anchor shareholder in any of the growth plans / strategies that ITC Hotels seeks to execute.”

All in all, ITC Hotels seems set for good times ahead. 

It wasn’t always like this. 

For the longest time, its sprawling hotels business was branded as a big drag on ITC and its stock. The segment, piddly in ITC’s grand scheme of things, was the market’s favourite whipping boy—after cigarettes, of course. Much blame was laid at its door for the prolonged going-nowhere of the ITC stock.

Until things changed radically.

Here’s what I wrote in March 2023.

Meanwhile, the hotels business was also criticised for being capital expenditure-heavy and low-return. “Conglomerates often suffer from conglomerate discount because management misallocates capital to weak segments,” said an analyst who does want to be named as they are not authorised to speak to the media. However, over the past few years, the company’s asset-right approach and reduction in capex in this segment has given investors some confidence. The post-Covid turnaround in the business has helped as well.

Once dragged down by cigarettes, ITC is now propped up by them, The Ken, March 2023

The last line proved prescient, and its outcome crucial. The post-pandemic boom in the hospitality business saw ITC’s hotel segment grow its margins and profits manifold to record levels in the years ended March 2023 and March 2024. This swing in fortunes played its part in the ITC stock breaking out of its inertia and doubling. 

It also set into motion the chain of events that culminated in the listing last week. It was something many investors had clamoured for to make ITC lean and capital-efficient. 

The time was right. The optics were good. 

Now, for every 10 shares they held in ITC, shareholders got one of ITC Hotels. The latter’s prospects seem bright.

Yet the stock falls. 

As it stands, ITC Hotels is the only Indian hospitality player that can hold a candle to the market leader, the Tata-group-controlled Indian Hotels. EIH, Lemontree, and Chalet are niche or much smaller players. 

Like its bigger peer, ITC Hotels, too, is adept at appealing to consumers whose purchasing power spans a spectrum.

A room at Ginger costs under Rs 4,000 a night on average. Staying at one of Taj’s palace-turned-hotels can set you back by over Rs 50,000. Two wildly divergent experiences. Not to mention a whole bunch of options in between. All offered by a single company. Whoever said you can’t be all things to all people?

Tata-owned Indian Hotels’ art of being all things to all people, The Ken, November 2024

It has hotels across formats—from ultra-luxury to budget—with 13,000 keys across 140 hotels in over 90 destinations (mostly in India and also in Sri Lanka). And like Indian Hotels, it has big expansion plans: to reach over 18,000 keys across 200 hotels over the next five years.

But on the bourses, the ITC Hotels stock is valued quite a few notches lower than Indian Hotels. The former’s market capitalisation is less than a third of the latter’s—even as its operating profit in 2024 was nearly half. 

On relative valuations, too, ITC Hotels with an EV/EBITDA of 34 times lags Indian Hotels at 43 times despite stronger operating profit growth and margins in 2024.

Is its smaller size the reason for ITC Hotels lagging? Not everyone thinks so.

Japanese research house Nomura had estimated that the ITC Hotels stock price could go up to Rs 300 after the demerger.

Maybe, ITC’s December 2024 quarter results on 6 February—that for the last time would include the hotel segment’s performance—will give the ITC Hotel stock the boost that it has missed so far. 

There should be room for growth. It just needs the market to check-in.

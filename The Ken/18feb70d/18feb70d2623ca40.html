Good Morning Ishan,

By next year, some 2.1 million Indian students are expected to go overseas, twice the number since 2019.

The Indian contingent makes for a substantive revenue stream for many foreign universities. Still, Australia is determined to cut down net migration by half by 2025 and is particularly training guns on immigrants who enter the Aussieland disguised as students.

On entering the country, some students either switch to cheaper universities or don’t attend classes, which the authorities inferred, led to poor retention rates.

Now that’s something study-abroad firms share the blame for.

“Two years ago, universities just wanted students. Now they want genuine students,” a study-abroad consultant told Alifiya, the author of today’s story.

In the firing line are some legit students and study-abroad startups. Particularly Leverage Edu, the company that has raised US$70 million over seven years and has “paused” its Australia business as of now. For a company largely dependent on one country, the UK, for its business, cracking another geography is crucial. Especially, since on 23 May, the UK government said it had taken several measures to ensure immigration routes aren’t abused.

How in all this Leverage Edu has become a victim of its own ambition is Alifiya’s story today. Check it out here.

Systemic to system

Change is in the air; most of us are feeling positive about what’s to come. My inbox was flooded with the World Environment Day releases and quotes as well as self-appointed celebs’ soundbites on the election results.

In that deluge, I spotted an email from Ashish Dhawan. For brevity’s sake, let’s just say he’s a super successful private equity guy who turned a philanthropist just in time to shake things up in the social impact space.

Dhawan’s email—with the subject line “Accelerating India’s economic growth”— was about a report that his new org, The Convergence Foundation (one among many that he’s spawned), has published about “Systemic impact exemplars”. It details and distils 20 unique approaches towards solving India’s development challenges.

All the orgs discussed there are known ‘doers’ in their own right. I’ll pick one today: Janaagraha. Being a Bengaluru resident, I’ve seen this org take shape as a movement for “participatory democracy’ and then evolve into a “city-systems framework”.

Reading the report, I learnt that Janagraaha’s advocacy has led to, among many other initiatives, the Nagara Raj Bill (Town Governance Bill) and Public Disclosure Law, which are now implemented in 11 and 25 states respectively. If 4,200 municipalities (and counting) are publishing audited results today, it’s thanks to Janaagraha’s urban governance crusade.

Systemic change can change the system.

PS: The email on 5 June carried an incorrect map of India. We sincerely apologise for the error.

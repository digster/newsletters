Today’s story reminds me of an episode years ago when I explained the difference between digital banking services provided by conventional banks and those offered by digital ones to a politics desk editor.

At that time, a handful of banking services already identified themselves as digital banks in Indonesia. They provided services on their apps—similar to those on conventional banks’ mobile banking applications—but included some new features, like different posts for savings and automated payment sharing with fellow users. Further, conventional lenders operating in the country owned these so-called neobanks.

I couldn’t give him a precise answer because the financial services regulator was still deliberating a playbook for such new institutions. Not long after that day, the OJK released a regulation on commercial banks that outlines the definition of and requirements for digital banks in 2018, among other things. It also states that a conventional bank could transition itself into a digital bank.

Similarly, rural banks in the Philippines are currently transforming themselves into digital banks after being acquired by conglomerates and fintech firms. However, this is despite the country’s central bank enforcing a moratorium on the issuance of digital bank permits since mid-2021.

Katrina, the author of today’s report, wrote about how those transformations are possible and what the future holds for these new digital banks.

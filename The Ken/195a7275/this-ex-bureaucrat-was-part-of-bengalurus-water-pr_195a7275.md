Good Morning Ishan,
The prospect of water scarcity makes for great dystopian fiction, from the cinematic world of Mad Max to novels like Paolo Bacigalupi’s The Water Knife. But our reality is more of a slow drip: a water tariff hike of one paisa per litre, notices from homeowner associations about lowering faucet pressure, and small budgetary adjustments that siphon funds away from irrigation projects.
Bengaluru’s dependence on groundwater and the Cauvery River, as well as the city’s elevation and constantly growing population, all make it at risk of water shortages. That crisis, however, is an opportunity for private firms like Denta Water and Infra, which is responsible for a range of lift irrigation and groundwater recharge projects in Karnataka.
Denta is successful enough that its January IPO had an issue size of Rs 220 crore. Investors rushed in: the offering was 221X oversubscribed.
Part of that equation was Denta’s “skilled and efficient” promoter, as the company said in its red herring prospectus. C Mruthyunjaya Swamy is a former chief secretary of Karnataka’s Minor Irrigation Department. He and three of his family members held 96% stake in Denta when it went public.
Denta isn’t alone. More and more enterprises are taking on government contracts to manage Bengaluru’s water supply and, hopefully, institute long-term solutions to the drought. This is unfolding at a time when the Bangalore Water Supply and Sewerage Board is in a sorry state, with the agency lacking funds and working at 30–40% of its intended capacity.

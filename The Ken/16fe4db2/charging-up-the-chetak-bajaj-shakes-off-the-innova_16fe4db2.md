Resurrecting an old brand for a new generation. Fitting a new electric interior inside an old scooter design. Is it a calculated risk or a (late) innovator’s dilemma?
Describe Chetak, Bajaj’s all new electric two-wheeler that launched last week, as you will. For Rajiv Bajaj, managing director of Bajaj Auto, it’s about creating something unexpected. He thinks he’s taken a page out of Tesla’s playbook. “Why is Tesla so successful? Because it doesn’t just sell a product. The brand tells a story,” he says in today’s story.
It’s another matter that Tesla has no legacy to worry about. Bajaj, however, has been a more cautious disruptor than Tesla, bringing back an old brand for EVs while keeping its cash cow—the top-selling motorcycles brands—out of this disruption.
I’m writing this email on the weekend when the world is paying tribute to the towering figure of business management, Clayton M Christensen. In the final chapter of his seminal 1997 book, The Innovator’s Dilemma, he called electric vehicles a disruptive tech and laid down, what looked later, a manual for approaching EVs. (Of course, it wasn’t a high-priced Tesla in the US or a stylish Ather in India that he could’ve predicted, but he got many things right.)
“If present rates of improvement continue, however, we would expect the cruising range of electric cars, for example, to intersect with the average range demanded in the mainstream market by 2015, and electric vehicle acceleration to intersect with mainstream demands by 2020”, he wrote.
In 2020, a mainstream two-wheeler maker in India has made an innovator, Ather, ‘look’ like an incumbent.
How Bajaj carved out a new unit termed ‘Urbanite’ (where the average age of the team is lower than the company’s); how it built Chetak ground up, (not lego-assembling it like many of the 36 electric two-wheelers in the market); how Bajaj views a suite of products to come off this brand resurrection…
Olina addresses these and many more questions in this engaging story.

Long and Short
Wed, 10 Dec 25
The story behind the numbers and motivations driving India's stock market.
Good Morning Ishan,
Indian IPOs tend to trigger a familiar ritual: a DRHP appears, someone Ctrl+Fs the offer for sale (OFS) section, and the market decides whether the investors are edging toward the exit. But Amagi Media Labs’* IPO document—recently [blessed](https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/markets/ipos/fpos/amagi-media-labs-gets-sebi-nod-for-ipo-to-raise-rs-1020-cr-via-fresh-issue/articleshow/125543133.cms?from=mdr) by the markets regulator—refuses to play along.
Amagi is a $1.4 billion-valued cloud-infrastructure firm that provides the invisible plumbing behind thousands of TV channels. It’s the backend that moves video signals, stitches ads into streams, and keeps your cricket match from buffering.
Yes, there is an OFS. (An OFS is simply existing shareholders selling some of their stake—money goes to them, not the company.) And sure, some well-known investors are selling. But what matters in public markets is not the number of shares but rather the percentage of ownership being diluted. And on that metric, Amagi’s OFS looks like routine portfolio housekeeping rather than a coordinated goodbye.
Premji Invest is selling only a slice of its position and will still hold roughly 27% after listing. Accel and Norwest will remain around 15% each. These are not investors exiting…just lightening up after a decade-long hold. If anything, the percentage they are selling is lower than or similar to what investors have sold in many recent tech listings.
Lenskart’s founder bought shares pre-listing; Wakefit did a small OFS plus fresh issue (issue booked just 32% by Day 2); Vishal Mega Mart [leaned on](https://the-ken.com/long_and_short/vishal-mega-mart-and-the-unexpected-rs-10200-crore-stake-sale-decoded/) an OFS-heavy structure. Even Physics Wallah founders [still hold](https://the-ken.com/story/how-physicswallah-escaped-the-startup-math-of-giving-everything-away/) almost three-fourths of their company. If there’s a pattern, it’s that there is no pattern.
Just before Amagi’s IPO paperwork hit Sebi, the founders—holding around 14%—bought 3.5 million shares at Rs 25 each. With no price band set yet, discussions on “discounts” could be premature
Meanwhile, the fresh issue makes the intent even clearer: Rs 1,020 crore coming in, of which Rs 667 crore is earmarked for technology and cloud infrastructure. This is not how companies wind down. This is how they prepare for their next phase of global scale. Even the optional Rs 204-crore pre-IPO placement reads like a company expecting an orderly market reception.
In essence, it says: we need more runway for global playout, streaming infrastructure, ad-tech pipelines, and maybe an acquisition or two.
And that’s really all the OFS deserves. Because the more interesting question isn’t who is selling how much. It’s: what exactly is the public being asked to buy?
The answer is a business that doesn’t resemble anything Indian markets have listed before.
Amagi is not a domestic consumer app, a marketplace, or an IT-services shop with smiling engineers and recoverable margins. It is a cloud-broadcast infrastructure company—one that built a business India didn’t need, because broadcasters here weren’t overhauling their playout tech or rushing to the cloud in the 2010s. So Amagi scaled globally into markets that desperately did.
Its systems now power 7,000+ channel deliveries, ferry 500,000+ hours of content across the world every month, and stitch more than 26 billion monetised ad impressions annually into FAST and CTV streams—the free, ad-supported streaming channels and connected-TV platforms that have become the internet’s version of cable TV.
On its ad-tech infrastructure alone, hours of viewing surged 95% year-on-year, while ad impressions rose 65%. These are statistics of a global backbone—one whose wiring you never think about unless it breaks during a football match and every viewer starts tweeting in despair.
And the financials track this transformation nicely.
Revenue rose from Rs 725 crore in FY23 to Rs 942 crore in FY24 to Rs 1,223 crore in FY25. Net loss shrank from Rs 245 crore to Rs 69 crore—a 72% improvement in a single year.
In deep tech, that is what an inflection point looks like: heavy upfront investment followed by sharp operating leverage as scale compounds. One more channel spun up, one more FAST feed added, one more region launched—and the economics begin bending in your favour rather than against you.
The company’s FY25 report added another detail that strengthens the “global” narrative: foreign exchange earnings of Rs 718 crore vs outflow of Rs 220 crore, leaving a net FX surplus of nearly Rs 500 crore. That means 59%-plus of Amagi’s FY25 income came from outside India. It is global in the most unambiguous sense: the money comes from abroad. (The IPO document doesn’t mention the revenue split geographically, though.)
Then there is the piece the DRHP touches on but doesn’t quite highlight: Amagi’s accumulation of deep-tech assets.
In FY25, it completed the acquisition and full integration of Argoid, an ML-driven recommendation and content optimisation platform, and advanced the integration of Tellyo, a cloud-based live video editing and distribution tool.
Together, they show that selling cloud playout is the entry point; building the layer that cements its place in the global ecosystem is the strategy.
Even the small operational details support the idea of maturing economics. The IT team cut redundant SaaS tools, retired duplicate sandboxes and plugins, and automated payments—together recovering roughly 400 staff hours a year.
It’s the kind of quiet operational craft that doesn’t make headlines but does make Ebitda.
But a sceptical analyst will immediately ask: how much of this operating leverage is structural, and how much is simply revenue outrunning AWS bills—for now? Usage-based infra businesses have a habit of looking efficient right before cloud costs slope upward again. Without granular disclosure on per-channel cost curves, renewal terms, or committed spend with cloud providers, investors may be pricing faith as much as they would be pricing fundamentals.
Which brings us to the fun part: trying to classify Amagi using the Indian market’s standard mental models. This is a company that breaks templates for sport.
Amagi is too SaaS to be infra, too infra to be SaaS, and too global to fit any domestic benchmark.
The closest analogues are AWS Elemental or France-listed Ateme, not Zoho or TCS. SaaS companies grow 20–40% annually with gross margins north of 75%. Infrastructure firms grow 10–20% with margins between 45–65% and meaningful client concentration.
Amagi inconveniently grows at 30%, carries infra-style dependability burdens—the operational load of a company expected to keep live signals running flawlessly across continents—and earns revenue in ways that resemble global network underlay more than seats-and-subscriptions software.
The concentration numbers reinforce its infra DNA: the largest customer contributes 11.5% of revenue; the top five, 24%; the top ten, 34%. It’s a far healthier spread than most infra companies manage, but the underlying customer types are pure mission-control. Global broadcasters, not SaaS seat-buyers.
And the operational risk makes the distinction clearer.
The company doesn’t get a tenth of its revenue from some random mid-market SaaS buyer; it gets it from large broadcast networks—think NBCUniversal, which has publicly recognised Amagi for supporting its cloud TV operations. In this industry, that isn’t unusual. Land one major network and you inherit an entire bouquet of channels, regional variants, and syndication feeds, which can easily add up to 10–15% of revenue.
It’s not concentration that should terrify anyone; it’s simply how the television business is structured. But the stakes are different: these are customers who don’t send Slack messages when something breaks; they escalate through legal teams because a “glitch” means a live signal went dark.
A CRM outage is annoying. A playout outage is a public event.
This hybrid identity creates a modelling dilemma. Value Amagi like SaaS and the infrastructure burden looks heavy. Value it like infrastructure and the growth looks suspiciously strong. Value it like a media company and, well, now you’re just being silly. Its economics originate in cloud workloads, not production budgets.
But the real challenge is cultural. Indian public markets are extraordinarily good at valuing predictable business models: banks, IT services, cement, insurance, platform consumer tech. They adore recurring revenue—but only when it comes from contracts and headcount, not usage-based cloud workloads. Mostly because Indian markets have never really had to price a company whose revenue spikes and dips with global streaming traffic rather than domestic contract renewals.
They tolerate losses—but only when the TAM is domestic and intuitively familiar. They like stories that fit into one sentence.
Amagi does not fit in one sentence.
That’s what makes the IPO a referendum on the market’s appetite, not the company’s narrative. A test of whether India can price a company whose revenue depends on American and European ad cycles, whose margins depend on cloud-region usage, whose uptime requirements look like telecom SLAs, and whose customer list includes global studios and FAST platforms rather than Indian SMEs. Its operations span five subsidiaries and four step-down subsidiaries, including the newly incorporated Amagi AI Pvt Ltd.
If the market understands this company—pricing it thoughtfully rather than force-fitting it into familiar categories—then an entire pipeline of Indian deep-tech startups suddenly has a domestic listing venue. AI infrastructure. Industrial robotics. Chip tooling. Cloud security. Software-defined hardware. All the hard things Indian founders quietly assume they must take abroad.
If the market cannot understand Amagi, then the signal is equally clear: Indian public markets still prefer their tech with the training wheels of predictability, headcount, and domestic customers attached.
After a decade in the shadows—powering the world’s TV channels from Bengaluru servers and AWS regions—Amagi is finally stepping into the public market spotlight. Its investors are stepping out just enough to make the timing interesting. Its financials are stepping up enough to suggest maturity. And its business model is stepping far enough outside Indian stereotypes to make everyone slightly uncomfortable.
The company is ready. The infrastructure is proven. The customers are global. The revenue is real. The losses are shrinking. And now, much like every good experiment, the result will say more about the testing environment than about the subject under observation.
*Baskar Subramanian, co-founder of Amagi Media Labs, is an investor in The Ken.

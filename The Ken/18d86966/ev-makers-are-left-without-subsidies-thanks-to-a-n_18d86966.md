Good Morning Ishan,
Of late, India’s central government has been behaving like today’s funds-strapped tech startups—reducing their workforce with a calculated focus.
Don’t believe me? This week, Business Standard reported that the number of central government workforce, excluding the central public sector enterprises, is estimated to decline by about 50,000 by March 2025.
For a young country, for a country on a growth path, and for a country that has put aside billions of dollars to spur manufacturing across industries, a lack of trained human resources is hurting where it hurts most—letting manufacturers stay the course. Or encourage new, even upstart, manufacturers.
More specifically, as Nathan reports exclusively, the 75-year-old non-bank that was brought in to clean up the mess in the electric vehicle subsidy scheme disbursement—the Industrial Finance Corporation of India—has itself become the problem.
Subsidies worth nearly Rs 674 crore ($81 million) have not been paid since July. The electric two-wheeler industry alone was supposed to achieve a target of selling 2.3 million vehicles in 2024 but several people say at the current rate, they’d barely hit 900,000.
An under-staffed IFCI, with little expertise to deal with 62 manufacturers, is crumbling under its new responsibility. As one EV industry executive put it: “On average, they have to deal with 80,000 electric scooter claims every month. This is no small feat.”
No point glorifying the workload when some processes, better expertise, and technology can solve this. And oh, not to forget a new consensus solution that Nathan’s story talks about.
Today government officials in Delhi have convened a meeting to discuss precisely this.

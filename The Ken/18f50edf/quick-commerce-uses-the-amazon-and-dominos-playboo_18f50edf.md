Good Morning Ishan,
The great, nay, “the greatest” Muhammad Ali’s iconic line “Float like a butterfly, sting like a bee.” seems to be the inspiration in some unlikely quarters—decades after the champion boxer delivered it.
Indian quick-commerce biggies Blinkit, Swiggy Instamart, and Zepto have been delivering the punches thick and fast—combining the packaging efficiency of e-commerce giant Amazon with the delivery speed of pizza chain Domino’s. And their knockout jabs and uppercuts are now no longer restricted to their familiar hunting ground: groceries. The quick-commerce companies have taken their 10-minute-delivery fight to a whole new ring—non-groceries—putting fear into the hearts of Amazon and its rival Flipkart.
Smartwatches, lipstick, toys, coolers, playstations and more, a wide and varied swathe of non-groceries are now being delivered quick-time. About 20–30% of the gross order value of quick-commerce companies now comes from non-groceries, according to a recent research report. And the list is only growing. I experienced this first-hand a few weeks back when a special pichkari (water gun) that the kid wanted on Holi landed at the doorstep soon after the order was placed.
How do these platforms manage to juggle groceries and non-groceries—chalk and cheese, as they are? It takes a lot—planned to a tee, complex operations, and meticulous execution, as Aakriti finds in today’s insightful story.
But do all deliveries really happen within 10 minutes, or is that just a hook? [Click here to take a look behind the scenes of dark stores](https://the-ken.com/story/quick-commerce-uses-the-amazon-and-dominos-playbook-to-go-beyond-groceries/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter) that make these pugilists tick and the challenges they face to deliver on their promise.
“Wonder where this is come from. Was not even double checked with Finance Ministry. Pure speculation. Sorry, @CNBCTV18Live speculation, particularly during #LokSabhaElection2024 (sic),” tweeted India’s Finance Minister Nirmala Sitharaman last Friday after the stock market crashed.
The trigger for the crash and the minister’s annoyance was a report that the new government that comes to power after the ongoing Lok Sabha elections will bring changes to the tax law—including introducing uniform capital gains tax on all asset classes.
The stock market, as expected, threw a fit and made its displeasure known. It always does whenever there is news, a rumour or even a whiff of any unfavourable change.
Equity as an asset class has been enjoying concessional tax treatment for years, and many stock-market participants want this to continue. A uniform capital gains tax could upset the apple cart. But as The Ken [had argued](https://the-ken.com/story/why-its-time-the-government-bit-the-bullet-on-simplifying-capital-gains-tax/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter) a year-and-a-half back, there is good reason and rationale for the government to bite the bullet on simplifying and rationalising capital gains taxes. Investments should be encouraged on merits rather than on sops. But guess, any such talk in election time is taboo—the stock market needs to stay buoyant and feel good.

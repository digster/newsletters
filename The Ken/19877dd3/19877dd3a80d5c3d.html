An obituary for roll-ups. An ignominious end was on the cards

Raise millions. Buy a bunch of online-first brands on the cheap. Scale the hell out of them. Talk about building a “house of brands”. Rinse and repeat. 

This was supposed to be “roll-up” companies’ world-beating playbook during the post-pandemic surge in e-commerce.

Sure, they did the fundraising and acquisitions. But everything else fell apart. Gradually, then suddenly. 

Darpan Sanghvi, founder of the Good Glamm Group, a roll-up venture once worth $1.2 billion, took to Linkedin last month to disclose what was a long time coming.

Our Lenders have decided to enforce their charge on the individual brands under the Good Glamm Group. What this means is that there will no longer be a group-wide solution which will allow all the brands to continue under a single umbrella. Instead, the brands will be sold one by one, and will operate individually instead of being under one umbrella and there will be new individual owners for each of the different brands. 

It mattered little that among Good Glamm’s investors were the big guns of venture capital (Accel, Prosus), private equity (Warburg Pincus), and e-commerce (Amazon). 

Days earlier, a Good Glamm rival, Globalbees Brands, had got some unpleasant news too. The directors of a company Globalbees had bought in 2021 initiated bankruptcy proceedings against it over unpaid dues. This followed the resignation of Globalbees’ chief executive and its investors’ representatives from its board. 

In January, the US company that kick-started the brief roll-up boom, Thrasio, was in trouble for similar reasons, almost a year after filing for bankruptcy.

“Mom and pop” vendors who sold their businesses to the company are also alleged to have lost out because some had not been fully paid for the transactions.

In the lawsuit filed in New Jersey in December, lawyers representing the company’s estate alleged that the executives propped up a “sham” business while touting its consistent profitability.

[…]

Thrasio bought 181 businesses between 2019 and 2021—at least one a week on average—typically by “deferring payment of a substantial portion of the purchase price to the small business owners”, according to the lawsuit. 

Executives who ran failed Amazon ‘aggregator’ sued over alleged sham business, Financial Times

Indian roll-ups’ fortunes plummeted so low that they sold some brands back to the founders. You don’t need a genius to figure out what went wrong.

When Thrasio’s Indian copycats set out on their grand adventure, e-commerce largely meant Amazon, Flipkart, and Myntra. Or in other words, marketplaces where tens of thousands of sellers hawk a dizzying array of products. Physical shopping, meanwhile, hadn’t fully emerged from its Covid shocks.

But just as Good Glamm, Globalbees, and Mensa began consolidating their brands, quick commerce took the wind out of marketplaces’ sails. Suddenly, every consumer brand wanted to figure out a way to reach homes within hours if not minutes. And brick-and-mortar stores staged a comeback. The budget-apparel chain Zudio did exceptionally well despite not selling online.

So, online-first brands scrambled to put together what they had never thought they would need: feet on the street.

But replicating in a couple of years what FMCG companies took decades to perfect was foolhardy, as skincare brand Mamaearth learnt soon after going public.

A distributor like Kumar typically acts as the intermediary between the brand and the kirana store. They buy stock upfront (usually in cash) and extend credit to the kiranas—usually for around 20 to 30 days. But for Mamaearth, with its fresh face in town, distributors were extending 45-day credit periods. They were taking on the risk, so they were banking on higher margins.

At the peak of this inventory build-up, small distributors sat on stocks worth Rs 10–15 lakh (US$12–18,000), and the bigger players held on to as much as Rs 40–45 lakh worth of product—far more than their average sales could justify. 

Roll-ups’ inability to gauge the future of quick commerce isn’t shocking. Nor is their misjudgment of offline retail’s complexity. But Sanghvi’s postmortem on Good Glamm’s collapse on Linkedin last week went far beyond either.

  • Acquired 11 companies creating a complex portfolio and business
  • Multiple companies, multiple founder DNAs, multiple opportunities to pursue, multiple problems to solve—and thus no focus
  • Entered 5 brands in 5 BPC Categories without having fully scaled in any 1 brand and category
  • Simultaneously tried to build a tech first DTC business and a traditional 0ffline business
  • And trying to create an independent media & creator house instead of just focusing on content creator for our brands

Or, everything that made the roll-up business a big draw for founders and investors in the first place.

Good Morning Ishan,

A few days back, an ad on Page 1 of a business daily grabbed my attention. Against a deep red background was the big bait in massive font—‘Double your investment in just 72 months’. Next to the text was a photo of two coconut trees with lots of coconuts—as if cocking a snook at that old ‘money does not grow on trees’ chestnut.

The ad also took me back to a number-hack rhyme I had written in Ka-ching!, our weekly newsletter focussing on fintech and finance, some time back. Here’s a bit from that.

If your money doubles
Let’s say, in 10 years
That’s not much actually
Just 7.2% p.a., dears

How do we know that?
It’s the ‘Rule of 72’
Gives the compound annual rate
At which a rupee grows to two

Just divide 72
With the number of years
Here, 72/10 is the answer
The air this clears

So, I applied this ‘Rule of 72’ to the numbers in the ad and figured that the yearly yield was 12.25%. Now, that’s impressive. And to its credit, the company that is raising funds through debt instruments hadn’t exaggerated the number.

But with great returns comes great risk. The ad underplayed this. The debt’s not-bad but not-great credit rating BBB- was tucked away in a small font—not quite visible unless you looked hard.

Should double-your-money ads be stopped? Yes. Should credit ratings and their meaning be shown prominently in such ads? Yes, that would do investors much good.

Displayed prominently in the ad, though, was the company founder’s motto—‘Conquer the world with love’—which made me smile.

The protagonist of today’s deep-dive story by Shivani could coin a similar slogan for itself—‘Conquer India with milk’. Amul, the country’s biggest dairy co-operative, has dominated the domestic milk and its byproducts market for decades. The household name has now set its sights on a new pasture in which it has a uniquely advantageous position: whey.

Who would have thought that something considered waste residue and a B2B product could one day become a buzzy B2C business? And, to boot, help solve a dogged problem in the country. What’s Amul’s secret sauce? Is it ‘whey to go’? Or not quite? This story has the answers and more.

John Werner Commented

“So much so that it signed a leasing agreement with a Bengaluru mall that states “no premium coffee chain” can operate on the same floor as a Starbucks.”

This practice of restricting competition is prevalent in the luxury goods industry: in Singapore, luxury malls introduced a “no same store” clause within a specific radius or specifically naming the malls where one was barred from opening a second store. These are commercial terms and not illegal. However, luxury brands want to be close to each other, and this is the reason the malls look and feel all the same. With largely the same offering. Let’s see if the competition clause gets prevalent in leasing agreements in India. Usually, the mall owners dictate those terms and conditions and not the other way around. Early days, I guess.

The sovereign gold bond tax change violates a golden rule. New rules should apply to decisions prospectively, not retrospectively

Finance Minister Nirmala Sitharaman has stoutly defended the change in taxation rules for sovereign gold bonds (SGBs) announced in the Union Budget on 1 February. Never mind that many feel short-changed by it. 

How do you invest in gold?

With the changes coming to gold sovereign bonds (SGBs) following the Budget, we’re curious how you approach investing in gold. Let us know below.

Take the short survey https://theken.typeform.com/to/VsygMmEG 

The change is that from April, capital gains tax exemption on SGBs will be available only to those who subscribed to the bonds in an original issue and held them continuously until redemption on maturity after 8 years. 

The implication is that those who bought the SGBs in the secondary market and redeemed them on maturity will now have to pay capital gains tax. As will those who bought them in the original issue or in the secondary market, and opted for early redemption after five years in the RBI-offered window. Depending on the period of holding, such investors will need to pay tax at 12.5% on long-term capital gains and at their slab rates on short-term gains. 

Two other categories of investors were anyway liable to pay capital gains tax even before—those who bought the SGBs in the original issue but sold them in the secondary market, and those who bought and sold in the secondary market.

In an interaction https://www.thehindubusinessline.com/economy/budget/fm-sitharaman-if-you-profit-from-sgb-secondary-market-government-deserves-its-share/article70582657.ece with The Hindu Business Line, the finance minister defended the move thus: “But you went through a secondary market, and then you are making a killing. Why shouldn’t I get something? And you’re not even holding it for maturity. Even if you hold it for maturity, you pick it up from somewhere else (secondary market). So I’m placing a bit of a premium on it.”

The SGBs, she said, were designed for long-term investors, and those who hold them until maturity, as originally intended, will continue to enjoy the promised tax-free benefits.

The trouble with the argument and the tax change on SGB is that they violate a golden rule—of not shifting the goal post after the players have started playing.

Now, there is no questioning the government’s prerogative to make and change tax rules. What needs to be questioned, though, is the transactions to which such changes should apply. The change, as per the Budget’s wording, will apply to all SGBs. This means that even SGBs bought before this Budget are subject to the change. 

That’s unfair.

Those who had bought SGBs in the secondary market earlier did so on the understanding that if they held them till maturity, there would be no capital gains tax. Also, those who bought the SGBs in original issues or in the secondary market before the Budget change did so on the understanding that if they opted for early redemption after five years in the RBI-window, there would be no capital gains tax. 

Their returns and risk calculations, and portfolio construction were based on the law that prevailed at the time of their investments. That informed their original intent. Surely, investors’ intent should matter as much as the government’s? In any case, the law at the time of investment matters more than anyone’s intent.

Just because an investor bought in the secondary market and “made a killing” due to the huge rally in gold prices over the past few years, it should not make them liable to tax, due to a change that comes later. Ditto for those who opt for early redemption.

Tax changes deserve ‘grandfathering’—which essentially means that a rule change is not applicable or mandatory on transactions done before the date on which the rule changed.

The fact is that neither the government nor investors in SGBs expected gold prices to shoot up like it did. While investors are understandably elated and making a killing, the government is having to foot this unexpectedly large bill and is not too happy about it.

The Budget change is nothing but a flex by the government to lessen its burden by taxing investors who were just playing by the original rules. The government seems to forget that the game could have gone any way—in its favour too if gold prices had stayed flat or fallen. Would it have compensated investors in that case?

This is not the first time the government has violated the ‘grandfathering’ principle. I had written https://the-ken.com/kaching/gimme-my-grandpa-too/ in 2024 how debt fund investors got the cold shoulder after the indexation-related tax change.

The government stopped issuing SGBs after 2024—the price risk and burden were getting too high and unviable. That’s understandable and fair. 

It would also have been fair to tax capital gains in transactions where the investor buys SGBs in the secondary market after this Budget announcement, and then later encashes them for a profit, whether by selling in the secondary market, or through early redemption or at maturity. Here, the investor will be walking in with eyes open and playing the game as per the changed rules. 

But by doing what it did in the Budget, the government is essentially saying heads-I-win-tails-you-lose. Will it make amends before the change gets passed in the Parliament? The tax game deserves fair play.

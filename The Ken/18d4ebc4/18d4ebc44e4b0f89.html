1. Wear your blinkers

 

What’s the first visual that comes to your mind when someone talks about blinkers? Is it this?

[Source: Wikimedia Commons]

“Blinkers”, also called “blinders”, are used to keep the horse focused strictly on the track during races. But our association with them is almost always negative. 

 

When we say someone has blinkered vision, we mean to imply they are short sighted, ignorant, biased, and unable to see the “full picture”. 

 

Like the horse above. 


But here’s another picture of humans wearing blinkers. Expensive blinkers.

[Source: Duke University]

 

The athletes in the photo above are wearing “strobe” glasses. These special glasses switch between clear and opaque modes rapidly—multiple times a second—meaning wearers see the world around them like really old black and white movies where scenes “jumped” between frames. 

 

Here’s an older video of NBA superstar Stephen Curry training with strobe glasses. 

 

Why, you might be wondering, would you want to see the world in jerky frame rate?

"Humans have a memory buffer in their brain that keeps information alive for a certain short-lived period," said Greg Appelbaum, assistant professor of psychiatry at Duke and first author of the study. "Wearing the strobe eyewear during the physical training seemed to boost the ability to retain information in this buffer."

 

The strobe eyewear disrupts vision by only allowing the user to see glimpses of the world. Users must adjust their visual processing in order to perform normally, and this adjustment produces a lingering benefit: once participants removed the strobe eyewear, there was an observed boost in their visual memory retention that was found to still be active 24 hours later.

 

Strobe Eyewear Training Improves Visual Memory (Duke University, July 2012)

So, turns out wearing blinkers isn’t all that bad? You don’t say. And what’s another word for blinkers, whether it’s for humans or horses? 

 

Constraints. 

 

Perhaps you turn in your best work when deadlines are imminent? Or maybe when you focus on one problem for, say, a few days, without any diversions? Maybe your most creative solutions have come when budgets and resources have been cut? 

 

Embrace those constraints. Because imposing constraints on ourselves in the right contexts can actually help us do better.

Recent surveys show that managers tend to consider compliance restrictions and a lack of resources as the main obstacles to innovation. This common wisdom suggests eradicating all constraints: by getting rid of rules and boundaries, creativity, and innovative thinking will thrive. Our research, however, challenges this wisdom and suggests that managers can innovate better by embracing constraints. We reviewed 145 empirical studies on the effects of constraints on creativity and innovation, and found that individuals, teams, and organizations alike benefit from a healthy dose of constraints. It is only when the constraints become too high that they stifle creativity and innovation.

 

[…]

 

According to the studies we reviewed, when there are no constraints on the creative process, complacency sets in, and people follow what psychologists call the path-of-least-resistance – they go for the most intuitive idea that comes to mind rather than investing in the development of better ideas. Constraints, in contrast, provide focus and a creative challenge that motivates people to search for and connect information from different sources to generate novel ideas for new products, services, or business processes.


Why Constraints Are Good for Innovation by Oguz A. Acar, Murat Tarakci, and Daan van Knippenberg, Harvard Business Review

“Creativity loves constraint” used to be a belief at Google too, as this decade old video from Marissa Mayer shows (like Will Smith, both Google and Mayer have lost their erstwhile cool sheen too).

 

Embracing constraints often frees us to think and operate better, because we now know that there are aspects we can consciously ignore. And when we know what we can ignore, we also know what we need to focus on. They are two sides of the very same coin. 

 

This is what Aneesh Reddy, the co-founder and CEO of Capillary Technologies realised when he went for his first 10-day Vipassana meditation. I met him earlier this week for the First Principles podcast and had one of the most candid conversations. Having started Capillary right out of engineering college, Aneesh checked off virtually the entire young-founder mistake checklist over the first decade of his entrepreneurial journey.

 

Raise too much, spend too much? Check. 

 

Product sprawl? Check.

 

Indiscriminate global expansion? Check.

 

Co-founder breakups? Check. 

 

Burnout? Check.  

 

But Aneesh had enough resilience and self-awareness to correct the course, which started with executive coaching mandated by his board, and then led on to Vipassana. 

 

As someone who’s been yearning to undertake Vipassana for many years now, I asked Aneesh why he does it every year. And aside from all the personal aspects, what professional clarity he got out of it.

 

He told me that 10 days of silence and meditation and self-awareness made him see that there were just 6-8 decisions in a year he made that really mattered for him and Capillary. His goal is to be more conscious about them and make them count.

3. #SilentSunday photos 📸

 

Welcome to another issue of Silent Sunday, Ishan. It’s Jonathan here. 

 

I grew up in suburban Chennai in the ‘90s and early ‘00s, so empty plots where my friends and I played cricket or climbed trees were the norm rather than the single-floor houses that dotted the landscape. There wasn't an apartment block in sight, but we did have a running acquaintance with a 6-foot rat snake (PSA: they're not venomous, and not to belabour the obvious, rather useful to have around if you have a rat problem).

 

By the time we moved closer to the city in 2005, there wasn't an empty plot left and the rat snake had long disappeared. To where, we knew not. But I wished him well. Maybe he, at least, had found another piece of open land and sky to call home. 

 

Me? I’ve lived almost all my life since with the sky only playing a faded backdrop to apartment blocks, metro lines, and traffic snarls—in three of India's metropolises. 

 

Chennai wasn't big on neighbourhood parks (we seriously need to up our game there), but Delhi’s parks and Bengaluru’s lakes have offered my wife and I at least some refuge. We’ve taken to spending our nights on the walking track that runs along our neighbourhood lake in Kaggadasapura, Bengaluru. It’s where we cool down after our workouts and escape the city.


This safe space, where families and children walk their pets late into the night, has also been where I’ve done some of my best thinking these past few months. The absence of intrusive distractions, the comforting strain of a brisk walk, the feeling of being home… all have played a part, I think.

I wrote a couple of weeks ago that photography is a passion I’m rediscovering, fuelled by the beautiful pictures First Principles readers have been sending in from around the world. These pictures are the first I’ve taken in any seriousness in years. 

 

This is my refuge. It’s beautiful, and I wanted to share it with you. 

 

Do you have a place like this, where you can clock your daily 10,000 steps and a half-hour or so of quiet thinking under the open sky? I’m sure I, and our ever discerning readers, would love to see it through your eyes… well, your camera lens.

4. #FPBooks 📚

 

Hi, welcome back to First Principles books! This is Anushka. And even as I’m writing this, I’m still thinking about a book that I finished rereading only a few hours ago.

 

It’s a work of fiction called Sorrow and Bliss by Meg Mason. It opens almost mid-scene to the life of a 40-year old woman, who has just been left by her husband. She then recounts all the years of her life—winnowed down by a certain mental illness—that led her to this point. The prose is raw, almost mad, and funny-in-the-British-way. The moment I finished it, I rushed to find an interview of the author, hoping to learn something about her process of writing a piece of fiction so grave, yet so funny. I found something interesting: in this interview, Mason calls Sorrow and Bliss a “post-hope project.” She first wrote a disastrous book, threw it down the drain, and lost all hope of actually producing anything publishable. So then, she just went into her garden shed and started working on something new with zero agenda—no one knew, not even her publisher. No one would ever see this book, she firmly believed. She was, she says:

 

“a bit drunk with it, because I didn’t care. It was like making this enormous meal from everything you have in the fridge, with no recipe, just throwing it all in. It just doesn’t matter. And it was the last hurrah.”

 

Of course, I am no Meg Mason, and most likely, if I write something for the sheer heck of it without the pressure to show anyone, it will not end up being a bestselling novel. But this did make me think about creative projects in general, and the fact that sometimes, the best work emerges out of a private, fun, stress-free endeavour. 

 

So, I was pleasantly surprised to see two recommendations from the First Principles community this time that also urged me to look into their writers’ lives and careers. Both are memoirs, of sorts. Our third recommendation for this week, though, sits on the other side of the spectrum.

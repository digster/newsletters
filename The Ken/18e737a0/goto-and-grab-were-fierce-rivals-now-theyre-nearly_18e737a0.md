Good Morning Ishan,
Today is a non-publishing day in India as it’s the festival of Holi. However, we have a story from Southeast Asia for you.
Whether or not you’re in the age group I’m about to mention, chances are you’ve benefitted from the Millennial Lifestyle Subsidy. But that’s changing, no matter where you are. Platforms that were previously willing to take a hit to their bottom line are trying to pull out of the red. Grab and Goto have both made headway in this regard.
For over a decade, the ride-hailing and food delivery rivals have been attempting to one-up each other (and, at times, even join hands). Now, they are in stasis, divvying up the market in Southeast Asia without rocking the boat too much.
That has led to a significant moment in the last three months of 2023: Goto reported its first adjusted EBITDA for a quarter (a modest US$4.88 million), while Grab reported a US$11 million profit.
While it might appear that Grab and Goto are companies in Southeast Asia with little bearing abroad, these firms are shaping up to be the region’s own version of Coca-Cola versus Pepsi, Ford versus General Motors, or Microsoft versus Apple, with a local lilt.
If both companies continue to strengthen their balance sheets, they might just redeem themselves in the eyes of shareholders. Check out Prima’s [report](https://the-ken.com/story/goto-and-grab-were-fierce-rivals-now-theyre-nearly-identical-companies/?utm_source=shared&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=shared_newsletter) about a crucial moment in the evolution of the Goto and Grab rivalry.
If I had to pick the peak moment for social media platform Reddit, it would be 2017, when r/place was in full swing.
For those who didn’t take part in the social experiment, r/place was a giant canvas where users could edit one pixel at a time. Communities banded together to plot out images in areas they claimed. There were turf wars. Messages of hate were paved over quickly. Here’s what the result looked like:
That image doesn’t look like much, but the process of making it was really, really fun. Engagement was through the roof, and the feeling of being part of something bigger was planted deep in most users, who did little more than flip the colour of a tiny cell.
Fast forward seven years. Reddit smashed it with their IPO. Shares closed at US$50.44, up 48% in the debut last week, and most investors on “the front page of the internet” were gleeful about the outcome. (There were those who put money in at nearly US$62 a share during the platform’s last financing round.)
Reddit’s current pitch is that it will license its users’ data to firms that are building large language models. There’s sound logic in that—the platform’s users are (mostly) human, so upvotes and downvotes reflect our natural behaviours and content moderation is performed by humans, too. When there’s a deluge of AI-generated content and bots masquerading as Johns and Janes, there’s immense value in that.
Redditors, however, aren’t as excited.
Now that Reddit has a ticker symbol, the platform’s user experience will likely change—more ads will blend in with organic posts, and content moderation rules may become stricter. Crucially, as the latest iteration of r/place in 2023 showed us, there’s a disconnect between the company’s decisions and what users want, from forcing third-party apps to shut down to deleting chat histories with nearly no notice.
Few platforms have managed to hold onto their core users over time. Anecdotally, popular subreddits are less active now than ever before. Maybe it has something to do with a platform that was the epitome of social snacking losing its spark.

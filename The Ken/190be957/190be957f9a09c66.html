Good Morning Ishan,

Early 2019 is when the craft-beer brand Bira 91 truly went mainstream. That’s also when I stopped rooting for it.

Central to both was one event: the launch of its “strong” beers, as brews high on alcohol are referred to in India.

Until this point, Bira’s cachet was that it only had three variants, each of which was distinct, and it didn’t care that 85–90% of the Indian beer market was made up of the really boozy kind.

Now, Bira was yet another beer that tried to be different things to different people. Much like Kingfisher, Carlsberg, and Budweiser—each of which has a strong variant.

I understand, though, why as a VC-funded brand, Bira could no longer afford to be cool by being niche.

“The company has done well to expand its manufacturing footprint to what is now the country’s fourth largest—behind Heineken (owners of Kingfisher), AB Inbev, and Carlsberg,” Ankur Jain, founder and CEO of Bira, told The Ken.

But, as Gaurav details in his story, the choice Bira made came with its own costs. For almost two years, Bira—last valued at US$520 million–has been in short supply at liquor stores and pubs.

What’s more, India’s regulation-heavy alcohol market, which changes dramatically from one state to another, has done Bira no favours. It could take anywhere between three days and a few months for a brewer to get paid.

In other words, this is a business in which you can’t be short of cash or resolve. Does Bira have enough of both? Let’s get to the story.

For Metro Brands, need clashes with desire. Can its discount aversion defy the e-commerce norm?

In early 2024, I wrote about footwear retailer Metro Brands’ plans for Fila, whose India licensee it had bought a couple of years earlier. This is what I said https://the-ken.com/tradetricks/trade-tricks-crocs-worked-wonders-for-metro-brands-can-fila-do-the-same/ then: 

Metro has already begun fixing the Italian-origin, South Korean-owned athleisure brand. It’s getting rid of Fila’s existing inventory at a discount because, as [Metro CEO Nissan] Joseph said https://metrobrands.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/10/MBL-Q2-FY-24-Earnings-Conference-Call-Transcript.pdf on a recent earnings call, it’s not in line with how Metro wants to position the brand.

This is interesting because Metro is not a big fan of discounts. Over 90% https://metrobrands.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/08/MBL-Annual-Report-2022-23.pdf of its products are sold at full price—the highest in the industry. 

In case you’re wondering, hawking almost everything sans discounts is unheard of among Indian shoe-sellers. Bata, the largest among them by revenue, manages to get rid of only 70% of its stock at full price. That Metro can do this—and at juicy operating margins (30%)—is partly why it’s worth 2X as much as Bata. 

In fact, Metro’s aversion to discounts undergirds its retail philosophy. So it’s no surprise that the company hammers this point home in its quarterly-earnings calls. 

But its long-held desire not to discount its products could run into Metro’s need to sell more of its wares online. 

Make no mistake, selling shoes online is a nasty business. Roughly three in 10 orders are returned, not least because shoe sizes are far from standardised. In fact, size has to do with almost 40% of returns in apparel and footwear, according to one estimate https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/industry/cons-products/fashion-/-cosmetics-/-jewellery/more-than-a-third-of-fashion-and-footwear-products-get-returned-in-online-shopping-report/articleshow/112142214.cms?from=mdr. 

But every brand and retailer wants to be online because they can’t have a store big enough to display their entire range. Plus, going digital isn’t optional if they want young shoppers—unless they are Zudio, which has combined trendy clothes with jaw-droppingly low prices to make its outlets a big draw. 

There are tactical reasons, too, as Joseph pointed out on an earnings call https://www.bseindia.com/xml-data/corpfiling/AttachHis/31a93362-2219-4637-9cb2-066c95e2a4f4.pdf earlier this month. 

“The two major markets with early monsoon were Gujarat and Maharashtra where we have a significant dispersion of stores. Nonetheless, we were able to have an almost double-digit increase in top line sales. 

[…] 

Our e-commerce business stayed its course and delivered another 45% growth. Fortunately, monsoons do not dampen online shopping.”

Pre-pandemic, e-commerce contributed a mere 2.5% to Metro’s sales. Two years ago, that figure was 8%. But in the June quarter, it surged to 13.4%. Discounts are inevitable for brands battling for shoppers’ attention in the morass that’s the e-commerce marketplace. So for Metro, the surge in online sales came at the expense of gross margins, even if the impact was minimal. But these are still early days for the retailer’s e-commerce bet. 

Investors are counting on Fila repeating what Crocs https://the-ken.com/tradetricks/crocs-is-2-8b-metro-brands-unlikely-secret-sauce/ did for the company. But Metro’s ability to resuscitate Fila is tied to discounting, especially online, if market leader Puma’s rise is any indication. 

After trailing Campus in shoe sales for two years, Puma claimed the top spot in 2022, when its footwear revenue was close to Rs 1,800 crore (US$217 million)—20% higher than that of Campus.

At first glance, these brands operate in two entirely different price segments. But go to any e-commerce platform, and you’ll see that Puma has products that aren’t significantly pricier than Campus’.

“If Puma sells at a 40–50% discount and is priced below Rs 2,000 (US$24), consumers will go for that,” said the first analyst quoted earlier. In fact, the German brand’s deep discounting is why it has pulled ahead of global peers such as Adidas and Nike in India.

Campus shoes beat Puma and wowed investors. Then came the troubles, The Ken https://the-ken.com/story/campus-shoes-beat-puma-and-wowed-investors-then-came-the-troubles/

But Metro is determined to unyoke deep discounts from e-commerce, as chief financial officer Kaushal Parekh made clear https://www.bseindia.com/xml-data/corpfiling/AttachHis/689d188b-521d-447f-9231-8cfdad78cc0d.pdf in May. 

To be very frank, we are growing across all the channels that are available for us to sort of tap. And obviously, our focus, as we have discussed this multiple times, is on growing our omnichannel business, which is predominantly full price product getting delivered from our store.

So it’s a mix of products that we see. Apart from that, obviously, we keep pushing and strengthening our positioning across various categories. So for example, men’s and handbags will be our focus area and we will—we expect that to sort of increase in the coming years.

So it’s like over a period of time strengthening and using all the avenues that are there to grow this channel, and most importantly, to grow this profitably. We don’t want to grow this by offering a significant discount. We want to limit that. But at the same time, we would want to target and be among the top players across all the categories that are available online.

In other words, Metro wants to pull off a high-wire act.

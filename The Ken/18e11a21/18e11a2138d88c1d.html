While teachers, students, and their parents try and find ways to adapt to the change, schools seek an 'international' route

Good Morning Ishan,

If little boys are made of frogs, snails, and puppy-dog tails, and little girls are made of sugar, spice, and everything nice (so said a 19th-century English poet), should they go to the same school together?

It’s a moot question.

The debate of single-sex versus co-ed schools is as old as schooling itself, Alifiya writes in her story today. For decades, nay, centuries, many schools in India—especially those that the elite sent their kids to—tilted towards the former.

From Mumbai’s Campion to Kolkata’s Scottish Church Collegiate School to Pune’s New English School—boys went to ‘boys’ schools’ and girls went to ‘girls’ schools’. Rarely did the twain meet.

But the times, they are a’ changin.

There is a rush now by legacy single-sex schools to turn ‘co-ed’—several are now enrolling students of the other gender. This trend, that started a few years ago, is picking pace now with states like Kerala and Delhi also pushing for the big change.

Not everyone’s biting yet, but for a growing number, the writing on the wall seems clear. For one, the modern workplace is increasingly becoming gender-neutral. Parents and educationists want kids to learn the ropes young. More urgently, many old single-sex schools are seeing a drop in enrolments, with rising competition from shiny new co-ed schools, including international ones.

Change is never easy though—especially paradigm shifts like these. The toughest part for schools, teachers, and students who make the move are adjustments to changes that are not seen but felt. Gender-balancing can be a delicate act. This richly reported story has the what, why and how of this changing dynamic. It’s a free read, do share widely in your circles.

Speaking of adjustments

Some big adjustment requests went out last week to and from the powers-that-be. The State Bank of India (SBI)—the self-proclaimed banker to every Indian—likely finding itself between a rock and hard place, petitioned the Supreme Court for more time to furnish details of electoral bonds.

Just days before the 6 March deadline to submit this information, the government-controlled banker has asked for an extension until 30 June (Psst: the general elections would be done and dusted by then).

“Coupled with the fact that two different information silos existed, this would mean that a total of 44,434 information sets would have to be decoded, compiled and compared,” the bank said in its application.

Now, that’s odd, coming from an entity that boasts of state-of-the-art IT systems and processes millions of transactions per day. Was it really so hard to compile this information from just 29 branches and the head office?

Then, there was the temporary truce brokered by the government between Google and Indian app developers. It seems ministers have told Google: “adjust maadi”, like they say in Bengaluru where many of these app developers are headquartered.

The key question: For how long?

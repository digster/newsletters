Good Morning Ishan,
Mutual funds from overseas have been leaving India in droves, but one is bucking the trend and coming out on top.
Mirae Asset Mutual Fund has outlasted major names like Goldman Sachs, JP Morgan, and Fidelity, which have all exited the country. Meanwhile, Mirae’s assets under management have grown over 230X over the past decade, going from Rs 692 crore to Rs 1.6 lakh crore at the end of March this year, among the steepest climbs in India’s mutual fund space in that period.
More than that, Mirae doesn’t have the banking and corporate affiliations that other top asset managers leverage to tap into captive investor bases. What the firm did instead was flip the script on how to run a foreign fund house, even if that meant powering through several choppy years.
Check out Anand’s [report](https://the-ken.com/how-mirae-defied-foreign-mutual-funds-india-jinx-to-break-into-the-top-10/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter) in which he cracks Mirae’s secret to longevity in India.
Over in the Philippines, electric vehicles have a growing presence on the roads, but not in the way you’d expect. Lightweight e-trikes are all the rage among low- and middle-income households. They’re relatively affordable and easy to maintain, so Filipinos are buying them—even though there are no subsidies involved.
In fact, e-trikes were in regulators’ blind spot. Local officials didn’t anticipate these vehicles to become so popular so quickly—Katrina estimates that there are 165,000 e-trikes in use in Metro Manila alone. At the moment, there simply aren’t any rules in place for their registration and usage.
Some officials want to ban e-trikes outright, but the president’s office thinks there should be a regulatory framework that supports these small EVs—after all, they run on batteries and fit the larger goal of vehicular electrification.
Katrina unpacks the details in her [story](https://the-ken.com/filipinos-love-for-an-ev-catches-the-government-off-guard/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter) today.
The Five-Year Gap
Here’s a big number for the week: China’s state-backed investment fund for the semiconductor industry is getting another 344 billion yuan, or US$47.5 billion. That follows an initial 138.7 billion yuan in 2014 and 204 billion yuan five years later.
The top-up is unironically, and with brimming chutzpah, called the Big Fund III. It’s meant to channel capital at all phases of chipmaking—research and development, design, manufacturing and fabrication, and then assembly, packaging, and testing. (In true Chinese bureaucratic fashion, the fund’s full title is a mouthful: the National Integrated Circuit Industry Investment Fund. But let’s be honest, we would all rather use the shorter, snappier name.)
The surface read here is simple: the US government is doing everything it can to limit Chinese entities’ access to specific types of technology, so the Big Fund is meant to throw money at the problem until solutions emerge, even though it has yet to release specific investment plans.
However, we can hazard a guess as to the fund’s targets. Advanced chips aside, a crucial bottleneck for China’s semiconductor sector is a shortage of advanced chipmaking equipment.
Intel CEO Pat Gelsinger said early this year that Chinese semiconductor manufacturers’ capabilities are five years behind those of their counterparts in the United States. But maybe a neat US$47 billion will change that. The countdown is on.

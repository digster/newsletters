Good Morning Ishan,

The pandemic upended not just how we worked. It also transformed how we got our health tests done. As work-from-home boomed, so did the trend of test-from-home.

Now, unlike the former, which is being rolled back slowly but surely post-pandemic, the latter is still going and growing strong in India. What started as a necessity has become a habit for many who are opting for the ease and discounted pricing offered by home tests. From routine checkups like complete blood count, and diabetes and thyroid checks to preventive screenings, a battery of tests is now available within the comfort of homes.

Vying to offer these are a host of branded players, including Pharmeasy-controlled Thyrocare, Healthians, Agilus, Apollo, Metropolis, Dr Lal Pathlabs, Max Lab, and more. Scores of diagnostic centres in the unorganised space are also onto this. What’s not to like? Quite a lot, actually.

Trouble is this home-testing habit comes with high risk, as Shivani, the author of today’s story, finds in her extensive investigation across players and tests. The results may not be accurate, as many users have been finding out to their dismay and distress.

What’s causing the results to be skewed? Let’s just say the road from the home to the lab is not a smooth one, and accidents are rife. Many players are also driving fast and loose. To know more, you need to read this important story here. Don’t miss it.

Claims, counter-claims, counter-counter claims

Folks of a certain vintage might remember the iconic tagline “The argument continues” from the Coffy Bite advertisements of the 1990s. Life, it would appear, indeed imitates art.

The argument has been continuing for nearly a decade now in the legal slugfest between media baron Kalanithi Maran who earlier controlled Spicejet and the airline’s current promoter Ajay Singh. In the latest twist, Maran is seeking Rs 1,323 crore (US$160 million) in damages from Spicejet. This comes in response to Spicejet’s claim for a Rs 450 crore (US$54 million) refund, which it had paid to Maran following a breach of contract terms in the 2015 sale of Spicejet from Maran to Singh.

A real head-scratcher—this merry-go-round of claims, counter-claims and counter-counter-claims.

Amid all this non-stop legal jousting, some things have stayed the same. Spicejet was in financial trouble in 2015, and it is in financial trouble in 2024. Not just Maran, even aircraft lessors have been circling Spicejet demanding money, and the airline has been fighting cases in the insolvency court. And while its rival Indigo Airlines posted stellar numbers in the recent March quarter, Spicejet’s last results filing was the September 2023 quarter in which it had reported a big loss.

The airline seems to be on a wing and a prayer, with the coming week crucial for it. An election upset against the ruling dispensation, if it happens, could spell trouble for Spicejet too, as Singh is considered close to the powers that be.

Business risk compounded by political risk, as it were.

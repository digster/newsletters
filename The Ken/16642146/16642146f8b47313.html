Stock exchanges are the ultimate, unforgiving arbiters of market sentiment. But what happens when stock exchanges themselves are subjected to the same treatment?
 
The result is quite the meta story. As India’s largest stock exchange, NSE, is going to find out soon. As will Prashasta Seth, the joint CEO of IIFL Wealth Management. Seth has invested nearly 16% of his $1.1 billion pre-IPO fund (the largest in India) into NSE. In the hope that there’ll be a multi-bagger rainbow at the end of the tunnel.
 
The “tunnel” is a three-year-old investigation into NSE’s decision to sell co-location space to some traders, so the latter could place their servers in the NSE's data centre (every micro or millisecond counts when it comes to stock trading).
 
Once an upstart that challenged the incumbent BSE, NSE today holds an 87% market share. Though it’s not listed, its shares are traded on unofficial or grey markets.
 
IIFL is expectant that NSE will list itself in 12 months. But word on the street is that market regulator SEBI is in no hurry to approve the exchange’s IPO. Some say NSE might just do a "reverse merger” with an already listed commodity exchange, MCX.
 
It’s all crazy. And amazing. And ironic.
 
Because an exchange that decides the valuations and futures of hundreds of companies each day is unable to do the same for itself.

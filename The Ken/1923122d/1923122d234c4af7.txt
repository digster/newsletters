Netflix’s last growth market. How Netflix does tomorrow depends on whether it can succeed and scale in India 
 By Praveen Gopal Krishnan https://the-ken.com/writers/praveenthe-ken-com/

Our story begins in 2022. 

It’s 19 April, and Netflix has just reported its first-quarter earnings for the year. Just a few months earlier, Netflix had given investors a guidance that it expects to gain 2.5 million subscribers in the first quarter. A fairly conservative number. 

In the earnings call, Netflix tells a stunned audience of analysts and investors that, for the first time in its history, Netflix has lost 200,000 subscribers. 

While they are still recovering from the shock, it gives them some worse news—Netflix expects to lose another 2 million subscribers the next quarter.

The company’s stock price crashed 20% following the call. This was the day that those analysts and investors had been dreading. After a decade and more of dominance and acquiring subscribers in every major country, just like Alexander the Great, Netflix had no more lands left to conquer.

So Netflix did what any company would do—it decided to extract more from less. It said it would crack down on users who shared accounts and passwords, and that it would build new ad-supported tiers and reduce spending on content.

It took them two years of toil, but it worked. 

As of yesterday, Netflix’s stock is trading at US$705—an all-time high since it went public. It added 8 million subscribers in this quarter (after adding 9 million odd in the previous one). Almost all of these gains have come from convincing people who had earlier shared accounts and passwords to upgrade and buy.

But now, analysts and investors are worried about how long Netflix can keep this up.

And there’s a growing belief that Netflix’s future rests not in the West, but in one specific country. India. 

Netflix has always believed in India. When Netflix lost subscribers everywhere in that first quarter of 2022, it gained subscribers in Asia Pacific—the largest market of which is India. At one point, Netflix CEO Reed Hastings said that the company’s next 100 million subscribers would be from the country. 

And now, the results are showing. 

In the latest quarter, India became the second largest market for Netflix in terms of paid subscribers additions. 

In many ways, India is now Netflix’s last growth market. 

It holds the keys to Netflix’s future.

In this week’s episode of Two by Two, we discuss what it will take for Netflix to win in India. 

We discuss if it’s even possible, and how it may pan out. 

And we spend 90 minutes doing that by breaking those three words—Last. Growth. Market.

How much does India matter to Netflix? What does winning in India look like? And how will Netflix do it? That’s the episode.

To figure it out, my co-host Rohin Dharmakumar and I sat down with two incredible guests.

First, there’s Kunj Sanghvi (VP Content at KukuFM, ex-COO InsideIIM). In his Linkedin bio, Kunj writes, “A story well told is the most profitable product in the whole world.” He’s one of the few people in India who understands how Indians consume and pay for content.

And true to form, he had some counterintuitive views about Netflix’s content strategy in India. He gave us an instructive breakdown of how Netflix missed wave after wave of content opportunities in India—from crime, horror, fantasy, and much more.

“So after after crime (genre), whether it is Brahmastra Kalki, the Stree Universe, even Tumbbad, which was a sleeper hit, you will see that there is a wave of fantasy rooted on Indian folktales, Indian mythology, and that is a wave that I think Netflix has missed massively. It requires higher investment. The VFX work on that is much more, the kind of talent you need to get that to be successful is much more than a Prajkta Koli (project).

[…]

Horror and fantasy are mirrors of each other. Fantasy is positive things happening in a magical way. Horror is a bad thing happening in a magical way. But essentially, the content base is mythology. There is a character called Ashwatthama in Mahabharatha who has been milked like crazy by this country. Asur, which was a breakout hit for Viacom, for Voot. Kalki which has recently been released. Hidden Hindu, which is a big publishing hit. All of it originates from Ashwatthama and builds a sci-fi or fantasy or horror story on top of it. There is a lot of this stuff that needs to happen in India and needs to be driven by companies that are leaders or want to become leaders in this country.”

Our second guest was Nishad Kenkre (Operating Partner at Verlinvest, Ex-Head of Revenue and Growth at Swiggy Instamart, Ex-Head of Strategy at Disney India). Nishad, by virtue of his experience in media and consumer tech, had some fantastic perspectives about the Indian market and what it means for Netflix.

“Netflix is—what?—US$300–400 million in India on a US$8 billion market, and on their own US$30—40 billion base globally. So India, let’s say for simplification, is one percent of global revenues. That’s small. If you’re going to be one percent five years from now, you have not won in India. It doesn’t matter what your content looks like, whether 10 more seasons of Kapil Sharma have been released.

What Netflix internally will be thinking is, ‘Hey, we want to win in India, which means when we are a 50–60 billion dollar company, maybe five years from now, India should be 5%, perhaps going to 10%. Outgrowing other markets… that’s when you look like you’re winning in India. 

If you do some rough math, you’re talking about taking that US$300 million of today to a US$3–4 billion range. You’re talking about a 10X growth. I think that’s what the path to winning from this point onwards is for Netflix.”

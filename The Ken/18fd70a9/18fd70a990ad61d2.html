The Big Story

Red Bull is the energy drink of the world. How did Pepsico make Sting the energy drink of India?

The FMCG giant’s energy drink is winning over e-rickshaw drivers, school-goers, and the 18–30-year-old crowd in India. And its bottler, Varun Beverages, is the biggest beneficiary

In 2023, Indians consumed 30X the volume of energy drinks they had compared to six years ago. That’s how hot this category is. And Sting, a Pepsico product, which now holds a lion’s share of about 90%, has been driving this boom. With a Rs 20 price tag for each PET bottle, it has unseated its rival Red Bull (sold at Rs 125 a can)—a brand which still leads the pack in countries like the US. At the centre of Sting’s growth is India’s youth, who seem unfazed by the contents of these drinks. [Read the full story here]

Also in the spotlight

At-home blood tests are soaring, and throwing up wrong results

The diagnostics industry is transforming post-Covid, favouring blood-sample collections at your doorsteps. Yet, the reliability hinges on the journey your sample undertakes

The pandemic sparked a home-collection boom, with accredited labs popping up everywhere like mushrooms. Users opt for home-collection for its convenience, ease, and economical pricing, ditching traditional testing methods. But phlebotomists and runners, sometimes inexperienced, hold the keys to a blood-test sample’s fate and accuracy. Since results hinge on proper sample collection and transportation, industry insiders say standards often aren’t met. [Read the full story here]

Big Business

What Mirae did in India that Goldman Sachs, JP Morgan couldn’t

From patient capital to impressive performance, many factors conspired to ensure longevity and rapid growth for the South Korean fund house’s Indian arm. The dream run faces challenges, though

Mirae Asset MF has been a striking exception to the trend of global financial firms calling it quits on the Indian mutual fund market. Not only has it lived to tell the tale, but Mirae has also ranked among the top 10 MFs in India. Without bank backing or corporate tie-ups, Mirae’s success has been driven by parental conviction, product mix, and performance record. But with its flagship schemes underperforming in the past couple of years, its growth momentum could hit roadblocks. [Read the full story here]

Electric moves

Filipinos’ love for an EV catches the government off guard

Filipinos, fed up with unreliable public transport, found a fix in the smaller, cheaper e-trikes. But their widespread adoption has bumped into regulatory hurdles

The Philippine EV revolution stands out because it’s driven neither by cars nor policy incentives. Electric tricycles are soaring in popularity, perfectly meeting the needs of Filipino commuters. For a long time, the government overlooked this growth. The Ken estimates that there are about 165,000 e-trikes in the national capital region alone. Now, the government is struggling to manage this growth, trying to find a balance between oversight and supporting the EV momentum. [Read the full story here]

Atomberg remade the market for fans. Now, it has to fight the one-trick-pony tag

The $450m company wants to replicate its success with fans in the kitchen-appliances segment. But here, disruption looks unlikely

Nine-year-old Atomberg has moved past heavyweights Crompton and Havells to become the market leader in energy-efficient BLDC fans. But with a government mandate last January on star ratings for fans, every brand is now scaling up its BLDC category. Atomberg has already moved on to its “next big thing”, though; it wants to take the spotlight for kitchen appliances. But unlike fans, it will have to rely on more than its BLDC motor to sway consumers towards buying a premium product. [Read the full story here]

Why is Mphasis not operating as a regular IT firm? Ask Blackstone

The listed IT company, largely private equity-backed, has prospered in a low-profile manner. But the investor will eventually need to secure a buyer. 

Mphasis, a 26-year-old mid-cap IT firm, stands out. Unfazed by market turbulence, it is the only listed IT company majorly owned by a PE firm. Blackstone has been driving efficiency boosts at Mphasis, including a gig-work platform and internal P&L tracking. But Mphasis saw revenues plateau last year due to tough market conditions in IT and BFSI, unlike its faster-growing peers. While Mphasis is currently downplaying its status, Blackstone’s eventual exit will demand investor attention. [Read the full story here]

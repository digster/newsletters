Naveen Tewari is the CEO and co-founder of Inmobi and Glance, two of India’s most innovative unicorns.
Ruchi Kalra is the CEO and co-founder of Oxyzo and co-founder of Ofbusiness, both of which are unicorn companies.
There are only two leaders in India who’ve each founded two unicorns, and we were exhilarated to have both of them as panelists for the grand finale. Naveen and Ruchi are leaders who have a track record of creating influential companies in areas where few believed were possible. Among founders in India, they truly understand the transformative ability of US$1 billion, and what it takes to build organisations and companies that sustainably create value over a long period of time.
Aditya Suresh, head of India equity research at Macquarie. His team has authored some of the best reports on quick-commerce in India. In fact, the original case itself had multiple references to reports authored by Aditya and his team.
Prof. Rishikesha Krishnan is the director of IIM Bangalore. He’s also one of India’s leading academics on corporate strategy and innovation, and has more than 200 publications spanning outlets that include refereed journals, the popular press, case studies, book chapters, and books.
Deepak Shenoy is CEO and co-founder of Capitalmind, India’s premier wealth management firm that provides investment portfolios built on rigorous analysis and expertise. Two of three quick-commerce companies in our case are public (with more on the way), so we were excited to have Deepak bring in his perspective as an investor and his expertise on the market side.
At the start of the grand finale, just minutes before the final presentations were about to begin, we invited the five judges to share what they were expecting from the finalists.
Each judge offered their own version of what winning looks like for them.
Ruchi emphasised the need for capital to create “sustainable outcomes” because “a billion dollars is a lot of money”. She added, “as much as we are judges, I’m really looking forward to learning from this competition”.
Naveen echoed the idea of learning from what the top participants had created, but also put the capital in perspective. “How you spend a billion dollars is not written in books… only a dozen people in India’s history have spent that kind of money, so it’s practically uncharted territory.”
Some of the other judges were even more stark in their assessment. “When I heard about quick-commerce, I always wondered how it would ever make money, and I still have that concern…” said Prof. Rishikesha Krishnan. “So I’d love to see how you make quick-commerce profitable and stand on its own feet.”
Deepak agreed. As a public-market investor, he said, “I’ll ask about where’s the money. I’ll care about profits, and will we get that without using the words ‘adjusted’ and ‘ebitda’.”
Aditya took a broader view beyond the numbers. In his words, “there is hypergrowth but there’s also hypercompetition. I’m interested in knowing how participants handled aspects like inflation, rent, delivery riders, and things like that.”
Over the following three hours, 10 teams formed by students from institutes like the IIMs, FMS, XLRI, and the Mesa School of Business each made a 10-minute online presentation to this panel on how to win as a 10-minute delivery company.
Team Metamorphosis emerged as the winner. They crafted a compelling strategy that positioned Bigbasket as a company that was best placed to offer a bouquet of products including Mealkits, quick commerce, and health services. The team offered a compelling vision, backed by solid financials and a clear path to profitability.
This grand finale was the culmination of a long, gruelling contest. In all, 450 teams from 32 colleges registered for the case competition. Nearly 100 teams submitted detailed solutions in the first round. Eventually, 46 teams were longlisted and sent in even more detailed answers to follow-up questions. The solutions were innovative and detailed; some were even outlandish. Ten teams made it to the final round to go head-to-head with each other.

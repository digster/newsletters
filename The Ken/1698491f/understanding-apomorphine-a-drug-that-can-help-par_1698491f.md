This is my colleague Suraksha’s first story for The Ken.
I know she is a bundle of nerves as most diligent reporters are—new place, new people, different editing style, multiple rounds of feedback, first story—but I think she needn’t worry. Minus my bias, we have an important, well-told story for you today.
It is on Apomorphine.
An injectable drug which can help contain the symptoms of Parkinson’s if taken regularly. The drug is rapidly absorbed by the body, providing relief to patients within six to nine minutes. Of course, the drug isn’t new. Across the world, in the United States and Europe, Apomorphine has been in use for two decades, and yet, until recently, Indian Parkinson’s patients couldn’t get it anywhere.
In 2016, over 575,000 Indians were suffering from Parkinson’s. And yes, this is a conservative estimate. Today's numbers are expected to be much higher.
Suraksha’s story travels. From a viral video uploaded by Mumbai-based Nanavati Hospital which oversells the effect of Apomorphine, to understanding what the drug can and can’t do, and the problem of its availability in India still. There are foreign companies who want to manufacture the drug, but there are issues. Price. Size of the market. And efficacy.
Our story sits at the intersection of a drug, life and corporate action.

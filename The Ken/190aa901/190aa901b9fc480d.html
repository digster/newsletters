AI faces a new, unlikely threat—Capitalism. The people with the money are the ones hitting the brakes on AI
By Praveen Gopal Krishnan
8 min read
View in browser

Good Morning Ishan,

Over the last few weeks, something strange has been happening—there’s a rising chorus of scepticism against AI. Interestingly, it’s not from journalists, or social activists, or labour unions, but from an unlikely group: venture capitalists (VCs), investment bankers, and economists. 

Essentially, all of them are saying a version of, “Excuse me—we don’t see how AI makes any sense, at least for the foreseeable future”. 

I mean, long-time readers know that I generally have a dim view of VCs. However, the one thing I’ve learnt over the years is that when VCs (and friends of VCs) say that something is over-hyped, then it’s probably worth paying some attention to. These are the folks whose entire business model is built on hyping an optimistic, amazing, shining future. There’s a reason why VCs, consultants, and banks routinely put out reports and stories that project incredible growth, rising income and consumption, and a wonderful, happy tomorrow where the nation is shining and the world is a happy place. 

And now these guys are the ones saying, “I don’t know about this AI thing…” 

Like I said, it’s worth paying attention. 

Let’s start with Sequoia Capital. 

A couple of weeks ago, David Cahn, one of Sequoia Capital’s partners, published a short report titled ‘AI’s $600B Question’. In it, he argues that there’s a “big gap between the revenue expectations implied by the AI infrastructure build-out, and actual revenue growth in the AI ecosystem, which is also a proxy for end-user value”. Essentially, he’s saying that several companies are spending a lot of money on AI, but nobody seems to be making money off it. Also, the more companies spend on AI, the bigger this gap becomes. 

That’s one way to look at it, sure, but I’d caution you from reading too much into what Cahn is saying. After all, Sequoia is one of the largest and most prominent AI investors globally. In 2023, over 60% of all their new investments were in AI startups. So they have a financial interest in the AI story. I suspect Sequoia is playing all sides of the AI boom—it’s funding companies, but also going around saying, “Hey just be careful okay?” This isn’t even the first time that Sequoia has asked people to calm down and be cautious. They did it in 2020 when Covid-19 struck (turns out they were wrong about this). They’ve also done it in 2022, where they warned founders that the good times were over (they were correct about this, but this was apparent to everyone). My point is, don’t read into what Sequoia is saying as a mea culpa. Sequoia’s motivations are much more complicated. 

But that being said, the substance of what Sequoia is saying is undeniable—who is making money off AI? If you eliminate Nvidia, Microsoft, Open AI (more on them later), then you’ll find that the answer is… nobody. 

In fact, it’s now apparent that the companies that said they were making money off AI were overstating their claim. 

Take Accenture, for example. For most of the year, Accenture has been hyping up the number of Generative AI project “bookings”. As the world’s largest technology services company, Accenture has been a beneficiary of companies approaching it to build and try out things using AI to reduce their costs. In the current fiscal year, Accenture claims to have over US$2 billion in such deals. A year ago, that number was in the low hundred millions. 

Great. Not bad. So Accenture’s revenues must be up?

Well…

IT services provider Accenture lowered its revenue forecast for fiscal year 2024 due to economic uncertainty leading clients to reduce spending on consulting services.

Accenture’s revised outlook on June 20 anticipates full-year revenue growth to be within the range of 1.5 percent to 2.5 percent, compared to its earlier projection of 1 percent to 3 percent. In the first quarter, the world’s largest IT firm had projected the metric in the range of 2 percent to 5 percent.

Randomly scrolling through my news feed last week, I came across this one comment by a politician that, for once, had me completely in agreement. But then, it's not just what he said, but also the way he said it.
It wasn't some sensational strategy or anything. Instead, it was the eccentric prime minister of the UK, Boris Johnson, explaining to a local media outlet why he favoured returning to the workplace. He said, and I quote, "Working from home, you spend an awful lot of time making another cup of coffee and walking very slowly to the fridge to hack off a small piece of cheese, then walking very slowly back to your laptop, and then forgetting what it was you're doing."
We've all done that, haven't we? Wandered off too long into a tea break that only ended at dinner time? I know I have.
Lately, though, I've come to appreciate the productivity that comes with going to an office. I recently began working from the lovely, cosy office we have here at The Ken. Not too often, just twice a week. It's good to see faces that I’ve previously only seen as pixels in Zoom calls.
But, in those two days, I've managed to wrap work on time and slip into the schedule that I have vowed (and failed) to start every week for the last two years. For a moment, it almost felt like a superpower that once existed but had long been forgotten.
Likewise, it has been a productive week for everyone at The Ken, as we explored a myriad of topics across the world of business.
On Monday, I wrote about the dilemma that private companies and their founders are caught in when the latter holds a stake in a venture, and the former expresses interest in getting a slice of the pie. How well they [navigate this conflict](https://the-ken.com/story/what-to-do-when-startups-love-the-same-ventures-as-their-founders/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter) becomes crucial to their companies' futures.
On Tuesday, our contributor Sridhar Dhulipala reflected on the [criticality of designers](https://the-ken.com/story/everyone-wants-to-be-a-designer-what-does-the-designer-get-to-be/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter) to the success of today's businesses. Despite how important they are, the career paths that currently exist for them are quite blurred. Product management is the way forward, he wrote.
Life has come full circle for marquee automakers, noted Jaspreet in Wednesday's story. The high percentage of bad loans on the books of the financing arms of those such as [Hero and Tata](https://the-ken.com/story/hero-tata-and-the-captive-financing-anchor-weighing-down-automakers/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter) has compelled India's financial regulator to nudge them for a cleanup. But, like every cleanup, this comes at a cost.
In a deeply-reported story on Thursday, Pratap dived into Meta's attempts to monetise [WhatsApp Business](https://the-ken.com/story/whatsapp-business-in-india-the-ace-up-metas-sleeve/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter), including a locking in a coveted partnership with government-owned oil and gas producer BPCL to be its official communication channel. Replacing the age-old SMS feature, though, would be no easy task.
On Friday, Prajakta and Anand teamed up for a visually stunning, data-led story on the changing relationship between Indian households and the precious yellow metal. From jewellery shops to spot exchanges, [gold](https://the-ken.com/story/indians-and-gold-old-affair-new-equations/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter) is undergoing a digital makeover.
And that's it from us. I'm looking forward to a restful weekend to keep that productive juices flowing for the week to come. I hope you have one too.

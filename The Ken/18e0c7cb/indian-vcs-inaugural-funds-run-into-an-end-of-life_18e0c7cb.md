Good Morning Ishan,
The second decade of this millennium can easily be called a period when Indian VCs came of age—raised their first domestic funds in the post-Flipkart years and broke into the circuit.
Most of those VCs are nearing the end of their fund cycle, but they find themselves unable to break out of the circuit. Nearly 30 domestic VC firms set up between 2010 and 2014 need to exit their first funds. But their hands are tied. Because, among other things, the regulator, Sebi, insists that the funds stick to their stated timespans.
It’s an emotionally, nah, financially, wrought issue. You can judge for yourself when most of the nine venture capitalists The Ken spoke with said they wanted to stay anonymous. They couldn’t risk commenting publicly and upsetting their investors.
The relationship between VCs and their limited partners is already under strain. The supply of secondaries is anywhere in the US$1.5–2.4 billion range, but few buyers are interested. And unlike the West, there’s no secondaries-only fund operating in India.
Arundhati has a [well-argued story](https://the-ken.com/story/indian-vcs-inaugural-funds-run-into-end-of-lifecycle-battle/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter) on a subject that needs a broader discussion.
I haven’t used ChatGPT at all. Not even tried playing with it when it was first released for public use. As a professional journalist making a living out of writing, I don’t need this tool, though many well-wishers working in this field have suggested I could use it for research. No, thank you.
But I know several people and small companies are deriving real value and productivity gains from generative AI. So, to learn that anyone building even a chatbot for their customer service in India would now need government approval seems like a ChatGPT-type hallucination.
Don’t get me wrong. I get everything about “responsible AI”, and I’d welcome it if the ministry defined use cases where large-scale deployment should be monitored. But for a country with a thriving computer science-led industry, this regulatory overreach is a shackle it can’t afford.

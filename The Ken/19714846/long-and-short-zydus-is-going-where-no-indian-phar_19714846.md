Long and Short
Wed, 28 May 25
The story behind the numbers and motivations driving India's stock market..
Good Morning Ishan,
A Gen Z mindset in a Baby Boomer body.
That might sound like a stretch, but it’s a surprisingly useful way to think about Zydus Lifesciences today—an old-school Indian pharma company with unusually experimental instincts.
The 73-year-old parent from which Zydus branched out in 1995 is unlike any Indian pharma company, especially from that vintage—experimental, global, and defining its unique identity.
While announcing the Q4 FY2025 results last week, Managing Director Sharvil Patel said all its businesses surpassed “our growth expectations”. Full-year revenue grew nearly 19% at Rs 23,241 crore, clocking a 31% rise in Ebitda at Rs 7,058 crore. Patel said the company “added new capabilities to ensure sustainable growth”.
Among the capabilities that the company has been adding over the last few years, the most notable is the French orthopaedic company Amplitude Surgical SA, which it acquired in March. Once the block deal is completed next month, Zydus will have paid €300 million in cash for a 100% ownership of this company, which makes “high-end” products for orthopaedic surgery, mostly related to knees and hips.
Financially, it’s a safe deal; Amplitude’s revenue has been stable at €106 million.
But for a 30-year-old pharma-and-wellness org to diversify into medical technology is a leap of imagination. No Indian pharma major has made that leap, at least not in recent times, which are changing faster than generics makers can find molecules to copy.
Even before Trump trained his gun on Big Pharma [earlier in May](https://www.theguardian.com/business/2025/may/13/drug-price-cuts-what-is-trump-planning-and-what-will-it-mean-for-big-pharma), Zydus chose to wade into the deep end of diversity, without kickboards and floaties.
Older pharma companies today are looking at consumer healthcare, or wellness, as an adjacent area to diversify into. Dr Reddy’s, Cipla, or Sanofi have all made their moves public. Zydus figured this out back in 2008 when it created a separate entity behind brands like Sugarfree and Glucon-D.
In FY2025, consumer health grew by 17.1%, better than the pharma division’s 10.7% growth over the previous year. It’s not just that sweeteners and energy powders make for good business; when done right, wellness products and clinical health are directly related.
(One of the breakthrough findings in neurogenerative diseases like Alzheimer’s came [last week](https://www.thehindu.com/sci-tech/science/does-neurodegeneration-start-when-blood-vessels-are-damaged/article69589975.ece). It showed that what we eat, what infections we carry, and how we medicate ourselves likely decide our protection against these disorders.)
Just as consumer healthcare and wellness is an easier-to-market business than pharma products, medtech approval is less onerous than pharma. The regulatory process is generally less demanding and sometimes offers expedited pathways to the market. Even for higher-risk devices, clinical requirements and timelines are shorter and less costly compared to drug approvals.
Two major certification and approval bodies, the US FDA and the European CE Marking, are usually recognised by most countries. A running joke among Indian medtech startups and founders is that domestic regulators exhibit “reverse racism” by asking for these global certifications to allow selling in the local market.
Medtech is not as big or as fast-growing an industry as global pharma, but it’s steady and recession-proof. The former is growing at [nearly 6%](https://www.biospace.com/press-releases/pharmaceutical-market-size-to-surpass-usd-2-82-trillion-by-2033), the latter is chugging along at about [4% ](https://www.marketsandmarkets.com/PressReleases/global-medtech-industry-outlook.asp)CAGR.
The 139-year-old Johnson & Johnson, the US behemoth that earned $89 billion in 2024, is a good example of how the two streams of businesses can be run under one banner. Incidentally, J&J was born as a medtech company selling surgical supplies. It entered the pharmaceutical business much later, in 1959, after acquiring McNeil Laboratories and Cilag Chemie AG.
Today, only ~35% of J&J’s total revenue comes from medtech.
Coming from the other side into medtech, Zydus has made the most expensive acquisition of its life, that too financing it entirely from internal accruals and borrowings. In his note last week, Sharvil emphasised the focus on “execution”.
Through Amplitude, Zydus will get one manufacturing plant (60–65% utilisation) and one international logistics centre. Apart from the potential global expansion, Zydus also has an opportunity to serve the Indian market, where 80–85% of medtech [is imported](https://the-ken.com/long_and_short/abbott-india-thermo-fisher-and-meril-hit-all-time-highs-then-medtech-madness-struck/).
Its growing appetite for medtech is also evident in the [recent](https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/industry/healthcare/biotech/pharmaceuticals/zydus-working-on-single-pen-device-for-adjustable-semaglutide-doses/articleshow/121347139.cms?utm_source=contentofinterest&utm_medium=text&utm_campaign=cppst) disclosure that it has acquired a “patented pen technology” to accompany the weight loss and diabetes drug, semaglutide, that is going to hit the market next year. Zydus “expects the device to be a key differentiator in the product” next year. And it better be because as one of the semaglutide manufacturers told me, “the semaglutide generic to hit the market next year will be a synthetic drug and more than 10 companies are making it”. It could lead to a glut or a price war.
Making the pen for injectable semaglutide work is one thing. Making a French orthopaedic manufacturing team hum to its business tunes is another. But once again, the pharma company seems to be saying—nothing ventured, nothing gained.

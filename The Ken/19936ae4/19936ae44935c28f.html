I’ve long avoided carbonated drinks like the plague, a habit ingrained by a strict mother whose views on the matter align closely with those of the Indian government. That these fizzy, hissing liquids are a “sin good” like any other—tobacco, gambling—and deserve the highest possible tax (40% GST under the latest regime).

So, as a teenager, my refreshments were restricted to the likes of Amul Cool and Tang, with my only real taste of Coke-Pepsi-Sprite coming during birthday parties of my leniently parented friends. 

Of late, though, I’ve noticed a change. Most of those same friends have shifted to “healthier” no- or low-sugar variants of these drinks. One of them swears by Diet Coke, while another is a staunch Coke Zero adherent. If restaurants don’t have these, they’d rather go without than order regular Coke.

This shift isn’t restricted to upscale eateries in tier-1 cities. It’s happening across the country, courtesy of Coca Cola and Pepsico—spurred by Reliance’s Campa Cola—offering sugar-free variants for Rs 10 a pop. These drinks now make up 55% of Pepsico’s overall sales volumes and 10% of the overall beverage market.

Why have companies pivoted so readily, you ask? Because behind the health claims lies an untapped profit story. Consumers, too, are getting their latest choice of poison at the same price; so good for them, right? Wrong.

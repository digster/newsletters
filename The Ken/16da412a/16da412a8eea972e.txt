Southeast Asian unicorn Grab started out a ride-hailing service, largely in Uber’s mould. Which meant its early operating principles were largely similar to Uber’s. Move fast, break things. Break first, apologise later. Spend fast, raise even faster.

It did that job so well that it ended up acquiring Uber’s Southeast Asia business in 2018, making it the dominant player in the region with a reported valuation of $14 billion. But then came Uber’s dismal IPO, followed by Lyft’s lacklustre one. The ride-hailing business’ charm wheels had come off for good.

That wasn’t all. The investor most impacted by Uber’s depressed valuation was SoftBank. Investors and media the world over are now questioning SoftBank’s strategy of trying to anoint market winners by pouring billions of dollars into a company of its choice. WeWork, whose valuations crashed from $47 billion to $15 billion ahead of a cancelled IPO, had got $10 billion from SoftBank.

Grab has got $5 billion from SoftBank. Awkward.

Meanwhile, with no imminent path to profitability, Grab is now positioning itself as a pan-Southeast Asian commerce infrastructure. In a recently released and self-produced “social impact” report, Grab claims it added $6.5 billion in value to the region in the year ended March 2019. 

This represents a 180-degree turn in Grab’s positioning strategy. From being a disruptive and unpredictable startup in its early days, to being a responsible enabler for local commerce. While the $6.5 billion number Grab claims to have added to Southeast Asian economies may be debatable, it's betting governments will see that it is in their interests to help Grab grow.

In other words, Grab is saying it should be too big to fail.

In today’s story, Jon juxtaposes Grab’s newest PR strategy against its business model, which is still far from being profitable or sustainable.

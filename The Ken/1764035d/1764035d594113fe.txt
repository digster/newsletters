The more we know about the SARS-Cov2, the more remains to be known. All through 2020, doctors have operated with partial knowledge, provisionally content with probabilities. Now, a wave of long-haulers is washing over them as вҖҳrecoveredвҖҷ patients turn up with strange patterns of symptoms.

The number is still not medically definitive, but one-in-three to one-in-two patients suffer ongoing symptoms. They are as varied as they can get. Where chronic fatigue, brain fog, inability to sleep for days have left doctors baffled, more serious after-effects like scarring of lungs, heart attacks, insulin-dependent diabetes, and temporary paralysis have sent them back to tweaking their treatment protocol.

Like a rogue tenant, the virus-infected cells refuse to leave the body, causing lingering symptoms. But doctors still canвҖҷt fathom why Covid adversely affects younger patients, while some patients in their nineties survive and do well. В 

Perhaps too busy with vaccine diplomacy, IndiaвҖҷs health ministry is mum about the long-haul effects of the pandemic. In Maharashtra, though, the Covid-response task force has asked hospitals and doctors to watch out for lungs, cardiac, brain, and hormonal after-effects.

But even these donвҖҷt explain the coma, acute joint aches, and a host of other symptoms that Maitri and Olina masterfully report in this story.

A clinical registry was set up in September to be the national electronic record of every patient passing through the public healthcare system. Another network of 15 centres is meant to collect and analyse patient data through their post-Covid OPDs. However, as the story shows, some of these centres are grappling with their own Covid symptoms and biases.

MumbaiвҖҷs noted pulmonologist Zarir Udwadia likens post-Covid lung fibrosis to a tsunami that follows an earthquake. Are we then setting up deep ocean buoys for its detection? The multiple illuminating stories that Maitri and Olina recount today can at least help us manage our own buoys.

Good Morning Ishan,
 
First, there was the blog post on 16 July. Then came the laudatory articles of the miraculous turnaround. Six days later, on 22 July, came the "Comeback Kid" award.

"The tenacity shown by Snapdeal’s founders — Kunal Bahl and Rohit Bansal — in pulling their company back from the brink of oblivion, was recognised unanimously by the jury as one of the biggest survival stories of the Indian startup ecosystem in recent memory."

The evidence: Bahl said Snapdeal 2.0—the new-and-improved e-commerce firm—saw consolidated revenue surge 73% in FY 2018-19, while losses were slashed by 70%.

Except, not quite.

Over 2017 and 2018, Bahl and Bansal liquidated, hived off or wound down nearly every single company that they'd acquired. 

Those sales doubled Snapdeal's cash reserves. But the selling price in each deal was lower than the buying price, and Snapdeal accounted for the difference as "exceptional items" in FY18 - Rs 470 crore worth of it. Strip this out, and suddenly you have a 32% increase in like-to-like losses in FY19.

And that's not the only veil over Snapdeal's financial statements. Multi-crore “miscellaneous operating expenses" and “trade payables” items have no breakup or explanation. Accounting entries from last year's statement have been completely rejigged this year.

Accounting magic? Sumanth, in our story today, prefers to call it ‘autosarcophagy’ - that's self-cannibalism, or eating oneself. 

But, enough from me. Over to Sumanth for an accounting 101: https://the-ken.com/story/snapdeal-autosarcophagy/ (13-minute read)

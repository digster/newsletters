Good Morning Ishan,
Well over a century after the Titanic sank in the North Atlantic, we may be getting closer than ever to figuring out how exactly it happened.
This is all thanks to a “digital twin” of the wreckage made possible by images captured over six weeks in 2022. The result is incredibly vivid; it’s as if the remains of the Titanic are on land and not 4km below the surface of the ocean.
This whole undertaking seems Herculean, but decoding one of the most infamous events of the 20th century is child’s play compared to truly understanding the human body—particularly its metabolism.
That’s why Jahangir Mohammed and his Mountain View, California-based startup, Twin Health, deserve your attention.
The five-year-old startup has created a Whole Body Digital Twin (WBDT) using data from fitness trackers and a glucose-monitoring patch. The digital twin, which has about 19,000 users in the US and India, has aided type-2 diabetes remission in some and improvements in hypertension and fatty-liver disease in others.
“Silicon Valley is full of consumer-health startups, but we are not a hobby; we are a proper medical service,” said Mohammed, who founded Twin Health with two others. Mohammed had earlier sold his cloud-based IoT startup to IT giant Cisco for US$1.4 billion.
Twin Health, which has raised US$195 million to date, is certainly onto something—which even the American Diabetes Association agrees with. Meanwhile, private-equity giant Blackstone convinced all its 14 portfolio companies to try out Twin Health for their employees, and the results are encouraging.
So, how did Twin Health pull this off? More importantly, how does Mohammed intend to scale a service that costs US$900 in the first year?

It is in the nature of Monday to be busy.
Following up on so many things which need attention. Ones you couldn’t get around to. Ones you must. I respect the privilege that comes with writing this email, more so, on a day like Monday. So I am going to get straight to today’s story by my colleague Sumanth.
We’ve been thinking, discussing. Reading the copious amount of commentary around it and chatting with people. And we finally have a good understanding of Walmart’s acquisition of Flipkart. We believe it is a $20 billion punt by Walmart. Popular opinion points to two drivers. First, that for a retailing behemoth like Walmart, this is a relatively small and cheap bet - a tactical punt that it can take without necessarily taking too much time or effort to decide. Second, Walmart is interested in Flipkart because of the burgeoning potential of e-commerce in India, something that analysts like Morgan Stanley have predicted will grow to $200 billion by the year 2026.
Now, either/both of these points might seem valid on the surface, but unfortunately, they are both palpably wrong. This is the meat of our story.
What Sumanth’s story isn’t is any sort of hagiography of Flipkart’s journey. As far as business goes, he says this is a great outcome. Barely two years back, Flipkart was witnessing wholescale executive leadership changes, steep valuation markdowns by investors and experts penning obituaries saying that the company “had no clue which way to go”. Fast forward to today. Not only has Flipkart successfully managed to hold pole position in India, its leadership has guided the company to the biggest payoff ever for an e-commerce company globally. $16 billion in cash.

Good Morning Ishan,

The operators of India’s largest transportation fleets are hitting the brakes on electric cars.

Uber had planned to have 5,000 EVs on the road by now, but the current count is only 1,800. Ola, the country’s largest ride-hailing company, hasn’t gone all-in. The consequence is that companies like Tata Motors, which fulfilled over 70% of four-wheeler EV sales in India last year, aren’t seeing bulk orders—a key element to drive the industry beyond its nascence. 

Fleet operators remain hesitant about EVs due to a range of factors, from maintenance issues to insufficient infrastructure and changes in subsidy policy. With that said, automakers walk a fine line between serving the demands of large fleets and designing cars that fit the mass market.

After all, who wants to buy a car only to feel like they’re driving their own taxi? Check out Shaswata’s coverage of the matter.

Over in China, the enthusiasm for EVs is only intensifying.

After Xiaomi released its first EV one month ago, it drew fanfare and ire. Critics and disappointed fans of the brand said the car looked like a copy of the Porsche Taycan, suggesting the smartphone maker was falling into its past ways of seeking design inspiration from other brands (chiefly Apple).

On the other hand, some of the 50,000 customers who put down deposits for Xiaomi’s SU7 liked that it projects a sense of accessible luxury with a price tag starting from a relatively-affordable US$30,000.

The SU7 gave Xiaomi a bit of pep in its step, putting it back in the media spotlight at a time when its phone sales are falling—26% down from 2021. More broadly, The company’s move into the EV space reflects a broader convergence of tech brands and automobile design in China, a situation that isn’t happening anywhere else in the world.

Check out Lu’s report on the significance of Xiaomi’s foray into EVs.

Look ma, no hands!

For forward thinkers, EV development is a stepping stone to mass-scale usage of autonomous cars. That was the main item on the agenda when Elon Musk landed in China on Sunday to meet with Premier Li Qiang.

The trip paid off because Tesla has “cleared some key regulatory hurdles” that previously prevented it from making its Full Self-Driving (FSD) software available to customers in China.

Part of the previous blockers was related to map data—a frequent sticking point when overseas companies need to use geographic data in the country.

In Tesla’s case, the solution is an agreement to use Baidu’s mapping license to collect data related to China’s public roads. Notably, the Chinese search-engine operator offers its own robotaxi service, which was first available during the 2022 Winter Olympics in Beijing. 

That doesn’t mean Tesla’s cars will be zooming down Chinese highways with nobody’s hands on the steering wheel anytime soon. There are other matters to resolve, like whether the US-based firm can transfer data collected in China to overseas locations for algorithm training purposes.

Cheaper Chinese rivals are gaining ground in Tesla’s second-largest market. Autonomous vehicles are already on the streets of major Chinese cities. They’re still a novelty, but less so with each passing day. Last year, there were 730,000 trips handled by robotaxis in Wuhan (mostly by Baidu), more than the combined 700,000 in Phoenix, San Francisco, and Los Angeles, according to Alphabet subsidiary Waymo.

By the time Tesla rolls out this feature in its cars, the cachet may already be gone. It’ll be something expected by customers, a “must-have” feature that prompts questions if it isn’t part of the package when one buys a battery-powered car.

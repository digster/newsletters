Zomato doesn’t want to make a big deal of its disruption. Blinkit doesn’t hurt kiranas or Dmart, or so Zomato claims

Good Morning Ishan,

Zomato has changed quite dramatically as a company since its initial public offering. And I’m not just referring to its businesses or its market capitalisation, both of which are now much, much bigger. 

The company thought it had figured out a new way of communicating with investors and analysts. It was, after all, the first of the much-celebrated Indian tech startups to go public in India. But it had to eat humble pie quickly, as I wrote https://the-ken.com/tradetricks/the-taming-of-zomato/ in May 2022: 

First, Zomato did away with the boring presentation most listed companies use to explain their results. Instead, it chose to have engaging commentary https://b.zmtcdn.com/data/file_assets/5a64d0bc5338e69aac53eaaf4d52d2491628607950.pdf—mixed with key data points—from founder and chief executive Deepinder Goyal and chief financial officer Akshant Goyal.

Then, it decided to throw a curveball by ditching https://the-ken.com/bfo/zomatos-not-one-for-quarterly-earnings-calls-what-does-it-mean-for-its-valuation/ the usual quarterly earnings calls with analysts in favour of annual ones. That pissed https://the-ken.com/tradetricks/zomato-and-tesla-have-more-in-common-than-you-think/ analysts off.

And now, in its letter to shareholders released on Monday, Zomato has responded not by agreeing to take questions from analysts every three months. But by asking the questions itself.

Wild, I know.

Still, for all its unconventionality, Zomato is becoming more of a typical listed company than it was when it went public in July 2021.

Zomato became even more typical once it did an 180-degree turn and started doing earnings calls every single quarter. And its letter to shareholders now hews closer to a standard results presentation in the details it offers. 

The one https://b.zmtcdn.com/investor-relations/ea5e8afdd60798d4e7da186a5f2bcd2a_1722506180.pdf it published last week was no different. Blinkit, Zomato’s quick-commerce business, once again took centre stage, doubling the value of the products ordered on its app and inching closer to profitability. 

But what stood out, even though it wasn’t entirely unexpected, was this question: “From which segment of retail are you taking share away as you scale? Kiranas? Or modern retail?” 

The answer, by Blinkit CEO Albinder Dhindsa, was the longest among those in the letter. But allow me to quote the most important part of it:

We know that we are not taking share away from kiranas, or from value focused large retail players like DMart [this was also mentioned in their conference call recently]. The value focused items available in these formats are hard to replicate in our business; especially in categories like staples, where price sensitivity is higher and we don’t have the ability to sell open SKUs that brick and mortar can. Most kiranas also offer personalised commerce – e.g. the khata system for their customers, while we sadly, aren’t able to.

It was only a matter of time before Zomato addressed this. Such has been the pace of growth in quick commerce that everyone wants to know who’s losing if Blinkit, Zepto, and Instamart are winning. (I joined my colleagues Rohin and Praveen on the latest episode of their new podcast, Two by Two https://the-ken.com/podcasts/two-by-two/is-zepto-a-gold-medalist-or-a-bronze-medalist/, to discuss this last week.)

My colleague Aakriti was one of the first to make a case https://the-ken.com/story/blinkit-and-instamart-threaten-kiranas-in-ways-that-bigbazaar-and-amazon-never-could/ for why kiranas need to worry. In April, she wrote: 

According to a survey of 900 retailers in nine cities done by Kirana Club in February, about 40% of them located in Delhi said that their sales dropped in the last one and a half years owing to quick-commerce apps. However, as high as 60% of surveyees in some tier-2 cities like Kanpur and Lucknow felt the impact. All of their sales were hit by 10–30%.

So why is Zomato so sure that Blinkit it’s not hurting kiranas? Maybe it has data that we’re not privy to. But I think it’s not about data at all.

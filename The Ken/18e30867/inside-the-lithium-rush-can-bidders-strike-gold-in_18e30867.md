Good Morning Ishan,
In today’s story, we take you to the heart of Korba—an industrial district in Chhattisgarh.
It is a speck on the Indian map. But, currently, that’s where a high-stakes auction for a lithium block is playing out. Lithium batteries are used in electric vehicles and mobiles; securing access to this resource is essential for India’s energy sustainability and geopolitical goals.
The country is seeing its first lithium block auction, with Reasi (in Jammu and Kashmir) and Katghora (in Korba) among the 20 critical mineral blocks up for grabs.
At present, India imports almost all of its lithium needs. So quite naturally, these auctions are poised to spark investor fervour.
But when Prateek Surisetti, our new contributor, took a trip to the Katghora block to assess its viability, he walked away with a sobering reality. [Here’s his first-hand account—it is a free read](https://the-ken.com/story/inside-the-lithium-rush-can-bidders-strike-gold-in-the-race-to-mine/?utm_source=daily&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter).
The unfolding water crisis has definitely put an end to Bengalureans being insufferably chirpy—what with their Tabebuia blooms and their near-perfect weather.
As its citizens once again scramble to live with this déjà vu-like reality, the water crisis has emerged as a front for another hotly debated topic. The return to work mandate. For the last many months, IT companies have been trying to get their employees to work from the office, with Tata Consultancy Services even issuing a final warning to its employees, last month.
Now, employees have found the perfect opportunity to resist the work-from-office diktat. Instead of appealing to their companies, they are now asking the state government to intervene on their behalf by making the case that if the government mandates work-from-home pandemic-style, it will allow them to go back to their hometowns, reducing the water burden on the state.
This is a bit bizarre because, if anything, working from the office will help people beat their domestic water woes, as it now becomes the companies’ headache to meet the needs of their employees in the office. Also, news reports during the lockdown only show that water consumption during that time went up both in India and other countries.
In any case, there is no escaping this reality wherever employees go. The United Nations has already warned that some areas in the Indo-Gangetic basin in India have already passed the groundwater depletion tipping point, and its entire northwestern region is predicted to experience critically low groundwater availability by 2025.
The sub-text in this appeal is that employees would rather grit through a water crisis at home than go to the office.

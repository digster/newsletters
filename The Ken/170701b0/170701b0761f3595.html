Good Morning Ishan,
 
I spent this Saturday afternoon answering a volley of questions from students of journalism. The seminar room at the Lady Shri Ram College for Women, one of the few colleges in Delhi University that offers an undergraduate programme in journalism, was something out of an archetypal high school Hollywood movie. I am thinking of Dead Poets Society. Not Mean Girls. But that’s just me.
 
Their questions were full of optimism, which was evident in the theme of their annual conference—"Do we Dare: Will Media Change the Universe"—along with the frustrated anxiety about the state of Indian media. Do editors let you write the stories truthfully? Are newsrooms representative of the diversity in this country? The underlying question was: how do we make the world better as journalists after we graduate?
 
Listening to my fellow panelists reminded me of everything that has made me ask these questions over the last decade since I started my career as a journalist. Yes, there are editors who avoid confrontation. There are advertising departments that push their agenda on the editorial team. There are journalists who are sometimes lazy. Overall, the sad fact is that an overwhelming feeling of doom continues to dominate most Indian newsrooms. 
 
It would have been a depressing start to the weekend for the students if I had not told them my story. Yes, I did feel like the character who plays the teacher and shows the way forward in those movies (in this case the twist is that the teacher was once rejected by this same college). Ever since I made the decision to join a fledgling subscription-based publication that was unknown in Delhi three years ago, I bid goodbye to all those problems. Unlike my peers in that panel who are fighting all-too-common battles everyday, I could proudly say that healthcare is a beat; people from diverse religions, regions and genders are represented in the newsroom; and we have no advertisers, neither government nor businesses.
 
My answers to the students’ questions were on the same lines as the lessons in The Ken's stories this week.
 
For instance, Olina, whose alma mater had hosted the conference, on Monday wrote a story about taking an ambitious risk. Ola Electric, part of the largest Indian ride-hailing service Ola, is stepping out of its comfort zone to build electric three-wheeler autos and lithium ion batteries. The company is already conducting a pilot in Gurugram, where it set up a battery-swapping kiosk for e-rickshaws. She argued that it may just find success.
 
But not all battles in the war are won. So ‘be patient’ was the message in Abinaya’s story on Tuesday. Six months after the #logout campaign by Indian restaurants, aggregators like Zomato and Swiggy are unhurt. Just like suppliers in the online travel and e-commerce marketplaces, restaurants are preparing for another battle. They are looking for government intervention to set ground rules for the food tech industry.
 
Some dreams do come true, argued Vandana’s midweek story on Cashify. The smartphone recycler swimming against the tide to create demand for used devices has fixed supply, raised $24 million from venture capitalists, and plans to go international. Cashify seems primed to be an Indian re-commerce success in a country where e-waste is hardly recycled. 
 
After consistently flagging the flaws in India’s second-largest startup in terms of valuation, OYO, Thursday was Sumanth’s I-told-you-so moment. Can OYO, which has been forced to abandon its grow-at-all-costs mantra to build a “sustainable path towards profitability” progress towards this holy grail, he asked. This was our free story of the week.
 
The week ended with Jum’s story that is an example of what all the editors at The Ken push us to accomplish—tell the story in the most engaging way; respect the readers' time and attention. It is a story about the first unicorn in the Philippines—Revolution Precrafted, which claims to sell luxury prefabricated homes. Turns out, to date, there’s little evidence that Revolution Precrafted has delivered a single prefab home to the market.
 
The last question a student asked the panel of working journalists was: how important is it that readers subscribe to read the stories we write? You know the answer to that question. It is you, the subscriber, who makes such newsrooms possible.
 
Have a great Sunday.

Once upon a time, in 2011, three friends started a company called TravelTriangle.

As time went on, TravelTriangle became one of the leaders in the online holiday packages market. It raised money from a clutch of well-known investors like SAIF Partners, Bessemer, and The Fundamentum Partnership. Revenue was rising; losses were increasing; one founder left active involvement a couple of years ago—all as startuppy as it gets.

So far, so fairy tale.

Then, around a year ago, everything started to unravel.

TravelTriangle went to the market to raise more money. Except that despite a lot of interest, no investor signed on the dotted line. Apparently, the company had a high rate of cash burn. A startup that blowtorched through cash—who ever heard of such a thing?

Anyhow, the existing investors first got mad, then pumped in money themselves to complete the funding round. The investors’ stake in the company rose. And then they vented their anger, triggering a founders vs investors corporate war.

The goings-on in TravelTriangle since then are nothing short of a courtroom drama. Literally, in some cases.

Sample this: the founder-CEO was to be fired last year > the courts stopped that > then he was fired last month > and unfired > is still CEO, but with 0 people reporting to him and a ridiculous cost-cutting target.

Well, that’s one version anyway. The investors have a different version. Oh, then along came the pandemic to add its another twist to the tale.

Vandana’s extraordinary story today is about how Murphy’s Law came to roost at TravelTriangle. It’s a story of power struggle, corporate politics, and its fallout.

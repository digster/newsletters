Things in edtech have changed too—some drastically, others more inconspicuously. Now, there’s nothing inconspicuous about marketing budgets and big-name celebrities endorsing products, but I must admit I’ve completely ignored one aspect about the great Indian edtech downfall so far: the slow ebbing away of edtech ads on TV, print, and online channels. Brand ambassadors such as Bollywood actors Shah Rukh Khan, Amir Khan, and Vicky Kaushal have decamped to other products. Wolf Gupta https://www.thenewsminute.com/atom/who-wolf-gupta-story-behind-whitehat-jr-s-fictional-prize-student-138382 has exited, stage right.

Storyboard 18 reports https://www.storyboard18.com/advertising/beyond-byjus-indian-ed-tech-players-ditch-glamour-for-smart-cost-effective-advertising-60175.htm that most marquee edtech brands slashed their marketing budgets drastically once the Covid user surge ended:

According to the FY24 filings of the ed-tech startups, Unacademy reduced its marketing and promotional spending by 33% to Rs 201.3 crore in FY24 from Rs 293.4 crore spent in FY23. Additionally, PhysicsWallah’s ad spend stood at Rs 37.3 crore, Simplilearn’s marketing and promotional expenses were Rs 204.79 crore, Vedantu’s ad expenses were Rs 23 crore, and upGrad’s were Rs 340 crore in FY24, respectively.

Byju’s, once the poster child of India’s edtech industry, spent over Rs 8,000 crore between FY16 and FY22 on advertisement and promotions.

The story goes on to report how Byju’s ad spends upset the digital apple cart for all its competitors back then. Byju’s outspent its rivals by a mile—2–3X more than industry standards to book ad slots, often blowing up more money in a quarter than other edtechs did in a year. Its spending went international when Byju’s expanded to the US. Its leadership speaking at big-ticket conferences became par for the course, as did brand deals with football legends.

Of course, all the buzz around Byju’s eventually evaporated. As was the case with Vedantu’s sponsorship of the popular game show Kaun Banega Crorepati, and Unacademy’s high-profile VFX ad featuring M S Dhoni. 

The vanity metrics era is over. What’s next?

From smaller corners of the world

There’s still no consensus on what exactly success metrics look like in edtech, unless we’re talking about test-prep. Even there, I believe metrics should evolve beyond All India Ranks, but that system is too entrenched to overhaul in a jiffy.

Where we should turn our attention to is K-12, or school edtech. A project mostly abandoned by the well-funded, or still-funded, edtechs, with the exception of companies like LEAD School. These metrics, as I pointed out in a previous edition, are slightly hard to determine at a universal level. But if edtech is really to be part of schooling, someone has to figure out what that looks like.

It will have to be based on evidence of improving student performance, year-on-year. That’s not an easy metric to capture, but some edtechs are trying. Namely, Filo.

The one-to-one tutoring platform where students can access help and doubt solving on demand recently commissioned a group within Johns Hopkins University to study the effects https://jscholarship.library.jhu.edu/server/api/core/bitstreams/d69d7520-2e82-4a0f-b12d-a481d659865c/content on students’ mathematics and English scores after they’d used Filo’s online product. 

Published this February, the study shows a positive correlation between student achievements and Filo’s use. The study wasn’t based on Indian students though—it was done on about 1,000 students across 38 schools in the state of Alabama in the US.

The study looks at two parameters: one, did students who used Filo improve more than counterparts who didn’t? And did more usage—more minutes spent on the app—lead to better outcomes?

In both cases, the study claims there was a positive correlation but not a significant change in performance when students used Filo. More crucially though, male and Black students—with Black students being a majority subset in the sample—showed improvement. The study highlights this as significant:

“Given that both subgroups often underperform in many school districts, these findings suggest the potential of the program to demonstrate significant overall impacts in future studies. Quite possibly, Filo tutors and district practitioners have insights they could share with program leaders regarding the positive outcomes with these two subgroups, and whether any particular effective practices used with them might be transferred to students in other subgroups.”

The Filo study is small, and was commissioned by the edtech itself. But it does provide a window into how more edtechs can start building evidence for their products and services, and use data to tweak what they’re offering to schools. 

It’s a far cry from the spending on ad spots and celebrities, but it’s probably disproportionately cheaper to gain influence this way. The question is, who’s going to take the plunge? Will investors pay for these kinds of metric evaluations? Or will they stick to the old game? If someone’s told you, I’m all ears!

Imagine this: you live in a large apartment complex, and you tell the residents about a fancy new mall in town.

One that’s quite different from those they are used to. One where the rules of the old malls don’t apply. One where they will be able to choose from a wider variety of sellers.

But they are in for a shock when they actually visit the mall. Forget having more choices than at a regular mall; there’s just a handful of sad-looking stores selling few things they’re actually interested in.

Walmart-owned Phonepe is in the same position with its app for India’s open e-commerce platform, ONDC, as them. After a three-month pilot in Bengaluru, Pincode is now in nine other cities. But there are just 6,000-10,000 orders daily.

Not the kind of numbers one would have expected after all the fanfare around Pincode’s launch. You can’t blame Phonepe, though. Its rival Paytm, which has been on ONDC longer, has also seen orders stagnate, and the reason for their woes is the same.

Phonepe and Paytm thought they would bring the buyers and others like them would get the sellers, and it would be a match made in heaven. But save Zomato-backed Magicpin, there are few credible or sizeable aggregators bringing brick-and-mortar stores online for ONDC.

So Phonepe and Paytm now want to roll up their sleeves and corral sellers themselves. After all, they both have a network of 30-40 million merchants thanks to their payments business.

But there’s a reason why too many companies haven’t bothered themselves with sellers: it’s very, very hard.

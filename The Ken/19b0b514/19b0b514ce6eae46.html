“Doodh, doodh… Piyo glassful doodh.” This endearing ad from the 1990s extolling the virtues of a glassful of milk took the fight to the cola majors. But in a country that is a world leader in milk production, getting that milk to the glasses of the millions of Indians remains a fight for most farmers even today. Nearly three-fourths of the 80 million farmers pouring milk into village-level collection centres are unorganised, unwittingly milking their livelihood and cattle to the point of self-defeat. It reflects a patchwork of peaks, plateaus and plunges.

Trying to solve for this, and with a good measure of success, over the past eight years, is an unexpected candidate—a veteran money manager. In an earlier life before he founded Nitara, Manish Jain was an investment banker, founded a private-equity firm, and also set up a portfolio management service. Safe to say he understood the daunting risks-reward paradigm of his new baby. But the thing is, not everything is weighed on the scales of risk and return, in the commercial sense of the terms. As Jain told Seema, The Ken’s editor in today’s insightful Counterthesis, “When I feel overwhelmed, I tell myself, if not 80 million, 10 million, if not 10 million, then 1 million. I have just one life to make a difference.”

Nitara means deep roots—a seemingly apt name for an entity that seeks solutions in the roots of problems. “Listen to the farm, not the farmer,” is just one of Jain’s many unconventional takes to improve productivity in India’s $30 billion dairy industry. Data, digitisation, genetics, nutrition, management—a lot goes behind the farmer-to-friend (F2F) approach of Nitara that claims to have mapped the entire gene pool of cattle in India. From dairy Hatsun to software major Infosys, Jain has drawn inspiration from a disparate bunch to try and deliver solutions focused on the farmer and the cow.

Don’t miss this deep dive on how Jain aims to fix a dysfunctional dairy system from the bottom up.

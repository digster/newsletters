Good Morning Ishan,

This week, India celebrated its 75th Republic Day. While it’s a national event, we also have our tiny little celebration at home. With my grandfather having dedicated almost two decades to the Indian Air Force, he instilled the tradition early on, ensuring that every year, all the kids in the house gather around the television to watch the parade.

For some of us, the temptation to wake up in Delhi’s chilly winter mornings was witnessing the acrobatics displays by IAF pilots, for others, the bait was the breakfast comprising samosas and laddoo. Regardless of the motivation, those were delightful moments that the entire family came together to celebrate. 

Now, years have passed, and distances separate us in different cities. But we still make sure to get on a video call to reminisce about samosas and our grandfather’s cool traditions.

Over at The Ken, too, we have made it a monthly custom to publish two standout Big Stories.

The first one delves into Domino’s—a global pizza brand that has long enjoyed a free run in India but is now seeing a loss in momentum. Blame it on the entry of other QSR categories, the increase in competition from niche pizza players and/or the might of aggregators. In this story, I argue why Jubilant Foodworks, the brand’s local franchisee, is no longer undisputed.

In the second one, Nuha wrote about Walmart-backed Flipkart, one of the largest e-commerce companies in India with IPO dreams. Now, all of this comes at a cost, which, in Flipkart’s case, is equivalent to a bruised and demotivated place of work under the CEO Kalyan Krishnamurthy. (Don’t miss Aishwarya’s graphic capturing the journey of its long-delayed public listing)

Earlier in the week, Olina wrote a newsletter about Kota—India’s coaching capital—which recently came under the Indian government’s radar. Guidelines for coaching institutions have been laid down under which they would no longer be allowed to enrol students under 16. This is a welcome move considering how the pressure-cooker-like situation the younger lot faces daily. The move will surely involve lobbying efforts from coaching institutions, she argues, considering how a lot of VC money has poured in.

Seetharaman highlighted the recent struggles of Hindustan Unilever, which is facing negative sales growth in Q3. It’s cutting the margins of its distributors to protect its growth, being challenged by regional/niche players. The newsletter underscores the impact of current market conditions on India’s FMCG colossus.

On Thursday, in Strait Up, my colleagues Brady and Katrina wrote about what’s brewing in the east and the west of the South China Sea (in Singapore and the Philippines). What particularly struck me was the story about a hard call taken by Chua Soon Hock, a veteran fund manager from Singapore, whose fund Asia Genesis Macro Fund was hit by a meltdown in China’s market. The fund manager decided to close the fund and, in fact, return the money that had been left to its limited partners. That’s a rare move when fund managers are egotistical about accepting defeats.

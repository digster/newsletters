“Too many efforts to help India’s MSMEs approached things from the wrong direction. They tried to fix demand-side problems. They didn’t try to understand what small factories actually needed.” 

That was Neal Thakkar, founder and CEO of Magma, a B2B industrial solutions provider and supply-chain enabler, talking about why India’s small businesses still struggled with basic supply-chain issues.

“MSME empowerment” has been a political catchphrase for decades now, but despite all the rhetoric—and a bunch of schemes (some relatively successful, some non-starters from day one)—India’s small businesses still struggle with many of the same challenges they did a couple of decades ago. A cursory google search will show you the same tired phrases peppered in headlines across the years: lack of adequate credit access; barriers to new markets; delayed payments and working-capital crunches. 

Some of that, at least, is partly due to the informal frameworks that much of India’s MSME ecosystem operates on. Too many of the country’s small businesses still rely on casual word-of-mouth networks to cobble together their supply chains and clients. At any point, this could mean a rotating cast of dozens of vendors, and intermittent, unreliable buyers—resulting in inconsistent cash flows, and the everpresent risk of sudden working-capital shortages. 

Several reports, including one released this summer by the Niti Aayog, recommend streamlining this tangled web and integrating it with the help of technology to boost India’s MSME sector into a higher orbit. 

That there is a large gap is obvious. As is the opportunity. 

And some platforms like Magma are finally starting to take it.

Flipping the perspective

Manufacturing integration is nothing new, scores of businesses have been devising solutions in the space for years. But Thakkar says Magma, founded in 2022, began with the understanding that the country’s smaller manufacturers were just not ready for any potential “China+1” shift by global manufacturers into India. 

Most were not even ready for the kind of reliability that large domestic manufacturers expected from their vendors. 

So first, India’s small factories needed to become “competent partners”. 

Thakkar says Magma took a cue from India’s largest industrial conglomerates and chose to plant its flag firmly in one region first: Gujarat, home to over 3,000 factories. And in one industry: ceramics. 

Fast forward three years, and it closed FY25 with a revenue of Rs 215 crore and nearly 400 companies on its platform, according to Thakkar, posting a profit every year since its launch. 

Magma’s pitch to MSMEs was simple: “a full-stack factory solution”. 

First, it would provide them with fully customised raw material supply—not just commodities but specialty chemicals and powders made to each business’ specific requirements, without a minimum order condition. Second, it would meet any green-energy requirements through its industrial gas and biomass unit, helping factories comply with regulatory mandates while reducing costs. And third, it would collect and recycle waste from these same factories, creating a circular economy where waste-collection payments often offset raw material costs.

It would also, critically, help them with working capital and facilitate relationships with other enterprises in the market for their products, including large conglomerates. One source aware of Magma’s operations told me that it offers a 30-day credit period for companies buying on its platform, but pays the suppliers immediately. 

Magma’s focus on small businesses was no accident. While large enterprises could solve supply-chain problems by setting up captive facilities, MSMEs had their hands tied.

“If a Reliance or Adani needs customisation they can easily spend Rs 20 crore out of their pocket and set up their own facilities and supply.”

Neal Thakkar, founder and CEO of Magma Industrials

Small businesses didn’t just need point solutions, they needed fully integrated partners who could help them handle procurement, lead generation, and recycling. 

Take the ceramics industry. Sector heavyweights like Kajaria do massive business (it made Rs 4,218 crore in revenue in FY25), but there are around 700 small factories that are involved, at least intermittently, in meeting Kajaria’s requirements. Often, these small units end up with excess capacity they can’t utilise, or excess production they cannot find buyers for. Without an integrated partner like Magma, they would end up buying from 20 different vendors, selling to assorted buyers, dealing with different credit cycles, and purchasing tons of unnecessary raw material because of minimum-order conditions. 

Magma helps small factories on its platform reduce this complexity to manageable levels. And once it is done “upgrading” them through its “interventions”, it categorises them as “Magma-powered factories”, which serves as sort of a quality certification for larger enterprise clients on its marketplace looking for dependable suppliers. 

Magma currently has 350 such factories on its platform, and 48 suppliers working exclusively for them. And big names like Reliance, Adani, Kajaria, Adicon, Simpolo already procure products from factories in its network.

“My mill produces around 1,500 tons of paper every day. I require waste paper that needs to be recycled for this business, and almost 900 tons of the waste paper is provided to me by Magma. Earlier, I used to work with multiple vendors, which was a hassle, but this last year and a half, Magma has simplified and helped me streamline the process a lot better.”

Jaimin Patel, owner and marketer of Usons Paper Mills

A question of scale

After the first year or so cutting its teeth in ceramics, Magma expanded into other sectors such as sanitary ware, polymer products, packaging, adhesives, and construction materials. Currently, while 30% of its business still comes from ceramics, the rest is split among other manufacturing categories. 

Thakkar says that despite the challenges, working with many small factories beats working with a few large ones—more customers means less risk. “Better to have Rs 20 crore receivables from 50 customers, than one,” he told me. 

But what next? 

Will its integration model, which has proven itself in one region and some 400-odd factories, work if it is scaled up to 4,000 factories? Or 40,000 factories, which is more along the lines of what India actually needs? 

It seems to have convinced some investors of the potential, at least. 

Magma has raised some $8 million from the likes of WEH Ventures, Titan Capital, and General Catalyst. General Catalyst, which has bet on companies like Anthropic, Spinny, and Indrive, usually cuts large cheques, but has come in fairly early in Magma’s case. Incidentally, General Catalyst led one of India’s largest seed funding rounds this May in PB Health, the Policy Bazaar group’s new healthcare arm. 

For its part, Magma is already diversifying out of Gujarat, with 40% of its operations now spanning at least eight states, including factories in Maharashtra, Hyderabad, and Tamil Nadu, and suppliers in Goa and Odisha. But each region and industrial cluster comes with its own unique challenges and requirements; what works for ceramics in Gujarat doesn’t work for paper in Tamil Nadu. 

And Magma, of course, isn’t the only business trying to exploit the MSME opportunity. It competes in certain areas with well-funded players like Procmart, which recently raised $30 million from Nandan Nilekani’s Fundamentum. Groyyo, which The Ken recently wrote about, is another company that is trying to gain an edge by working directly with MSMEs in the apparel sector, banking on similar kinds of supply-chain integration to solve similar problems. 

The initial signs are hopeful. 

And India certainly won’t complain if private sector-led integration kicks off a long-overdue MSME revolution.

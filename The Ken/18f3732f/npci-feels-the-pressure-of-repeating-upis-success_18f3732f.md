Good Morning Ishan,
Uneasy lies the head that wears a crown.
Having tasted runaway success with its payments product UPI, India’s quasi-payments body, the National Payments Council of India (NPCI), is under tremendous pressure to keep laying the golden egg.
Since its launch in 2016, NPCI has been steadily releasing features around UPI and other products to keep the fintech world humming. There’s no dearth of high-profile people wanting to bask in the glory of this Digital Public Infra, including the highest public office in the country.
April 2024 witnessed nearly 13 billion UPI transactions worth more than Rs 19 lakh crore. In terms of volume, UPI alone accounts for over 80% of overall digital transactions in the country.
What made UPI so successful was both organic and inorganic. An encore cannot be engineered at will. But thanks to political compulsions, NPCI is expected to do exactly that. As one executive summed it up: “The expectation is to keep building something new and shiny in very little time…there is no clarity [among employees] on the specifics of what NPCI is chasing.”
Rounak has a riveting read today. And he says it all began in September 2022 after Dilip Asbe, NPCI’s chief executive was reappointed as the CEO and MD for the next five years. In the months that followed, Asbe attended a series of closed-door meetings with officials from the Ministry of Finance, the Ministry of Electronics and Information Technology (MeitY), and the Reserve Bank of India (RBI).

Astrazeneca’s court admission shouldn’t be a distraction
We have a tendency in India to get distracted easily. Politicians know this well and use it effectively. Astrazeneca’s admission in the UK court that its Covishield vaccine can cause a rare case of blood clotting is, in many ways, water down the bridge for Indians.
Clinician researchers say not only is the risk rare but it’s high only in the first few weeks after the vaccination. It’s been a long time since most Indians had their second or third shot. We are long past the risk now.
The vaccine we should be discussing now is Hepatitis-B as cases of viral Hepatitis are on the rise both in India and globally. Today, Kerala is dealing with a big outbreak of Hepatitis-A, which is water- and food-borne and can be controlled accordingly. Hep-B can be prevented by vaccination at birth (or any time for adults if they missed it in their childhood).
And unlike Hep-B, Hep-C is curable. Incidentally, 2024 is the 10th anniversary of the miracle Hep-C drug, which once propelled India into international fame for making inexpensive generics when the drug sofosbuvir (brand name Sovaldi, by Gilead) sold for $1,000 a pill in regulated markets. People travelled to India to buy the drug.
The World Health Organisation has called for Hep-B and Hep-C eradication by 2030. India has both the means and the drugs to achieve this on its own.

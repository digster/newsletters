One of the things I love most about reading business stories is spotting the parallels between seemingly unrelated industries. 

Take for instance the venture capital-fuelled trend of “rollup e-commerce” (like Thrasio in the US or Mensa Brands in India). It’s when a company flush with venture capital starts buying up many, often dozens, of independent e-commerce brands with the goal to “roll them up” into a sum that’s larger than the parts. How? By unifying things like marketing, customer support, tech infrastructure, analytics etc. so that they’re done more efficiently and cheaply across a large set of businesses. Individual entrepreneurs who sell out like the cash they get up front, while also getting to continue running their businesses.
 
It’s no different from how private equity funds operate when they back one company in a sector with cash, so that it can then acquire others to “roll up” into a sum that’s larger than the parts. 
 
Today’s fascinating story from Maitri Porecha and Seema Singh chronicles how this is happening in India’s massive eye care sector. 
 
Jodhpur-based ASG Eye Hospital seems to be leading the pack when in July, it raised Rs 1,500 crore ($187.5 million) from private-equity (PE) investors General Atlantic and Kedaara Capital. It’s evident that part of this money is to finance the buyout of Vasan Eye Care, which has been under insolvency proceedings since 2017.
 
One of the companies that lost the bidding war for Vasan Eye Care was the listed company Dr Agarwal’s. It has raised a substantial Rs 1,050 crore ($131 million) from PE firm TPG Growth in its private arm, Dr Agarwal’s Health Care Ltd. Like ASG, Dr Agarwal’s has been looking to acquire standalone clinics or small nursing homes to build a larger eye-care chain. 
 
That’s the buyer side of rollup eye care. 
 
On the seller side, eye doctors with small independent practices are looking for connections to PE-backed buyers. Selling a private practice to an investor-backed chain means receiving money upfront, a seat at the decision-making table, and handsome rent in case the building is owned by the doctor. 
 
Meanwhile the carrot that the buyers dangle is “common marketing to increase the patient footfall” and “money to invest in new equipment.”

Why couldn’t Stripe become the Stripe of India? It's the world’s most powerful fintech, and yet, it failed to capitalise on the world’s second-largest startup market
By Praveen Gopal Krishnan
9 min read
View in browser

Good Morning Ishan,

“There are some fundamental differences between India and the US. 

I think everybody knows it. It’s obvious, but just to state it explicitly, on most metrics, we are one-tenth the size of the US. Online commerce. Digital payments. All one-tenth the size of the US market. 

And if you look at monetisation, it gets even worse.”

That’s Vikram Bhat, formerly Chief Product Officer at Setu. 

It’s partly his theory of why Stripe couldn’t make a dent in India. 

I’m listening because Vikram understands startups, the internet, and most importantly, products. Here’s a (non-exhaustive) list of companies where Vikram was the CPO of: Good Worker. Capillary Technologies. Lendingkart. Ekstep Foundation. Aditya Birla Online Fashion. He’s been at the apex of product management for over a decade. 

I press Vikram further. 

India being a smaller market didn’t stop similar companies, like Amazon, or Uber, or Apple, or Google. All global, well-funded, powerful companies who made India a priority, despite its size, have seen some success. 

What did Stripe miss?

“Stripe kind of forgot how it grew initially. When it started, Stripe’s founders were the ones who were actually going and trying to identify the growing segments of the market.

“It doesn’t look like they followed that playbook when they started in India, because I think if they had applied the same playbook, it would have been clear to them that India is the next biggest growing market for startups.”

I’m still unconvinced. 

Stripe, the world’s mightiest fintech, currently valued at US$70 billion (and at US$95 billion at its peak), could not make a dent in India. It spent seven years trying. It had a great product, a massive untapped opportunity, and didn’t have much competition. 

And yet, it failed. Why?

There’s an internet quip that was quite popular until recently. The Amazon of China is Alibaba, the Uber of China is Didi, the Google of China is Baidu, the Apple of China is Xiaomi. In India, the thinking was: the Amazon of India is Amazon, the Uber of India is Uber, the Google of India is Google, and the Apple of India is Apple. 

In this week’s episode of Two by Two, The Ken’s latest business podcast, we discuss why Stripe couldn’t become the Stripe of India.

And to find the answer, my co-host Rohin and I are also fortunate to be joined by Arundhati Ramanathan, The Ken’s Deputy Editor. 

Arundhati has a lot to say about why Stripe lost. She would, because she has seen it up close. Arundhati has written over two hundred stories at The Ken, and is one of India’s preeminent journalists. She’s documented the rise, stumbles, and secrets of India’s fintech sector all the way from 2016. She began as a young, rookie fintech reporter when demonetisation struck, and in many ways, her career parallels the rise of digital payments. I feel fortunate because the day we’re recording is also Arundhati’s birthday. 

I ask Arundhati to tell me what her reporting told her about why Stripe lost in India. She mentions that regulation played a big role.

She has three stories to tell about three regulatory blows.

The three blows against Stripe India

Blow 1: India’s digital payments switched from cards to UPI in 2017.

Stripe is great with cards. They are primarily a “card” company. But India doesn’t have a single payment mechanism. According to Arundhati, “Stockbroking needed more UPI and e-mandates. Foodtech relied on UPI and credit cards. Insurance wanted net banking and debit cards. Even within e-commerce, preferences varied—customers used UPI or Pay Later options for low-value items and EMI for high-value ones.”

Blow 2: Then came localisation.

In April 2018, the Reserve Bank of India (RBI) mandated that all companies store a copy of their customer data within the country, giving time until October of that year to comply. Stripe took 18 months.

Many companies raced to complete things before the deadline. Mastercard couldn’t onboard new customers until it complied. Stripe was banned from launching a UPI integration, even though it had the product ready. 

Stripe India privately said they could have done it in six months if they had a local team. 

But complying required Stripe to redo its entire payments architecture. Ground up. It took 300 engineers, and they did it because they had seen signs that other countries like Turkey and Indonesia may go the same way regulation-wise. 

In one of Arundhati’s stories, a senior Stripe executive in India says, “To their credit, Stripe disrupted its global product roadmap to make way for India.” The irony, however, was that when Stripe could finally add its UPI product to the mix in 2021, it didn’t have a market to launch it in.

Blow 3: Payment aggregator licence.

Then, in July 2022, the RBI started giving out payment aggregator licences in India because they wanted payment aggregators to “own and take responsibility”. Stripe was the first foreign payments company to acquire one. In the aftermath, merchant onboarding was even paused for a while for players like Razorpay, Cashfree, Paytm*, PayU. While media reports said that Stripe had also received a suggestion from the central bank to pause onboarding, the company denied it. 

Despite this, Stripe could not take advantage of this situation. 

The straw that finally broke the camel’s back came when the RBI started insisting that payment aggregators spend time and money doing physical merchant KYC—a move that may slow merchant onboarding by 90%, according to experts.

Here’s Arundhati.

“Stripe could have been the Stripe of India. Because back then, the online payment aggregator system or the ability to accept payments, which is what these companies do, was still not where it is today. India was still net banking-focused, card-focused. A lot like the US, where cards are predominant, here too, cards were a large part of how one would make payments. And back then, you had Billdesk, which was still utility-focused, and Citruspay, which was still this scrappy startup, and a few more, like CCAvenue.

Stripe did have the right to win India and the payment aggregator space.

Their strategy was to grow with startups, identify some of those high-growth companies, and kind of latch on to them, like what it did with Shopify, Uber. Even before they officially came to India, they were letting companies do cross-border payments. So that’s how some of the merchants, like say the crowdfunding platform Ketto, was already working with them. So they thought that they could work with the global startups that were going to set up in India, and take those relationships and grow in India. But I don’t think it panned out that way.”

And then we come to the question: how does Stripe US look at Stripe India as a business opportunity?

Stripe had local engineering talent that could build the payment products locally, but they didn’t have autonomy. “Central teams in San Francisco worked on core tech required for products needed for, say, auditing funds coming in or making refunds. It created too much dependency,” says the second former Stripe executive quoted in Arundhati’s story from July 2022.

Stripe followed this approach because it wanted to drag and drop new payment methods into the countries it entered. In doing so, it missed the country that was on the cusp of changing the way people made digital payments. 

“Our point of contention was that we want enough resources to build everything at once, but the leadership at HQ’s point was that we can’t allocate 30 resources without knowing what the returns on that are going to be,” a former executive from Stripe India says in the story. 

Stripe went down this loop of “get revenue to get resources, but get resources to get revenue”. 

Chicken and egg.

“For a local startup, a US$2–5 billion opportunity is good enough to chase. But for a US$100 billion-company like Stripe, it needs at least a US$10 billion opportunity to make it worth the while,” a payments company CEO says in the same story. 

And perhaps that’s the biggest reason why Stripe couldn’t become the Stripe of India.

“India is not an anomaly for Stripe”

And Rohin, my co-host, takes it even further, saying Stripe’s journey in India might be coming full-circle, with them scaling back their operations and going back to the invite-only model with which they first entered the country in 2017.

But things are bound to change and Stripe might have to roll with it.

“This fact, that Stripe seems to be retreating from India, seems to be retreating from Indonesia. 

For me, that’s both an existential threat and an opportunity. I see that the internet that Stripe built itself for is fundamentally being changed due to various reasons.

First, of course, there’s a splintering of the internet into various geographies, and each country imposing its own rules, laws, etc. That’s the new reality, it’s only going to become worse.

So, if Stripe thinks it can continue doing what it did because the world will stay like that, that’s not happening.

Second, what India represents is not a market that can’t be cracked. But essentially, what’s again going to happen to various financial markets around the world. 

Which is the regulators and the central banks working very closely to cut down the fees for payments to offer real-time payment settlements to enforce stronger regulations.

So if you think that India was an anomaly, India is not an anomaly.

In fact, UPI is being seen as a model in various countries around the world.”

So maybe what got Stripe here won’t be the thing that fuels its growth going forward in other markets as well.

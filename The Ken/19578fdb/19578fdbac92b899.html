In the spotlight

ICICI Prudential MF’s investment chief warned of a crash. Did the fund house listen?

S Naren alerted everyone to get out of mid- and small-cap stocks—“lock, stock, and barrel”. The fund house had ringfenced its portfolios by then but left a breach in some walls

View in Browser

Sankaran Naren, ICICI Prudential MF’s chief investment officer, is considered an OG of India’s mutual fund industry with a lifetime of experience and winning. So, the market veteran’s advice in January to get fully out of “absurdly valued” mid-caps and small-caps amid a market rout sent shockwaves, drawing both bouquets and brickbats. But it wasn’t the first time Naren had sounded the warning. He’d spotted the bubble way back, cut exposure to smaller stocks and even stopped lump-sum flows into such schemes. Yet, regular investments through SIPs were allowed to continue in ICICI Prudential MF’s small-cap and mid-cap funds. Was the OG’s hand forced, and were his words censored? [Read the full story]

Shifting ground

Sanofi India’s big FMCG move is recategorizing prescription meds

Like Zydus, which has had a separate consumer business for years, many pharma companies are chasing newer, better, easier profits

Sanofi India demerged its consumer arm and listed it as a separate company. Many others like GSK and Johnson & Johnson have played similar hands before. Prescription drugs come with regulatory headaches, while consumer health units let pharma giants dabble in the FMCG dream. While some of them sell protein bars and glucose powder, others sell their good old pills, just over the counter. Either way, the FMCG wing gives pharma companies a direct line to consumers, and better profit margins to take back to the parent. [Read the full story]

First, Cult.fit’s group classes got everyone’s attention. Now, “Cult injuries” do

The $1.5 billion-worth fitness chain set out to make group workouts cheap and fun for all. But it’s only doing more harm than good

As fitness studios exploded across Indian cities after the Covid pandemic, Cult.fit rose to prominence on the back of its fun, accessible classes that appealed to sedentary urbanites. Meanwhile, doctors noticed a sharp rise in workout-related injuries. Majority of those injured fell in the “most vulnerable” 35-45 age bracket. The most common injuries were back pain, sprains, and tennis elbow, with some needing surgical intervention. [Read the full story]

Dhan is the stock-broking underdog that Chryscapital and Hornbill are after. But why?

Stock markets are crashing, and Sebi is stepping up checks on F&O trades. It’s not a great time for brokers in India. Yet, all eyes are on a four-year-old upstart. What gives?

Private-equity firms Chryscapital and Hornbill Capital are looking to invest $150–200 million in Dhan, a four-year-old discount broker. The interest seems odd at a time when the stock market is crashing and Sebi has tightened rules around derivatives trading—a double whammy for stock brokers. Dhan’s robust financials, promoter pedigree, and a differentiated product are working in its favour; the active user base is up 20X in three years. Even as market leaders like Zerodha warn of a possible 30–50% hit on topline, thanks to curbs on F&O trades, does Dhan have what it takes to weather the events? [Read the full story]

Private schools are losing students. Government schools aren’t winning either

Private schools in India are facing a double whammy: fewer students each year and poor learning outcomes. Government schools, on the other hand, are having a bit of a comeback

View in Browser

Private schools are seeing a drop in student enrollment, as per the latest data from the government data. But the story varies for individual schools across the country—some show drop, while others see a rise. Private schools are also falling behind government schools in getting their students back on track after the Covid pandemic. On the other hand, government schools are shifting gears from being broken and behind. [Read the full story]

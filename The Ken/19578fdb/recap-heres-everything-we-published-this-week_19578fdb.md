[ICICI Prudential MF’s investment chief warned of a crash. Did the fund house listen?](https://the-ken.com/story/icici-prudential-mfs-investment-chief-warned-of-a-crash-did-the-fund-house-listen/?utm_source=weekly_email&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=weekly_recap_card)
S Naren alerted everyone to get out of mid- and small-cap stocks—“lock, stock, and barrel”. The fund house had ringfenced its portfolios by then but left a breach in some walls
Sankaran Naren, ICICI Prudential MF’s chief investment officer, is considered an OG of India’s mutual fund industry with a lifetime of experience and winning. So, the market veteran’s advice in January to get fully out of “absurdly valued” mid-caps and small-caps amid a market rout sent shockwaves, drawing both bouquets and brickbats. But it wasn’t the first time Naren had sounded the warning. He’d spotted the bubble way back, cut exposure to smaller stocks and even stopped lump-sum flows into such schemes. Yet, regular investments through SIPs were allowed to continue in ICICI Prudential MF’s small-cap and mid-cap funds. Was the OG’s hand forced, and were his words censored? Like Zydus, which has had a separate consumer business for years, many pharma companies are chasing newer, better, easier profits
Sanofi India demerged its consumer arm and listed it as a separate company. Many others like GSK and Johnson & Johnson have played similar hands before. Prescription drugs come with regulatory headaches, while consumer health units let pharma giants dabble in the FMCG dream. While some of them sell protein bars and glucose powder, others sell their good old pills, just over the counter. Either way, the FMCG wing gives pharma companies a direct line to consumers, and better profit margins to take back to the parent. [Dhan is the stock-broking underdog that Chryscapital and Hornbill are after. But why?](https://the-ken.com/story/why-is-private-equity-suddenly-chasing-dhan-a-stock-broking-underdog/?utm_source=weekly_email&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=weekly_recap_card)
Stock markets are crashing, and Sebi is stepping up checks on F&O trades. It’s not a great time for brokers in India. Yet, all eyes are on a four-year-old upstart. What gives?
Private-equity firms Chryscapital and Hornbill Capital are looking to invest $150–200 million in Dhan, a four-year-old discount broker. The interest seems odd at a time when the stock market is crashing and Sebi has tightened rules around derivatives trading—a double whammy for stock brokers. Dhan’s robust financials, promoter pedigree, and a differentiated product are working in its favour; the active user base is up 20X in three years. Even as market leaders like Zerodha warn of a possible 30–50% hit on topline, thanks to curbs on F&O trades, does Dhan have what it takes to weather the events?

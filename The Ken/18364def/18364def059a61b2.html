Fintechs and the RBI need to have a heart-to-heart

 

If you are reading this newsletter and work in fintech, you were likely at the Global Fintech Fest held in Mumbai on Wednesday, at the Jio World Centre. The fintech hype I saw there first-hand was real. Fintech founders were celebrities. I was even asked to click a picture of a person with the founder of a unicorn.

 

If that wasn’t flattery enough, the statements of Reserve Bank of India’s (RBI) Governor Shaktikanta Das the day before should have sent fintechs giddy with excitement.  

 

Das, who was launching the UPI-Rupay credit card facility, called fintechs India’s force multiplier. India’s Chief Economic Advisor V Anantha Nageswaran topped that, saying the market size of India’s fintechs is expected to reach US$1 trillion by 2030.

 

But ask fintechs in private—are you excited?—and the gloom tumbles out. 

 

“This is atmospherics,” said one executive.

 

“We are experiencing Stockholm Syndrome,” said a fintech founder.

 

Another invoked Schrödinger: “We are alive and dead at the same time.”

 

All of this was off-the-record. 

 

The reason for these less-than-happy sentiments is the back-to-back regulatory blows they’ve received recently—from the RBI’s digital lending guidelines to the ban on pre-paid instruments backed by credit lines. 

 

This is something fintechs wouldn’t dare say to their banking partners, let alone the regulator, even though the RBI now has a fintech department. The regulator says play within the guardrails. But in some cases, fintechs feel it also removes the rails altogether, leaving nothing to guard. Like in the case of prepaid instruments backed by credit lines. 

 

What India’s fintechs really need to do to get to that eye watering US$1-trillion market size is have an honest conversation with the regulator. For starters. 

 

Maybe that’s where disruption really starts.

The alt data spell for credit inclusion

 

By Pradnyee Kantak

 

Last month, the Reserve Bank of India’s (RBI) Working Group on Digital Lending published a long list of guidelines for lending apps. I’m sure you read Arundhati’s excellent take on it in Ka-Ching! back then, as did I. 

 

Frankly, these rules (and several recommendations) have been a long time coming. Because the explosive proliferation of lending apps in India these past few years has also been accompanied by steadily worsening levels of malpractice. 

 

Just this Tuesday, for instance, RBI Governor Shaktikanta Das spoke about the need to rein in such apps (again!) after a rather disturbing series of incidents—including borrowers killing themselves due to harassment by recovery agents.

 

"… Das said that while these apps have served the needs of various segments, some of them have also raised concerns around usurious interest rates, unethical recovery practices and data privacy issues."

 

RBI flags concerns over digital lending apps, Livemint

 

Two things struck me here, and they’re really the core of my argument today:

 

1. Yes, usury and malpractices have grown. But these apps have exploded for a reason—they’ve “served the needs of some segments”.

 

I mean, just look at how India fares in terms of access to credit.

Shredding down this credit barrier has long been a national finance goal of sorts for India. Which brings me to the second point. 

 

2. Data privacy is already a concern. But data can also be the magic bullet that solves the credit access problem.

 

Or, to be more precise, alternative data can. 

 

You can’t borrow because you haven’t borrowed because you can’t

 

This is really the crux of the credit inclusion problem. Without records of borrowing and repaying history, it is very difficult to gauge creditworthiness by traditional methods. And this excludes millions of people who can’t furnish such information. 

 

But alternative data, basically any data points that weren’t traditionally used to judge creditworthiness—like utility bill payments, e-commerce history, even social media activity—can provide an out from this mess. 

 

In fact, they already are. 

 

Many companies, in India and globally, now use credit-scoring models using alternative data to enhance predictive accuracy. They collect and analyse a wide array of data sets to predict the creditworthiness of a particular borrower. Like Upstart, Lenddo, Nova Credit in the United States, or Karza Technologies and CreditVidya in India—all of which use AI and machine learning tech to offer alternative credit-scoring services to lenders. 

 

There are companies working with intermediation models, alternative data aggregators, storage infra providers… It’s become big business. And for good reason. Using such alt data to build credit profiles opens up a huge segment of hitherto unserved and underserved markets for lenders.

 

There are challenges though. 

 

The data downside

 

First, there’s the massive amount of data that needs to be analysed. Artificial Intelligence and Machine Learning (AI/ML) models become better at this the more data they process, but this takes resources. It also places a lot of importance on the sanctity of the training set used, though—the more biased the data set here, the more biased the output.  

 

Then there’s the blackbox effect. 

 

What’s that? Well, once your AI/ML models identify data variables with high-predictive power, you sometimes end up with a ‘Blackbox’—models that give good predictions, but even their makers don’t know why. Basing large credit systems on such models can be risky. 

 

There’s also the danger of possible ‘score-shoppers’ as such models proliferate—borrowers who may exploit the different systems to get lenders to misjudge their credit-worthiness. 

 

But for borrowers, the biggest downside is the problem we started off with. Data privacy is a big deal when you’re talking about making a business out of collecting and analysing individuals’ data. How much should such firms be allowed to track? Should they even be allowed to track anything? What happens when there’s a data breach? 

 

One way to address this would be to store only non-personal data. Singapore-based Credolab, for instance, says its algorithms can access as many as 1.3 million features from smartphones to produce a credit scorecard. But it also claims it doesn’t have access to personal details at a granular level because it collects only meta-data. 

 

Much of the work will need to be regulatory, though, and focused on weeding out bad-faith actors. The RBI Working Group’s recommendations already include the setting up of an independent body to verify and authorise lending apps. To be styled the Digital India Trust Agency (DIGITA), it may help reduce the likelihood of data falling into the wrong hands, and the strong-arming or defrauding of borrowers. 

 

All this said, alternative data-based credit scoring offers huge potential for a country like India. With the right systemic backing, it could be just the spell that helps inclusivity trump invisibility.

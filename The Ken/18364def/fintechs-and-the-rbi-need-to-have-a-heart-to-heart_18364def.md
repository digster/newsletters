Shredding down this credit barrier has long been a national finance goal of sorts for India. Which brings me to the second point.
2. Data privacy is already a concern. But data can also be the magic bullet that solves the credit access problem.
Or, to be more precise, alternative data can.
You can’t borrow because you haven’t borrowed because you can’t
This is really the crux of the credit inclusion problem. Without records of borrowing and repaying history, it is very difficult to gauge creditworthiness by traditional methods. And this excludes millions of people who can’t furnish such information.
But alternative data, basically any data points that weren’t traditionally used to judge creditworthiness—like utility bill payments, e-commerce history, even social media activity—can provide an out from this mess.
In fact, they already are.
Many companies, in India and globally, now use credit-scoring models using alternative data to enhance predictive accuracy. They collect and analyse a wide array of data sets to predict the creditworthiness of a particular borrower. Like Upstart, Lenddo, Nova Credit in the United States, or Karza Technologies and CreditVidya in India—all of which use AI and machine learning tech to offer alternative [credit-scoring services](https://tracxn.com/d/trending-themes/Startups-in-Alternative-Credit-Score-Providers) to lenders.
There are companies working with intermediation models, alternative data aggregators, storage infra providers… It’s become big business. And for good reason. Using such alt data to build credit profiles opens up a huge segment of hitherto unserved and underserved markets for lenders.
There are challenges though.
The data downside
First, there’s the massive amount of data that needs to be analysed. Artificial Intelligence and Machine Learning (AI/ML) models become better at this the more data they process, but this takes resources. It also places a lot of importance on the sanctity of the training set used, though—the more biased the data set here, the more biased the output.
Then there’s the blackbox effect.
What’s that? Well, once your AI/ML models identify data variables with high-predictive power, you sometimes end up with a ‘Blackbox’—models that give good predictions, but even their makers don’t know why. Basing large credit systems on such models can be risky.
There’s also the danger of possible ‘score-shoppers’ as such models proliferate—borrowers who may exploit the different systems to get lenders to misjudge their credit-worthiness.
But for borrowers, the biggest downside is the problem we started off with. Data privacy is a big deal when you’re talking about making a business out of collecting and analysing individuals’ data. How much should such firms be allowed to track? Should they even be allowed to track anything? What happens when there’s a data breach?
One way to address this would be to store only non-personal data. Singapore-based Credolab, for instance, says its algorithms can access as many as 1.3 million features from smartphones to produce a credit scorecard. But it also claims it [doesn’t have access](https://asianbankingandfinance.net/financial-technology/in-focus/how-credolab-reduces-banks-credit-risks-through-smartphone-metadata) to personal details at a granular level because it collects only meta-data.
Much of the work will need to be regulatory, though, and focused on weeding out bad-faith actors. The RBI Working Group’s recommendations already include the setting up of [an independent body](https://bfsi.economictimes.indiatimes.com/news/policy/rbi-plans-independent-body-digita-to-verify-authorise-lending-apps/93480981) to verify and authorise lending apps. To be styled the Digital India Trust Agency (DIGITA), it may help reduce the likelihood of data falling into the wrong hands, and the strong-arming or defrauding of borrowers.
All this said, alternative data-based credit scoring offers huge potential for a country like India. With the right systemic backing, it could be just the spell that helps inclusivity trump invisibility.

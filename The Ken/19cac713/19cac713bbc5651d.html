Just yesterday, outside a decidedly upmarket salon in south Kolkata, there it was. A sharply cut, almost aggressive-looking Mahindra EV, parked between a German luxury sedan and something British with a vanity plate. The driver wasn’t the archetypal small-town strongman or a fleet operator doing the maths on resale value. It was a young woman in athleisure, juggling a coffee and her phone, entirely at home in a car brand that, not too long ago, was shorthand for rugged utility and rural heft.

For years, Mahindra’s identity was welded to the Bolero-and-Scorpio image of tough roads, northern heartland, and practical muscle. Then came the reinvention of the Thar, which went from a 300-units-a-month cult off-roader to a five-figure monthly brand by adding mainstream refinement to personality. And today, it is selling electric SUVs priced at nearly Rs 30 lakh, and over 40,000 of them went out within 10 months of deliveries beginning. Even more striking is that a large chunk of buyers are first-time Mahindra customers.

In today’s interview with Seetharaman, Rajesh Jejurikar, CEO of Mahindra’s auto and farm businesses, explains how that demographic change was engineered rather than stumbled upon. The company chose to lead its EV charge with polarising designs—the BE 6e and XEV 9e—at a time when the national mood on EVs was, in his words, “worse than neutral”.  The idea was not to make the cars economical arguments on wheels, but objects of desire that could stand shoulder to shoulder with global luxury EV brands, complete with independent testing and high-voltage launch theatrics. 

The bet is to win over innovators first, let them de-risk the category for everyone else, and expand the brand’s centre of gravity from semi-urban strongholds to metro driveways. It has helped make Mahindra India’s second-largest carmaker in 2025, and one that rests on a simple filter Jejurikar returns to repeatedly: only play in segments where the brand has a “fundamental right to win”.

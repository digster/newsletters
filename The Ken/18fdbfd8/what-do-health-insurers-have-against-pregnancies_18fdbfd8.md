Good Morning Ishan,
Timing and planning parenthood around life and career milestones is quite common now in India. But how common is it for couples to time and plan their parenthood around their maternity insurance covers? Wait, what?
Seems strange, but it’s a problem knocking at the doors of many—with maternity costs on a steady climb and long waiting periods to start enjoying insurance benefits.
An expert revealed a peculiar hack to Narayanan, the author of today’s story. “If a man looking to marry in a couple of years can purchase a health-insurance policy with maternity benefits, he can add his spouse to the plan after getting married and then get full maternity cover for the wife from day one [since he would have fulfilled the waiting period by then].” Practical, funny—all at once.
But the troubles around maternity insurance in the country are no laughing matter. Long waiting periods are just one part of the problem. The world’s largest youth population is also faced with low insurance limits. Traditional health policies offer just a fraction of the expenses, whether for normal deliveries or for the much costlier and increasingly common C-section deliveries. The contrast in incentives is stark. For insurers, maternity is a high-probability event and they want to restrict the coverage to protect their bottom lines. For would-be parents, pregnancy is their only foreseeable big-ticket medical cost, and they expect maximum cover to protect their pockets. Hard to find fault with either side.
Is there a way around the conflict and can couples get more bang for their premiums? Narayanan digs deep and comes up with some nifty (even if costly) solutions and products in [today’s well-researched story](https://the-ken.com/story/what-do-health-insurers-have-against-pregnancies/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter).
With most exit polls over the weekend predicting a comfortable win for the BJP-led NDA in the general elections, many expect the stock market to rally today. But if you ask me, the breathless run in the indices over the past many months—interspersed with the occasional spurt of nervousness—seems to have more than factored this outcome. The market now seems “priced to perfection” with valuations quite high and little room for disappointment on outcomes, whether in the political arena or companies’ earnings.
The days to come might just be a case of “buy the rumour, sell the news”, with market participants who bought on expectations of a BJP win looking to book profits.
In any case, like cricket, elections in India can be a game of glorious uncertainties with twists in the tale, and many a slip between the cup and the lip. Exit polls can go wrong too, as they did spectacularly two decades ago. The proof of the pudding will be in the eating—on Tuesday.

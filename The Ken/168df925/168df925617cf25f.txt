Good Morning Ishan,

Irony twists itself in myriad ways. Nope, I’m not talking Indian politics.

In July, China rolled out the red carpet for Indian pharma companies, especially for those making cancer drugs. It was soon after a Chinese movie, Dying to Survive, went viral with the story of a man who was importing cheap cancer medicines from India.

India has been the saviour in the past, too. At the height of the HIV/AIDS crisis in the early 2000s. In the middle of the 2014 miracle cure of Hepatitis C, offensively priced at $1000/pill. And on several other occasions in between.

Now, when cancer is becoming a household disease, Dhaka is turning out to be that go-to pharmacy.

There are distributors importing these drugs; there are direct pickups from Dhaka; there is drop shipping, via courier. Sources estimate thousands of such enquiries are made from India every day through these channels. And, you guessed it right, there are WhatsApp groups facilitating this.

But it’s illegal. These drugs, protected by patents in India, but not in Bangladesh due to its Least Developed Country status, cannot be sold here. And yet they are. How?

Because these drugs are a nosebleed. In a first-ever analysis by the WHO in December, the price of many cancer drugs in India are as high as in developed countries, when adjusted for the Indian purchasing power.

If Hollywood’s Dallas Buyers Club memorialised the AIDS crisis (and the smuggling of unlicensed drugs)—way too late IMO—a Dhaka sellers’ club is in the making. Patient advocacy groups in India are thinking of forming an ‘anarchic club’ of their own to source these drugs in bulk. The regulator is left hand-wringing, at least for now.

Ruhi has got an important story today, a telling account of how twisted India’s healthcare system is.

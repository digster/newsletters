Now popular perception prevails that India has gotten rid of polio. For good. So ingrained is this perception that almost everyone is like, no we’ve dealt with this problem; it has destroyed the lives of millions of our children, but we are now past that. No more. Well, my colleagues, Gayathri and Ruhi have an update. A while back, some 1.5 lakh kids in Uttar Pradesh, Maharashtra and Telangana, three large states of India, received polio vaccines. Except these were contaminated with poliovirus Type 2. It is a serious breach. For many reasons. 

The Type 2 virus can maim, paralyze and kill. 

The world (including India) stopped vaccinating children against Type 2 virus in 2016. 

There’s the possibility that non-immune children can get infected from children who have received this contaminated vaccine. 

I don’t need to tell you this. This is bad news. If polio type 2 manifests in the community, it would be a serious setback to the global and India's polio eradication efforts. India's last wild polio case was in 2011, and it was certified polio-free in March 2014. The last case of polio type 2 was seen in 2002.

How did this vaccine get contaminated? How does it affect India’s fight against polio? What are drug makers saying? What are the next steps? All of that is in today’s story. 

One important bit. The government of India and the World Health Organization (WHO) obviously know about this contamination. Both are trying to keep this low key, for now. With good reason; nobody wants panic. Also, the government is giving injectable polio vaccine (IPV) shots to the children in the affected areas. It doesn’t help hat IPV is in short supply.

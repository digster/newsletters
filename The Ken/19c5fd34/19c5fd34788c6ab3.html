1. Insights from Kalpana Morparia

I met Kalpana in Mumbai at a recording studio tucked away in the basement of an office building in Worli. Finding the studio once you were downstairs felt like navigating a maze. But once you did, it was like you’d walked through a portal.

Here are a few of the interesting excerpts from our conversation. A friend told me he’d listened to it twice already!

On resilience

Rohin Dharmakumar: I want to talk about resilience now. Resilience is a really hard thing, but also a very desirable thing. Everyone wants to be resilient but it’s also a thing that you can covet, but never directly attain. Your career over these 45-plus years has had lots of instances of resilience. For instance, like how early on in your life you faced speech complications and you were confined to your home for nearly a year where you couldn’t speak much. What was that period like, and do you think it influenced what happened after that?

Kalpana Morparia: So the speech thing was—I fumbled lines in an assembly where the entire secondary school was present. Now, for a 13-year-old to fumble her lines was not—did not deserve a death sentence, but my teacher almost administered a public humiliation which in some ways was worse than a death sentence. So that got me to feel that I’m just not good at public speaking. And one day one of my Head of Treasury at that time, he asked me to go and speak at a bankers training college. And I had prepared for what I thought was the subject. I was very scared to go because I thought I’m so poor at public speaking. And when I went there, they’d actually switched the topic.

Anyway, I said what I did, but I got glowing feedback from that session.

So I—you know, as you were talking about resilience, it struck me that you’ve had a bad failure, you know, you’ve really been pulled down on something, you’ve been humiliated. And then suddenly something happens and you get a good stroke about how good you are at that one thing that you thought you’d miserably failed at.

And that confidence surge that it gives you, is enormous.

***

On ambition and self-redundancy

Rohin Dharmakumar: Still sticking with careers. I think one of the other things that’s changed is ambition. Many young professionals today have a complicated relationship with ambition. Many actually consider it uncool to admit that they’re ambitious. This wasn’t always like that. I’m sure it wasn’t—I mean, I’d love to hear how it was in the 70s and 80s. In the 90s, 2000s, etc., when I joined the workforce, it was okay to say “I’m ambitious, I want more,” etc. but it’s not the same today. Did you identify as ambitious in your career, and were you open about it?

Kalpana Morparia: See, ambition also sometimes has a very negative connotation, right? So if you were to ask the 35-year-old Kalpana, she would have told you, “Of course I’m not ambitious.”

Rohin Dharmakumar: What about the 25-year-old?

Kalpana Morparia: Uh, she would have said, “Of course not, I’m here to enjoy life.”

But the way I would prefer to articulate it now with the benefit of hindsight is the desire to be successful. You know, so you don’t want to fail, right? You want to succeed in what you’re doing, and once you’ve succeeded in doing what you’re doing, you want the next big thing, next big challenge, for you to succeed again.

That brings me, maybe not entirely an answer to your question, but brings me to something which is one of my favourite management mantras: is that for you to really climb the corporate ladder, you need to make yourself redundant in your own role. So in some ways knitting it with the question you asked me earlier, for you to be ambitious, you need to ensure that what you’re currently doing, you’re completely redundant.

***

On mentorship style 

Rohin Dharmakumar: I’m told you’re a great mentor. Verbatim exact quote from someone that you and I both know: “Best mentor I ever had.” That’s from Siddharth. How would you describe your mentorship style?

Kalpana Morparia: So, you know, I would actually even struggle to call it mentorship. My belief is that people you work with spend more time with you in office than they actually spend time with their families. And therefore for me, they are part of my family, right? So of course, all of us are task-oriented; we won’t be able to deliver what we’re delivering otherwise. But for me, they were human beings. And if they came in with a long face, I would be the first to ask them, “What’s wrong?” I was not afraid of talking to young people if they’d not already got married, after they’d got married whether, you know, they—when are they starting their family? Maybe women are more comfortable doing this; I was always very comfortable doing this. So for me, it was just a great connect that I had with them. And yeah, these are fancy words—mentorship, sponsorship, all of that—but for me, it was having a conversation with them which went beyond work. That’s why I’m a very poor manager of time. 

Rohin Dharmakumar: Hmm, what’s the connection? But you did say you were results-driven as well.

Kalpana Morparia: Results-driven, but once we finished discussing the work, I would spend 10 minutes talking about them.

Rohin Dharmakumar: So that’s your mentorship style? That: don’t make it just about the professional?

Kalpana Morparia: Don’t make it just about professional. Because even now, you know, in some of the boards I’m on, they have a mentorship program. And I always start with asking them, “Please tell me about yourself. Where were you born? Where did you study? What did you do? What drives you?” Because that’s the only way you can create a personal connect with that individual and then of course they’ll open up on their career challenges and whatever it is. The same is true for Siddharth.

***

On spirituality and self-acceptance

Rohin Dharmakumar: Is there a view on anything—because you said earlier that you held fairly strong views—is there anything that you held strongly 10 years ago that you’ve genuinely changed your mind about and come to a 180-degree turn?

Kalpana Morparia: Yeah. So 10 years ago, if you’d asked me what is my biggest failing in life, I would have said being childless. I think over the last 10 years, I have evolved somewhat in my spiritual journey. I’m a big follower of the Bhagavad Gita and Krishna as my, you know, go-to God. I find that detachment is probably easier to handle when you don’t have a womb connection. So that’s one strong belief that I feel less bad about now than I did earlier.

2. Music that lingers

Hi there!

First up, happy (belated) Valentine’s Day!

I don’t know where you are, but here in Bengaluru (at least Indiranagar’s 100 Feet Road), the streets are filled with hopeful romantics today.

I am certainly not one to talk about romance, so I will leave that to the man I’d trust the most:

People call these things ‘imperfections’, but they’re not. That’s the good stuff. And then we get to choose who we let into our weird little worlds. You’re not perfect, sport. And let me save you the suspense. This girl you met, she isn’t perfect either. But the question is whether or not you’re perfect for each other. That’s the whole deal. That’s what intimacy is all about. You can know everything in the world, sport, but the only way you’re finding out that one is by giving it a shot.

Sean Maguire (Robin Williams), Good Will Hunting, 1997

May the Force be with you to take the shot! (Weird crossover, but I think it works.)

Now, on to business:

Last week, I asked you to share songs from film and TV that linger long after the credits roll.

Over the past week, I have been listening to all the wonderful submissions—an assortment of songs and soundtracks from film, TV, and even a couple from video games! But what really got me were the stories of why they were special.

Some songs were tied to loss and grief, while some spoke about growing into songs they once hated as kids. Some scores brought back echoes from childhood and even coming-of-age moments like dingy college rooms, a film on a laptop screen, and a lot of fun.

Here are some that stuck with me, organised not by genre, but by the feeling behind them (in their own words):

Tejus from Bengaluru, on “Thenpaandi Cheemayile” (Nayagan, 1987):

“My dad plays music all the time in the car. I hear this song all the time when he plays it. It was always gloomy and sad. I hated it. I never knew where this song came from. I eventually grew older, slowly connecting with it over time. Finally, I watched the movie Nayagan with my dad—he felt the need for me to know the origin of the song. Knowing where it comes from now only connects me far more with it. It reminds me of my Tamil roots and the young boy I once was. Nostalgic, gloomy, and sad.”

Daksha, buffering his way through movies, music, and books on “The Gentle Hum of Anxiety”  (The Social Network, 2010):

“When I first heard it in 2010 as a 21-year-old with ‘nothing to worry about’ back then, it was just the scene and the music. If I’m not mistaken, the track appears when Andrew Garfield (who plays Eduardo Saverin) realises that he has been voted out of Facebook, culminating in a high point in the tensions and his later acceptance that this is how things are. The piano and the synth perfectly captured the moments.

As I grew up, the song stayed with me, and the accompanying feeling became inescapable as I began to feel anxious about many things in life. Anxiety has a connotation with a fast-paced lifestyle and a rush to do things, but the song gave a soothing feel to anxiety. This has been my fallback option on many nights to just unwind and hum along. And the title of the track does it all justice.”

The ones that stuck:

Rahul from Mumbai, on “Divenire” by Ludovico Einaudi (as heard in Bangalore Days, 2014):

“I heard this while watching Bangalore Days for the first time in a packed hall with batchmates. This song plays during the final race in the third act. I’ve been listening to this for 11 years now. It was the feeling of being there. That was probably one of my most memorable experiences of watching a Malayalam movie in a theatre, with all my friends, and the atmosphere was buzzing with anticipation. The music elevated the tension in the scene. At that moment, it just felt like something that would stay with me for a long time (and it has!).”

Anon, on “Lux Aeterna” (Requiem for a Dream, 2000):

“I remember watching the movie in Delhi, just out of boarding school in a dingy and smoky room, which was our first year of college. It was our usual gang, doing things kids do in college, and I put the movie on. It was the start of what one could mildly call ‘the exploratory phase’ of our lives. There were highs and lows after that, both extreme. No one said a word till the last of the credits rolled. I remember after the movie, everyone was quiet for a while. It stayed with us all.”

The ones that stung:

Madhumitha Dhanasekaran on the This Is Us Original Score (by Siddhartha Khosla):

“I lost my mom when I was 15. For anyone who has experienced the loss of a parent, or a different kind of parenting than usual, or even has dug into the childhood they had, the parenting they had, the show will have a huge impact. And the music that connects each of these highly impactful storylines stays with you; it becomes the BGM to what you feel when you see the show, what it brings up for you, because trust me, it will bring up stuff.”

Nupur, freelance writer on “Ek Tukda Dhoop” (Thappad, 2020):

“The film is about rooting for her and watching her win the case, but when the film ends, and they part ways, the quiet pain of two lovers separating is communicated through this song. ‘Toot ke hum dono mein, jo bacha woh kam sa hai. Ek tukda dhoop ka, andar andar nam sa hai’ (transl: ‘After what’s broken between the two of us, what’s left is too little. There’s a piece of sunshine inside me, which now feels damp’).”

The childhood echoes:

SleeveNotes from Bengaluru on “Have Yourself A Merry Little Christmas” (Judy Garland):

“When we first got cable television growing up, TNT used to play at night after Cartoon Network. During the holidays, our family would watch at least one movie each night (sometimes more). This song comes at a sad point in the movie when the family in question learn that they need to move to New York for their father’s work, before the long-awaited World Fair in St. Louis. The youngest daughter (played by Margaret O’Brien) is very distraught about the thought of leaving behind all her toys and snowmen, and Judy Garland (who herself has significant reasons not to leave St. Louis) sings this song to comfort her, in her powerful voice.”

Venkat from Besant Nagar on “Pazhamudircholai”  (Varusham Padhinaaru, 1989):

“I grew up listening to it right from when I was three. This song always, without fail, evokes a mix of childhood nostalgia, playing with cousins, happiness, and yet a tinge of melancholy in me.

The song by itself is sung with great gusto by the inimitable Yesudas. It has a Carnatic base, but the arrangements feel contemporary even now, exuberant even. It makes me happy, but it tears me up as well.”

The serendipitous finds:

Desai Het from Mumbai on “The Long Road” (Dead Man Walking, 1995):

“I discovered it by accident while listening to Pearl Jam, the recommended song had a totally different vibe, it was serendipitous lol, the movie it is from is quite grim, and I never watched it. It’s just the music and the lyrics of the song, to me it describes the fleeting and impermanent nature of all experiences, and how, ‘We all walk the long road,’ whatever that road might be.”

Geriani from Surat on “A Million Roses” (from the series My Mister):

“I stumbled upon this series while going through the filmography of Lee Sun-kyun, the lead actor of Parasite, who committed suicide in 2023. The song in the series beautifully captures the heartache of love unfulfilled.”

3. Community recommendations

Hello everyone! This is Tanim with book recommendations from our FP community. 

How is your reading coming along? Have you made resolutions to stay on top of your reading game this year, or is this going to be a free-flow kind of year? 

I haven’t been able to get much reading done over the past few weeks because they’ve been quite hectic but January was a good start for me, so I am ready to bounce back. 

What was your first read of the year? Did it live up to your expectations? Why or why not? Share your thoughts with us here. 

Vishnu, our community manager and now also a fellow contributor to First Principles has started a new Friday tradition of curating a trivia session for us. One of the recent ones had a book-related question, so I had to share it with you. 

Stratemeyer Syndicate is a US publishing company that publishes books for children. In 1927, they released the first book in the ______ series—written by several ghostwriters, under the collective pseudonym Franklin W. Dixon.

Three years later, they decided to create a female counterpart series, and ________ was born, published under the collective pseudonym Carolyn Keene.

Easy guess. But for those of you who don’t know, he is talking about the Hardy Boys and Nancy Drew series. 

The series is a good example of how quality sometimes suffers when ghostwriters are taken advantage of. Most of the Hardy Boys books written from a first-person POV are fun to read, but the ones written from a third-person point of view are painful to get through. I sincerely hope no child encounters the latter first, otherwise they might never read the series at all. 

I felt betrayed when I learned that ghostwriters were a thing. I am cool with pseudonyms. And I have read ghostwritten books that I have enjoyed. But churning out bad books while riding on the love for the series is unacceptable. You don’t heal from that easily. And you forever have trust issues with ghostwriters.

Now, onto the book recommendations from the community: 

Shubhangi M, a conscious listener of Two by Two, shares her thoughts on Accidental Feminism by Swethaa Ballakrishnen:

“Mentioned in one of the episodes of Two by Two, ‘Accidental Feminism’ traverses into accidental gender parity in Indian law firms and how it fares compared to global law firms, consultancy firms, and the traditional counterpart, litigation. Tracing various factors and circumstances that came together to create a slightly more equitable environment for women professionals, the book is an insightful read into law firms, societal structures, and dynamics.”

Pritha Saha recommends Convenience Store Woman by Sayaka Murata:

“This book really challenges the idea of what is normal. Society has a way of isolating people who define their own ‘normal’ – that is deviated from the established rules. Hence, we have no option but to act in front of society, while we may express our actual selves behind closed doors. But which is easier? Putting up the facade or living your own life with little regard for comments from your neighbors and friends? The ‘Convenience Store Woman’ is an impassive woman, drawn to the sounds of the store, living her life by the rules of the store. Society looks down upon her, and demands that she get married or change her job to another which looks more ‘ambitious’. It does not matter to anyone that she is actually fine living her life the way she wants! The author has almost written herself into this. It’s a great read!”

Dinesh J offers his insights on The Wolf Totem by Jiang Rong: 

“This is a semi-fictional novel covering the equation between man and animal(s), especially the wild ones on the backdrop of Chinese revolution in the 1960s. The dynamics centre on the apex predator of the grasslands—the Mongolian wolves and its impact (largely positive) on how it, as a species, helps maintain equilibrium in the ecosystem and food chain of the area. The book quashes and argues against unpopular opinions like a) herbivores, unchecked, can destroy an ecosystem, but still treated as harmless/cute in the conventional way, b) domesticating/caging wild animals lead to understanding their way of life, c) Communism when boiled down to its actual fundamentals can cause imbalance and systemic.”

Venkat K R recommends Put the Moose on the Table by Randall Tobias: 

“This is one of those rare management books which is narrated as a fable. The setting is simple yet exotic enough that the characters catch your attention. It’s a fairly easy read and makes you reflect on your corporate experiences.”

And to wrap things up, Lalit Jadhav shares his thoughts on The Midnight Library by Matt Haig:

“As a human evolving everyday and making different choices shaping our life, we keep thinking ‘What if I had made that another choice that time?’ This book gives a great perspective on what an alternate life could be like and gives answers to why we are never satisfied with the life we are living today.”

What India’s 19,300+ pin codes say about its retailers. Only one in ten Indians lives in a pin code with more than five organised stores
By Seetharaman G
4 min read
View in browser

Good Morning Ishan,

It’s hard to wrap your mind around any estimate for the size of India’s retail industry. For instance, management consultancy BCG recently pegged it at US$820 billion, and estimated that it would hit US$2 trillion within a decade. In other words, by 2033 India’s retail sector will be half as big as its gross domestic product right now. 

Over 85% of retail is supposed to be mom-and-pop stores selling groceries or clothes or electronics. I’m not sure how reliable this number is because I have no idea how one can arrive at a plausible figure for the contribution of small, independent shops.

Which is exactly why I was excited to find an interesting proxy in a research note from global broker Sanford C Bernstein last week.

Bernstein maintains a database of 20,000 outlets run by India’s top 30 retail and restaurant chains. (Eateries are retail-adjacent, for location can make or mar both.) And it has mapped them to India’s over 19,300 pin codes. It turns out that just over a fifth of these pin codes are home to all these stores. And only 5% have more than five organised stores.

Put differently, only about a tenth of the population lives in pin codes with over five shops or restaurants to choose from.

View in Browser

This is partly a function of how some of India’s retailers have spread their footprint, particularly Dmart.

The grocer, India’s second largest, has used to great effect its strategy of choosing high-density cities and setting up multiple stores there. The idea is to capture as much of the demand as it can in specific neighbourhoods and maintain a lean supply chain by having merchandise delivered to supermarkets located close to each other. 

In Mumbai, where it began its journey over two decades ago, Dmart still doesn’t have a store in the southern part of the city, where real estate is among the priciest in India. Plus, Dmart is only in 23 cities and doesn’t have a single outlet in Bihar or West Bengal—both among India’s most populous states. 

Since Tata Group’s value-apparel sensation, Zudio, has deliberately targeted Dmart’s discount-hungry shoppers, it doesn’t have a store in south Mumbai either. But it has 21 shops elsewhere in the city. 

But even where Indians don’t have access to a supermarket or a large apparel store, they may still be able to enjoy a meal from a global fast-food brand. Domino’s has more than 2,000 outlets in almost 430 cities. This is partly a function of how ubiquitous Zomato and Swiggy increasingly are—Zomato, for instance, delivers food in over 1,000 cities and towns. 

There’s an undeniable shift in consumption from standalone shops and eateries to those run by regional or national chains. This, Bernstein believes, opens up the market for such players to beyond just the top 10% of consumers.

We believe a deeper, longer, and more profitable emerging opportunity in consumer India is to also focus on serving the needs of the middle of the pyramid.

[…] 

The “Next-30”, as we call it, has a population cohort of 430 million. It has a GDP of US$1,400 bn, it captures ~41% of India’s total household spend, and ~38% of India’s discretionary household spend.

A market big enough for Dmart investors to think that maybe, just maybe, the impact of quick commerce is overstated.

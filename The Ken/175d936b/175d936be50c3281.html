There’s a basic trope that underlines most standard fantasy novels. The heroine discovers a magic wand/sword/other obscure artefact or power that opens up a whole new world for her. Everything is shiny and new, and unlike anything she’s ever experienced before. It’s an unbelievable high.
 
And while every good story has a villain, the heroine can’t beat them without undergoing a transformation herself. And not without collateral damage either. A parent may get murdered. A love interest may be held hostage. A brother may, er, get Covid-19.
 
Point is, she has to understand how to wield her new power before she can defeat the villain. For the heroes of today’s story—co-living platforms—the turning point in the story came in the months following the lockdown.
 
The co-living industry was flying high before Covid hit. It had more than 250,000 beds across the country at that point, and the supply was projected to grow 5X by 2023. The $6.6 billion market was even expected to double by 2025. Co-living had found its magic.
 
But the industry stuttered to a halt when the lockdown was announced at the end of March. A good chunk of the rooms was still booked, keeping official occupancy levels high, but the properties resembled ghost towns.
 
Operators needed to cut costs and fast, because the spectre of increased sanitation costs was looming. They even renegotiated contracts with asset owners, who stand to gain in the long run.
 
Because not only is demand cautiously picking up, but co-living platforms have also found their inner reserve of strength. Pre-Covid, the industry found growth in rapid addition of beds. Now, companies are measuring metrics they’ve never needed to before.

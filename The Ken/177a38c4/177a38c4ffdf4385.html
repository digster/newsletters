When it comes to venture capital and private equity “multi baggers", there are some axioms people assume to be true.

For instance, that it’s easier to generate them via an investment in a privately held company than a public one. Or that it’s easier to multiply investments of the size of a few tens of millions of dollars than it is a few hundreds of millions. Or that it's virtually impossible to achieve this in mature and ultra-competitive sectors like IT outsourcing because there’s always a bigger, cheaper, better or closer rival your customers can turn to.

In 2016 global private equity behemoth Blackstone acquired a 60% stake in Mphasis, one of India’s mid-tier IT services companies, for a steal.

The seller was HP, which had been saddled with Mphasis as part of its 2008 acquisition of Electronic Data Systems, which, in turn, owned Mphasis. Mphasis was lost in the grey ocean of IT that was HP.

So Blackstone not only managed to get 60% of it for a bargain basement price of $825 million, it even got HP to commit to giving Mphasis a minimum of $990 million worth of outsourcing revenue itself.

In today’s story, Anand explains how Blackstone managed to pull off a multi-bagger that was seemingly hidden in plain sight. Its stake is now worth $2.4 billion and it wants out.

But good news for Blackstone may turn out to be bad news for Mphasis though. There’s the fact that 4-5% of Mphasis’ revenues roughly a year ago came from Blackstone’s portfolio companies. This was expected to grow sharply, but will it after Blackstone's exit?

Also, HP's revenue commitment is set to end soon.

Does a double-whammy await Mphasis then?

Or could it be that Mphasis is still undervalued, given its 25X price-to-earnings multiple, compared to its mid-tier peers at 28-39X or industry leaders at 30-37X. Perhaps that’s why as per reports, four PE players—Carlyle, Bain, Brookfield, and Permira—are in the race to buy it. Or do the headwinds justify Mphasis' relatively lower valuation?

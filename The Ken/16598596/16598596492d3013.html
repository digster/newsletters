You might not know it, but the Indian government named 2018 the Year of Millets. If you weren't aware of this, I don't blame you. Millets, once a staple of Indian nutrition, have had their place in the Indian diet usurped by wheat and rice following the Green Revolution. 
 
Since then, wheat has remained the staple in North and West India, whereas rice more than holds its own in the South, East and North East regions. This two-staple approach—our over-reliance on wheat and rice—has its drawbacks. Not only are they water-intensive crops in an increasingly water-stressed country, but they are also susceptible to drought and disease. Ideally, to keep the agricultural sector resilient, we need a greater spread of staples. Hence the government's focus on millets.
 
But just think about your last meal. Odds are, it included wheat or rice. For some of you, maybe even both. Weaning ourselves off these staples—both in terms of cultivation and consumption—is not going to be easy. So what then? Well, we should aim to cultivate the hardiest, most resilient staples we possibly can.
 
This was easier said than done for rice, the first crop to have its genome sequenced. Mapping its genome allowed breeders to create hybrids with better disease and pest resistance, increased flood resistance, etc.
 
Wheat, however, is a whole other story. In fact, it's our story today. In it, Roshni (who's making her debut with us) talks about India's contribution to the sequencing of the bread wheat genome. Why is this special? Simple: With 107,891 genes (five times that of humans) and a genome 40 times more complex than rice, bread wheat befuddled scientists for decades.
 
Ultimately, scientists from countries across the world collaborated to crack the wheat code. Among them, Indian scientists. This is the story of those Indian scientists. Their struggles just to get government approval and funding, their efforts, and the profound implications their work could have for a country that produces 98 million tonnes of wheat annually.

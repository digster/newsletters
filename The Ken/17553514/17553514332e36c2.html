Good Morning Ishan,
 
As India transitions from a free-wheeling Wild West of venture capital-funded innovation and disruption to one where large conglomerates and restrictive regulations rule the roost, the 152-year old Tata Group sees an opportunity.
 
No business house touches Indian consumers the way the $113 billion-revenue group does. From tea and salt to air conditioners and clothes; from airlines and hotels to satellite television and insurance, Tata—one of the country’s most recognisable brands—has a lot to offer.
 
It sees Reliance, a rival conglomerate that has dramatically transformed itself into India’s digital conglomerate of choice (or without). It sees the $25 billion that the world’s best-known technology giants and PE investors lined up to invest in Jio Platforms, Reliance’s digital holding company. It looks at itself as the only Indian business house that is present across the retail spectrum—apparel (Westside), grocery (Star), electronics (Croma), and jewellery (Tanishq).
 
And it wonders, if Reliance could do it, why can’t we?
 
Or perhaps it wonders, not everyone will be able to invest in Jio Platforms. Maybe we can be their option.
 
And the answer presents itself: a super app. A super app that connects all the Tata businesses together. The only app customers will ever need. An app that will have global investors queuing up to invest billions in.
 
There’s just a problem (actually there are many, but let’s stick to the most important one for the purpose of this email): Tata Group companies are fiercely independent and do not like sharing customer data with each other.
 
In his extensive story, Seetharaman traverses the length and breadth of the Tata Group to figure out how real its super app ambitions are.

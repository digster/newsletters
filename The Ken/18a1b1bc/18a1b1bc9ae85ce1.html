Deadlines make the world go around, and the Indian stock market has two looming.

In about a week, the country’s capital-markets regulator, the Securities and Exchange Board of India (Sebi), has to deliver to the Supreme Court its probe report on Adani Group, the ports-to-power conglomerate led by billionaire Gautam Adani.

And by around mid-November, high-risk foreign portfolio investors (FPIs) will be required to make additional, granular disclosures around ownership, economic interest, and control. This move aims to reduce the opacity around ownership of these FPIs and prevent FPIs linked with company promoters from masquerading as public shareholders.

Cracking down on shady FPIs is important because they are seen as a channel for round-tripping money by unscrupulous promoters.

One could easily argue that these deadlines have a common link. After all, the January report by US-based short-seller Hindenburg Research—that did a number on the Adani Group stocks—alleged that shareholding by certain FPIs in the Adani entities violated Sebi’s minimum 25% public-shareholding norm for listed companies.

But Sebi denies any connection.

In any case, Sebi’s squeeze puts the spotlight on many other big corporate groups such as OP Jindal, GMR, Hinduja, Aditya Birla, Bajaj, Murugappa, Tata, Larsen & Toubro, and Bharti Enterprises, as well as the FPIs that invest in them.

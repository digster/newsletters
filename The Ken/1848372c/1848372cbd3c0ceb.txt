Good Morning Ishan,

Until I read the magnificent book Empire of Pain last year, I was unaware that the somewhat controversial practice of drug promotion by pharma companies through symposiums and conferences is more than 70 years old.

In the 1950s, big pharma like Pfizer, J&J, and others adopted continuing medical education (CME) programmes with gusto. Over the years, CME, the gust to launch new drugs, would become a squall and eventually the hurricane that irrevocably hurt the industry’s public creds.

Indian pharma companies picked up the practice from global Big Pharma and then, understandably, improvised it for Indian medical sensibilities. From changing the nameplate at the doctor’s clinic to revamping their home interiors to booking restaurant tables, medical reps have to do it all.

Why am I saying this? You know it, we all have doctors in our friends-family network.

It’s a pity that the field force, which has long been the core of pharma sales and a channel for taking the latest from the company to the doctor and bringing back valuable feedback, is up in arms against their employers.

Earlier this month, a Supreme Court-appointed committee began consultations on this contentious issue: giving statutory backing to the seven-year-old UCPMP—Uniform Code for Pharmaceuticals Marketing Practices. This follows a case filed by the medical and sales rep association asking to legalise the Code.

Unsurprisingly, people privy to the early discussions say the pharma companies have two main demands: relax the norms related to the freebies distribution and the CMEs.

Under pressure for growth, the industry is also laying off its sales force in large numbers. In some cases, companies are getting affidavits signed by the reps to prevent them from going to court.

In her meticulously reported story, Maitri captures the tumult in an industry under tremendous weight to keep healthcare costs low.

You know how long those gym resolutions last. Once the adrenaline level settle down, one realises that round is a shape too. And doesn't need gymming. Plus, eating healthy is as good as working out.
That's what Curefit, which operates Cult, Indian biggest gym and fitness studio chain, found out last year. It was also the year it doubled down on health food, which yielded a fascinating set of numbers.
While 90% of Curefit app users came to book gym sessions, about half of them tried Eat.fit at least once. Eat.fit's share of Curefit's overall revenue doubled from 15% to 30% in 2019 (about 60% revenue is from gyms, 10% from others). The markup on cooked food can be as much as 100-150%. Curefit now runs the second largest chain of cloud kitchens in India, according to co-founder Mukesh Bansal.
2019 was the year, Ruhi writes in today's story, when Curefit realised that come-for-the-gym-and-stay-for-the-food was its most workable business model for growth. It is also a model that attracted funding—in February, a Temasek-led round allegedly valued the startup at $800 million.
But then Curefit has deliberately, or inadvertently, landed slap bang in the middle of India's foodtech fight, which comes with heavyweight competitors like Swiggy and Zomato. Is Curefit ready or this workout? That's Ruhi's story:

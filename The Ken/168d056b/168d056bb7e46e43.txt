The story goes that the physicist Wolfgang Pauli’s first wife left him for another man. He didn’t seem to mind her departure much—but he was rather miffed about just whom she had chosen over him: “Had she taken a bullfighter I would have understood—with such a man I could not compete—but a chemist—such an average chemist!”

There’s always been a bit of one-upmanship among scientists of different disciplines. You know the drill. There’s the stereotype of the physicist who thinks everything else can be reduced to physics. The old jokes about how chemistry is just applied physics and biology is just applied chemistry (and everything is maths).

And when it comes to pop science, a science writer was complaining to me just a few weeks ago, chemistry all too often gets overshadowed by its flashier friends biology and physics. It’s a shame, because chemistry is just as much a part of, well, everything.

It’s by understanding just this that researchers were recently able to figure out that elephants who raided crops because humans have encroached on their habitats were less stressed than those who didn’t. (Because they had food the whole year round.) The scientists pulled that off by combing through elephant faeces for chemical signals of stress.

These chemical signals are all around us, my colleague Roshni writes in today’s story on chemical ecology—the study of how living beings use chemicals to interact or communicate. 

For a little over three years now, researchers from 10 Indian institutes have been working together on SCENIC, India’s first large-scale project on chemical ecology. And it’s drawn in scientists from field after field: botanists, chemists, microbiologists, mammalogists, entomologists.

Drones are flying everywhere. During the Pahalgam terrorist attack last week. In the Ukraine-Russia warfare escalation this week. For drug surveillance on the borders; over farmlands in the Indian hinterland.

One company that’s making the most of drone buzz-iness is Optiemus Infracom, a 31-year-old firm that distributes and assembles electronic devices. Last week, when it announced its tie-up with Taiwanese drone maker Avix Technology for making drones in India, the share price jumped 7%.

That came days after it was locked in at a 10% upper circuit on 16 April, when the company said it was partnering with the Chinese smartphone brand Realme to make AIoT products (which marries AI and IoT to produce smart, connected devices).

The company, whose market capitalisation now exceeds Rs 4,500 crore ($530 million), has been talking up its manufacturing moves for some time now.

Its wholly-owned subsidiary, Optiemus Unmanned Systems (OUS), displayed four new drones—which certainly have Indian names even if all the components are imported—at the defence expo Milipol India 2025 last week. In January, it said it had tied up with Taiwan’s Kunway Technology to “launch their products in the Indian market and localise as per the need of the defence and homeland security.”

It’s strange that a company that has no history of manufacturing made its second drone-manufacturing deal in less than five months. As much as 92.94% of revenue in FY2024” came from “wholesale trading of telecommunications and allied products,” it said in its latest annual report.

In 2023, when Optiemus formed OUS with an investment of Rs 25 crore, its revenue goal for the business was to get to “Rs 600–900 crore by the end of calendar year 2025.” The company has revised it to “revenue guidance is still under pipeline” in more recent quarterly filings.

Optiemus Infracom reported Rs 57 crore in net profit on revenues of Rs 1,528 crore in the year ended March 2024, per market-data provider Screener. The trailing 12-month revenue stands at Rs 1,931 crore.

Playing fast and loose with headline numbers and tech claims is becoming common in the fledgling drone industry.

Among the 106 “manufacturers/importers” certified for their drones by the DGCA, the national civil-aviation regulator, Optiemus or its partners don’t figure anywhere. The DGCA does not make a distinction between manufacturers and importers. And there lies one crucial chink in India’s drone policy, which even after an amendment in 2022, is unable to curb imports.

‘Not-made-in-China’ tag isn’t enough

There’s probably no drone company in India which can claim to build any product without Chinese components. And it’s not just Indian companies, which anyway have their weakest link in the missing domestic electronics know-how. China controls 80% of the global drone market and offers everything on the cheap.

But as the anti-China sentiment gains ground, Taiwan has a new-found ambition. It’s also where Optiemus has found its own not-made-in-China narrative.

Taiwan has coined a term, “non-red”, as it aims to build its local and export market in drones. It started in 2022 when Lai Ching-te, its president, said he wanted Taiwan to be the “Asian hub of UAV supply chains for global democracies.”

As The Economist reported last week:

American military officials have described a plan for Taiwan to similarly hold off a potential Chinese invasion by creating an “unmanned hellscape” of sea-and-air drones in the Taiwan Strait. To achieve that, Taiwan would need many more drones. It signed a deal to buy nearly 1,000 small attack drones from America in late 2024. But it is also trying to build its own. Since 2022 the government has built a new drone-research centre, subsidised local companies to develop AI imaging chips and flight controllers, and awarded $210m in drone-procurement contracts for Taiwan’s armed forces. It aims to produce 15,000 homemade drones a month by 2028.

Back in India, the government’s 2021 ban on sourcing Chinese drone components has had little effect. And as The Captable reported recently, “more than 60% of the components” in Indian drones are from China.

The ban has failed to make any impact because the policy doesn’t make a distinction between local manufacturing and white labeling. And companies like Optiemus are more than happy announcing partnerships for what mostly seems like the latter.

Unquestionably, there’s an emerging local market, in defence and elsewhere. And there’s a blueprint from disbursing subsidies under FAME and PLI where local value addition is measured before money is released. Indian officials have figured this out by trial and error and the learning is available to adopt in other ministries.

For instance, the ministry of agriculture will soon procure 15,000 drones to distribute to farmers under the Rs 1,261-crore Namo Drone Didi programme. Now, it’s known that most Taiwanese vendors have expertise in mapping and agricultural spraying. Does that mean white-labeled drones easily find their way into this subsidy scheme?

Those like Optiemus will continue to make tall claims—retail investors of this small-cap company and public-sector buyers of its drones must watch out for its local capabilities.

In its FY2024 annual report Optiemus said: “OUS will be adopting a ‘drone-as-a-service’ strategy to reach out to target markets and will be training around 6,000 pilots to manage a fleet of 5,000 drones by the end of 2025.”

It is a flight of imagination, at best.

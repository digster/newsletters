It doesn’t make sense.
India is a massive, growing economy with a perennial power deficit problem. And ReNew Power is not only the largest of its renewable power companies but also the one with the best pedigree. Started by former Wall Street investment banker and subsequent CFO of the Aditya Birla Group conglomerate, Sumant Sinha. Funded by the likes of Goldman Sachs, Asian Development Bank and other marquee investors.
And yet, after filing and getting the green light for its IPO with the market regulator in May this year, it has now apparently called it off.
This shouldn’t be happening, but it is. The reason? Cooling investor appetite for high valuations. ReNew was looking for a valuation of nearly $4 billion, a huge jump after its last valuation of $2.3 billion in just March this year.
Other power companies whose IPO valuations or dreams have gone cold are Sembcorp Energy India, the Indian arm of Singapore-based Sembcorp, backed by Temasek Holdings. And ACME Solar Holdings, which after filing for an IPO last year is yet to refile its draft papers with the regulator.
It’s ironic that most of these renewable power companies are caught up in only what can be described as a perfect storm - enormous debt piles, falling tariffs, distressed buyers and rising input costs. If only there was a windmill to harness this storm.
Read today’s story by Pooja and Sidhartha to understand why India’s renewable power industry is going through such a bleak phase. And when the storm might abate.

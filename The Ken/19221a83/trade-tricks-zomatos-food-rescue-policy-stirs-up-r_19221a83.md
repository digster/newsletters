It’s Aakriti for Trade Tricks this week.
Ever wondered what happens to the food you order from Zomato but cancel while it’s still on its way?
In most cases, it goes back to the restaurant and ends up in the bin.
A terrible waste, all of us can agree, so when Zomato rolled out a “food rescue” policy earlier this month, things sounded pretty nice on paper. The plan was to relist such orders on the platform at a discount, and then other customers could choose to buy them.
This way, the food wouldn’t be wasted. And Zomato and the restaurant could make money from a new customer. Win-win.
Except, many of its restaurant partners didn’t really like it.
In fact, so many of them didn’t like the policy that Zomato rolled it back in just a few days.

Miffed restaurateurs I spoke to, though, have a far more weighty list of criticisms. And they argue that the launch and the rollback are symptomatic of larger problems in the restaurant-food aggregator relationship.
When it first launched food rescue, Zomato had said:
This policy is designed as an attempt to reduce food wastage and through this policy, we believe that our commitment towards sustainability and social responsibility will make a positive impact on the environment.
But one restaurant owner from Delhi told me that they saw it as a “greenwashing” practice at best, and an attempt to squeeze out as much revenue as possible at worst. “There are so many technicalities involved that it opens a Pandora’s box of troubles,” they said, requesting anonymity since a large chunk of their online business comes from third-party aggregators.
One such problem was stale food, a concern that came up in conversations I had with three separate restaurants.
How long has the order been sitting, all packed and taped up and getting cold, on the restaurant’s countertop?
“Indians are uncomfortable with stale food being served to us,” noted Pranav Rungta, vice-president of National Restaurant Association of India.
Stale food would lead to bad reviews. Bad reviews would lead to a bad reputation. And restaurants, more than most, live and die by their reputation. Zomato was trying to “remonetise orders” at risk of the restaurants’ reputation, one irate restaurateur told me.
Even Avanti Hiremath of Green Grain Bowl Salad, a cloud kitchen, who said it was a good thing that the food wouldn’t be wasted, said that older orders should only be available for reordering for a short period of time—like a 10–15-minute window.
“There are certain food items, be it hot or cold, that need to be consumed within a time frame, per food safety standards. In any metropolitan city, on an average, food travels 15–30 minutes to reach the customer’s house. Going by those standards, there are high chances food safety is anyway jeopardised.
“We don’t have clear laws for food safety in terms of delivery. These are needed.”
Other restaurants cited Domino’s example, noting there was a good reason why the pizza chain tried to deliver its orders within 20 minutes (pizzas become soggy after a while) and had spent so much time on R&D on pizza boxes and temperature-control cases.
Zomato plans to roll out another pilot (or what it calls a “pre-release”) of a new food-rescue policy in seven to ten days. And this time, it says it will ensure three things:
i. the parcel will be listed for reorder for only 5–7 minutes after being cancelled
ii. it will only be visible to customers near the delivery executive
iii. it will not be rolled out for categories like ice-cream, tea, etc., where certain temperatures need to be maintained for quality
This may take care of the immediate concerns restaurants had with the old food-rescue policy, but many restaurant owners and representatives I spoke to don’t seem in the mood to be so easily placated. Their larger sense of dissatisfaction comes from the belief that many of the policies food aggregators devise are not in the restaurants’ favour. And that there isn’t enough dialogue with them before these policies are rolled out. (Zomato and Swiggy have over 270,000 restaurants listed on their platform.)
One common refrain, for instance, is that the aggregators are trying to squeeze restaurants through high commissions and high advertising and promotion charges. According to restaurateurs, about 45–50% of the order value of an order goes directly to the aggregator, leaving them with miniscule margins.
My colleague Gaurav [wrote](https://the-ken.com/story/swiggy-ads-restaurants-need-them-but-dont-want-them/) about this earlier this year:
As for small restaurants, spending on ads narrows their margins significantly. For instance, Shivaprasad Shetty, who runs seven restaurants in Karnataka’s Udupi region, said the business spends around Rs 18,000 (US$217) a month on ads—roughly 10% of their monthly revenue—besides 28% commissions.
Add to it the ingredients and packaging costs, and they end up making losses on deliveries, he added. That’s why they run ads only when they need to boost sales or during festive periods.
Another point of discontent are features like the “win-back” initiative, which restaurant owners say pit restaurants against each other. Under this policy, if a customer gives a bad rating to a restaurant, its competitors get a nudge from Zomato to send a replacement meal to the customer. This would help the competition win a new customer, but leave the restaurant which got the original order bearing the cost. Multiple Zomato restaurant partners I spoke to say the majority of restaurants rejected the feature when it was proposed to them.
Zomato’s Ranjan, however, told me that the intent wasn’t to pit restaurants against each other. He says that there are three buckets that bad reviews from customers go into: those attributable to Zomato, those attributable to restaurants, and those that could be attributed to both. Only reviews from customers who had severe issues directly attributable to the restaurant would trigger the “win-back” nudge. And even then, it would happen only if customers recorded severe issues such as finding a fly in the food, and then refused to be compensated with a coupon or a replacement from the original restaurant, he said.
In its favour, Zomato has also been quite agile in terms of rolling out and then rolling back policies when there is resistance, according to Rungta.
“But they need to spend more time talking to people before rolling out those policies.”

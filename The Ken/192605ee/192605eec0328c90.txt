1. Ticking the right boxes

Hello dear readers, it’s Hari here with this week’s books section. 

Last week, I put out a call for recommendations for books that could help us learn how to write better. And the first of those recommendations I want to show you today was actually suggested by two of our readers. I take that as a strong sign that it does something very right. 

Both Anusha Mysooru and Darshan Regi Kalathil wrote in about George Saunders’ A Swim in a Pond in the Rain. https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/53487237-a-swim-in-a-pond-in-the-rain?ac=1&from_search=true&qid=2xzsCEcg5U&rank=1

https://the-ken.com/?post_type=fp_newsletter&p=1478592?utm_source=ro_hdr&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=View in Browser https://the-ken.com/?post_type=fp_newsletter&p=1478592?utm_source=ro_hdr&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=

I haven’t managed to pick up the book yet, but from what I read of it, it seems to be a very interesting look at how narratives function in fiction and how literature helps us see the world differently and with curiosity.

Here’s what Anusha and Darshan have to say about it: 

Anusha Mysooru: “It is specifically meant for short story lovers as George Saunders dissects seven Russian short stories, and what they’re doing right and why”

Darshan Regi Kalathil: “George Saunders breaks down his favorite short stories by Russian writers and explains why they are considered great works of literature. You get to read these short stories, and get a creative writing lesson at the same time.”

The second book on the list today is The Pyramid Principle https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/124582544-the-pyramid-principle?ref=nav_sb_ss_2_21 by Barbara Minto, recommended by Abhay A Gandhi.

And here’s what Abhay had to say about it: “This was the most effective book that helped me improve my business writing. 80% of the improvement in business writing is driven by improving structure which is easy to fix.”

And the last recommendation I have on today’s list isn’t really connected to our theme today (except perhaps as an example of a very well-written book), but I did find it to be a very interesting piece of historical fiction. The book is called A Fine Balance https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/5211.A_Fine_Balance?ref=nav_sb_ss_1_14 by Rohinton Mistry, and it was recommended by Pritha Saha, who also sent in a very nice photo of their own copy as well.

As for what’s keeping me busy these days, I’ve been reading a couple of books. I’m slowly, but steadily, getting through Ret Samadhi and The Checklist Manifesto https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/6667514-the-checklist-manifesto?ref=nav_sb_ss_1_23, both of which I have talked about in earlier editions. 

The Checklist Manifesto in particular, I must say, is a very illuminating book.

It’s fascinating to me how something as boring (and apologies to anyone I’m going to offend with my next words) as a checklist can make such a world of difference. And I’d like to ask you, our readers, if you know of any other such books—ones that break down concepts using interesting narratives rather than just dry academic language.

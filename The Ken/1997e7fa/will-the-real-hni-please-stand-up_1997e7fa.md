Ka-Ching!
Thu, 25 Sep 25
A weekly newsletter about how finance is getting supercharged by tech in India, and how you can make money work for you.
Good Morning Ishan,
In March this year, global real-estate research and consultancy firm Knight Frank released its latest wealth report. It resulted in all the (by now) usual headlines: Indian millionaires and billionaires rising in number again; the wealthy and ultra-wealthy investing increasingly more in luxury real estate; younger millionaires/billionaires buying even faster cars… It’s the tried-and-tested way most daily-news publications have covered wealth reports for years.
I, however, ended up in an entirely different rabbit-hole—one I have returned to every now and then these last few months.
You see, one headline number that was plastered across most of those headlines was this: India’s high-networth individuals (or HNIs, as it’s fashionable to call them) rose 6% in number in 2024 to reach 85,698.
Right. Good for them, I guess.
But… who exactly is a HNI?
Knight Frank, for its part, offers us its own definition: people with over US$10 million in net worth. So, about Rs 88 crore.
But that’s one private entity and one definition.
There are many others, set both by regulatory authorities and the private sector, that span an incredibly wide spectrum.
For instance, if you go by how market regulator Sebi categorises HNIs—based on the limits it sets for different products—then it could be the ability to invest Rs 50 lakh, which is the minimum investment required to avail of portfolio management services (PMS). Or, if you go by the eligibility criteria for investing in an Alternate Investment Fund (AIF), it could be the ability to invest Rs 1 crore.
It could even be as nominal as the ability to invest Rs 2 lakh in one go, if you base it on how IPO allotments are made. According to current guidelines, if your bids for an IPO total up to under Rs 2 lakh, then you are categorised as a retail investor and issued shares from the retail investors allotment. If your bids are cumulatively more than Rs 2 lakh, then you are placed in the HNI pool. The Association of Mutual Funds in India (AMFI) has a similar definition for HNI—i.e., an individual who invests a lump sum of more than Rs 2 lakh in a mutual fund.
In fact, the sheer variety in the definitions for ‘HNI’ seems to only be exceeded by the arbitrariness of the classifications.
Why does it matter?
Because, according to the wealth-management experts I spoke to, the lack of standardisation and, in some cases, the outdated nature of definitions, make it difficult to frame the right guidelines for the right wealth classes. And for the right type of financial and investment services and products to reach the right investors.
One of the things a market regulator tries to do is limit risk exposure for investors who may not be informed enough or financially well-off enough to avoid getting their fingers burned in risky investments. Thus, for instance, Sebi’s focus on protecting retail investors.
But the categorisations it uses for IPO allotments implicitly acknowledge that not all “HNIs” who fall within the relevant definition of HNI are the same. There are “small” HNIs (those who bid between Rs 2 lakh and Rs 10 lakh) and “big” HNIs (with total bids more than Rs 10 lakh). The way wealth class-based allotments are mandated by Sebi, the higher up you are in the HNI bracket, the greater the chances of you being allotted shares.
But just because you are able to meet the investment requirement for HNI categorisation, it doesn’t mean you have the same capability to deal with risks as other, more well-heeled HNIs.
After all, given general trends in inflation, income, and lifestyle, is being able to invest Rs 2 lakh in an IPO (or Rs 2 lakh in one-go in a mutual fund) really enough for HNI treatment?
The official guidelines do change with the times. But usually, the process can be quite slow. For instance, the minimum threshold for availing PMS products—a limit imposed by Sebi to protect retail investors from more specialised, high-risk investment strategies—took decades to get where it is now. It started at Rs 5 lakh in 1993, went to Rs 25 lakh in 2012, and finally to Rs 50 lakh in 2020.
It isn’t just regulatory protection that can fall within the cracks without proper standardisation of definitions and regular updates to them. How different segments of the private sector classify HNIs and non-HNIs have a direct impact on what kind of services and products reach which people.
Rishabh Nahar, partner at Qode Advisors, a PMS firm, thinks standardisation is important, but “only if done thoughtfully”.
“At present, there are multiple definitions—AMFI has one, insurance regulators another, banks a third. This creates confusion for investors and unnecessary complexity for product design and policy implementation. A single, clear framework would help regulators identify systemic risks, create better consumer protection rules, and simplify tax and compliance policies. It would also reduce mis-selling, where someone with just Rs 2 lakh is pitched products meant for the ultra-wealthy,” Nahar told me.
It could go the other way too. Products or services certain individuals need may not be available to them because they fall below minimum thresholds.
Because the current reality, according to wealth managers, is that the HNI band is just too broad. “The gap between a genuine retail investor and someone with a few crores to spend is far too wide to be placed in the same category,” Nahar says, “We need graduated tiers that reflect today’s realities—perhaps something like affluent retail, emerging HNI, core HNI, and UHNI. This would make product design and investor protection far more precise and effective.”

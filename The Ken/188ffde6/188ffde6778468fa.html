Good Morning Ishan,

There seems to be a frenetic pace of activity in the drone industry. It’s buzzing, some will even say.

Just a few days ago, the US and India signed a $3 billion drone deal. Ideaforge, a drone supplier to the Indian Army, is in the market to raise Rs 567 crore ($69 million) via its initial public offering.

But the future growth for this industry, which derives around 70% of its revenues from the defence sector, is expected to come from non-defence areas such as agriculture, logistics, and infrastructure surveys. These may account for 70% of the drone startups’ revenue by 2030, according to a report by EY and Ficci.

Companies like the 4-year-old Redwing are trying to make the most of this. And why not? Drones can move more things faster. “A drone could be used to carry 300-500kg of cargo over long distances in an hour, a task that would otherwise take 2-3 hours when using a car,” an investor told The Ken.

But for e-commerce companies, drones opened a fresh set of problems.

“Sometimes, the destinations were high-rises or next to a busy road where we couldn’t drop the packages,” an e-commerce executive said.

What is also not helping the sector live up to its full potential is regulation.

This is despite the government being gung-ho about it. While the latest set of rules for drone operations in India came in August 2021, India is yet to establish a fully comprehensive set of guidelines for the use of delivery and logistics drones. A plan that has been stuck for 18 months.

As a result, the drone startups now have to navigate the impending regulations and find more commercial use cases that can bring in more funding, writes Anushka in today’s story: https://the-ken.com/story/ideaforge-soars-but-most-indian-drone-startups-await-a-clear-signal-to-fly/ (8-minute read)

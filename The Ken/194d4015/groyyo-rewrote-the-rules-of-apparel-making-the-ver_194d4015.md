Good Morning Ishan,
Apparel manufacturing is a hard game to play.
Just ask Fashinza, which had to return investor money to big-name venture capitalists like Accel and Prosus last year. Or Geniemode, which caused one garment manufacturer significant financial losses due to operational delays and poor communication. Neither was able to crack it.
It’s no surprise, then, that brands are now demanding faster, more agile supply chains so that the latest trends can hit the shelves faster. A manufacturer that would earlier operate on a 150-day production timeline now needs to deliver in half the time, while minimum order sizes have been slashed from 6,000 pieces to 600.
Enter Groyyo.
The four-year-old aggregator may have finally cracked the code to build a network of unorganised factories to manufacture branded garments.
Groyyo’s claim to fame has two prongs, both contrary to the formula its rivals developed. One, it partners with small and medium factories, whose flexibility is a better fit in the fast-fashion era when compared to their larger counterparts. And two, it relies on international brands like Next and Mango for over 90% of its business, as they offer fatter margins than domestic brands.
Sounds simple enough, but pulling this strategy off is a different story. Yet, Groyyo has not only made small-batch apparel manufacturing viable but also turned it into an Ebitda positive Rs 430 crore business, writes Pranathi. Read her well-researched [story](https://the-ken.com/story/groyyo-rewrote-the-rules-of-apparel-making-the-very-ones-that-put-fashinza-out-of-business/) today to understand how the company is dodging the many pitfalls of the sector.
ITC Hotels made its stock-market debut with a bang—except in the wrong direction. After a promising price discovery, the stock plunged 30% on listing day and has only sunk further since. That’s strange because the numbers tell a different story: strong margins, ambitious expansion plans, and a debt-free balance sheet. So why the sell-off? Forced passive fund exits? Market jitters? A valuation gap with Indian Hotels?

Good Morning Ishan,
There’s a scene in Rahul Jain’s 2021 documentary [Invisible Demons](https://www.nytimes.com/2022/09/22/movies/invisible-demons-review.html) that really rattles me. It’s nighttime in Delhi, and the filmmaker is out on a National Highway. With his camera, he captures the tiny airborne particles that people in Delhi inhale with every breath of air. The specks fill the frame.
We have always known the causes of air pollution in the Indo-Gangetic Plain, and Delhi has been at the epicentre of this story for more than a decade. Most readers and listeners are also cognisant of the attempts to bring it down to permissible levels, yet the worst-affected regions remain smoggy.
In this week’s episode of Two by Two, we decided to look at this critical issue—a “wicked problem”, as one of our hosts called it.
A “wicked problem” is a complex social or cultural issue that is extremely difficult to solve due to its interconnected nature, lack of clear definition, multiple perspectives, and the absence of a definitive solution.
Hosts Rohin Dharmakumar and Praveen Gopal Krishnan were joined by an excellent panel of guests who didn’t hold back when they shared their thoughts and opinions. There was Alok Mittal, co-founder of Indifi, a fintech enabling lending for SMEs; Roshan Shankar, founder and CEO of Saroja Earth, an institution that creates and scales market solutions to reduce air pollution through strategic R&D, open policy advocacy, and grassroots work; and Mohit Beotra, co-founder of the Air Pollution Action Group (A-PAG), an organisation established in 2019 to clean up India’s air.
Our panel reflected the many different types of people who need to come together to clear the haze over India.
Roshan: Alok is the first step. He’s the education part. I am the agitation bit, and Mohit is the organisation bit.
So if that’s Ambedkar’s triumvirate of change, you need to first educate people, I will agitate them, and Mohit will organise us to do something useful out of it.
We’ll add this: Two by Two is the platform where their voices are heard.

Step 1: Educate
Many people comprehend air pollution as an abstract problem—one that they encounter in their daily lives but don’t measure precisely. To convince people to do more than acknowledge the existence of a problem and actually take action, the message has to be more intense than the air’s really bad today.
By tracking the relevant data and giving it quantitative resolution, the issue’s gravity becomes much clearer.
Alok: I think that one of the things that we need to do is to make granular monitoring of air pollution available on an open platform, where I can see the difference from my home versus half a kilometre away.
Today, the pollution data that we get is based on very few sensors. Delhi has fewer than 40, and we are interpolating from all of those sensors to kind of create a granular map, but that granularity of data doesn’t exist.
Alok’s point rings true. [This article](https://www.newindianexpress.com/cities/delhi/2024/Nov/25/delhi-the-aqi-conundrum) from The New Indian Express sheds light on how the location of the sensors is also key when calculating the Air Quality Index, or AQI.
China’s smoggy overcast in the previous decade came up as a reference, and Praveen echoed Alok’s thought with an anecdote.
Praveen: I travelled to Beijing for work somewhere around 2014 or 2015.
I was unfortunate to be there when the pollution was quite bad.
But I distinctly remember going from various parts of Beijing, and even in hotels, even in offices, there was always the [AQI] number in front of you.
I don’t think they ever tried to hide that number. It was always there.
The AQI is a great way to generate stronger awareness about air pollution in India. It gives people a way to refer back to the past and justifiably say how quickly the quality of air is going down. By measuring that accurately and constantly providing that information, people in India might be spurred into action, whether it’s lobbying for changes in public policy or developing cleaner technologies.
Step 2: Agitate
Once the problem is given shape and definition, the question is: who will act?
Determining who takes the next step is actually the second barrier to reaching a solution, based on Roshan’s thought process.
Roshan: Everyone lies.
Taiwan does citizen-led, open-source air quality measurement and management. It has to be done by the people because there is an incentive for the politician to lie because the votes won’t come.
There is an incentive for the bureaucrat to lie because they won’t get promoted if they look bad in their work.
There’s an incentive for the technocrat to lie because you don’t want to be the person who says: well, maybe rice stubble, you cannot make ethanol out of it in a feasible manner till you actually do something useful with technology and throw a few hundred crores at it.
The market has an incentive to lie.
That’s the thing with hard problems. Entrenched powers don’t necessarily prioritise the well-being of broader society. Change might not begin unless a billionaire makes it their mission to solve the problem, Roshan said half-jokingly.
Even so, Roshan has faith in one set of individuals to agitate and influence change—young people. After all, it’s the next generation that has a stronger incentive to ensure the environment they inherit is clean and liveable.
Likewise, opposition leaders can also spark action, Roshan said, because they are willing to challenge the status quo.
Step 3: Organise
If our premier educational institutes put some serious money into scientific and technical research, we might find solutions in unexpected places.
Rohin: I saw this very [interesting report](https://a-pag.org/wp-content/uploads/2024/10/IIT-Delhi_Impact-Assessment-Study.pdf) prepared by the Indian Institute of Technology, Delhi, which measured air quality between July 2023 and March 2024 in three localities in Delhi.
The PM2.5 concentrations came down by 26.6% in Jahangirpuri, 15.7% in Rohini and 15.3% in Karol Bagh.
What was really fascinating for me was what caused those to come down.
The things that caused it to come down were paving unpaved roads, repairing potholes, and fixing broken footpaths and dividers.
Mohit: You have taken A-PAG’s lead project and made it sound really simple.
This is a long road, and it’s important that we fund researchers and academics who can examine the hard problem and come up with innovative solutions.

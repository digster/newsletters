Kids everywhere use smartphones now. To play games, watch movies, surf websites, and yes, even study at times. From Turkey to Cambodia to Bangladesh, students also carry their phones into classrooms. And that’s proved to be a challenge for educators.

Smartphones are a massive cause for distraction. With constant access to the world outside, or friends inside the classroom, it isn’t hard to imagine why teachers may struggle to keep their students’ attention. There are other harmful effects, too, from being online all the time. The chances of cyberbullying or being exposed to inappropriate content is higher. And social media can negatively impact how young women think about their bodies.

Which is why there’s now a global movement to ban smartphones in schools. Within India, states like Gujarat and Delhi have issued directives to teachers and parents on how to limit students’ exposure to social media and phones. The government is “worried” about what this exposure is doing to children’s mental health.

It’s worth noting, though, that such states have only issued “guidelines” and haven’t implemented a total ban on phones in schools. And a judgement by the Delhi High Court this month called a total ban “unrealistic”. The court said instead that kids should be taught to use phones responsibly.

There’s some truth to the sailed boat argument—there isn’t any point trying to ban an integral mode of communication for a generation that’s never known a world without screens. But there are political and practical reasons, too, for not banning cellphones. For governments, it’s important to have more technology in schools, because it’s a stated goal of India’s flagship National Education Policy. Banning cellphones, or restricting screen time, may be at odds with what policymakers, at least in spirit, want to happen in schools.

The second, and more practical reason, is that for large swathes of school-going children in India, phones could become a lifeline for a better quality of education. This started during Covid as a necessity, when all of a sudden, content from around the country and the world was available to Indian students. It wasn’t a perfect system. Not all of it was quality content, or relatable, or even uniformly accessible given the differences in affordability and connectivity for different segments of students. But it exposed the staid and rigid education system to new possibilities. Kids could learn online, provided they had the right support. The belief also fuelled numerous edtech plans in India, if only briefly.

The effects of Covid linger: a report on the state of elementary education in rural India shows that close to 50% of kids between six and 16 have access to a smartphone, though the usage varies between boys and girls. Average screen time is between 0–2 hours, and there’s relatively little variation in use between different parental income groups.

The report, in particular, highlights how much children now use their phones for education.

“ …while smartphones are primarily used for entertainment purposes, such as playing games, downloading, and watching movies, and listening to songs, their role in education is also noteworthy. We observed that a notable portion of children, specifically 34.9% of the children, rely on their smartphones to access study materials, demonstrating how these devices serve as valuable tools for learning.”

These findings echo what the Annual Status of Education Report highlighted in last year, that nearly two-thirds of students who know how to use a cellphone used it to swap notes, watch educational videos online, or solve doubts.

Not too long ago, smartphones were the only link to the classroom for many. That exposure has had unintended consequences that governments now feel they have to step in to solve, partially because schools can only police what happens within the classroom and their premises, not what happens at home. 

The High Court in Delhi has set some reasonable guidelines to help limit screen time for kids, which include teaching kids about responsible use, depositing their phones during the school day, and restricting use in classrooms and shared spaces. But the real question is, who implements these guidelines? And will schools restrict smartphone usage, even in places where kids may depend on them for study material and exposure to quality content? Or are these guidelines only meant to restrict the use of social media? 

Chances are, we haven’t heard the last of this from the government.

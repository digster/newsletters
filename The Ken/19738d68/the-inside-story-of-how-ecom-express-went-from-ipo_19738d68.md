Good Morning Ishan,
Say you’re a board member of a logistics company. You’re getting ready for an EGM, maybe even putting on a tie. Then the chairman logs in and says: Oh, by the way, the company was sold last month. I wasn’t told either.
This isn’t corporate satire. It’s exactly what happened at Ecom Express.
In April, Delhivery struck a deal to acquire nearly all of Ecom for Rs 1,400 crore. Except not really—Rs 1,100 crore, after subtracting Ecom’s money that Delhivery will now use to integrate it.
That’s a steal for a company with Rs 2,500 crore in annual revenue—and a Sebi-approved IPO just eight months earlier. A Rs 7,000 crore listing plan, no less.
So, why the fire sale?
Its largest customer, Meesho, had walked away. Two of the co-founders had passed away. The new CEO had no logistics experience, but did have a very generous compensation package. What followed was a steady unravelling.
Also, Delhivery did what any good acquirer does before a takeover: it publicly questioned Ecom’s claims in its DRHP. Something about fake pin codes. Just enough doubt to derail its IPO—and justify a markdown.
The best part? The board didn’t discuss any of this. Not the sale. Not the IPO withdrawal. In fact, the minority shareholders read about the deal in the papers. The real winners, though, were the venture-capital funds with waterfall clauses.

20+ unicorns chase $10B from IPOs. But what’s in it for retail investors? Just this week, Shiprocket and Groww filed confidential DRHPs with Sebi, joining the long line of unicorns looking to mine the public markets this year

By Suprita Anupam
6 min read

Good Morning Ishan,

You are reading our subscriber-only newsletter Ka-Ching!—a weekly commentary on fintech and personal finance

All money isn’t created equal. 

A rupee from your wallet doesn’t carry the same weight as a rupee from a VC, or a startup founder, or even an angel investor. It’s something a passive investor and one of Infosys’ co-founders once told me. 

Money is relative, he said. Its real value swings this way and that depending on when you have it, and how much. 

Just ask a startup founder—what would they do if they had to raise $1 million? The answer will almost certainly depend on whether they already have $100 million in the bank or just $100,000 that’ll run out in a few months. In the first case, they might not even deign to consider the opportunity. In the second, they’ll probably jump at it, even if it means giving away a fat chunk of the startup.

Early money is almost always the most powerful—people who get in on the ground floor get a much bigger piece of the pie. A Rs 1 investment in the first year could well turn into Rs 100 in a few years. But for people who invest later, like retail investors who pick up shares in an IPO, their money has little of the heft that VC money has, because the juiciest portions have already been sliced and served. The conventional paradigm, of course, being that VCs as early investors take the highest risk, and so deserve the biggest returns. 

Just this week, logistics platform Shiprocket and stock broking platform Groww joined the long line of unicorns looking to list on the public markets this year. There are over 20 such companies now, looking to raise a cumulative $10 billion at a massive total valuation of over $100 billion. But many of them aren’t yet profitable. (In 2021, market regulator Sebi, faced with multiple startups listing abroad, had permitted loss-making companies to file for IPO.) Their valuations have also been pushed sky-high by VCs. So what is on the table for retail investors? 

Are we being asked to jump in when the risk is still high, but the rewards might only be… meh? 

Is that why, despite the significant level of noise being made around unicorn IPOs, there doesn’t seem to be much excitement on the ground? 

View in Browser

Take Ather Energy, which listed earlier this month. Its IPO got just enough demand to become fully subscribed, but it was hardly blockbuster stuff. In contrast, serious attention—and money—is flowing toward companies like NSE India, Tata Capital, HDB Financial, and Hero Fincorp in the grey market, none of which are flashy startups. Granted, other startups such as Zomato, coworking startup Awfis Space Solutions, and Zaggle have managed to deliver decent returns to IPO investors, but they are by no means the norm. Stock prices of Paytm*, Ola Electric, Nykaa, and Mobikwik, in contrast, have seen a fall. 

The difference, if you ask traders and market analysts, is that the latter group has built trust and delivered returns in the grey market, according to data sourced from various platforms tracking unlisted shares. They have also delivered consistent profits. Even NSE India, which has been dealing with a dispute with market regulator Sebi, is now offering to settle the matter for $118 million, Bloomberg reported this week, and is expected to get listed later this year. 

A cofounder of a trading platform that deals with unlisted shares put it plainly:

“Some of these unicorn shares aren’t even getting demand. Availability is limited, sure—but there’s not much appetite either.”

This appetite gap is showing up in returns to investors. Stocks of long-time IPO aspirant Oyo**, for instance, crashed by almost 60% between December 2021 and May 2024 in the grey market, before starting to recover after the company’s profits began improving. Meanwhile, share prices of NSE India, Tata Capital, and HDB Financial have more than doubled over the past year, as seen in the charts below from data platform Unlistedarena.

For large VCs, though, this IPO rush is mostly about exits. They’ve been waiting a while to cash out. 

Prosus, for instance, doubled its money with Swiggy’s IPO last year—raking in Rs 4,150 crore with a partial exit. Its remaining Rs 10,800 crore investment in Swiggy is now worth about Rs 23,000 crore. Others are gearing up to give their VCs exits too. Urban Company’s offer for sale accounts for 77% of its IPO size—Accel will offload shares worth Rs 433 crore in the startup; Elevation Capital, Rs 346 crore; Tiger Global, Rs 303 crore; VY Capital, Rs 216 crore; and Bessemer, Rs 173 crore. 

Then, there’s Lightspeed, which is looking to cash in its big bets on Physics Wallah, Oyo, and Zepto. Peak XV wants exits via Groww, Meesho, Ola, and Pine Labs. 

So here’s the question retail investors are asking: if the insiders are lining up to exit, why should we line up to enter? After all, most of the companies are either loss-making, or have very high valuations, or both. 

Market sentiments remain a bit uncertain. Global trade tensions aren’t helping. And startups are struggling to raise late-stage capital. Which means IPOs are now the default option for many, even if not necessarily the best one.

But if you are a retail investor with the choice of investing your hard-earned money in an IPO, would you go for loss-making startups with sky-high valuations or legacy firms with a proven record of making money (even if they are a bit boring compared to all the glitz and glamour of the former)?

If you ask me, the retail rupee deserves better than the high-risk, low-reward gambles many startup IPOs seem to be offering.

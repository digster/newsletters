BigBasket, India’s largest online grocer by far, emerged as one of the last ones standing out of India’s bruising online grocery wars. A war of capital attrition that started around 2013, peaked in 2015, and then popped in 2016.

It capped off its success early this year with a $300-million fundraise from the Alibaba Group. The funding was basically a validation of its Big Kahuna status in the sector, and the size of the round, a clear warning sign to competitors to back off. 

But while BigBasket may have won the online grocery battle, the war shifted under its feet to focus on micro-delivery. One where dozens of narrow and nimble startups popped up and started attacking lucrative niches within its baskets, and the behaviour of its customers.

Faced with the prospect of its best customers switching to nimbler competitors, and leaving only low-value or low-frequency items in their BigBasket basket, it decided, if you can’t beat ‘em, join ‘em. Or better yet, buy ‘em.

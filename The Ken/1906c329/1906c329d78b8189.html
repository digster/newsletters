Good Morning Ishan,

It takes two minutes—just about the time you’ll need to read this email—to swap a depleted battery out of an electric vehicle and replace it with a charged pack. For the many drivers whose livelihoods depend on how many kilometres they can cover in a shift, that speed matters immensely because the alternative is lengthy waits on the side of the road, a cable tethering the vehicle to a charging pylon as the power gauge slowly ticks up, 1% at a time.

That’s all to say that battery-swapping stations are an important piece of infrastructure if we want to see commercial fleets ditch gas for electricity. You’d think regulatory bodies would be doing everything they could to give battery-swapping service providers support to build out quickly.

As it turns out, you’d be wrong!

Nathan’s story for the day covers companies in this line of business. Sun Mobility has 630 stations across India, with another 10,000 in the works through a joint venture with the state-owned Indian Oil Corporation. Battery Smart has over 1,000 stations in operation, and raised US$65 million in series-B funding in June to take that number higher.

These firms and others built their networks without any public support. “Although the government says they love all its children equally, it was clear they loved charging more than swapping,” a co-founder of Raceenergy, another battery-swap company, told Nathan.

That void gave investors an opportunity—their cash became more valuable, especially at a time when fewer deals were happening. The consequence? You’ll need to read Nathan’s story to find out.

Good Morning Ishan,
 
I failed a (post-facto) blind test for chocolate milkshake last month. In a casual discussion on Fonterra Future Dairy in Mumbai, Mr Kishore Biyani, founder and CEO of the Future Group, asked me whether I liked my chocolate milkshake thick or watery. I said, don’t laugh at me, watery, but FFD’s blind test showed people overwhelmingly preferred it thick. So was the product, which launched a few weeks ago.
 
The point Mr Biyani wanted to make was that, by joining hands with the Future Group, the New Zealand-based dairy cooperative Fonterra has so many touchpoints with customers that they’d get their product (and the customers’ pulse) right. Some 5 million people every day actively engage in commerce with the group. The CEO of a fast-growing startup in the fresh foods space calls this ‘velocity’ of sale, an absolute critical ingredient in this business.
 
That, of course, is a big differentiator between what Fonterra is doing and what the French food MNC Danone tried to do before quitting last year, but it’s not all.
 
The market for value-added dairy products is heating up. Parag—which bought Danone’s Indian yoghurt plant—and FMCG majors like ITC and Britannia, are launching more such products. Lactalis, another global dairy major which has acquired a few businesses in India, wants to control milk procurement here. Fonterra is still keen. It wants in on the Indian market, which, already worth $78.5 billion and growing at 15%, is poised to emerge as the largest dairy producer by next year.
 
Right from the get-go, Fonterra is avoiding following Danone’s missteps. And even though each of its products is developed at its Auckland lab—something Mr Biyani repeatedly emphasised—Fonterra is also looking to develop products and distribution beyond its tie-up with Future Consumer.

Where do tall Indians even shop?. At a seasonal fair called ‘Mercy of the Brands’

By Rounak Kumar Gunjan https://the-ken.com/writers/rounakthe-ken-com/

Tomorrow, he’ll dish out another issue of Long and Short https://the-ken.com/long-and-short/stories/. It’s only fair he gets a breather, right?

But the proverbial show must go on. So for Trade Tricks this week, it’s me, Rounak—a forever back-bencher for reasons that will become clear very quickly. And I want to use this edition to talk about a lifelong peeve. 

Why do Indian apparel brands hate tall people? 

Don’t get me wrong. I know I can’t expect too many options in a country where the average height https://discovery.ucl.ac.uk/id/eprint/10116213/?utm_source=chatgpt.com for men is under 5’6”, and even lower for women. But no one warned me it would be this crazy. 

For as long as I can remember, I’ve always stood out (quite literally). School, college, concerts… I’ve always been one of the tallest. In fact, at 6’3”, I’m an inch taller than quintessential Bollywood flagpole Amitabh Bachchan. And nearly as tall as his son, Abhishek Bachchan. Yes, the temperature up here is cooler, and of course, I bleed Complan instead of blood.

I could face my fate with more equanimity if the problems stopped at low lintels, tiny bathtubs, short railway berths, and cramped aircraft seats. But shoes? Trousers? Shirts? 

If I pick up a trouser of my waist size from any regular store, the length is so short I end up looking like Chaplin—only worse. Full-sleeved shirts are never really full-sleeved. Thankfully, clothes can be stitched to order, but footwear is an entire sob story in itself. 

For instance, as of 14 April, Amazon, Flipkart, Myntra, and other major e-commerce websites have a total of zero footwear products in sizes 32 cm or above. The last time I found a sneaker I loved—in terms of looks, fit, and budget—was about four years ago. Pretty much every piece of footwear that I’ve ever purchased has been a compromise on at least one of the three fronts—mostly fit. Even then, it usually takes me weeks to find a likely pair. 

And it’s not like I’m the only one with the big-feet problems.

Tall people, tall problems

In 2023, the Central Leather Research Institute in Chennai conducted a country-wide survey on foot sizes in India and submitted its recommendations to India’s commerce ministry. As per the ministry spokesperson I spoke to on Monday, the average foot size for Indian men was 25.5cm. For women, 23.5cm. But there were over 7 million Indians with sizes larger than 30 cm at the time. And I’m sure those numbers have only gone up since. 

But footwear that actually fits is ridiculously difficult to find for a lot of us. And when we do, they’re often very expensive. I can’t count the number of times I’ve helplessly ogled at New Balance’s 574s, Asics’ Gel-9s, Adidas’ Sambas, or Puma’s suede classics. Some of the Nike ranges that offer these sizes retail at Rs 15,000 and above.

The Linkedin-addicted part of my brain can’t help asking: why aren’t companies identifying this gap and solving the problem? Why is this so difficult? 

“Deadstock,” said a Delhi-based footwear retailer.

“I’m fine with turning away one customer a month due to lack of size options. But the cost of keeping this size is far more than selling the occasional one.”

Multi-brand footwear store owner in Connaught Place, New Delhi

My 7 million-strong big-footed compatriots may make up a market as numerous as the entire population of Hong Kong, Bulgaria, or Paraguay, but in the Indian context, that’s not even an entire Lucknow.

Retailers across Chennai, Chandigarh, Mumbai, Kolkata, and Noida have similar answers. And yeah, I checked. Because once, I desperately wanted to buy Nike’s AirMax 90, but not even the official website had my size. 

If you believe the retailers I spoke to, tall people might have it relatively easier in states such as Punjab, Jammu, Haryana, and Kerala because there’s some demand in these parts. But eastern and western India see negligible sales of plus-size footwear. And discount strategies don’t help. After all, it’s not like the items remain unsold because of their cost. It’s a case of no takers. So not stocking up makes complete business sense. 

And even if you were a startup looking to build a business in a cozy niche, a potential market of just 7 million people doesn’t sound too ambitious, does it? An old report https://www.wsj.com/articles/feet-are-getting-bigger-and-many-people-wear-shoes-that-dont-fit-right-1405466145 by the Wall Street Journal talks about how offering such large footsizes “fosters special loyalty from the customer who needs a certain width”. Unfortunately, it doesn’t look like many brands share that belief in India. 

There do seem to be some exceptions. Like Bacca Bucci, a Delhi-based footwear company which launched Project Plus in 2023, under which it now makes shoes up to UK size 15. Its co-founder Anuj Nevatia did not respond to my calls and messages on Monday. 

For now, it looks like people like me will just have to soldier on. Never mind that the boots don’t fit. 

In case you, too, are part of this ignored company and are looking for some options for tall people, I’ve found that Bonkers is a decent bet for bottoms, Spykar and Rare Rabbit for shirts, and Adidas and H&M for longline tees.

For everything else, you’ll have to wait on the mercy of brands.

IPOs are when companies truly grow up, never mind SoftBank. Like human beings, well-adjusted startups too mature into confident and growing companies. By that count, Uber had one of the most privileged and cloistered childhoods in startup history, something it must learn how to deal with as an adult.
 
Could Slack be the well-formed adult public investors are looking for? In today’s story, Sumanth tries to answer this question by parsing its S-1.
 
Like Uber, Slack, too, doesn’t generate profits yet. But unlike Uber, its losses aren’t growing in lock-step with its revenues. Better yet, each dollar it invests in sales today leads to $1.11 next year in profits. And because Slack is a subscription-based company, profits from new customers don’t vanish with each ride or transaction.
 
Slack’s gross margins are a whopping 87%, but tempered by sales & marketing expenses of 58%.
 
There’s a lot for everyone to learn from Slack’s birth, early childhood and teenage years, as it has been an “exemplar all along points of the spectrum”, says Sumanth. But its passage to adulthood is what’s most important.
 
Especially for SoftBank, the “pit-stop” to an IPO. Because if Slack’s IPO isn’t a success, the pit-stop may not be all that necessary for the next generation of valuable growth startups.

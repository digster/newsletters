Good Morning Ishan,
 
If there is one thing the coronavirus has been unable to lock down, it's the sheer will to survive.
 
And not just of humans, but also businesses and startups (excluding those that pivoted from unrelated businesses to become e-grocers; what were they thinking?).
 
So it's heartening to see that some in India's two-wheeler ride-sharing sector went back to the drawing board to figure out their post-pandemic survival plan. Lest we forget, ride-sharing, hospitality and aviation are the three industries that are facing the brunt of this crisis.
 
The most natural move for Bounce, India's largest two-wheeler ride-sharing platform, as well as a few others like ONN Bikes, was to have longer rental periods. Less natural was the move to sell off some of their vehicles.
 
But their most unnatural shift comes with the advent of a lease-to-own model for their bikes and scooters. It's a smart way to become asset-light in a world that frowns upon the word "sharing".
 
Not just that, these companies are also sorting out the messy financing bits, including almost halving the down payment one needs to make for a bike. It's also not a one-size-fits-all leasing model—the mobility company, an individual or a dealership could be the owner of the bike. The promise of a new sales channel has also convinced bike manufacturers. It seems like a win-win-win-win situation.
 
But, as Olina, the author of today's story, puts it, "It alters the company's DNA drastically." These mobility companies will now have to stitch together a series of legacy businesses like leasing, financing, sales, re-sale, and servicing. They get into direct competition with dealerships. And the "tech" part of their business seems to be receding to the background.
 
I asked Olina if the lease-to-own model means I have to dodge fewer of these bikes parked willy-nilly on roads or footpaths? She, sadly, couldn't get the answer to that, but she does have a cracker of a story on the new business model of ride-sharing.

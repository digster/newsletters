1. Creating and embracing dilemmas 🤗

“In any moment of decision, the best thing you can do is the right thing, the next best thing is the wrong thing, and the worst thing you can do is nothing”—Theodore Roosevelt

 

Most of us hate conflict and would rather avoid it. And when it comes to decisions, we subconsciously choose the path of least resistance. 

 

While this is perfectly okay for the hundreds of minor decisions we make each day, it fails us when we need to make big decisions whose consequences (good or bad) play out over years, or even decades. 

 

Because those decisions have tradeoffs, risks, consequences, and conflicts. And by avoiding dealing with them consciously, we make suboptimal decisions. 
“If you take the best text in economics by Mankiw, he says intelligent people make decisions based on opportunity costs—in other words, it's your alternatives that matter. That's how we make all of our decisions.”

Charlie Munger  

That’s from Poor Charlie’s Almanack. It’s a tome of a book on the late Charlie Munger, Warren Buffet’s partner and equal in Berkshire Hathaway. There’s a review of the book later in this edition.

 

I used Munger’s quote here to illustrate how decision making is always about alternatives. Do you switch jobs or stay on? Do you start up or aim for that promotion? Do you buy a house or continue to rent? 

 

Often, we pay much less attention to our alternatives. Which means, the above dilemmas get reduced to simplistic binaries devoid of conflict. 

 

Should we switch jobs? Should we start up? Should we buy a house?

 

To help us make better decisions, we need to bring back the conflict and complexity. We need to actively frame them as dilemmas. 

 

And one of the best ways to frame and tackle them is through a much-maligned MBA tool: the 2x2 Matrix.
“The simplest 2 × 2 problem-solving behaviour involves looking at the other side of an issue before reaching a conclusion. A simple “what-if” exercise will accomplish this. Dilemmas are a more interesting case. Dilemmas pull us simultaneously in competing directions, each compelling in its own right. Although dilemmas rarely feel good, they often contain the seeds of deeper understanding and a superior solution than we are otherwise capable of finding. The trouble with our experience of dilemmas is that they generally happen to us, and we feel out of control.”

The Power of the 2x2 Matrix—Using 2x2 Thinking to Solve Business Problems and Make Better Decisions—by Alex Lowy and Phil Hood  

As an MBA-turned-journalist(-turned-entrepreneur later), I’ve drawn much ridicule from journalist colleagues over the years for my love for 2 x 2 matrices. But I swear by them.

 

Because the 2 x 2 Matrix helps our brains visualise the tension and conflict in complex decisions. For instance, one of the most important ways to think of your time in a professional context is through the decades-old Eisenhower Matrix. Adding the secondary axis of Importance makes us question the very primacy of the axis of Urgency.

 

Or, if you’d prefer a more recent example, the Jeff Bezos Matrix. The Irreversibility of a decision makes us see the Consequentiality of a decision more critically.

 

Then, there is the “Doing Things Right Matrix”, which forces us to balance efficiency (doing things right) versus effectiveness (doing the right things). Management guru Peter Drucker explained it best: “There is surely nothing quite so useless as doing with great efficiency that which should not be done at all.”

In the spirit of the annual appraisal season, I’ll leave you with my final 2 x 2 Matrix: the Archetypal Individual Dilemma. It forces us to balance the fulfilment of our professional needs against the contributions we make to our organisations. 

Both above images are from the book I quoted from earlier, which is a fairly breezy and interesting read while being packed with dozens of ready-to-use 2 x 2 matrices for all sorts of individual, team, and organisational decision making: The Power of the 2 x 2 Matrix: Using 2 x 2 Thinking to Solve Business Problems and Make Better Decisions—by Alex Lowy and Phil Hood.

In the words of the authors: “If this book could teach only one lesson, it would be to encourage people to learn and solve problems through the intentional creation and resolution of dilemmas. By challenging ourselves and those around us to think at higher logical levels, we raise the quality of deliberation and the decisions and agreements we reach.”

 

So, what dilemmas are you going to construct, embrace, and resolve before 2023 ends?

4. #FPBooks 📚

We have three book reviews in this edition.

The first one comes from Harshit Bansal, a second-year college student from Delhi University “trying to become the best version of himself”.
 
The Courage to be Disliked—by Fumitake Koga and Ichiro Kishimi
“The book is written as a conversation between a youth or you could even say us and a philosopher. The youth keeps asking questions with the intent of proving the philosopher wrong and every time how beautifully the philosopher answers. It talks about how we don't live to satisfy the expectations of others nor do they live to satisfy ours, separation of tasks, adlerian psychology which talks about the purpose of an event and not looking to them as cause and effect, denying the desire of recognition and many more. I believe after reading this the reader will feel much lighter. I definitely did.”

The last one comes from me. I’d started reading Poor Charlie’s Almanack earlier this year, but never quite got round to finishing it because it is long and oddly structured. But after Charlie Munger’s death last week, I decided to plod on through and finish it. The first 30-40% of the book is a good read, but after that, it gets harder to finish because it’s ten different speeches made by the late Munger over the years.

 

Poor Charlie’s Almanack —Edited by Peter D. Kaufman

 

This review I found explains my thoughts about the book, so I’ll just link to it. 
“This book is for Charlie’s admirers or people who want to glean something about what made him an uncanny, successful investor. I am well aware that many people are in love with this book, however, it is probably a reflection of their respect for Charles Munger rather than an objective measure of the quality of the book. I found Poor Charlie’s Almanack to be both interesting and frustrating to read. What I found most interesting about this book was Charlie’s hunger for wisdom and his ability to think accurately and unconventionally. This book certainly contains good advice, and Mr. Munger makes a persuasive case for all professionals – especially those in business – to conduct themselves at all times with integrity. Mr. Munger has a broad range of interests across multiple disciplines and appears to read college textbooks for fun. He demonstrates himself to be an unconventional thinker, and he offers convincing criticism of the postsecondary education system that is stunted by its lack of multidisciplinary approaches. Most importantly, Charles Munger shares the importance of psychological factors to better understand business and investing.
 
What I found frustrating about this book is that it is physically very awkward to read. It is large and heavy (over 5 pounds), and the glossy pages reflect light, making it hard to see the text. As the book mostly contains speeches and quotes, it lends itself to a poor organization of useful ideas. There is much redundancy of content and anecdotes as Charlie repeats his best ideas in his speeches. The reader has to hunt around for the pearls of wisdom. The sidebars feel forced in many cases and, at times, contain irrelevant information. Mr. Munger makes references to having a checklist of ways he evaluates businesses to invest in but that is mostly a tease as he does not reveal what he actually does. Instead, he suggests that investors – especially large ones – study and emulate Berkshire Hathaway. Peter Kaufman, the editor of this book, attempts to stitch together Charlie’s approaches in a section titled “An Investing Principles Checklist” but it is certainly not the checklist that Charles Munger uses.”

5. #FPHabits ✅

A habit recommendation from Manisha Yadav, who is a “Governance Consultant @Samagra, Passionate for impact and creating beautiful things through art, communication, and technology”:

 

“Not eating after sunset, no matter what comes in the way—it's a non-negotiable.”
“In March 2020, I kicked off my fitness journey, starting with a major overhaul of my eating habits. Now, I'm a die-hard foodie—I practically live for my meals. So, why the fitness kick? Simple: I wanted to indulge in more food. But here's the twist—my job in sales wasn't the most ideal for sticking to a complicated plan as I was out and about on the field. I needed something easy, sustainable, and, most importantly, functional. The whole five smaller meals routine? Not my style. Plus, I wasn't keen on the constant blood glucose spike, the science didn't make sense there for me. So, I opted for three substantial, freshly-made meals at specific times, and snacks got the boot.
 
I also waved goodbye to three troublemakers: Maida, fried foods, and sugar. It worked wonders for my energy levels. I shed 12 kgs in the first year and have managed to keep it off even as I've upped my food intake. Three big meals at fixed times. Last meal not beyond 6.30. Steer clear of Maida, fried stuff, and sugar on a day-to-day. It works. Everyone around me now knows it, so I don't even have to face the peer pressure of indulging in, say, the sweet treats or the samosa party. They don't even try to offer! What a relief. And here's the cool part—I still get to indulge in my favourite Italian feast and that irresistible pain au chocolat, but only on the sly, a few days a month. Living the fit life without missing out on the good stuff!”

And what has that led to?
“Fitter, healthier gut, amazing energy levels. Most importantly, minimised decision making. Lesser options to choose from.”

6. #FPLinks 🔖
“A sport is easily one of the best mediums—whether an individual is actively involved or is a passive participant—to learn the lessons of leadership and perspectives. Usually, our narratives of sporting legends are focused mostly on the males. Even sporting rivalries are pivoted that way. Fedal. Messi vs. CR7. On the contrary, this long essay—arguably the most interesting, the most emotional and the most stirring long-read I came across this year—talks about the tennis rivalry of Chris Evert and Martina Navratilova, their on-field battles and camaraderie, how they bonded with each other, AND most importantly, how each of them is the other's moral and emotional support and sounding board till date, especially in the wake of their cancer journeys.”

—Giridharan Raghuraman: “A lover of various forms of expression. Dosas and long-reads are my staples."
Bitter rivals. Beloved friends. Survivors—The Washington Post
 
After 50 years, Chris Evert and Martina Navratilova understand each other like no one else can. When cancer came, they knew where to turn.

And here are two wonderfully disconnected but related pieces on the importance of silence and white space, whether in podcasts, poetry, or design. 

 

Smart Speed Broke My Brain—by Robb Knight

 
“I haven’t listened to a show at standard speed for years. After I turned off smart speed and listened to a show I realised how calm I felt compared to usual. It was relaxing. I had time to process what was being said without also trying to hear the next part of the conversation. I realised for the past decade I’ve been on high alert just to listen to a podcast, concentrating really hard to consume all the information. I was stressed from something that’s supposed to be enjoyable. I was accustomed to readying myself to listen to a show. Smart speed had broken my brain.”
And:
 
Functional White: Crafting Space & Silence—by Orlando White
“The white space is just as important as the text in a poem, whether it’s the counter that shapes an O or S, a line break after a word or a caesura within a line. With space, one can shape sound and language to create a poetic field. And with that, one may express a silence. When we write or type, our focus seems to be on a small percentage of the paper, which is, where the text is placed. But around it there lies a pause, a momentary rest that hibernates. Much like a semibreve rest within a measure or like a crease pattern after unfolding a sheet of paper: space holds rhythm and structure. Plus, what are letters, words, punctuation, and sentences without the music and form of the page?”

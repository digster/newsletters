How Sebi and 328 shareholders minted India’s priciest stock in a single day. The stock’s price discovery does not obviate its risks though 
 By Anand Kalyanaraman https://the-ken.com/writers/anandthe-ken-com/

On 29 October, Elcid Investments made history by dislodging https://the-ken.com/story/why-mrf-nestle-and-other-pricey-stocks-are-happy-being-exceptions/ India’s leading tyre maker, MRF, as the country’s priciest stock. From about Rs 3, the share price of this almost-unknown investment firm shot up to over Rs 2.36 lakh (US$2,800) in a single day. Since then, it has been hitting the 5% upper circuit every trading day, closing at Rs 2.87 lakh a piece on Tuesday.

Dhiraj Mittal, a shareholder of Elcid, expects the stock to hit the upper circuit daily till it reaches Rs 4 lakh or so a piece. “That’s a price at which many shareholders might be ready to sell,” he told The Ken.

Mittal, a chartered accountant, knows a thing or two about Elcid. He was part of a small club of less than 330 minority shareholders in the company and had led a crusade of sorts when its promoters mooted a proposal to delist the company in 2022. The floor price that the promoters set for the delisting then was about Rs 1.61 lakh against a share price of about Rs 17. Nice? Nope. The intrinsic value of each Elcid share, Mittal thought, was much higher at about Rs 6 lakh. Here’s how:

With a 4.3% stake in Asian Paints, Elcid is a promoter entity of the paints major. “Based on this, each Elcid share was equal to 203 shares of Asian Paints, and the true intrinsic value was about Rs 6.3 lakh,” Mittal had told The Ken then. The Vakil family—promoter of Elcid and part of the promoter group of Asian Paints—holds as much as 75% stake in Elcid, with minority shareholders like Mittal holding the rest. The promoters have been wanting to take Elcid private for a very long time now.

As with most delisting proposals, the fly in the ointment has been the price, in addition to the many other complications, about which Anirudh Somani and I wrote https://the-ken.com/story/asian-paints-jindal-group-promoters-vs-minority-investors-the-fault-lines-in-delisting/ in September 2022.

The delisting proposal fell through, but it wasn’t the first time. The first delisting attempt, in 2013, also failed due to disagreements between Elcid’s promoters and its minority shareholders over the ‘fair’ price.

A lot has changed over the past week, though.

The ‘price discovery’ for the Elcid stock got underway, which might enable its delisting sooner or later.

But how exactly was the price discovered?

The ‘special call auction’ mechanism https://www.sebi.gov.in/legal/circulars/jun-2024/introduction-of-a-special-call-auction-mechanism-for-price-discovery-of-scrips-of-listed-investment-companies-ics-and-listed-investment-holding-companies-ihcs-_84319.html put in place by market regulator Sebi in June did the trick. Sebi rolled this out for listed investment companies and investment holding companies that were trading infrequently and at a price significantly lower than their book value (assets minus liabilities). Elcid fit the bill to a T.

In this special call auction, there are no price bands—for both the upper and lower price circuits—at which trading in a stock generally halts for the day.

The circuit limits for Elcid earlier, at 5% of the previous day’s closing price, meant that there were no trades happening. Who in their right mind would sell a share for single or double digits when the perceived intrinsic value ran into the lakhs of rupees?

In fact, to enable price discovery, minority shareholders like Mittal sought the removal of the circuit filters back in 2022 as well.

The 29 October special call auction did just that. Nine stocks, including TVS Holdings and Maharashtra Scooters, were part of the auction. But it was the Elcid stock that really took off, zooming a cool 6,700,000%. The circuit filters were back the next day, and the stock has been hitting its 5% upper circuit limit since then.

So, who bought and sold Elcid’s shares in the special auction? While public shareholders hold 50,000 shares in Elcid, trade volume on the counter that day was just 241 shares.

Existing shareholders did participate in the trade in small quantities—on both the buy and sell sides—to enable the auction to go through, said Mittal. As per Sebi’s rules, “The special call auction session shall be treated as successful if price discovery is based on orders from at least 5 Permanent Account Number (PAN) based unique buyers and sellers.” If the call auction is not a success on day 1, it shall continue on the next day, and till such time as the price is discovered. Besides, the special call auction mechanism shall be provided only once a year.

That was an opportunity Elcid’s minority shareholders could not afford to miss. The price discovered in the special auction was close to what Elcid stock was quoting in the unlisted market, claimed Mittal, adding that by becoming India’s priciest stock, Elcid has shot to the limelight, generating a lot of interest in the stock and its perceived intrinsic value. That has translated into trades, even if in small quantities, on the subsequent days. “For many, it is becoming a collectable, giving them bragging rights of owning India’s most expensive stock—akin to having a share of Warren Buffett’s Berkshire Hathaway.”

But the big trades will happen only at a higher price, he added, around the Rs 4–4.5 lakh levels. “Long-term investors in stocks like Elcid might be fine with a discount of 25–30% to the intrinsic value. They will not settle for a bigger cut.”

That’s one of the key reasons why it’s quite risky for new investors to buy the stock now. There are no guarantees in the stock market, and there’s no assurance that the stock will continue to hit the upper circuits even if minority shareholders like Mittal would want it to.

An added big risk is that Elcid’s promoters might take another shot at delisting—now that the price has been ‘discovered’. And the price they offer might not be too high compared to what the stock trades at in the market. Sebi’s easing https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/markets/stocks/news/sebi-amends-rules-introduces-fixed-price-process-for-voluntary-delisting/articleshow/113704761.cms?from=mdr of the delisting rules issued in September could tilt the odds in the promoters’ favour.

Mittal thinks that Elcid’s promoters should indeed try to delist now, given the Asian Paints stock has been subdued for a few years. “The valuation of Asian Paints is likely to go up further in the coming years and so will Elcid’s then.” But he added that any delisting attempt should be at a fair price—around the Rs 4–4.5 lakh range. “At current levels, there is still a big gap.”

Elcid’s promoters have an ace up their sleeve, though.

Just a couple of days before the delisting proposal in 2022, Hydra Trading—an investment entity controlled by the Danis, another promoter group of Asian Paints—bought about 9% of Elcid from a minority investor Rajan Shah of 3A Capital. Hydra Trading is also categorised as a minority shareholder in Elcid.

It seems reasonable to bet that it will accept Elcid’s promoters’ offer in any delisting proposal as and when it comes.

“Still, it’s not easy,” countered Mittal, adding that other minority investors will have to cooperate for any delisting to go through.

How the cookie will crumble is up in the air now.

Punting on the Elcid stock is high risk—not to mention high value.

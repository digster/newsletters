In pursuit of profit, the company is sacrificing the very qualities that transformed it from a disruptive startup to a foodtech behemoth

Deputy Editor in Southeast Asia

Good Morning Ishan,

We love a nice Skunkworks story—smart people doing innovative things, red tape snipped, flush with resources. Yet, that rarely happens in practice.

What’s more likely is that cash is burned so that products or platforms get made. They may even have a meaningful run, only for the business model to hit a wall because they simply can’t reach the point that makes them profitable.

Swiggy wanted to give its moonshots a fighting chance by setting up a team called Swiggy Labs, where the company’s “tinkering culture” could thrive. But a change in attitude, where unit economics became more important, has killed off the spirit of experimentation.

The result is the termination of several initiatives as the foodtech giant seeks profitability, as well as a cultural change. This is particularly significant as it’s heading to the bourses. Gaurav sheds light on the finer details and impact of this shift in today’s story.

Dare to detox

Today is Nyepi, the Day of Silence in Bali. It’s a day of self-reflection, meditation, and fasting. For 24 hours, there are to be no fires, no work, and—for those who take it to greater lengths—no talking. Power and internet services are cut off in some parts of the Indonesian province. (There are places where people look the other way, like in resorts.)

Rides become a bit more difficult to book starting the evening before the holiday. Restaurants stay closed, so meal-delivery services become moot. From the outside looking in, the age-old practice now encapsulates a compulsory digital detox.

If you’re reading this, chances are you’re a very plugged-in individual.

Nyepi makes me think of one of Cal Newport’s books, Digital Minimalism, which was published in 2019 but has only become more relevant as time passes. When notifications on our phones prime our behaviour, and every facet of work involves staring at a screen, there’s merit in actively decluttering the streams that draw our attention.

If you decide to read the book I mentioned, consider getting a physical paper copy!

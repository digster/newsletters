Good Morning Ishan,

One man’s misery is another man’s fortune. There’s no other way to describe what’s happening in the automotive industry. Midyear, when Indian auto sales hit a 19-year-low, Chinese-owned MG Motors, made a record.

Barely two weeks after bookings opened for its internet-connected car Hector, MG had to stop taking orders because of the overwhelming demand. In October, MG became the 10th most selling car brand in India overtaking Germany’s Skoda and Japan’s Nissan. Last week, MG unveiled its electrified SUV that hits the market in January.

As Indian automakers face a perfect storm—of regulations, economy and market forces— at least half a dozen Chinese companies are zipping straight into the parking lot. Even pledging to invest nearly $5 billion in India over the next three to five years.

It’s almost too easy for the Chinese. Nearly copy-pasting the smartphone playbook. Just as Xiaomi introduced mid-range smartphones with features then only available in high-end smartphones, MG and others are luring Indian customers with feature-rich cars. A Maruti Suzuki executive estimates that at least 50-60% of luxury car features have been brought down to the mid-price (Rs 15-20L) segment by MG Motors.

Hungry for experience, Indian buyers are willing to trade “cheap and unreliable” adjectives for “value for money” with respect to Chinese products. “The connected car you see today is laying the foundation for future mobility,” says a Great Wall Motors executive, who believes that it is just a matter of time before these features make their way to lower-end models.

To this if you add EVs, where 50% of a made-in-India EV anyway comes from China, the future of mobility starts to look predominantly Chinese. Because one needs to go big to gain scale and get a chance at EVs. (Which is why regions like the EU are looking at mobility as a matter of sovereignty.)

India is making some noises, too. But today, let’s hear what Pranav Balakrishnan and Pranav Shankar have to say. It’s a compelling story, that must travel.

Cloud kitchens—restaurant brands that only existed for delivery—were a phenomenon before the pandemic upended the restaurant industry. But since then, they’ve become even more indispensable to the industry, as consumers shifted to ordering in food in droves.
 
The first wave of cloud kitchens consisted of companies like Rebel Foods and Freshmenu, which launched virtual brands and only offered delivery. This was followed by aggregators like Zomato and Swiggy jumping on the bandwagon with their own cloud kitchen services or, in the case of Swiggy, their own restaurant brands as well.
 
Rebel Foods, arguably India’s largest cloud kitchen operator, operates various brands like Faasos, Behrouz Biryani, and Mandarin Oak out of the 325 cloud kitchens it has nationwide. 
 
Swiggy started its cloud kitchen service—Swiggy Access—in 2017. By early 2020, the company claimed to have created over 1,000 kitchen-only spaces for its restaurant partners. The pandemic, however, forced Swiggy to scale down its cloud ambitions very fast.
 
A new generation of players are emerging who consider themselves the third wave of this trend. They argue that copying Rebel Foods or Swiggy is hard for not just the average restaurateur, but also for ambitious chains. 
 
They’re positioning themselves as “neutral” hubs, where any restaurant can set up operations in as little as a week. 
 
There’s Kitchens@, each of whose hubs can host as many as 40 customer brands, including both large quick-service restaurant chains such as Dominos, Taco Bell, and SubWay, as well as smaller, local restaurants. Rival Kitchens Centre is present across 20 Indian cities, with around 80 hubs in total. 
 
The new generation of players wants to democratise cloud kitchens the way Amazon’s AWS democratised cloud computing. Meaning, anyone can spin up a cloud kitchen in just weeks and be guaranteed that the platform will not become a competitor too one day.

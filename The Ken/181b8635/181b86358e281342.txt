The most visible phase of India’s plastics ban https://www.livemint.com/news/india/single-use-plastic-ban-cpcb-issues-lists-of-items-prohibited-from-1-july-11655551855447.html is kicking in from today. 

Plastic straws, cups, glasses, flags, balloons, earbuds, and sundry such items will become history. Not because consumers suddenly became plastic-phobic at the turn of midnight on June 30, but because sellers and suppliers of such products will now be penalised by regulators.

Nearly 150 years after the first man-made plastic celluloid was invented, a global effort is being mounted to wean us from this now ubiquitous material. Life has come full circle. If the arrival of synthetic plastics freed us from nature and its associated supply constraints, today we are turning back to nature for clean alternatives. 

So India is banning single-use plastics. 

Let’s be clear, this will upset many plastics carts for many industries and sections of society. If you recall, in March, I had written about Reliance Industries’ US$6-billion single-use plastics business and the risks it faces https://the-ken.com/greenmargins/the-looming-6-bn-hole-in-reliances-pocket/ from just such bans. 

And right now,  juice and beverage giants, which account for only 0.05% of the total volume of these plastics, are staring at a scenario where their sales could fall by ~Rs 3,000 crore https://www.livemint.com/companies/news/beverage-manufacturers-wary-of-single-use-plastic-ban-from-1-july-11654708278026.html (US$384 million). 

The sector’s problems arise from an otherwise humble component of beverage packaging—the straw. The integrated plastic straws that come with many packaged beverages can’t be used anymore. So they will have to be replaced with paper straws, which are both more expensive and have to be imported. 

As for the compostable plastic that Indian regulators have allowed as a substitute, here’s the trouble…

Is India’s plastics policy just replacing one problem with another?

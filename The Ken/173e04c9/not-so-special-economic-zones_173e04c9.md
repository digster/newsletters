The consequences of this move go beyond the fortunes of the companies themselves. Units in SEZs account for
[60%](https://mercomindia.com/basic-customs-duty-solar-manufacturers-sezs/) of India’s cell-manufacturing capacity and 40% of its module-making capacity. Making the majority of the local manufacturing industry uncompetitive when the rest cannot meet the overall demand will put the brakes on India’s solar energy expansion. India’s installed solar power capacity has surged nearly 9X to over 35.3 GW in five years.
Solar manufacturers with facilities in SEZs have proposed an
[alternative](https://www.financialexpress.com/economy/solar-equipment-makers-in-sezs-pitch-for-equalisation-levy/2048442/) to the customs duty: an “equalisation levy” of 1-2% on SEZ units to make up for the concessions they receive to put them on a par with their Indian competitors outside SEZs.
If the government doesn’t play ball, it will be a repeat of an unsuccessful earlier attempt to discourage solar imports. In 2018, India levied a 25%
[safeguard duty](https://mercomindia.com/safeguard-duty-25-percent-solar/) on these imports, which partially affected SEZ units. While companies producing cells within SEZs were not affected, those that imported cells to assemble modules were given no relief.
SEZs, owing partly to intractable troubles with land acquisition, are not quite what they were touted to be. Since 2000, approvals have been given for
[423 SEZs](http://sezindia.nic.in/upload/5ecf8244e174fFACTSHEET.pdf), but only 240 are operational.
If India erects more tariff barriers in its quest to be
atmanirbhar (self-reliant), SEZs will lose their edge. On the other hand, exempting them from import levies, which will be accompanied by something like the equalisation levy, will take them closer to their peers in the DTA. Either way, SEZs will not be very special.

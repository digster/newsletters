Not-so-special economic zones
 
Seetharaman
 
India can hurt itself in more ways than one with its protectionist stance against China. The most obvious impact is due to India’s dependence on China for imports. Under a fifth of India’s merchandise imports are from China and so are more than two-thirds of India’s electronic components. 
 
Then there are the unintended consequences. Take special economic zones (SEZs), for example. An SEZ, according to the government, is
A specifically delineated duty free enclave and shall be deemed to be foreign territory for the purposes of trade operations and duties and tariffs. 

Goods and services going into the SEZ area from DTA shall be treated as exports and goods coming from the SEZ area into DTA shall be treated as if these are being imported.
DTA is domestic tariff area, which refers to the rest of the country. 
 
SEZs were introduced in 2000 as a way to attract foreign capital and boost exports. Sounds great. But only till India decides to disincentivise imports, in which case companies operating in SEZs end up becoming collateral damage. The movement of goods from SEZs into the DTA is considered importing. 
 
India is planning to levy a basic customs duty of 15-25% on imports of solar cells and modules. Primarily to target China—which accounts for 80% of India’s solar imports—but also to promote local production. This has raised the hackles of Indian solar manufacturers in SEZs, which could also be subjected to the duty.
“In the absence of a level playing field, domestic manufacturers in SEZs will have to shut shop and may see job losses to the tune of 15,000 people," said Saibaba Vutukuri, CEO of Vikram Solar. These units can provide up to 400,000 more jobs in the country moving forward as the focus shifts to domestic manufacturing, Vutukuri added.
No basic customs duty relief to SEZ solar manufacturing units yet, The Economic Times
The consequences of this move go beyond the fortunes of the companies themselves. Units in SEZs account for 60% of India’s cell-manufacturing capacity and 40% of its module-making capacity. Making the majority of the local manufacturing industry uncompetitive when the rest cannot meet the overall demand will put the brakes on India’s solar energy expansion. India’s installed solar power capacity has surged nearly 9X to over 35.3 GW in five years. 
 
Solar manufacturers with facilities in SEZs have proposed an alternative to the customs duty: an “equalisation levy” of 1-2% on SEZ units to make up for the concessions they receive to put them on a par with their Indian competitors outside SEZs. 
 
If the government doesn’t play ball, it will be a repeat of an unsuccessful earlier attempt to discourage solar imports. In 2018, India levied a 25% safeguard duty on these imports, which partially affected SEZ units. While companies producing cells within SEZs were not affected, those that imported cells to assemble modules were given no relief. 
 
SEZs, owing partly to intractable troubles with land acquisition, are not quite what they were touted to be. Since 2000, approvals have been given for 423 SEZs, but only 240 are operational. 
 
If India erects more tariff barriers in its quest to be atmanirbhar (self-reliant), SEZs will lose their edge. On the other hand, exempting them from import levies, which will be accompanied by something like the equalisation levy, will take them closer to their peers in the DTA. Either way, SEZs will not be very special.

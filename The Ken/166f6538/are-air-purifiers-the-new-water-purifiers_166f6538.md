If you are in India, chances are you are inundated with news about the seasonal surge in air pollution. On the colour-coded air quality index, Delhi’s Vantablack status on Thursday morning was nothing anyone had seen anywhere.
If you are outside India, perhaps even then you haven’t escaped the news, thanks to the first-ever WHO conference last week on air pollution as a global health emergency.
Take a deep breath. Today’s story isn’t about how bad the air quality is; it’s about how the Indian air purifier industry is on the cusp of breathing easy smack in the middle of a national air pollution crisis.
It may have taken Barack Obama’s 2015 visit to India—and a subsequent purchase of 1800 BlueAir purifiers by the US embassy—to jumpstart a slow market into action, but it hasn’t slowed since. Annual unit sales are doubling, and they are also coming from places like Meerut, Agra, Dehradun, Kanpur and Pune. Brisk online sales (per Amazon India data) give a sense of gradual adoption outside nerve centres in north India.
In 2016, most purifiers averaged at Rs 30,000 ($411.2). Now you get a purifier for half that amount. Even water purifiers didn't see such price competitiveness is such a short span. Unsurprisingly, a ‘me too’ market has emerged, where any differentiation flies, even length of the extended warranty.
All brands, including early movers like Philips, Honeywell, BlueAir, are united in one debatable fact: indoor air is 5-10 times worse than ambient air. Everyone wants a piece of the pie. More so because in regulatory and certification vacuum, there’s no way to be sure whether products tested abroad surefire work in Indian conditions.
UK’s Dyson wants to put in $500 million in the Indian market, but all the model has is a good fan and good looks, says a consultant who’s been testing many brands. "Prestige, Havell's, and even Usha-Shriram have gone to China, picked up a product that looks half-decent, slapped their labels, and marketed the sh*t out of it. It's almost out of control."

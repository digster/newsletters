Good Morning Ishan,
First up, if you are among those who believe we dish out SoftBank jokes in our emails and The Nutgraf far too often, then you have my apologies. They are one too many, I agree.
Therefore, I’m going to steer clear of any SoftBank references today, even though our story is about burgeoning VC ambitions. This time in Southeast Asia.
Firms like Golden Gate Ventures, Jungle Ventures, Wavemaker Partners, and many others have impressively grown their fund size, as much as 20-24X in seven years. If not as a consequence of the prevailing hyper growth among large startups in the region, it’s certainly seen as a corollary. Less than a third of early stage-funded startups could raise Series B rounds until 2018. Which means that the region’s promising companies need growth funds, leaving more room for venture capitalists to grow.
VCs also have Limited Partners’ (LPs), who are showing significantly increased interest in the region. They find China too risky, where valuations are dizzyingly high, and the trade war is showing no signs of a letup. They need SE Asia to hedge their capital.
A report by Golden Gate and business school INSEAD estimates there will be at least 700 startup exits in the region between 2023-2025. Even if you take that number with a pinch of salt, there’s enough action in the area for new LPs like US pension funds and university endowments, which have traditionally focused on more conservative markets, to place their bets.
Still, there is a bunch of investors who think a reason why regional VC firms are raising larger funds is to play the “management fee game”. It’s also raising a legit concern that seed-stage investing may suffer as funds move to bigger cheques.
Is VC scale-up as fraught as startup scale-up? Reporting from Bangkok and Kuala Lumpur, Jon and Ka Kay have got it all, including the people and the zeitgeist.

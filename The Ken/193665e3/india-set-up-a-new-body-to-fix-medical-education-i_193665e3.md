Good Morning Ishan,
In every episode of the television medical drama House, MD, the titular Dr Gregory House must diagnose a patient suffering from a mysterious illness. And almost every time, one of his colleagues raises the notion that the patient might have lupus—a disease in which the body’s immune system turns against itself, attacking the healthy cells.
To this, the main character invariably—and usually correctly—dismisses the thought: “It’s never lupus!”
But if he were to read today’s story, he might concede that India’s medical-education system is suffering from a similar kind of autoimmune disease, where the problem is its own watchdog—the National Medical Commission.
Why else would the governing authority deem respiratory medicine a non-essential department for undergraduate medical students, especially when the nation chokes on polluted air every year? Or leave the positions of board officials tasked with investigating cases of professional misconduct vacant?
So question doctors and former members of the commission, who allege that the body’s decisions are increasingly baffling and contrary to its mandate of fixing medical education.
Today, Alifiya records and diagnoses the symptoms that afflict the NMC with a [story](https://the-ken.com/story/india-set-up-a-new-body-to-fix-medical-education-it-now-needs-to-fix-that-body/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter) just as biting as Dr House’s wit.

Earlier this week, the Reserve Bank of India (RBI) made a couple of key announcements.
First, it proposed the linking of credit cards with UPI. And the world immediately lost its collective mind. CEOs fired up their laptops and wrote long Twitter threads about why this was a gamechanger. News articles tried to go beyond the three-sentence-long announcement about how the new system would work, and predicted winners and losers. People with the word storyteller in their bios ran over to LinkedIn to write explainers about UPI and credit, and how this would change the lives of millions of Indians #financialinclusion.
Second, for credit and debit cards, it increased the limit for e-mandates for recurring transactions. And nobody even noticed it. The RBI magnanimously decided to raise the limit to Rs 15,000 (~US$190) from the Rs 5,000 (US$64) at present. Excellent. Now, the thousands of companies who buy software subscriptions can start transferring people out of their recurring subscription authorisation department and ask them to do real finance work instead of entering OTPs on websites.
I mean, look, all of this is useful. But, I think most people have missed the key takeaways behind both these announcements.

The World Chip Design League is heating up. India isn’t even on the points table. Move and countermove are crowding the global IP space, especially in chip design. India is only now waking up to what its young chip-design ecosystem needs the most—more money

You are reading our Premium subscriber-only newsletter Make India Competitive Again—a weekly commentary on what India should do to thrive in the chaos unleashed by Trump 

Over the last three years, Joe Biden’s CHIPS Act has played no small part in shaping the global semiconductor supply chain. The US’s desperation to reduce its reliance on China is diverting many US semiconductor manufacturers to seek alternatives in emerging markets like India. 

Donald Trump has made it clear he wants to tear it up https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-03-07/what-is-the-chips-act-why-does-trump-want-to-end-it into a million little pieces. 

Last week, Bloomberg reported that the Trump administration is moving to scrap Biden-era export rules for AI chips, wants to restrict US AI chips from being used in Chinese AI models, and has warned US manufacturers that the use of Chinese AI chips “anywhere in the world” would put them on the wrong side of US export controls. Other changes in semiconductor policy are expected very soon. 

The world’s biggest chip makers are already starting to turn their ships to catch the new winds. 

Nvidia, Financial Times reports https://www.ft.com/content/c886a4c0-da75-4ea7-8230-6ffd18815fa4, is considering building a research and development centre in Shanghai that would help it stay competitive in China, where sales have slumped due to tightening US export controls. With one key caveat:

The R&D centre would research the specific demands of Chinese customers and the complex technical requirements needed to satisfy Washington’s curbs. 

The actual core design and production will remain overseas, however, due to legal sensitivity around transferring intellectual property to China. Nvidia said: “We are not sending any GPU designs to China to be modified to comply with export controls.”

If semiconductor manufacturing has become a quagmire of uncertainty and geopolitical sensitivity, things upstream are even more tense. 

Trump, who had already rather outlandishly accused Taiwan of stealing America’s chip industry on the campaign trail last year, has been quite vocal about breaches of American intellectual property rights (both perceived and real). And it’s clear his administration sees chip manufacturing and design as a national security issue, with access to US IP tightly controlled and only sparingly granted. The US government even put India on a priority watch list https://www.hindustantimes.com/india-news/ip-violations-by-india-now-on-trump-s-trade-policy-agenda-101741459990863.html for IP violations in March.

The Indian government, for its part, has been working on its own plans to secure its interests in the semiconductor sector. Ambitious ones at that—its Semiconductor Mission’s budget is Rs 76,000 crore. But going by the results, no one is really calling it an unqualified success. While some projects, such as those of Murugappa Group, Micromax, and Tata Electronics, seem to be chugging along, other have backed out. 

Like Zoho, which suspended its plans https://www.reuters.com/world/india/zoho-suspends-700-million-chipmaking-plan-latest-setback-india-sources-say-2025-05-01/ for a $700 million chip-making facility early this month. And Adani Group, which scrapped talks https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/industry/cons-products/electronics/adani-pauses-talks-with-israels-tower-for-10-billion-india-chip-foray-sources-say/articleshow/120762935.cms?from=mdr for a $10 billion chip-making project just a few days before that. In the case of Zoho, at least, this was due to its inability to find the right technology partner and that the company “did not have that confidence in the tech”. 

The same kind of tech that, if you ignore the scale, India’s fledgling chip design companies are struggling to develop. 

And for which India had allocated a grand total of just Rs 1,000 crore when it first announced design-linked incentives (DLI) in its semiconductor mission.

Waking up slow

Clearly, recent events have convinced India’s policymakers of something that was evident from day one: Rs 1,000 crore doesn’t come close to what’s needed to kickstart India’s chip design sector. 

As I wrote in my January 2025 story: 

Under the scheme, the government reimburses half of the amount of investment (with an upper limit of Rs 15 crore) done by a fabless venture. When DLI was introduced in 2021, the government aimed to “nurture” 100 chip-design companies over five years and ensure at least 20 such companies have a turnover of over Rs 1,500 crore. But thanks to the slow run rate, only about 12 companies are “reaping” the scheme’s benefits so far. The desired turnover remains an aspirational goal. The cost of designing and taping out a chip is higher for India’s nascent fabless ecosystem compared to mature industries in China, the United States, and Europe. This, despite the country being home to one-fifth of the global semiconductor-design talent.

[…]

Any fabless company would require US$10 million on average to build even a basic, non-cutting-edge technology chip or system, according to the earlier-quoted technology advisor. That’s far above the funds usually made available for the firms.

Take Morphing Machines, for example. Bootstrapped for over 15 years, the company has just one major client—aircraft equipment manufacturer Safran. Despite having a product with use cases across sectors, co-founder Deepak Shapeti says that undercapitalisation forces them to juggle clients carefully. “I can’t take on more than one client at a time because my team cannot handle it.” Morphing Machines currently employs just around 50 people. 

The government’s new $4 billion (Rs 34,000 crore) DLI scheme is India’s solution for this. According to Livemint, the scheme will https://www.livemint.com/technology/global-tech-patents-design-incentive-scheme-semiconductor-electronics-us-meity-tech-oem-chip-fab-ev-ip-google-qualcomm-11744718064173.html both increase the number of design companies it incentivises and the quantum of incentives each company will get. 

The concern, of course, is whether it’s too late for these moves. 

Money is certainly the biggest problem for India’s chip design firms, but hardly the only one. Trump’s shenanigans are up there among the top risks. 

“Even prototyping my chips, and the supply chain that exists within fabless operations (for sourcing IPs, for example)…. all of it is dollar-dependent. So when policymakers are making such sharp moves, I don’t know how I should charge my customers,” admits Shashwath TR, co-founder and the chief executive of Mindgrove Technologies, one of the few highly funded semiconductor startups in India. 

And even in instances where all of the above aren’t factors, there’s still the customer, who in most cases has to be convinced that putting money into Indian-made chips is a good idea. “Customers want to know if the chip works, and whether it’s being used in other products,” a senior executive of a multinational fabless company noted. But given the length of time needed to convincingly prove product quality, most Indian startups fail before they can even try. 

“A design life cycle is more than a year… and then you have to manufacture it, do validation testing, and then be able to do a prototype and deploy it. For startups with limited funding, this multi-year process is a high-stakes gamble.” 

And it’s a gamble most can take only if they believe a loss won’t sink them entirely. 

How long until India’s new DLI scheme inculcates its chip designers with such confidence?

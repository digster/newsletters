Make India Competitive Again
Mon, 06 Oct 25
What India is doing, will do, and should do—to not just survive but thrive in the chaos unleashed by Trump.
Good Morning Ishan,
A Rs 76,000 crore production-linked incentive programme; around Rs 65,000 crore of which is already committed; with 10 major strategic projects [approved](https://www.pib.gov.in/PressNoteDetails.aspx?NoteId=155130&ModuleId=3) across the value chain—ever since its launch in 2021, the Indian Semiconductor Mission (ISM) has made no bones of the fact that it is one of the most ambitious things the country has attempted in decades. Especially given this is an area where India has displayed scant muscle on a global scale before.
But setting up large fabs and factories doesn’t automatically mean you will have the people to run them.
Which is why, in July 2023, the ISM launched a separate skilling programme to train up the manpower India’s fledgling fabs would need as they started going into production.
Enter Lam Research, a 45-year-old California-headquartered company that makes equipment and software for semiconductor makers.
And SEMulator3D—Lam’s virtual fab simulator.
Over the past two years, Lam, the Indian Institute of Science (IISc), and ISM have partnered to first pilot and then expand a training programme for Indian college students aimed at providing them with the skills needed to operate a semiconductor fab. The latest iteration aims to train 60,000 students by 2032.
IISc opens up its Centre for Nanoscience and Engineering (CeNSE)—India’s most sophisticated research fab—for a “train-the-trainer” initiative, skilling faculty from other institutes who are to then train their own students on their own premises.
Lam contributes the virtual simulator software that everyone will learn on.
And the ISM [funds](https://www.cense.iisc.ac.in/lam_training/) the whole thing.
And there’s good reason for the government picking up the bill. According to a report by staffing services provider Teamlease, India’s semiconductor industry is expected to face a shortage of 250,000–300,000 professionals by 2027. Jaswinder Ahuja, a member of ISM’s advisory committee and the former MD of semicon design major Cadence, puts it bluntly:
The problem is, the training programme gives students very little of the one thing they need the most.
Hands-on learning.
To be fair, the programme is a “good starting point”, as an IISc professor told me recently.
| A look at the IISc research fab, Image credit: Keshav Pransukhka |
The training runs for four weeks: for faculty, the first two weeks are hands-on fabrication and characterisation at CeNSe, whereas the last two weeks are dedicated to completing ten Lam-designed assignments on SEMulator3D. So far, faculty from 60 institutions have undergone the course. More than 1,600 students have been trained so far, too. Out of which 100 will be invited to IISc for further training.
“The goal is to take the number of students trained to 4,800 by the end of next year,” said the IISc professor. And eventually, to 60,000.
Given the difficulty in providing access to CeNSe for everyone on the programme, though, it is Lam’s simulator that is pulling most of the weight. “We’re virtualising these processes, making them accessible to students in a fast, scalable way. This will democratise semiconductor education across India,” Rangesh Raghavan, corporate vice-president and general manager, Lam Research told [Business Standard](https://www.business-standard.com/companies/news/lam-research-expands-virtual-semiconductor-training-to-20-indian-varsities-124091101363_1.html) last year. Lam has a few training centres in places like China and South Korea, but this is the first such programme of its kind the company has been involved in.
But the issue with relying so much on a simulator, however sophisticated, is already becoming apparent. A senior product engineer at a semiconductor design firm explained why.
“Training on SEMulator3D provides a basic understanding of the process, but running a fab is not running a simulation,” he pointed out. “Each fab operates with its own tightly guarded recipes—the exact mix of pressure, temperature, gases, and timing that go into building chips. Thus, a student trained only on Lam’s software won’t just walk into the job.”
And the semiconductor industry isn’t all that labour-heavy in the first place. “Fabs are very autonomous factories,” said the product engineer, “not a lot of people are required on the floor.”
For example, the upcoming Tata factory in Gujarat, which has received a subsidy of Rs 27,000 crore, plans to onboard only around 2,000 engineers.
That is Rs 13.5 crore per job.
And even when they hire, fabs can be pretty selective. “They prefer students with a Master’s or PhD degree from the top six IITs or IISc, or those who have some prior experience in a fab,” the engineer said. Most of the students trained so far under the programme are undergraduates at tier-2 colleges.
The result is that no one who has undergone the Lam-CeNSe training has been hired for a semicon job yet. Tata, for its part, is sending its employees to its JV partner, PSMC in Taiwan, for training. Two batches have already returned, and the third is expected to leave in October. [Kaynes](https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/tech/technology/kaynes-semicon-eyes-january-2026-for-mass-production-at-sanand-osat/articleshow/123705253.cms?from=mdr), too, is planning to send engineers to Taiwan. Even Lam Research, which employs around [2,100](https://www.linkedin.com/company/lam-research/people/?facetGeoRegion=102713980) people in India, has not promised to hire any of these trainees.
The professor says that these students will have to “pursue a more advanced degree or be trained on the job” to become truly job-ready, but while programmes similar to Lam’s are being conducted by companies like [Spring](https://timesofindia.indiatimes.com/city/hyderabad/malaysian-firm-to-train-10k-youth-from-telangana-in-chip-design/articleshow/121786416.cms?utm_source=chatgpt.com) Semiconductor, [Cadence](https://www.entrepreneur.com/en-in/technology/semiconductor-companies-partner-with-academia-to-bridge/479886), [Applied](https://www.entrepreneur.com/en-in/technology/semiconductor-companies-partner-with-academia-to-bridge/479886) Materials, and [Samsung](https://iisc-iswdp.org/), there are few specialised courses in universities that would fit the bill.
| A look at the IISc research fab, Image credit: Keshav Pransukhka |
For Lam, of course, things could work out well in the long-term. It is foregoing revenue from the use of its software, but if most of India’s semicon workforce is trained on its product, there is a good chance many companies will adopt it in the future. Cadence, which had used a similar strategy, has a [36](https://www.incrementalreturns.co/p/cadence-design-systems-mission-critical-software?utm_source=chatgpt.com)% market share in its segment today.
But India’s ambition of cultivating a top-notch semicon workforce requires a lot more than just training provided on a simulator.
It requires many, many more training fabs in colleges across the country. And significant investment to build them.
A good starting point would be to begin expanding the capabilities of existing research fabs.
IIT [Bombay](https://inup-i2i.in/inup_wrapper/iit_bombay.php), IIT [Delhi](https://inup-i2i.in/inup_wrapper/iit_delhi.php), and [BITS](https://www.bits-pilani.ac.in/hyderabad/clean-room-micro-and-nano-fabrication-facility/?utm_source=chatgpt.com) Pilani, for instance, all have labs similar to IISc now. And according to the professor, proposals have been sent to the electronics ministry to expand the industry training capacity of CeNSe, “to double the size of our clean room and introduce production-grade tools.” The lab has already trained 130 engineers for Tata’s upcoming fab.
Simulators, after all, can only take you so far.

We’re going to mix it up a little this week, like we occasionally do here, and take a quick look at a few recent developments in Indian retail and commerce that we haven’t explored so far. Either because we didn’t think they merited a full edition of their own, or because we simply missed covering them. 

Here’s a quick and dirty round-up.

FOMO: India

Yet another overseas retailer has finally wisened up to the fact that the Indian market is an opportunity that you can’t just pay lip service to any longer. American fashion retailer Abercrombie & Fitch Co., has announced a multi-year franchise partnership with Flipkart-owned Myntra to launch both Abercrombie & Fitch and Hollister stores in the country. The foray marks a significant step for both brands, especially given it will span the entire distribution spectrum—from brick-and-mortar stores to regional e-commerce sites and branded digital storefronts. 

The partnership is to be facilitated through Myntra’s business-to-business wholesale arm.

Abercrombie & Fitch is expected to launch its stores and begin online sales in India in the second half of FY25, according to a Myntra spokesperson.

[…]

Myntra’s agreement with Abercrombie & Fitch Co. underscores its growing portfolio of international brands, building on earlier partnerships with Mango, Nautica, and British retailer Next. The move also positions Myntra in direct competition with Reliance Retail and Nykaa, which have been aggressively securing rights to bring international fashion brands to India.

Reliance Retail, for instance, recently partnered with Britain’s Asos and is set to bring Chinese fast-fashion retailer Shein back to the Indian market.

Myntra, meanwhile, continues to dominate India’s online fashion landscape, offering over 9,700 brands, including Mango, H&M, Levi’s, U.S. Polo Assn., Tommy Hilfiger, and Marks & Spencer. The addition of Abercrombie & Fitch and Hollister further cements its position as a go-to destination for premium global brands.

Myntra to bring Abercrombie & Fitch, Hollister stores to India in a franchise deal, Livemint

A parent-eat-child world..?

LG Electronics India last week filed a draft red herring prospectus for an IPO that is expected to raise over Rs 15,000 crore (US$1.8 billion), which would make the issue one of the largest stock listings in recent memory. 

The issue will see its parent company—global electronics giant LG—offload 15% of its stake in its wholly-owned Indian subsidiary. Reports say that the proceeds from the offer for sale might go in large part towards setting up the company’s third factory in India, which is expected to absorb an investment of Rs 5,000 crore. 

The listing is certainly a big one, but one detail I found interesting—perhaps amusing, even—was a little snippet of information in its disclosures. 

One of the risks that LG Electronics India believes may threaten its future prospects in the Indian market is… its own parent company.

“While our promoter is currently not engaged in businesses that compete with ours in India, the promoter may in the future engage in businesses that compete with ours because we do not have any exclusivity arrangement with them,” the company said in the draft prospectus. “This may give rise to conflicts of interest, which may adversely affect our business, financial condition and results of operations.”

LG Electronics has floated an indirect wholly-owned subsidiary, Hi-M Solutek India, which specialises in LG commercial air conditioner service and maintenance. These services include the provision of system air conditioner construction materials, equipment integrated service and maintenance, building energy diagnosis and operation services, and special facility engineering services.

“Currently, Hi-M Solutek India only provides services for our products. However, we do not have an exclusive contractual arrangement with them and there is no assurance that Hi-M Solutek India will not expand their business in the future to compete with ours or to provide services for our competitors,” LG India said.

LG India worried about competition from parent Company, The Economic Times

Apple ascendent

For the second year running, American electronics major Apple’s smartphone shipments to India have surpassed its South Korean rival Samsung’s in terms of value. And this time, by quite a margin. 

According to The Economic Times, which cited market researcher IDC India, the value of Apple’s iPhone shipments to the country grew 24% year-on-year to reach just shy of US$8 billion in the nine months ending September. Samsung’s shipments stood at US$5.23 billion for the same period, widening the gap between the two companies in terms of value market share, with Apple standing at a record 27% and Samsung at 17.7%. In 2023, this figure had come in at 23.5% for Apple and 22.5% for Samsung. 

While this widening gap between the two is a reflection of Apple’s growing popularity in India, it is also a factor of the price bands the two companies play in. To quote The Economic Times:

“Over 75% of shipments from Samsung are driven by less than $400 price band, while more than 85% shipments from Apple are driven by $700 price band. Hence the wide gap between the value share of both brands,” said Navkendar Singh, AVP, devices research at IDC.

[…]

Singh said Apple’s growth this year was led by previous generation iPhone models made affordable with financing options and discounts, especially on ecommerce platforms during Diwali, high aspirational value attached to its products, and higher resale value of iPhones compared to Android smartphones.

[…]

Counterpoint’s research director Tarun Pathak said the share of ₹30,000-plus handsets will increase to a record 20% this year from 18%. “Apple is expected to continue its double digit pace of growth in India next year too” he said.

Apple’s India output shines, sales catching up, The Economic Times

It will, however, be quite a while before the Indian market reaches the kind of value generated by China for Apple, even though the still low-level penetration of Apple products in India offers the company quite a lucrative opportunity.

The penetration of iPhones in India, the world’s second-largest mobile phone market, is still modest at 6-7 % of the total smartphone market, with the remaining 94% comprising the Android platform used by rival brands such as Samsung, Oppo, Vivo and Xiaomi, in addition to some local brands.

“This gives Apple a long runway to increase the size of its domestic sales and market share,” an analyst said.

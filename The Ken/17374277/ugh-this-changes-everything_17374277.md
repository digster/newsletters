Ugh. This changes everything
Olina
The N95 respirator has finally revealed its true face.
And it has a valve in it.
On Tuesday, the Indian Ministry of Health and Family Welfare [warned against](https://indianexpress.com/article/lifestyle/health/health-ministry-warns-against-wearing-n95-mask-with-valve-cause-covid-19-6516600/) the widespread use of N95 respirators with valves for anyone other than medical professionals. The reason? Valves on these masks only work one way. That is, they filter our viral particles from getting into your mouth, but don't filter what you exhale. It protects you from getting Covid, but doesn’t protect others if you have Covid.
So it's not just useless. The health ministry says it’s actively detrimental.
This is terrible news for the hundreds of thousands of valved-mask manufacturers, who flooded the market with cheap knock-offs of 3M mask designs. “These valves are industrial designs that have been hastily adapted for a consumer product,” one PPE kit manufacturer told me as early as May.
It’s not like the science behind these N95 respirators has been completely absent.
Here’s an excerpt from the Mayo Clinic, an American medical non-profit:
|
“Some N95 masks, and even some cloth masks, have one-way valves that make them easier to breathe through. But because the valve releases unfiltered air when the wearer breathes out, this type of mask doesn't prevent the wearer from spreading the virus. For this reason, some places have banned them.”
|
|
|
Five months into the pandemic seems a bit too late to arrest the flood of N95 masks in the market. For instance, these valved masks do brisk business in Delhi, a city that already suffers from catastrophic air pollution every year. If the government does have the gumption to enforce this, things might get worse.
Already, the government advisory links to a page for “homemade cloth masks”. Covid-19 safe masks are those that use a mix of protective fabrics. Usually, one of the layers has to be melt blown. DIY masks are unlikely to make the safety cut. If everyone decides to ditch the valve, other masks might just get too expensive.
Unlike in the US, where mask-wearing is a political lightning rod, donning a mask in India isn’t a problem. Despite the science, the alternatives, and the political will, how did N95 respirators infiltrate the PPE market?
Two reasons.
a) The Indian government itself seemed unaware of how the virus works. Hence, a government advisory from early March:
|
|
|
|
Source: Ministry of Health
|
b) The World Health Organization (WHO) also has a plum role to play in this fiasco. Here’s a news report from March:
|
“Dr Poonam Khetarpal Singh, Regional Director, WHO Southeast Asia, said: "Airborne spread has not been reported for COVID-19. Based on the information received so far and on our experience with other coronaviruses, COVID-19 appears to mostly spread through respiratory droplets (for instance when a sick person coughs) and close contact. This is WHO recommends hand and respiratory hygiene."
|
|
|
Awkward.
Both the government and WHO could have avoided this had they just joined the dots between the science of the virus and the design of a mask. Unfortunately, manufacturers joined the dots between panic and market first.

LinkedIn, citizen journalism… and censorship
Rohin
|
|
Just a day ago, he had over 180,000 followers on the site and a history going back nearly a decade. Now, he didn’t exist.
[Dr Anirudha Malpani](https://www.indianangel.in/about-me/) is a well-known Indian infertility specialist. He is also that rare practicing doctor who is also an angel investor in startups. As a consequence, he ended up as a rather [prolific writer](https://www.indianangel.in/).
The subject of many of his critical posts had been Byju’s, India’s only edtech unicorn that is [also a decacorn](https://techcrunch.com/2020/05/01/indian-education-startup-byjus-is-fundraising-at-a-10b-valuation/).

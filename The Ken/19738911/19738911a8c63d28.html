Reliance Industries’ media release, in the last week of May, on market regulator Sebi’s final go-ahead for the group’s mutual-fund business, made no bones of its win-big-or-go-home plans.

“I am confident that JioBlackrock Asset Management will play a transformative role in shaping the future of financial empowerment in India,” said Isha Ambani, non-executive director of Jio Financial Services.

Boilerplate PR? Unlikely. The Ambanis’ reputation for disruption precedes their companies.

Many others, too, expect Jio Blackrock MF to “transform” and “disrupt” India’s Rs 70 lakh crore mutual-fund industry. After all, it is a 50:50 joint venture of a financial-services firm backed by India’s richest man, Mukesh Ambani, and an international investment giant. “A powerful combination of Blackrock’s global investment expertise and Jio’s digital-first innovation,” as the release stated.

Wouldn’t Jio Blackrock then do an encore of what Reliance Jio Infocomm did to India’s telecom industry about a decade ago? Unleash price wars, decimate most of the competition, innovate, disrupt, and dominate?

Unlikely, again. At least, not anytime soon. 

For one, the competitive dynamics of India’s mutual-fund industry today and the telecom industry then are poles apart.

Also, Jio Blackrock MF is bypassing a key cog in the wheel—the mutual-fund distributor. It’s a brave move—one that could get better returns for mutual-fund investors. But it could also backfire for the company.

To me, the operative part of the media release, which also announced the appointment of Blackrock veteran Sid Swaminathan as the venture’s chief executive, was this:

“JioBlackrock’s digital-first customer proposition, delivering institutional quality products at a lower cost directly to investors, will enable more people in India to enjoy the many benefits of access to the capital markets.”

So, unlike regular plans that are sold through distributors, Jio Blackrock will offer its investors only direct plans of its mutual-fund schemes through the digital route; its website confirms this. No distributor means not having to worry about distributor commission. Which also means lower cost and better returns to investors.

But in India, individual investor money is still primarily distributor-driven. Despite their share rising over the years, direct plans still account for only 27% of individual investor assets.

Moreover, none of these “digital-first”, “lower cost”, or “direct plans” are new in India’s mutual-fund space. Zerodha Fund House, launched in 2023, has a similar premise. But it is yet to make a dent.

The league tables in India’s mutual-fund industry are dominated by bank-sponsored players like SBI, HDFC and ICICI. Their massive branch distribution network gives them a huge moat. Fund houses such as Quantum Mutual Fund, which favoured direct plans in the past, weren’t able to scale much. In contrast, a relatively newer player like Bajaj Finserv MF rose up the ranks faster, primarily by courting distributors.

Can Jio Blackrock buck the trend?

“It is different from Zerodha and Quantum in the sense that it will be willing to spend big on marketing,” said a senior mutual-fund executive from another fund house. But there are limits to this spend-your-way-to-glory in the mutual-fund business. Expenses and incentives incurred towards specific schemes have to be accounted for in the scheme itself and not at the level of the fund house, according to Sebi’s rules. “This could reduce schemes’ returns,” the executive added.

Besides, marketing may not have a big impact until the fund house has built a track record and can showcase healthy returns. This will take years, if at all. Unless backed by massive distribution, size and scale in this business invariably follow either strong returns, like in the case of Quant MF, or a solid long-term track record, like Parag Parikh MF.

Jio Blackrock might be hoping to convince a huge captive customer base, running into the millions, across the multiple businesses of Jio and Reliance Industries. But that’s easier said than done, too. “Data-protection laws have restrictions on sharing customer information across group companies,” said the senior executive.

In any case, it’s not as if a captive customer base gives assured business. If that were so, Zerodha Fund House and Groww Mutual Fund would have scaled massively by now. They have a huge, and arguably more relevant, customer base in the discount-broking businesses run by their parent companies.

So, can Jio Blackrock innovate big to win big?

The scope for that too seems to have shrunk. India now has about 45 fund houses, and more are in the offing. A lot of slice-and-dice and innovation has already been done and continues across fund houses and schemes, across asset classes (equity, debt, hybrid) and styles (active and passive). From smart-beta funds to schemes launched on new indices, both old and new fund houses are trying to stand out.

Then, can Jio Blackrock pull a familiar rabbit out of its hat? Can it slash its expense ratio—the cost charged to customers—and dangle this carrot to scale up?

“Difficult,” the senior executive said, pointing out that expense ratios in passive funds are, anyway, quite low—even below 0.1% in some cases. “And if [Jio Blackrock] cuts the expense ratio sharply in active funds, to say 0.5% or less [or under the current average], nothing stops other fund houses from doing the same.”

Many incumbents are also deep-pocketed, have made big profits over the years, and can play the pricing game if need be. Besides, it’s not as if those who offer the lowest expense ratios always scale. Navi Mutual Fund, for instance, advertised its low costs aggressively but is still low down the pecking order on assets under management (AUM).

Surely, the Reliance and Jio brands can help?

Maybe. But if pedigree and brand power ensured wins, LIC Mutual Fund—promoted by India’s most dominant insurer and a household name—would have been in the top rung in AUMs, and not languish around the halfway position. This, despite being in the business for decades.

Jio Financial Services, of course, will be hoping to scale Jio Blackrock MF big and fast. It’s one of the key businesses on which its exalted valuation stands—a number that helped its stock enter the Nifty 50 index.

“Jio and Reliance Industries’ group companies will likely put their surplus in the short-term liquid and money-market schemes of Jio Blackrock Mutual Fund,” said the senior executive. “It could be a group mandate. This will see the fund house garner sizable AUMs in a year or so, even if it earns low revenue.”

Group companies may not need convincing. But everyone else will. Jio Blackrock has its work cut out.

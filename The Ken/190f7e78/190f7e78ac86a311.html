Dear Ishan, welcome to this Sunday’s edition of First Principles.

When I write these newsletters (usually Saturday late afternoons), I imagine you’re reading them on Sunday early afternoons, because that’s when we send them out. But I know many of you read it through the week while others read it in different countries and time zones.

Unlike weekday writing, weekend writing has a certain inescapable vibe to it. Sunday reading is unlike anything else, or so I’d like to believe.

Earlier this week, I met Piyush Shah, the co-founder of Inmobi, a company that started out in 2007 as a mobile search engine. We discussed how the holy grail for founders is to be able to build organisations that are perpetual, so to speak—ones that are able to both ride and survive major waves that strike every decade—and ones that attract and retain talented people drawn to a long-term purpose.

As both a founder and business journalist, I told Piyush that I was fascinated by the concept of timelessness and by organisations that sustain, evolve and renew themselves every decade. How did they manage to play a multi-decadal game?

“Every few years, we invite entropy into our organisation,” he told me.

That was such an interesting phrase that I absolutely had to write about it!

entropy noun

broadly : the degree of disorder or uncertainty in a system

Do we invite entropy, or does entropy attract us?

1. Entropy, curiosity, and phase-change

“Your future self guides you by directing your attention in the present” – Carl Jung

A good friend recently sent me a link to this short and interesting video on X (previously Twitter). It’s a five-minute presentation and talk by Tom Morgan, whom I will introduce later. Its title? “The Most Offensively Taboo Idea in Western Civilization.”

The video talks about the differences between the left and right sides of our brains, specifically around how we see the world around us and make sense of it. Please take five minutes to watch it.

Morgan talks about curiosity as an “evolutionary answer to combinatorial explosiveness.”

The world we live in is far too complex (and perhaps getting even more so) for us to even fathom all possible choices, let alone explore them. Thus, we must ignore the vast majority of choices and pick only a teeny-tiny minority.

But which ones?

Morgan is a “Curiosity Sherpa” with Sapient Capital, a wealth manager that handles over US$9 billion for its “ultra-high-net-worth individuals” (read: the 1% of the 1%). He argues that curiosity is the method through which we figure out which choices to make.

“What we are attracted to narrows our options,” he says. We normally think of curiosity as just an innate trait in some people, but we rarely spend time discussing what sort of things they are curious about.

“Can you force yourself to be interested in something?” he asks. No, I don’t think we can. By extension, we cannot force ourselves to be curious about everything too.

Thus, each of our respective curiosities is unique to us, almost like a signature. They attract and “pull” us toward things that might be our niches. Curiosity is evolution’s way of helping us find our purpose. I’ll re quote a quote from Morgan’s talk—one that might have sounded like trite-somethings our older relatives tell us at family gatherings, but perhaps means something different now?

“Follow your bliss and doors will open where there were only walls” – Joseph Campbell

I followed Tom Morgan to YouTube and found a longer video where he talks about curiosity as an evolutionary advantage.

It was there he said, “Evolution, in the form of curiosity, helps guide you towards greater complexity: your unique niche.”

Suddenly, the phrase “Every few years we invite entropy into the organisation” made so much more sense.

It gets better. I found a longer article from Morgan that integrates one of my favourite movies, The Matrix, with curiosity, “phase change,” and perhaps the most powerful narrative storytelling arc: The Hero’s Journey. Here’s a diagram from it.

View in Browser

We humans have watched each other succeed and fail over hundreds of thousands of years. We encoded the key patterns and processes in narratives. This is why the study of myth is the ultimate information alpha.

The great mythologist Joseph Campbell noticed that one story was so ubiquitous across time and culture he called it the “monomyth” or the Hero’s Journey. This legend remains a dominant subtext of our most popular movies and TV shows. It’s the precise structure of 11 of the top 15 highest-grossing movies ever. The multibillion-dollar Marvel, Pixar, Disney, Star Wars, Harry Potter, and Avatar franchises are overwhelmingly founded on this distinctive narrative arc.

The insight that blew me away, thanks to the remarkable work of Brett Andersen, is that the monomyth reflects the universal process of a paradigm shift, or phase change in any complex adaptive system. It applies equally to our societies as it does ourselves. This means if you can identify where you are on the map, you can anticipate the future.

The structure of the phase-change narrative is simple and precise. An incomplete model encounters an anomaly. The flaws in the model create dissonance, which eventually breaks the existing stale frame. The model then incorporates the anomaly and emerges in a more complete and complex form.

The Story that Changes The World – Tom Morgan

2. The Silent Sunday Monsoon Special

Hello, Ishan! Welcome to First Principles Silent Sunday; it’s Jonathan here as always. If you saw last week’s issue, you already know what today’s is about. If you didn’t, well, the title probably gives it away. 

Monsoons trigger very complex emotions, at least in me. Do I enjoy the heavenly temperatures and mornings spent with my wife, wrapped in a cosy blanket and nursing a steaming mug of tea? Or do I curse the muck, mud, and madness on the roads, all of which I have to navigate, both on my way to and back from work? Do I enjoy the occasional rainbow? Or curse the gloomy skies? It’s a tough one, and until I began writing this section of First Principles, I usually defaulted to the latter. 

Yeah, the pleasant temperatures meant I wasn’t sweating for half of every day, but better that than wondering what went into making that sickeningly brown puddle on the road I just stepped in. 

All the Silent Sunday contributions we’ve been getting over the past few weeks, though, have made dwelling on the negatives pretty hard. It’s impossible to ignore that monsoons are pretty. And beautiful. And perhaps just a little charming. 

For me, my favourite-est moments this season have been spent hanging out with a cup of hot tea—strong, with just a dash of milk—brewed by the corner cheta just a minute’s walk from our office in Bengaluru.

3. Monsoon Bookshelf

Dear reader, hello and happy Sunday! This is Anushka, here with a list of books, as usual—this time, a rainy list. If you’ve been grooving to our monsoon playlist or had a chance to look through the stunning monsoon photographs curated by Jonathan, you probably saw this coming. I had a hunch that when the feeble, cloudy sun turned its light on the books section, it wouldn’t be quite the same. A lot of our monsoon music creates the perfect soundscape for rainy afternoons when time seems to slow down. It’s often relieving, romantic, or sometimes melancholic. 

But literature? That’s intense!

I’m not surprised, really. Writers have a knack for turning a harsher lens on the world, seeing things as they are. Just yesterday, my parents sent me a long video of their drive through waist-deep water. It took them two hours to cover just 10 kilometres, which is quite unusual in my hometown of Vadodara compared to here in Bengaluru. I can’t be sure they were listening to any vibey music in the car. 

The dark, noisy rain beating and thrashing against empty house windows? The perfect setting for a murder. A thunderous uproar before the rain bursts upon a parched population? What better way to convey yearning and relief? You know writers can’t resist a plot device. 

It’s evident in the recommendations we have this week from my wonderful colleagues here in The Ken’s newsroom. 

Akshaya, who works with me in the podcast team, immediately recommended Life of Pi by Yann Martel. The way rain is used to create such stellar imagery in that book is hard to get out of your head.

View in Browser

Monsoon—rain, storm, thunder, and nature used as imagery—is impossibly powerful. 

My colleague Snigdha recommends Patrik Svensson’s The Book of Eels.

Svensson combines nature and literary writing in this extraordinary book, which essentially asks, “How do we know the things we know?”

View in Browser

Rain is inseparable from nature, Akshaya tells me. For instance, in the book The Hidden Life of Trees, the writer Peter Wohlleben (a German forrester) talks about how rain completely transforms a tree. Akshaya promises that I will never look at trees the same way again once I read this book.

This brings me to my colleague Praveen’s recommendation, which sits on the opposite end of the spectrum—what happens to nature without rain? He suggests Love Across the Salt Desert by K.N. Daruwalla. He says it’s a lovely short story.

Lastly, we have two recommendations that are perfect examples of how rain stays in the background of stories—quietly but visibly creeping in, just like a character.

Ruhi, my colleague from the Delhi office, recommends Mirror Made of Rain by Naheed Phiroze Patel. And Abhirami recommends Mexican Gothic by Silvia Moreno-Garcia, where rain adds to the horror element of the plot.

4. Are startups no longer fun? Are they no longer as disruptive?

Startups used to be the promised land where ambitious, young people spent their time doing exciting, ground-breaking, and challenging work. And why not? Back in the day, startups promised to change the way business was done.

Ideas became organisations and pulled in people who threw away cushy jobs for the thrill of building something fun and interesting. They moved cities and changed their lives to fulfil their aspirations of being a part of something big and having real impact—as opposed to being a cog in the wheel.

But lately…all of these exciting organisations seem to have hit a wall. In some cases, they’ve settled down and become boring companies—for whom innovation and excitement aren’t what they’re chasing anymore. Incentives aren’t lucrative anymore, and the mission doesn’t seem that clear anymore.

And how have the disruptors settled down?

The same edtechs that said they would disrupt the old-world coaching institutes seem to have become them. The lucrative careers at startups that promised asymmetric growth with more than 100% hikes are no longer a reality—most startups are under a cash crunch, scaling down, and laying people off.

What's your favourite ad?
 
Mine is the iconic Cadbury ad in which a woman comes dancing onto a cricket field, capturing the collective heart of the nation. Plus, every ad in Amul's record-breaking campaign.
 
The new-age ones don't much catch my fancy. Today's story on TikTok's advertising monetisation strategy reminded me how starkly ads have changed over the years.
 
From print, radio, billboards and TV to social media now. Forget cyberspace; even literal space is a target. Russian startup StartRocket wants to fly its satellites to reflect sunlight and create logos or other ads visible from Earth. Pepsi was to be its first customer, but it pulled out. Thank heavens!
 
Back on Terra Firma, traditional ad agencies are finding it tough to keep ad dollars from flowing to the likes of Facebook, Google, Twitter. Anyone hosting content for free (like most other publishers!) is bound to make viewers 'pay' with ads.
 
The latest is TikTok, the viral short-video app owned by China's ByteDance. The same app India banned for 20 days in April for hosting pornographic content. 
 
While that threw its nascent ad efforts into a bit of a tizzy, it regrouped and powered on. It now has a content moderation team that ensures ads don't appear besides inappropriate content. A new sales head leading a team that pitches to brands (and increasingly big brands) directly, bypassing agencies. 
 
It also has a couple of unique ad formats. None more so than one which monetises the omnipresent #, hitherto used only to group content. Then there's TikTok's sheer potential: a popular app on which users spend quite a chunk of time.
 
However, a job well begun is a job half done. TikTok still has a long way to go before it can mount a credible challenge to its social media peers. Can it? I'll let Nilesh answer that: https://the-ken.com/story/tiktok-on-advertisers-clock/ (12-minute read)

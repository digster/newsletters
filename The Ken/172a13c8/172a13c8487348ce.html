Good Morning Ishan,
 
For a good part of the last decade, employee stock ownership plans (ESOPs) offered the promised land of riches. First Infosys, and then Flipkart set benchmarks. For listed and acquired companies.
 
Then, the blitz-scaling bug hit the startup world. Any amount of money the companies raised, it wasn’t enough to take them to profitability and allow for share buyback; it also wasn’t enough to allow for secondaries for the benefit of employees. IPOs in Indian tech have been a mirage. Now, acquisitions have gone rare.
 
It’s estimated the top 30 unicorns in India record close to $80 billion in valuation, 10-15% of which resides in ESOPs. Employees, meanwhile, turned blasé. To quote from today’s story: “The whole equity game was at its peak when the understanding of equity was low. People understand the pitfalls of it better now. So equity as a real differentiator is going down.”
 
But startups are a tough lot. Or shall we say, thick-skinned? Struck by the pandemic, they are bent upon using ESOPs as a tool of leverage, even as they cut salaries and perks.
 
No surprise, then, that the sector, which until now had 5-10% of its equity locked in the ESOP pool, is growing it by 25%. Big brands like Zomato, OYO, Grofers, and Bounce are cash-strapped and replacing salaries with higher value equity.
 
Is it that straightforward? When most will not cross the Covid chasm, let alone regain the old valuations.
 
Employees are flocking to wealth management firms and investment banks that aggregate buyers and sellers for shares of unlisted companies to extract some value.
 
Look, ESOPs are an emotional subject. Infosys gave out 35% of equity, excluding the founders’, to employees. Five years ago, when I asked co-founder NR Narayana Murthy if he regretted being so generous—the ESOP then was worth $11-12 billion—he said that his only regret was that “many of them did not deserve it”.

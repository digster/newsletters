Good Morning Ishan,
As India’s largest eyewear retailer, Lenskart, prepares for an IPO, the old and new disputes with its franchisees are tumbling out and reaching courtrooms.
Besides FIRs in Indore, Mysuru, and Dharwad, the legal battle spans the Karnataka High Court as well as the Delhi International Arbitration Centre.
The 15-year-old company has been a roaring success with more than 2,500 stores, including 400+ outside India, in markets like the UAE, Singapore, and Japan. Much of this scale-up came on the back of its franchise-owned, company-run model.
But Lenskart is shifting away from that strategy, and in that process, it’s bruising its franchise partners.
Allegation 1: unfair competition as Lenskart opens its own stores close to the franchise-owned stores.
(This is a brutal but familiar business practice of assessing local demand. Several Indian startups, including Zepto and Blinkit, do this.)
Allegation 2: financial opacity and “fraud”. More than a dozen franchisees claim that, despite agreements promising transparency, the company’s accounting practices were anything but clear.
(Many have reported having received GST notices based on Lenskart’s reported data—numbers they claim don’t match their own records.)
With revenue topping Rs 5,600 crore in 2023–24, Lenskart is now Asia’s leading omnichannel eyewear retailer. Its upcoming IPO is much-awaited but franchisees that have become roadkill in this journey aren’t taking the risk-you-take-reward-I-reap deal. At least this time.

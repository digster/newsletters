Good Morning Ishan,
If you have recently sat in a hospital lobby wondering why your cashless-insurance card suddenly doesn’t work, you have probably stumbled into one of India’s most passive-aggressive corporate wars.
Hospitals and insurers are locked in a perpetual blame game over who’s breaking healthcare faster. Hospitals say insurers haven’t updated tariffs in years, even as medical inflation runs high. Insurers say hospitals bill Rs 120 for gloves that cost Rs 80. Everyone insists they are the victim.
The latest battlefront: Star Health, India’s biggest standalone insurer, suspended cashless services at Max, Medanta, and Manipal hospitals last month, accusing them of demanding rate hikes “beyond inflation”. Hospitals have hit back, accusing insurers of cartelising to push prices down. Before long, 15,000 hospitals were threatening to block cashless services for multiple insurers in retaliation.
Insurers are bleeding. Distribution costs are sky-high, and even after commissions taper, overriding fees keep the pressure on. Their ratios are in the red, and a new GST rule just made it worse. Hospitals aren’t exactly thriving either; they can’t afford to freeze rates, and insurers can’t afford to raise them.
This week, both sides will meet to draft a “white paper” to end the chaos. But really—it’s more ceasefire than peace.

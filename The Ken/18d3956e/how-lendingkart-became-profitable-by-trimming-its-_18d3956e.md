It’s a truism that every fintech will become a lender if it lives long enough. But there has been no consensus on what kind of a lender.
Should it build its own book, which would mean taking on the hard task of raising capital but getting to make meaty margins?
Should it simply not take on the headache of bad loans and underwriting and dumb it down to just distributing the loans on behalf of other lenders?
Or should it take the middle path of co-lending? One in which a fintech sources the loan and contributes a proportion of the loan amount, and a lending partner, which can be a bank or a non-bank, provides the rest. Even non-performing assets are split between the two.
If profitability is the ultimate measure of success, then numbers for the financial year of 2023 throw some answers.
Of the handful of fintechs that recorded a profit during the fiscal, the one that stood out the most was Lendingkart, which lends to small businesses. It saw a profit of Rs 119 crore (US$14 million), an impressive swing from a loss of Rs 203 crore (US$24 million) in the previous financial year.
The company grew its assets under management (AUM) to Rs 5,005 crore (US$602 million) by steadily increasing the share of co-lending loans—to over 60% of its AUM in the year ended March 2023 from about 6% three years ago.

We have a Big Story for you today.

25,000 rupees, or US$300—that’s how much it costs for an election candidate to create and disseminate an attack video made by professionals using AI. Slinging mud at one’s opponent works, but generative AI is also being used in less destructive ways: to customise clips for voter outreach and as a tool to assist with campaign strategising.

These expenses add up, but they’re worth it, according to people who spoke with The Ken. Besides, they’re necessary at a time when nearly every campaign is earmarking part of their budget for these methods. Abhirami interviewed more than 20 people for her story—individuals representing Gen AI startups and political consultants—to gauge the impact of AI-generated content on the electorate in India, as well as to figure out the degree of this technology’s adoption ahead of this year’s election in India.
Don’t miss it—it’s a free-read.

Then, in our other article for the day, Lu spoke with China-based merchants who sell all manner of cheap goods on Temu, the rapidly rising marketplace. Their playbooks cover familiar themes, but more importantly, they all depend on a tax rule that makes it possible to keep prices low. For customers in the United States, for instance, shipments valued below US$800 are exempt from duty and taxes.

Now that Temu’s white and orange packages are flooding into logistics channels, regulators are weighing the possibility of adjusting that dispensation. That spells trouble for a platform whose major selling point is affordability that beats major names like Amazon and Coupang. Check out Lu’s report to find out just how much this matters to Temu.

The internet is a dead place

Surely, like you, I’m getting used to talking to pieces of software. From the automated agents used by banks to order terminals in restaurants, our interactions are increasingly with lifeless things masquerading as thinking beings. The same goes for our communications online. Yancey Strickler, the former CEO of Kickstarter, made that point on his blog in an entry titled “The Dark Forest Theory of the Internet”, observing that we’re clustering together in private corners—as part of subscriber lists or on apps like Discord—that have the physical world’s social texture, without optimisation and indexing, where people’s behaviours aren’t being actively shaped and engagement metrics don’t matter.

The AI and misinformation expert Nina Schick, who authored Deepfakes: The Coming Infocalypse, believes it’ll take just a year for synthetic content to overwhelm textual, audio, and video content created by humans. If it sounds like she’s crying wolf, consider the fact that every two weeks, ChatGPT and its users create more text than has ever been written for published books. Maybe thinking about the Turing Test is no longer enough. We need to know more than what or who the other side is; we need to be able to understand why they’re engaging us.

This is not a diatribe against AI. If you have a loved one who suffered from an incurable disease, know there’s a good chance that someday, an advanced piece of software will fold the right simulated protein to discover a wonder drug and help patients with the same sickness. The benefits of developing Gen AI are endless. It’s just that we’ll need to wade through a lot of harmful consequences and try to emerge unscathed.

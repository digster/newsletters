Right now, the temperature outside is 25.3 degrees (although it “feels like” 34 degrees due to 86% humidity). For comparison, the temperature around the same time the last two days was 32.8 degrees and 34.2 degrees, respectively.

I hope this brief respite becomes the sort of the daily peak summer respite Bengaluru is known for—it gets really hot during the day, but by evening, the clouds get heavy and provide succour with mild showers. I’d even venture out and say we’re in the final stretch of Bengaluru’s peak summer.

The banyan fig next door has started fruiting. Though not fully ripe yet, the fruits have already started attracting birds and squirrels. A few weeks ago, there was even a monkey resting in the tree, a fact that drove Gabru mad (the monkey barely paid heed to all his barking).

Speaking of squirrels, I’ve only recently noticed how Gabru perks up every time the squirrels start their high-pitched pik-pik-pik squeaking. Because, like Pavlov’s dog, he too has figured out that it usually means he’s about to see a lurking cat outside.

Peak summer also means peak mango season. Years ago, I decided that once you were clear about your favourite varieties, it was best to focus all your efforts towards them. So our mango consumption this year has been Imampasands, Alphonsos, Malgoas, and Banganapalli’s in a 70%, 15%, 10%, 5% split. What’s yours?

1. Chekhov’s Gun and Feedforward Loops

At work, I can often be the crankiest of people to deal with. My colleagues at The Ken will easily attest to that.

“Why are you asking this question to a subscriber if you don’t know yet what you will do with their response?”

“Why are you adding this feature if you aren’t sure how many users will value it?”

“Why are you calling for a meeting when you haven’t thought about what’s in it for the others who attend it?”

The common thread for me across most such interactions is, what do you intend to do with X later on?

I’m also an unabashed collector and user of analogies and metaphors. And the best ones have a way of making themselves appropriate in a variety of situations.

One of my all-time favourites is “Chekhov’s Gun”.

The phrase is attributed to Russian playwright Anton Chekhov (1860-1904). Chekhov is considered to be one of the greatest writers of all time, a master of the short story. In letters to colleagues outlining his writing techniques, he has explained that if you introduce something in a story—say, a gun—then that gun should later be used. Otherwise, the audience is left wondering why it was brought to their attention in the first place, as it did not further the plot or characters. This could lead to disappointment or frustration.

Chekhov himself explained it pithily, “If in the first act you have hung a pistol on the wall, then in the following one it should be fired. Otherwise don’t put it there.”

Once you absorb this concept, you’ll start to notice it in movies and TV shows. If the camera focuses on something rather mundane—a door left ajar, a light that won’t switch on, a mug of coffee left untouched on a counter—then chances are the director is going to use it in a later scene. They are all “Chekhov’s guns”.

One could even call them feedforward loops, because of how they loop forward to the narrative arc. You see a “gun” in a scene, and you wonder, “hmmm, I wonder when this is going to be used in a later scene”. That’s the loop.

(Mind you, “feedforward loops/control in Systems Thinking refers to something else entirely, so please don’t take it in that sense.)

Marshall Goldsmith, the famous leadership coach and bestselling author, has been one of the strongest proponents of feedforward instead of feedback.

We can change the future. We can’t change the past. Feedforward helps people envision and focus on a positive future, not a failed past. Athletes are often trained using feedforward. Racecar drivers are taught to, “Look at the road ahead, not at the wall.” Basketball players are taught to envision the ball going in the hoop and to imagine the perfect shot. By giving people ideas on how they can be even more successful (as opposed to visualizing a failed past), we can increase their chances of achieving this success in the future.

Now, in life I am a strong believer in narratives. I believe the best professionals, products, organisations, and leaders have a compelling narrative that makes them stand out. Often these narratives are assembled (or massaged) with the benefit of hindsight, but sometimes they’re the result of deliberate and hard choices made over the years.

Sometimes, we’re only able to “see” these with the benefit of hindsight, which often correlates with age. And while your respective mileage may vary, I do think that it was since my mid-40s that I became better at “stepping back” and “seeing” things over a longer time scale measured in years. I see how so many of the choices I made in my career led me to where I am today, even if they seem “serendipitous”. 

Ironically, the price I had to pay for being better able to see my past stretching back decades was losing the ability to see things up close: presbyopia (our eyes get weaker post the mid-40s).

I’ve quit jobs twice in my career to write fiction. Neither attempts ended up in a book. But they brought me closer to a different form of writing—business journalism.

Despite training as an engineer and manager, the job that I spent the most number of years in was that of a business journalist. It made me appreciate the craft of journalism from the inside of a newsroom.

I briefly started, ran, and shut down a mobile app startup in my late 30s. Its failure only enabled a “fallow” period that allowed me the space to start The Ken with my co-founders.

If I had the ability to transport myself back in time, at each of these junctures, my decisions and the paths they took me down represented “Chekhov’s guns”.

“Oh, so he’s quitting his job to write a book? I wonder where this is going to come up later in his life.”

“He’s starting a mobile app startup which is most likely going to fail. I wonder how this will play out later in his life.”

But not every “gun” in a scene ends up getting used later on. Some are merely Red Herrings. They do not mean anything more than what they are.

Since we can’t all wait for the effects of our choices to play out serendipitously over the years, how best do we differentiate between a choice that’s a “Chekhov’s Gun” (implying it will change our professional lives or careers for the better over time, even if we don’t quite know how) and a “Red Herring” (a diversion from our true career, and one unlikely to confer any lasting benefit)?

I’d be lying if I told you I knew exactly how. But I do know that being conscious and honest to yourself while making the major decisions of your career can significantly reduce the chances of you walking down a Red Herring path. I’d offer you these four questions at such times:

  1.   Is this what you want to do?
  2.   Will doing this make you happier?
  3.   Is this what you want to be known for?
  4.   Will you regret it if this doesn’t end up making you wealthy?

2. Chetan Maini’s years between CEO and co-founder 🎙️

Hi folks, Anushka here. A few weeks ago, Rohin spoke to Chetan Maini, the co-founder and chairman of SUN Mobility. You may remember when we spoke about the episode in this newsletter a few weeks ago; about how Chetan—who built India’s first electric car, Reva—loved combustion engines, building model cars, and planes, and of course, the electric mobility space. And how, after stepping down from Mahindra Reva, he built what he believes is the future of this space: Battery-as-a-Service. 

But—what happened in the few years between?

Chetan was up to a lot of things. He was travelling the world. He still helped Anand Mahindra with strategy. He even helped BMTC take a part of its fleet electric. But most importantly—he wasn’t in a rush. He wasn’t actually looking for any opportunities. He was just spending some time spotting potential and possibilities. 

It’s a broad sort of description to a gap year, right? But it makes complete sense when Chetan explains it. In our most recent episode of the First Principles podcast, Chetan talks about how these years shaped a possibility into an idea, and helped him find his purpose—all over again. Listen here:

What you see above is, bar none, my favourite movie shot of all time. For all my distaste of the overreliance on it, it’s undeniable that there’s something about monochrome that’s just… classy. Striking. Otherworldly. Insert your adjective of choice. Chances are, they’ll fit. 

In a world rife with colour, the novelty and escape that B&W imagery offers definitely feels like an easy answer to why this is so. And it probably has a lot to do with it. A more interesting explanation is the relief it offers our overloaded brains. Here’s a decent breakdown I found of both hypotheses:

To state the obvious, we live in a world of color. It’s what we’re used to. By choosing black and white, artists consciously seek to transport us to another realm, a different kind of world. For some, this represents an intriguing step towards the surreal, the opportunity to take a step away from reality and to consider the artistic subject in unexpected and exciting ways. This is especially true given that eye-catching images in black and white can look uninteresting in color, and vice versa. For many photographers, black and white therefore offers the chance to transform mundane, often lackluster subjects, into things of absolute beauty. 

[…]

Color is often the most striking, attention grabbing feature of many photographs. When you remove this, the focus of the image has to shift to other compositional elements. Photographers and viewers alike are therefore forced to think about tone, texture and light in new ways. The depth of the image thus acquires a further importance. This is created by light contrasts, not color, and allows us to appreciate the craft in the texture and luminosity of the image even more. It is for this reason that many artists train in black and white photography, even if their portfolio is exclusively in color. Learning the skills behind monochrome photography can lend their color photos more depth and generally improve their practice.

Why Artists Choose Black and White Photography, Artsper

4. Butterflies, faith, and the Holocaust

Hi, dear reader, happy Sunday! It’s Anushka. This week’s book recommendations led me down a mess of a rabbit hole that ended on the Wikipedia pages of the Hiroshima and Nagasaki bombings. 

The first book, Fluke by Dr Brian Klaas, expands on chaos theory—and the role that pure chance plays on your life. He basically says that the world may be driven by completely random events, and this is bewildering simply because it really fights our illusion of control. Before this starts making me sound like the garden variety manifesting-affirmation-postive vibes guru, let me put this into context. Reading about this book reminded me of the Butterfly Effect—a phenomenon within Chaos Theory coined by meteorologist Edward Lorenz. It comes from Lorenz’s theory that the flapping of a butterfly’s wings—such a small, tiny thing—may alter the trajectory of a tycoon. Applied to our lives, this theory suggests that small incidents may have a massive impact on our lives. 

This may sound… obvious, but reading about it—especially in the context of a mental model—sharpened my understanding of just how crazy the butterfly effect can be. And that’s what led me to the Hiroshima and Nagasaki bombings. Apparently, a quick visibility-related decision completely changed where the bombs would be dropped, changing the trajectory of hundreds of people’s lives. 

There’s a similar theory about The Holocaust—and in fact, a lot of other major world events, like the India-Pakistan partition.Which brings us to Independence, the setting (and title) of another one of our recommendations in this edition. Let’s get to it!

Have you ever thought about exercise as a privilege? Well, it is. Even though this column is based on a UK study, I don’t think its observations are not equally relevant anywhere else.

The economics of running by Soumaya Keynes (Financial Times)

View in Browser

Running is both a relatively efficient way to burn calories and easy to track. That a chief executive would slot it in between a 4am wake-up and their 5am carrot juice cleanse makes (some) sense. More generally, people with higher socio-economic status are likelier to see exercise as a way to challenge themselves. And the less affluent are more likely to be on their feet at work, sapping energy for other activities.

Over time, Britons are becoming less and less likely to run, or to follow other fitness pursuits. (The pandemic only temporarily reversed that trend.) The drop is most extreme among students, and weakest among manager-types, skewing the sport older and richer.

Given Jonathan’s B&W-themed photo section today, I felt this piece on Casablanca is even more appropriate.

 

Playing it over and over again: how Casablanca was made by Tom Shone (The New Statesman)

At one screening in the late Sixties, according to the New Yorker’s David Denby, during the final reel the sound failed and the audience, speaking as one, recited the actors’ words for them, finishing the film up to its famous last line, “Louis, this could be the beginning of a beautiful friendship.”

That kind of quasi-religious devotion is not, generally, inspired by hokum. “Despite the artificial nature of the film it still speaks with uncommon poignancy to the exile condition,” writes Noah Isenberg in We’ll Always Have Casablanca, a devoted history of the film and its afterlife in countries such as Hungary and East Germany, where uncut versions of it circulated like samizdat. Nearly all of the 100-plus actors and actresses in the film were immigrants hailing from more than 34 different nations. Bogart was the lone American; you also had Bergman (Sweden), Claude Rains and Sydney Greenstreet (England), Paul Henreid (Austria), Conrad Veidt (Germany) and Peter Lorre, originally from Slovakia by way of London, who said that, like Brecht, he had changed countries “oftener than our shoes”. Hungarian SZ Sakall, who played the head waiter, lost three sisters to the concentration camps.

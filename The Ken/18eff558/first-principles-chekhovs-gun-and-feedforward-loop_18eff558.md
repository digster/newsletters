At work, I can often be the crankiest of people to deal with. My colleagues at The Ken will easily attest to that.
“Why are you asking this question to a subscriber if you don’t know yet what you will do with their response?”
“Why are you adding this feature if you aren’t sure how many users will value it?”
“Why are you calling for a meeting when you haven’t thought about what’s in it for the others who attend it?”
The common thread for me across most such interactions is, what do you intend to do with X later on?
I’m also an unabashed collector and user of analogies and metaphors. And the best ones have a way of making themselves appropriate in a variety of situations.
One of my all-time favourites is “[Chekhov’s Gun](https://www.torontofilmschool.ca/blog/chekhovs-gun-definition-examples-and-tips/)”.
The phrase is attributed to Russian playwright Anton Chekhov (1860-1904). Chekhov is considered to be one of the greatest writers of all time, a master of the short story. In letters to colleagues outlining his writing techniques, he has explained that if you introduce something in a story—say, a gun—then that gun should later be used. Otherwise, the audience is left wondering why it was brought to their attention in the first place, as it did not further the plot or characters. This could lead to disappointment or frustration.
Chekhov himself explained it pithily, “If in the first act you have hung a pistol on the wall, then in the following one it should be fired. Otherwise don’t put it there.”
Once you absorb this concept, you’ll start to notice it in movies and TV shows. If the camera focuses on something rather mundane—a door left ajar, a light that won’t switch on, a mug of coffee left untouched on a counter—then chances are the director is going to use it in a later scene. They are all “Chekhov’s guns”.
One could even call them feedforward loops, because of how they loop forward to the narrative arc. You see a “gun” in a scene, and you wonder, “hmmm, I wonder when this is going to be used in a later scene”. That’s the loop.
(Mind you, “feedforward loops/control in Systems Thinking refers to something else entirely, so please don’t take it in that sense.)
Marshall Goldsmith, the famous leadership coach and bestselling author, has been one of the strongest [ proponents of feedforward](https://marshallgoldsmith.com/articles/try-feedforward-instead-feedback/) instead of feedback.
We can change the future. We can’t change the past. Feedforward helps people envision and focus on a positive future, not a failed past. Athletes are often trained using feedforward. Racecar drivers are taught to, “Look at the road ahead, not at the wall.” Basketball players are taught to envision the ball going in the hoop and to imagine the perfect shot. By giving people ideas on how they can be even more successful (as opposed to visualizing a failed past), we can increase their chances of achieving this success in the future.
Now, in life I am a strong believer in narratives. I believe the best professionals, products, organisations, and leaders have a compelling narrative that makes them stand out. Often these narratives are assembled (or massaged) with the benefit of hindsight, but sometimes they’re the result of deliberate and hard choices made over the years.
Sometimes, we’re only able to “see” these with the benefit of hindsight, which often correlates with age. And while your respective mileage may vary, I do think that it was since my mid-40s that I became better at “stepping back” and “seeing” things over a longer time scale measured in years. I see how so many of the choices I made in my career led me to where I am today, even if they seem “serendipitous”.
Ironically, the price I had to pay for being better able to see my past stretching back decades was losing the ability to see things up close: presbyopia (our eyes get weaker post the mid-40s).
I’ve quit jobs twice in my career to write fiction. Neither attempts ended up in a book. But they brought me closer to a different form of writing—business journalism.
Despite training as an engineer and manager, the job that I spent the most number of years in was that of a business journalist. It made me appreciate the craft of journalism from the inside of a newsroom.
I briefly started, ran, and shut down a mobile app startup in my late 30s. Its failure only enabled a “fallow” period that allowed me the space to start The Ken with my co-founders.
If I had the ability to transport myself back in time, at each of these junctures, my decisions and the paths they took me down represented “Chekhov’s guns”.
“Oh, so he’s quitting his job to write a book? I wonder where this is going to come up later in his life.”
“He’s starting a mobile app startup which is most likely going to fail. I wonder how this will play out later in his life.”
But not every “gun” in a scene ends up getting used later on. Some are merely[ Red Herrings](https://www.torontofilmschool.ca/blog/chekhovs-gun-definition-examples-and-tips/#what-is-the-difference-between-chekhov-s-gun-and-a-red-herring). They do not mean anything more than what they are.
Since we can’t all wait for the effects of our choices to play out serendipitously over the years, how best do we differentiate between a choice that’s a “Chekhov’s Gun” (implying it will change our professional lives or careers for the better over time, even if we don’t quite know how) and a “Red Herring” (a diversion from our true career, and one unlikely to confer any lasting benefit)?
I’d be lying if I told you I knew exactly how. But I do know that being conscious and honest to yourself while making the major decisions of your career can significantly reduce the chances of you walking down a Red Herring path. I’d offer you these four questions at such times:
- Is this what you want to do?
- Will doing this make you happier?
- Is this what you want to be known for?
- Will you regret it if this doesn’t end up making you wealthy?

Color is often the most striking, attention grabbing feature of many photographs. When you remove this, the focus of the image has to shift to other [compositional elements](https://blog.artsper.com/en/a-closer-look/photography-techniques-processes/). Photographers and viewers alike are therefore forced to think about tone, texture and light in new ways. The depth of the image thus acquires a further importance. This is created by [light](https://www.artsper.com/us/contemporary-artworks/light-games) contrasts, not color, and allows us to appreciate the craft in the texture and luminosity of the image even more. It is for this reason that many artists train in black and white photography, even if their portfolio is exclusively in color. Learning the skills behind [monochrome](https://www.artsper.com/us/contemporary-artworks/monochrome-tag) photography can lend their color photos more depth and generally improve their practice.

Hi, dear reader, happy Sunday! It’s Anushka. This week’s book recommendations led me down a mess of a rabbit hole that ended on the Wikipedia pages of the Hiroshima and Nagasaki bombings.
The first book, Fluke by Dr Brian Klaas, expands on chaos theory—and the role that pure chance plays on your life. He basically says that the world may be driven by completely random events, and this is bewildering simply because it really fights our illusion of control. Before this starts making me sound like the garden variety manifesting-affirmation-postive vibes guru, let me put this into context. Reading about this book reminded me of the Butterfly Effect—a phenomenon within Chaos Theory coined by meteorologist Edward Lorenz. It comes from Lorenz’s theory that the flapping of a butterfly’s wings—such a small, tiny thing—may alter the trajectory of a tycoon. Applied to our lives, this theory suggests that small incidents may have a massive impact on our lives.
This may sound… obvious, but reading about it—especially in the context of a [mental model](https://fs.blog/the-butterfly-effect/)—sharpened my understanding of just how crazy the butterfly effect can be. And that’s what led me to the Hiroshima and Nagasaki bombings. Apparently, a quick visibility-related decision completely changed where the bombs would be dropped, changing the trajectory of hundreds of people’s lives.
There’s a similar theory about The Holocaust—and in fact, a lot of other major world events, like the India-Pakistan partition.Which brings us to Independence, the setting (and title) of another one of our recommendations in this edition. Let’s get to it!

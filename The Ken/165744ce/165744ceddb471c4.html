Good Morning Ishan,
 
Beautiful August Sunday to you.
 
I am unlike my colleague Ranjan. I don’t watch television news. At all. 
 
Pay me and I won’t watch it. Harass me and I wouldn’t care. Coax me and I won’t give in.
 
But when the opportunity comes to sit across from and listen to or read what one of the foremost television anchors in India has to say about his profession, his lament on how things have come to be and his perspective on the state of affairs in the country, the mood and the politics; well, you will find me there. All ears. Front row. 
 
In many ways than one, today’s story is that.   
 
A detailed conversation with Rajdeep Sardesai around his latest book. One of India’s best known television anchors and currently a consulting editor with the India Today Group, Sardesai comes on at 9PM, with his prime-time programme News Today on the group’s television channel. His recent book, Newsman, is a collection of his essays and columns from the past four years on politics, the Narendra Modi government and the Indian media. These essays point to a country that’s changing rapidly. Aggressive brand of politics. Check. Polarisation in public discourse. Check. Polarisation in the media business. Double check. Name-calling with words like presstitute, libtard and anti-national; you know what, keep on checking those boxes. 
 
Sardesai, the man in the thick of it, puts it rather well: “It is while engaging in a toxic Hindu-Muslim and ‘national’ versus ‘anti-national’ arguments, driven by a rising Islamophobia, that one feels almost caged in a television (TV) news studio.” 
 
Hmm...must be pretty amazing and well-paying for someone to last so long in it. 
 
Except, Sardesai says quitting isn’t the answer. “[Writing] gives me some space to be out of this ugly, them-versus-us, noisy space. I am [in that space] because every time I say enough, I tell myself, well, if everyone walks away… it's a sign of giving up.”
 
I highly recommend today’s piece.
 
The writers, my colleagues Pranav and Ranjan, have done a good job of bringing in different perspectives. There are answers to questions such as, what exactly ails the television news business and when did it all start? Has public discourse really suffered in the last four years, or is it a figment of Sardesai’s imagination? Questions that get lost in the outrage of the Delhi columnist prose writer.

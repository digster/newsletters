Good Morning Ishan,
 
I’ve always had, you could say, a fondness for pretty basic food. As children, my brother and I used to be overjoyed with dal and rice. Wait, there’s salad too? Life is great. And yoghurt? Paradise.
 
For most of the last month though, I found myself gorging on junk/takeout pretty much every other day. Culminating in a doctor telling me rather gently, listen, take it easy, eh? I was aghast. How the mighty have fallen. Woe. But in any case, for the last week or so, I vowed to reset my schedule, with a renewed focus on wholesome eating. (Exercise will come another day.)
 
And if I may say so, our stories the week gone by were a fine mix, a sort of balanced diet for the mind. 
 
We started the week with Harveen’s story about Indian music streaming service Gaana deciding to take on YouTube—specifically, in music videos. The audio streaming market is heating up fast, with deep-pocketed companies like Apple, Amazon, Google, Spotify, Jio and Airtel in the mix. Gaana, it seems, decided it wants to stand out somehow. But can it work? (Bonus news: Just a couple of days after this story, YouTube launched a standalone music subscription service in India.)
 
Then we had Ruhi’s piece offering a peek behind the curtain at a quiet war over vaccines for whooping cough, a disease that has seen a global resurgence in recent years. A resurgence linked to a vaccine manufactured by pharma giants GSK and Sanofi—which rule the Indian market on whooping cough treatment. It’s a fascinating story about the kind of influence pharma companies and doctors’ associations have over the private healthcare system.
 
Moving on to Wednesday, that apogee of the working week, Vandana dove deep into another often overlooked problem: India’s lack of standards for testing telecom equipment. Naturally, quality, reliability and security concerns abound. The country imports 97% of the equipment it needs, and all manufacturers have to do is self-certify them, a process, Vandana points out, akin to asking students to check their own exam papers.
 
On Thursday, Ashish took us through yet another twist in MakeMyTrip and OYO’s on-again-off-again relationship. Just a year after an agreement that listed OYO’s hotels on MakeMyTrip’s booking platform, the budget hotel aggregator wants to renegotiate. And it has some stiff terms—like MakeMyTrip having to guarantee it will sell a minimum of 10% of OYO’s rooms. What’s in it for MakeMyTrip? Read and find out.
 
What next? Well, Friday saw Roshni explore the global PC chip war between a dominant Intel and a revived AMD—a fight in which India is a key battleground. After years of being a bit player, AMD has made a comeback in the past two years, undercutting Intel’s prices and wresting market share across segments. India, Roshni says, has become the 49-year-old chipmaker’s biggest commercial PC market, and the battle’s only begun.
 
And last but not least, Suraksha made her debut with Saturday’s story on a rather worrying situation. Parkinson’s disease patients in India have little to no access to a drug that’s been available elsewhere for over 15 years. This is a country with over 850,000 such patients. What’s more, two companies (one Indian, on British) have launched drugs to tackle Parkinson’s—but one can’t match demand, and the other doesn’t have clearance.
 
It’s a hefty set of stories. Chew on them at leisure, and as always, let me know what you think.
 
Cheers,

Everyone has an inner circle—a group of close friends who advise each other, whose companionship is exclusive, whose loyalty is immutable.

If they each start a business, they might all buy from each other and lend each other money. After all, there’s a circle of trust, so why ditch the group? This arrangement might stand even if someone from outside the circle offers a better deal. After all, if something isn’t broken, then don’t try to fix it. Everyone could win together in the long run.

There’s a grandiose version of that framework in Japan’s corporate world: a network of big companies that own stakes in each other, sign long-term agreements, and stick together through thick and thin. It’s called a keiretsu (translation: “series”).

Plus, there’s always a designated bank to backstop risk. Even though there’s cross-ownership, these companies are (mostly) independently run. It’s a relationship that’s meant to bring about stability, shared growth, and protection from hostile takeovers—a particularly important trait given how Japanese family-controlled conglomerates were dissolved after World War II.

Without getting too deep into the historical details of keiretsu, here’s what Toyota’s network looks like:

Among Japanese automotive-components manufacturers, Denso makes powertrains, JTEKT makes steering systems, Tokai Rika makes vehicle interiors and electronics… you get the idea.

The general concept is that Toyota’s success depends on the suppliers and manufacturers of its vehicles’ components and raw materials, and vice versa. Toyota itself guarantees orders, and its suppliers prioritise those orders. Every entity in the network has the incentive to coordinate with others. A shared long-term relationship is built into every keiretsu; it also creates operational patterns and dependencies that are resistant to change.

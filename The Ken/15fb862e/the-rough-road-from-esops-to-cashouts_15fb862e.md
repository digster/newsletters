Last month, we kicked off our ESOP coverage with an analysis of Flipkart’s $100 million share buyback. Today, we’re looking closer at the nascent, unpredictable, and often opaque world of secondary share sales for India’s technology startups.
As companies stay private for far longer periods, employees are left holding shares (often via the ESOP route) that on paper are multiplying in valuation, but in reality, are very hard to profit from. Selling such shares often requires the approval of a company’s board and/or its other investors. Buyers are wary because they come with lesser investor protection rights. Meanwhile, regulators like the SEBI continue to place restrictions on how private shares can be bought and sold, leading to the premature closure of efforts to set up marketplaces for them (Tracxn tried and failed).
For the rank and file of startups, making any meaningful money from share sales continues to remain a pipe dream, as Payal’s second ESOP story shows.

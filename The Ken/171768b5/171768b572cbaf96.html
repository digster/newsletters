Good Morning Ishan,

It’s very likely that by now, you’ve either downloaded the app or have ignored incessant nudges to download it. Much water has flown down the Aarogya Setu since it was released on 2 April as the contact-tracing and containment tool for Covid-19.

Some 25 million people have downloaded the app. But for it to be effective, at least 2/3rd of the population must use it. But like everything Covid, the app is hard to decode, and instils fear and mistrust.

Don’t get me wrong; this is no time to debate public health and personal privacy. Given the nature of the virus, every country needs a tool and a strategy that uses the existing digital infrastructure. Even arch rivals Apple and Google are working together on such an app to alert people—an opt-in feature—if they’ve been in touch with an infected person. Others are working on making smartwatch data (from any brand) useful in predicting emerging risk zones.

So, using an app from the government isn’t problematic as such. What is, though, is how the app makers have gone about it. It’s a black box. The app collects users’ names, age, profession, health history, and smoking preferences. It also asks for their GPS location, despite GPS not playing a role in contact tracing.

What is it solving for? Well, as Pratap and Shreedhar report in the story, the think tank NITI Aayog took the help of developers from popular consumer startups such as MakeMyTrip and 1mg. The focus was on data collection and aggregation, not so much on the use of data. Even when the app idea itself is not unique. (Why copy if you can’t get one better. #JustSaying.)

Our writers Shreedhar and Pratap show how Aarogya Setu can still get better. If not, international resources, like the Harvard-initiated Mobility Network, which is already sharing information with state governments such as Maharashtra, Telangana, Kerala, and Tamil Nadu, will fill the void.

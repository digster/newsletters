Good Morning Ishan,

Glassdoor, the careers and salaries portal, got bought for $1.2 billion in cash yesterday. One of its most potent assets were the tens of thousands of unvarnished and honest reviews about working for companies, from current and former employees. Their intangible value would easily be in the hundreds of millions of dollars.

But reviews are hard. Especially for hotels. Getting 1,000 reviews on an aggregator’s website will take the typical hotel 18-24 months. *If they’re pesky, great or awful.* If only there were a way to cut down that time.

If only.

Now you’re a subscriber to The Ken, so I don’t need to tell you what money laundering is. But did you know about review laundering?

review laundering

noun

The concealment of the origins of online marketplace reviews, typically by means of mass reviews posted by employees or paid agents.

I totally made up that definition. But after reading today’s story, you'll think I didn’t.

Yesterday’s Part 1 story about MakeMyTrip—the undisputed leader of India’s chaotic but growing online travel and hospitality space—was about its slow but inexorable drift into the gravitational pull of Softbank, the Jupiter of India’s Internet ecosystem. And about MakeMyTrip’s attempts to get closer to Softbank without colliding into the other planets already revolving around it - Oyo and Paytm.

Today’s Part 2 starts with MakeMyTrip politely sticking a knife in the backs of Oyo’s competitors Treebo and FabHotels, by booting them from its platform. Then the sudden appearance of hundreds of reviews for Oyo’s properties on the MakeMyTrip platform. Only to culminate in a coordinated knife-twisting by dozens of Oyo’s recruiters, each offering exploding job offers to Treebo and FabHotel’s employees.

I felt like I was reading a Walking Dead episode.

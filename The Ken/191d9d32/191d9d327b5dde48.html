Why on earth should you care about Unimech? It’s an unlisted company with a modest Rs 200 crore (US$24 million) in annual revenue, barely a blip on anyone’s radar.

Well, for starters, it has just filed papers for a Rs 500-crore IPO. That’s always exciting, right?

Or maybe because just in July, it secured funding for the first time in eight years, raising Rs 250 crore at a valuation of Rs 3,250 crore. Sure, that sounds like a lot of money and optimism.

Or maybe it’s because we are in a stock-market rally where investors will throw money at anything vaguely promising, and Unimech looks like it’s trying to hop on that wave.

So yes, yes, and yes. But also, no.

“No” because Unimech isn’t what it seems. It’s trying to pass itself off as a defence stock.

Unimech mainly makes aerospace tooling and components—important, yes, but not exactly the tanks-missiles-and-ships type stuff and other high-tech equipment you associate with defence companies. The likes of Hindustan Aeronautics, Bharat Electronics, Paras Defence, and Data Patterns. But in a market where stocks in this space are trading at stratospheric valuations—The Ken wrote in July that some of them were at about 100X profits—Unimech listing some of them like Paras and Data Patterns (India) as peers is no surprise.

After all, post-Covid India loves defence stocks.

Thanks to the government’s push for domestic production, stocks up and down the defence supply chain are raking it in. Just last week, after a nod to a massive proposal to enrich defence capabilities, another round of headlines hyped up the sector again: “Defence stocks to buy,” they screamed.

So, can Unimech really convince investors of all kinds that it belongs in the gold rush? Are there more such companies sticking the defence-stock label? More importantly, can all of these keep up the disguise long enough?

With Donald Trump on his way out, the Indian dream—a Silicon Valley job and an eventual green card—is now a lot more attainable. Already, there's talk of the incoming administration lowering the H1-B skilled visa drawbridge, the first step towards a US posting.
Today's story is about Druva, a company that lived this dream, albeit in simpler times. A cybersecurity firm that began in Pune, it relocated to the US in 2012, struck paydirt, and is a veritable leader in its field.
For perspective, when Druva decided to up and move, it had a few hundred clients. Today, it has a few thousand and is among Amazon Web Services' largest cloud data storage consumers. It has crossed the $100 million mark in annual recurring revenue, and a funding round last year placed it firmly in unicorn territory, with a valuation north of $1 billion.
For all of this, Druva hasn't really lost touch with its Indian roots. It still maintains a presence in Pune, even ramping up its Indian workforce over the last few years. This, however, is largely to service its global clientele. Its Indian user base is a comparatively insignificant part of its overall business, despite the company indicating it would like to go deeper in the country it started in.
It's not like India doesn't require the services of a company like Druva. Data breaches among Indian companies are becoming more common with each passing month. Last month, two publicly listed Indian pharma companies—Lupin and Dr Reddy's—were hit by ransomware attacks within weeks of each other. The latter even had to pause its worldwide operations.
So, why isn't Druva finding as many takers in India as it does overseas? That's what Shreedhar explores in today's story, along with Druva's big hope for India—the impending Personal Data Protection law.

Good Morning Ishan,
 
I don't envy National Payments Corporation of India (NPCI), the country's quasi-regulatory retail payments body. It currently has a Cain and Abel situation on its hands.
 
On the one hand, it unleashed a real-time mobile-based payments system called UPI, which now does two billion transactions a month. This is much more than what credit and debit cards do combined.
 
On the other hand, card network RuPay, NPCI's other creation, is suffering an existential crisis.
 
Both RuPay's debit cards and UPI do the same thing—debit your bank account when you pay. But their stories couldn't be more different.
 
Pranav, the author of today's story, reports that UPI is being given the star child treatment within the NPCI. This, despite RuPay seeing roaring success when the government promoted it via its financial inclusion programme, the Pradhan Mantri Jan Dhan Yojana. The programme enabled about 300 million people to get access to bank accounts and debit cards for the first time. RuPay ended up expanding its reach to 600 million bank account holders, with a 58% market share as of 2019.
 
But UPI is drawing blood from all debit card networks—RuPay, Visa, and Mastercard.
 
RuPay was born out of legitimate need. Visa and Mastercard, both American card networks, preferred working with private banks. This left behind the users of smaller banks. RuPay set out to balance that when it launched in 2012. But the banks soon realised that RuPay was turning out to become a cost centre, instead of a revenue centre, for the very banks it was meant to serve.
 
Banks are shunning RuPay once again, thanks to the government's move to charge merchants no fees for accepting digital payments via RuPay debit cards or UPI.
 
While Visa and Mastercard at least have credit cards to bail them out, RuPay does not. Instead, in what could turn out to be a blow to the domestic card network, the NPCI is trying to push credit via UPI.

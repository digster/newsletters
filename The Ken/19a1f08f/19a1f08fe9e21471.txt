Good morning Ishan, and welcome back to First Principles Sunday after Deepavali—a time of sweets, lights, and family!

How did your Deepavali go? Mine was unexpectedly eventful.

I started the day thinking I’d use the Monday holiday to (sigh!) clear my badly clogged inbox. I know, right? Then, quite unexpectedly, I caught myself telling myself, “Come on, Rohin, don’t be such a loser. It’s Deepavali. Enjoy it!”

So, I picked up a bunch of unread books from my desk and went outside to our balcony, where the wonderful early winter sun was casting a soft and warming glow on everything. By the time I got into a chair and decided what book to read, Gabru had also plonked himself in the sun next to me.

Over the next couple of hours, I had my mind blown by incontrovertible arguments, uncompromising rebuttals, and acerbic wit written nearly 90 years ago. I’d read Dr. B.R. Ambedkar’s Annihilation of Caste.

The short book is an undelivered speech on the evils of India’s caste system written by Ambedkar, bookended by letters that explain its origins—the invitation, the discomfort, the revocation—and criticisms (by Mahatma Gandhi) and rebuttals (by Ambedkar himself).

There is enough on the internet about the book, so I won’t attempt to summarise it. But I will say I was both blown away and saddened by the timelessness of Dr. Ambedkar’s arguments and of the reality he describes from 1936.

Then, as the sun became brighter and hotter, Gabru and I moved inside. I switched to Joe Sacco’s comic book, Journalism.

By the evening, it had started to rain, continuing a tradition that’s been going on for years. It always rains on Deepavali day in Bengaluru.

Once the rain stopped and darkness fell, we switched on the numerous LED balcony lights that are now de rigueur and lit terracotta oil diyas with little glass chimneys on them that remind me of mini-Petromax lamps.

This was the start of what I’d like to imagine the scariest period of the year for Gabru, Bribo, and other dogs. As the sound of firecrackers started building up from 7:30 p.m. to their crescendo around 10 p.m., Gabru’s shivering backside was virtually attached to one of us.

Bribo, too, was nowhere to be found that night, or for the next two, when I went with his dinner. The day before, on Sunday, I found him roaming the roads in a confused and stressed state (with dogs, the stressed and excited states both look the same!) due to the still-few firecrackers that were being burst. I took him back to his kennel, but he was too stressed to eat dinner.

It would be Thursday by the time the firecrackers died down and Bribo was around in his usual spot for me to feed him. I say this merely to illustrate how different the experience of firecrackers can be for animals.

Anyhow, Deepavali day ended with me watching the brilliant Hindi folk-horror movie Tumbbad, based on the recommendation of one of our newest colleagues at The Ken, Vishnu. He joins us as our first “Community Manager” after having tried his hand at video production, ghostwriting, building a Motorsports Academy with a former F1 aerodynamicist friend at Red Bull Racing, and other “random” gigs. When not crossing off movies from his Letterboxd watchlist, you can probably catch him hosting or attending pub quizzes in Bengaluru.

Like I told you, unexpectedly eventful!

1. Living a life you like while solving poorly defined problems

About a month ago, a young reader in her final year of college wrote me an email with some details about her professional interests and some appreciation for the First Principles and The Ken. She ended her email with the following line, in bold.

“If I may, I’d love to know—what’s something you wish you had known when you were 22?”

My one-line reply to her was, “Doing things that give you joy and make you happy is the best first principle to organise your career.”

I also remember when I was that age. What would I tell my younger, 22-year-old self? Heck, I don’t remember even consciously thinking about happiness and joy when I was 22, much less optimising my life and career choices around them.

But hey, doesn’t each generation avoid the mistakes of the last? Doesn’t each one get smarter than the last?

The roundabout answer to that came by way of a wonderful essay by psychologist Adam Mastroianni, shared with me by a close friend just a couple of days ago.

“Why aren’t smart people happier?” https://www.theseedsofscience.pub/p/why-arent-smart-people-happier asks Mastroianni.

Please do go read it first. This is the part that really spoke to me. (emphasis mine).

“There is, unfortunately, no good word for “skill at solving poorly defined problems.” Insight, creativity, agency, self-knowledge—they’re all part of it, but not all of it. Wisdom comes the closest, but it suggests a certain fustiness and grandeur, and poorly defined problems aren’t just dramatic questions like “how do you live a good life”; they’re also everyday questions like “how do you host a good party” and “how do you figure out what to do today.”

One way to spot people who are good at solving poorly defined problems is to look for people who feel good about their lives; “how do I live a life I like” is a humdinger of a poorly defined problem. The rules aren’t stable: what makes you happy may make me miserable. The boundaries aren’t clear: literally anything I do could make me more happy or less happy. The problems are not repeatable: what made me happy when I was 21 may not make me happy when I’m 31. Nobody else can be completely sure whether I’m happy or not, and sometimes I’m not even sure. In fact, some people might claim that I’m not really happy, no matter what I say, unless I accept Jesus into my heart or reach nirvana or fall in love—if I think I’m happy before all that, I’m simply mistaken about what happiness is!”

Why aren’t smart people happier? https://www.theseedsofscience.pub/p/why-arent-smart-people-happier by Adam Mastroianni

This isn’t the central argument of the essay, but I loved it because it links happiness—incredibly subjective, hard, and undefined—to people’s abilities to handle “poorly defined problems”.

I am not implying a one-way causality. Do “people who feel good about their lives” end up being people who are “skilled at solving poorly defined problems”, or do the latter eventually end up applying their principles to their own lives, thus optimising for their own happiness?

“Skilled at solving poorly defined problems” is what I’ve belatedly come to realise defines many of us at The Ken who enjoy working here. We are by no means an easy or comfortable workplace, but there is a certain, predictable evolution that most of us have gone through.

In fact, one of our most interesting and ambitious experiments is our current “second decade” https://theken.typeform.com/to/lF79Lsm9 hiring effort to try and hire talented and driven people without well-defined JDs (job descriptions).

A soon-to-be colleague called me earlier this week after we’d made him an offer. He had a major question. “What is the JD?” he asked me.

“It doesn’t exist. We’ll create it together once you join us. Don’t worry, we won’t demand results from you before you’ve gotten a chance to understand things and formulate your own desired JD. We’ll start from there,” I told him.

I said this is an experiment because I understand the numerous ways in which this could backfire and fail too. But I don’t want to provide “well-defined JDs” to people because, in my experience, they then tend to limit their thinking and ambition to something someone else wrote.

Instead, we should get a chance to write the rules of our own game.

I tried to describe this to four of my newest colleagues a few weeks ago as part of our “onboarding” presentation, which I decided to revamp after many years.

Now, I’m sure many of my colleagues, current and former, might disagree with my assessment. But here’s how I see the four stages of working at The Ken (I used an emoji to capture the essence of each).

Stage 1 (0–1 year): 😐 “What is this game? What are its rules? Do I even want to play it?”

Stage 2 (1–3 years): 🥲 “This isn’t an easy game, but I can do it.”

Stage 3 (3–5 years): 😳 “This game is hard, but I seem to be enjoying playing it. You know what, I might actually be good at it!”

Stage 4 (5–∞ years): 🤪 ““Playing Calvinball””

We’ve talked about “playing Calvinball” in a past edition https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/killing-your-darlings-and-playing-calvinball/, so if you want to go give it a read, I’ll wait. (I view Calvinball as the ultimate metaphor for playing the “poorly defined problem” called life!)

Most people, including me as recently as three or four years ago, assume that we are happy at work because our workplaces are happy.

What I’ve learnt is that “hard” workplaces—ones that constantly “push” you forward and also “pull” you towards the future—are the ones that make you professionally happy.

Joy doesn’t come from being comfortable and taking it easy, but from pushing yourself towards newer, better versions of yourself. Remember “Flow” https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/treasure-your-frustration from an earlier edition?

“In our studies, we found that every flow activity, whether it involved competition, chance, or any other dimension of experience, had this in common: It provided a sense of discovery, a creative feeling of transporting the person into a new reality. It pushed the person to higher levels of performance, and led to previously undreamed-of states of consciousness. In short, it transformed the self by making it more complex. In this growth of the self lies the key to flow activities.”

To complete the circle, which started with an email, here’s another. Parashuram (also a First Principles reader) wrote to me a week before the 22-year-old did, in reply to the edition on Psychological Runway https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/psychological-runway/. Buried in that email was a link to a 1994 essay called “The Road to Self-Renewal” https://gsb-courses.stanford.edu/building-innovative-brands/wp-content/uploads/sites/25/2022/04/johngardner-roadtoself-renewal2.pdf, written by the late novelist, critic, and teacher, John Gardner. Nudged by Parashuram’s email, I went back and re-read the essay.

Please do go read it in full yourself, too. There’s way too much wisdom in it for me to summarise, but here’s one excerpt that resonated most with me. 

“Life isn’t a mountain that has a summit. Nor is it, as some suppose, a riddle that has an answer. Nor a game that has a final score. Life is an endless unfolding and, if we wish it to be, an endless process of self-discovery, an endless and unpredictable dialogue between our own potentialities and the life situations in which we find ourselves. By potentialities I mean not just success as the world measures success, but the full range of one’s capacities for learning, sensing, wondering, understanding, loving, and aspiring.

Perhaps you imagine that by age 45 or even 55 you have explored those potentialities pretty fully. Don’t kid yourself!”

The Road to Self-Renewal https://gsb-courses.stanford.edu/building-innovative-brands/wp-content/uploads/sites/25/2022/04/johngardner-roadtoself-renewal2.pdf by John Gardner

And here’s what Parashuram’s email to me read.

“Read the essay The road to Self-Renewal by John Gardner mentioned in the article, it was written before I was born, yet more true to me.

Felt calm and enlightened by it.

Loved the quote ‘At the end of every road you meet yourself’”

2. Budding readers

Hello Ishan, it’s Tanim with your books section for this week. 

I came across a fascinating piece of information: according to a report https://www.oecd.org/content/dam/oecd/en/publications/reports/2002/11/reading-for-change-performance-and-engagement-across-countries_g1gh2e9f/9789264099289-en.pdf released by the Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD), a child’s enjoyment of reading is a more powerful predictor of educational success than their family’s socio-economic status. 

It got me thinking about how we get children to read, and that’s the question I want us to explore this month. If you, too, have experiences or suggestions that you would like to share with the community, please write to fp@the-ken.com mailto:fp@the-ken.com. (Don’t forget that if you read something fun during the Deepavali holidays, you can share all about it here https://theken.typeform.com/FPBooks?typeform-source=the-ken.com.) 

For my part, I started by reflecting on my own childhood. My first thought was that my mother’s strictness—limiting TV and internet—was what made me a reader. But then I remembered an earlier time, before those rules, when TV was allowed. Even then, I loved book fairs. I would invent fake school assignments so my parents would buy me story books, making the purchase feel educational rather than a mere leisure activity. I was especially drawn to collections, eager to devour many different stories in one volume. 

This made me realise that while discipline has its place, insisting on a hardcore approach that bans all other entertainment isn’t the best way to encourage children to read. So, what does work? The internet is filled with “advice” on this front, and every child is unique, so don’t lose heart if most of those tips don’t work. Having said that, based on the reading I have done and my own experience, I’ve noticed that it is best to always start with stories that children want to hear and read, and then slowly introduce them to newer things. 

I saw this happen with my little sister, who was never a big reader. My recommendations always fell on deaf ears. But we had a breakthrough on a long train journey from Guwahati to Chennai. I had bought a bootleg copy of Twilight, and without any prompting, she picked it up and was hooked. She then discovered thrillers and loved them, so I kept buying them for her, even though they weren’t my top choices. After that, I never had to prompt her again. We started reading Hardy Boys and Nancy Drew together. She even finished The Girl with the Dragon Tattoo before I did. 

Which brings me to another point. Series are a great way to encourage a reading habit because, as a paper https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/00131881.2023.2236123#d1e371 published on Taylor and Francis notes, they “generate anticipation for the next installment” and create a natural, self-sustaining reading cycle. 

Another paper https://academic.oup.com/pch/article-abstract/7/6/404/2648418 published on Paediatrics & Child Health (PCH), the official journal of the Canadian Paediatric Society, suggests that for an impatient reader, you should “choose books that have movement”, and that “books with short chapters or ‘cliff-hangers’ encourage children to keep reading”. 

But essentially, just surround them with books and keep reading yourself. They’ll eventually pick one up. 

This isn’t about restriction, but connection. Meet them in a world of stories they already love and you can build a bridge to a lifetime of reading.

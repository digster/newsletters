Good Morning Ishan,

It’s never been so good to be in the travel business. Indigo, India’s largest airline, made almost a billion dollars in profits in FY23. Nasdaq-listed Makemytrip, the country’s biggest online travel agency (OTA), seldom saw its share price cross US$40 until last September. Now it’s trading at over twice as much.

So it only stands to reason that Makemytrip’s rival Cleartrip would be on a tear too. But its revenue has stagnated at a measly US$12 million since the pandemic, while losses have surged to US$81 million. Since February, Cleartrip’s CEO and CFO have left the company.

This is not what Flipkart would have had in mind when it bought Cleartrip on the cheap in 2021. So Gaurav set out to understand why Flipkart’s plans for Cleartrip came to naught. Hint: Flipkart thought online travel wasn’t all that different from e-commerce.

The new totem pole of global listed companies

At the start of 2023, no one would have expected Nvidia to become the investor favourite it is now, much less eclipse Apple to become the world’s second-most-valuable company.

The chipmaker’s shares have since soared 8X, taking its market cap past US$3 trillion on Wednesday. If the artificial-intelligence boom is responsible for Nvidia’s staggering rise, it’s also key to OpenAI backer Microsoft’s numero uno status, Meta’s 5X rise from its all-time low in October 2022, and semiconductor giant TSMC’s place in the top 10.

If AI is rejigging the hierarchy among tech giants, the advances in weight-loss drugs have catapulted pharma majors Eli Lilly (which makes Mounjaro and Zepbound) and Novo Nordisk (Ozempic and Wegovy) into a league until now reserved mostly for tech and oil companies. Eli Lilly is worth nearly US$800 billion, and Novo Nordisk, US$630 billion.

Such investor optimism is not without reason: Zepbound is expected to be the best-selling drug in history. If that isn’t a once-in-a-generation phenomenon, I don’t know what is.

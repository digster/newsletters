Good Morning Ishan,
Being a writer forces clarity of thought upon you, whether you want it or not. Take, for instance, Siddhartha Mukherjee, assistant professor at Columbia University in the United States. You may know him as the author of the Pulitzer-winning book on cancer, Emperor of All Maladies. In addition to being a physician, oncologist, professor, researcher and author, Mukherjee also manages to somehow run four startups.
But you know it’s the author in him that explains the concept of treating cancers early on with such clarity: “If you start with 1 billion cells, there are 1 billion routes to resist [the treatment], if you start with 100 cells, there are 100 routes to resist [the treatment]. So detecting early and cheating early has been successful,].”
Mukherjee is part of a 3-person founding team at Immuneel, a cancer treatment startup that wants to drastically reduce the price of cutting-edge CAR-T cell therapy for Indian patients to $50,000.
Yes, $50,000. Because the comparable costs from global majors like Novartis and Gilead range from $375,000-$475,000.
CAR-T cell therapy is a radical new form of cancer treatment that involves extracting T cells, a type of lymphocyte that greatly assists immunity, from patients and then genetically engineering and multiplying them before finally inserting them back into the patient. Where they go out and destroy the cancer cells.
Mukherjee’s co-founders are equally formidable—India’s leading biotech entrepreneur Kiran Mazumdar-Shaw and Kush Parmar, a US-based physician-scientist who runs 5AM Ventures, a fund with $1.5 billion under management.
Immuneel, their startup, has already raised over $15 million from a bunch of wealthy individuals. It intends to inverse the drug development model, by in-licensing CAR-T tech from the US and making it viable in India. Indian patients could get this as early as next September, at one-tenth the price of current offerings from pharma majors.
In China, there are hundreds of clinical trials underway on CAR-T; in India, none. “Don’t tell me going from zero to being the leader isn’t a massive opportunity,” says Parmar.

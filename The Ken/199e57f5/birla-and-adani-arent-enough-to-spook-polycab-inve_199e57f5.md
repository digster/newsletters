Long and Short
Wed, 15 Oct 25
The story behind the numbers and motivations driving India's stock market.
Good Morning Ishan,
On Monday, Asian Paints’ woes piled up when the Supreme Court rejected its plea against a probe by the antitrust regulator into alleged abuse of its market dominance.
Earlier this year, Birla Opus, a feisty rival, [persuaded](https://www.reuters.com/sustainability/boards-policy-regulation/india-antitrust-body-orders-probe-into-asian-paints-after-birla-complaint-2025-07-01/) the watchdog to investigate Asian Paints’ attempts to offer dealers incentives—such as foreign travel—in return for exclusivity and for upping sales targets for those who also sell Birla Opus.
When the Aditya Birla Group’s Grasim Industries made its high-decibel entry into paints with Birla Opus in early 2024, analysts [knew better](https://the-ken.com/tradetricks/asian-paints-glorious-success-is-longer-predictable/) than to write it off.
Unlike the other new entrants, Grasim—which also owns India’s largest cement producer—seems to have gotten Asian Paints investors worried. The company’s shares slid 5% in the two days after the Grasim announcement. One analyst could barely conceal his excitement at Grasim’s attention-grabbing launch: “Grand. Audacious. Scaled-up startup.” Another analyst said Asian Paints continuing to be numero uno, while likely, is not a “shoo-in”.
Despite a number of incumbents and new entrants, the business of paints has largely been the story of just one company.
Maybe it’s time for a new protagonist.
Asian Paints is still the protagonist in decorative paints. But Grasim has been the perfect foil, [leaning](https://the-ken.com/tradetricks/pidilite-birla-and-the-fight-for-glory-in-paints/) into its white-cement dealership network and undercutting Asian Paints. It notched up a [7%](https://www.reuters.com/world/india/birlas-big-paints-bet-rattles-asian-paints-india-reign-2025-05-13/) market share within a year of its launch at Asian Paints’ expense. Asian Paints shareholders are clearly rattled. The company has lost a quarter of its market value in the past year, while the Nifty 50 index has been flat.
If the Birla Group could definitively win round 1 of its fight with a veritable colossus, it had to be taken seriously when it set its sights on any new business. Which it did when Ultratech Cement, another Birla company, announced the launch of its wires-and-cables business in February, with an investment of Rs 1,800 crore ($203 million) over two years. Weeks later, the Adani Group followed suit.
With [400 companies](https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/news/company/corporate-trends/adani-aditya-birla-groups-to-compete-in-wire-cable-industry-after-cement/articleshow/119752021.cms?from=mdr), a third of the industry being unorganised, and no company with more than a fifth of the market, wires and cables is fragmented enough to appeal to a large entrant. Naturally, shares of incumbents such as KEI Industries and Havells tumbled.
Polycab India, the market leader, plunged to its lowest in a year during that month. But this was little more than a knee-jerk response by its shareholders.
Polycab shares have since leapt 60% and reached their highest on 10 October. Last month, Polycab dethroned Havells as the most valuable company in consumer electrical goods. The rally in Polycab—whose market cap is now Rs 1.15 lakh crore—has made its chairman and managing director, Inder Jaisinghani, the 22nd richest Indian on the Bloomberg Billionaires Index.
Polycab’s revenue has tripled since its April 2019 listing, and its profit has quadrupled. And the stock is up 14X, and is among the priciest in the sector, relative to earnings. But investors are still salivating over it. Why? Chirayu Upadhyaya, the head of investor relations at Polycab, offered a clue on the company’s [analyst call](https://cms.polycab.com/media/w0rksm1p/q1fy26-earnings-call-transcript.pdf) in July.
If you consider the kind of spends that the government has been doing since December of last year, they have continuously spent almost Rs 1.26 trillion on a monthly basis on average. If you compare that with the April ’24 to November ’24 data, it was roughly only Rs 0.64 trillion per month. So, we have definitely seen a lot of pick-up in terms of investment from the government side, and that is translating into demand for cables. Of course, this effect is not immediate, it comes with a lag. Hence, this sector is continuing to see very good growth.
Looking at the annual target that the government has taken for this year and considering whatever they have spent so far, they still have to invest roughly around ₹ 1 trillion per month in terms of infrastructure growth. This will continue to translate into very good demand for cables.
What’s more, the central government’s capex in April and May was already a fifth of the budgeted outlay for FY26, the highest in seven years, Gandharv Tongia, Polycab’s chief financial officer, pointed out. Cables and wires, the latter of which is dependent on the real-estate sector, together contributed 85% to Polycab’s top line of Rs 22,400 crore in FY25.
State spends aside, property developers are bound to build more homes priced under Rs 50 lakh to meet the shortfall. There’s already a deficit of 10 million units in the top eight cities, according to real-estate consultancy Knight Frank, and the gap is set to triple by 2030.
Investors will find much to be happy about even beyond Polycab’s mainstay. Its second-largest business—fast-moving electrical goods such as fans, lights, switches, and solar inverters—turned the corner in the March quarter, becoming profitable 11 years after its launch.
Ultratech’s plans for wires and cables aren’t clear yet, and Polycab will certainly not remain unscathed. But as long as the government keeps its spending tap turned on, Polycab shareholders will see little reason to be upset.

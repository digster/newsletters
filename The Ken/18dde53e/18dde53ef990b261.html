Good Morning Ishan,

Located on the edge of Chinatown, Daeng Racha—one of the oldest restaurants in Bangkok—is known for its hoi tod (oyster omelettes). Nobody is sure when the joint served its first customer, but most estimations fall between 100 and 120 years ago.

Food prep here starts at around 3:30 a.m. Oysters are cleaned and shucked. The batter made from tapioca starch and rice flour is thinned so it’s silky smooth. And eggs are stacked up.

When it’s almost 9:00 a.m., the coals are lit, and the oysters are cooked in sizzling hot oil on a heavy cast-iron pan before a ladle of batter is poured into the mix. An egg is then cracked over the gooey pile. It’s cooked a little longer and, finally, served with plenty of green onions and sweet chilli sauce. Service ends whenever they’re out of oysters, which doesn’t take long.

But for a dish that’s seen as simple “street food,” hoi tod is difficult to get right. It’s a symphony of textures—chewiness from the oysters, fluffiness from the batter, and crispness from the egg.

Visit the other hoi tod eateries nearby, and you’ll find the same three ingredients but different philosophies behind how they come together. These are still in omelette form, yet distinct enough for Chinatown residents to have a fanatic attachment to one shop or another.

I like hoi tod because it’s a big dish of comfort served first thing in the morning. The recipe was brought from Southeast China to Siam by folks who found wealth in growing and trading rice centuries ago. These oyster omelettes look largely the same even to this day. People enjoyed them enough that at least a handful of eateries have not only survived through the generations but are also thriving.

Moral Hazard: "In economics, moral hazard occurs when someone increases their exposure to risk when insured. This can happen, for example, when a person takes more risks because someone else bears the cost of those risks."
 
Venture capital in India has a moral hazard problem, but one that is hard to accurately diagnose. One symptom of this is the rising number of partner-level exits in leading VC funds, most recently from Kalaari Capital.
 
It is widely known that many Indian startups have an addiction problem—they’re addicted to fundraising, with each successive round creating the impetus for the next. The rush of ever-rising valuations and the smell of burning venture money have replaced the boring path of building a sustainable and profitable business for many.
 
It is much lesser known that Indian venture capital also has an addiction problem; one that mirrors that of the startups they’re funding. Having worked out the impossible math of delivering real returns to their own investors and then earning their 20% “carry” fee, VC founders and partners have instead chosen the easier way out. Raising more and more money through successive funds, such that the 2% management fee they’re entitled to each year itself becomes the pot of gold much before the rainbow ends.
 
"Once a VC firm’s top priority becomes “how soon can I raise my next fund”, its organisational culture and worldview narrow down to short-term thinking. What is the incentive to slog for 10 to 15 years and take a company towards an IPO or acquisition exit when it is much easier to make more money by raising a follow-on fund?”
 
Sumanth peers back the VC curtain to reveal an entire generation of investors who have prioritised short-term, homogenous thinking optimised largely for paper exits to the alpha players in the ecosystem - Tiger Global, Softbank and Tencent.

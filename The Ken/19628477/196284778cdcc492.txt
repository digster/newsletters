Ola Electric didn’t stop digging

While the world reeled from the Trump tariffs last week, Ola Electric finally hit an important milestone on Friday.

Ola Electric Mobility on Friday said it has rolled out the first Roadster X motorcycle from its Future factory in Krishnagiri, Tamil Nadu. The electric bike is set to hit the road this month.

“The Roadster X series represents our commitment to taking the EV revolution to the next level. Today’s rollout celebrates not just a new product, but a new era for us and the industry, as it represents the next phase of revolution in electric mobility,” Ola Electric chairman and MD Bhavish Aggarwal said in a statement.

Ola Electric rolls out Roadster X bike from Tamil Nadu plant, The Hindu https://www.thehindu.com/business/Industry/ola-electric-rolls-out-roadster-x-bike-from-tamil-nadu-plant/article69438924.ece

As I’ve written previously, Ola Electric is facing a perfect storm https://the-ken.com/the-nutgraf/everything-everywhere-ola-at-once/ across all of its businesses and is fast slipping into irrelevance. It relinquished its early lead in two-wheeler electric scooters, and is being overtaken by incumbents like TVS and Bajaj. The company does not just need a new narrative—it needs new cutting-edge products. Enter the Roadster. For more than a year, Ola Electric has been teasing electric motorcycles which it hopes will turn the tide. In fact, Ola Electric has been pinning its hopes on the Roadster for so long that it made not one but two https://motoroctane.com/news/283327-after-service-is-this-the-new-ola-problem separate launch announcements several months apart. It even missed its initial launch dates, but hey, finally, here we are.

The reason why Ola Electric needed this launch was because, well, the last few months have been somewhat bizarre for the company… even by Ola Electric standards—which, as you will probably agree, has a pretty high bar for bizarreness. 

And that’s because there’s an intriguing mystery at the heart of it. It’s a question that analysts, journalists, and experts are puzzling over: 

How many vehicles does Ola Electric actually sell in a month?

Nobody knows this answer. 

I’ll explain. It’s a hell of a story. 

But first, some background. Historically, the auto industry in India has one important facet that distinguishes it from most other sectors—the sales data for almost every major player is publicly available on a website. This is enforced through the Motor Vehicle Act, which requires that automakers register vehicles within seven days of being sold. All of this is available through a central portal called VAHAN, and anyone can access it to figure out retail sales data of every automaker, every month. This is a crucial input for analysts, investors, and reporters, who typically use this data to figure out market share, trends, and make recommendations. 

So far so good. 

Well, earlier this year, while Ola Electric was at the height of fighting a two-front war—rising service complaints and declining sales, it decided that this was the best time to open a third front. 

Here’s the story from late February.

Only one out of three Ola Electric scooters sold in February were registered. The company, promoted by founder billionaire Bhavish Aggarwal, claimed to have sold 25,000 scooters in February, but only 8,390 were shown as registered on the government’s VAHAN website.

The discrepancy is due to renegotiation of contracts with Rosmerta Digital Services Pvt. Ltd. and Shimnit India Pvt. Ltd., which carry out the vehicle registrations for the Bengaluru-based maker, according to an exchange filing on Feb. 19.

“As a result of these ongoing negotiations, the registration numbers for the month of February 2025, as reflected on the VAHAN portal, will be temporarily impacted, while there will be no change in sales.”

Ola Electric Says 25,000 Scooters Sold In February But Only A Third Registered, NDTV Profit https://www.ndtvprofit.com/auto/ola-electric-says-25000-scooters-sold-in-february-but-only-a-third-registered

If you are a public company in the business of making automobiles and you’re in the middle of a bad narrative, the timing of this seems… unnecessary. Most people are already spooked about your company’s performance. Now, you’re messing around with the one reliable source they had to track how you’re doing and creating room for all kinds of conspiracy theories to take root. Essentially, Ola Electric is telling everyone, “Don’t look at the website numbers. The reality is different. Here’s how many vehicles we actually sold in February. Trust us.” 

Okayyyy. 

For Ola Electric, this is the definition of a self-goal. It’s an unnecessary distraction. If I was Bhavish Aggarwal, I’d be furious. I imagine I’d start by firing the people in the company who chose this specific time to renegotiate contracts with vendors responsible for making sure sales numbers are updated on the Vahan portal. I’d then rehire them just for the satisfaction of firing them again. 

So why do it? 

Well, according to Ola Electric, the reason is… to save money.

Ola Electric Mobility Ltd. plans to process vehicle registrations in-house to cut costs, according to people with knowledge of the matter, even as the electric two-wheeler maker grapples with slower sales.

The Bengaluru-based EV firm has set up an in-house team to process vehicle registrations at one-fifth of the cost charged by vendor Rosmerta Digital Services Pvt. Ltd., people aware of the matter said on the condition of anonymity. While Rosmerta charged Rs 1,500-1,800 to register one EV, Ola Electric believes it can do so at Rs 300 or so.

But Ola Electric is yet to settle its dues of Rs 17-18 crore with Rosmerta.

Ola Electric To Process Vehicle Registrations In-House To Cut Costs, NDTV Profit https://www.ndtvprofit.com/auto/ola-electric-vehicle-registration-cost-cut-sales-slowdown

Wait… what?

So the reason nobody knows how many scooters Ola Electric sold in February is because Bhavish Aggarwal decided to take some ridiculous actions to cut costs, but instead, he ended up making things worse. I wonder where he got the idea from https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/c23vkd57471o. Apart from the timing of this exercise, I also have to ask questions about its effectiveness. Assuming Ola Electric sells 25,000 scooters a month, and assuming its guess about cost reductions by moving the registration process in-house is correct, it saves a grand total of… Rs 3 crore a month. In the previous quarter, Ola Electric’s expenses were Rs 1,500 crore. So, essentially, someone at Ola Electric decided that all of this—the disruption in sales, the confusion, the explanations, the loss of trust—was worth the 0.6% of expenses saved as a result of it. 

I wish I could tell you that this is where Ola Electric stopped digging, but it didn’t. 

Remember Rosmerta Digital Private Ltd, the vendor who was processing registrations for Ola Electric until this point? Well, they also swung into action and dragged Ola Electric to the National Company Law Tribunal, claiming that they hadn’t been paid for services provided. In their petition, Rosmerta requested the tribunal to commence an insolvency proceeding against Ola Electric Technologies.

Ola Electric’s share price fell https://www.newindianexpress.com/business/2025/Mar/17/ola-electric-shares-skid-seven-per-cent-after-insolvency-proceedings-filed-by-rosmerta by 7% when the news broke.

In a few days, Ola Electric issued a statement that it had settled pending dues of Rs 26 crore with Rosmerta. In return, Rosmerta agreed to withdraw the petition. Everyone sighed in relief.

Again, I wish I could tell you this is when Ola Electric stopped digging. 

Remember when Ola Electric clarified that it had actually sold over 25,000 vehicles in February as opposed to the 8,000-odd registrations on the website, a mismatch it attributed to “negotiations with vendors”? Well, as it turns out, the Ministry of Road Transport and Highways was perturbed by the size of this discrepancy and asked Ola Electric to clarify the 25,000 number. So Ola Electric sent them a letter with the details. 

And well, I’ll quote the rest from Bloomberg, because they broke the story.

Ola Electric counted its yet-to-be launched electric motorcycles and e-scooter bookings in its February sales number, making its market share look larger as it struggles to win back investor confidence.

The SoftBank Group-backed firm told India’s Ministry of Road Transport and Highways in a March 21 letter that it added customer bookings for 10,866 third-generation e-scooters as well as 1,395 Roadster X motorcycles in its February sales record, citing them as “confirmed orders.”

Roadster motorcycles have not been rolled out so far, while third-gen scooter deliveries began in March. These two categories together accounted for nearly 50% of the 25,207 “confirmed orders” in February, Ola’s letter shows. The ministry had asked the company to clarify on its monthly sales number.

Ola’s February sales added e-motorbikes not even rolled out yet, Bloomberg https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-04-07/ola-s-february-sales-added-e-motorbikes-not-even-rolled-out-yet

So until now, while the rest of the industry was reporting registrations, Ola Electric decided to report “bookings”. In a statement to Bloomberg, Ola Electric clarified that “sales in February have been calculated based on the full payment received from customers”. In other words, the company claimed that the 25,000 number it reported to the ministry were people who had paid them fully for the scooters and motorcycles, and not part payments or token amount bookings.

To their credit, Ola Electric stuck to this version of the story for a full 24 hours. 

One day later, Ola Electric issued a clarification https://www.olaelectric.com/investor-relations/announcements to the stock exchanges slightly modifying what they’d said to Bloomberg the previous day. In it, the company said, “To be clear, the sales figures for February 2025 represent genuine customer demand backed by financial commitment, not token-amount bookings. Nearly 90% of these orders were paid in full at the time of placement.”

Nearly 90%.

Of course, because Ola Electric is Ola Electric, they also made it clear they were the victim here. In their statement, they say that they “have faced consistent negative commentaries from some quarters” and that they “continue to work with integrity despite facing unsavoury claims and attacks”.

Excellent. 

I’ll end with an answer to a question I asked earlier. Is there any other conceivable reason why Ola Electric decided to go through all this trouble? Could they have done it as part of a grand strategy? As Bhavish Aggarwal has said before https://in.mashable.com/tech/80928/olas-bhavish-accuses-elon-musk-of-following-his-business-ideas-internet-says-kangana-ranaut-of-entre, Elon Musk seems to copy everything that he does. And as we also know, Elon Musk is a genius. If the student can play four dimensional chess routinely, how good must the master be?

Well, as it turns out, Ola Electric was ostensibly doing all this registration in-house to launch something new yesterday.

Ola Electric has launched Hyper Delivery, an initiative that is focused on offering same-day registration and delivery of vehicles in the country. The pilot program has been commenced by the EV manufacturer in Bengaluru and will be expanded across the country in a phased manner in the quarter. It is worth mentioning that the initiative allows consumers to purchase EVs online at any Ola Electric store. Additionally, the consumers take home their fully registered vehicle within hours.

This service will be supported by the automation of multiple steps in the vehicle registration process, bringing it entirely in-house. It will also eliminate the need for middlemen and will help in streamlining the overall experience. All of this will be achieved by integrating artificial intelligence in the EV manufacturers’ operations. The brand claims that utilizing AI has significantly shortened processing times, resulting in a more streamlined and effective registration and delivery process.

Ola Electric Introduces Hyper Delivery Promising Same-Day Registration, Delivery, NDTV https://www.ndtv.com/auto/ola-electric-introduces-hyper-delivery-promising-same-day-registration-delivery-8084143

As always, quick-commerce is the tail that wags every business in India. Everyone is prepared to disrupt their entire business and dig up their entire foundation if they can proudly claim they can provide instant gratification.

And nobody is immune from it. 

Especially not Ola Electric.

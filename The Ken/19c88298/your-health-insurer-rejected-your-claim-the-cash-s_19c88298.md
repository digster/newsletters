A stitch in time saves nine, they say.
But when Soumya, a Bengaluru resident, needed nine stitches instead of six, her hospital revised the bill, and her insurer rejected the higher claim.
Like most people, Soumya had preemptively bought an insurance policy so she wouldn’t have to worry about money in an emergency. Yet when the moment arrived, money was all she could think about.
In FY25, Indian health insurance companies rejected claims worth Rs 30,000 crore—a nearly 17% jump from the previous year—according to a 2025 [report](https://irdai.gov.in/document-detail?documentId=8375620) by India’s insurance regulator.
Nearly one in eight claims were rejected. Of the 37.2 million claims filed, 4.6 million were either denied or left pending.
As a result, grievances in general and health insurance jumped 41% in FY25, with Star, Care, Niva Bupa, and Aditya Birla each recording over a 20% increase.
The Ken ran a poll in a recent [story](https://the-ken.com/story/narayana-healths-viren-shetty-on-learning-how-much-insurance-is-about-selling-and-how-little-about-health/?searchTerm=insurance), asking subscribers who they trusted more—a hospital or an insurer—to not interfere with their course of treatment. The majority of the 420 voters clicked neither or said it depended on transparency, rules, and enforcement.
Their biggest frustration? Not knowing why their claims were rejected.

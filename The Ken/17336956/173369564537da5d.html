As associations go, the association of the world’s oil producing countries, OPEC, is a formidable one. Its members control over 80% of the world’s crude oil reserves and work closely—when they’re not fighting each other—to control the supply of oil for the world’s economies, and by extension, the price of oil.
 
But the world is past peak oil. Countries around the world are in various stages of transitioning to cleaner sources of energy. The most promising of which is the sun’s bountiful energy, which shines down upon countries each day.
 
Now, imagine an association comprising the world’s sunniest countries, each eager to turn sunlight into electrical energy and use it to power their economies. Imagine a continuous cycle of energy being generated as the sun moved across these largely tropical countries. Since each country would generate too much or too little energy for its own needs, and be unable to generate energy once the sun disappeared from over it, imagine a pipe carrying solar energy. A pipe that ran across countries, continents, and even the oceans—an underwater submarine cable that connected Africa, the Middle East, India, and Southeast Asia.
 
In 2018, India conceived this plan and called it OSOWOG—One Sun, One World, One Grid.
 
This came three years after India and France unveiled the International Solar Alliance (ISA), an organisation made up mostly of tropical countries and the first treaty-led multilateral organisation to be headquartered in India.
 
Indian Prime Minister Narendra Modi is hoping for OSOWOG and the ISA to act as geopolitical counterweights to China’s $200 billion Belt and Road Initiative.
 
But, as Seetharaman’s story today lays out, sometimes dreams of piping electricity across the world and underneath oceans can seem like, well, pipe dreams. Because India has neither the capital nor the manufacturing prowess to translate these dreams into reality for the dozens of countries that are part of the ISA.
 
If India’s objective was to counterbalance China, it would need to find an alternative to the country that makes two-thirds of the world’s solar modules. India itself, which grew its solar capacity 9X in the last 5 years, imports 85% of its solar modules.

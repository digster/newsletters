Ed Set Go
Mon, 02 Jun 25
The one stop to understand the biggest shifts in education, written by the best edtech journalist in the country..
Good Morning Ishan,
The year was 2017. A government keen on international recognition hatched a complicated plan to put India’s universities on the global map. They would select the top 20 universities across the country, and fuel and fund them into becoming “Institutes of Eminence” or IoEs. Public universities would get Rs 1,000 crore each to bring up their levels; private universities would receive only the tag. The selected universities would also have more freedom to hire faculty and customise courses. With all these improvements in place, the government believed there was no reason why Indian universities couldn’t rank among the top 500 in the world.
Just shy of a decade into this endeavour, it looks like the scheme hasn’t worked. The first sign of distress is that the committee set up to select these IoEs has been defunct since 2021. Once the old committee ran its course, the government was supposed to select new members. But it never did. Maybe getting an eminent group together during the Covid years wasn’t top priority. But what about in the years that followed?
Without an expert committee to make recommendations, the process of naming IoEs has stalled. The fate of at least four private and two public universities remains undecided. But what’s worse is that names have begun to fly off the list, too.
The debate over the IoE tag has resurfaced because of a political conflict and standoff over Jadavpur University (JU) campus. The university, which was initially named in the original IoE shortlist, was later struck off. Some believe that JU lost its status because of a [heckling incident](https://www.theweek.in/wire-updates/national/2025/03/15/ces10-wb-juta-ju.html) that turned violent:
JU has been witnessing campus protests after state Education Minister Bratya Basu was heckled and his car stopped on campus during a protest. As he was leaving, the demonstrators alleged the minister’s vehicle injured two students, triggering arson and a fresh wave of vandalism by a section of protestors demanding early holding of the union elections.
The official reasoning of the University Grants Commission (UGC)—now the de facto decision-making body on IoEs—is a [mysterious Rs 3,000 crore ](https://theprint.in/india/education/why-jadavpur-still-hasnt-got-ioe-tag-anna-university-wont-get-it-its-all-about-the-money/624777/)budget proposed by the Union government for JU. Neither JU nor the state government, it seems, wanted to commit to such a large budget for improvements. The Union government, though, thought it necessary and promised to fork over Rs 1,000 crore on the condition that the state government and JU cough up the rest.
I’m sure you can guess what happened next. Without the promise of these funds, the UGC and the expert committee decided against including JU’s name in the final IoE list. Furious over being snubbed, something hardly in its control, JU is now demanding a 75% refund of the application process money it doled out.
I won’t get into Anna University’s (AU) whole tryst with IoE selection, but suffice to say that it faced similar budgetary constraints and arguments over changing its reservation policies.
This debate over who gets to be on the IoE list has carried on almost since the Union government first announced the category. Why does it matter now?
This is how I look at it: with global avenues to study abroad shrinking, or at least changing, Indian students may actually want to stay back and have the opportunity to get into world-class institutions in their home states. Indian universities, now more than ever, need the funding to amp up their research facilities, living conditions, and faculty bench strength. It’s not a new ask, just a more urgent one.
The second problem is trickier to solve because it involves politics, red tape, and UGC’s longstanding culture of dominance.
Once the expert committee [ended](https://indianexpress.com/article/opinion/editorials/express-view-institutions-of-eminence-mired-in-red-tape-8526821/) its term and no new members were announced, it set off a predictable chain of events. The old committee wrote to the government that it was time to pick new members. The education ministry was supposed to select new members, who would then be “notified” by the UGC.
It’s difficult to put a finger on where the block is, but it might be this. The expert committee was supposed to function autonomously in selecting IoEs. We can’t say for sure how autonomous it really was, because neither did prominent national universities make the cut, nor did the government ever spend the allocated Rs 1,000 crore per IoE.
Now, it has been proposed that the expert committee be done away with completely, and the authority to select IoEs should lie with the Higher Education Commission of India (HECI).
Great. But, wait. The HECI doesn’t exist yet. So who’s going to run the show?
This [paragraph](https://indianexpress.com/article/express-exclusive/why-the-delay-old-expert-panel-out-no-sign-of-new-new-education-policy-casts-a-shadow-8520616/) from The Indian Express may hold some clues:
The rationale behind creating an empowered body was that the scheme should be driven by a committee of renowned people because it was felt that the UGC had a controlling mindset and may not serve the objective of the IOE scheme which was to give these 20 institutions more autonomy. So the UGC’s role was minimised and limited to forwarding recommendations of the Empowered Expert Committee,” said another source involved in the drafting of regulations.
The ball is now shuffling between the Union government and the UGC when it comes to IoEs. Till the HECI is set up, there likely won’t be any more IoEs added to the scheme. Even if they are, there is no oversight on when they receive the funds to build out their capacity, and how these funds will be used.

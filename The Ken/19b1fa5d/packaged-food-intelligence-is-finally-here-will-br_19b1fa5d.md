Make India Competitive Again
Mon, 15 Dec 25
What India is doing, will do, and should do—to not just survive but thrive in the chaos unleashed by Trump.
Good Morning Ishan,
Since 2023, Natfirst, a product-intelligence firm, has been building up to tell consumers what’s in their packaged foods.
In July 2023, it launched its Truthin rating system (TIRS) to rate the nutritional content in packaged foods, something the government has been trying to implement for years now.
Earlier this month, the Truthin app and this very ranking system were awarded by the Indian Council of Medical Research-National Institute of Nutrition (ICMR-NIN), at a first-of-its-kind event where the government called on startups to showcase tech and ideas that would improve India’s health and nutrition.
Typically, the government has refrained from partnering with early-stage startups for policy-related tech. But now it’s calling on them, since existing infrastructure for building health and nutritional awareness isn’t cutting it.
This is the stamp of approval that Ravi Teja, co-founder of Natfirst, and his team were waiting for.
Since 2014, the Food Safety and Standards Authority of India (FSSAI) has been trying, and failing, to implement a system that issues warnings upfront about a product’s nutritional value. In 2022, they released a draft of guidelines for front-of-package nutrition labelling (FOPNL) that follows a health star rating system. Following public petitions and the Supreme Court directives earlier this year, the system should have come into effect in July 2025. But it’s already December, and nothing yet.
The Rajya Sabha committee’s report on FSSAI’s Food Safety & Standards (Labelling & Display) Amendment Regulations will be tabled in parliament later in [December](https://x.com/milinddeora/status/1999356694518988994?s=20).
The hold up so far seems to be that no ranking system is efficient enough. When brand and industry experts raised alarm bells over the proposed health stars, the regulator withdrew implementation.
Natfirst’s ranking system tried to solve for this. While health stars are based on nutrition math, calculated per 100 grams, and allow for ultra-processed food to hide behind a decent rating, the Truthin rating system (TIRS) focuses on ingredient transparency, using specific labels and warnings to highlight harmful additives and processing levels.
While regulators pit one option against another, food brands oppose the move altogether.
“The relationship of FSSAI with the food industry is long, and quite cosy,” said Arun Gupta, MD and convener of think tank Nutrition Advocacy for Public Interest (NAPi) and former member of the PM’s Council on India’s Nutrition Challenges.
But given the recent ceremony, ICMR seems increasingly interested in Truthin’s ranking system, based on intelligence from 55,000 SKUs across 100 categories. It still isn’t perfect, but it is better than FSSAI’s choice of health stars.
Gupta too was perplexed why FSSAI wanted to go for health stars, when its [own study](https://www.fssai.gov.in/upload/uploadfiles/files/IIMA_FoPL_Report_22_02_2022.pdf#:~:text=In%20this%20context%2C%20India's%20food%20regulator%2C%20Food,helping%20to%20choose%20healthier%20packaged%20food%20products) by IIM-Ahmedabad, which it spent approximately Rs 2.6 crore on, concluded that the “warning label is better than the star rating”.
The idea was to tell consumers what is in the package. The aim of labelling, Gupta said, should be to reduce the consumption of unhealthy foods. Something that has worked in countries such as Chile and Mexico, where consumption decreased by more than 20% over two years after warning labels were implemented.
But the FSSAI is trying to please everyone all at once. It especially doesn’t want to upset food brands and is taking its sweet time, even exceeding government grace periods.
In the medical journal The Lancet’s recent report, [“Ultra-processed foods: time to put health before profit,”](https://www.thelancet.com/journals/lancet/article/PIIS0140-6736(25)02322-0/fulltext) India was glaringly snubbed despite being one of the top four markets for packaged food in the world, representing over 21% of the 7 billion consumers.
The report, which points to a correlation between the uptick in consumption of ultra-processed foods and the rise in non-communicable diseases (NCD), didn’t even mention India in a footnote.
That’s because there is a grave data deficit in the Indian packaged food market. FSSAI has not even adopted a specific, legally binding definition of “ultra-processed food” yet.
So, Natfirst decided to work on an India-specific report, “The state of Indian packaged foods,” which analysed 24,557 products across 30 categories. It is currently on its way to being peer-reviewed and is due to be published this month.
The Ken got a peek at some parts of the unpublished report. It plainly points to the exact categories that ignore advised nutritional thresholds and are popular amongst consumers, but the unhealthiest in formulation.
For established brands, this public data by companies like Natfirst exposes their laziness. No brand seems to be innovating on problem ingredients, heavily relying on pre-existing common formulations. For new brands, product investigation is a goldmine of once-inaccessible and very expensive information.
“We get needed insights in a couple of days—a time-saving factor of 30X,” said Yash Parashar, CEO and founder of Mamanourish. “Developing and launching a product by even a month faster equals savings of 3–4X of the monthly burn rate, from the early revenue.”
Founded in 2023, Parashar’s traditional Indian healthy snacks brand would have interns take photos and write down ingredients to calculate nutritional profiles of competitor products. An exercise that took months and didn’t give accurate results, thanks to India’s highly fragmented and unregulated packaged foods market.
“The alternative would be to spend probably crores, an amount of money we never had as a new brand, on large market-research firms like Nielsen,” said Parashar. Now, the brand works with Natfirst.
Teja says the same info that lets consumers pick which products to not buy arms brands with the information to sell better. This could improve their existing marketing via reports from Natfirst that highlight the brand’s “missed opportunities” or present a deeper fix, like in-depth data from different SKUs in a category.
Natfirst is now helping a bunch of such brands with reformulation and identifying how to improve nutrient content without high costs.
For instance, for one brand, Teja claimed, a 0.8-gram protein increase put them in the high-protein category officially, while they were already a high-protein product, given the difference was minimal.
Better ingredients and nutrient profiles are possible in most large categories. It’s only a matter of adoption.
“Consumers are accustomed to the taste of these biscuits,” Krishnarao Buddha, then Parle exec, had told The Ken in 2022 about the Parle-G biscuit. “No matter what FOPL, most will continue to buy it.”
Back then, The Ken [had written](https://the-ken.com/story/food-labelling-decision/) about the inefficiencies of the ‘health star’ rating system that Gupta pointed to.
Buddha, had said FOPL was good for consumer awareness, but there was “no chance Parle would reformulate” its Parle-G biscuit. A cult-favorite across economic classes, its recipe is high on sugar and carbs.
Food brands at the time were cocky, and the arrogance came from their stronghold on regulatory matters. For instance, at the 2022 FOPL consultation, of the 22 stakeholders present, 16 were food and beverage companies.
But this was before the recent protein and no-sugar waves set in across economic classes. Fad or not, consumers are now more conscious, looking for healthier options in the popular biscuit, juice, and instant-food categories. Natfirst’s data from the unpublished report confirms this uptick.
Now, legacy food brands and newer health-centred brands want to work with product investigation companies like Natfirst to either tweak existing products or reformulate to earn the coveted ‘healthy’ tag.
Natfirst seems to have paved an untrodden path for others to follow. Last month, Labelblind, a Mumbai-based digital food labelling SaaS company, received [$500,000 in funding](https://hospitality.economictimes.indiatimes.com/news/operations/food-and-beverages/labelblind-secures-500000-seed-funding-to-transform-global-food-regulatory-labelling/125602073).
This shows a shift, even if minor, in the temperament towards this sector. Labelblind was, not too long ago, struggling in India despite being assigned a project to study baseline nutritional values for FSSAIs’ FOPL project.
With delayed implementation, the company that lost momentum then is now working on automating regulatory compliance for domestic and global markets.
Natfirst says this is all a win-win, but some industry experts see a problem with PI companies armed with useful data being in bed with both the regulator and brands.
Gupta, for instance, is suspicious of systems that maintain a rating as a commercial product. Unless evaluation and assessment deem them ready, such commercial systems might just serve certain brands instead of doing what they are intended to do—shape public policy and help manage crises caused by high sugar, fat, and salt levels in packaged foods.
“It is a distraction from policy making,” Gupta added. “Onus should be on the government, not the people, to find out what’s unhealthy.”
For him, these could be seen as awareness tools rather than policy drivers. Even then, he thinks, unless such information is made open-source, it might confuse people further and delay the policy implementation and the broader goal of addressing obesity and NCDs.
But now that ICMR-NIN are taking an interest in product intelligence and tech companies like Natfirst, the lines may soon blur.
Natfirst’s Teja acknowledges Gupta’s concern, “Ultraprocessed ingredients, in fact, are seeping into our food. But, we need to first measure to then be able to make informed choices,” he said.
Teja is all for going open-source eventually. If the information is out there for everyone, the conflicts of interest fade away, he believes. Brands buck up to be better because consumers are armed to buy better.

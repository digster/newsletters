Good morning, dear Ishan. We were walking back to the office this Monday afternoon after grabbing a post-lunch coffee from the neighbourhood Blue Tokai in Indiranagar. I don’t remember how, but my colleagues and I ended up discussing cities and how our imagination of them is shaped by what we read and watch.

We discussed the outsized influence of Mumbai (then Bombay) in popular culture as I was growing up, and its waning now. We discussed the rising impact of Bengaluru. And then we got to New York.

I’ve never been to New York. But one of my younger colleagues said they loved every bit of it during a visit a few years ago. “It was everything they show in the movies, and more. It was so full of life and energy!”

I was immediately reminded of the wonderful lines from Mary Schmich’s 1997 essay, “Wear Sunscreen” (yes, the same one sampled by Baz Luhrmann):

“Live in New York City once, but leave before it makes you hard. Live in Northern California once, but leave before it makes you soft.” –Mary Schmich

It’s the same with organisations, I thought. I did not say this out loud.

Though I did not express my thoughts at that moment, subscriber Hetal Sonpal expressed his thoughts on last week’s First Principles–when I talked about nature baths, Shinrin-yoku–by trying to get an AI to imagine the scene I had described.

I was fascinated by this, not because the image is accurate (it isn’t), but because something like this–a photorealistic imagination of written words–is now both possible and trivial.

As Arthur C. Clarke put it, “Any sufficiently advanced technology is indistinguishable from magic.”

1. Generational Golems

I won’t lie. I’ve been a Golem.

I don’t mean that I was once just mud and clay, brought to life through magic and sorcery. (Although if I really was, would I have recollection of that?)

Instead, I mean that I’ve been guilty of being a bad manager in the early years of The Ken. (I’d like to think I am somewhat better now.)

“The Golem effect describes the process where superiors (such as teachers or managers) anticipate low performance from a subordinate, causing the very behavior they predict. In Hasidic mythology, the Golem was a creature built from clay and mud, designed to serve its master, reports the Journal of Management Education. However, the creator of the Golem knows that given the opportunity, the creature will bring about trouble and destruction, something which eventually comes to pass.

Managers who contribute to the Golem effect believe that certain employees lack the skills, potential or willingness to succeed. This leads to a change in leadership style, where managers may:

  •   Set more explicit targets and deadlines
  •   Assign more routine tasks
  •   Monitor employees on a regular basis
  •   Emphasize operational concerns instead of strategic ones

Whether explicitly communicated or not (often these beliefs aren’t), managers make it clear to their subordinates that their trust in them is limited. When faced with this reality, employees do, in fact, become less motivated and less likely to achieve, thus completing a self-fulfilling prophecy. When managers view the employee’s inferior performance, they may double down on their overbearing management style. In this way, the cycle sustains itself.”

Business Psychology: Golem Effect vs. Pygmalion Effect –Breschia University

When you’re an early and inexperienced manager, you don’t realize how big a difference your perception of a colleague can affect their actual performance.  

Self-reinforcing loops are one of my favourite parts in systems. Each participant does something that they think makes sense, but only makes things worse. And the result they dread is the one they’re actually creating.

I’d like to present this illustrative visual, aptly titled, “Map of the Degenerating Spiral Triggered by the Boss.”

View in Browser

The visual is from a 1998 paper from INSEAD, titled “Inside the Golem Effect: How Bosses Can Kill Their Subordinate’s Motivation.” It is a wonderful read, and remains relevant word-for-word today, over 25 years later. Do please read it.

When managers or leaders make up their minds about a colleague, they end up assigning people into their “in-group” or “out-group”.

Those in the favoured in-group get much more support, time, autonomy, and space to make mistakes. Those in the out-group get a much tighter leash, with more rules, more checks, and less trust.

I am sure every single one of us has, over our careers, been part of both in-groups and out-groups. Many of us will also be honest enough to admit that we have subconsciously created and managed such groups in our workplaces.

I know I have. And if I’m really being honest, I still do to an (hopefully, limited) extent.

The really worrying part is that I feel the Golem Effect is now playing out across generations. Gen Z is losing not just interest, but belief in careers.

“According to the latest Next Gen of Work report by freelance job platform Fiverr, its survey of 12,000 workers born between 1995 and 2012 found a majority saying the model of working one’s way through the corporate ranks will soon be sleeping with the fishes alongside cradle-to-grave employment. That view explains why many cohort members are not only disinclined to comply with workplace expectations and demands, but think conforming to a mold in order to keep a long-term job has become a road to nowhere.”

Meanwhile, companies–which are mostly run by Gen X-ers and Millennials–are giving up on Gen Zs.

Both sides are losing belief in the other and, in the process, ending up becoming what the other side thinks of them.

Neither of these can be good over the longer run. The only winners will be AI companies and their investors, the ones that stand most to profit when work and careers have no meaning left.

How do we break out of this doom loop? Can we?

I’ll quote from the INSEAD paper.

“To interrupt the vicious circle, both parties have to sit down to investigate and decrease the uneasiness in their relationship. This initiative must come from the boss, since unappreciated subordinates may fear being further branded as “incapable” or “a whiner”, and reinforcing their boss’s lack of confidence in them. The aim of the intervention is not to apportion the blame for triggering or sustaining this unhealthy relationship. What needs to be examined are the behaviors which are sustaining the unhealthy relationship – what are the intentions behind these behaviors and how are these behaviors being interpreted?

[…]

Second, these managers have real faith in people’s innate desire and capability to develop and improve. This means that they do not subscribe to the dangerous notion of potential. Potential may not unlimited, but nor can anyone tell how much another person has. When managers get it into their heads that certain people have “limited potential”, they tend to behave accordingly, and will often get the response they expect.

Third, these managers see people as valuable in themselves, not just as resources. This helps them to distinguish between the person and the performance, to withhold labelling and hasty attributions, and therefore allows them to react constructively when dealing with low performance.”

Inside the Golem Effect: How Bosses Can Kill Their Subordinate’s Motivation by J.F Manzoni and J.L Barsoux 

2. Reframing identity

Hello everyone, it’s Tanim here. 

A big thank you to everyone who sent in their recommendations. This Sunday, we’re featuring what our readers have been diving into over the last few weeks. 

If you’d like to share what you’re reading and why with the community, you can tell us here. For next week, we’re featuring all things mythology, so do send us your best reads on that theme here. 

As I looked over this week’s selections, it struck me that they all boil down to one powerful idea: “reframing identity.” It’s incredibly affirming to see that people still turn to books to understand the reality around them and their place in it.  There’s no algorithm for genuine insight—and here’s where a writer’s unique perspective and our own experiences step in. 

These recommended books are a testament to that. They come packed with in-depth research, unlimited imagination, and the undeniable magic of words. And that, in my book, is a pretty fantastic formula for a fulfilling read.

Here’s what we are reading:

Dalit Kitchens of Marathwada by Shahu Patole

“Food habits of any group offer fascinating insights into group dynamics and how individuals live, think, and celebrate. This food memoir is a fascinating look at the culinary habits of two Dalit communities in Maharashtra: the Mahar and Mang communities. These are not generally known in India at all, given the history of social discrimination. In a country rampant with divisions, it’s incredibly healing to understand another person’s point of view. What better way to pick up empathy along the way than to hear it in a memoir straight from the horse’s mouth, right? This book was eye-opening for me, and an intrepid reader could pick up many new recipes and tidbits of information.” –Divya

View in Browser

Brotherless Night by V. V. Ganeshananthan

“This one is among the best I’ve read in recent years. Maybe that’s because it concerns a subject I’m deeply fascinated by: the civil war between the Sri Lankan government and the LTTE from 1983 to 2009. It is the story of Sashi, training to be a doctor in Jaffna, and how she loses her brothers over the twenty-odd years of the conflict. V. V. Ganeshananthan writes brilliantly about the rage, helplessness, mind-numbing grief, and eventual despair that comes from losing everyone and everything you once called home.” –Karthik Venkataraman

The Joy of Small Things by Hannah Jane Parkinson 

“Every essay in this book turns a banal day into an extraordinary one. Why? Because it brings you tête-à-tête with every small thing that has ever brought you joy, but you didn’t have the words to acknowledge or feel it. And now that you do, you will see those things everywhere.” –Nupur Agrawal

View in Browser

Quarterlife by Devika Rege

“This recommendation is in keeping with Tanim’s fascinating review of the political murder mystery, “The Proof of My Innocence,” which has now also made it to my wish list. “Quarterlife”, too, conjures a strong political mood and shines a light on the differing but inchoate political and cultural sensibilities of a group of young Indians (and a foreigner) desperately trying to find their own sense of identity and purpose, even at the cost of decimating bonds with those that are near and dear to them. I must also add that this doesn’t feel like a book written by a debut writer and has, incidentally, won the ‘Mathrubhumi Book of the Year’ as well as the ‘Ramnath Goenka Sahitya Samman’.” –Amar Chandramohan

View in Browser

Unreasonable Hospitality: The Remarkable Power of Giving People More Than They Expect by Will Guidara

“Will Guidara was twenty-six when he took the helm of Eleven Madison Park, a struggling two-star brasserie that had never quite lived up to its majestic room. Eleven years later, EMP was named the best restaurant in the world. This book talks about the massive jump, which Will achieved through unreasonable hospitality—principles that can easily be adopted by any business. It teaches us how to transform ordinary transactions into extraordinary experiences and urges us all to find the magic in what we do—for ourselves, the people we work with, and the people we serve.” –Janhavi Raja

Anatomy of a Breakthrough by Adam Alter

“If you’ve ever felt stalled, this book offers more than encouragement—it offers a blueprint. In Anatomy of a Breakthrough, Adam Alter argues that feeling stuck is inevitable, yes—but it’s also fixable. “The path forward begins with clarity: separate what you can control from what you can’t, and focus your energy where it truly matters.” This one sentence captures the first shift the book asks of us. Rather than battling every wind and wave, decide: what’s mine to manage? What isn’t? And where should I invest now?” –Pushkar Sohony

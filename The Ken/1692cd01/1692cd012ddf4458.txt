No. Sit down. Relax. Nothing is going to happen to your Rs 500 notes at midnight. That’s not what our story is about. 

Now don’t get me wrong. Shifting to a cashless economy isn’t necessarily a win-win outcome for everybody—just look at other countries. I remember visiting the US a few years back, where there are apps which tell you the nearest ATM that does not levy a withdrawal charge. Or China, where I found out that incentives are heavily skewed against using cash thanks to a combination of network effects, financial incentives and big state surveillance. 

Instead, our contributor Srikanth Rajagopalan is suggesting something far more ambitious—an actual, workable, proposal to make electronic payments work in India. By aligning the right set of incentives with the right set of stakeholders. To do this, he argues, India needs to do a set of things—which he freely admits, "...sounds simplistic. And maybe wiser people have perhaps thought of this before, but gave up in the face of incumbent banks blocking change. Years of accumulated systems and processes are behind this."

I could tell you what his ideas are all about. They are quite ingenious. Let me give you three hints: 

a) It does not involve UPI. 

b) He turns the fee structure upside down.

c) No QR code products are created or harmed. 

Have you figured it out? No? You should read his story then. It’s free. So read, share—and tell your friends in banking about us. Then ask them about demonetisation. Tell us what they told you.

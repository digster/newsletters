Good Morning Ishan,
Bengaluru’s ongoing water crisis must count among the most dissected infrastructure problems in the Indian media. Mega troubles hitting megacities make mega news. Understandable. Hopefully, sustainable solutions will emerge from all the finger-pointing. After all, a crisis must never be allowed to go to waste.
Ironically, what is being wasted a lot now in Bengaluru and across India is water. Blame this, in no small measure, on the widespread use of reverse osmosis (RO) water purifiers at homes. An average RO purifier wastes about three litres of water for every litre of purified water. Besides, it often strips the water of essential minerals. No wonder many experts are looking askance at the technology.
So, when Urban Company—India’s leading home-services marketplace with 5.3 million users—recently entered the water-purifiers market, it was quite perplexing that it chose RO. Not only that, this new kid on the block is pricing its products at a tidy premium to those of market leaders Eureka Forbes and Kent. Sure, it’s a big opportunity: India’s water-purifier market, pegged at $1 billion in 2022, is expected to quadruple in five years.
Even so, why is Urban Company going the RO route? Does it have a secret service or two up its sleeve? Is the move sustainable? What if the tide turns? Shivani and Shaswata have the answers in [today’s informative story](https://the-ken.com/story/urban-company-wants-to-ignite-the-ro-water-purifier-market-when-ro-itself-is-under-fire/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter).
Yesterday was the last day of voting by ICICI Securities’ minority shareholders on a contentious proposal—to delist the brokerage through a share swap arrangement with parent ICICI Bank. Last August, The Ken [wrote](https://the-ken.com/story/icici-securities-has-165923-shareholders-its-delisting-benefits-one/?utm_source=web&utm_medium=socials&utm_campaign=social) about how just one shareholder—ICICI Bank—had stacked the deck against nearly 166,000 minority shareholders of ICICI Securities by choosing this unusual route to take the brokerage private. Many shareholders of the brokerage were irked by the timing, valuation, and share-swap route that does not allow proper price discovery.
The proposal will go through only if at least two-thirds of the minority shareholders of ICICI Securities who cast their votes approve the deal. And ICICI Bank, it appears, has gone on overdrive. There have been complaints on X from many ICICI Securities retail shareholders about ICICI Bank employees calling and pressuring them to vote in favour of the delisting. Some say this reeks of desperation, and some wonder if such tactics are even legal.
Meanwhile, Quantum Mutual Fund is said to have voted against the deal, while Norges, Norway’s sovereign wealth fund, is said to have voted in favour. The final results will likely be known today. How LIC, Fidelity, and retail shareholders have voted will be crucial in deciding the proposal’s fate.
“If ICICI Bank rams through this deal, it will be like a permanent ink stain on a white shirt,” a minority shareholder had earlier told The Ken. The bank doesn’t seem to mind.

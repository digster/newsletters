Good Morning Ishan,

I'm very open to flights of fancy.

About two years ago, I was buying cigarettes from my local panwadi (Delhi lingo for paan/cigarette seller), and his usual black/menthol/citrus rack suddenly sported these cigarette-like plastic things. "Curiouser and curiouser," I thought. Curious, I bought.

That was my only e-cigarette, and honestly, it tasted funny. I didn't like it very much.

Two years later, Ashish, equally curious from what I could tell, wrote a whole email about vaping. And I found myself thinking, why did I dislike it so much? Surely, a smoker would be more than okay vaping? 

Yesterday, I took another drag from an e-cigarette. Turns out my old one had some horrible flavour, and the nicotine hit my throat hard.

Which brings me to Ruhi's story today. Vaping is suddenly catching on as more and more smokers opt for what they think is the healthier alternative to cigarettes. They see vaping as an easy way out. Except, that may be far from the truth. Vapes can contain nicotine levels of up to 32 mg, as opposed to a piece of Nicotex gum (2-4 mg), or even a cigarette—"10 mg of nicotine, but only about 1 mg of it is absorbed."

"In developed countries, vaping has been brought under legal scrutiny, with set standards and approvals in place. India is just getting started. Indian state governments that have banned vaping and vapers associations have taken the matter to court. Vapers are pleading with the governments to do what the developed countries did—help smokers quit. But India has a counter-argument: we are not them, and do smokers really quit?" We have a committee in place that's calling for a blanket ban. 

Some state governments have led the way. Punjab, Karnataka, Jammu and Kashmir, Bihar and Mizoram have all banned vaping over the years. The rest of India may follow suit. Vapers' associations are hoping otherwise. But in all of this, a basic question stands out. Would a ban really make a difference?

Read Ruhi's story to see how India deals with the ban of cigarette alternatives. And how it doesn't. Oh, and did I mention it's this week's free piece?

Is Zudio walking into the age-old retail trap? Few retailers can resist the temptation to make their stores roomier, especially when things are going swimmingly 
 By Seetharaman G https://the-ken.com/writers/seetharamang/

Just four years ago, Zudio, Trent’s wildly successful value-clothing chain, was one-third of Westside—the company’s flagship brand—in terms of average store size. 

Now, Zudio is almost half as big. 

The average Zudio outlet is 9,800 sq ft. Westside stores measure over 21,400 sq ft. 

Should this be a problem? Zudio is the reason Trent, once a lightweight in the Tata Group, is now its fourth-most-valuable company. In just three years, Zudio’s footprint has quadrupled to 635 outlets in 190 cities. 

Trent was able to take Zudio far and wide because it sold everything under Rs 1,000, and also because the stores were compact. Delhi, for instance, has 21 Zudio stores and a mere eight Westside outlets. 

But few retailers can resist the temptation to make their stores roomier, especially when things are going swimmingly.

Although Inditex, which has operations in more than 90 countries, has long sought eye-catching stores in landmark buildings, the shift to even bigger spaces serves two new purposes. 

It enables the company to accommodate a proliferation of new product lines under the Zara brand, which now encompasses cosmetics, footwear and sports clothes as well as the homeware of Zara Home. 

It also allows space for the growing role stores play in online sales, either as mini-warehouses from which packages are shipped out or as sites where consumers can pick up orders and return unwanted goods.

Zara owner bets on big stores to keep sales space growing, Financial Times https://www.ft.com/content/5364bea4-4f97-49ba-88fb-845d3b648b96

If you have hordes of shoppers walking into your store, why not give them more space to check out a wider range of products? Not only does this make them buy more, it also draws in a new group of customers. 

That’s the simple logic behind the temptation to make stores bigger. It’s no different for Zudio, as my colleagues Sudeshna and Seema’s story https://the-ken.com/story/tata-zudio-makes-a-no-brainer-entry-into-beauty-products-heres-why-anyone-can/ from last October illustrated. 

Zudio Beauty quietly made its debut last week, its racks brimming with deodorants, makeup-remover wipes, and sheet masks–displaying low but fixed prices.

It’s no surprise that Trent is now looking to replicate Zudio’s fast-fashion success in the value-beauty space—by embracing the same trifecta: no advertising, no discounts, and 100% in-house brands.

But this strategy can even trip up retailers who have seen far worse. British grocery chain Tesco learnt its lesson a decade ago.

After years of growth in the UK, Tesco suffered five profit warnings last year as analysts said it had opened too many large stores, let prices rise and shop floor standards slip. All supermarkets are under pressure as shoppers switch to the internet, small local shops and discounters such as Aldi and Lidl, but Tesco has been hardest hit as a result of its scale.

Tesco reports record £6.4bn loss, The Guardian https://www.theguardian.com/business/2015/apr/22/tesco-suffers-record-64bn-loss

The average Tesco store has since shrunk in size by 10%. 

Closer to home, I have written about how Dmart, India’s second-largest supermarket chain, supersized https://the-ken.com/tradetricks/dmart-and-the-supersizing-imperative/ its stores in the hope of selling its customers more clothes, shoes, and bed linen, which are more profitable than grocery. But Dmart didn’t anticipate the rise of chains such as Zudio, which took away https://the-ken.com/tradetricks/zudio-wanted-dmarts-apparel-shoppers-dmart-is-now-hurting/ its fashion shoppers. And investors duly punished https://the-ken.com/tradetricks/trade-tricks-dmart-is-not-used-to-being-in-a-funk-for-so-long/ the company. 

There are no signs yet that more capacious Zudio stores are hurting the chain. But what is indisputable is that Zudio’s rising contribution to Trent’s revenue has pared the company’s profit margins. This is fine as long as Zudio keeps growing and Trent’s absolute profits are ample. 

But a saturated market awaits Zudio, if not immediately. And at that point, I don’t think Trent would want to be saddled with sizeable Zudio stores flogging low-margin wares that aren’t exactly selling in droves.

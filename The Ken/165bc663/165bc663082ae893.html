Good Morning Ishan,
 
Does this happen to you? Like, you are on to a question, and you’ve been thinking about it, turning arguments over and around in your head, reading bits and pieces, but you still can’t say for sure, if you’ve really thought it through. 
 
I’ve been on to something like this. For days. Ever since my colleague Roshni pitched her story. And the question is: 
 
Is there any right way to do good in today’s world? 
 
Like, once you do something which makes you feel good, is it okay to feel good about yourself? Or is that selfish? Is it okay to talk about it to others? I don’t know, maybe tell friends & family as an inspiring story or small talk, or something that just makes you feel better about yourself. Perhaps, even superior to those around you. Something inherent, like I’m better than those around me. Is that okay? Is it okay to talk about it on social media? Putting updates of you doing good, one after another. On Instagram. And Twitter. And Facebook. Maybe hoping that it will generate momentum and inspire thousands of others. Maybe hoping that if nothing else, people will at least notice and it will make you feel good. Or let’s say when you are giving money for a cause, how much should it be? Should it be commensurate with your net worth? Maybe. Maybe not. But why? Who defined these commensurate terms? Is there a law on it? Should one be hauled over the burning coals of societal morality and be punished with indignation and charges of selfishness? Is it okay to do good for things that you care about, or is that again being selfish? 
 
Ah. I could go on and on and on. Told you, these thoughts have been churning…
 
Now, the good thing is that Roshni has made much more progress than I have. In today’s story, she looks at our society through the prism of social media. Where even as the floods in Kerala were ravaging the state, there was massive mobilisation on Twitter, the microblogging platform. In the first three weeks of August, 2.62 million tweets were shared under #Keralafloods, as the state and ordinary citizens campaigned to provide aid. But there was an ugly side to it. A phenomenon that's best-called donation shaming.
 
Roshni says that the politics of giving, especially when it comes to public proclamations of philanthropy, can be cruel. And this creeps up, time and again, raising several questions about intent and prejudices. Or as this one line in the copy says, publicising your donation is like retweeting praise.  
 
You can read the piece here: https://the-ken.com/story/donation-shaming-kerala-floods/
 
The piece is clear and precise in capturing how we are progressing as a society. It is also free to read.
 
Enjoy your Sunday. I hope to get more thinking done.

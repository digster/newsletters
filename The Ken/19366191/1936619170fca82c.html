Amazon is not yet in quick commerce. But it’s already different from the pack. It has something the incumbents don’t, thanks to a 2018 acquisition
By Seetharaman G
5 min read
View in browser

Good Morning Ishan,

You are reading our subscriber-only newsletter Trade Tricks—a weekly commentary on Indian retail and commerce

About time!

That’s the only plausible response to the news yesterday that Amazon is finally joining the free-for-all that is quick commerce. The e-tailing colossus is likely to launch its instant-delivery service in a month or two, The Economic Times reported. 

Amazon has named the app Tez (Hindi for “quick) for now. I guess Google’s graveyard is as good a place as any to shop around for placeholder names.

On a serious note, Zomato’s Blinkit, Zepto, and Swiggy Instamart have a very tight hold on the rapid-delivery market. And Flipkart and Bigbasket are trying really hard to nudge the door open for themselves. Now picture Amazon making its way in this messy scenario. 

Even if we know nothing about what exactly Amazon has in the offing, what is evident is that Amazon is already unlike its five rivals. 

Because it’s the only one that runs supermarkets.

In 2018, Amazon teamed up with private-equity firm Samara Capital to buy More, a struggling supermarket chain, from the Aditya Birla Group. After toying with stores of all sizes and losing hundreds of crores, More decided its sweet spot was 2,000–3,000 sq ft.

That turned out to be the fix More was desperately in search of, as chief executive Vinod Nambiar explained to me in an interview in September:

The focus was on: how do you assort in a small store? Today, I can put 5,000–5,500 SKUs [stock-keeping units] in a 2,500 sq ft store, which is what Dmart puts in an 8,000 sq ft ground floor. This is about technology, planogramming, inventory management, and replenishment cycles. 

Suppose I get a shipper box of deodorant, which is 60 units, at my distribution centre (DC), I’ll only send two to a store. So, we are carrying very tight inventory. When we bought More, we had 43 days of inventory. Now it’s 30.

The decision to go small brought More within inches of profitability in the September quarter. 

But More matters more to Amazon’s core business. Over one-third of the supermarket chain’s 780 stores double up as fulfilment centres for Amazon’s e-grocery business. Amazon has shut down warehouses in places with a dense network of More stores. 

The idea of using a grocery store to deliver online orders has been fraught with pitfalls. Grofers, the predecessor to Blinkit, and Reliance-backed Dunzo both gave it a shot. 

And failed spectacularly. 

The biggest problem was that the supermarkets they relied on were run by someone else, and these companies could only do so much to persuade those shopkeepers to use technology to keep track of their inventory. 

Chaos ensued. 

Shoppers would place an order thinking a certain product was available at a particular store, only for the Grofers executive to discover on reaching there that the item had sold out.

Plus, these outlets did just fine without the business these e-tailers brought them. So they couldn’t care less.

More is different. Amazon owns 49% of it, and likely bought it with the intention of furthering its online business. 

Had More not decided to become a neighbourhood-supermarket chain, there would have been no question of Amazon using it for its quick-commerce venture. It’s their compactness that allows More to open dozens of stores in a city, and these outlets are as big as a typical warehouse used by Blinkit or Instamart. More has over 75 supermarkets in Bengaluru, Swiggy and Flipkart’s home turf. 

On paper, this would allow Amazon to deliver within 10 minutes in a number of neighbourhoods. (The fastest Amazon can deliver groceries right now is two hours.) But there’s immense complexity in operating a store that’s equally efficient as a supermarket and as a warehouse, as Nambiar told me:

If you give me an order and I only have 10 minutes to pick it, I need a dedicated picker waiting for your order. A picker can typically do 40–50 orders a day. These are dedicated people because it’s a skill. They are different from store assistants; it’s not a customer-facing job. Think of this like a factory person versus salesperson.

In a lit store, some things are critical. When pickers [who help with online orders] and shoppers become equal, you screw the experience of the shoppers… But remember, these shoppers come in blocks. And online orders come before the shoppers. If an online order is to be delivered from 6 a.m. to 8 a.m., we pick it at four in the morning. So, no interference with shoppers. But at a small store, when online orders cross 60–100 a day, we have to move some orders to another store.

Amazon’s track record in delivering groceries is nowhere near as disastrous as Flipkart. But it is strikingly unremarkable. 

Until three years ago, it had two online-grocery services: one for large FMCG packs with next-day delivery (Amazon Pantry) and another for smaller packs and perishables with two-hour delivery (Amazon Fresh). Then it combined the two in late 2021. 

But before it could take advantage of the move, the swift rise of quick commerce threw Amazon for a loop. 

It’s now in recovery mode. Of course, Amazon can’t entirely rely on More, which doesn’t have a single store in Mumbai or Pune. But it may not be pulled into the quick-commerce land grab quite as quickly as any other new entrant.

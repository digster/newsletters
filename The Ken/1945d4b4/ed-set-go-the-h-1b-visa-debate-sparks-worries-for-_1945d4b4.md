Mon, 13 Jan 25
The one stop to understand the biggest shifts in education, written by the best edtech journalist in the country..
Good Morning Ishan,
Happy Monday! It’s T minus 7 days until the Donald Trump inauguration here in Washington DC, and sparks are already flying between the more MAGA elements of the coalition and his new favorite, Elon Musk. The anti-immigration sentiment exploited by Trump’s presidential campaign is revving up again, and Indian tech workers employed by American tech giants are now right in the eye of the storm.
Musk, a vocal opponent of “illegal immigration”, has thrown his weight behind the visa category most used by Indian software engineers to seek specialised job opportunities in the US. Musk, like other big tech entrepreneurs, doesn’t want to stem the flow of skilled workers to the country. The MAGA crowd, for their part, want to massively reduce all kinds of immigration to preserve American jobs for Americans.
Stuck in between are Indian students and professionals who’ve spent hundreds of thousands of dollars to study in the US, with the hope that getting an H-1B visa would help them make back some of that investment, repay their loans, and eventually get a green card. They’re worried that all this rhetoric around the “[outsider](https://www.nbcnews.com/news/asian-america/h1b-visas-debate-immigration-ramaswamy-musk-trump-rcna186173)” is going to upend their plans.
Missouri resident Madhav Rao Pasumarti holds an H-1B visa working in IT managing product processes. Over the last couple weeks, he’s watched as he and others who share his visa status have been called “invaders” and “cheap labor.”
He said it’s hurtful and baffling, and he pointed out that visa holders like him are people who want to contribute and have much at stake.
Now, there are several legislative reasons why capping H-1B visas isn’t immediately possible, even if the new administration decided it wanted to place such curbs. But experts warn that opponents could slow down the visa process to a point where it becomes too frustrating for anyone to apply for it.
Even before the latest H1-B storm clouds gathered, Indians living in the US have been warning others to think deeply about their decision to leave paying jobs in India to move to America. I spoke to one such product manager, Sudeepa Kolli, back in February 2024, when her X account was blowing up with retweets and mentions. Kolli had become somewhat of an influencer who was trying to put the spotlight on how difficult and competitive the tech job market is, and how coming to the US without a solid plan to land a top-tier job would be [disastrous](https://the-ken.com/edsetgo/move-over-finfluencers-the-study-aboard-gurus-are-here/).
Kolli’s page is a record of what’s happened to tech hiring in the US: companies that over-hired in the post-pandemic boom are now trimming their employee numbers. Kolli talks about friends and colleagues still struggling to land jobs, doing part-time contract work, or paying agents to get them onto payrolls that keeps them eligible for a visa.
“I’ve heard so many stories from my friends. The time between classes that’s meant for projects and networking, things that can help you land jobs… students work shifts to pay their expenses,” says Kolli.
It’s an uncertain time for immigrant students and workers all over the world. What’s certain, though, is that dependable routes of migration to countries like Canada and the US aren’t as open as they used to be.
In response to the challenge, Indian study-abroad businesses are trying to wedge open other opportunities. Take Upgrad co-founder Mayank Kumar’s [new joint venture](https://www.business-standard.com/companies/people/upgrad-s-mayank-kumar-oyo-s-ayush-mathur-unveil-new-venture-borderplus-125010601199_1.html) with former OYO executive Ayush Mathur. Called Borderplus, it wants to create a pipeline of skilled, blue-collar workers to countries such as Germany, South Korea, and other developed nations. The duo has identified gaps in the skilled labour requirement in such countries and aim to create a talent pipeline of sorts from India. They’re starting with healthcare workers.
The programme intends to train interested workers both online and through offline centers, work on their language skills, and connect them to “global opportunities”. Borderplus already has its first cohort in place, which will be trained over six to nine months. It’s unclear, though, how Borderplus could continue their support once the candidate lands a job in another country.
There’s going to be an inevitable tussle with visas, changing immigration policies, and rising resentment against outsiders. Blue-collar skilled workers are also less likely to have the social and economic safety nets that at least some Indian tech workers have back home. Borderplus’ success will come from ensuring the longevity of such work-abroad options for its graduates. Racking up admission numbers or opening up training centers is crucial, but it’s only the first step.

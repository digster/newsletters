Good Morning Ishan,
Not too long ago, middle-class families in India probably had men and women using the same shampoo. At least I grew up seeing family packs serve all members.
And then brands started telling men that a woman’s shampoo was not for them. Soon separate skincare products followed. And now, even compacts and concealers—products that were once restricted to stage artists.
“Every man wants to look like Shah Rukh Khan, but they don’t want to be seen wearing makeup,” says Rahul Shah, the founder of Yaan Man, which sells “makeup” for men.
If one in five beauty products in the last few years were targeted at men—Indian men’s grooming market generated $13 billion in 2022—it makes for an interesting story on what goes in those products.
Maybe “Men are from Mars, Women are from Venus”, but their beauty products come from the same chemicals. Take HUL-owned Pond’s, for instance. The brand’s face wash for men says “energy bright” on its packaging but promotes niacinamide, a form of vitamin B3 known to make the skin supple, in the same product for women.
Incidentally, HUL acquired Minimalist for Rs 3,000 crore last week, a brand that broke the men-women segregation mould. Nuha wrote the definitive [story](https://the-ken.com/story/four-year-old-minimalist-does-for-skincare-what-loreal-couldnt/) on Minimalist last year, and today she brings us a fresh perspective on why it’s easier to sell to men now.

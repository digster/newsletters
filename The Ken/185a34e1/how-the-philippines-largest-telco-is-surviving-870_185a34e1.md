Everyone has a nest egg. Maybe it’s in a biscuit tin, stashed under your bed. Maybe it’s in a Web3 wallet, tracked on a blockchain. For most of us, it’s somewhere in between—a mixture of conventional investments and bank accounts, meshing high-risk ventures with funds that yield slow but dependable growth.
But what happens when the most conservative part of your portfolio takes a dive? What if 20% evaporates in mere hours?
That’s what happened to folks across the Philippines (and some in other parts of the world) in mid-December, when the telecommunications company PLDT had US$1.12 billion of its market value wiped away in one trading day.
Termed the “bluest of the blue” chips, because the near-century-old company has been listed on the Philippine Stock Exchange for eons, PLDT isn’t supposed to behave like this. Yet a budget overrun to the tune of US$870 million that went undetected for four years sank its share price.
Because of PLDT’s status, this situation can almost be seen as a breach of unspoken trust. You may expect the company to have a hole in its balance sheet, but a change in public policy regarding telecommunications towers encouraged the company to sell more than enough infrastructure to keep its financials intact.
Maybe that’s a coincidence. Maybe it’s dumb luck. But there are plenty of shaken investors who aren’t as confident in the company’s governance as before.
Stock prices aside, the budget overrun traces back to 2019, and its auditors didn’t disclose these issues in the past two years. For the folks whose portfolios were nuked, there are questions that need answering, and expected tower sales that will yield revenue don’t negate the fact that something glitched within PLDT.

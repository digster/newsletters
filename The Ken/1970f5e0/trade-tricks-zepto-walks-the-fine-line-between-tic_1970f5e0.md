Trade Tricks
Tue, 27 May 25
A weekly newsletter about the biggest changes in commerce—focusing on shifts that matter to you..
Good Morning Ishan,
I was ordering groceries on Zepto a few days ago when I noticed a new nudge suggesting that I replace one of the items in my cart with a cheaper alternative. Apparently, this is the new swap-and-save feature that Zepto rolled out on 15 May.
Why?
Well, if you go by a [tweet](https://x.com/Devendra_Meel/status/1922901983909830970) from the company’s chief business officer Devendra Meel, it is intended “to pass on value to customers in terms of savings at the last leg of the purchase journey”. Meel’s tweet was in reply to another post analysing the possible reasons for Zepto’s new rollout, and one which made quite a few good points—like helping the company move expiring inventory soon or push higher-margin options.
But there’s one possibility that is particularly interesting to me: building customer trust.
I think many people would agree (me included) that Zepto has a bigger image problem than the other quick-commerce majors in the Indian market. Just try googling the name of any quick-commerce app followed by “dark pattern”, which is a term for deceptive practices that subtly manipulate users into doing something they never intended to do, like spending more money on an order.
It’s not like Instamart and Blinkit are saints; in fact, Zepto founder Aadit Palicha even accused a rival company’s CFO of orchestrating [a smear campaign](https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/tech/startups/aadit-palicha-ceo-ipo-bound-zepto-alleges-smear-campaign-by-rival-cfo-as-quick-commerce-race-heats-up/articleshow/121397552.cms?from=mdr) against his platform recently. But some time spent trawling internet consumer groups and my own experience with these apps show that things are just a bit worse when it comes to Zepto.
For instance, on Zepto’s checkout screen, the app shows you a list of the items you have in your cart along with their prices, but when you scroll down, you only see the final amount you need to pay. It takes an extra click to see your bill broken down, which will then show you that the company charges a handling fee and delivery fee on top of the item total, plus other charges for late-night deliveries or surge pricing when applicable.
The kicker? The line item labelled “item total” doesn’t match the total value of your cart, and it takes one more click to see that this is because of the inclusion of GST, not just on the items you’re purchasing but also on the extra charges.
There’s also the extra friction when it comes to using features like “free delivery” and “free cash”. The company’s Zepto Pass subscription promises free deliveries, but careless users may not realise that this perk isn’t applied automatically. It has to be manually activated. Every single time you place an order. (This subscription was tweaked and [rebranded](https://yourstory.com/2025/04/zepto-retire-zepto-pass-switch-zepto-daily-quick-commerce) as Zepto Daily last month).

The platform also incessantly sends you notifications about crediting Zepto Cash to your account. But users [on social media claim](https://x.com/Rahul_alt/status/1922222878008787238) that the actual discount they get is lower than the amount the platform says you can use. I wouldn’t know—every time I’ve tried to spend my Zepto Cash, the platform refuses to let me because of “high demand”.
| Source: Twitter |
In fact, Zepto can be so vexing to users that there’s an honest-to-god community of [4,000-plus people on Reddit](https://www.reddit.com/r/FuckZepto/) dedicated to calling out “anything unethical related to Zepto”. No such equivalent exists for Blinkit or Instamart. (There’s one for Swiggy, but that has only amassed a few dozen members and two posts since it was created a few months ago.)
So if you’re Zepto and a lot of your customers think you’re trying to pull a fast one on them, perhaps swap-and-save isn’t a bad idea to win back some credibility. And as mentioned earlier, it might help you out on the business side too.
There’s just one hitch. When you tell customers to buy Brand X instead of Brand Y, you risk alienating the latter.
And its new feature particularly hurts brands that sell premium products, as [pointed out](https://x.com/Rishhari/status/1922904735142814045) by Rishabh Harish, founder of eco-fashion brand Wellbi. It also opens the door for Zepto to possibly squeeze them even further by charging them to advertise their products as a suggested swap.
Sure, quick commerce is a rapidly-growing distribution channel for brands, but the margins it offers still aren’t that good. With Blinkit’s parent company Eternal and Instamart’s Swiggy both listed and Zepto on the road to its own IPO, the pressure on platforms to show profits is only mounting. That means aggressively [monetising businesses](https://the-ken.com/story/google-facebook-and-blinkit-the-new-battle-for-indias-ad-money/) as well as consumers, without antagonising either of them.
Here’s another piece of news from 16 May, just a day after Zepto rolled out its swap-and-save feature: the company is offering an analytics subscription named Atom for brands, [reportedly](https://yourstory.com/2025/05/quick-commerce-brand-zepto-monthly-subscription-analytics-tool-atom-aadit-palicha) priced at Rs 30,000 a month, which shows data on how their categories are performing on Zepto.
Clearly, Zepto wants to be in the good books of both its brands and customers.
But it’s a tightrope walk more often than not.

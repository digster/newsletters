Good Morning Ishan,
A wise woman I know advocates JOMO, or joy of missing out, as a far superior alternative to its much more famous sibling FOMO (fear of missing out). Her logic? FOMO often results in rushed, wannabe, sub-optimal choices while JOMO lets you look beyond the obvious, cut regret, and make the best of the possibilities ahead.
The powers-that-be driving India’s Rs 10,370 crore ($1.2 billion) AI Mission should pay heed. As Seema analyses incisively in today’s story, Indian taxpayer money is now being spent on fighting FOMO first instead of building a competitive advantage and solving large problems using AI. The latter two—crucial needs of the hour—might end up being surprise by-products, at best, or casualties, at worst.
It’s no secret that India has been a late starter in the now-ubiquitous, unstoppable, and inescapable AI race. But the country’s frenzied bid to make up for lost time, especially after the Chinese Deepseek made big waves in January, is resulting in an incoherent do-something-do-anything approach that could well turn counterproductive.
Is there a better way? Does India have what it takes to make a mark in AI and make AI count? Yes, with some caveats. The mission needs vision, and quick.
[Seema’s reporting](https://the-ken.com/story/your-guide-to-indias-rs-10000-cr-ai-mission-thats-running-on-fomo/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter) lays out the what and how. Let’s dive in.
Haldiram’s reportedly sold a 10% stake to Temasek at a $10 billion valuation, setting the stage for a bigger battle with its smaller but fast-growing rival, Bikaji Foods. While Bikaji has thrived by expanding nationally and bypassing wholesalers, Haldiram’s newly merged entity could upend the competition with a stronger supply chain and aggressive expansion. Meanwhile, Bikaji’s attempt to break into the western snacks market has stumbled, just as investor enthusiasm for its stock has started to wane.

Happy New Year!
Like you, I’m quite happy to have 2020 behind me. I can’t wait to see what unfolds in 2021, and for things to go back to some semblance of normalcy.
On this Sunday, instead of a regular recap email, I’d like to go back and talk about 2020 one final time, and the most important lesson it taught us—all thanks to our subscribers. And what this means for you—a reader of The Ken in 2021.
When 2020 started, and the Covid-19 pandemic pushed all of us indoors, we asked subscribers what they expect from The Ken. Broadly, the feedback fell into three categories:
“Things are moving too quickly. Can you keep me up to date so I don’t fall behind?”
“There’s too much information out there. Can you tell me what I should listen to and what I should ignore?”
“I read the news. I am just not sure what any of it means in the long-term”
2020 was a year that was fast-paced, eventful, noisy and unclear—unlike any other year before. That’s why we invested in creating another set of products to keep our subscribers informed.
Our newsletters.
We’d already created The Nutgraf the previous year—a weekly newsletter that connected the dots about a significant event, delivered in a memorable style. To it, we added two other newsletters. We launched Beyond The First Order as a daily newsletter analysing the long-term effects of the pandemic. Then there was Strait Up, our weekly newsletter from The Ken Southeast Asia that cut through all the noise to tell you what mattered in the region.
We used these three newsletters to tell the story of 2020. Thousands of our subscribers experienced 2020 solely through them. Hundreds wrote to us telling us how they felt more informed, intelligent and even calmer—all while the news cycle jumped from one pointless outrage to the next.
Let me show you.
Back in March, when India went into lockdown, we immediately analysed two things. The fiscal
[cost](https://the-ken.com/bfo/12/) of the lockdown caused by shutting down both demand and supply, and the
[impact](https://the-ken.com/the-nutgraf/four-horsemen/) on the four big supply chains—people, food, healthcare and money, all of which turned out to be the big problems that India struggled with in the next
[several](https://the-ken.com/bfo/115/) months. To explain the fiscal cost, we used the example of how mothers “freeze” long-running Monopoly games in place after telling players to call it a night.
Then in April and May, as things became more clear, governments started creating digital tools to monitor its citizens, under the guise of contact tracing (Remember Aarogya Setu?). We wrote about the structural impact of this on
[surveillance](https://the-ken.com/the-nutgraf/panopticon/)—told through a fascinating architectural concept by Foucault, the Panopticon.
Then came stimulus measures by various countries. We wrote
[a](https://the-ken.com/bfo/14/) lot
[about](https://the-ken.com/bfo/36/) it. We talked about which ones would work, and which ones wouldn’t. When India announced a stimulus, newspapers reported it with much fanfare.

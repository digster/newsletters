India’s only listed gaming company—facing boss battles despite big wins in esports—is at a crucial checkpoint

Delivered to Ishan, Subscriber to The Ken.

Fri, 05 Apr 2024

VIEW IN BROWSER
The Spotlight by Arundhati Ramanathan
Deputy Editor

Good Morning Ishan,

India’s first publicly listed gaming company, Nazara, with a Rs 5,000-crore (US$611 million) market cap, is now at a place far from the excitement that surrounded its US$80 million initial public offering (IPO) in 2021.

The company is now looking less like a gaming one, and more like an esports company. The growth of its esports vertical is, for the first time, outpacing its “core gaming business”.

In fact, esports accounted for over half of the company’s Rs 914 crore (US$109 million) revenue during the first nine months of the last fiscal. Gaming pulled in just over a third.

And despite its gaming arm wilting in a post-pandemic slump—troubled by mounting customer acquisition costs (CAC) and economic headwinds—Nazara plans to double down on this segment.

To do this, it even raised Rs 750 crore (US$90 million) from investors like Nikhil Kamath (co-founder of discount broker Zerodha*) and two of India’s biggest asset-management companies, SBI Mutual Fund and ICICI Prudential Mutual Fund.

Gaurav looks at what it will take to get Nazara out of this slump.

Tesla’s route to India is via Berlin

After years of a will-it-wont-it flip-flop, Tesla made its intentions clear for India.

The world’s largest EV maker is set to send a scout team to India at the end of April to lock down manufacturing sites in either Maharashtra, Gujarat, or Tamil Nadu as part of its plans to set up a $2–3 billion electric-car plant, reported Financial Times.

But before you get your made-in-India Tesla, you may get your made-for-India Tesla. And the route for that is via Berlin.

Yesterday, the Hindustan Times reported that Tesla has now started the process to make right-hand-drive cars for the Indian market in its Berlin factory. The report adds that Tesla hopes to have them on Indian roads later this year.

What will truly complete the loop is when Indian-made Tesla cars will be exported to other regions.

There is one line that everyone here at The Ken is tired of writing in their stories:
"Disclosure: Vijay Shekhar Sharma, the founder of Paytm, is an investor in The Ken"
Vijay's presence on our cap table requires us to add this disclosure to every story where Paytm is mentioned. You would think that we would mention Paytm in only a small sliver of our stories, but the fact of the matter is that we end up having to mention the company in a lot of stories.
Why so?
Because Paytm is a startup that seems to currently have very few peers in India when it comes to sheer ambition - starting from wallets and payments, the company is now attempting to morph itself into a comprehensive financial services platform offering everything from plain vanilla bank accounts to insurance and mutual fund products. Further, it seems to view every online market—from e-commerce to ticketing to travel—as a potential customer acquisition funnel, segueing that into a moat based on a large customer base and ubiquitous distribution. So whether you love it or hate it, there is no denying that Paytm is an important company in the Indian startup ecosystem.
If the company wasn't important already, it became much more important this week by virtue of adding a new name to its own cap table.
Berkshire Hathaway.
The legendary investment firm founded by the "Oracle of Omaha" Warren Buffet, one of the greatest investors of all time.
Berkshire just picked up a stake in Paytm, representing not only its first direct investment in India but also its first startup unicorn investment anywhere in the globe.
What does this deal mean for Paytm and for India as a whole? What drove Berkshire to make this investment? What does the future hold?

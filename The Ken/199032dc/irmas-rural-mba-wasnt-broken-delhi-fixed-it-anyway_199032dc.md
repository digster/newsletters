Good Morning Ishan,
Renaming cities, streets, universities, and libraries is now a national pastime in India. So why does it matter that the Institute of Rural Management Anand, or IRMA, has been named “Tribhuvan” Sahkari University—the inexplicable quotation marks are the government’s—through a recent act of Parliament?
After all, Tribhuvandas Patel was the founder of the dairy-co-operative giant Amul, which inspired IRMA.
The new moniker is the least of IRMA’s problems, though. A singular B-school that has retained, and consistently burnished, its rural-management chops for almost five decades, IRMA now has to “play mother hen to a flock of other institutions”, as Inderpal writes in his first story for The Ken.
On IRMA’s 60-acre campus in Anand, an hour’s drive from Vadodara, three new schools are already taking shape—running at a minimal capacity for now. The government also wants to create a national grid of hundreds of co-operative institutions, with IRMA at the centre.
IRMA’s success in training conscientious managers willing to look beyond cities is largely because it has been left to its own devices. But it will no longer be unshackled. “IRMA was number one in a category it created,” Inderpal argues in an excellent deep dive. “Now it is one of many in a system not of its choosing.” [Read it here](https://the-ken.com/story/irmas-rural-mba-wasnt-broken-delhi-fixed-it-anyway/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter).

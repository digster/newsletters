Who doesn’t like ice cream? Most hands won’t go up at this almost-rhetorical, fully-blasphemous poser. Some shockingly will. This minuscule minority includes the management of India’s largest FMCG company, Hindustan Unilever (HUL). Earlier this week, HUL’s board of directors gave its in-principle approval to demerge the company’s ice-cream business into a separate listed entity. That’s corporate speak for the giant taking the first steps to carve out a part of itself and house it in another company. Shareholders of HUL will get shares in the new entity too. That will come later.

For now though, this move is a head-scratcher. HUL’s ice-cream business boasts big brands like Kwality Wall’s and Cornetto, and brought in a cool Rs 1,750 crore in revenue last year. Margins are decent, and growth is almost a given, thanks to the country’s huge market and hot summers. The market is booming, with many new players like Hocco and Noto also jostling for a slice of the growing ice-cream pie. Why wouldn’t HUL want to keep this lucrative opportunity within itself?

Mysterious are the ways of giant corporations and their foreign controllers. Not just HUL, its UK-based parent, Unilever, is also spinning off its ice-cream business, the largest in its category globally.

Is the child merely following the parent, or is there more to it? Aakriti, the author of today’s story, digs into the ice-cream cup and finds many reasons—strategic, operational, financial, and practical—behind HUL choosing to put the business in the deep freeze. Let’s just say it’s not core or cool enough.

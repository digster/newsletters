Dmart’s e-commerce bet has gone from counterintuitive to obsolete. Sticking to its convictions served Dmart’s supermarkets well. But it has hurt its online venture

Good Morning Ishan,

A couple of weeks ago, my colleague Aakriti wrote a provocative story https://the-ken.com/story/zepto-is-a-quick-commerce-bronze-medallist-acting-like-a-gold-medallist/ on quick-commerce company Zepto’s latest fundraise. She argued how despite being smaller than Zomato-owned Blinkit and Swiggy Instamart, Zepto had managed to get the attention of both VCs and public-market investors. 

Hence this headline, which I love: Zepto is a quick-commerce bronze medallist acting like a gold medallist

There was yet more proof of this last week when Zepto co-founder and chief executive Aadit Palicha said https://www.moneycontrol.com/technology/zepto-can-become-bigger-than-dmart-in-18-24-months-aadit-palicha-article-12763557.html that his company would be bigger than Dmart sooner than later.

“Dmart is a US$30 billion company and they are only 4.5X our size in sales. If we continue to execute well, we will continue to grow 2-3X every year. And potentially surpass them in the next 18-24 months.”

I wouldn’t be surprised if this gave you pause. I was certainly a bit baffled.

Dmart is not your garden-variety grocery chain; it’s India’s most-valued listed retailer and also the country’s second-largest operator of supermarkets. In fact, Dmart even makes global retail giants look ordinary, as I wrote https://the-ken.com/tradetricks/dmart-and-investors-rekindle-their-love/ in April.

I wonder if Radhakishan Damani, the legendary investor and astute entrepreneur who founded Dmart two decades ago, read the story and said to himself: “I’ll have whatever this guy is smoking.”

Damani, and Dmart’s loyal investors, have every reason to make light of Zepto at this point. Its size notwithstanding, Dmart is yet to set up shop in large swathes of the country. Its only stores in Uttar Pradesh, India’s most populous state, are restricted to the National Capital Region, and it doesn’t have a single outlet in either of two other large states, Bihar and West Bengal. But for the likes of Zepto and Blinkit, there’s no market worth speaking of beyond the top 10 cities, at least not yet. Which is why my guess is that Damani is not losing sleep over the rise of quick commerce. 

This edition is not about Zepto, though. It’s not even about Dmart’s supermarkets. Instead, Palicha’s statements got me thinking about Dmart’s own attempt at cracking e-commerce. 

Dmart Ready went live in 2017, the same year as Dmart listed to a rapturous response. But it wasn’t until the pandemic struck that Dmart started thinking seriously about selling online. That had a lot to do with its closest brick-and-mortar rival Reliance Retail launching its own ecommerce bet Jiomart in 2020. 

Here’s what I wrote https://the-ken.com/story/dmarts-e-commerce-tortoise-squares-off-against-the-jiomart-hare/ in August 2021, roughly a year after Jiomart arrived on the scene:

Dmart Ready is not cut from the same cloth as its big-spending peers such as BigBasket—now majority-owned by the Tata Group—Reliance Industries’ JioMart , Amazon, SoftBank-backed Grofers, and Walmart-owned Flipkart. It is the only online grocer to stay away from free delivery regardless of the order value. And till recently, it also stood out among the pack by offering pickups. Amazon is now reportedly running a pilot in Bengaluru, allowing customers to pick up their orders from the More supermarket chain, which it co-owns.

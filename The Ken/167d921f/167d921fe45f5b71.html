Good Morning Ishan,
 
When I sat down to write this email, I promised myself: no sappiness. Our readers have had a fair dose in our recent weekend stories and emails. But a few words on a blank doc and I’m already nostalgic.
 
Our Going Home series this month likely made you so as well. Where is home? Where you grew up or where your child grew up (or is growing up)? Where you got your first school certificate, your college degree or your first salary? Where you rented your first independent flat or bought one? Where you started your first business or retired from a job? Or where people no longer looked at your business card to ask, "Where are you from?"
 
Nearly 20 years ago, I’d hear this often in Bengaluru (still Bangalore then). As I struggled to settle into a new job, a new city, a new marriage and hour-long Kannada lessons at the Press Club, every other professional meeting would end in, "Oh, you are from the North. Where’s home?" For many years, I’d duck the nosiness, until that day in 2005 in Mexico City. At a massive water conference, a delegate walked up to me only to ask, “Are you a Kannadiga?” Terribly jet-lagged, I found my perfect answer: “I’m a Bangalorean.”
 
I guess, for many, home isn’t where you are, but who you are. And for those like Janice Pariat, home is indelibly etched in memory as a place where Christmas rituals, held strong from an idyllic childhood, are performed.
 
Pariat, an award-winning author who lives in Delhi, writes: “The thing with rituals is you’re never quite aware they’re being formed. They grow year after year in quiet repetition, marking time, knitting you to your loved ones. For us, it didn't matter that our tree didn't always slip into the same corner, that the mantelpiece changed, and the Christmas cards arrived to frequently varying addresses.”
 
For the final piece in the series, she travels to Shillong, one of her "homes", to celebrate Christmas, to stand before the tree for a family photograph, to mark the passage of time, the inevitability of ageing, the steadfastness of love.

I’ll see you in January. Merry Christmas!

Once upon a time, a procurement coordinator ordered supplies, signed off on invoices, and complained about budget cuts. Now, they are “intelligent procurement analysts”, making AI-optimised sourcing decisions.

Accounts clerks used to shuffle spreadsheets and argue with people about expense claims. Now, they’re “finance insights analysts” feeding data into machine learning models—presumably with the same Excel, but with a bit more Python thrown in.

Talent recruiters are not just posting jobs anymore. They’re “compensation consultants”, “HR data analysts”, or “digital employee experience managers”.

Welcome to the AI upgrade: (almost the) same job, fancier role name, new skill set. And Accenture is right in the thick of it.

Enter: Learnvantage. It’s the billion-dollar platform Accenture launched last year to reskill not just its 700,000 employees, but also its clients’ workforces for this brave new AI world. Think of it as a full-stack reinvention machine. Accenture tells companies how to automate their processes, then uses Learnvantage to train their employees for whatever roles are left standing.

To speed this up, Accenture has been on a buying spree. It picked up Talentsprint in India, known for AI-skilling programmes, and has invested in US-based Workhelix, a company that helps break jobs into their component tasks to figure out what should be automated and what needs upskilling.

Of course, Accenture isn’t alone in this bet. TCS, Infosys, and McKinsey are circling the same pie, all trying to sell companies on the future of work. Catch Abhirami’s story here.

Flipkart co-founder Sachin Bansal doesn’t like his new venture to be called a fintech. That’s a tad “less serious”; fintechs “don’t have their books”.

For someone who’s ploughed in ~Rs 4,000 crore ($528 million), more than half of what he made from his stake sale in Flipkart, it’s a fair ask for Navi Technologies. Especially when you see that even before it has acquired a banking licence, Navi is taking banks head-on in the most significant loan category—home loans.

For one, it’s a matter of timing.

Home financing rates are at historic lows. But for banks, offering low interest rates is a no-brainer. At low repo rates and high CASA, banks enjoy a very cheap cost of funds. Licensed housing finance companies, too, are able to raise money to lend at cheap rates, giving them headroom to offer low interest rates. Navi has neither. But With Bansal owning 97.8% of the company, ambition is its tailwind (its repo rate, CASA, and what have you.)

Secondly, it helps accelerate Navi’s audacious goal to become a modern tech-first bank in the shortest-possible time. Entering the home loan space would grow its asset under management far quicker than smaller ticket personal loans. And since home loans have the lowest defaults among all categories, this’d make Navi’s non-performing assets (NPA) ratio look even better—it's already ahead of the curve with 4.9%.

Since the two big home lenders—Indiabulls Housing Finance and Dewan Housing Finance Ltd—have ceded ground, the market is ripe for new 

entrants. However, the two failed businesses have also made builders wary of novice lenders. Especially when 50% of loan originations happen at the builder’s site.

After launching personal loans in mid-2020, Navi thought long and hard about its next product. In choosing lending over cards, it ticked many 

boxes internally. Now it has external boxes to tick as well. Arundhati has a lucid and insightful take on one of the most ambitious financial services companies in India today.

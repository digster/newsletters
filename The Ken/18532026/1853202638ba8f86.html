For over seven decades, speedy jeepneys have been the way to commute across the Philippines. Over 300,000 of these repurposed American military jeeps account for 40% of all motorised journeys. And Filipinos—often being one of the two dozen in a vehicle designed for 16—have preferred the adventure of a jeepney ride because of the speed, convenience, and apparently, the adrenaline rush associated with it.
 
These can be flagged down anywhere, and you can pay for your ride in cash as you stuff yourself into the vehicle. In most cases, drivers will even drop you off in front of your house rather than at an actual bus stop.  
 
However, one venture, the 9-year-old Global Electric Transport (GET), is trying to get Filipinos to change old habits with its all-electric Comet buses. The electric vehicle (EV) operator isn’t trying to change the market on its own—it has tied up with a range of companies to build supporting infrastructure and generate demand for its e-buses. 
 
In a story rich with business details and consumer stubbornness, Isa tells us about how this company is trying to get people to drop old commuting habits and switch to a cleaner, safer, and more comfortable option.

If GET built its business slowly over the past decade, its plans were recently catalysed by government incentives to trade in the old gasoline guzzlers for EVs. But will it be enough?

Not really. It isn’t just the adamant commuters GET has to win over. Equally reluctant are the drivers who don't want to buy costlier vehicles just yet.

Even two weeks after Diwali, its return gift—toxic, unbreathable air—still lingers. So why do I still wait for it every year? Because Delhi’s Diwali party scene is amazing. Parties with cousins, friends, ex-colleagues, and current ones… the list is endless. 

At one such party last month, a younger cousin introduced me to a non-alcoholic spirit. My reaction, along with most of the older folks in our group, was an eyeroll (obviously). Most of us were millennials, born before 1996, while a few, like my cousin, were Gen Z. 

This moment highlighted an apparent trend: a gaping gap in alcohol consumption between the generations. Gen Z seems to prefer no-alcohol drinks, while millennials are the exception. A Livemint report from May confirmed this, noting that in India, Gen X and Gen Z are more likely to choose to drink less, while millennials continue to drive alcohol consumption. 

This shift isn’t just an Indian phenomenon; it’s part of a global “sober curious” movement, evidenced by the meteoric rise of companies like Athletic Brewing in the US, which is dedicated entirely to non-alcoholic beers. 

But if this is a mega trend, why is Varun Beverages, the largest bottler of Pepsico in India, looking to add alcohol as another revenue stream? Isn’t it counterintuitive for the beverage giant to enter this category?

According to an Economic Times report from yesterday, Varun Beverages is in exploratory talks with Pepsico “to expand their partnership to distribute and sell alcoholic drinks in India”.

In fact, during an analyst call last week following its Q3 results, Ravi Jaipuria, the chairman of RJ Corp (Varun Beverages’ parent company), stated that they plan to launch into this ready-to-drink (RTD), low-alcohol category in select African countries. When asked about introducing this category in India, here’s what Jaipuria said:

“Well, RTD and low-alcohol products are becoming large all over the world, and there is a good scope here also. In India, you can’t advertise any alcohol products. So, I mean, we have kept ourselves open, and hopefully, we look at it. We are starting with Africa, and let’s see what we can do in India.”

Experts don’t see this move as counterintuitive. While they acknowledge the sobriety trend among Gen Z, they argue the broader market potential remains huge. 

“While I don’t discount the trend, if you look at the market from a larger lens, there is indeed a lot of scope. Gen Z in India are at a count of about 382 million, while millennials are 330 million in 2024. So even if only 10% of millennials drink, and a lesser percentage of Gen Z drinks, it’s still a huge consumer base,” Amulya Pandit, a senior consultant at market research firm Euromonitor International, told The Ken.

Pandit added, “People may be consuming less alcohol in terms of volume, but they are consuming high-quality alcohol such as single malts, scotch, and botanical-infused gins.” 

In fact, in 2024, while the global alcohol market remained nearly flat at 0.6% growth, India’s grew by 5.3% year-on-year, according to Euromonitor International.

Meanwhile, Varun Beverages, while a top beverage maker, has been facing headwinds with new competitors like Reliance’s Campa entering the market. This quarter, the company saw flat growth, reporting only a 2% rise in revenue to Rs 49 billion and a 2.4% volume increase to 273.8 million cases. While the company discounted any competitive impact—blaming monsoon rains—analysts said Reliance’s influence is undeniable. 

This pressure is likely why Varun Beverages is now gauging diversification opportunities, a point the company stressed in its analyst call.

As people move towards healthier lifestyles, the market for carbonated drinks has been saturating in the West. This is what prompted global beverage makers like Coca-Cola and Pepsico to partner with alcohol giants such as Anheuser-Busch Inbev, Bacardi, and Diageo around 2021–2022 to introduce low-alcohol, ready-to-drink beverages. 

Typically, spirits have 30–50% by volume, while these RTD versions contain only about 5–7%. In these partnerships, Coca-Cola or Pepsi would license their brand to the alcohol manufacturers, who would produce the drinks. 

As Livemint reported, low-alcoholic beverages are becoming the go-to choice in India, too. So, Varun Beverages now wants to extend its partnership with Pepsico to enter this new category. However, there are significant challenges.

Unlike in the West and Africa, where the distribution chain for non-alcoholic and alcoholic beverages is the same, that is not the case in India. 

“Varun Beverages’ biggest strength is its distribution might, which goes to the smallest of villages. But you cannot introduce these RTD beverages at a usual kirana, can you?”

An FMCG analyst told The Ken

Furthermore, alcohol in India is a state subject, meaning taxation differs across states. This would prevent Varun Beverages from introducing products at uniform prices nationwide.

Lastly, and perhaps most importantly, India has largely been a spirits-consuming country, where consumers prefer drinks with high alcohol concentration. For instance, while beer is consumed more than spirits globally, the opposite is true in India. The global average beer consumption is 25 litres per person, but in India, it’s almost one-tenth of that, at just 2 litres per person, says Euromonitor’s Pandit.

These are likely the reasons why Coca-Cola’s Lemon Dou, a low-alcohol lemon drink introduced two years ago in some Indian states, has not gained significant traction.

It’s too early for Varun Beverages to decide how big the low-alcohol drinks market could be, but it’s surely something to keep an eye on.

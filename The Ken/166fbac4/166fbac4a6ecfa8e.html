Good Morning Ishan,
 
Here in Delhi, there's a chill in the air. Having spent most of my life in much warmer climes (Bangalore, briefly Kerala, the UAE and Tamil Nadu), really cold winters—when temperatures drop to the single digits—were a bit of a wonder when I shifted here a few years ago. 
 
By now I'll admit the novelty's worn off a bit, but this is still the most enjoyable season in this city. Summer's sweltering heat seems far, far away, and there's all sorts of great fruit coming in (grape time!). Plus the cats are in cuddle mode by default.
 
Speaking of cats, did you happen to catch our Sunday story on online "fashion police" calling out brands and designers for copycat work? It's a trend that's rocked the fashion industry globally, changing the power dynamics of haute couture. And it's really taken hold in India over the past year or so, writes Chandni, our contributor for this piece. But it's not all black and white. Read the story to find out more.
 
Also in the past week was Roshni's story on air purifiers. Indian cities are seeing their air quality deteriorate by the day (especially Delhi, and sadly, more so in the winter). But bad news for our lungs is good news for appliance makers like Philips, Honeywell and a whole host of others—air purifier sales are skyrocketing, and everyone wants in on the game. (And yes, we do have a purifier at the Delhi office.)
 
Moving on to the rest of our line-up, we have Sid's piece (free read) on the business of credit rating agencies, and the conflicts of interest involved. The "issuer pays" model is clearly flawed, and it hasn't changed despite default after default and crisis after crisis—which is what partly led to India's own Lehman moment as massive infrastructure lender IL&FS defaulted on its debt. Sid dives in to find if there's a way out. 
 
Another Indian company that's having a crisis of a different sort is Foodpanda, writes Salman in Monday's story. The online food delivery startup has been struggling for years in a market dominated by Swiggy and Zomato (with UberEats now in the picture), but its latest owner, Ola, thinks it can turn the company around. And its plan, Salman says, is to focus more on food and less on restaurants. Okay.
 
Then we had Shashi's piece on the impending rise of the Indian music label T-Series as YouTube's top channel. In the process, dethroning YouTube sensation PewDiePie, who's been number one in terms of subscribers for nearly five years. (His fans are not pleased.) On the flip side though, more eyeballs have not really turned into very much more money for T-Series. The low pricing of online ads in India are to blame, says Shashi. 
 
And finally, our last (or rather, first) story of the week was the third part of our Simplicity Paradigm series. The writers, Saurabh Mukherjea and Anupam Gupta, take us into the core of their philosophy that simplicity trumps all. They make a case for it rooted in psychology, finance and more, and lay out a path to setting up your own framework for a simpler, better life. It's also a free story, so go ahead and share it around.
 
Enjoy the rest of your weekend. We'll be back tomorrow.
 
Cheers,

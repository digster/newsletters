Good Morning Ishan,
 
Are there times when it seems like your days are just an endless stream of meetings, messages, and emails, from the second you wake until the
minute you crash with the harsh glow of the phone on your bedside table? 

Well, remote-work has blurred the lines between work and home for everyone. 

And trust the French who look upon workaholism in disdain and are obsessed with work-life boundaries to come up with a term for this—“le blurring”. If you’re guilty of checking work emails before going to bed or the first thing in the morning, you’ve been ‘le blurred’. 

But the French were also ahead of our times in identifying that the sacred boundary between work and life was under threat. In 2016, France passed what’s popularly known as the ‘El Khomri’ law. It was the legal right for workers to “disconnect from work”.

Maybe everyone should take a leaf out of the French playbook?

Anyway, we book-ended this week with stories by Arundhati and Olina on how the pandemic has changed the dynamics of work in India. 
In the first part on Monday, they explored how companies are grappling with the question of measuring productivity when they can’t see employees working. After all, 40% of the respondents to The Ken’s survey said their productivity had fallen. This despite 56% of them saying they’d extended their WFH day by one to three hours. 

In part two on Friday, which was also a free story, they spoke to companies that are struggling with decisions about remote-work, hybrid work, and throwbacks to the pre-pandemic times of physical offices. In The Ken’s survey, nearly 91% of those who had started a new job in the last 1.5 years felt “disconnected” from their new work environment. Solving that problem isn’t going to be easy. 

The other stories by our ace reporters this week also had a common thread—the ‘biggies’ or the ‘wannabe biggies’ in our ecosystem.
On Tuesday, Pranav went behind the scenes of India’s electric vehicle industry to write about how two companies–Ola Electric and Ather Energy–have come to dominate the segment. The funding gap between Ather and UltraViolette, the next highest-funded EV startup, is US$133 million. Investors aren’t quite willing to back new EV upstarts unless they can show how cheaply and quickly they can bring a product to market when compared to Ather. 

On Wednesday, Munsif took us into the world of India’s direct-to-consumer (D2C) sector, projected to touch US$100 billion by 2025. But as India’s e-commerce aggregators try to mimic the US-based unicorn Thrasios, they run the risk of running into a wall with Indian e-commerce sellers who have a family legacy. The two questions from sellers they’re having to find answers to are ,“Why would I sell to a barely-year-old business?” and “What do I do after cashing out from a profitable business venture?”

And Ruhi, on Thursday, had a fascinating story about ‘star doctors’. You know, the ones who have a couple of decades of specialist expertise, attract 2-5X more patients than peers, and get rewarded with a share in the hospital’s profits. Well, the pandemic has turned the tables on these ‘stars’. Hospitals are reducing their reliance on them as younger, more digitally savvy doctors hog the limelight due to an increase in teleconsultations. A new star is born. 

In our daily newsletter, Beyond the First Order, Jaideep dipped his toes into the online world of sports betting in India. Not to place his own bets of course, but just for some research on how legal those advertisements from companies like bet365 and Betway are. Rohin looked at the results of New York’s first ranked-choice election—where voters didn’t just select one candidate to give their vote to, but as many as five—and how the world may need to adopt new voting systems. And I wrote about the new mutual fund launch from Navi and how low-cost alone doesn’t quite cut it for index funds. 

And on Saturday, in The Nutgraf, Praveen asked why fintech unicorn CRED is eyeing the media and content business, either through content of its own, or through aggregation.  The answer, he says, lies in CRED’s real ambition. An evolution from just being a ‘payments app’ into an ‘actual currency’. 

Now before you head back to enjoying the rest of your Sunday, let me leave you with another term—aside from ‘le blurring’—to slip into your next social conversation.

Yak shaving. While productivity tools can be great, if you spend all day ‘trying’ to manage your time, you may never get to the task itself. And any tasks that distract you from an original goal is yak shaving. A rabbit hole, if there ever was one.

We’re not out of the pandemic-woods yet, and everyone’s trying hard at life. It’s okay to cut yourself some slack. Disconnect. At least, I’m planning to take a break and disconnect soon. I don’t know yet what I’ll be doing, but it won’t be work.

It was the best of times, it was the worst of times, it was the age of IPOs, it was the age of lukewarm listings, it was the epoch of investors, it was the epoch of speculators, it was the season of Waaree Energies, it was the spring of Bajaj Housing Finance, it was the winter of Hyundai India—in short, the IPO terrain was so booming and so uncertain that some of its loudest whispers came not from the exchanges of Mumbai… but from the streets of Rajkot.

This may be a tale of two cities, but it’s hardly a Dickensian story.

Mumbai might be where companies go public, but it’s Rajkot that’s turning out to be an oracle of IPO success. This tier-2 city in Gujarat is where the rubber meets the road, or, perhaps more fittingly, where a stock meets the street. Here, companies chasing billions find their fates tied to the grey-market premium (GMP)—a speculative, unofficial price that signals how much extra speculators are willing to pay over the IPO price.

Take Hyundai, for instance. With a shiny US$3.3 billion offering, the company thought it was all set for a blockbuster debut. It rolled out roadshows and draped itself in hype. But Rajkot took one look at that Rs 1,960 ($23) price tag and decided it was a bridge too far. When it said “pass”, retail investors followed suit, sending ripples far beyond Gujarat. And mind you, this is from the country’s third-largest source of IPO applications.

This Rajkot influence isn’t exactly new, either; it goes back to the days when Dhirubhai Ambani was courting local investors for Reliance Industries’ IPO. The clout, however, endures.

So, what makes the city a kingmaker of IPOs? A mix of stock-market culture. Seasoned brokers. And a knack for guessing the game. Just look at Waaree Energies, which achieved an 11X oversubscription from retail investors by knowing how to win over Rajkot.

Good Morning Ishan,

For entrepreneurs timing their IPO, there’s nothing like riding the wave to “hot issue” markets.

Zomato did that in 2021 to list at a 53% premium. Then the hot issue turned cold; the company took over two years to close above its list price. Since then, Zomato has been on a tear, trebling its market cap to a stonking US$19.8 billion.

Swiggy must be rewinding this short public markets journey of its rival a hundred times as it prepares to list. Because even though investors have confidence in the business model, it’s the relative pricing of Swiggy’s shares that will swing the sentiments.

The Bengaluru foodtech’s investment bankers have an unenviable job at hand. Swiggy’s market share in food delivery is down to 45% from over 60% in 2018 and to 32% in quick commerce from a high of 52% two years ago.

For investors though, the evaluation couldn’t be easier—every major business of Swiggy’s (food delivery, quick commerce, and restaurant reservations) is mirrored in Zomato.

In a sharp analysis today, Seetharaman argues why the mood among investors is boiling down to “How cheap can you get Swiggy’s shares for?”

Will smartphones’ exports rub off on the job market?

India is celebrating the rise of the exports of smartphones, the fourth largest item on the list now.

Apple has been responsible for this to a great extent but Google isn’t far behind. Its contract partner Dixon will make 100,000 Pixel 8 smartphones, of which a third will be exported.

Electronics manufacturing, in general, is at the heart of this resurgence with none other than Tata Sons’ chairman taking the driving seat at the group’s semiconductor bet.

But the talent mismatch in all this is inescapable. Someone shared the IIT-Gandhinagar placement data. It was shocking to see that less than 30% of materials engineering, 44% of electrical engineering, and 60% of mechanical engineering graduates were placed this year.

One IIT placement data is not representative, but it’s not different across other institutions. If tech manufacturing has to take off in the real sense, a healthy demand and supply of engineers in disciplines other than computer science (where placements are only slightly better) must also hum along with the export numbers.

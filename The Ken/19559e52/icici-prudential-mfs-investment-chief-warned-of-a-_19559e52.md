Good Morning Ishan,
Imagine running one of India’s biggest mutual funds, spotting a market bubble from a mile away, setting the cat among the pigeons, and still not being able to stop it completely…at your own fund house.
That’s exactly what happened to S Naren, the investment chief at ICICI Prudential Mutual Fund. In January, he issued a dramatic warning to investors: get out of small- and mid-cap stocks—“lock, stock, and barrel”.
He wasn’t subtle. He called valuations absurd and said even long-term SIPs wouldn’t deliver returns unless you stuck with them for 20 years. And when Naren speaks, people usually listen. Market veterans praise his deep investing chops, no-nonsense approach, and willingness to admit mistakes. As one former fund manager put it, “You have to take Naren seriously.”
And yet… ICICI Prudential keeps accepting those SIPs. Why? Because even if you’re the CIO, there are pulls and pressures—and stopping the flow entirely is bad for business.
A leaked transcript revealed just how frustrated Naren was: “I tried my best to stop small- and mid-cap SIPs… but failed miserably. I’m not able to convince my sales colleagues.”
Ouch.
Meanwhile, the market did exactly what Naren predicted. Since January:
Nifty 50 fell 6%.
Mid-caps dropped 11%.
Small-caps? 13%—gone.
It’s not that ICICI Prudential didn’t see this coming. For over a year, it reduced its exposure to risky small- and mid-cap stocks and even blocked lump-sum investments.
But SIPs? Those kept flowing—because when you manage Rs 8.8 lakh crore, stopping the money train isn’t so easy.
In today’s story, Anand digs into:
-
What happens when the market’s OG sees a crash coming—but can’t hit “stop”
-
How ICICI hedged its bets and insulated some of its portfolio but couldn’t shut the floodgates

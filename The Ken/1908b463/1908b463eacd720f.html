The Big Story

How many hospitals can PE firms buy before patients say, ‘Enough!’?

With 18 buyout deals and counting, PE firms’ hunger for Indian healthcare assets will only increase. State and central governments are increasingly choosing to become just payers, but they can’t afford to be onlookers

After exhausting the US market, PE firms are looking for large hospital deals in India to build “platforms”, the new PE buzzword. Of the 80-plus deals in healthcare since 2020, 18 have been buyouts, most of them in the last year alone. With a frothy stock market, everyone is rushing to list. Bankers are even queuing up before PEs, asking them to list their portfolio companies [Read the full story here]

Also, in the spotlight…

Diabetics don’t need glucose sensors. Nor do fitness buffs. So who do they really benefit?  

Abbott made a killing by helping healthtechs sell CGMs to non-diabetic Indians. But even its most aggressively sold pitch—CGM for diabetics—is under threat

Primarily used by people with diabetes, Abbott is pushing continuous glucose monitoring (CGM) devices globally to target the non-diabetic consumer market. The company, the sole seller of CGMs in India, has long positioned its product for diabetics since it first launched in the country in 2015. 

The device soon became a fashion statement among non-diabetics, thanks to healthtech companies like Ultrahuman and Healthifyme. But the fad among non-diabetics has run its course. Even as diabetics need the device more than anyone else, it’s out of most of their reach [Read the full story here]

It’s edtech

Masters’ Union touted placements to rival IIMs. Now, it struggles to place its students

With over half of its PGP students still unplaced, the alternative MBA school has scaled back intake for the next batch to dodge embarrassment 

Placements have been Masters’ Union’s strongest asset, the weapon it wields to compete against the country’s top B-schools. In a tough job market where even IIMs and ISB have struggled, Masters’ Union grapples with over 50% of graduation batch students still seeking placements.

Placements are not just a source of pride but crucial for its financial goals, including next year’s fees and the introduction of new courses. While it has hired aggressively for its placements team, co-founder Pratham Mittal is also mentoring students one-to-one [Read the full story here]

Fintech and finance

For fintechs, RBI is the boy who cries wolf

Earlier this year, the banking regulator audited fintechs and flagged concerns with regard to daily operations, pricing, and credit risk. Fintechs see this as ‘RBI overreach’

The Reserve Bank of India isn’t happy with the rise in fintechs’ unsecured loan book. It conducted forensic audits on those companies and suggested changes in the way they conduct daily operations.

Some fintechs, however, feel the exercise was more about finding fault with them than an attempt to contain risk. There exists no authority to challenge RBI’s diktats yet, but is it time to consider setting one up? [Read the full story here]

A Filipino TV giant lost its network. Then it made a Hollywood-inspired comeback

ABS lost its broadcasting crown in 2020. Now, pivoting to new markets, it’s staging a turnaround with fresh audiences, though the road to full recovery remains winding

ABS is the Philippines’ leading multimedia entertainment company, producing the country’s top music acts and blockbuster movies. Once the country’s biggest TV broadcaster, ABS fell from grace—and profitability—when it lost its operating franchise in 2020.

Instead of trying to reclaim its lost kingdom, ABS set its sights on new markets. Creating a new audience is helping ABS regain revenue, but it still hasn’t made a full recovery from its downfall [Read the full story here]

Battery swapping didn’t get the love charging did. Then came Zomato and VCs 

EV battery-swapping companies have attracted double the VC money this year than in 2023, thanks to their partnerships with quick-commerce firms looking to reduce their downtime and costs 

Despite India’s venture-capital funding crunch, companies banking on battery swapping have attracted double the money this year compared to 2023. Venture capitalists like the sector for their association with quick commerce. But battery swapping, as a solution, may not work beyond that, say experts.

The government has turned a blind eye to the sector, depriving the companies of any subsidies and, in turn, levying a higher tax. The EV ecosystem now waits eagerly for positive news from the upcoming 2024 union budget [Read the full story here]

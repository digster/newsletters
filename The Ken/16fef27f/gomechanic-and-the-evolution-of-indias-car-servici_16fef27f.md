I'm sure you have seen that stock picture—called The March of Progress—to depict the evolution of man. The one in which a knuckle-dragging chimp transforms into a homo sapien, usually in five stages.
That was the image that jumped to mind when I read Pranav's story today on the car servicing industry in India. Or more accurately, GoMechanic's ambition to speed up the evolution of this industry by aggregating the numerous independent workshops.
It has the promise of any aggregation business: a massive opportunity, of 300,000 workshops; low penetration, with only 7,000 authorised service centres; a growing market, despite the steep drop off in car sales last year; the possibility of healthy commission margins, if one plays their cards right.
The pitfalls of the aggregation business can be summed up in one word. (No, not OYO.) Carnation. This chain of car workshops buckled in 2017 under a mountain of debt and the founder's fallout with his former employer, Maruti, India's top-selling carmaker.
The story of Carnation is a double-edged sword for GoMechanic.
On the one hand, the industry trailblazer's path serves as a ready reckoner. "A lot of things that exist today—us, GoMechanic, other startups—is because they (Carnation) saw some of the teething issues," says Mihir Mohan, the founder of Pitstop, another car workshop aggregator.
On the other hand, it's not easy to shake off the ghosts of the past.
"A challenge that we face is this negative mindset by a few stakeholders in this ecosystem because of the Carnation fiasco," GoMechanic co-founder Kushal Karva says of convincing workshop owners and spare parts makers to join its platform.

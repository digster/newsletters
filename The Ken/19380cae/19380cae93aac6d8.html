Love your plateaus. “Where in our upbringing, our schooling, our career are we explicitly taught to value, to enjoy, even to love the plateau, the long stretch of diligent effort with no seeming progress?”—George Leonard
By Rohin Dharmakumar
16 min read

Good morning, dear Ishan. I hope you’ve had a good week, wherever you are. Bengaluru has gotten a few shades darker and some degrees colder, as our mild winters set in. Early mornings are dark, cold, and night-like, provoking conversations with yourself in bed each day, one half trying to convince the other why both of you deserve to skip today’s workout or run.

No? I guess it’s just me then.

Today is also the first day of December. Another year is about to have gone by. It’s the month to be reflective, tired, and perhaps even a bit nihilistic.

No again? Just me?

Okay then.

I’ll say this, this December feels different. I feel like I’ve both levelled up as well as plateaued. This was an eventful year professionally, with my wonderful friends and colleagues at The Ken pushing the envelope in so many directions. 2024 felt like the first “normal” year following the pandemic, where we went back to the freeform and collaborative style of working that we built The Ken for.

As a professional, I thought I pushed myself to learn and do new things.

Personally, I started learning Kannada earlier this year. I decided I wouldn’t rush myself, but that I’d take most of the year to get to a state where I felt comfortable conversing with others. I went through one chapter of my book (it had around 50) each day, during my office commute. After the first pass of the book, I started a second. This time I started highlighting words and phrases I didn’t remember easily.

Then, for a while I plateaued. But for the last month, I’ve been trying to converse with people in my newly learnt Kannada. I feel like I’m 50–60% there in terms of speaking (holding a casual conversation in real time), and about 70–80% in terms of understanding the other person speaking.

I’m starting a final third pass of the book, while continuing my conversations.

Once that’s over, I want to start learning Tamil in 2025. (Learning Kannada was easier since I knew Malayalam. Learning Tamil will be much easier, I hope, once I know Malayalam and Kannada. These three southern languages share a lot in common.)

Unlike my past attempts at learning languages (Indian and foreign), this time, the biggest difference for me was not being rushed. I told myself I’d take a year, perhaps even more to get a reasonable state of fluency in Kannada. And that I wouldn’t rush myself or try to set milestones and deadlines. And that I wouldn’t mind some stagnation that was inevitable.

It was liberating.

No, I am not building up to New Year resolutions. That most of them fail should tell you all there is to know.

Instead, I am talking about the liberating and yet motivating feeling of pursuing some goal over a long and unbound time period. To quote Bill Gates, “most people overestimate what they can do in one year and underestimate what they can do in ten years.”

Replace the one year and ten years with short-term and long-term, and you have a recipe for endless growth.

1. Learning to love your plateaus

There’s really no way around it. Learning any new skill involves relatively brief spurts of progress, each of which is followed by a slight decline to a plateau somewhat higher in most cases than that which preceded it. The curve above is necessarily idealized. In the actual learning experience, progress is less regular; the upward spurts vary; the plateaus have their own dips and rises along the way. But the general progression is almost always the same. To take the master’s journey, you have to practice diligently, striving to hone your skills, to attain new levels of competence. But while doing so—and this is the inexorable fact of the journey—you also have to be willing to spend most of your time on a plateau, to keep practicing even when you seem to be getting nowhere.

Excerpt From Mastery—The Keys To Success And Long-Term Fulfillment by George Leonard

I started re-reading Mastery earlier this week. It was partly in reaction to an experiment where I started five books at the same time last weekend.

1. Meditation for Mortals by Oliver Burkeman

2. Peak Performance by Brad Stulberg and Steve Magness

3. The Artists Way by Julia Cameron

4. How Leaders Learn by David Novak

5. The Align Method by Aaron Alexander

Why did I do that? Because I wanted to challenge myself with multiple different perspectives on varying topics at the same time. I told myself I’d read about a chapter each from each of the books every other day.

Next to Meditation for Mortals and The Artists Way, the “leadership” books (Peak Performance and How Leaders Learn) felt very glib and prescriptive. Like books generated by a large language model trained on HBR archives.

I abandoned the two books, as by now we all know that “finishing” books isn’t worth anything if you’re not enjoying reading them. In fact, Burkeman’s book even had an apt section.

Fortunately, there are three pieces of advice for navigating a world of infinite information that are more genuinely helpful. The first is to treat your to-read pile like a river, not a bucket. That is to say: think of your backlog not as a container that gradually fills up, and that it’s your job to empty, but as a stream that flows past you, from which you get to pick a few choice items, here and there, without feeling guilty for letting all the others float by.

[…]

The second piece of advice is to resist the urge to stockpile knowledge. At least where non-fiction sources are concerned, it’s easy to fall into the assumption that the point of reading or listening to things is to add to your storehouse of knowledge and insights, like a squirrel hoarding nuts, in preparation for a future when you’ll finally get to take advantage of it all.

[…]

The closely related final rule is to remember that consuming information is a present-moment activity, like everything else. It’s not merely that a fixation on retaining facts is a poor way to reap the benefits of reading. It’s also that any focus on ‘reaping the benefits’ risks obscuring the truth that a meaningful life, in the end, has to involve at least some activities we love doing for themselves, here and now.

Excerpt From Meditations for Mortals by Oliver Burkeman

I also abandoned The Artists Way after a few chapters because it didn’t speak to me. Thus, I decided to re-read Mastery to “make up” for the books I’d given up.

I loved the book the first time. This time, I found additional layers of meaning in it as a parent to a teenager.

True pursuit of mastery, argues Leonard, is not a race to a finish. It isn’t even a pursuit of a goal. But just a journey with oneself in search of joy. It’s when we enjoy doing things simply, not because they’re milestones on a career path someone defined for us.

In such pursuits there will be numerous “plateaus”—phases where we may not necessarily be improving but are still playing. Because it still brings us joy.

To an external observer or outsider, it may appear that we’ve stagnated.

Early in life, we are urged to study hard, so that we’ll get good grades. We are told to get good grades so that we’ll graduate from high school and get into college. We are told to graduate from high school and get into college so that we’ll get a good job. We are told to get a good job so that we can buy a house and a car. Again and again we are told to do one thing only so that we can get something else. We spend our lives stretched on an iron rack of contingencies.

Contingencies, no question about it, are important. The achievement of goals is important. But the real juice of life, whether it be sweet or bitter, is to be found not nearly so much in the products of our efforts as in the process of living itself, in how it feels to be alive. We are taught in countless ways to value the product, the prize, the climactic moment. But even after we’ve just caught the winning pass in the Superbowl, there’s always tomorrow and tomorrow and tomorrow. If our life is a good one, a life of mastery, most of it will be spent on the plateau. If not, a large part of it may well be spent in restless, distracted, ultimately self-destructive attempts to escape the plateau. The question remains: Where in our upbringing, our schooling, our career are we explicitly taught to value, to enjoy, even to love the plateau, the long stretch of diligent effort with no seeming progress?

Excerpt From Mastery—The Keys To Success And Long-Term Fulfillment by George Leonard

As we end the year, I can think of at least a few areas where I feel comfortable in my current plateaus, both professionally and personally.

Perhaps that is what makes this December feel different.

2. Bribo update

Thank you to everyone who wrote in to check on Bribo. He is recuperating at Cartman Hospital, where the doctors tell me his injuries are responding well to the daily change of dressings.

(Cartman Hospital was founded by Professor N S Ramaswamy, the Founder-Director of IIM Bangalore, Padma Bhushan Awardee, and Chairman of the Animal Welfare Board of India. I love their very obvious all-dogs-are-equal philosophy.) 

I met him a few times during the week. The first time, he seemed a bit aloof and angry, only warily taking the treats that I’d brought for him. Surrounded by strange and new dogs and people, separated from his mum and home, I can imagine how lost he must have been.

Fortunately, the next time he was very happy to see me. He wolfed down all the chicken jerky strips I’d brought and then quietly whimpered as I petted him, perhaps thanking me, or perhaps asking me to take him back to his home.

View in Browser

It’s a good thing we can’t communicate directly, because I’d have no heart to tell him that his mother Svelty had disappeared too two days after I’d brought him to the hospital.

The night I’d taken Bribo, Svelty refused to eat the food I brought her. She sniffed around, came to me for some petting, but did not eat anything. 

The next night she was nowhere to be found.

I asked around for a few days, finding no answers. Till one of the locals told me they saw her last Sunday with a different pack of dogs inside the neighbouring forested area that belongs to the Army. Survival on the streets is hard, which is why streeties normally band together for safety.

Bribo and Svelty were a pack. Without Bribo, Svelty seems to have figured her only chance of survival was to join a new one.

I keep looking for her every morning and evening, as I walk Gabru. I still haven’t seen her. 

At one level I knew this was inevitable when I started feeding and caring for them. Street dogs have less than half the life of those we bring into our homes as pets, because of diseases, accidents, and injuries sustained during fights. Caring for them means accepting that. But like with love in general, it’s better to have loved and cared for your streeties for some time rather than to have never at all. My life this year was happier and richer partly because of Svelty and Bribo being part of it. 

But this also makes me nervous for Bribo for once he’s suitably recovered from his injuries. Will the hapless one-year-old be strong enough—physically and otherwise—to survive by himself? I am not sure.

But if there are any of you who’d like to consider adopting a sweet, intelligent, and very well-mannered one-year-old dog with a long and stately snout and leopard-like spots (brindling), please write to me at fp@the-ken.com. He will make a wonderful, handsome, and loyal companion. (Alas, Gabru is much too possessive, fearful, and aggressive for us to even consider bringing a second male dog into our house.)

4. Works of Stone

Hello, dear Ishan. It’s Hari again with this week’s book recommendations from the First Principles community. 

Last week, we discussed the book Lust for Life by author Irving Stone—a poignant dive into the life of one of the most talented (and tragic) artists in world history, Van Gogh. Well, Lust for Life is hardly Stone’s only great book, and for this week’s edition, Ramesh Emani wrote in recommending a few other great works by Stone:

This is a follow-on recommendation for the book ‘Lust for life’ by Irving Stone. I wanted to add that all the biographies of Irving Stone are great. Some of them are, The Agony and the Ecstasy—about Michelangelo, The Passions of the Mind—about Freud, The Origin—of Charles Darwin.

These are great biographies and I was influenced by them. These books may not be known to the younger audience of FP.

1. The Box: How the Shipping Container Made the World Smaller and the World Economy Bigger by Marc Levinson

“The history of transportation of goods must be happening since time immaterial. The book walks us through the history of how complex, difficult it was to move goods in any form ( sacks, boxes, drums, etc ) through Road , Rail or Air.. there was no standard form of packing and it used to result in colossal waste of time, space being wasted due to no standardisation of packaging through across whether storage, shipment , loading or unloading of goods.

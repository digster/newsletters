Are lenders drawing the curtains on India’s credit-card frenzy? Things seem to have started slowing down in recent months 
 By Rounak Kumar Gunjan https://the-ken.com/writers/rounakthe-ken-com/

Have you noticed a respite in the number of unsolicited calls pushing credit cards down your throat? I have, at least—though I don’t have any numbers on hand saying it’s a nationwide trend. Initially, I thought it was something to do with the clampdown https://www.livemint.com/technology/tech-news/trai-takes-strong-action-to-combat-spam-calls-in-india-over-13-000-entities-register-for-message-traceability-measure-11732110716870.html on spam calls in general, but it turns out it may be a very conscious choice on the part of India’s lenders. 

A bit surprising, yes? I mean, why would lenders suddenly decide to stop cashing in on the considerable demand for credit cards in India? They certainly had no qualms these past several years; total outstanding credit cards doubled to nearly 107 million between October 2019 and October 2024. 

But things seem to have started slowing down in the recent months. According to the Reserve Bank of India’s (RBI) monthly releases https://www.rbi.org.in/scripts/ATMView.aspx?atmid=159, an average of ~911,ooo new credit cards were issued every month in 2022, which rose to around 1.2 million in 2023. But for the ten months ending October 2024, this monthly average has dipped by more than 40% to ~739,000. 

Of course, the foremost reason for lenders being careful is a decision the RBI took in November last year, when it increased https://www.businesstoday.in/personal-finance/news/story/rbi-increases-risk-weight-on-consumer-credit-exposure-of-banks-nbfcs-405984-2023-11-16 the risk weight on credit-card receivables of banks and NBFCs. For banks, it was raised from 125% to 150%, and for non-banks from 100% to 125%. This essentially meant that lenders would have to set aside more capital for their credit-card receivables—a bummer of sorts, for sure. 

But there’s more going on, as is evident from the trajectory of two lenders which rely heavily on credit cards—RBL Bank and SBI Card.

An early realisation

RBL Bank is the only lender in India that has voluntarily slashed its overall credit cards in circulation. Here’s why that’s interesting.

RBL is India’s fifth-largest credit issuer. Nearly 25% of its loanbook consists of credit cards, according to a senior RBL executive. But between July and October this year, its total outstanding credit cards fell by 106,045 units. Basically, the bank wiped out 2% of all its credit cards in circulation.

“Every month, some cards go out of circulation and some new ones are issued. It’s not as if the bank is not issuing new cards. The total outstanding figure is the net of discontinued cards and new ones. In the last few months, the bank has issued fewer new cards compared with discontinued ones,” said the senior banker.

There are two major reasons behind this fall in card count: first, a larger-than-usual proportion of RBL Bank customers availed themselves of credit cards but didn’t activate them. As per RBI guidelines, a credit card customer is required to make a transaction within the first 30 days of the card being issued. In case they don’t, the bank reaches out to the customer  with a request to activate. If there’s still no response, the card is closed on the 37th day following the date of issuance.

But the second reason is more pertinent, I believe. RBL Bank, the senior executive told me, has realised that the income these credit-card customers generate is no longer as lucrative compared to the cost of acquiring and servicing them. 

Lenders are only interested in customers who revolve their dues or convert them into monthly instalments. Because that’s where the interest income lies. But in the last year, the bank has noticed a drop in the asset quality of its credit-card customers, and the amount spent in collecting the dues is making the interest income less and less attractive. 

While RBL Bank’s gross NPA ratio improved to 2.88% in the September quarter compared to 3.12% in the same quarter of the previous year, credit card-related NPAs have gone up, according to the banker. 

And not just for RBL Bank.

“… (even) young millennials are using the entire limit and directly defaulting and turning into an NPA without even revolving the loan.”

Suresh Ganapathy, research analyst with Macquarie, to The Economic Times https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/wealth/spend/credit-card-defaults-on-the-rise-what-is-the-punishment-for-credit-card-default/articleshow/113690164.cms?from=mdr

RBL Bank, the banker told me, is now alright with lower-than-industry credit-card growth and wants to focus on the quality of its customers. So far this year, credit cards outstanding for all lenders combined has grown at about 7.4%, while for RBL Bank that number is around 1.2%.

And it isn’t just issuance rates that seem to be slowing in the credit-card space, which brings us to the second lender I mentioned in the beginning.

A few other reasons

SBI Card is India’s second-largest credit card issuer after HDFC Bank. It is the only listed pure-play credit-card issuer in India. 

For the quarter ended September, the company posted a 32.9% drop in net profit compared to the same period last year. Mostly because its credit costs rose by 30% year-on-year, squeezing margins. Even its market share suffered, dropping from 19.2% to 18.5% year-on-year, while its customer spending share declined from 18% to 15.7%. 

It shares one reason for its underwhelming numbers with RBL Bank—its asset quality has suffered. As of the September quarter, SBI Card’s gross NPAs increased to 3.27% from 3.06% in the previous quarter.

Second, high-spending, good-quality credit card customers are mostly concentrated in tier-1 cities. According to a Mumbai-based analyst who tracks the sector, SBI Card’s largest base of customers come from tier-2 and tier-3 towns. The lender has been trying to break into the wealthier circles of tier-1 cities, but that’s not worked very well so far. The result is that SBI Card sees a lot of low-ticket transactions, brings in lower interest income, and loan collection costs are high. 

Third, it’s becoming clear that co-branded credit cards are the up-and-comers of the moment. From a current market share of about 10%, co-branded cards are expected to comprise about 25% of the credit-card market within the next four years, according to a report https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/tech/technology/cobranded-credit-cards-to-form-25-of-market-by-fy28-report/articleshow/112814981.cms?from=mdr by fintech Hyperface and consultancy firm Redseer. 

And that’s another area SBI Card is lacking in. Its customer profile and geographical footprint has hindered its appeal among premium brands seeking affluent, urban customers for co-branded partnerships, the analyst told me. The brands seem to think they are better off joining hands with banks with a large base of customers in tier-1 cities.

RBL Bank seems to have grasped this trend in favour of co-branded cards, because despite its caution in the last couple of months, it has also launched a credit card each with Indian Oil Corporation, Mahindra Finance, and DMI Finance.

Perhaps seen in isolation, what’s happening with RBL Bank and SBI Card might not seem all that significant. But taken together, and considering the general slowdown in new issuances, are we seeing the curtains come down on India’s credit-card frenzy?

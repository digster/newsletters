Edition 26
Sunday, 31 December 2023

Good morning. How poetic is it that the last Sunday of 2023 is also the last day of 2023?

The last few weeks of the year have been magical in Bengaluru. The belated winter is finally here.

It isn’t the bone-chilling winters of North India (I know, I grew up in Delhi) where you need to bring out your woollens and jackets. On the contrary, we still switch our fans on at night.

Instead, it’s been a fortnight of crystal-clear blue skies, cold and crisp air, and bright sun that hits your face at the slanting angles that makes every hour look like a version of the golden hour.

So much so that some of us at The Ken have fallen into this wasteful habit of walking down to the nearby Blue Tokai after lunch for a mid-day fix of caffeine. Our walks are languid and our conversations unhurried.

Enough about me. I hope you’ve had an eventful year.

Over my professional life I’ve now learnt that it isn’t happiness or prosperity that I wish for, whether for myself or my friends and colleagues. That is a by-product, if at all.

Instead, it’s eventfulness and interestingness. Like the often-repeated Chinese curse/blessing (but possibly apocryphal): “May you live in interesting times.”

I have not reached that age yet where I long for sameness and predictability. But I am also past that age where I despised sameness and predictability. Alas, such is middle age.

But I’ve been fortunate that in 2023, I hit walls, discovered paths, and travelled journeys as a founder, writer, parent, husband, friend, and individual.

I even managed to fulfil a long-held desire to create a sealed terrarium. Here it is, on my home desk.

I love the idea of trying to recreate a miniature version of a closed-loop ecosystem, complete with moisture cycles, inside of a glass jar. (A few folks have already told me I made a rookie mistake of adding too much soil, especially for a sealed terrarium. I hope to correct that with my second project.)

But one of the most deeply rewarding of my 2023 projects has been the First Principles Sunday newsletter. I absolutely love what we’ve got going here and am going to give it my best in 2024.

But it’s more fun doing it together! Thus, 2024 is going to be a community-centric First Principles year. The common theme running across most new ideas and innovations you will see in this newsletter (but also, podcast) is Community.

It’s a community that already brings together a few hundred thousand talented, diverse, and interesting people across the world together each Sunday. Over topics as eclectic as mental models, books, music, habits, and photographs.

What other themes or topics should we be exploring?

What else could we be doing together as a community?

Send me your ideas over email, whenever you have the time. I’m at fp@the-ken.com

Enjoy your Sunday and last day of the year. Tomorrow is both a new week and a new year!

I’ll leave you with two lists. The first is of our 2023 editions and their lead topics. Perhaps you haven’t read them all. Perhaps there’s a friend or colleague who doesn’t know about us.

The second one is of all the books recommended by the First Principles community in 2023, compiled by my colleague Anushka Mukherjee.

All of us, including Gabru, will see you next year :)

 

Edition 1: “Two Doors”

https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/a-new-sunday-newsletter-from-the-ken/

Most great founders have this “bifocal” ability to see their world using micro or macro lenses, depending on the context. 

One of those bifocal abilities is to look at a major decision that needs to be made, and asking if it is a one-way door or a two-way door. 

One-way doors only allow you to go in, but not come back. In other words, they represent irreversible decisions. Walking through them is thus a tougher decision. 

Two-ways doors and reversible decisions are relatively easier to make, since they can be undone with some minor damage.

Edition 2: “Start from ignorance”

https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/start-from-ignorance/

“The key mental model that I use in my meetings is to start from ignorance. If we knew nothing about the issue at hand—what would be the first steps that we would take and how we would go about solving the problem,” he emailed me back from Amsterdam airport.

…

Ignorance, or “lack of knowledge or information”, is usually assumed to be a bad thing. But that’s a one-dimensional view of it. Ignorance can also free us from pre-existing notions and conventional wisdom.

  ******

Edition 3: “Imagining failure”

https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/imagining-failure/

He told me his favourite mental model was dreaming about failure and then making peace with it.

“Making peace with the worst-case outcomes is important to keep taking bets and to ensure you don’t shy away from them. Or else you’ll keep getting disappointed (when you fail), and then you’ll reduce the number of bets you make. Everything I say and do internalises that. ‘This is the worst that can happen. Can you make peace with it?’,” said Nithin. 

  ******

Edition 4: “The best decisions are not always logical”

https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/the-best-decisions-are-not-always-logical/

“This is going to sound counterintuitive,” he said, “but the best decisions are not logical.”

Because then they’d sort of be obvious, and hence easy, and hence more likely to lead to paths taken by others?

Yes, he replied. “We have to learn to trust our instinct and gut.”

  ******

Edition 5: “Failing fast, cheap, and forward”

https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/failing-fast-cheap-and-forward/

Vineeta told me that Sugar tries to encourage more of its employees to see failure as a prerequisite for success. “Every year we collate over 150 failure stories, and then publish 25-30 into a book,” she says. 

Why? “Because organisations and Indian culture make it harder for people to accept failure. And they try to avoid failing.”

  ******

Edition 6: When everyone brings data, in who do you trust?

https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/when-everyone-brings-data-in-who-do-you-trust/

 

“I talk to customers. It’s a simple First Principle, but a lot of times, companies forget about it. The idea isn’t to understand the how, but the what or why. As organisations get larger, they start to have large teams of in-house experts. They give you a filtered view, based often on market research. Internal expertise, in many ways, becomes a bottleneck,” he said.

“Which is why I constantly seek out customers to talk to, usually one per week, to give myself a frame of reference. We all know the difference between a data-driven versus heuristics-driven approach. I believe that anecdotes matter. I use anecdotes to validate. And when data clashes with anecdotes, I always trust anecdotes,” he continued.

  ******

Edition 7: "Invert, always invert"

https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/invert-always-invert/

While we do this, I have observed myself often asking to "Invert". I had first read this mental model from Charlie Munger, who said, "Invert, always invert". Munger used it to poke holes in his investment theses to make them sounder.

I have ended up using them in my entrepreneurship decisions to balance my natural over-optimism. You can simplify the understanding of this model by rephrasing it as "what can go wrong? What am I missing? What would be the opposite of what I am thinking right now?"

This mental model forces my natural optimism with preparation of "stop-loss" and putting checks and balances on bet-size and execution-steps.

  ******

Edition 8: “Don’t build what customers are asking for”

https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/dont-build-what-customers-are-asking-for/

The one principle Amrish says he relies on most frequently at Pine Labs is to not build products that customers are already asking for.

If that sounds counterintuitive, you’d be forgiven for thinking so.

I ask Amrish to clarify. He says many businesses make the mistake of looking to the market and customers to understand what to build. Such an approach will only end up creating me-too products that rivals have already built or can easily copy once you build them.

  ******

Edition 9: “First-person versus third-person decision making”

https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/first-person-versus-third-person-decision-making/

I ask Screwvala to elaborate on what he meant. “I’m talking about placing yourself at the centre of your choices and decision making. In the first person. Most of us place ourselves in the third person. We don’t own our choices,” he says.

I think I’m starting to get his answer. The “first person versus third person” is an important construct for both writers and journalists. The vantage point or perspective of a storyteller changes the way a story is perceived by a reader. The “Rashomon Effect”.

  ******

Edition 10: “Don’t compete”

https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/dont-compete/

“I don’t like the idea of competition. It’s a terrible idea. Because the way it works is that some competitor launches a product, and your reaction is to compete and do the same thing but better,” he said on Wednesday morning as we spoke.

Instead, he told me, his framework was to double down on your own and innate strengths, instead of fixing weaknesses. “Such a framework forces you to think differently. Because it’s unlikely your competitor is playing a game built around your strengths.”

  ******

Edition 11: “Write like you speak. Speak like you think”

https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/write-like-you-speak-speak-like-you-think/

Whether it’s “speaking like you think” in a conversation, or “writing like you speak” in an email, the common thread is being yourself and being self-aware about it.

The feedback loop that exists in both cases allows us to observe ourselves. The moment you feel, “That doesn’t sound like me”, it’s a powerful signal for you to think again about what you’re about to do. It may not be a classic First Principle, but it has helped me communicate my thinking and decisions far more effectively over the years.

  ******

Edition 12: “Subtract. Delete. Forget”

https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/subtract-delete-forget/

There’s scientific research that also suggests that forgetting many things is good for the brain.

…

At our workplaces, subtraction is a powerful decision-making tool. Because it forces us to think about two things very clearly—constraints and outcomes.

Everything we do is bound by constraints, like the hours in a day, the dollars in a budget, the days to launch, or the members in a team. Subtraction makes us focus on optimising the resources we have to generate the best possible outcomes.

  ******

Edition 13: “Don’t fill categories (or checklists)”

https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/dont-fill-categories-or-checklists/

“So, when you launch new products in mutual funds, there are certain categories that Sebi (the markets regulator) allows you to launch in. And one of the ways to think about new products is, ‘let’s fill categories’,” she answered.

Meaning, look at the list of categories that a regulator (or rules maker) has listed out, and then build products to fulfil those.

What does Radhika do instead?

“My point is, let’s build good products. We’ll find a category, or we’ll create a category. And I think that is the difference between scarcity and abundance. One starts from a free space where you’re focused on the consumer first, and one starts from a constrained space. And then, when you start with constraints, you build much less than the constraints.”

  ******

Edition 14: “What do you choose to be bad at?”

https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/what-do-you-choose-to-be-bad-at/

Some of us will come up with answers after thinking on the spot. Those are what I call the “shallow” or “unconscious” bads. We don’t have much conviction in them. They’re the kind of superficial answers most of us have come up with sometime in our careers when someone asks us, “what are your weaknesses?”

But there are some of us who have spent time contemplating what they’re bad at, and more importantly, what they’re okay being bad at. These are the conscious bads.

And counterintuitively, knowing what you’re truly bad at is a liberating experience. And it makes it easier for you to become better at the things you’re good at.

  ******

Edition 15: “Embrace the opposite”

https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/embrace-the-opposite/

“When things are good, I tell my team to kill things that are working. And when things are bad, I tell them to imagine if everything went right, what would you do? That’s my job as CEO,” he said.

What was that? When things are good, he told his team to kill things? Why?

Because, he said, as CEO, it was his job to break the default habits and dominant mindsets inside his organisation. When times are good (for example, when companies are flush with funding or the economy is booming), teams within organisations tend to coast along. They take good times for granted. “Killing things” during such times forced his organisation to rethink from the ground up, for the better.

  ******

Edition 16: “Being a conduit”

https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/being-a-conduit/

“If I have to take a decision, how will I play it back to the stakeholders on whose behalf I am taking that decision? I am responsible on behalf of shareholders and stakeholders. Thus, can I convince them? Is it good for the company? If yes, then I have internalised that (the tough decision).”

…

“Can I be the conduit to answer that question on behalf of shareholders?”

Then it made sense. Ruchi placed herself as not just a guardian of the company’s present and future on behalf of its shareholders and stakeholders, but also as a conduit.

  ******

Edition 17: “Training yourself to listen to yourself”

https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/edition-17-training-yourself-to-listen-to-yourself/

“I can’t put it in words. The closest I can come is perhaps learning to look beyond the mountains, to the horizon,” he replied.

Intuition is a ridiculously notorious reasoning construct to explain. Everyone has a different explanation for it, and no two people can ever agree on how it works. But, there is a reason we also call it “gut feeling”.

…

“Often, my instinct has been guided by principles that may cost or hurt in the short run, but are absolutely right for us in the long run and make us who we are. For example, our focus on quality, customer delight, etc.,” he said.

  ******

Edition 18: “Do you have a capex or an opex mindset?”

https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/do-you-have-a-capex-or-an-opex-mindset/

“Avoid talent that would not give you operating leverage,” he replied instantly. “In everything we do, every business we enter, our focus is to improve revenue per employee. You can’t be a tech business if your revenue only grows with your headcount. So, we stay away from businesses that are operations-heavy, require lots of headcount, or are not tech-friendly. Our revenue per employee has always gone up, and if it doesn’t, we may have to take some hard calls one day. If you think about WhatsApp, it was built with [just] 30 employees and served over 1 billion users. That was solid operating leverage. ”

  ******

Edition 19: “First, stop feeling guilty. Then, decide”

https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/edition-19-first-stop-feeling-guilty-then-decide/

When was the last time you replied with just the word “no” to a request from a colleague, family member, friend, or even a stranger? Chances are, unless they were asking you for something trivial or factual (“do you want sugar in your tea?” or “will you be in Bengaluru next week?”), we rarely say just “no”.

…

Saying “no” is important because decision-making is as much about declining to do things as it is about agreeing to do them. In fact, as one progresses through their lives and careers, deciding what you are not going to do becomes more important than what you will. Because we get better as we do the things we love. 

  ******

Edition 20: “Minimize your regrets”

https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/edition-20-minimise-your-regrets/

“My favourite mental model is that of regret minimisation. Because just asking this question, what will you regret more, just really helps to clarify. What will you do when you look back one year out? Two years out? 10 years out? What will you regret more?”

Amazon founder Jeff Bezos has sworn by this concept too.

But on the face of it, “regret” is a curious emotion to base our decision making upon. Because it implies things went wrong or mistakes were made. They’re the opposite of what we associate leadership and decision making with.

  ******

Edition 21: “Let go of your past”

https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/let-go-of-your-past/

Why do we do this?

Because, it’s hard for us to walk away from what we have built. It could be careers, products, or business models. We just have an incredible bias in favour of preserving and building upon, whether as professionals, teams, or organisations.

But often, the seeds of exponential and non-linear growth lie in places that aren’t already in our paths. It’s why “startups” exist to disrupt old and established incumbents. Because they can build products and business models without being encumbered by the past. They have no “baggage”.

  ******

Edition 22: “Working outcome-backwards”

https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/working-outcome-backwards/

“My own initial career choices were ‘today-forward’. I hated science for a few years in school, so I took up commerce as a reflex. In hindsight, it was a bad choice. Because I am fundamentally a builder. And accounting and finance wasn’t something I ever liked,” she recollects.

Why is outcome-backward better than today-forward, I ask Smita.

It’s because our thinking always depends on the context, she says. When we work today-forward, our context is the present. And that means all the messy constraints, realities, and frustrations that surround us. Our imagination and thinking, thus, tends to become a reflection of that: transactional, conservative, iterative.

Imagining a path backwards from a future outcome frees us. It makes us more creative and unconstrained.

 ****** 

Edition 23: “Creating and embracing dilemmas”

https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/creating-and-embracing-dilemmas/

I used Munger’s quote here to illustrate how decision making is always about alternatives. Do you switch jobs or stay on? Do you start up or aim for that promotion? Do you buy a house or continue to rent?

Often, we pay much less attention to our alternatives. Which means, the above dilemmas get reduced to simplistic binaries devoid of conflict.

Should we switch jobs? Should we start up? Should we buy a house?

To help us make better decisions, we need to bring back the conflict and complexity. We need to actively frame them as dilemmas.

  ******

Edition 24: “Navigating the future”

https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/navigating-the-future/

Whether it be about careers, products, or even organisations, it is the job of the leader to create compelling maps of what the future might look like. Where is the competition? Where are the opportunities? What are the dangers? Which are the allies?

While maps are great to get a sense of what the world ahead of you might look like, navigating it requires a compass.  

  ******

Edition 25: “Value problems more than solutions”

https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/value-problems-more-than-solutions/

In organisations, lack of awareness about circles of competence leads to what Krish referred to as the HIPO problem—Highly Paid Person’s Opinion. In other words, senior leaders going off suggesting solutions to problems they are not competent on.

…

This is where we stumble upon the important corollary—our bias for solutions over problems.

When his senior colleagues point out problems in other functions at Chargebee, Krish says he respects that.

But, respecting someone for pointing out a problem should not imply getting carried away and also accepting their solution.

I can't think of a story more universal than the Tortoise and the Hare. Remember? "Slow and steady wins the race," we were all tutored early in life.
So when I read today's story on Purplle and its slow-and-steady approach towards growing its beauty retail business in India, I wondered if Nykaa was the Hair. (pun intended)
Purplle predates Nykaa—it was founded in 2011, a year before the latter. And yet, Nykaa, with its long line of luxury beauty products, international brands, and offline presence, is the reigning cosmetics e-tailer today. It is also the largest-funded player—it has Rs 350 crore ($49.1 million) funding in total.
But never forget the Tortoise. Purplle recently got an infusion of $38 million from Goldman Sachs and Verlinvest. And just FYI, Goldman Sachs previously held stakes in Korean cosmetics major Carver, before the company was sold for $2.7 billion to Unilever in 2017.
Not just that. Purplle's revenue has grown 3X, its losses are down 70%. And it has gotten there on the back of its "value for money" segment.
As Vandana puts it, Purplle—itching to call it Turtlle—realised that there was "an aspirational demand to look beautiful without emptying one’s wallet."
Enter Good Vibes, a range of affordable skincare products; and cosmetics brands StayQuirky and NYBae, Purplle's cheaper alternative to Lakme and Maybelline.
Nykaa, meanwhile, is running ahead with high-end brands such as Hermes Paris, Etude House, Laneige, and Nyx, and with celebrity tie-ups with Bollywood actors Katrina Kaif and Janhvi Kapoor.
Right around the corner, though, are the gods of horizontal e-commerce. Flipkart and Amazon. Can the Hare or the Tortoise cross this finishing—in more ways than one—line?

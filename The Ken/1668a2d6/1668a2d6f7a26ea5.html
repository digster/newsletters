Good Morning Ishan,
 
I’m usually chirpy about Friday but today is a tough day. I hope you have been following the developments in the case of Jamal Khashoggi. A journalist with the Washington Post who disappeared from the Saudi embassy in Istanbul on 2 October. After weeks of investigation, it is now certain that Khashoggi is dead. Several news reports confirm that Khashoggi was killed inside the Saudi embassy and his body dismembered. God bless his soul. 
 
Anytime something like this happens, irrespective of where you live in the world, it just shakes your confidence.
 
Back in India and back at The Ken, we’ve had a busy, working week. Today’s piece is from my colleague Harveen. She says that things have come to a head between private sports broadcasters like Star and Sony and the government-owned Prasar Bharati, which runs the Doordarshan network. Just days back, the information and broadcasting (I&B) ministry proposed an amendment to the decade-old Sports Broadcasting Signals (Mandatory Sharing with Prasar Bharati) Act, 2007. The Act requires private sports broadcasters such as Star India and Sony to share their live feeds of sporting events that are of national importance with Prasar Bharati, which the latter retransmits on its own terrestrial and DTH networks. With the new amendment, DD can air these on private cable and DTH platforms as well, directly cutting into subscription and viewership of private sports channels.
 
National importance is a term best used to obfuscate genocide.
 
In business, it is to obfuscate incompetence. 
 
This bit is relevant from Harveen’s story: “In the absence of a clear definition of what constitutes national importance, multiple industry executives indicated that the central government, which notifies such events, is abusing the Act’s provisions. According to them, the government often selects events that are less about public interest and more in line with improving DD’s viewership and advertising potential.”
 
Today’s piece is sharp, timely, well reported with a clear point of view. You can read it here; it's free: https://the-ken.com/story/sports-mandatory-sharing-act/
 
I didn’t get a chance to wish you. So, happy Dussehra. I’ve often thought about this good and evil thing, and am not sure if I’ve been able to make up my mind on it. I will, however, say this, I hope you win. I hope you are happy. 
 
Have a great day.

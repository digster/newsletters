[India’s e-bus dream is finally coming to life. The power grid can’t keep up](https://the-ken.com/story/indias-e-bus-dream-is-finally-coming-to-life-no-one-told-the-power-grid/?utm_source=weekly_email&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=weekly_recap_card)
From Bengaluru to Mumbai, cities are adding e-buses faster than they can wire up depots. And neighbourhoods are feeling the drop
India plans to put 50,000 electric buses on the road by 2030, but lags in upgrading grids to sustainably power them. The result is an ad-hoc arrangement: buses plug into the same 11kV distribution lines meant for homes and corner shops. The strain is already visible on the ground. Without better planning, India’s clean mobility push could swap tailpipe emissions for voltage fluctuations and brownouts. [The price Havells paid to become a household name](https://the-ken.com/story/the-price-havells-paid-to-become-a-household-name/?utm_source=weekly_email&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=weekly_recap_card)
Keeping dealers and customers happy hurts the AC maker’s profitability. An imminent energy-rating change and a weak summer fused it out
Havells reported a 5% increase in revenue in the September quarter, but its AC segment, Lloyd, saw an 18% decline from the year ago. Lloyd’s profitability has been under pressure for some time now, thanks to the brand’s soft corner for dealers and customers. Since Havells acquired Lloyd, the total capital employed in the brand stands at over Rs 3,000 crore. Yet, it has remained the third-largest AC player in India—the same position it held before the acquisition. But its first major hurdle is to get over its huge pile of unused inventory, which could soon be branded not saleable.

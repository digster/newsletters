“Nobody liked the pool service; not the drivers, not the passengers, not even Ola and Uber themselves.”

This statement from our story has to be the best-kept secret of the shared mobility business in India.

Ride-hailing companies sold the idea of sharing cab space with strangers as a way to transform urban transportation, to fix congestion and pollution in megacities. To acquire market share. 

Consumer behaviour towards such services is bound to change after the lockdown lifts; and executives from these companies admit that Covid-19 is “a huge threat” to a category that was struggling even before the pandemic. Shared rides roughly constituted 10% of Ola’s overall GMV in India. They also comprised only 15% of Ola’s total rides, lagging behind Uber’s worldwide average of 20%.

Competitors moved in rapidly to fill the space. Those like Quickride and Redbus’ rPool—carpooling apps for anyone who owns a car—all-electric cab company Lithium, and office cab aggregators like Move-in-Sync and Routematics are slowly capturing the market.

While the new ones made it a B2B service, Ola and Uber actively discouraged corporates from using the pool service. Nor did they expand the service beyond a few select cities.

Like ride-hailing companies elsewhere, today Ola and Uber have retooled their services for the pandemic, offering their rides to healthcare workers during the lockdown or off-hours. But once the curb on movement lifts, how will ride-hailing, money-losing companies look at the pool—will a post-Covid world see the end of Ola’s and Uber’s pool ambitions? Or will they bring it back in a new avatar? 

Pranav Balakrishan and Pranav Shankar try to get some answers in the story today.

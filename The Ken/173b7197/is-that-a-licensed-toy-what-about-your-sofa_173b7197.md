Is that a licensed toy? What about your sofa?
Rohin
India attained independence in 1947. But in less than a decade it would embark on a disastrous [economic path](https://www.financialexpress.com/archive/redefining-the-hindu-rate-of-growth/104268/). One on which it trudged along for nearly 4 decades before being forced to abandon it in the face of sovereign bankruptcy. The cornerstone of the [“Licence Raj”](https://spontaneousorder.in/india-before-91/) (literally, being ruled by licences) era was a dogged belief in import substitution as a means to become self-sufficient.
|
Life under license raj was characterized by scarcity of resources. The choices people had available to them in their day-to-day life were very limited. For example, cars were available in one colour, and only two to three brands of cars or scooters existed in the market to choose from. The specialty of License Raj was that licenses were themselves made a commodity, and a scarce one, at that. Hence, if a company wanted to expand production, it needed a license to do so, which was not easily available. A ‘market’ for licenses developed; licenses had a price. Business competition under the License Raj meant getting licenses before your competitors. Often businesses acquired licenses, not to produce, but to stop the other from expanding. As Dhirubhai Ambani said, the art of managing government relationships was most critical to business success.
|
|
|
India’s “Licence Raj” ended in 1991 when it was forced to pledge 20 tonnes of its gold and agree to reform its economy. The years since 1991 have been [India’s finest](https://www.cato.org/publications/policy-analysis/twenty-five-years-indian-economic-reform), in terms of economic growth.
Now, India stands at the abyss of a second Licence Raj. And once again, it seeks import substitution; once again, for self-sufficiency.
Last week, the government mandated that anyone who wanted to import a colour television [would need a licence](https://timesofindia.indiatimes.com/business/india-business/government-licenses-tv-imports-to-support-local-production/articleshow/77274188.cms). Today, [Economic Times reported](https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/news/economy/foreign-trade/building-atmanirbhar-bharat-licences-likely-for-furniture-toys-and-sports-gear-imports/articleshow/77324296.cms) that the list was already growing to include “special furniture, air-conditioners, leather, footwear, agro-chemicals, ready-to-eat food, steel, aluminium, copper, textiles, electric vehicles, auto components, TV set-top boxes, CCTVs, sports goods, ethanol & bio-fuels, and toys.”
|
The government had launched the Atmanirbhar Bharat Abhiyan, or self-reliant India programme, in May as part of a strategy to reduce dependence on imports and revive the economy in the wake of the Covid-19 pandemic.
|
|
|
Import substitution for national self-reliance. If it walks like a duck, quacks like a duck, then it must be a… [duck](https://www.business-standard.com/article/opinion/licence-raj-returns-120080200967_1.html)? From today’s editorial in the Business Standard.
|
It is inexplicable that all the lessons as to why import licensing is counter-productive have either been forgotten or ignored by the Union government. It not just reduces the pressure to be competitive and efficient and extracts consumer surplus from the great mass of the Indian people to transfer to inefficient producers, but also provides ample opportunity for corruption. Lobbying for placing some goods and not others on the restricted list will become a priority. Others will struggle to gain one of the import licences themselves, which will become lucrative ways to essentially earn arbitrage profits. Even by the standards of protectionism, tariffs always outweigh quotas and licences as a method of restriction. And tariffs themselves do not solve the other problems. It is clear that the Union government has set out to undo 25 years of openness. But it should remember that growing openness is what provided prosperity and growth to India.
|

Pay up, Facebook and Google!
Rohin
“Freedom of the press is guaranteed only to those who own one,” said the late American journalist AJ Leibling.
In India, the largest of those presses are owned by the Times Group, which is by far not only the country’s largest media group but also its most profitable one. The crown jewels of the group are Times of India, the world’s most read English daily, and the Economic Times, the world’s second most read English business daily. Both have spewed handsome revenues and profits for decades for their parent.
Not enough though, because things are bad these days. Even for the Times Group. Which might explain this editorial it carried yesterday.

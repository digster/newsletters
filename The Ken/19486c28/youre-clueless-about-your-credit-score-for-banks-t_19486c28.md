Good Morning Ishan,
There is one credit card bill from my past that I’ll never forget. I received it in July 2007. While paying it off, I keyed in the wrong payment amount and was US$0.31 short. The following month, my FICO score, which is similar to India’s Cibil, dropped by 80 points. It felt unfair, but at least I knew what tanked my score.
That awareness isn’t as available to consumers in India, who can do everything right and appear to be in sound financial health, but still be deemed high-risk borrowers for reasons that are unknown to them.
Today’s story starts with Purav, an engineer who earns Rs 60 lakh each year and, by all accounts, has a bright future ahead of him. But he can’t seem to secure a bank loan. In fact, he’s been turned down six times.
The reason? His credit score is below average. Purav had no idea why he wasn’t creditworthy, especially given his stable income and what he thought was a stellar financial record.
Four credit bureaus are approved to compute credit scores in India: Transunion Cibil, Experian, Equifax, and CRIF High Mark. Knowing their names, however, doesn’t get you any closer to understanding how they determine whether you can take out a loan. “There’s a large black hole around credit scores,” one banker told The Ken.
Purav isn’t an outlier. For today’s story, Rounak spoke to eight people who found out their credit scores were low even though they didn’t commit major financial transgressions. Meanwhile, bankers told us how these ratings are shrouded behind opaque evaluation processes, and that improving one’s credit score is often a slow, gruelling process.

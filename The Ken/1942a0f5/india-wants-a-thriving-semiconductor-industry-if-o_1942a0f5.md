Good Morning Ishan,
Allow me to ask a question: Name one fabless semiconductor company.
I bet you’d name either Nvidia or Arm if you are closely or remotely associated with the tech world or follow the news. The former is the poster child of the AI boom, the latter of the mobile revolution.
Very few of you would be able to name any Indian semiconductor company.
It’s not that they don’t exist in India. On the contrary, at least 220 such companies operate in India, either as independent businesses or captive centres of global companies. But you are perhaps scratching your head for their names not because they are far removed from our everyday life, but because they are teeny-weeny by revenue.
To address this issue of scale, when the design-linked incentive (DLI) scheme of the US$10 billion-Indian Semiconductor Mission was introduced in 2021, it aimed to “nurture” 100 chip-design companies over five years and ensure at least 20 of them reached a turnover of Rs 1,500 crore.
Well, the largest and most notable of them, Saankhya Labs, was acquired by the Tata-owned Tejas Networks in 2022 after struggling to stay afloat for some time. Its revenue then was Rs 90 crore.
But the DLI so far has “benefited” just about 12 companies. Its modest budget of Rs 1,000 crore is just one of the issues.
Tomorrow, on 4 January, Union electronics and IT minister Ashwini Vaishnaw will kick off a four-day mega event on semiconductors in Bengaluru. I hope everyone there reads Shristi’s story that lays out why DLI cannot be imagined as PLI (production-linked incentives) and why chip designers have a “design” issue with the incentives.

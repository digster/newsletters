The life of most digital companies today is held in tension by two opposing forces: make money, but not lose market share; grow but not burn more money. India’s largest digital payments company Paytm epitomises this. Its board’s latest message: “Don’t lose territory to PhonePe”.
At the same time, post-WeWork IPO fiasco, SoftBank’s (also an investor in Paytm) message is: focus on profitability.
Now, that must be hard for this shape-shifting company which burnt hundreds of millions of dollars to reach a pole position only to risk ceding it, thanks to tech and regulatory changes. It’s dipping its toes in every conceivable digital service—wallet, banking, e-commerce, insurance, lending, advertising, travel, content, gaming…
If you’ve attended a big fat Indian wedding, you wouldn’t have missed a certain Mr X or Ms Y piling on their plate —from desi starters to western main course—driven either by FOMO or let’s-see-what-sticks. Paytm’s palette of offerings looks kinda similar. From the outside, I must add. (Paytm founder* and CEO Vijay Shekhar Sharma declined to meet, even for a no-agenda, no-story meeting.)
But Arundhati has reported like a pro. Paytm is now borderline profitable. Revenue is trickling in from other sources like Paytm Games where its earnings grew 59X in six months. Custom products for content company ByteDance are adding to transaction volume. And so on.
In short, the grand design Paytm is putting together in India is very similar to what its largest backer Ant Financial, the financial service affiliate of Alibaba did back in China. Ant has even helped Paytm with growth hacks. While Ant Financial expects an encore of its performance with Paytm, there are some fundamental differences that won’t swing things in Paytm’s favour.
Much is riding on what Paytm manages to pull off. As a rival says, “If Paytm doesn’t succeed, it will affect India’s whole fintech narrative.”

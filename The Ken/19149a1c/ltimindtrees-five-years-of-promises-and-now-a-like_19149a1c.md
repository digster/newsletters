Good Morning Ishan,
LTIMindtree became part of the Nifty 50, the benchmark stock market index that comprises India’s largest companies by market capitalisation, in 2023. The firm is known for being a product of the first hostile takeover of a tech company in India when conglomerate Larsen & Toubro absorbed Mindtree, an IT-solutions provider.
It may lose that status after August.
The list changes frequently, as market caps ebb and flow. But LTIMindtree’s case is special, as Abhirami says in today’s story from India. The company looks like a juggernaut, but revenue growth has been mostly flat, appreciating only a little over 7% in the 2024 fiscal and missing its US$5 billion top line target by around Rs 7,000 crore (US$833 million). The company’s stock price, too, has underperformed over the past year.
The fact is LTIMindtree hasn’t been able to elbow its way up to hold its own against names like Tech Mahindra, Infosys, and Wipro. A question emerges: would LTI and Mindtree have created more value for shareholders if they hadn’t merged at all?
Abhirami dived into the matter. Don’t miss her [story](https://the-ken.com/story/ltimindtrees-five-years-of-promises-and-now-a-likely-nifty-50-exit/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter).
Elsewhere, we look at a cost that newly converted electric vehicle owners often overlook when weighing their transition.
Getting insured as an EV driver in China has become costly—and complicated. The annualised premium for a run-of-the-mill BYD electric sedan sets back a driver by 3,800 yuan, or close to US$530. But coverage for a comparable car that burns fossil fuels can be as much as 80% lower.
There are a range of factors that explain this, from EVs requiring costly repairs even for minor incidents, to actuarial models being unable to take into account the changing designs of this class of vehicle.
Beyond China, the issue is prevalent in other markets where EVs are becoming the go-to choice for anyone who buys a new car.
Insurers in the country have been hiking premiums, but this vertical is still deep in the red. It’s an uncomfortable situation that isn’t tenable for either service providers or customers and reflects the growing pains that more markets will experience as they put more electric cars on the road.
Lu has the full story. [Check it out](https://the-ken.com/story/you-can-buy-an-ev-in-china-but-can-you-afford-to-insure-it/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter).

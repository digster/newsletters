Good Morning Ishan,
Zoho is what some in India call hatke, or different.
Breaking away from the convention that Software-as-a-Service (SaaS) firms function only in big cities, it established centres in rural areas. This gambit paid off nicely both for folks in and around such areas and for the company, too. Also, unlike many of its peers, Zoho has stayed bootstrapped and unlisted since it was founded in 1996—keeping itself away from the pressures of show-me-the-returns investors and quarterly earnings calls.
It helped that the SaaS major has never been loss-making. This, despite keeping prices low, at least relatively, for its clients. Oh, and its founder, Sridhar Vembu, does not believe in resorting to what’s become the rage these days—lay-offs.
Turns out, Zoho has also brought its hatke approach to the zeitgeist of our times—artificial intelligence.
You see, integrating AI in ops can be expensive for businesses. SaaS firms that depend on the likes of Open AI, Anthropic, and Meta have to pay an “AI tax”—code for high costs. Not very different from the “cloud tax” they pay providers like Amazon, Microsoft, and Google. Now, opting out of these taxes is not really an option for most SaaS companies. But, you guessed it, Zoho has chosen to walk it alone and do it differently with its own large language model, like it did with its own data centres. That’s right—its own homegrown AI solution.
Why is Zoho doing what it is doing? How? Is this my-way-is-a-different-way leading to the right destinations? Are Zoho’s clients on board the bus? Read Abhirami’s fine [story](https://the-ken.com/story/zohos-llm-isnt-trying-to-win-the-ai-race-its-trying-to-survive-the-saas-war/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter) to find out.
What happens when AI at work isn’t optional anymore? Across the board, companies are investing in AI tools for their teams. But with that access comes a new kind of pressure to work smarter, move faster, and think more creatively. So what does that actually look like in practice? And how does it feel on the ground? In the latest episode of 90,000 Hours, Razorpay CEO Harshil Mathur, The Ken‘s Deputy Editor Arundhati Ramanathan, and others break it down.

Stop hating on China. Embrace it. For Indian electronics manufacturers eager to move up the value chain, tying up with Chinese companies is becoming inevitable

Good Morning Ishan,

You are reading our Premium subscriber-only newsletter Make India Competitive Again—a weekly commentary on what India should do to thrive in the chaos unleashed by Trump 

I was busy demolishing desserts on a long-overdue vacation when US President Donald Trump decided to play roulette with the global trade order. I had no option but to check emails and make calls to understand an event the media was treating like every Marvel movie release combined—total information overload. 

If that wasn’t enough, there was the massive Rs 23,000 crore performance-linked incentive (PLI) scheme the Indian cabinet approved for electronic component makers at around the same time. 

As a journalist covering the manufacturing beat, it has been a mad, hectic April. 

No doubt, companies in the sector feel the same way. 

If there’s something Trump has brought back to the world economy, it is the rapid shrinking of the definition of “short-term”. Forget weeks or months, policies can now flip in a matter of days, if not hours. And manufacturers understand the need to strike new alliances (or renew old ones) if they are to emerge from the chaos in better shape than they went in. 

The phones have been buzzing non-stop, said one independent consultant who works in the sector. Several Indian manufacturers are now on the lookout for technology partners and opportunities for joint ventures.

But India has, for a while, tried quite hard to avoid one particular partnership that could do much to boost Indian companies up the value chain. 

China. 

The reluctance isn’t surprising, given the history. But consultants and industry executives now say it may not be long before it is unavoidable.

If you can’t beat them…

For all the government’s efforts to wean India’s companies and its market from Chinese dependencies, and dent China’s dominance in the $4 trillion global electronics supply chain, nothing much has come of it.

In recent times, interest in partnering up with Chinese firms only seems to have increased among Indian enterprises. For instance, Dixon Technologies, India’s largest electronics contract manufacturer, joined hands with Hong Kong-based HKC earlier this year to manufacture display modules. It is also looking to sign them on for its upcoming display fabrication plant. Others such as Syrma SGS, Micromax, and Zetwerk are primarily looking at Chinese companies to establish strategic partnerships, Moneycontrol reported https://www.moneycontrol.com/news/business/companies/indian-firms-race-to-forge-china-ties-ahead-of-rs-25-000-crore-electronics-component-incentive-scheme-rollout-12977644.html earlier this month.

In this newsletter’s inaugural edition last week, my colleague Seetharaman wrote about how India’s efforts https://the-ken.com/newsletter/make-india-competitive-again/make-in-india-is-a-tariff-war-sticker-job/ to prop up manufacturing has neither increased its share of global economic output nor improved employment. Of course, that doesn’t mean it’s a sector that can be ignored. But how, exactly, are Indian manufacturers supposed to take advantage of shifting tariffs and trade relationships? Is there a different lens India can bring to bear on its electronics sector ambitions? 

“Frankly, they (China) are ahead of us in terms of the technology and maturity they have in the manufacturing processes,” said Shrinivas Kulkarni, chief financial officer of Cyient DLM, an original design manufacturer of aerospace, defence, and medical components. Right now, the company’s supply chain depends largely on Israel, with less than 1% being directly sourced from China. But Kulkarni says Cyient DLM can’t rule them out for too long. 

“For consumer electronics and auto components, the industry is very China-heavy.”

Shrinivas Kulkarni, chief financial officer of Cyient DLM

The need to find reliable partners and suppliers—whether Indian or foreign—is all the more urgent after Trump’s tariff shake-up. And while Indian manufacturers are looking to countries such as Vietnam and South Korea, Chinese firms are very much on everyone’s radar. 

The advanced manufacturing that Chinese firms bring to the table is hard to ignore, according to a senior executive in an Indian consumer electronics and AC components maker. The mobile phone, for instance, is a very challenging product to make, but China has managed to line up all the necessary ingredients—skilled labour, a deep and scalable parts ecosystem, raw materials. 

“Whatever challenges you can imagine in any product, they are there in the smartphone. But China has mastered all of them.”

That makes Chinese electronics firms a very lucrative option for Indian manufacturers to partner with, especially for those aiming to move up the value chain.

Competitive collaboration

In fact, this was exactly how China scaled up its manufacturing capabilities. 

“Several years ago, compressor manufacturers in China—like Highly—had a joint venture with Japan’s Hitachi. Similarly, GMCC tied up with Toshiba. But by now, Chinese companies have invested in improving those technologies and have grown their operations and expertise,” pointed out the senior manufacturing executive. 

This is something that played out in the automobile sector too. At one point, Chinese firms sought technology partnerships with European manufacturers. Now, that script is flipping.

“In July of [2023], Volkswagen announced a $700mn investment in Chinese EV maker Xpeng, giving it a 5 per cent stake and a seat as an “observer” on its board. The following year, the two companies said they would jointly develop “intelligent connected vehicles” for the Chinese market.

Hundreds of VW engineers have been working with Xpeng in Guangzhou and Hefei, learning first-hand from the Chinese group’s expertise in developing smart driving architecture. 

The project is one of a flurry of similar tech-focused tie-ups and joint ventures, including between Mercedes-Benz and Hesai, a developer of laser-based object detection systems, Stellantis and Leapmotor, Tencent and Toyota and BMW has with Huawei.”

Europe helped teach China to make cars. Now the tables are turning, Financial Times https://www.ft.com/content/9de3f441-5288-4a0a-afed-09b811475d93

Industry executives I spoke to say this is a viable model for a country like India, which is looking to increase domestic value addition in the electronics supply chain. Currently, that figure is between 15–20%, depending on which dataset or report https://www.financialexpress.com/business/industry-insights/indias-pli-scheme-for-electronic-components-a-game-changer-for-local-manufacturing-and-global-ambitions/3803251/ you read.  

Let’s face it, cheap labour and low-cost manufacturing is no longer a defensible pitch, with serious competitors https://www.escatec.com/blog/outsourcing-electronics-manufacturing-to-asean such as Vietnam, the Philippines, and Thailand now in the field. India has also been struggling to keep its export edge in low labour-cost sectors such as apparel and footwear, with Vietnam and Bangladesh beating us to the punch. According to the World Bank https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2024-09-03/india-losing-to-smaller-rivals-in-manufacturing-world-bank-says, India’s share in global exports of apparel, leather, textiles, and footwear grew from 0.9% in 2002 to a peak of 4.5% in 2013, but then declined to 3.5% in 2022. In contrast, Bangladesh and Vietnam’s share rose to 5.1% and 5.9%, respectively. 

Embracing Chinese companies with significant market share, technical knowhow, and deep pockets as technology partners could help India break out of low-margin, low-value manufacturing. Especially in the area of complex electronic components, which forms over 40% of the $4.3 trillion electronics market. 

But this requires India to move past its reluctance to work with Chinese companies. For instance, the independent consultant noted how processing paperwork in India for Chinese firms can take far longer than for others: “One month versus five months, sometimes.”

In many cases, however, it is already difficult, if not impossible, to avoid Chinese participation in the supply chain. To quote one senior analyst at a global business consulting firm: “Let’s say an Indian company wants to manufacture a bare printed circuit board here. It has two parts: a peg board and the substrate. 90% of these are currently made in China. So completely ruling out China isn’t an option.”

For the executive and many others like them, it’s now a case of “if you can’t beat ‘em, join ‘em”.

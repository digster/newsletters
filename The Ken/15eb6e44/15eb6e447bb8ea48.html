China is everywhere, especially in India. From smartphones to viral mobile apps to medicines to telecom equipment to Diwali decorations to crackers to venture capital.
 
Take India’s e-commerce sector for instance. The list of companies with sizeable Chinese investments reads like a veritable Who’s Who: market leader Flipkart, largest e-wallet and e-commerce marketplace Paytm, largest e-grocer BigBasket, largest e-learning provider Byju’s, largest travel booking site MakeMyTrip, etc.
 
Chinese companies and funds have invested close to $2.4 billion in Indian companies since 2016, says Tracxn.
 
The 9 weeks that Indian and Chinese armed forces recently spent jaw-to-jaw in Bhutan surely could not have been easy on these firms. There were sparks of nationalistic outrage from citizens of both countries, with some dangerously tending to the dreaded “B” word - Boycott.
 
Now, China has used boycotts to a devastating effect against countries it feels have slighted their nation - Japan, South-Korea, Norway, Philippines, etc. It’s worth pondering what might have happened if, heavens forbid, things got worse between the two countries in Doklam.
 
The Doklam backdrop and its possible repercussions on India-China trade and Indian e-commerce brands are the focus of our story today. It’s written by Nitin Pai, the co-founder of Takshashila Institution, a centre for research and education in public policy. Nitin is both a sharp observer of geopolitical trends as well as a person unafraid to speak his mind.

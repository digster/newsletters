Kumar Birla takes yet another shot at fixing his spotty record in retail. His group started off big in grocery and apparel, only to become irrelevant

Good Morning Ishan,

When someone mentions the Aditya Birla Group, you think aluminium, cement, and maybe financial services. Certainly very large businesses. Some are quite profitable. But Birla is not a name you associate with consumer brands. 

Not a lot of them at any rate. I don’t want to incur anyone’s wrath by calling the telco Vi, the result of a merger between Vodafone and Birla-owned Idea Cellular, a popular brand. 

The group’s decision to venture into decorative paints in February was partially an attempt at remedying this longstanding problem. Paint was a commodity until Asian Paints came along and created one of India’s most enduring consumer brands; one that’s also a very lucrative business.

Combining an iconic mascot https://theprint.in/feature/gattu-the-mischievous-asian-paints-mascot-who-ruled-indian-hearts-for-over-50-years/481697/ created by the famed cartoonist RK Laxman in 1954 with some memorable marketing campaigns certainly contributed to Asian Paints’ rise. 

But the other, more important factor is what Asian Paints did with distribution. If ITC relied on the tried-and-true formula of taking FMCG products to mom-and-pop stores through distributors (who are exclusive to one company) and wholesalers (who sell brands of multiple companies), Asian Paints chose to upend the way paints were traditionally distributed. It did away with wholesalers and distributors altogether, and started supplying directly to dealers. 

And since the margins dealers get from selling paint is very low, it means they have to keep exhausting their inventory very often. But they can’t buy too much of it all at once given how much space it takes up, so Asian Paints restocks their inventory three-four times a day!

This virtuous cycle has helped Asian Paints gain control of 60% https://www.cnbctv18.com/market/stocks/asian-paints-berger-nerolac-dulux-akzo-noble-shalimar-grasim-jsw-capacity-expansion-market-share-capex-16278611.htm of the organised decorative-paint market—more than 3X the share of its nearest rival, Berger Paints.

Asian Paints’ glorious success is no longer predictable, The Ken https://the-ken.com/tradetricks/asian-paints-glorious-success-is-longer-predictable/

Now, the Aditya Birla Group has chosen yet another business in its quest to build a brand that’s a household name. It opened its first https://www.livemint.com/companies/news/aditya-birla-group-25-bn-revenue-consumer-business-paints-jewellery-11722003141970.html jewellery stores, called Indriya, over the weekend. “This year, we have redoubled our bet on the dynamism of the Indian consumer, by launching two major new consumer brands—in paints and jewellery,” said Kumar Mangalam Birla, chairperson of the group. 

It’s not terribly surprising that the group wants in on Indians’ obsession with gold. Titan is the third-most-valuable Tata company because of Tanishq, which convinced consumers to look beyond their family jeweller. Tanishq now contributes 85% of Titan’s sales and over 90% of its profits.

What’s interesting, though, is how middling the Aditya Birla Group’s track record has been in retail.

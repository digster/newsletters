Friction is a story.
My personal favourite is point G. This is from the Reserve Bank of India’s (RBI) dissent note made public last week. With respect to the Inter-Ministerial Committee for the finalisation of amendments to the Payment & Settlement Systems Act, 2007. Here’s what point G says: There has been no evidence of any inefficiency in payment systems of India. The digital payments have made good and steady progress. India is gaining international recognition as a leader in payment systems. Given this, there need not be any change in a well-functioning system.
Ergo, the Payments Regulatory Board must remain with the RBI and headed by the Governor of RBI.
Another matter altogether that fintech companies would have you believe otherwise. In discussions both public and private, they’ve said RBI is breathing down their neck. My colleague Arundhati who has been following this business closely says the RBI is viewed as many things. Conservative, for one. Opaque on decision making, for two. Tough and an institution which doesn’t truly understand the needs of developing a vibrant payments ecosystem; that’s three. There is obviously more to this which makes it a number too many. And so these companies have been pushing for a regulator for the payments ecosystem that’s independent of the RBI.
They believe the RBI has a regulatory monopoly. It must be broken.
This recommendation to break up payments from the RBI was the result of a 10-month long inter-ministerial deliberation among the who’s who of Indian regulatory superpowers. The Unique Identification Authority of India, Ministry of Electronics and Information Technology (MeitY), Department of Financial Services, Department of Legal Affairs and the RBI itself put together a report calling for changes to the existing decade-old Payments and Settlement System (PSS Act 2007). And the committee just stopped short of calling the RBI what it really meant: ill-equipped.
Some disagree. They say what the RBI brings is autonomy, and that’s different. Good different.

Good Morning Ishan,

A mid-afternoon swim on the rooftop pool. Music streaming in through Bose speakers. A tap down the aisle to dispense draught beer…It’s not Friday musing, in case you are wondering. Arundhati just got back from a WeWork co-sharing place, and she describes it thus.

(I’ll be honest, this sounds out of the world. Maybe also because none of the media offices I’ve worked at were any plush, leave alone this hip.)

It’s the kind of environment startups would thrive in, but WeWork in India is mostly inhabited by corporate employees. It’s true, enterprise membership for the third most highly valued company in the world is now growing faster than startup membership globally, it’s pronounced in India because startups are a teeny-tiny minority among its 10K users. If the place is ‘filled with corporate drones’, it’s by design. To hedge against the unpredictability of startups, especially when WeWork incurs a loss of 10% on every seat.

Valuation like a tech company, roots sunk in real estate, approach like a design studio—WeWork may mean different things to different people, what with life-size branding like WeLive (co-living), WeRise (health and fitness), WeGrow (education). In India, it seems to be a valuation play where occupancy matters more than revenues.

To that end, enterprises are great occupancy enhancers even though most of them today are only making pit-stops at co-working spaces. Capex works out better for large enterprises in the long run than opex. And they are also mindful of the fact that co-working culture isn’t exactly the culture they’ve carefully built at their respective organisations.

In her story today, Arundhati looks hard at the deeply discounted economics of WeWork in India, a joint venture with real estate’s Embassy Group. WeWork is acquiring some of the top grade supply of buildings in the business districts. What plan is at work here?

"I ask not for a larger garden, but for finer seeds." -  Russell H. Conwell 
 
Ask Indian farmers. They’ve watched finer seeds from multinationals increasingly dominate the Indian market, slowly and surely. Now, agriculture in general, and seed market, in particular, are going to see some serious seeds of change being sowed as digital agriculture moves to farms.
 
Germany’s Bayer completed its acquisition of Monsanto in July. It’s now the largest agri company in the world with a control over 30% of the global seed market and a quarter of the pesticide market.
 
In India, Bayer, Monsanto, or both, have been dominant players for branded hybrid seeds of cotton, rice and at least 10 vegetable seeds. Bayer holds 40% of the hybrid rice market in India. In fact, Bayer has among the largest distribution networks in India—about 3,500 field salespeople, 3,000 dealers and 60,000 retailers. 
 
The Big Ag just got gigantic.
 
Where most Indian companies focus narrowly on seeds or chemicals, the multinationals hold a bird’s-eye view of the entire farming sector. And as things stand, India’s small ag isn’t challenging their prowess. No one is even trying, apart from naming their products ‘Jadoo’, ‘Moneymaker’, ‘ATM’.
 
On its part, the competition regulator has laid down a seven-year compliance roadmap, including free access to agro-climatic data, but it’s a market where Big Ag is already winning.

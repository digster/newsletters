Good Morning Ishan,

Tall claims of infallible cybersecurity emanate from every institution worth its salt these days. Banks, social networks, government agencies, you name it. In a society that is increasingly aware of the value of personal data and its potential for misuse when in the wrong hands, every organisation understands the value of data protection and privacy.

Or so it would seem. 

Yet inevitably, we see businesses shown up. The personal data they have so carefully collected compromised. Due to negligence. Due to complacency. At great cost to both the organisation and the individual.

Closer home, India is slowly waking up to these realities. But not soon enough it appears. This is worrying, considering India is home to the world's largest biometric ID system—Aadhaar. If breached, it could be disastrous. And there have already been alleged leaks, giving credence to these fears.

To allay them, UIDAI, the government agency in-charge of Aadhaar, decided last year that every one of its ecosystem partners would have to undergo a systems audit. This would prove UIDAI's claims of security once and for all. It was meant to be reassuring. But my colleague (and today's author) Vivek decided to go deeper. He filed an RTI query to understand how these audits had been done in the past. Turns out, UIDAI didn't know either. They had no records of any such audits. Reassuring? Not so much.

The UIDAI is not the only one to be jolted out of its slumber though. Various sectoral regulators are all waking up to the pressing need for data privacy and protection. But they're all acting independently, their diktats either overlapping or overreaching. Needless to say, this isn't good for business.

Delhi’s about to get its second smog tower. The first one was erected on 4 January, 2020 to battle the lung-lynching levels of air pollution Delhi endures at the onset of every winter. This new tower, a three-way collaboration between IIT-Bombay, TATA projects, and the University of Minnesota, has already [sparked](https://www.hindustantimes.com/cities/work-on-installation-of-smog-tower-has-started-centre-to-sc/story-shlTUUipud8hIOrnePoVdN.html) a political tug-of-war. IIT-Bombay wanted to pull out of the project, but the Supreme Court swiftly shut its escape hatch.
A smog tower is a contraption meant to purify the air around it and reduce the level of particulate matter. This sounds like a useful device. But there are some obvious, and some non-obvious problems with it. I called Santosh Harish, a research fellow at the Center for Policy Research in Delhi and Editor at the Collaborative Clean Air Center, about smog towers and their impact on long-term, anti-pollution policies.
TK: Between planting more trees, washing roads, and house plants that allegedly reduce pollution, are smog towers the worst red herrings of the lot, to use your own words?
SH: It’s hard to compare bad ideas. While the impact of planting trees on air pollution is overstated, at least that can have other benefits for the city. The trouble with a smog tower is that it can seem like a solution, despite no scientific basis.
It looks like governments are making progress, as a physical manifestation of it. It doesn’t impose any new cost on polluters, so it doesn’t leave any interest group worse off and that’s always a plus for governments. It’s a tech-fix, and those are always popular. Since they have never been tried as a serious intervention anywhere, politicians here can claim they are at the cutting edge of innovation.
It’s also incredibly expensive for a method that hasn’t shown any real results anywhere. That’s the most dangerous thing, that it seems like a plausible solution to the general public.
TK: What’s the “science” behind a smog tower?
SH: It doesn’t function very differently from an average air purifier. There is some kind of filtration technology. It will suck in polluted air and release cleaner air. In its proximity, pollutant levels in the air should be lower. But for this to really work, the pollutant levels have to be a lot lower at significant distances from the tower, not just when standing next to it.
TK: Is it easy to measure this?
SH: It’s the hardest thing to attribute a dip in a particular pollutant level to a particular source. There could be various sources. The pollutant levels also change from day-to-day because of weather conditions. To test the tower out, we need to basically measure what the air quality is like with it switched on and off.
TK: So like an RCT (randomised controlled trial) for air quality?
SH: Sort of. Basically, switch the tower on-off on the basis of a randomised schedule to determine the impact on air at different distances. Ideally, there needs to be a benchmark for what the air quality should be at those distances, calculated by the scientists designing this. Then you can figure out if the tower is making any impact or not, and whether the costs are justified. There’s no scientific evidence from any country right now to indicate that it does work.
TK: Has the current project between IIT Bombay and TATA shared such benchmarks? Or any study designs to evaluate the smog tower’s impact?
SH: No, not yet at least. They needed to meet a higher bar for transparency even before the project got started. It’s very opaque.
TK: What do you think will be the second- and third-order effects on policy-making?
SH: Piloting this is a slippery slope and we should not do it. No one is going to wait for the pilot's evaluation results to come out. You are going to have a set of people who’ll claim the tower is a failure. Another group will claim the intervention is going quite well. The evaluation is clearly not an easy exercise. In all this uncertainty, the loudest voice will win the argument.
Independently, other Indian cities may also want to install smog towers. And a large amount of time and money will be wasted before we realise that nothing changed.

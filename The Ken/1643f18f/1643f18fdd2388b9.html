First, the taps dried out.
 
The tourist who brought in revenue was suddenly a social pariah. Warm smiles quickly turned into direct rebukes. The price of drinking water skyrocketed. Schools shut down. Slowly, the town emptied out.
 
This isn’t some dystopian drama on Netflix. This was the summer of 2018 in the erstwhile summer capital and the north Indian hill station favourite, Shimla.
 
Opening lines of our story today. I was witness to this in the last week of May. In hot and dry Shimla, everyone, from cab drivers to hoteliers, made it clear that tourists were not welcome this season. Couldn't blame them. The Shimla Municipal Corporation’s (SMC) website was being updated an unprecedented number of times with water supply timings and phone numbers of valvemen who turn on the municipal water connection.
 
The Shimla water crisis is emblematic of all that ails India’s water supply—drying aquifers, old leaky infrastructure underground and sluggish utility providers that are still not woke enough.
 
Think tank Niti Aayog is ringing alarm bells. By 2030, reads its June 2018 report, 40% of the country won’t have access to drinking water. The demand-supply gap that it has projected should itself evoke a reaction from mild panic to utter horror, but their admission that the estimates suffer from weak data sources must brace you for more disturbing actual gaps.
 
 It’s not that states are not doing anything. They are, but all efforts are skewed towards managing the supply. Because in India’s vast water spaces, pricing and tariff are unsexy terms. “Cities aren’t allowed to bring up tariff revisions for four to five years. When they do, the proposed hike is so much that obviously, the state doesn’t approve it,” to quote from the story.
 
Then there is politics, party politics I mean. Despite that, there are pilot examples that show managing the demand has enormous upsides.
 
Our contributor Olina Banerji has a thorough story on why India’s focus on supply, and not demand, of water, has brought us to this brink and could spell its doom if not corrected.

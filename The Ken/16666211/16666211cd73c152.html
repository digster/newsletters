Sometime towards the second half of 2014, the website zebpay.com went live. It had just one box. An email address to get “early access”.
 
By the first half of 2015, the site went live. "Zebpay is the fastest and easiest way to Buy and Sell Bitcoins in India,” it said. It also featured the following quotes:
 
"Virtual currencies [bitcoins] will certainly get better, much safer and over time will be the form of transaction, that’s for sure.” - Raghuram Rajan, RBI Governor
 
"Millennials trust bitcoin more than FIAT (national currencies).” - Chairman of NYSE, the world's biggest stock exchange
 
If you were considering buying bitcoin, endorsements from the governor of India’s central bank and the head of NYSE seemed like pretty powerful signals.
 
There was also a management team—one that claimed to have been involved in bitcoin since its early days in 2011 and started India’s first bitcoin exchange in 2012. And advisors including Nishith Desai, the founder of one of India’s largest law firms, and Arjun Handa, the vice chairman and MD of an $800 million company, Claris Lifesciences. It boasted offices in Ahmedabad, India and Singapore.
 
"Zebpay is on a mission to upgrade your financial destiny,” it said by 2016. It claimed to have 200,000 users. That number would go on to touch 3 million, as per the company.
 
It was a dream run.
 
And then, on 28th September, Zebpay, one of India’s oldest and largest cryptocurrency exchanges, sent an email to its 3 million users. It was shutting down its exchange operations. Ironically hoisted by their own endorsement petards from the RBI governor and NYSE chairman. Central banks and fiat currency linkages.
 
Why did Zebpay shut down? That’s the question Sumanth and Sidhartha try to answer today. https://the-ken.com/story/zebpay-regulatory-arbitrage-policy-scapegoating/

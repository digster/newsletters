Do you fly kites?

My favourite activity, when I get home early from work, is to look at the sky and watch kites perform—both the paper kind and the birds. I used to think flying kites was something people did only during Makar Sankranti, but in this part of the town, kids have made it part of their everyday routine. Without fail, whether it’s raining or not, at least five or six kites grace the sky with their colourful patterns, often blending with their avian namesakes when they fly too high. 

Their biggest nemesis? Electric poles and tall trees. Many kites have been strangled by the branches, leaving behind the broken hearts of little kids. Some can’t even be retrieved for a proper funeral; you can see them trapped in the lush green every day—forever captive and on display.

If I were to paint that scene, I would caption it “dead dreams”. It’s not as negative as it sounds, because as human beings, we have the potential to always dream new dreams or keep hoping for the old ones to be fulfilled. Either way, every day we dream. For little things, big things. For peace, for love. For our kites to escape when we tug at them.

2. Theme of the month: Christmas

Hello Ishan, at the risk of sounding cliché, I had to pick Christmas as this month’s theme otherwise it would sit in the back of my mind until next December, and I would have to decide once again whether to keep it as a theme or not. 

Anyway, while shopping for my Santee’s gift last week at Bookworm (my favourite bookstore in Bengaluru), I happened upon this shelf. Can you spot the lone Christmas-themed book on it? 

View in Browser

The first book that comes to mind when I think about Christmas is Charles Dickens’ famous classic, A Christmas Carol. It’s actually an odd one out for this theme, because it’s quite different from most books that fall under this category today. 

I look at the recent covers and titles and they all look so merry, even the recent retelling of A Christmas Carol by Joshua Ingle sparkles all over.

For this month, I picked up The Secret Christmas Library by Jenny Calgan, a sequel to last year’s The Christmas Book Hunt. I actually wanted to choose Mary 90: My Very Best Recipes by Mary Berry, but it was way out of my league. I thought it might be a change from the classic Christmas book. 

My chosen pick met all the expectations I had for it: cosy romance (check), lavish meal (check), hopes dashed and revived (which I like to call the resurrection), and some gentle critique on Christmas (Christmas movies can ignore this, but books need to have this take). 

“It’s okay,” she said instead. ‘The idea that Christmas has to be super-happy for everyone was invented by supermarkets to sell you stuff from September onwards. And it makes less happy people even more unhappy. It’s an arbitrary date.”

The “she” here is our protagonist, Mirren, who goes on a hunt inside an old castle to find a rare book that will save its owner from financial ruin. The vibe of the book was like a scene from a Christmas movie. It would make for a fun movie, but I think I am not a big fan of Christmas-themed books—especially if they also try to pass off as mysteries. The predictability of the inevitable resurrection reduces the impact of the ‘conflict’, forcing authors to work extra hard to wrap readers in the mystery being unravelled. 

But one thing that the protagonist said stayed with me was this little nugget:

“That doesn’t matter,” said Mirren. “At Christmas, everything old is new again.”

I couldn’t agree more.

Rachel also picked up a Christmas read, and here’s what she has to say about it.

“The Vanderbeekers of 141st Street is a heartwarming novel about a quirky family of seven who’ve just been told by their reclusive, curmudgeonly landlord that their lease will not be renewed. Considering how deeply connected the family is to their home and community in Harlem, this is devastating news.

It’s a perfect book for children, especially during the holiday season. It’s a reminder of the warmth that can be found with loved ones in tough times. The author is so good at making the reading experience immersive. You feel the children’s heartbreak upon learning that they might have to leave their beloved home right after Christmas. Each of them assumes it’s because of something they did, which is honestly just them being children. You’ll also feel the hopeful resolve, conceived in that special way only children can, when they decide they’ll convince the landlord to let them stay.

Even if the moral of the story is not new—it’s basically “home is where the heart”—the characters are unique and adorable enough that it doesn’t really matter. And for young children, it still feels like a fresh approach.

Also, the hand-drawn images of the houses and the floor plans could be a bridge for children transitioning from picture-heavy books to ‘regular’ books. The familiarity of illustrations is retained, helping readers envision the more detailed parts of the story, though the story stands just as well without them. 

Overall, it’s a very sweet story, perfect for kids who are just starting out as readers. With colourful, engaging characters, a clear moral, easy-to-read language, and an immersive setting, it’s a charming holiday pick.”

As the winter settles in (or the summer sun shines for our friends down south), what’s the book by your side? Share your current cozy read here, and I’ll feature your recommendations for the community.

4. To Santa's rescue

On a cheerful note, the Bengaluru office of The Ken celebrated Christmas early. Our podcast team led the festivities, so you know their enthusiasm and diligence extend far beyond their voices and research. 

It was a really fun evening, and my favourite part was exchanging Secret Santa gifts. Meanwhile, our team in Delhi is also busy brainstorming gifts for their “Santee”, as they are celebrating this week. 

And of course, we have our tree, decked in all its festive glory.

Now, while Secret Santa is a highlight of the season for me, choosing the right gift amid everything else can sometimes feel overwhelming. As someone who has played Santa and struggled to find thoughtful, useful, creative gifts, I think it is my responsibility once again to remind you of the amazing gifting guide we curated this year with your help. It’s a much better starting point than a frantic scroll through a 10-minute delivery app. 

And even if you aren’t part of a Secret Santa, don’t forget to celebrate yourself before the year ends. This is the perfect moment to sit back and reflect as we begin to wind down for the holidays. Once the next year starts, you will forever be busy “planning” for the next thing, so take a moment now and pat yourself on the back for all you have done. Or perhaps, gift yourself something nice.

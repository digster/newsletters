Safety and hygiene come at a cost. They should have a price too

Rohin

As India opens up its shopping malls, theatres, and shops, safety and hygiene measures have become the equivalent of a door for most businesses. If they don’t exist clearly and visibly, no one is going to walk in.

But safety and hygiene measures aren’t cheap. From thermal guns, hand sanitisers and dispensers, to extra cleaning staff, masks and PPEs, businesses are now saddled with a new cost head they’d never accounted for. The “new normal” for salons and restaurants looks somewhat like this.

Source: The Telegraph https://www.telegraphindia.com/india/tip-of-covid-19-iceberg-study/cid/1779564

Source: Twitter https://twitter.com/SiriRph/status/1264927196038258693/photo/1

But how will businesses make the economics work? There are only three ways.

(a)  By paying for these from their own reserves or profit margins

(b)  By charging customers extra

(c)   Some combination of (a) and (b)

In a well-functioning market economy with adequate competition, companies will pick either of these options. And in return, customers will pick who they want to give their business to. 

Except, Indian customers want to have their safety and hygiene cake and eat it too. 

A section of India’s Twitter and WhatsApp user base has been up in arms since the weekend over what they see as usurious overcharging by Naturals, a large beauty salon chain. An image of a bill https://twitter.com/_romilkapoor_/status/1269986144667627522 where a customer was charged Rs 150 (US$2) by a Naturals Salon as “Safety and Hygiene Fee” went viral, because the actual services (a haircut and beard trim) cost just Rs 250 (US$3.3).

The attitude of the average Indian customer towards a salon or restaurant when it comes to sharing part of the brutal economic costs of running a business during Covid-19 is merely a reflection of Indian central and state governments’ “Not our problem, figure it out” motto.

India’s governments keep piling on rules, regulations, and SOPs (standard operating procedures) on its businesses around Covid-19 safety and hygiene, without caring much for the economics of it. As a result, businesses are hit on both ends—higher costs for procurement of additional staff and healthcare products, and the inability to generate additional revenue to cover these costs.

Faced with such odds, many small hotels and restaurants are choosing to remain shut https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/industry/services/hotels-/-restaurants/operating-procedures-a-plateful-small-hotels-may-keep-the-doors-shut/articleshow/76276689.cms.

Not understanding or caring for the economics of running a business is a widespread phenomenon. CK Kumaravel, the co-founder of Naturals Salon, took to Twitter to post this video https://twitter.com/ckknaturals/status/1269532220856782852 in response to his customers saying his hygiene charges were “unjust”. Here’s a largely verbatim transcript of what he said.

Our basic services—haircut, shave, waxing and threading—are called “door busters”. We don’t make money on them, but actually we lose money.

The next set of services—facial, pedicure, hair spa, and hair colouring—we make good money on. And we also make a lot of money on bridal makeup, hair straightening, nail art, and such services. 

Unfortunately, after Covid-19, the customers who are coming to us are only coming for basic services. And our entire business model is gone for a six. 

We have two choices before us. Either we increase the price of our basic services, but I don’t want to do that because that means even after a vaccine or cure is found, the same charges will continue. Instead, I only want to collect the money we are incurring as an extra during this period to ensure the safety of our customers and my staff. That is exactly what we are charging. We are even trying to bulk-purchase to reduce costs further.

All I want to assure you is that we are not here to fleece your money and take advantage of this scenario. This is a genuine cost that we’re incurring, and that is what we’re passing on to you.

We work in a franchise system where more than 400 women entrepreneurs are there, a lot of whom are first-generation, first-time entrepreneurs.

CK Kumaravel, co-founder of Naturals Salon

Why Parle-G’s highest sales ever is worrisome

Arundhati

As long as Indians drink chai (tea), Parle-G, India's (and at one point the world’s) largest-selling biscuit brand is not going anywhere. At Rs 5 (US$0.07) for a 70g packet, it is India's favourite tea-time snack.

But just before Covid-19 struck, India was headed towards an economic downturn, and the harbinger was Parle-G. It was going to cut 10,000 jobs as it stared at falling sales among its rural consumers. 

Our [lower-priced] product has been hit. We’ve tried to do whatever we can do. Tried to push the product, educate the consumer...but the tax [Goods and Services Tax] hike and economic slowdown has impacted sales. This is the first time when there is such a sustained decline in demand for so long.

Mayank Shah, category head, Parle Products, to India Today https://www.indiatoday.in/business/story/parle-g-mayank-shah-1590304-2019-08-22

It was considered a portent of worse times.

Poor people not being able to buy biscuits worth less than 2% of their daily wages – suggests that the economic slowdown is having a particularly severe impact on the poorest population in the economy.

Why rural India can’t afford to buy biscuits, Hindustan Times https://www.hindustantimes.com/india-news/why-rural-india-can-t-afford-to-buy-biscuits/story-ytwoy8zBhiH7bZuoX7QOKM.html

But ironically, India’s lockdown, the worst of times for most businesses, has put the spring back in Parle-G's steps. It saw its highest sales in eight decades during the lockdown and even grew its market share, said Mayank Shah, category head, Parle Products.

"It was primarily because of an increase in demand—consumption was high because people bought more during the lockdown, made new dishes and hoarded food, as a result snacking occasions in India went up," said Shah. "As a result of the high growth Parle as a company has gained market share of over 500 bps," he said.

Shah said that overall biscuits as a category did well, largely as families consumed more packaged snacks at home. Moreover, on-the-move migrants relied on its value packs for sustenance. Parle-G is hugely popular among middle-income households because of its affordable price points.

Parle Products clocks record sales during lockdown, Livemint https://www.livemint.com/companies/news/parle-products-clocks-record-sales-during-lockdown-11591703530132.html

It was also in large part due to its deeply-penetrated distribution. Cue well-meaning, armchair economists seeing Parle-G's rebound as economic revival. 

But what if it’s the opposite? After all, Shah also says:

“This is a common man’s biscuit; people who cannot afford bread – buy Parle-G.”

Un-gathering for elections

Ben

Elections involve hustings, rallies, speeches, and crowds gathering to vote. With Covid-19, that’s no longer possible. Countries that did hold elections in these times had to improvise. Remember how South Korea handled it https://the-ken.com/bfo/21/? The country established new norms for elections:

Temperature checks, frequent disinfection, safe distancing at polling booths
	Masks and gloves became part of campaign attire
	Rallies were replaced with online videos and vans blasting party messages
	People who were sick could vote in separate polling booths

Singapore has now come out with its own guidelines https://www.gov.sg/article/how-voting-will-be-safely-conducted-if-done-during-covid-19-situation for elections during this pandemic. Besides following some of the South Korean measures, Singapore, precise as ever, made plans to add more polling stations so that the number of voters per station can be reduced from 3,000 to 2,400.

Voters will also be assigned a two-hour window in which they are encouraged to vote—another effort to spread out voters across the day, something that wasn’t heard of in previous elections. Before they leave their homes, voters can even check the queue status of their polling stations on a government website.

These guidelines are practical and likely to become the new way elections are held in the future. Safe distancing, frequent disinfections, more polling stations, and assigning time slots are how crowding can be easily dealt with. Democracy calls for people’s voices to be heard as one, even if they stand several feet apart.

Smile like you mean it

Olina

The sales of chewing gum and mints in the United States are down https://www.news18.com/news/india/gum-mints-and-snack-bar-sales-slump-as-us-consumers-order-online-amid-covid-19-crisis-2642149.html 30% year-on-year. As shoppers switched to online groceries, chewing gum and mints—usually impulse buys when waiting in check-out lines—became an unusual casualty of the pandemic.

In India, with paan shops shut during the lockdown, retail sales of Mondelez-owned Halls, Perfetti Van Melle’s Chlormint, Centre Fresh and Mentos, and Nestle's Polo fell. Paan shops are neighbourhood kiosks in India that sell a range of sundry products, from tea to tobacco. A huge retail avenue for mints and gum are also young smokers who usually want to mask their breath after a smoke break. So you see how shut paan shops could lead to a dip in sales for mints and gum.

To step back, the finer point here is about “fresh breath” and the feedback loop of social etiquette. Public life enforces a discipline. Minty-fresh breath seems to be a big part of it.

Mints and gum were primarily sold on the promise of confidence, either through fresh breath or white teeth. This confidence was never directed at self, but at the service of others...Even if you want to be confident inside your house, there's always mouthwash. So, it is understandable that sales have plummeted.

Karthik Srinivasan, communications consultant, to ET Brand Equity https://brandequity.economictimes.indiatimes.com/news/marketing/minty-ing-behind-the-mask/76158372

Basically, mouthwash > mint during the lockdown.

Along with fresh breath, mints and gum helped market a confident smile in public. But what do you do when everyone needs to wear a mask?

Source: YouTube

Go after #MaskBreath

It didn’t take long for brands to put a spin on this. Ice Breakers, a brand of mints and chewing gum owned by Hershey’s, has come up with a “mint before you mask” campaign https://www.moreaboutadvertising.com/2020/05/maskbreath-is-a-thing-hersheys-has-a-cure-for-halitosis/ (because, you know, mask breath is REAL). The company is marketing it as a cure for halitosis. 

The emphasis has clearly moved from society to self. Companies now want individuals to see mint and gum as a self-mood elevator, instead of something that makes them socially more acceptable or confident.

Since we aren’t going to come within kissing distance of anyone outside the house (sorry, blind dates), one’s relationship with the self has become marketing fodder.

As for the million-dollar smiles we’re missing out on, Perfetti Van Melle India has a new idea https://brandequity.economictimes.indiatimes.com/news/marketing/minty-ing-behind-the-mask/76158372:

We will soon be launching a campaign under Happydent where the consumer shall get a chance to ‘unmask their smiles’ by getting their smiles printed on the masks. We believe consuming products that uplift the mood during these stressful times will definitely hit the cord among the target audiences.

Rohit Kapoor, director of marketing, Perfetti Van Melle India

You know things are bad when companies want to sell fake smiles to push products.

When the world is one giant gap, make gap lemonade

Rohin

India is a large, populous, and still-developing country. Too many people with too few opportunities, jobs or resources means a scarcity mentality silently embeds itself into collective psyches. And if you’re wired to scarcity, then you’d abhor gaps. 

Because gaps are wasteful.

Perhaps in no area are gaps looked down upon as much as higher education and careers. A student with a year-long gap seeking admission to a sought-after college will almost certainly lose out to someone who hasn’t taken one. It’s the same story with jobs too, where a candidate with a gap longer than six months will be viewed with suspicion.

Did you flunk your last exams? Were you so bad at admission tests that you didn’t get in anywhere? Were you fired from your last job? Were you just wasting precious time?

Scarcity-minded college administrators and hiring managers see gaps as evidence of either wasteful and privileged indulgence, lack of abilities, or failure.

This post https://medium.com/@abhiban1/the-indian-stigma-around-the-gap-year-8b8154e2f6e0 by Abhinav Banerjee, a management consultant and design researcher, explains both the tremendous pressure Indian school kids face when it comes to cracking competitive exams, and the stigma around gap years.

Which is why the concept of taking a year off in the interregnum between finishing school and starting college—a “gap year”—has never really caught on in India. Except, the entire world has been caught up in a giant gap for the last three months.

What does this mean for Indian parents (and occasionally, their children) who have been planning eagerly for years to court US universities? This LinkedIn post https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/gap-year-after-grade-xii-bhandari-vivek/ by Vivek Bhandari, the founder and CEO of edtech company Scholarly, explains their predicament.

Indian students that were applying for foreign admissions usually got their admission decisions by April and spent the next few months planning their finances, loans, travel, and visa applications. Unfortunately, this process is at a complete standstill, and it appears that US universities will have greater clarity about foreign admissions by July. Most universities are suggesting the following options to their foreign freshmen students.

Defer admission to Fall 2021
	Enroll now and do classes online till physical classes resume sometime in 2021
	Wait and watch

Vivek Bhandari, founder and CEO, Scholarly

Is there another option?

Even during normal times, some students opt for a Gap Year. They use this time for travel, social service, pursuing a business idea or preparing for admission to a higher ranked university. In many cases, the students enrich themselves and expand their minds with new learning and experiences. However, for a student that was not planning for it, the sudden specter of a Gap Year can be quite daunting.

Vivek Bhandari, founder and CEO, Scholarly

Unlike in India, gap years are accepted much more easily in the US and Europe. There are even entire companies and non-profits that have made turning gap years productive and resume-friendly into their business models. Companies like Gap Year Association https://www.gapyearassociation.org/, Gap Year https://www.gapyear.com/ and Year On https://www.yearon.com/program-overview. Year On, for instance, offers gap year programmes https://www.yearon.com/program-overview costing US$19,000-24,000.

Will Indian students and their parents finally warm up to a gap year?

A new wearable device

Nadine

Is it a piece of clothing? Or is it furniture? Indonesia is about to let two-wheel taxis back on the street. Drivers have to follow hygiene protocols… and wear this:

Source: dramaojol.id https://www.instagram.com/dramaojol.id/

The wearable shield is meant to prevent the spread of droplets from the driver to the passenger on the back seat, and vice versa. Yesterday, the Indonesian transportation ministry made the device a requirement https://bisnis.tempo.co/read/1351490/aturan-baru-kemenhub-driver-ojek-online-wajib-gunakan-penyekat. Gojek and Grab, the two biggest platforms for on-demand bike taxis in Indonesia, said they’ll comply, but that it will take a while until all drivers are equipped with the wearable.

Ride-hailing in the skies?

Savio

Everyone in the business of transporting humans has been clobbered by the pandemic. And they are desperate for travellers. So much so that Lufthansa, Germany’s largest airline, has taken a leaf out of ride-hailing companies and agreed to travel empty to your doorstep to pick you up.

Lufthansa group has offered to fly empty planes to India and carry passengers only on its outbound flights to destinations in Europe, senior government officials said on Tuesday.

Scheduled international passenger flights have been suspended in India since March 23 due to the coronavirus pandemic. Therefore, there is a pent up demand for outbound international travel, especially among non-resident Indians (NRIs) who are in a hurry to go back either for personal or professional reasons.

Lufthansa offers to fly in empty, take out fliers back to Europe, The Economic Times https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/industry/transportation/airlines-/-aviation/lufthansa-offers-to-fly-in-empty-take-out-fliers-back-to-europe/articleshow/76276555.cms?utm_source=ETTopNews&utm_medium=HP&utm_campaign=TN&utm_content=23

You’d think e-commerce was a solved problem in India, given the dominance of the Flipkart-Amazon duopoly. Thanks to many billions of dollars in investments over the years, the two giants are able to offer incredible product catalogues, competitive prices, and really fast delivery times across most parts of a vast country like India. 
 
But, says Praveen Khandelwal, “We will show the world how e-commerce is done.”
 
Khandelwal isn’t the CEO of a SoftBank-bankrolled rival that wants to disrupt Flipkart and Amazon. Instead, he’s the secretary-general of Confederation of All India Traders (CAIT), a trade body representing millions of small and medium traders. By virtue of that, he is also one of the nine committee members of a new government-run organisation called the Open Network for Digital Commerce, or ONDC.
 
ONDC, notwithstanding its boring name, wants to be the bull in not just Flipkart’s and Amazon’s china shops, but also perhaps Zomato’s and Swiggy’s and OYO’s and Uber’s and Meesho’s and Urban Company’s. No, I am not exaggerating, though I do not know if the ONDC thinks it is.
 
Realising that the source of power of tech platforms are, well, their proprietary platforms, ONDC wants to encourage the creation of “open networks” that “democratise e-commerce”. 
 
The success of the UPI open protocol for money transfer has convinced the Indian government that it can replicate the same in multiple sectors. E-commerce, food delivery, ride-hailing, hotel booking, professional services…the list is only limited by the imaginations of the regulators and bureaucrats.

Good Morning Ishan,

Abiding by the law in letter and not in spirit is frowned upon. But this can be a fine art—with deep planning and meticulous execution. Amazon India knows a thing or two about it.

You see, India’s foreign-investment rules forbid e-commerce marketplaces from exercising ownership or control over inventory and holding equity in sellers on their platforms.

So, a couple of years back, after much regulatory heat, Amazon India shut down Cloudtail—its largest seller then and joint venture with the family office of Infosys founder Narayana Murthy. And earlier this month, the e-commerce giant followed it up by exiting Appario, another top seller and joint venture with the Patni Group. Appario is being acquired by Clicktech Retail, a third-party seller whose promoters are linked to Dalmia Bharat Group.

Compliance checkbox clicked? Yes. Nice.

But has Amazon India really rid itself of anti-competitive behaviour allegations? Has it provided a level playing field to all sellers on its platform? Not quite, finds Gaurav in today’s insightful story. The new big sellers on the platform—Clicktech and Cocoblu—still get special treatment, but in an ingenious way. Let’s just say, Amazon’s invisible and visible hands continue to pull the strings. Read about it here.

Inheritance tax disinherited

It’s peak heat time in India, literally and politically. The latest hot potato this big election season is the mere mention of inheritance tax—a levy on assets inherited by a person. Both the BJP and Congress have disinherited it in quick-time. It would be political harakiri to advocate any tax during poll time, especially an unpopular one that was abolished decades back. Interestingly, not so long ago, past Finance Ministers from both these parties—P. Chidambaram and the late Arun Jaitley—had hinted at bringing back inheritance tax for effective wealth distribution.

The tax is, in fact, par for the course in many countries. But in India, the matter has been given many quiet burials. Just like all the talk over the years about tax on income of rich farmers. The political cost-benefit math simply does not allow room for all this.

Good Evening Ishan,
 
It’s Shreevar here, and I’m back with another confession. 
 
The Hollywood film Moneyball, directed by Bennett Miller and starring Brad Pitt, is one of my favourite movies of all time. 
Which is a bit of a surprise because I’m not really a fan of: 
 
1. baseball,
2. statistics, or
3. the early-2000s.
 
Truth be told, though, the movie is about so much more.
 
Brad Pitt, as baseball coach Billy Beane, works with a bunch of ‘underperforming’ athletes to change the fate of his club. He gives inspiring monologues (written by legendary screenwriter Aaron Sorkin), negotiates with the senior management, reinvigorates his players’ morale by focusing on their strengths and hard statistics… 
[ Photo credit: Columbia Pictures ]

But that’s not really how it works in companies. Brad Pitt isn’t your boss. 

 

When dealing with underperformers, companies often apply an old trick from the management playbook. It has the words ‘improvement’ and ‘plan’ in it, but in many workplaces, it plans to improve no one. 

 

In this episode of Cost to Company, I speak to Sharthok Chakraborty (co-founder, Klaar), Nimesh Mathur (Investor, Advisor, and HR Leader), and Sumit Singla (HR Consultant) to discuss one of the most contentious tools of the workplace: the Performance Improvement Plan (PIP).

 

You’ll be hearing conversations, insights, and anecdotes which barely ever make it out of water cooler chats and internal grapevines.

 

Don’t miss out.

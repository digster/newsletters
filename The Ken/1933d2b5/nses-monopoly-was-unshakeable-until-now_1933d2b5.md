Good Morning Ishan,
In early 2021, my colleague Anand brought his analytical chops to a deep dive into the National Stock Exchange of India’s (NSE) untrammelled [dominance](https://the-ken.com/story/how-nse-india-turned-from-solution-to-problem/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter).
Very public controversies, frequent technical glitches, and the BSE’s (formerly Bombay Stock Exchange) desperate attempts to measure up—nothing seemed to make a dent in the NSE’s influence.
No one would have thought the boot would be on the other foot in just under four years.
Alarmed by the surge in individuals trading in futures and options—with nine out of 10 losing money—stock-market watchdog Sebi recently issued a series of diktats that strike at the heart of the NSE’s lucrative empire (its FY24 profits were Rs 8,300 crore).
Among Sebi’s orders is that a stock exchange can offer an options contract settled within a week only on one benchmark index. So last week, the NSE pulled the plug on Bank Nifty options so that it could continue with its contract on the Nifty 50, the exchange’s marquee index.
But the Bank Nifty weekly’s demise could wipe out a stonking 30% of the volumes in the NSE’s largest business, Anand estimates in his fascinating [story](https://the-ken.com/story/nses-monopoly-was-unshakeable-until-now/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter) today. What’s more, never have the BSE’s chances looked so good in recent memory.
Oddly enough, though, the NSE’s unlisted shares continue to be on a tear, making it one of India’s 15 most valuable stocks. What gives?

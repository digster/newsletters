Good Morning Ishan,
In August, my colleague Atul [wrote](https://the-ken.com/story/openai-offers-free-chatgpt-licences-schools-see-hidden-costs/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter) a revelatory story about OpenAI’s attempts to partner with a network of 1,800 private schools. However benevolent OpenAI’s “free” ChatGPT licences to these schools seem, no one will mistake it for charity.
For a company that has raised more money than Jordan’s GDP, OpenAI would leave no stone unturned in getting much of humanity used to its products.
It’s not just OpenAI, though, that’s eager for Indian schools to embrace AI. Since 2019, three years before ChatGPT’s launch, the Central Board of Secondary Education, with Intel’s help, has had an AI elective for ninth-graders and above. In 2023, CBSE added a 15-hour AI module beginning in sixth grade. More than 18,000 schools now teach it.
But CBSE turned heads recently when it said it plans to introduce AI lessons for kids as young as third-graders. But if you are picturing ChatGPT or Gemini spitting out instant answers for these students, think again. This “AI” experiment won’t involve any technology. No screens, no chatbots.
In [a companion piece](https://the-ken.com/story/cbses-big-ai-experiment-teaching-machines-without-the-machines/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter) to his OpenAI story, Atul unpacks how India’s largest school board wants to make millions of students AI-ready.

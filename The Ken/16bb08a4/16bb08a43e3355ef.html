Today’s story is about cloud kitchens. Perhaps you know what cloud kitchens are. Perhaps you don’t, but you have heard about them.

Never mind. Let me explain.

For that, I need to go back to the beginning.

Historically, food-tech companies in India were started to solve a specific, narrow problem. Find a place to eat. Or book a table. Or deliver food home. Or because Softbank told you to do so. But then, these companies discovered two problems. First, the diversity wasn’t optimal: Too many restaurants in some areas. Not enough in others. Second, there wasn’t enough money to be made by focusing on narrow, specific, verticals.

In order to win food-tech, you couldn’t just distribute what already existed. You needed to create it. And you needed to do everything. Not just delivery, discovery, payments or whatever it is that Foodpanda was doing.

Enter cloud kitchens. As Daljit S. Kochhar, our contributor for the story writes,

“A cloud kitchen is essentially a kitchen without a storefront or seating; it’s delivery-only. There are no expensive rentals, fit-out costs, service personnel etc. The menus can be curated for home delivery and they can operate at all hours. The standardised tech on offer by platforms is a great leveller. It ensures that entrepreneur chefs setting up cloud kitchens can whisk away business from an established restaurant”.

He describes it as a pot of gold for the food-tech industry. 

Pots of gold are slippery things. Daljit points out all the things that can go wrong if you believe that cloud kitchens are the answer to all your problems. To be precise, he lists five, specific challenges. And explains how companies like Swiggy and Zomato can learn a thing or two from Domino’s. 

I know. You are probably sceptical. I was too.

Good Morning Ishan,
 
The past week has been nothing short of a blur. But what a colourful blur it has been.
 
Our stories have tackled different, challenging subjects, with some kickass storytelling, if I may say so myself. So when PGK asked a pointed question about my experience of The Ken as a reader reading on the app, I paused. As a member of the editorial team, sometimes it becomes hard to look beyond the quality of our stories and appreciate the quality of the product as a whole.
 
The answer came to me. Access. 
 
The best thing about reading on The Ken app, in my experience, is knowing that, as a paid subscriber, I have access. It was an interesting revelation. A revelation that stayed with me long enough to view our individual stories this week through that lens. Interestingly, quite a few of them had to do with that privilege.
 
Look at Suraksha's piece on oxytocin, for instance. One patient activist group and three pharmaceutical companies at loggerheads with the government over this critical drug—one that ensures the safety of birthing women. The government has given a public sector company sole domain over oxytocin manufacturing. Essentially, for many mothers in need, this is a classic case of "Access Denied". This one's a free story; don't miss it.
 
With Harveen and Ashish's piece on Netflix and its numbers, one had very little access, for the video streaming service is a very secretive company. But the two of them dug some stuff out anyway—five numbers that help understand where Netflix stands in India. Go on, read it. 
 
Rohin's piece on InMobi's Glance, interestingly, was about not needing to access. Here's a lockscreen service that will entertain you with news, fashion updates, high-quality videos, games, whatnot. And all you need to do it just glance at your phone, no need to unlock it. It's not even on the Play Store, but has more daily active users than TikTok. What?
 
Olina, as always, asked an important question—what about accessibility? Her piece on urban mobility, with a focus on Bounce's refurbished e-scooters vs Yulu's upgraded e-bike, sure helped answer some questions about the next shift in our chosen mode of transport, and how we'll get there.
 
Seema's piece was about a man in pharma who has all the access in the world, pretty much. Arun Kumar’s first public company has 51 subsidiaries. His second has 21. His third, and most recent, is also likely to follow suit. But, apparently, there's a method to this madness.
 
And finally, Roshni's story touched upon growing demand for K-beauty products in India. These are products that have had such high demand in the past that the lack of supply meant shady WhatsApp groups to access them. No kidding, go read it.
 
That's all we had this week. 
 
Now, since it's the VERY last day of the month, if you're left with some free gift credits, send your loved ones some of these stories and gift them (no, don't hate me for saying it) access.
 
Have a happy Sunday.
 
Regards,

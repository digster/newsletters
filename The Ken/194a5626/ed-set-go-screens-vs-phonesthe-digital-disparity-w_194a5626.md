The one stop to understand the biggest shifts in education, written by the best edtech journalist in the country..
Good Morning Ishan,
It’s been a week! Donald Trump’s inauguration as President of the United States has revved up the anti-immigration rhetoric and put work visas like the H-1B and J-1 firmly in the sights of the border czars that now have a free run of the administration. Through the weekend, I thought about the Indian edtechs who’ve diligently built pathways for students to come to the US to study and work. Even if the threats to H-1B never fully materialise, the world’s borders are no longer as porous as they once seemed.
This means that India will have to rely on its own institutions to create the kind of world-class opportunities that students seek outside. This can’t start once students get to their higher education. The opportunities, the exposure, and the training will need to begin in school.
The National Education Policy (NEP), launched in 2020, was meant to usher in the tech age of school education in India. The government did make some strides, especially during the Covid-19 pandemic, to prop up tech platforms that could help students learn, like SWAYAM and DIKSHA. The Prime Minister has also doubled down on the need for “AI-driven” learning in classrooms, which has the potential to make this learning more equitable for all children. At least that’s what he [told](https://www.businesstoday.in/technology/news/story/ai-and-education-how-india-plans-to-democratise-technology-in-schools-according-to-pm-modi-and-bill-gates-423339-2024-03-29) Bill Gates:
PM Modi articulated his vision for leveraging AI to revolutionise education, stating, “We aim to democratise technology in schools and ensure that every child, regardless of background, has access to quality education.” This bold ambition underscores the government’s commitment to leveraging technology as a catalyst for inclusive and equitable education.
The mission is lofty, but the ladder underneath is rickety. The government has to only look at its own data to know that we are far from the AI path we want to be on.
The Indian education ministry’s data [shows](https://dashboard.udiseplus.gov.in/report-new-v.4-demo2025/static/media/UDISE+2023_24_Booklet_nep.f2ab818294eb169c0adc.pdf) that there’s a wide variation in the kind of technology that schools have access to. Only 20% of government schools have “functional PCs”, compared to 60% when it comes to private unaided schools.
Even within government schools from different states, the use of tech products such as tablets, laptops, smart boards, and other tools that enable virtual classrooms vary:
1. In Delhi, for instance, 70% of government schools had “functional Smart Classrooms used for teaching with Digital Boards/Smart Boards/Virtual Classrooms/Smart TV availability” in the 2023-24 school year. That number drops to 8% in Jharkhand and 3% in West Bengal.
2. When it comes to “functional tablets”, 97% of government schools in Chandigarh have them, while only 10% of similar schools in Tamil Nadu have them.
3. Even when it comes to electricity, out of the over 97,000 government schools in Rajasthan, only about 62,000 run with functional electricity. In Maharashtra, out of about 95,000 schools, only slightly over 50,000 government schools have functional electricity.
The data is an interesting deep dive, and it reveals not just interstate disparities in infrastructure but also across the types of schools. Private schools generally have better access to technology than government or government-aided schools.
There are some bright spots though.
Access to mobile phones, for the express purposes of learning, seems to be more equitably distributed than laptops or tablets. While on average, access in government schools remains lower than private schools, in states like Tamil Nadu, where PC access is low, almost 94% of government schools have access to mobile phones for “teaching purposes”. In states like West Bengal, Nagaland, and Bihar, access to phones for teaching remains in the single digits.
The data indicates that the efforts to equip Indian students with cutting-edge tech skills is going to take a lot, and it has to start at least with the right infrastructure in place. Only then comes the right learning material, the right teachers to train students, and a job market that can absorb this new talent.

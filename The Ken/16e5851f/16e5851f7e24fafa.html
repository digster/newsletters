It is by now a trope that "xx% of all acquisitions fail”. But can you guess xx?

A web search will tell you it’s 70%. And 83%. And 90%. Or, 70-90%. And 60-80%. The truth is it doesn’t matter. It’s a large enough number. A number that is sufficiently this side of 50% to say, “most acquisitions fail.” In fact, there’s even a phrase called the winner’s curse, which says most of the shareholder value created in an acquisition goes to the seller, not the buyer.

Which side will Flipkart, Walmart’s largest ever acquisition ($16 billion) fall? There are no clear answers as yet, but things are starting to look hairy at India’s #1 e-commerce retailer as it chafes under the ownership and control of the world’s #1 retailer.

Abinaya goes behind the front-page headlines and into the small and big changes that Walmart is making at Flipkart. These range from cutting costs to overhauling its tech stack to vacillating on hyperlocal deliveries to restructuring Flipkart’s massive delivery arm, Ekart.

As the culture of a bottomline-focused Walmart clashes with Flipkart’s spend-to-win-at-all-costs, there are sparks flying. Senior execs are resigning or being reshuffled. The number of defective orders Ekart registered during this year's Big Billion Days sale reverted to levels last seen five years ago.

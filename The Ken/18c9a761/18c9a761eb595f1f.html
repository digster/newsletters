1. Valuing problems more than solutions 🤔

There are founders who come across as fluid, opinionated, or gut-driven. And then, there are those who seem more conscious, deliberate, and measured.

Krish is the latter.

He thinks through his responses. His answers are measured and calibrated. You get the sense that he doesn’t like venturing opinions on things where he doesn’t consider himself well-equipped.

He tells me his favourite mental model is Circles of Competence, because he’s discovered and rediscovered it across two distinct times—as an early-stage founder and then, as the CEO of a large global company working with senior and experienced executives.

“In the early stages Chargebee was mostly comprised of trained software engineers. And the training that we went through at work shapes us in very different ways, and yet, everybody has opinions about how they believe they have a solution to a problem,” Krish tells me.

He recollects a time early in Chargebee’s history when he wanted to get his engineering team to add some extra fields for their customers’ shipping addresses. “I thought, I am a software engineer. I know the obvious solution. And I was getting frustrated about why my team was not implementing it fast enough,” he says.

By way of an answer, Krish says he “got a masterclass” from his co-founder and CTO, Saravanan K P.

“I can put a generic set of fields, but does that solve the problem? The customer will be happy, right? You will get off my back. But we also have to think about the API, and how I'm going to expose it. The underlying data model has an impact on the API. So when a third party system connects, it should be able to know what the data structure of that object is, which is the beauty of APIs—that systems talk to each other…” explained Saravanan.

I interrupted Krish to tell him that I appreciated the point he was making, but that I also must send him one of the most hilarious videos I’ve watched on tech architecture: Microservices. Every one of Krazam’s videos is gold.

To Krish’s credit, he took my utterly irrelevant digression in his stride. His CTO Saravanan’s approach was “the right way to architect a solution,” he says. “I realised I am a software engineer from the services world offering a solution without understanding how a product engineer thinks about it.”

This is the fundamental concept behind circles of competence, a mental model articulated most sharply by Warren Buffett and Charlie Munger at Berkshire Hathaway.
"If we have a strength, it is in recognizing when we are operating well within our circle of competence and when we are approaching the perimeter."—Warren Buffett
 
"lf you have competence, you pretty much know its boundaries already. To ask the question [of whether you are past the boundary] is to answer it."—Charlie Munger
 
Page 65, Charlie’s Almanack

In organisations, lack of awareness about circles of competence leads to what Krish referred to as the HIPO problem—Highly Paid Person’s Opinion. In other words, senior leaders going off suggesting solutions to problems they are not competent on.

This is where we stumble upon the important corollary—our bias for solutions over problems.

When his senior colleagues point out problems in other functions at Chargebee, Krish says he respects that.

But, respecting someone for pointing out a problem should not imply getting carried away and also accepting their solution.

If we’re not consciously avoiding the bias to see problems and solutions as a bundle deal, then we end up making bad decisions, says Krish.

The right approach is to separate the problems from the solutions, especially if they are being presented by the same person (who is outside of their circle of competence).

I do a ‘hard relate’ with Krish’s observation. I tell him most organisations—and leaders—have a bias towards solutions. You’ve probably heard a version of “don’t come to me with a problem if you don’t have a proposed solution” sometime in your career.

I’m guilty of it too. Perhaps it was the same engineering-driven mindset for solutions that Krish recollected from earlier on in his entrepreneurial journey. Or perhaps I was just subconsciously passing along my lived management trauma experienced over the years to my own colleagues.

When leaders and organisations incentivize employees to only highlight problems along with proposed solutions, either of two things happen:

(a) People come up with and propose shallow solutions

(b) People keep quiet about problems, since they can’t (or don’t want to) think of solutions

With (b), your colleagues end up being forced to keep secrets, says Krish, referring to a Youtube talk by Alan Mullaly, the former CEO of Boeing and Ford.
[9:31 minutes]... a victim or they're gonna be made fun of then they're gonna be reluctant to share what the real situation is because most companies the policy still is you only bring an issue to your supervisor or your leader if you have a solution so now you're managing a secret you really don't know what the situation ...
Working together—Alan Mulally

What is the alternative then, you may be wondering. It is simply to value problems and spend more time finding, understanding, and discussing them. Instead of rushing to solutions.

Everyone can come up with shallow solutions. Heck, in a post ChatGPT world, you can generate dozens of apparently good solutions in minutes.

But no one can generate valuable problems. They lie all around us like rocks and stones. But find the right one and spend enough time chipping off its edges and polishing it, and you will have a diamond.

This reminded me of OYO Rooms founder Ritesh Agarwal talking about the value of good questions
“I think entrepreneurs, often, may not need to be told the answers. They may just need to be asked the right questions.

Like, a lot of times you may have an answer about how you'd like to make decisions. But only when you're asked the hard questions, do you end up thinking, well, this may be the solution to it.

I try to collect good questions.”
 

Ritesh Agarwal of OYO on building a business on differentiation, communication and resilience, Episode 30 | 7 December, 2023

Because the best problems often unlock the greatest opportunities. Which we would never realise if we were biased towards solutions.

“You’re saying, wait, hold on to your solution. Let’s not rush to solutions. Instead, let's discuss the problem. Let’s spend more time on it,” says Krish.

I get what he’s saying. He’s recommending deliberately slowing down things when someone brings up a good problem. So that more time and people can be devoted to understanding it.

Yes, replies Krish. “If you zoom out, then what you're saying is that organisations incentivize people to point out problems even beyond their areas.”

Amen to that!

You read that right. Over these past few months, the First Principles community has shown itself to be almost unbelievably talented. We now have regular community-contributed sections on photos, books, habits, and even music, so I thought… why not original art? 

 

So this week, we feature our very first set of paintings, all three of which are from Nithyanand Shankar. A Bengaluru-based artist, Nithyanand says he paints on the side while working as the CXO of a listed company. He uses “an earthy palette to paint people and their emotions in a figurative style” and the subjects he often chooses “are young Buddhist monks who exude a strange combination of innocence and serenity, and women expressing complex emotions”. 

 

1. God Rays—“A large piece painted on commission for a new home in Bengaluru that shows a monk deep in meditation oblivious to the beautiful rays of light streaming through a latticed window.”

2. Letting Go—“A recent piece that brings alive the emotion of letting go in a surreal setting that is larger than life” 

3. Young Monk—“A monk whose innocence shines bright through his enigmatic expression”

More of Nithyanand’s pieces are available for viewing and purchase at www.nithyanand shankar.com. He also does commissioned artwork and can be reached at nithyanand.shankar@gmail.com.

6. #FPBooks 📚

Our book recommendation this week comes from Rushabh Mota, “a lifelong curious learner and a HR professional with experience in four different industries.”

 

Four Thousand Weeks—by Oliver Burkeman
“This book was a stark reminder of the reality and mortality of our lives. It also dispels the myth about time management and productivity that we have been fed with over the years. One of the First Principles that I have related with strongly is—Minimising regrets, Accept our finitude, Live Fearlessly. After reading this book, I realised that I have effectively 1,450 weeks left in my life (80-33-8 hours of sleep) and in an instant, I will be out of here. This book made me realise that we are mortals and the importance of living our lives consciously and to the fullest in a practical way.”

7. #FPHabits ✅

Our habit submission this week comes from Sandeep Gautam, “the chief solution officer and cofounder of PeakMind, a venture focussed on enhancing psychological well being of adolescents… a programmer, passionate about psychology, and poetry.”

 

“Writing a sonnet almost daily”
“I write a 14-line poem based around psychological themes almost on a daily basis. Initially, in 2021, I took a challenge to write a sonnet daily and continued an unbroken streak for more than a month. Since then, I write daily off and on—not depending on a muse to strike but intentionally taking time to reflect on the day’s learning and experience, and distilling it into a poem. I actually combine two of my habits—I had developed reading some psychology content daily as a habit early on, either reading articles on the web or making some progress on reading a book, and this has served me well. I typically find inspiration and material in what I am reading, if not the events and emotions of the day. I post my sonnets on my poetry blog—https://thefoolsquest.blogspot.com—and also share them on social media. Encouraged by the response of my readers, I published a poetry collection recently titled 'Flotsam and Jetsam' that has the theme of suicide prevention predominantly.”

And how did this help Sandeep?
“When I was a psychology blogger (started way back in 2006 ) I was very inconsistent when it came to posting; although my blog— https://the-mouse-trap.com—was once a top 100 science blog in the world, it fizzled out as I could not be inconsistent. This time around, I am much more intentionally consistent and focused, and it leads to a great sense of accomplishment. Also, writing a sonnet each day refreshes and recharges me instantly.”

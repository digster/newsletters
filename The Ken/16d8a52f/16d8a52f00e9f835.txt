Good Morning Ishan,

$1,095.

That's the number Juul's 'savings calculator https://www.juul.com/calculator' flashes on the website. It's how much the e-cigarette maker estimates you'd save each year by switching to Juul. "You" being someone they assume spends $7 on and smokes 20 cigarettes every day.

So, how much would I save? I smoke about nine cigarettes daily and buy a pack of 10 worth Rs 150 ($2.10). Input > Enter. Nothing.

Not 'nothing happened', but Juul says I'll save nothing - $0. I played around a bit and figured the minimum computation requirements - spend $5 and smoke one cigarette a day. Strange, because $5 (Rs 355) would buy me nearly 24 cigarettes in India. What does it expect me to do with the other 23 each day?

It's a moot question now anyhow. Because two weeks back, India banned the sale of all e-cigarettes. So, Juul's savings pitch doesn't matter. Or does it?

This wonky calculator pitch is emblematic of Juul's lobbying efforts against the ban. Of using Big Tobacco's playbook to fight to save a market worth about $50 billion.

Juul tried the medical fraternity: a former president of India's largest body of doctors wrote articles saying e-cigarettes could prevent cancer. 

It furnished scientific papers. Except it did not disclose an author's conflict of interest — very 20th century Big Tobacco.

It touted employment and commercial benefits. How, the government asked, when the grey market imports satiated demand?

It sought sympathy from e-cigarette firms. However, far from support, they blame Juul's aggressive lobbying for uniting groups that made for strange bedfellows. 

Juul seems to have miscalculated at every step. Badly.

It has one last throw of the dice - the courts. Juul has already lawyered up and got its top Indian importer to file a petition challenging the ban.

More wonky math?

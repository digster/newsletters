“Speed thrills but kills”. Oft-seen on many Indian roads, this warning is to be ignored at one’s own risk. Surprisingly, though, zipping fast and loose and playing with peril is Rapido, the country’s bike-taxi pioneer.

Not just itself, but worse, this market leader is also putting many of its passengers in danger. The sexual harassment that Bengaluru-based Athira Purushothaman endured during and after her Rapido trip in July is a stark case in point.

Purushothaman isn’t alone. With over 750,000 daily rides and counting, Rapido is in the fast lane. And so are the complaints against its riders—theft, impersonation, sexual harassment—to name a few. Many serious grievances seem to have evoked little response from the company.

Rapido’s track record is much worse than its closest competitors, Uber and Ola, finds Shivani, the author of today’s story. What gives?

Is careful onboarding of drivers and passenger safety being sacrificed at the altar of growth? What are the consequences—for the company, its riders, and its customers? What does the rule book say? Are bike-taxi platforms like Rapido even on sound legal ground in the many places they operate in?

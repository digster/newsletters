Zepto and Zomato’s ‘Indianness’. Why are the two suddenly hungry for Indian capital?
By Seetharaman G
5 min read
View in browser

Good Morning Ishan,

You are reading our subscriber-only newsletter Trade Tricks—a weekly commentary on Indian retail and commerce

Yet another month, yet another fundraise totalling hundreds of millions of dollars. That was November for Zepto. 

In its third funding round since June, the quick-commerce company hoovered up US$350 million. But that isn’t half as interesting as the fact that all of it came from Indian investors. “We pulled off the largest domestic fundraise for a startup in Indian history,” Zepto co-founder and chief executive Aadit Palicha boasted to Mint. 

This is in line with the company’s desire to be majority Indian-owned by next year. Local investors currently control just over 30% of Zepto. It’s improbable that any of its overseas backers would want to sell their holdings in the company before Zepto’s listing, given how white-hot quick commerce is right now. 

So, Zepto will continue to raise boatloads of funds to realise its dream of being an “Indian” company. 

Zepto is not alone, though. 

Days after its announcement, Zomato, which owns Zepto rival Blinkit, told the stock exchanges it has raised Rs 8,500 crore (US$1 billion) in a share sale to institutional investors, and disclosed the names of those who had bought more than 5% of the newly issued shares. Four Indian mutual-fund firms—Motilal Oswal, ICICI Prudential, HDFC, and Kotak—together accounted for nearly half the fundraise. 

In fact, my colleague Aakriti argued weeks ago that changing its shareholding in favour of Indian investors may have been a reason why Zomato wanted to raise money, despite already having a lot of it.

Currently, foreign institutional investors own about 52% of the company, down from 58% two years ago. Meanwhile, domestic institutional ownership has climbed to 17% from 6.4% in the same period.

Antfin Financial—an affiliate of Chinese conglomerate Alibaba Group—has seen its shareholding fall to 2.1% as of September, down from about 6.4% two years ago. Alipay, another Alibaba entity, no longer holds over 1% stake, and Fidelity Investments has reduced its share from 2.5% to 1%.

Why are Zepto and Zomato suddenly hungry for Indian capital? 

Answering that requires a quick detour into India’s e-commerce rules. A company running an online marketplace can have up to 100% foreign direct investment. Amazon and Flipkart, owned by US retail giant Walmart, are cases in point. But a company that owns any of the inventory it sells cannot have foreign capital. 

So quick-commerce apps would be in violation of this rule if they carried their own inventory. In fact, that’s what a national body of small traders recently alleged. But Zomato, Zepto, and Swiggy, which went public last month, claim they are marketplaces, with sellers or franchisees running the warehouses and paying the companies a commission on every sale. 

Bottom line: these platforms are in the clear as far as foreign-investment rules are concerned. Plus, at least in Zomato’s case, foreign portfolio investors, with an over 47% stake as of September, far outweighed foreign direct investors, who owned a shade under 5.3%. The former invest in the listed securities of a company and are not as long-term or as active as foreign direct investors, to whom the e-commerce rules apply. 

So why does it matter where quick-commerce companies’ backers are from? 

Because despite the companies’ claims to the contrary, there is increasing evidence that their rise has dealt a severe blow to neighbourhood grocers in India’s top cities. To persuade the government to remedy this, the traders’ body has added a litany of other complaints, including predatory pricing and preferential treatment to certain sellers. These charges may not pass muster, but the fact is that rapid-delivery apps now have a target on their backs.

They have to be nimble-footed in their response, hence the attempt to reshape themselves through their shareholding. But I’m not sure this is an effective tactic for a listed company. (Zepto has reportedly hired investment bankers to plan its listing, and is likely to join Zomato and Swiggy on the bourses sooner rather than later.)

A privately held company has greater control in rejigging its ownership, as is clear from what Zepto is trying to do. But if you are a listed entity, even one majority-owned by Indian institutions, there is nothing that stops investors from selling their holdings and tipping the balance in favour of foreign counterparts in just a couple of quarters.

But for now, at least Palicha believes there’s much to gain by being an Indian company, as he told The Economic Times:

“Becoming a majority Indian-owned company builds trust — not just with policymakers, but also with the public and shareholders. It’s an advantage in the long run for creating a strong, trusted brand,” Palicha said.

He also wants to leave the “perception of being a foreign company” behind.

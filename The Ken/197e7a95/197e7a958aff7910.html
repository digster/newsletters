I closely follow retail and e-commerce for a living, and even to me, the mattress business has been somewhat of a blind spot. Furniture, not so much, thanks to the likes of Pepperfry and Urban Ladder, not to mention a leviathan such as Ikea.

It took a while for VC-backed mattress entrepreneurs to get in on the action in a largely unorganised sector. But when they did, they made incumbents like Duroflex quite uncomfortable, as my colleague Nuha argued over a year ago.

Earlier, if a customer wanted to buy a mattress, they would have had to spend anywhere between Rs 40,000 (US$480) and Rs 1 lakh (US$1,200). But today, brands like Wakefit offer products that are 25–50% cheaper, for mattresses with quality “at par with that of traditional players”.

Besides affordable prices, these new-age brands have adopted disruptive promotional methods. Think flashy marketing, sleep internships (yes, you read that right), 30-to-100-day sleep trials, and cheap pricing.

The category disruption that Wakefit, last valued at US$275 million, brought wasn’t small. So much so that the 60-year-old brand, Duroflex, found it necessary to launch a subsidiary, Sleepyhead, in 2017 to cater to the ever-growing online demand for mattresses. Especially among first-time consumers.

Now, the disruptor wants to join 54-year-old Sheela Foam, which owns Sleepwell and Kurlon, on the stock exchanges. When Wakefit Innovations, backed by Peak XV Partners, filed to raise Rs 470 crore ($55 million) in a share sale a few days ago, I was eager to read the IPO document to, among other things, fix the aforementioned blind spot. 

The document has the usual suspects: rising incomes and sleep awareness —> pricey homes and mattresses. 

What I didn’t expect was a curveball related to one of my pet peeves: direct-to-consumer (D2C). 

In the early days of this newsletter, when wearables maker Boat wanted to go public, I wrote that Boat was anything but a D2C brand. 

Marketplaces such as Amazon and Flipkart were responsible for 86% of Boat’s sales in the year ended March 2021. And Boat depends on 51 distributors and 180 sub-distributors to reach 23,000 retail stores, which brought in another 12%. Boat’s own website accounted for just 2% of its sales.

In effect, middlemen, which include Amazon and Flipkart, are the very reason for Boat’s existence.

What pushes my buttons is that sizeable online sales, even when facilitated by gatekeepers such as Amazon and Blinkit, are enough to call a brand D2C. I even joined my colleagues Rohin and Praveen on their podcast, Two by Two, last year to bust this fallacy. 

So you can imagine my surprise when I discovered that Wakefit’s own website and stores accounted for 55% of its revenue of Rs 1,000 crore in the April–December 2024 period. That’s as good as D2C can get for most of India’s internet companies. 

Wakefit’s growth in recent years has meant a higher dependence on multi-brand online and offline retailers, including Pepperfry and Pai International Electronics. But the fact that its own channels control over half its sales is certainly impressive. But the credit is not all Wakefit’s. It’s to do with what Wakefit sells. Venture capitalist Deepak Shahdadpuri, who knows a thing or two about consumer brands and has backed Wakefit rival Sleepycat, said as much on Two by Two.

In an extreme example, where I buy a product once every 10 years with high intent, I’m willing to spend time to go to the mattress company that I want.

But if I’m going to buy coffee every week, and I know it’s part of a basket of 30 products, imagine me going to Sleepy Owl to buy coffee, going to Epigamia to buy yoghurt, going to Veeba… I’ll go to 30 websites and put in 30 OTPs and 30 PINs. There is no consumer who will do that and add friction to his or her life.

We need to understand our job as a brand is to make the consumer’s life very easy. If that consumer has a particular preference for purchasing a particular product, keep in mind that the same product might be bought differently on different occasions.

But as exciting as an internet brand can be, it would be foolish to dismiss the gains from selling things the old-fashioned way. 

Wakefit knows this. It ran one retail store in March 2022. As of December 2024, it had almost 100. (Its products are also sold at 1,100 multi-brand outlets.) This is more important than you think. Having the shopper in person means the salesperson at the store can upsell or persuade the customer to buy that weighted blanket she didn’t know she wanted. 

Small wonder, then, that the average order value at a Wakefit store is close to 60% higher than sales made on its website. Wakefit is planning to use the funds from the IPO to more than double its own-store count. It’s not stopping there, though. 

While its average store is under 3,500 sq ft, Wakefit has Ikea-like aspirations too. With a third of its sales coming from furniture and other furnishings, Wakefit wants to set up “jumbo” stores, measuring 50,000–200,000 sq ft. The first such “big-box” outlet will be in Bengaluru.  

That’s as unlikely a destination as any for an internet-first brand.

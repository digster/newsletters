I’m always ready to be surprised when I start writing to you each week. Because, as I’ve told you earlier, the act of writing (on your own, not via LLMs) is also an act of thinking, which is also an act of connecting the dots.

21 grams.

That was the magical connection that formed in my head literally five minutes ago, I kid you not.

You see I was going to tell you that 21 grams is the amount of ground coffee in my first coffee of the morning each day. Yes, I know that’s a large shot, a “triple” in espresso parlance. Or if you want to be Italian about it, “triplo”.

My subsequent coffees are 18-gram shots, mere “doubles” or “doppios”. I don’t recollect when was the last time I used our single shot espresso basket. Just 14–15 grams of coffee? I’ll pass.

Back to 21 grams. Some of you might remember it as the gritty and dark 2003 movie by Alejandro Iñárritu, starring Sean Penn, Naomi Watts, and Benicio del Toro. Do you remember why the movie was called “21 grams” though?

Because 21 grams was once thought as the weight of the human soul.

Thus, in an entirely unpredictable but convenient plot twist, I learn just as I’m writing to you with the First Principles Coffee Edition why 21 grams is the amount of coffee it takes to refresh my soul each morning.

Don’t you just love the symmetry and beauty of that?

1. The First Principles Coffee Survey

I love (creating) surveys, and I cannot lie!

And I love it even more that you trust me and The Ken enough to be candid with us each time we use surveys to try and unearth interesting insights on various topics.

Over the years, I’ve understood that good surveys unearth latent emotions and unexpressed opinions. Hence, I usually shy away from basic, multiple-choice questions that merely end up as pie charts and bar graphs stating the obvious.

For the coffee survey, I asked you about your coffee versus tea preferences. I asked you about your favourite roasters or brands, and their blends. But the interesting questions were about coffee methods or gear.

I tried to get a sense of what you loved, but also what you hated (sort of). I asked you about things that you were curious about, but also about what you weren’t.

Before we dive in, a HUGE thanks to my wonderful designer colleagues. Kavipriya OG illustrated and animated the lovely, throbbing main image that perfectly manages to capture both the vibe of a Sunday and of coffee. All the creative infographics are by Sakshi Modi.

(Many of you made solid product recommendations. I’ll be taking them into FP’s 2025 Gift Guide, out next month!)

“First among equals”

Growing up in Delhi as a Malayali in the ’80s and ’90s, tea was the drink I was culturally imbued with. It’s a habit that’s stuck. Tea is still my first drink of the morning (my wife taught me how to create our own blend to balance taste and flavour, by mixing Darjeeling and CTC), but after that, it’s almost all coffee for the rest of the day.

“Unexpected, not undisputed”

This one about our preferred methods to brew coffee really had me doubling up. The winner, by far, was also the method very few people were curious about—South Indian-style filter coffee.

View in Browser

Yes, of course, this is influenced by the fact that a significant number of respondents must be South Indians. Makes sense, given The Ken is headquartered in Bengaluru. And we know that South Indians, especially Tamilians, are fiercely loyal to their traditional coffee method.

It’s hard to dislodge tastes and preferences that most likely took root in us via cultural transmission within homes and families. Those tastes endure. But could it be that such fierce loyalty, born of tradition and culture, sets such a high bar for alternatives that few can match up to? 🙂

Conversely, why are so few people curious about it? And why do some even consider it overrated? I’ll hold my theories for now.

Compare this to the French Press and Pourover, both methods seem split equally between those who love them, are curious about them, or consider them overrated.

Could it be that these methods have sort of a bimodal outcome depending on personal preferences or technique sensitivity? As in, when they’re good, they’re good. But if they’re bad, they’re terrible?

Or could it be that taken together with the Aeropress’ similar detractor scores, they represent a sort of average Joe (heh) backlash against high-touch “hipster” methods? Methods that are talked up quite a lot online, but require careful technique to avoid inconsistent results?

Nonetheless, it was interesting that the leading source of curiosity was the Aeropress, followed by the Moka pot. I’d not have guessed that. But then, why do so few people swear by those methods? 🙂

“Blue Head, Long Tail”

Blue Tokai has been my favourite roaster and café for years, so I was quite happy to see so many others sharing the taste. Their M S Estate coffee is the only one I’ve been buying for months now. It’s that good and consistent.

Even at The Ken’s offices, it’s Blue Tokai beans all the way. Their Amaltas Blend is what we’ve been getting since January.

That said, cracks have started forming in our daily post-lunch café loyalty. The last month has seen me and my regular collaborators switch some of our days to Nerlu café, which is a few hundred meters from our office.

Coming back to the list, Nescafe is at number two because it also subsumes Nespresso too.

“Technicolour Third Wave”

Whether single estates or blends, I’m torn between experimentation and consistency. On the one hand, once you find a great coffee that’s “perfect”, it seems pointless to keep looking for newer ones. What are you looking for? Better? Or different?

On the other hand, there is much joy to be had sampling coffee with different roasts, blends, estates, states, or even countries. Do we do it in search of “the best”, or simply to experience the glorious variety that exists?

View in Browser

“The Aficionado’s journey is arduous”

Once upon a time I had fallen down the hi-fi music rabbit hole. Thankfully, the mainstreaming of convenient, albeit shitty-quality streaming music rescued me from my own devices.

These days though, I’m happy in my coffee rabbit hole.

So, yes, I buy whole beans each week. Measure them on my espresso scale. Grind them in my single-dose grinder. Pour them into my bottomless portafilter basket. Tamp them with my spring-loaded tamper. And finally, extract it from my dual-boiler semi-automatic machine.

The brands and models are irrelevant, because the methods and mindsets are what matter more.

While we do occasionally have a pour over or French Press, the espresso is our anchor and soul space. It is a time-tested method that works. In an increasingly digital and formless world, the espresso machine is your steampunk refuge. Embrace it.

2. Introspections

Hello Ishan, it’s Tanim with your usual Sunday book section. 

The search for meaning is a cornerstone of existence. For me, it’s a vampire that haunts the quiet hours after my bedtime. Daylight hours are filled with sun, activity, and people—a convenient shield against rumination. But in the dark, alone with my thoughts, the spectre surfaces. It’s not always harmful, but it’s persistent, sucking the certainty from my life and making me question everything. 

My defense? Bedtime reading. 

I find a companion in the writer. I escape the cycle of my own life choices by immersing myself in their protagonist’s struggles. Their conflict is a powerful distraction, though they sometimes steer uncomfortably close to our own realities. Of course, this distraction is more than mere entertainment; it’s filled with moments that spark introspection about life itself. 

Today’s recommendations are two such introspective books that shine a light on the mosaic of human experience—both picked out from The Ken’s newsroom. If you have any similar recommendations, please share them with the community here. 

View in Browser

Seetharaman G, The Ken’s deputy editor, recommends The Young Man by Annie Ernaux. He says:

I have been getting through Annie Ernaux’s bibliography. She is (perhaps) the only memoirist to have won the literature Nobel. I haven’t come across anyone with the ability and willingness to look back at various phases of their life with as much clarity and write about them in such clinical, unsparing detail. This is her describing a former boyfriend in The Young Man (emphasis mine):

“His gestures and reflexes were dictated by a continual, inherited lack of money.

He was a young man of his times, who lived by the rule of ‘to each his own shit’. Work for him meant nothing more than a constraint with which he did not wish to comply, if other ways of life were possible.”

My top pick among her books, besides The Young Man, is Happening, which is about her getting an illegal abortion when she was in college.

Neha Mehrotra, The Ken’s desk editor, recommends Martyr! by Kaveh Akbar. She says:

I really loved this recent novel Martyr! by Iranian-American poet Kaveh Akbar. It’s basically about this poet—Cyrus Shams—who has an encounter with a dying artist, which throws into question everything he’s believed about himself and his past. It sounds dramatic, but it’s not really. Instead, it’s very quiet, introspective, and involves a lot of thinking about art and identity. Plus, it’s set in New York City, which I always love.

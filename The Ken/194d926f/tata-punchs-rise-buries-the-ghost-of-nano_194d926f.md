Good Morning Ishan,
No motorhead would want to be caught dead in a Maruti.
But the cars are cheap, have great fuel efficiency, and come with reliable after-sales service. Naturally, Maruti Suzuki is the default choice for millions of value-conscious Indian consumers. Four in 10 cars sold in India in 2024 carried the Suzuki badge.
But Tata Motors spoiled Maruti’s year-end celebrations by snagging the prized tag of India’s best-selling car, which the latter had held on to for four decades on the trot.
Tata Punch edged past Maruti Suzuki’s WagonR by selling over 200,000 units in 2024. Five years ago, the odds of Tata Motors making India’s favourite car were laughably long.
But Tata Motors began talking up the one thing that wasn’t part of Maruti’s sales pitch: safety. After its Nexon became the first Indian car to bag the full five stars in a global crash test, Punch followed suit at its launch in 2021.
But only attributing Punch’s success to safety would be undermining the car. Suprita unravels Punch’s swift rise to the top of India’s cut-throat car market in his [story](https://the-ken.com/story/tata-punchs-rise-buries-the-ghost-of-nano/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter) today.

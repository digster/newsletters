In a classic twist of situational irony, Bengaluru—India’s very own Silicon Valley—is turning out to be a hotspot of unexpected setbacks. Particularly in Devanahalli, just a 15-minute drive from the city’s international airport.

This part of the country’s tech hub houses Taiwanese electronics maker Foxconn’s new factory and is home to an array of electric-vehicle (EV) battery manufacturers. Yet, some of these companies are finding themselves tangled in a web of unmet promises.

As my colleague Shristi puts it, “The outskirts of Bengaluru are dotted with projects stuck in various stages of limbo.”

Take the Exide Energy Solutions facility, which has been in the works for over a year. It sits a mere 30km from Foxconn’s site, yet it’s struggling with under-supplied water and electricity.

And it’s not alone. International Battery Company (IBC), a California-based startup, has hit a funding wall. C4V, which had grand plans for a 5GWh battery plant, has gone radio silent. Meanwhile, NSure Reliable Power Solutions’ project is delayed, and Log9’s pivot indicates that this industry is still searching for solid ground.

This all comes at a time when other states are going full throttle to establish semiconductor projects and electronics manufacturing centres, aiming to become the new hubs of production.

The irony deepens when you consider the problem in two contexts.

First, the city: “The talent density in Bengaluru is very high. You need material science and electronics experts, and they’re all here,” said the co-founder of one of India’s cell-manufacturing pioneers. In fact, the city’s climate is also a factor in cell longevity. Second, the sector: Demand for these batteries in India is set to rise alongside the growing need for EVs.

What seems like regional trouble has roots that stretch beyond India’s borders—read how Shristi unravels the complexities in her first story.

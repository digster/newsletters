Good Morning Ishan,
 
Years ago, as a friend and I walked down a small street in Chennai, I spotted a handwritten sign outside a roadside shop that proclaimed “Egg omelettes” were available.
 
Brow wrinkled, I asked the aforementioned friend, doesn’t an omelette by default mean eggs? I mean, what other kind is there? “A non-egg omelette, of course,” was the blithe and entirely serious response. Don’t worry, I had no idea what that meant either. I chalked it up to some oddity of language and moved on in life.
 
But it might just have been a little prophetic. Because now, there’s a legit Indian startup trying to sell scrambled eggs without eggs. And another startup selling milk without milk. And of course, “achari chicken tikka” without chicken.
 
The past few years, my colleague Roshni writes, have seen dozens of companies around the world chasing “alt” meat—either lab-grown or made from plant matter. And investors are piling in (US-based Beyond Meat went public a couple of weeks ago, and the stock has more than tripled since). All in a race to decide the future of meat, driven by concerns over climate change, environment sustainability and animal cruelty.
 
In India, where poultry consumption has been spiking and beef eating may be underreported, institutes across the country are already working on growing meat from animal cells. An incredibly expensive task at the moment (the first lab-grown burger, six years ago, cost a quarter of a million euros).  
 
At the same time, companies like Good Dot are betting on plant-based meat for the nearer future. Deftly combining substances from apple fibre to chickpea protein to bamboo cellulose, they have one big goal: make something that tastes as good as real meat, or even better. And possibly also sell to the Indian vegetarian—killing two birds with one stone, only without the birds.
 
Today’s story is a little special—it’s the first of a series of “Future of…” pieces we intend to do over the coming months. Documenting a world where everything from housing to sleep is in flux, and, as my colleague Roshni puts it, how today’s disruptions could dictate tomorrow’s norms.

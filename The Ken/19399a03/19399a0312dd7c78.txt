The mystery of usury. What led to the RBI's decision to ban a few NBFCs from disbursing loans? 
 By Hari Krishna https://the-ken.com/writers/harithe-ken-com/

Picture this: You need some quick money. You ask the people around you, but no one has cash to move around on short notice. And you don’t have savings or investments that you can dip into quickly.

You decide to check your bank balance through your UPI app once again, even though you’re aware there isn’t enough money in your account. But on the app, you see another option. A loan. Even better, a loan that will apparently be disbursed very quickly, in just a few minutes. 

You tap on it, then enter a few key details to get yourself verified. The repayment schedule seems flexible enough. You only hesitate when you see the interest rate. It’s a bit on the higher side—definitely far more than your bank would charge you. But your bank isn’t going to disburse anything in just a few minutes. And with that flexible payment schedule, you feel like repayment won’t be too much of a hassle. 

So you hit the apply button, and your account is credited within just a few minutes. 

Banks, too, offer pretty much the same level of convenience and flexibility, but only when you have a fairly good credit score. Without one, it’s hard to avail any kind of unsecured loan. 

This gap is what many fintechs and NBFCs promised to bridge. They would open up credit to millions, they claimed, both individuals and small businesses. 

But in October this year, the Reserve Bank of India (RBI) asked four of these NBFCs to temporarily stop disbursing loans to customers. Navi Finserv Limited, Asirvad Micro Finance, Arohan Financial Services Limited, and DMI Finance Private Limited were charging “usurious” rates of interest, the central bank said. 

Of the lot, Navi Finserv has been allowed to resume https://www.business-standard.com/companies/start-ups/rbi-lifts-supervisory-restrictions-on-sachin-bansal-led-navi-finserv-124120200848_1.html its lending services as of 2 December, but that does nothing to change RBI’s choice of words. Usurious literally translates to “an unconscionable or exorbitant rate or amount of interest”. 

In this week’s episode of the Two by Two podcast, hosts Rohin Dharmakumar and Praveen Gopal Krishnan sat down with Ateesh Tankha, founder of Alsowise Content Solutions Private Limited and former head of Citi Merchant Services, and Mithun Sundar, Chief Partner Officer at Microsoft India and former CEO of Lendingkart, to break down some important questions around small unsecured loans in India. 

Just what is going on in the segment? What are the players involved doing? And what can the regulator do to protect everyone’s interests?

The banks in the fray

The private banks that partner and extend credit to NBFCs have their own interests to protect. After all, if these NBFCs give out loans to ventures or individuals who aren’t able to repay, then the banks stand to lose a lot. Which is why they hedge and charge the NBFCs a slightly higher rate of interest as protection. 

Ateesh and Mithun summed it up beautifully:

Mithun: My assumption is that the actual net borrowing rate for a bank, or its cost of capital, has been going up year-on-year. Because the current account surplus has been going down, and people are putting money in other places… people are saving less… And hence, they are also borrowing more from the open capital markets. So there’s a little pressure on the banks.

Second, the NBFC is getting the money from the bank at the 16–20% interest… And additionally, there’s another operating cost—you have to spend money on going and finding customers. And that cost is higher for an NBFC because of the kind of customer they are trying to reach. 

Ateesh: …because it can be a pure profit play, right (for banks)?

And if you think about it, I am essentially sub-leasing an activity to another entity because I am unable to—and in some instances, I don’t want to—take on the hassle of going to tier-three towns, villages, etc. Because overheads for marketing and origination fees, etc., I’ve given it to these people. They seem to be more efficient.

I’m going to charge them a rate, I have a minimum guarantee, and as long as they don’t go insolvent, I earn a very, very handsome profit from the entire operation.

Where does the RBI stand?

The RBI has always stepped in and made interventions when necessary. But it has also been hesitant with its answers when the aggrieved—in this case, the NBFCs—have approached it. Which brings us to a question Praveen posed during the conversation: what is the RBI’s motivation to step in?

Mithun: One, that’s a lobby question.

Second, there is a motivation, but even if you remove that, as a regulator, you will always be doing slightly arbitrary checks and balances.

You can’t assume perfect competition. The RBI may have a point of view that goes: “Hey, listen, I’m seeing a lot of fast and loose things in how these loans are being underwritten.” And is that a lot? Is it a little? That’s always going to be a debatable question.

I can sense that they’re pushing this back down a little before they allow it to expand. 

[…]

For me, the biggest hypothesis at this point is the strength of the banking sector… they’re asking for regulation in a part of the market that they are getting short-changed in, while the original market is also sort of compressing and getting more challenging.

The borrowers

The biggest edge that NBFCs have is that they are willing to go places in the country where credit is very hard to come by, and then enabling the customers in those locations to access it. But the big question, of course, is whether these customers are going to be able to repay the loan. 

Take a look at some of the figures Ateesh quoted during the discussion: 

Ateesh: The RBI may be looking at certain macroeconomic indicators, which is making it quite fearful for the overall health of the economy.

So in 2021, for instance, when you look at people with a household income of Rs 3 lakh or less, up to 28% had multiple unsecured loans. By 2023, that 28% had jumped to 72%. So the RBI could be scratching its head and thinking, “these people have so many loans… how on earth are they going to pay it back?” 

Because the RBI knows that there’s a lot of rural unemployment. The RBI knows that there’s a lot of rural retail inflation. And the RBI can see what kind of direct benefit transfer is happening from the government to these people. 

When the RBI adds all of this up, it worries them. Will allowing fintech companies and NBFCs to have a free run eventually lead to destabilising the economy? 

So it may have very little to do with usury… 

It may have very much to do with the ability of these families and households to pay back the loans that they owe.

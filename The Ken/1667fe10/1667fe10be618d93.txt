Good Morning Ishan,

Me:  Alexa, Can you check (editorial emails) if all reporters have sent their weekly story pitches?

Alexa: Yes, most have sent, barring three.

Me: Send a reminder to those who haven’t.

Alexa: Email, Slack, or text reminder?

Me: All three.

Well, that’s a very unusual Alexa conversation. Not exactly what Amazon has built or is training its intelligent assistant to do. But that’s my Alexa wish list. A kind of superhuman editorial assistant that I can use in real life (or work) situation.

Amazon’s Alexa is being trained to be an exceptional sales assistant, one that can show you products, answer your queries and manage to sell products or services. The tech today is nascent, but going by the 270% year-on-year increase in voice searches (on Google), voice commerce is just getting started. Amazon is encouraging it through discounts on items purchased through Alexa.

Not in India though. You still can’t make a transaction through Alexa (because of the required two-factor-authentication), but Amazon is busy building its skills store. 40,000 developers, 19,000 skills in just about a year. It ranges from launching games to ordering food to imparting English language lessons in remote schools. It reminds you of the fun and folly of the app store days.

Except, voice skill is no app. Not in the ease of development, use, or even remuneration for developers. At least not yet. Amazon says, even if a skill is going to be used only by one person, it is not going to stop building that. On their part, it appears, developers are solving their own problems rather than creating a user base. As for users, like, say GoIbibo or IndusInd Bank, it’s a mixed bag. 

In her story today, Arundhati takes you inside the Alexa India skills store. She says at this point, it’s more about winning developers’ mindshare, something Amazon learnt from AWS. Most of the skills getting built are for kids, also a category with the highest traction.

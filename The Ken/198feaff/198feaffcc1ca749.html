Good morning, Ishan! It’s once again the last day of the week and the month. As always, I’m writing to you from Saturday. It’s nice and sunny. The skies are bright and blue. And there’s a nice breeze to boot!

Since winter (or what passes for it in Bengaluru) is about to set in, I decided to write sitting in the balcony on a late afternoon. The white noise of passing traffic, rustling plants, and the occasional passing jet airplane is strangely soothing.

Though he’s been a persistent balcony barker, of late Gabru’s also getting really good at what I’d call “deep howling”. It’s usually reserved for moments when his best friend, Stella (also a black indie, like him), walks by the balcony. His throaty and heartfelt “awooooooo” is cute, amusing, and wondrous in equal measures. It makes me think “Call of the wild!” every single time! Stella, in turn, gives him a few barks of her own from down below before trotting off.

Here he is, right now, curled up at my feet.

If there’s a better template for a lazy and content weekend, I’ve yet to find it. Sorry, I missed an ingredient—coffee!

It’s funny how long I’ve been writing to you without talking about coffee. How can any day, much less an indulgent weekend, be complete without coffee?

Like most Indians of my generation (kids of the 80s), I grew up in a tea-drinking household. Love for tea is ingrained in us Indians.

I got into coffee about a decade or so ago. Or more like, fell!

If you ask me what is singularly *the* best purchase of my life, I’d have to say our espresso machine. We bought it right at the beginning of the pandemic in 2020. I would not be lying if I said it was a key reason we got through that dark period still relatively sane. It’s been our daily companion and source of joy.

The 10 minutes it takes to weigh beans, grind them, make the espresso, steam the milk, and then mix it all together (we’re primarily cappuccino drinkers) is both my domain and mind space. It is both a ritual and a reward.

My most recent “upgrade” (you sort of live between them once you’re down the coffee rabbit hole) was a grinder that also has a single-dose attachment. This allows me to switch beans literally cup to cup, instead of having to go pack to pack. And my current favourite coffee is Blue Tokai’s MS Estate.

1. Can you afford to wait?

I’ve been going through the hundreds of people who applied to us after I said I was looking for talented and ambitious colleagues to help imagine and build the next ten years of The Ken. The sheer number and quality of responses was overwhelming enough that it took me longer than I’d budgeted for to go through all the applications and set up first conversations.

You must have heard some versions of the adage, “The best candidates aren’t actively looking for a job.” It means, as employers, you must figure out how to find them and convince them to join you.

I don’t fully agree with it, but there’s still a lot of merit in it.

There’s a corporate variant of this too, which goes, “The best companies are bought, not sold.”

The reason I brought up this example is because in both cases—whether a professional or a company—not needing to make a decision can often be a position of strength.

If you aren’t actively looking for a job, then it means you can continue with whatever you’re doing till you find a job that you want.

If you aren’t looking to sell your company, then you can wait until someone comes to you with an offer that might convince you to.

In both cases, your ability to wait things out and not be rushed into making a decision is your superpower. You can play the long game. You can reduce the number of times you need to make a life- or career-altering decision.

In the words of the late Charlie Munger, “You don’t make money when you buy and you don’t make money when you sell.  You make money when you wait.”

The legendary financial success of Berkshire Hathaway, the firm headed by Munger and his partner Warren Buffet, was owed to its ability to play the long game. The two called it the investment discipline of “waiting for a fat pitch”. The analogy referred to a baseball batter waiting for a ball thrown by the pitcher down the middle of the plate, making it easiest to hit hard.

“Look at those hedge funds—you think they can wait? They don’t know how to wait! In my personal portfolio, I have sat for years at a time with $10 to $12 million in treasuries or municipals, just waiting, waiting….

“As Jesse Livermore said, ‘The big money is not in the buying and selling… but in the waiting.’”

Poor Charlie’s Almanack: The wit and wisdom of Charles T. Munger

But what enables a company or a person to not make a decision, but instead, to wait for the right opportunity?

I’d say it’s two things—financial stability and strategic clarity.

If sustaining your current life or business requires a constant and increasing flow of money, then you will need to make decisions that bias you towards the short term. If you’re a company, perhaps it’s your burn rate or debt.

I think this is the most practical way to think about debt: As debt increases, you narrow the range of outcomes you can endure in life.

That’s so simple. But it’s different from how debt is typically viewed, which is a tool to pull forward demand and leverage assets, where the only downside is the cost of capital (the interest rate).

Two things are important when you view debt as a narrowing of endurable outcomes.

One is you start to ponder how common volatility is.

I hope to be around for another 50 years. What are the odds that during those 50 years I will experience one or more of the following: Wars, recessions, terrorist attacks, pandemics, bad political decisions, family emergencies, unforeseen health crises, career transitions, wayward children, and other mishaps?

One-hundred percent. The odds are 100%.

When you think of it like that, you take debt’s narrowing of survivable outcomes seriously.

How I think about debt, Morgan Housel

If you’re a professional, perhaps it’s the lifestyle you’re used to, your mortgage repayments, or your desired wealth.

Financial insecurity tends to put us on a timetable where we need to make decisions, regardless of whether they’re the right ones for us in the long term.

Which brings me to the second point—strategic clarity.

There’s no point in waiting for the right time if you don’t have a sense of what you’re waiting for.

What are you waiting for?

A great analogy, again from Munger and Buffett, is that of a “ticket with twenty slots”.

“I could improve your ultimate financial welfare by giving you a ticket with only twenty slots in it so that you had twenty punches—representing all the investments that you get to make in a lifetime. And once you’d punched through the card, you couldn’t make any more investments at all. Under those rules, you’d really think carefully about what you did, and you’d be forced to load up on what you’d really thought about. So you’d do so much better.” 

Warren Buffett, quoted in Poor Charlie’s Almanack

The world conspires to force you to make a decision. But can you resist it?  

As Rudyard Kipling said, “If you can wait and not be tired by waiting…”

If you could make, say, only 10 major decisions in your entire career, what would they be?

1. If you use AI in your work, how is it changing how you work? How is it changing you?

“This is a superbly articulated thought process—thanks for writing this. Vibed completely. I am a music composer, and I do use AI for certain activities. The idea of “passes” has existed in our field for ages, for as long as clients have. So it’s not that new to us. The only difference seems to be communication. Telling the AI “exactly” what you need seems to be the key. That “exactly” comes down to an infinite spectrum of experiences, aesthetics, languages, tropes, etc., that first needs to be made very, very clear in your own head before we even type a prompt into any generative AI interface. So essentially, it’s just like making music. Be clear first. Then approach any tool you wish. Otherwise, everyone and everything can take you down endless rabbit holes, and what comes out might not always be what you wanted.”

Swastik Acharyya

1. If you use AI in your work, how is it changing how you work? How is it changing you?

AI has surely made my life easier, especially for research purposes. I use the “deep research” tool to carry out sector/industry analysis, investment analysis. With a single prompt, it provides enough text to get started and go down the rabbit hole with the attached sources. Other than that, it immensely helps in gauging various compliance, regulatory, and taxation aspects, along with scenario analysis. Earlier, I used to have a negative attitude towards unfamiliar problems. For example, coming from an investment background, I used to get annoyed when I had to work on taxation and operational issues. Yes. While using deep research, I generally don’t need to check multiple tabs, pages. This helps me to stay focused on a single window.

Getting answers to normal day-to-day work related questions. Basically, as a replacement for “Google search”.

4. As you got more comfortable with AI tools, what newer or novel use-cases did you evolve towards using them for?

Recently, I used chatbots to analyse my health report. Gave them the last two reports to do a trend analysis.

5. What are the top 3 AI tools you use at work, ranked in order of importance? If you can, tell us what you primarily use them for. 

Gemini (research), ChatGPT (everything), Gamma (powerpoint deck), Fathom (record meetings).

3. Unhappy endings

Hi Ishan, it’s Tanim here. 

Before we dive into the community’s top recommendations this week (and this is where you can send in your own recommendations), here’s what I just finished reading.

What does it feel like to face the inevitable death of someone you love? To desperately want to let them go, while fighting just as hard to hold on?

For Connor, the protagonist of A Monster Calls, this agonising conflict doesn’t just define his life, it also brings a monster to his door. 

View in Browser

This book was written by Patrick Ness based on an idea from the late author of children’s books Siobhan Dowd, who began developing the story while battling cancer herself. Perhaps it was her way of expressing her grief and truth about living with the illness, because the story follows Connor as he struggles to process his mother’s advancing cancer.

In just over a hundred pages, this book does so much. You walk with Connor as he grapples with his mom’s illness, bullying at school, and the terrifying future he faces without her. These truths gnaw at him, demanding to be faced, even as he desperately tries to push them away. 

It’s a perfect bedtime story… if you don’t mind shedding a few tears by the end. 

“Stories don’t always have happy endings.” This stopped him. Because they didn’t, did they? That’s one thing the monster had definitely taught him. Stories were wild, wild animals and went off in directions you couldn’t expect.

Here are two recommendations from the community for you this week. 

The first one is Everything is Tuberculosis by John Green, sent in by Tulika Jain. 

In Tulika’s words: 

“Everything is Tuberculosis chronicles John Green’s journey exploring the past, present, and future of the world’s most infectious disease. Its main premise is that tuberculosis is not merely a disease caused by bacteria; it is a disease that continues to exist because of colonialism, racism, inequitable resource distribution, and capitalism. It’s not very long—I finished it in two sittings—but is somehow both all-encompassing and deeply personal. It’s the only non-function book that has ever made me cry.”

View in Browser

The second one is The Prisoner in his Palace by Will Bardenwerper, sent by Navin Agarwal.

Navin says: 

“The book is based on firsthand accounts from the American guards, government officials, interrogators, and others connected to Saddam Hussein’s detention and trial. It offers insights into his personality behind bars without diminishing the severity of his crimes. Overall, it presents a nuanced portrait of a notorious dictator—as a human being—and reveals the profound impact this experience had on the soldiers tasked with guarding him.”

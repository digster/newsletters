Trade Tricks
Tue, 02 Dec 25
A weekly newsletter about the biggest changes in commerce—focusing on shifts that matter to you.
Good Morning Ishan,
On a cloudy Sunday afternoon in Hyderabad, Technosport’s new flagship store in Tolichowki is overflowing with customers. By most measures, this shouldn’t be a crowded store. Not for an activewear brand with no celebrity face, no discounts, and minimal marketing.
Yet here they are: students, runners, retirees, gym-goers, construction workers, filling out every inch of the 4,200 sq ft space. They spill across the white-and-black flooring, surrounding two mannequins frozen mid-sprint under a bold “MADE TO MOVE” sign. The yellow silhouette of a man running—Technosport’s logo—flashes from racks packed with t-shirts, joggers, and shorts.
“Nobody had heard of us before this. We’re only now getting known,” said Puspen Maity, the CEO. “But from day one, we built the company with one idea: the product is the hero. If we can give every Indian international-quality activewear they can actually afford, the rest will follow.”
True to its promise, its basic tees sit at Rs 400 at full price, unusually low where most shoppers have been trained to expect ‘premium’ pricing, especially for apparel that looks and feels like global activewear.
Technosport has existed since the mid-2000s, but its rise is recent and steep. Since 2022, it has grown 40–50% every year. In 2024, it [crossed](https://retail.economictimes.indiatimes.com/news/apparel-fashion/apparel/technosport-crosses-rs-300-cr-revenue-mark-plans-7-ebos-this-fiscal/111757322) Rs 300+ crore in revenue and sold a staggering 25 million units of activewear—the highest volume of any brand in India, according to a senior representative at the company. Today, it moves nearly two million garments a month, competing with Nike, Adidas, Decathlon, and homegrown stars like HRX. The latter, co-owned by Hrithik Roshan, declared a Rs 1,000 crore revenue in 2024.
This little-known brand from Tirupur becoming India’s highest-volume activewear seller isn’t a marketing story. It’s a business model lesson.
Global sportswear brands like Nike and Adidas built their India strategy around the gym-goer and the weekend runner—the premium urban consumer willing to spend Rs 1,500–2,000 on a t-shirt. Even homegrown brands, inspired by them, targeted the same, small aspirational slice. Decathlon, often seen as the benchmark for affordable performance wear in India, still designs for organised sport and leisure. Technosport is solving a different, more Indian problem.
In Coimbatore, a delivery rider wearing Technosport’s basic tee said he buys the brand because “it dries in 10 minutes and doesn’t smell”.
“That driver is part of our target customer base. If you have a body, you’re an athlete,” said a senior executive at the company, co-opting a quote from competitor Nike’s co-founder.
And there lies Technosport’s biggest insight: in India, movement isn’t a lifestyle choice, it’s a livelihood.
It recognised a large, ignored customer base: Indians whose daily lives are physically demanding. Farmers, fishermen, delivery riders, factory workers, drivers and construction labourers—millions who spend hours in heat and humidity through long workdays.
For them, activewear is a solution category, not a fashion category. Sweat, odour, and UV exposure are everyday discomforts that demand functional apparel. Yet global brands charge 4–5X more for products with similar underlying technology—like moisture-wicking fabric or UPF 50+ protection, which means only one in fifty harmful rays gets through.
Basic tees at Adidas cost Rs 1,100–2,000. HRX sells them at Rs 700 or more at full price. Decathlon offers tees starting at Rs 550 without discounts. Technosport plants itself at the opposite end of that spectrum. Its basic tee starts at Rs 319, without discounts—much lower than all the brands above.
And the reason it can deliver that quality at that price, according to two sources in the firm, is one edge most competitors lack: in-house manufacturing.
Most Indian activewear brands—HRX, Cultsport, Alcis—share the same origin story: design a garment, outsource production to a vendor, and rely on marketing to do the rest. Even Decathlon, which manufactures a significant portion of its products in India, still operates through a broad supplier ecosystem spread across dozens of partner factories.
Technosport, however, built an entire manufacturing universe of its own. The company knits its own fabric, dyes it, processes it, cuts it, and stitches it. Every stage happens inside Technosport’s factories in Tirupur.
“Because we manufacture in-house, everything is in our control,” Maity said. “The fabric, the pricing, the quality, the supply. Everything.”
That control is why Technosport can deliver performance apparel comparable to global brands at one-third or one-fifth the price.
Others can’t easily copy Technosport’s model because India lacks a strong activewear manufacturing ecosystem. Yarn suppliers are few, and performance-grade knitting, dyeing, and processing units are scarce. So most brands—global and Indian—import fabric or finished garments, or outsource to vendors who produce for multiple labels, where quality and innovation slip out of their control.
This is why Technosport rarely needs celebrity endorsements or deep discounting to drive sales.
“Many people forget the ‘product’ in the 4Ps,” a senior executive said. “Once you crack the product, other competitors can’t follow because that capex, knowledge, and R&D journey takes decades.”
This is a classic ‘build the machine before building the brand’ story.
India’s athleisure boom over the last decade has largely been a D2C story. Brands were born on marketplaces, fuelled by algorithms, discounting, and relentless performance marketing.
HRX, for instance, spent heavily on customer acquisition to stay in front of shoppers endlessly scrolling through Myntra, according to a source close to the firm.
Technosport’s path couldn’t be more different.
The brand began offline, long before D2C existed, selling through mom-and-pop shops, general trade counters, and multi-brand sports retailers. These stores enforced discipline: stable prices, predictable margins, and virtually no deep discounting. It was a product consumers already trusted, even if they didn’t yet recognise the logo.
That changed only in the last five years, when the company shifted into modern retail and opened exclusive brand outlets (EBOs). It plans to invest about Rs [200 crore](https://www.business-standard.com/companies/news/activewear-maker-technosport-to-invest-200-cr-to-open-300-exclusive-stores-125091400161_1.html) into 300+ exclusive stores over the next two years. Twenty-eight outlets are already operational, including the largest one in Hyderabad and some in Bengaluru. South India remains the core market, but expansion in the East, West, and North is now accelerating. The company expects its 40–50% growth rate to continue and projects revenue will reach Rs 1,000 crore by 2026.
This “offline-first, product-first” approach explains the sudden spike in visibility. For years, Technosport was the kind of brand people wore without noticing the label, like a fisherman on a hot afternoon in Nagapattinam.
Now the logo is on high-street stores and in crowded malls. But the philosophy behind it hasn’t changed: build a product that works for the average Indian, price it honestly, and let it find its way.

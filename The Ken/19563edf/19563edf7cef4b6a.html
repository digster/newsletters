The big Birla bet no one is talking about. The group has a shot at striking it big in B2B e-commerce

By Seetharaman G
4 min read
View in browser

Good Morning Ishan,

After rattling incumbents and their investors with his group’s entry into the decorative-paints market, billionaire Kumar Mangalam Birla did something similar with another industry last week.

Ultratech Cement, the most valuable company in Birla’s empire, said that it would now make cables and wires by investing Rs 1,800 crore ($206 million) over two years. Investors of companies such as Polycab India and KEI Industries panicked in response and wiped out a fifth of their value in a single day.

Not everyone is convinced this is a business Ultratech should be in; the stock slid 5% after the announcement. But the fact that there are 400 companies making cables and wires, with the highest market share at a mere 20%, means a new entrant of Ultratech’s size (market cap: Rs 3 lakh crore) could inflict serious pain on its rivals.

Ultratech’s parent, Grasim Industries, has already taken the fight to the once unassailable Asian Paints, as Kuldip Raina, a top executive at rival Shalimar Paints, told me in January.

“Birla Opus has brought in the kind of price disruption we have never seen in paints,” said Raina. The price difference between the top four players is 5–6%, with Asian Paints commanding the highest, he added. But Birla has launched its products at a 12% discount to Asian Paints.

But the dark horse among the Aditya Birla Group’s ambitious new businesses is one that few are talking about.

In 2023, Grasim rolled out an online store for building materials—everything from cement and steel to bath fittings and switches. The startling rise of startups such as Ofbusiness and Infra Market caught Grasim’s attention.

Ofbusiness runs a marketplace for such products, and Infra Market aggregates spare capacities of small manufacturers to supply similar items for infrastructure and housing projects. They are worth between $2.5 billion and $3 billion and have that one thing most VC-funded companies don’t: profits.

What’s not to like about such a business, especially when the products in question are right up the Aditya Birla Group’s alley?

It’s odd, then, that Birla Pivot hasn’t gotten the attention it deserves from analysts and investors. One reason is the optics around it. Birla Pivot doesn’t need anywhere near the same kind of capital as, say, paints. Grasim’s total capex for paints in FY25 is almost Rs 3,000 crore. For Birla Pivot? A mere Rs 20 crore.

The other reason is the ease of assessing the paints business, thanks to multiple listed companies in the space. Equity analysts, however, are not familiar with the economics of selling construction materials online. 

Birla Pivot may not be as profitable for Grasim as its other businesses. Infra Market and Ofbusiness have operating margins of under 6%, while Grasim’s is almost 3X. 

But the absolute profits could be juicy enough. 

In FY24, its first year of operations, Birla Pivot had sales of Rs 1,000 crore. In another three years, it plans to grow its revenue more than 8X. Ofbusiness’ top line in FY24 was Rs 19,300 crore, and Infra Market’s was Rs 14,530 crore.

Like Infra Market, Birla Pivot is eager to have its own brands across a range of categories, which already include tiles, plywood, and bathware. On a recent Grasim earnings call, the chief executive of Birla Pivot said its ultimate goal is to “give almost like a B2C kind of fulfillment experience to B2B”. There’s the rub.

The Aditya Birla Group has a spotty reputation in consumer businesses, so I wonder if its “B2C” benchmark is high enough.

When someone mentions the Aditya Birla Group, you think aluminium, cement, and maybe financial services. Certainly very large businesses. Some are quite profitable. But Birla is not a name you associate with consumer brands.
Not a lot of them at any rate. I don’t want to incur anyone’s wrath by calling the telco Vi, the result of a merger between Vodafone and Birla-owned Idea Cellular, a popular brand.

The group’s decision to venture into decorative paints in February was partially an attempt at remedying this longstanding problem.

This is a perfect picturization of a typical feature of Indian workplaces. If you do something too well, like writing code or copy, you’re asked to stop doing it and instead manage five more people doing it less efficiently than you did. You’ve even been taught to look at this as development. They call it a promotion, and they often cap your salary unless you choose to make the shift. 

In turn, we grow up learning to see this ladder as a natural progression. But everyone who has ever climbed this ladder knows that no one, absolutely no one, prepares you for what is about to hit you. 

Managing people is a different and utterly perpendicular skill set to what you might have been promoted for. Teams often don’t need managers to be experts in a field; instead, they need people who can point them in the right direction and allow them to fail and eventually learn. But that seldom happens.

The 2022 layoffs didn’t spare anyone. And it didn’t spare these middle managers too. 

There is a gaping hole at the centre of how we live our work lives, how we manage people, and what this means for anyone who is or wants to be a manager someday.

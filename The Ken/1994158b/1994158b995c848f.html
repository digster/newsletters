This is the last edition of The Nutgraf. 

No, I’m not going anywhere. 

Starting from next Saturday, there’s going to be a brand-new (and exciting!) product that’ll hit your inboxes and podcast feeds, and I’m going to be a part of that. I don’t want to reveal too much yet, but let me just say that it’s tremendously scary and fun—which is pretty much what makes anything worth doing. Oh, and also, I won’t be alone, there’ll be a couple of other familiar faces from The Ken. Keep an eye out. 

So… er… what happened?

I suppose this is the part when I’m supposed to say things like discontinuing The Nutgraf was one of the most difficult decisions I made. 

But I’m not going to say that because, frankly, that’s not true. In many ways, the decision to stop The Nutgraf was easy—and it came to me with as much clarity as the decision to start it six years ago. My colleagues have been incredibly supportive and excited about what I’m going to do next. If anything, the fact that both the launch and conclusion of The Nutgraf were met with the same warmth, encouragement, and positivity says everything you need to know about The Ken. This is the greatest privilege in the world.

However, it was pretty obvious to me that the newsletter had hit its final chapter. 

And that’s because the world in which The Nutgraf was born and thrived in has also reached its last and final chapter. In product management, people describe this idea of a “product-market fit”, which is essentially this belief that an early-stage product must continuously pivot and change until it “fits” what the market needs. The underlying assumption is that the market is static, and so the product needs to be dynamic. 

But the reverse also happens, albeit less occasionally. Sometimes markets change and enter a period of upheaval, and thus, existing products, no matter how successful they’ve been in the past, no longer remain relevant. 

And I believe that’s more or less what happened. 

The old world is gone, and the new world is here. 

And with it, the old rules are gone, and nobody knows what the new rules are. 

Just as I can confidently state that we’re at the moment when the previous era of India’s product and technology revolution has ended, I can also pinpoint when that era began. And that date is March 2006, when an article was published in The New York Times with the headline, “Is the Next Silicon Valley Taking Root in Bangalore?”

Twenty young engineers, mostly from the Indian Institute of Technology, India’s premier technology school, peer into computer monitors in the no-frills office of Read-Ink Technologies, a start-up company housed in a small building in the bustling Indiranagar neighborhood of this city. 

Bangalore’s flourishing outsourcing companies, including Infosys Technologies and Wipro, have attracted worldwide attention with their global clients and tens of thousands of workers. Less known are the many technology start-ups, like Read-Ink, that have taken root here in recent years.

The new firms are drawn by the region’s big pool of engineering graduates, many of whom have expertise in esoteric new technologies. That advantage, coupled with labor costs much lower than those of Silicon Valley, is starting to turn Bangalore, long a center for lower-end outsourcing services, into a center of higher-end innovation.

Some of these firms are self-financed, others have capital from the West. Some are run by foreigners. Others are founded by Indians, including returnees from overseas. 

I was one of the twenty engineers at Read-Ink when this story was published. That’s where I began my career, two decades ago, straight out of engineering college. This was the beginning of the beginning.

Since then, I’ve lived through India’s great startup and technology revolution as an insider. I’ve witnessed it at companies building products that were at the forefront of the e-commerce—and later, the mobile—revolutions. And then, at The Ken, for the last six years, every Saturday, I’ve written about it as an outsider-insider. Even in my role, I’ve gone from Product to Operations, and back to Product again. 

But now, 20 years later, we are at the end of the beginning. 

Last week, Bernstein Research put out a note that symbolised this moment, that was titled OpenAI, Perplexity’s India push—a wake-up call for Indian AI. The opening sentence of the report is, “India is staring down a strategic tech crisis.” The authors make several sharp points about how AI now threatens to disrupt practically everything that India has achieved thus far, and urge radical changes in policy. Particularly, this section stood out to me: 

With such a resource constraint and lack of decisive government support, India Inc has done what it could do best: using foreign LLMs to build tailor-made solutions for different industries – be it medical, finance or HR. While these, too, serve a growing need, there are two primary risks India is taking here. The first is contracting itself to a niche service ecosystem, much like the IT service giants have catered to their clientele. Here, a few firms that can manage deep expertise will survive, but many others will perish, but scale will be far more difficult since even firms in other countries can form their own application layers. Many startups are counting the Indian cultural norms as their differentiating factor and if data protection laws keep strengthening – application layer in AI will become more and more localized.

The second is a bigger risk, which is when these large behemoths gain enough sector knowledge over a period of time to render any of the vertical LLM ineffective and not worth the extra money. Many startups thriving on “sector expert” themes may be the casualties here. 

Perhaps this is more pessimistic than what you believe, but it’s hard to come up with strong counterpoints. Sure, AI may be overhyped, but the fact that we’ve now entered a different era seems to be apparent to me. Even if AI never attains the best version of itself, the world has already decisively changed. And everything from capital, geopolitics, and a cultural shift with work, careers, and society is playing its part. The old world is not coming back. 

And this new world is filled with paradoxes. 

One of these is the capital paradox. India’s VCs love to talk about their ample “dry powder” for AI on podcasts—but on the ground, there’s little sign of AI startups actually scaling or getting funded. Despite staring down an existential crisis, companies like Infosys and TCS are sitting on huge piles of cash reserves which they are deploying with stock buybacks—a telling sign that they don’t have any other good ideas on what to do with it.

Then there’s how AI is used. Most of the AI startups I’m seeing around me are trying to be a part of the new world, but I’m not sure they’re going far enough in sacrificing their past. India’s founders and CEOs are still talking about what percentage of their code is written using AI—the language of efficiency and productivity. SaaS companies are pivoting to AI and adding agents while simultaneously sticking to the same playbook they’ve developed over decades. 

I’ve seen AI startups who believe their advantage could come from building products that truly understand India—whether that means language or culture. Recently, a founder I was speaking to made an offhand observation that despite the buzz, all Indic voice AI use-cases seem to lead to the same destination: debt collection.

There are other clues that show how things really are. Like how Sarvam AI, which is the vanguard of India’s AI mission, keeps changing its mind between making its model open-source or closed. Or how Indian AI startups that start to see some early traction immediately move to the Bay Area. And the less said about Krutrim, the better. 

I’m not even getting into the societal, cultural, and political consequences of this technology and what it means for the lives, careers, and futures of Indians. I think this seems to be far more consequential than, say, e-commerce, food-delivery, or ride-sharing.  

But the most important paradox doesn’t come from the head, but from the heart. 

For the past six years, I’ve shown up in your inboxes simply because I’ve loved writing this newsletter. Maybe The Nutgraf will come back at some point in the future. Maybe it won’t. Maybe you’ll come along with me to where we’re going. Maybe you won’t. If you do, perhaps the places we go to may feel less enjoyable. Sometimes you’ll miss the old days. I’ll miss them too. 

Thank you for reading, for all the emails and feedback, and for spreading the word about The Nutgraf. I hope you’ll stick around for what’s next.

But like everything I wrote about in today’s edition, this too must come to an end—and make space for what’s next. 

One of my favourite books I’ve read recently is The Creative Act by Rick Rubin, which I read when I did a ten-day trek through the Langtang Valley in Nepal a couple of years ago. I’d recommend it wholeheartedly for anyone, and there’s a line from it that I’ve been thinking about of late: 

“Sometimes the best thing you can do is destroy what you’ve made. In that destruction, something more honest can emerge.”

I’ll see you next week. 

Regards,
Praveen Gopal Krishnan

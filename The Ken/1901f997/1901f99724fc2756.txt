Good morning Ishan! Sunday seems like the worst day of the week to talk about momentum. I mean, it’s the day when we allow ourselves the time and space to slow down and come to rest.

But perhaps for that reason, Sunday is the best day to think about momentum. For the same reason that summer is a good time to think about the monsoon. It’s easier to understand and imagine the importance of something when we’re not in it.

We understand momentum in the sense of physics—“the product of an object’s mass and velocity.” But we rarely think about it in the context of our own lives, or how it influences our decisions and career trajectories.

I kick myself for not truly understanding two vital aspects of career momentum for perhaps the better part of a decade or two! Firstly, how much easier it is to succeed when you are able to use your existing momentum consciously. And secondly, how much harder it is to achieve momentum when you’re in a state of inertia.

And perhaps the most counterintuitive thing I learnt around building momentum when you’re in a state of inertia is to not spend too much time thinking about the first step.

1. Letting go of the first one

“Our first idea was somewhat along the similar lines of UrbanClap. The only thing we could actually do (during) the weekends. We started trying to write what is called as a business plan. Like, we thought, okay, the idea has to be unique, even after reading so much from Hacker News, Joel Spolsky’s blog, Basecamp (37signals), and all of those things. 

And still, we fell into the trap thinking we are supposed to come up with an original idea.”

That was Krish Subramanian, the co-founder and CEO of subscription billing platform Chargebee, last valued at US$3.5 billion. I spoke with Krish for the First Principles podcast last June https://the-ken.com/podcasts/first-principles/krish-subramanian-chargebee/.

Most founders of successful businesses rarely talk about their early bets that didn’t work out. But among those who do, like Krish, there is a certain happiness at getting it out of the way. They realise—of course with the benefit of hindsight—that their first startups were just not meant to be. But they absolutely also realise that they still needed to be done.

It almost seems like a dichotomy. If many first startups were simply not meant to be, how could they also need to be?

Because for many professionals who harbour hopes of starting up at some point, even a failed first startup provides the necessary momentum to start the second. The one that matters.

It was the same for me. I’ve nursed dreams of starting up since, well, finishing my MBA in 2001. Over the years, I registered numerous domain names for companies yet to be created; created complicated Excel-based business plans; read everything there was about pitch decks; and tried to convince friends and former classmates to start up.

I finally did manage to start up in 2014, only to fail and fold in 2016. But even the momentum of deceleration and shutting down was enough to propel me into joining up with my co-founders to start The Ken that very same year.

“Because we give a disproportionate amount of credit to the uniqueness of the idea, thinking that is what makes a company successful. But it’s probably not even one percentage of eventual success of any company. Right? It comes down to execution and persistence, which is 99% of the effort,” said Krish.

This isn’t just about starting a new company. It could just as easily apply to, say, moving into a new line of work or getting fitter. Don’t fixate too much on the first step.

Instead, imagine needing to get past it (or just getting it done). Take the weight or expectations of success off it. The only thing that matters is, quite simply, taking the first step.

You’ll be amazed at how much easier it is to take that first step once you have reduced your expectations from it.

A wonderful concept I came across to describe the first step is as a “transition” step.

“Our minds and bodies have an incredible capacity to adapt to just about anything. The hard part is rarely being in the new normal, it’s adjusting to the new normal.

The hard part is the transition.

Now bring to mind that thing you are having a hard time getting started on. I’m willing to bet that your greatest struggle isn’t actually doing the thing, it’s getting started on doing the thing.

The biggest challenge to moving forward on anything is the transition to working on it. It almost always represents a shift from doing something comfortable (a warm bath, sending simple emails, knocking straightforward tasks off a to-do list, completing transactional conversations) to doing something uncomfortable (a cold bath, starting that proposal, initiating that hard conversation, facing a blank page).

We tend to think that getting traction on our most important work requires that we be skilled and proficient at that work — but that’s not quite right. The real thing we need to be skilled and proficient in is moving through the moment before the work.

Once we make the shift, then doing the work itself, consistently and over time, will make us proficient at the work.

Which means that the skill we really need to develop — and it is a skill — is transitioning.”

How to Actually Start the Task You’ve Been Avoiding by Peter Bregman, Harvard Business Review https://hbr.org/2018/05/how-to-actually-start-the-task-youve-been-avoiding

2. Why loiter? and other assorted works 

Dear reader, welcome back to another edition of the First Principles books section. This week, we have an absolutely random collection of book recommendations for you. There is—forgive me for sounding so cliched—something in here for everyone. Truly. 

For instance, there was a perfect one for me. I was pleasantly surprised to see a book recommended by someone from the community that’s been on my reading list for… years, perhaps. 

I’d say it’s a book about walking through cities, fundamentally. But the authors use a different word, one that really makes all the difference. 

It’s a book about loitering.

I hope I’m not the only one who immediately thought of school teachers screaming over the din as you scurried through corridors: “Stop loitering! Get to your classrooms!”

But this is the exact opposite: the book talks about the ease and comfort of loitering at your own will, in a city of your own residence. Without being in a rush and without being scared. It’s a right, the authors will tell you, but for women, it’s a privilege.

Among other things, the book talks at length about women’s access to public places. The three authors made “a case for loitering as a fundamental act of claiming public space and ultimately, a more inclusive citizenship. We believe the right to loiter for all has the potential to undermine public space hierarchies.”

‘Why Loiter?’ Ten years have passed since the seminal book on women alone in cities was published, Scroll.in https://scroll.in/article/993810/why-loiter-ten-years-have-passed-since-the-seminal-book-on-women-alone-in-cities-was-published

Why Loiter? was recommended by Giridharan Raghuraman, who says that “personally, the book helped me think beyond the easily understood ‘male privilege’ and ruminate on a few facets of intersectionality.”

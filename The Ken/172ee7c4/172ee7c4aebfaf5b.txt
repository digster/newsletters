Its investors call it the "fastest-growing SaaS company in history https://twitter.com/RajanAnandan/status/1263049178193641472".

Even in a world where hyperbole is par for the course, this is probably an audacious claim. All the more so given the fact that this is a SaaS company with zero revenue.

Yet it has raised $60 million in its latest funding round with the total funding now touching nearly $90 million.

At a valuation of close to $300 million.

How can a B2B SaaS company with no revenue raise so much money? Why was it valued so high?

The company in question is Khatabook, a digital bookkeeping app for small merchants. Admittedly, Khatabook has two things going for it. Firstly, it is in a "hot" space—the digitisation of small businesses.

Secondly, it is seeing great usage traction with millions of users (albeit all non-paying users). But neither of these aspects would entirely justify the funding raising or the valuation.

In today's story, Arundhati gives us a rare glimpse into the "VC calculus" behind these big numbers—using Khatabook's funding deck to explain how its founders packaged their pitch, and how and why such a narrative appealed to investors.

She also throws light on the largely unknown story of Khatabook's origins and traces how a one-man outfit evolved into a highly-valued VC darling. It is a story that establishes that while product-market-fit is important, the founder-investor-fit is often far more critical.

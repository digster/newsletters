Good Morning Ishan,

The late TN Seshan—the formidable former chief election commissioner of India—once remarked that his native Palakkad district in the southern state of Kerala is known for its cooks, crooks, and civil servants.

Move a bit further up north, and you’ll find that the Udupi district in Karnataka, too, has many claims to fame. Its Krishna temple (that draws visitors in droves), entrepreneurs, restaurateurs (the ubiquitous Udupi eateries are found across the country), bankers (think UA Pai and KV Kamath), and an education hub (Manipal). Plus the sea and a river too.

Add a brave, new experiment underway, and Udupi could soon see the addition of another feather in its cap—as the petri-dish for the Open Network for Digital Commerce (ONDC). The government wants to make the ONDC—by sponsoring it as an e-commerce platform of platforms—as mighty a success as Unified Payments Interface (UPI).

Udupi is ground zero for what the ONDC could look like. Launched early this year, the ONDC is in the pilot phase with nearly 100 sellers in 35 cities.

Arundhati, the author of today's story, travelled to the beautiful coastal region, clicked fab pictures, and met various stakeholders, including Anup Pai, the founder of eSamudaay. It is leading the Udupi charge as a seller aggregator. Seller aggregators essentially hand-hold and bring sellers to the ONDC. eSamudaay wants to be a "decentralised local digital marketplace."

The ambitions are big, to put it mildly. The ONDC wants 30 million sellers and 300 million shoppers onboard by 2024. And Pai says, “Udupi is a $3-4 billion economy, and we want to be able to create many $1 million and some $100 million companies out of there instead of one $1 billion company.”

This is easier said than done, though. The ONDC's fortunes may lie in tier-2 and -3 cities like Udupi, with the Network's success depending on it having many buyers and sellers. But getting millions of sellers in these places to the Network is a lot of effort, writes Arundhati.

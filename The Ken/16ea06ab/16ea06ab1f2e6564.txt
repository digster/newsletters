During a particularly rushed dinner a few nights ago, my fidgeting fingers brushed against something I hadn't noticed before. On the underside of the spoon, in worn engraving, were the words 'Jet Airways'.

Now, I haven't the faintest clue when I pilfered this spoon, but it was likely many moons ago. Over the last 15 years, as airlines looked to mainstream air travel by appealing to budget passengers, luxuries like complimentary in-flight meals, steamed face towels, and steel cutlery have all been junked.

Jet Airways was never a budget air carrier. It prided itself on being a full-service experience. Meals, beverages, multimedia entertainment, the works. But this wasn't feasible in a world where airfares were plummeting so fast you expected oxygen masks to drop from the ceiling. By the time Jet Airways finally looked to make meaningful cutbacks, it was too late. Earlier this year, the airline, once India's second biggest, went out of business.

As the budget air travel revolution has swept the globe, the aviation business has become increasingly difficult. High operational expenses, low margins. Throw in competition, and you have a seemingly zero-sum game. This is what's currently playing out in Indonesia—Southeast Asia's largest aviation market. Except there, competition seems to have bittered to the point where fair play has gone out the window—and AirAsia off digital platforms.

As Nadine reports, a cartel of domestic airlines allegedly pressured Indonesia's largest online travel agent (OTA), Traveloka, into keeping low-cost carrier AirAsia off its platform. Today, Malaysia-headquartered AirAsia has withdrawn from all Indonesian OTAs. It's a peculiar scenario, but one that also has lessons for the region's other aviation markets.

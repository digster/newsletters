Good Morning Ishan,
In Mohali, Punjab, there’s a robot sitting in a water tank. It’s not broken. It’s probably recovering.
The students who built it just came back from an underwater vehicle challenge in Singapore. They didn’t win, but that’s not really the point. The point is that this little robot—and the university where it was made—probably shouldn’t exist.
Plaksha University is a daring idea: 100-odd founders raised Rs 1,500 crore to reinvent what engineering education in India looks like. It has no departments. Its robotics major is an undergrad course. Its wood-and-metal makerspace has a popcorn machine. And its vice-chancellor says things like: “We are going to build the most ambitious space robotics programme in India.” It’s the type of statement that makes you want to ask a follow-up question.
Can they really?
Because Indian engineering colleges are factories. They make coders. Plaksha wants to make engineers who can think. Its founding chancellor, Shankar Sastry of UC Berkeley, thinks “engineering has gone too hard over on vocation”. He’s not wrong. So, Plaksha’s curriculum blends AI with ethics, robotics with anthropology. You can take a class called Technology and the Anthropocene, and then spend your afternoon stitching fabric in the makerspace.
Meanwhile, a molecular biologist is hunting for genomic clues to Punjab’s cancer crisis. And the dean of research—who did both pre-med and pre-engineering—is teaching robotics to undergrads.
It’s either the future of Indian higher education. Or a very expensive experiment. Probably both.

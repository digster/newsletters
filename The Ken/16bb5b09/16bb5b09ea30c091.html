You know the mantra for most startups: find a problem and solve it. And the more tech one throws at the problem, the better. Technology is out to revolutionise all industries. 
 
And agriculture, the oldest industry on the planet, has no dearth of problems. Especially in India. Litany of woes. Check. Getting worse by the year. Check. Huge market. Check. An everlasting industry. Check. 
 
Cue technology.
 
Agritech is not new, but has been gaining prominence lately. Not least when Microsoft CEO Satya Nadella said last November that India stood to benefit a lot by using AI in agriculture. And agritechs do use AI and machine learning to crunch data for an ideal solution. But that happens only after data gathering: soil samples are collected for analysis, satellite or meteorological data forecast weather conditions, while the farmer fills in relevant local intel.
 
And there's the catch—the quality of inputs. Soil conditions can change; weather can differ within the span of a few kilometres; and a farmer's inputs are prone to error. All of which makes a software-based solution a tricky proposition.
 
There, however, is one startup that has gone a different route, combining hardware and software. Fasal's sensors, planted along with the crop, measure soil humidity, leaf moisture levels, air pressure and the like. This mix of ground-level data and machine learning could be a game changer. But it comes with one problem—a higher bill than other agritech solutions. 
 
Sharath, in his first story for The Ken, uses Fasal to examine the budding agritech industry in India. What technology works best? Which business model holds the most promise? And most importantly, can farmers can afford it?

...and sorry for butchering Rudyard Kipling in the subject. But that really was how investors, regulators, and consumers had seen tech companies and telecom companies for the last two decades.
 
Telecom was once a sexy, cutting-edge, cash spewing industry species. It controlled technology through its "walled gardens” and consumer wallets through its lucrative billing relationships. Being regulated and licensed ensured competition too was kept in check.
 
Then came the tech companies. Starting from their own niches—like search for Google, e-commerce for Amazon, or social networking for Facebook—they kept growing and growing. In the process, they threw up more users, cash flows, innovation, and products every year.
 
Till they became the giant mega corporations that straddle the world today, using their reach and power to expand into every segment from payments and communications to logistics and devices. Being unregulated meant they “evolved” in a much more competitive environment, and into much more dominance, and aggressive corporate species.
 
Sooner or later though, the two species were bound to run into each other.
 
That happened this year, argue Seema Singh and Prajakta Patil. Seema is our Editor and Prajakta, our Head of Design. Together, using words and visuals, they argue how tech is slowly seeping into more and more areas that were once considered the domain of telecom companies. In some cases, this merging is consensual and opportunistic. In others, its clear tech feels it doesn’t need telecom.
 
It's a fascinating window into a brave (and a scary) new world that awaits us in 2021 and beyond. One that even regulators aren’t ready for. It’s best described by this provocative quote from Peter Adderton, founder and chief executive officer (CEO) of Boost Mobile, a wireless communication operator in the US and Australia.
 
“In the next five years, telcos as we know it may not exist. We may be buying communications (voice and data) directly from Amazon (Project Kuiper), Google (Project Loon), SpaceX (Starlink), OneWeb, Boeing, Samsung, and even Facebook.”

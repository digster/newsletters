"Behind every good business is a great accountant."
And Zomato certainly seems to have one. Because, while the pandemic has ravaged other businesses, the food delivery company claims it has accelerated its "journey to profitability". And it has the numbers to back it up.
Its revenue for the year ended March 2020 doubled to nearly $400 million, while losses ticked up by less than 20% to just under $300 million. Its first-quarter numbers are even more stellar. Zomato lost only $12 million in these three months, including a marginal $1.5 million loss in June 2020.
$1.5 million vs a monthly average of about $24.5 million in the last financial year!
In other words, Zomato is on the verge of profitability. Its unit economics are already positive—swinging from negative Rs 47 ($0.63) a year ago to Rs 27 ($0.36) now. Plus it has charts and everything (however creatively drawn) to back up its claims.
This takes Zomato a full circle to about two and a half years ago when founder and CEO Deepinder Goyal said the company had become profitable.
Last October, when Zomato released its FY19 results, its loss had exploded nearly 25X year-on-year. At that time, I had (in my email for Sumanth's story) written that the company faced a Sisyphean task as far as achieving profitability was concerned.
Zomato is on the verge of the profit peak now, if you go by its numbers. But it seems to me that either the boulder has shrunk to a pebble or the mountain has become a molehill. Or maybe one is using binoculars.
Because what financial statements reveal is just as crucial as what they hide.
Sumanth not only draws back the curtain fully on Zomato's most recent financial report in today's story but also helps explain how the company managed the numbers. And more importantly, if they are sustainable, going forward.

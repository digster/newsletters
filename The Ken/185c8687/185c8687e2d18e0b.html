Good Morning Ishan,
 

One of the consequences of the Great Funding Boom of 2021 was that we finally understood the natural limits of India’s internet market. As longtime readers know, I’ve written about two of these: 

 

First, there’s India’s California users, numbering about 10 million users, who are the primary drivers of our internet economy. Back then, I wrote about how the number of active online purchasers in India is the population of Sweden, and why all consumer internet companies are fighting for these users.

 

Second, there are about 10,000 software engineers, who represent the breadth and depth of India’s quality engineering talent. Remember, this was when companies were throwing everything from international world cup tickets to BMW bikes to get their hands on these engineers, only to promptly lay many of them off one year later.

 

Limits become apparent when there’s a surplus. That’s what happened in 2021. Everyone got a lot of money and suddenly went looking for online purchasers (to buy their products) and for developers (to make their products). As a result, we learnt that both of those resources are much shallower than we assumed it to be.

 

But limits also become known when the opposite happens, i.e., when there’s an extreme shortage. 

  

For the last two years, nearly every tech sector globally saw a surge in demand, which led to higher production driven by cheap capital.  

 

With one notable exception. 

 

Smartphones. 

 

Due to a shortage of components, particularly at the entry level, product launches were delayed, leading to reduced inventory which eventually led to a decline in smartphone sales. This led to a global supply chain disruption, followed by geopolitical tensions involving China, Taiwan and the United States. 

 

As a consequence, we now know another limit. 

 

Here it is.
For nearly half a decade, India added mobile data users at a tearaway rate—these were the users who formed the engine of the nation’s digital economy. This growth was the primary reason for investing vast resources to create infrastructure for services like payments, travel, and commerce.
 

Since early 2021, though, the number of users in India who access broadband internet using their mobile phone has flatlined. As of October 2022, the most recent month for which data is available, that number was 789 million users—the same as that in August the previous year. 

 

This number is consequential, because it reveals deeper, more consequential limits.
Why have India's mobile internet users stopped growing?

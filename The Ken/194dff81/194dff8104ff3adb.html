Good Evening Ishan, 

I hope you are feeling great and breathing clean air somewhere. I, unfortunately, am not. The changing weather and Delhi’s abysmal air quality (sometimes I feel like I can see the PM10 floating in the air) have conspired to afflict me with flu-like symptoms all week. Which is why I don’t have an Inciting Incident edition for you today. 

However, I do have some very interesting article recommendations if you’d like to scratch that reading itch.

Making weighty subjects light

TED, a non-profit that believes powerful ideas powerfully presented can bring about change, recently launched a story-based animated show called Ada on its Youtube channel TED-Ed. 

I think the show is a great watch for anyone interested in using storytelling to make weighty subjects easier to digest. Forbes carried a review about it last week.

The series is written and directed by Elizabeth Cox, founder of independent production company Should We Studio, which, as its name suggests, was set up to tackle weighty topics. The show’s protagonist is a young woman who wants to make a mark on the world by making it better and explores the world’s numerous issues and problems in her head ranging from post-apocalyptic energy sources, and artificial wombs to the consequences of creating super-intelligent AI.

You know, the small stuff.

Unlike the traditional shorts that appear on TED-Ed, the series will aim to educate and stimulate debate on the issues it raises via a narrative, and based on the two episodes that I’ve seen so far, it should succeed handsomely.

[…]

Most importantly though, Ada is a fun watch and demonstrates that animation as a medium is in fine fettle as a medium that proves that you don’t have to be a huge studio to bring great storytelling to the screen.

A journey of a thousand miles begins with a single step

The snowflake method of writing is a way of designing a novel that is based on a fractal structure known as the Koch snowflake, made famous by Randy Ingermanson. This planning process starts with a one sentence story summary that crystallizes into a perfect first draft with a three-act structure.

View in Browser

You can read all about how to go from a one-line summary to a full-blown novel here: https://kindlepreneur.com/snowflake-method/

Creating space for elaborate narratives

The latest edition of the Nieman Storyboard newsletter speaks about how Will James, an audio documentary producer, helped create serial podcasts with the goal of giving complicated stories more space. 

His advice for anyone trying something similar:

Reporters have to make the case that audiences already get so much information in discrete bites, they’re confused, and they’re crying out for reporting that helps them synthesize all of it. And the reporter needs to demonstrate that their project will fill that need — and that they have a plan to deliver.

The newsletter is a great read, so do check it out here.

Good Morning Ishan,
Buy early, hold, wait for an IPO, profit.
That’s a gross simplification of how to make money by throwing money at unlisted companies, one that has been seared into the canon of investing. It’s a line of thinking that has enticed many people in India to pile their cash into platforms where unlisted shares are bought and sold, all in hopes of achieving rocket-ship returns.
The prospect of easy gains makes people ignore the risks. What was once the domain of informed investors is now bloated with retail traders after a quick buck.
“They are betting that these stocks would go public, list, and stay at prices higher than their unlisted market buys,” said the founder of a platform that lets users buy and sell pre-IPO shares.
The outcome is a great sense of disappointment following what the same founder calls “irrational exuberance”. This was the case with the public offering of HDB Finance in late June, then again with the IPO of National Securities Depository Limited (NDSL) last week. The difference can be stark: those who paid Rs 1,250 per share in the unlisted market had to contend with a Rs 760–800 price band for the IPO’s asking valuation.
Profits are elusive for retail investors who dabble in unlisted shares, but this space appears to scratch the gambler’s itch for many. For now, Sebi has not formed regulations for this type of investing, so there’s no avenue for appeal when things go south. That regulatory vacuum is starting to raise alarms. Some industry voices are calling for rules that, at least, bring transparency and accountability to the space, if not rein it in entirely.
Until then, retail investors are left holding the bag. Anand investigates in [today’s story](https://the-ken.com/story/sebi-watches-as-investors-in-unlisted-shares-gasp-for-breath/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter).

Good Morning Ishan,
Doctors in India are slammed, but I was surprised to find out yesterday just how overwhelmed they are. The doctor-to-patient ratio is a catastrophic 1:834 nationally, and climbs to 1:11,000 in rural areas. This means Indian doctors see many more patients per hour than their global peers. Naturally, the amount of time they can spend with each patient falls drastically—to two minutes per consultation.
Imagine being a doctor who needs to get every note right, for hours each day, switching contexts dozens of times per hour. Transcription errors can trigger drastic consequences, such as malpractice lawsuits that wreck careers, issues with insurance claims, or worse yet, detrimental patient outcomes.
This context is where multilingual transcription could be a lifesaver. A handful of local startups are creating AI-powered tools for this use case, and Microsoft is attempting to leverage its existing integrations with Indian medical facilities to stake its own claim. None of this solves the root problem of India lacking doctors, but it could keep your medical records straight the next time you’re admitted to a hospital.
Besides, India still needs regulatory clarity before anyone can determine if AI transcription services in patient consultations are aspirational pilots or imminent transformation.

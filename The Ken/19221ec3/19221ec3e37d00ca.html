Apple’s top electronics supplier in India, Tata Electronics’ hiring strategy is straight out of the Chinese tech-manufacturing playbook.

In August, it announced that it’s hiring 4,000 women from the hilly northern state of Uttarakhand for its iPhone-assembly plant near Kolar, Karnataka, and for manufacturing other tech components in its Hosur facility in Tamil Nadu. Tata is even building dormitories to house this talent.

While this hire-and-house model is one that electronics manufacturer Hon Hai Precision Industry (better known as Foxconn) popularised in eastern China’s “iPhone city” and also brought to Chennai, it’s now finding favour among others as well.

And why not. It makes complete sense. When companies have their employees living in facilities housed by them, there is bound to be lesser attrition. Especially when they come with precision-manufacturing skills that are in high demand.

In fact, the US$155-billion Indian tech-manufacturing industry is facing a demand of nearly six million workers by 2025–26, according to the Electronic Sector Skill Council of India, a non-profit organisation.

But companies like Tata Electronics have to contend with the situation where there is both a short supply of skill and high unemployment in the towns where they manufacture. More than a quarter of the educated youth in Tamil Nadu and nearly one-fifth in Karnataka are unemployed, according to a report by the International Labour Organization, a United Nations agency.

That means the conglomerate’s plans are likely to run into political walls.

New rules meant to enhance banks’ locker services for clients are having the opposite effect, unlocking a window of opportunity for private players

Good Morning Ishan,

The law of unintended consequences is playing out in the safe deposit locker space in India. A complaint by an aggrieved bank customer a few years back saw the Supreme Court issue directions to the Reserve Bank of India. The central bank, in turn, directed banks to rework their locker agreements and sign revised contracts with their customers.

The repeatedly extended deadline for this—December 2023—has come and gone, but nearly half of the customers have still not signed on, say bankers. A recent survey shows that many customers want to give up their bank lockers.

That’s odd—considering that the waitlists for lockers have traditionally been long, and it has always been difficult, if not impossible, for new customers to snag a locker with their friendly neighbourhood bank. What gives?

The spirit of the court order was to provide succour to locker holders. But that’s not quite reflected in the changes—the devil, as always, is in the detail. Many customers are baulking at the onerous conditions, limited protection, and the steep hike in locker rentals that the new rules have brought about.

But do customers really have an alternative? Ateesh Tankha and Ganga Narayan Rath, the authors of today’s story, argue that the failings and disinterest of banks open a window of opportunity for private non-bank players and a choice for customers seeking lockers but without the many hassles. Click here to know more.

RBI loosens, RBI tightens

Now, the RBI may have given a long rope to banks on lockers, but it has been repeatedly cautioning them over the years to crack down on illegal forex trading. The latest ‘stay alert’ warning to banks came just a few days back.

“We continue to see banking channels being used by certain persons or entities to fund activities on unauthorised forex trading platforms. This warrants enhanced vigilance by the banks,” RBI governor Shaktikanta Das said.

Not just banks, the RBI has on several occasions cautioned customers too to keep away from unauthorised forex trading platforms, many of which are based abroad and facilitate short-term punts on a wide array of forex pairs. The risk of loss is seriously high, and there’s no recourse if such platforms play truant.

Worse, customers will be violating forex laws, and their bank accounts could be at risk, too. The RBI’s fearsome reputation will likely make banks fall in line. But can punters get over their greed and let good sense prevail? That’s the million-dollar question.

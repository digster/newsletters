That's the question we asked Vidit Aatrey, the CEO of Meesho. He confessed he couldn't think of another one that was doing so.
 
But I am getting ahead of myself. Let's start at the beginning.
 
For an entire decade, there was only one way to sell products online in India. Set up a website. Work with vendors. Stock their inventory. Integrate with the payment gateway. Work with third-party logistics (or if you were well-funded, build one of your own). Do an annual sale. Raise funding from Tiger or Softbank. Deep-discount. Get rolled over by Amazon. Lobby for a protectionist e-commerce policy from the government. This used to be the norm. But there's evidence that something is changing. B2C inventory-led e-commerce isn't growing fast enough. And so, other models started to look attractive. Could a company sell without following this path?
 
That's where companies like Udaan (a B2B marketplace) and Meesho (a B2B2C company) that aims not to acquire end-customers, but an army of resellers. At the face of it, the model is simple.  Want to sell? Cool. Meesho will connect you to suppliers, who will take orders from you and ship products out. You bring customers. No need for a website. No need for building a boutique. Just use...WhatsApp. As my colleague Salman says in his story today, "Aatrey says that Meesho aims to be the ‘Robin Hood” of small sellers (particularly homemakers and students), setting them free from the shackles of inventory ownership".
 
Oh also, this Robin Hood has raised $65 million from investors thus far.
 
It's a noble goal, but I know what you are probably thinking. This is just another trend. Can Meesho scale beyond a point? They don't own the end-customer experience. Their acquisition and training costs are too high. Will the margins work out? Questions. Questions. 
 
So what's the answer?

1. Habits, Streaks, and Goodhart’s Law

Are you a fan of habits? Micro habits? Atomic habits?

Who isn’t these days? We have internalised the importance of habits as the method to, well, productivity.

If you’re a fan of habits, and if you aren’t a Boomer or Gen X, then chances are you might also be a fan of “streaks”.

Anyone who’s ever found themselves marching around their bedroom to reach 10,000 steps, or frantically completing a Duolingo language exercise to stay on the leaderboard, will be familiar with the mantra “Don’t break the streak!”

Streaks can motivate us, help us build good habits and aid us in reaching our goals. If you’re looking for a way to stick to a New Year’s resolution beyond January, a streak could help.

Why Do Streaks Help Us Meet Our Goals?

Streaks work for two reasons: what behavioral economists call loss aversion and potential gain, said Dr. Katy Milkman, a behavioral scientist at the University of Pennsylvania.

Let’s start with gains: A streak can be profoundly motivating for people, Dr. Milkman said, “because there’s something bright and shiny that they can reach for. There’s a prize, in a sense, that’s making you more motivated to get a thing done.”

Loss aversion, Dr. Milkman said, is the idea that the pain of losing something can be more extreme than the pleasure of gaining the same thing. So once you’re on a streak, research has suggested, the fear of losing it can be stronger than the motivating power of simply gaining another day.

These dual motivators can keep us on track to reach our goals. Plus, being able to cross something off your to-do list every day can add meaning to your life.

A Surefire Way to Be More Productive by Nell McShane Wulfhart, The New York Times https://www.nytimes.com/2025/01/31/well/mind/habit-building-streak.html

Over the years, I’ve relied on “streaks” to help me track calories, drink more water, read books, complete work tasks, meditate (try to!), and even spend quality time with family. I’ve purchased and used apps that let you set up and track your streaks each day. 

I hoped to be an “elite performer https://jamesclear.com/habit-tracker”.

But I’ve happily fallen off both the streaks and habits wagons for the past few years. I realised I was getting more attached to the process of tracking completion and to the process of habit formation than the things I wanted to do.

I realised that habits, streaks, and apps had insidiously become my goals, rather than my original goals like, say, eating healthier or building strength or doing the things I enjoyed. I had become dependent on them to “motivate” myself, instead of relying on my own senses and thinking. Motivating myself became more important than enjoying myself.

I hadn’t planned to write about this topic this week, until I came across two things that fired a number of connected neurons in my head.

The first was this app, Tasktile https://macmenubar.com/tasktile/.

“Tasktile turns task tracking into a visual experience with a GitHub-style contribution calendar. It keeps users engaged by transforming daily tasks into a clear, motivating progress map.”

That second line reminded me of why I had stopped relying on habits and streaks. The act of tracking something ends up becoming the thing that motivates and engages us.

The second example was from the most unlikeliest of news items this week—AI benchmarks. For a while now, it has been clear that AI startups have been gaming benchmark scores https://www.technologyreview.com/2024/11/26/1107346/the-way-we-measure-progress-in-ai-is-terrible/, so that each new version can show that it was “better” than previous ones. Meta’s latest Llama 4 model was the most recent one to claim it was the best. But maybe not https://www.theregister.com/2025/04/08/meta_llama4_cheating/.

AI companies have figured out how benchmarks and tests are done, so they focus on gaming and winning those itself.

Why did these two unrelated examples remind me of habits and streaks?

Because of another unrelated principle, aka Goodhart’s Law https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goodhart%27s_law. It is described as, “When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure.”

I know I’ve taken a few too many conceptual leaps till now. But artificially engineered and maintained “streaks” means the same to me. “When a streak becomes the goal, it ceases to be a good streak.”

This Medium post by Claire Gong https://medium.com/@clairecgong/you-arent-lazy-this-is-why-habit-building-apps-don-t-work-for-you-and-what-to-do-instead-73dee6b3db59 explains this in much greater detail.

The main tactic the habit-tracking apps use to get you to commit to the habit is this same streak-based gamification.

Every day you mark all your habits as completed, you add a number to your streak. The idea is to “never break the streak” because everything resets.

Yes, there are psychological reasons why streaks are effective for consistent action — namely, the progress visualization and the sunk-cost fallacy. You are less likely to abandon something the more you invest in it.

While these streaks can be initially motivating, there is a dark side that I don’t see people talk about.

Our brain is conditioned to avoid the pain of losing something. When we start to take pride in how long our streak is, we start to be driven by avoiding the negative consequence of losing it rather than the intrinsic enjoyment of the activity.

This motivation is entirely extrinsic, which poses the real risk of the desired actions becoming completely dependent on the validation from a perfect streak.

The validation becomes a drug that gives you an illusion of productivity.

[…]

Researchers found that many habit apps are far too one-dimensional and rigid to support our diverse practical and emotional needs.

Most of the trackers offer only a binary checkbox. While some of the more robust apps such as Productive are offering timer-based or count-based tracking, the items being tracked are the real culprit here.

The real problem is that we try to turn some things that can’t become habits into habits with these tools.

Habits by definition are behaviors we repeat so often that they come automatically and don’t require any conscious effort for us to do. But a lot of the things we are tracking with habit trackers are actually better categorized as deliberate routines that DO require conscious ongoing effort.

Mindless habit tracking can actually limit our growth.

We can become chained to the arbitrary notion of “go to the gym for 1 hour a day 6 times a week” without pausing to actually reflect if that is the best suited course of action for our current needs.

In fact, intentional effort and deliberate practice is usually key to making meaningful improvements in that area of life. As your level of familiarity and efficiency grows, You want to experiment with different routines and continuously tweak them so that it grows with you.

It can become a trap to think that if we can just continue the streak for 60 something days, it will become effortlessly automatic.

You Aren’t Lazy—This Is Why Habit-Building Apps Don’t Work for You (And What To Do Instead)

By focusing our attention and our brain’s reward mechanisms on maintaining streaks instead of finding time for things that give us joy, we suppress our ability to grow.

This isn’t to say we should never use habits or streaks. I do rely on them every six months or so, when I want to force myself to pay attention to something. But I am conscious about not relying on the methods beyond a month or two. I don’t want to become dependent on them.

I don’t maintain a streak note for the number of Saturday afternoons I’ve written to you. Or the nights I’ve gone to feed Bribo. Or the times I’ve read a book. Or launched a new product with my colleagues.

I don’t need to.

3. Audiobooks

Hello, dear Ishan. It’s Hari here. 

I didn’t know we had a fairly large number of audiobook listeners in the community until last week, though I don’t know why I should have been surprised. Audiobooks have become an entirely legitimate (some might even say better) way to enjoy our favourite books, and as the flood of recommendations from First Principles readers show, there are entire worlds worth exploring underneath Audible’s unrelenting push for a few select bestsellers. 

As I said last week https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/winning-versus-being-on-the-winning-side/, with audiobooks, it’s all about the person reading for me. And I can’t get enough of Stephen Fry. Well, there was a whole lot more of Stephen Fry in the recommendations we received. I guess you really can’t escape him if you are into audiobooks. And I don’t think anyone’s complaining, either.

One of our readers, Pradeep Kumaresan, sent nearly a dozen recommendations! Most others also suggested more than one audiobook. 

I had to put them through a filter in order to digest them myself, so I decided to weed out all highly dramatised audiobooks. And there went all the high-production versions of The Sandman comics and dramatised reads of George Orwell’s 1984. Don’t get me wrong, they’re great, I’ve purchased all of them. But there were too many of those. 

But even after that, the list remains fairly long. So I’m going to stash away some for a later date. 

For today, here are four recommendations, two fiction and two nonfiction. And let me just say, two of the titles are read by Tom Hanks and Meryl Streep! 4. An Ode to Spring

Hello Ishan, it’s Jonathan here. Welcome to another edition of First Principles Silent Sunday. 

As is evident from all the mango shops popping up along our sidewalks (at least if you are reading this from India), Summer is here. Already, heat is taking over conversations, just like it will take over pretty much everything else for the next four months. Summer has its own joys, savoured during brief respites snatched from the pervasive heat, but for me at least, it is usually something to be endured. What gets me through every summer are really the seasons that bookend it—the yearning for a refreshing monsoon, and the memory of a colourful spring. 

I asked you last week for springtime pictures, and you delivered in the dozens. 

Thanks to you, today’s Silent Sunday is a stroll through the memories of Spring. An ode, and a remembrance. And a reassuring reminder that the season of bloom will be back in just a little while.

For a long time, the US has been subsidising global innovation, spending more on research than any other country in absolute and relative terms. As Trump continues to throw one wrecking ball after another into the American scientific enterprise, other countries have woken up. 

Britain said last week that it will spend $116 billion on research and development (R&D) over the next four years, funding https://www.reuters.com/world/uk/britain-allocate-116-billion-rd-spending-plan-2025-06-07/ everything from new “drug treatments and longer-lasting batteries to artificial intelligence breakthroughs”.

Indian agencies upped https://www.pib.gov.in/PressReleaseIframePage.aspx?PRID=2107056 their annual science and technology budget earlier this year, but this is also one area where the country has regressed to the mean.

Thirty years ago, India spent https://www.indiascienceandtechnology.gov.in/sites/default/files/file-uploads/indicators/1532608253_National%20Expenditure%20on%20R%26D%20in%20Relation%20to%20GDP.pdf 0.6% of its GDP on R&D; in 2025, it spends the same—0.6%. The figure occasionally inched up to 0.8% between then and now, but the standard statistical tendency has played out over the decades. 

The latest data from the Unesco Institute of Statistics shows India shares its ranking for R&D spending with Namibia and Malta, lagging behind smaller economies like Kenya, Russia, and Brazil. As a percentage of GDP, the US (3.6%), China (2.6%), and South Korea (5.2%) spend far more, with a large chunk of the money coming from the private sector.

For instance, industry contribution to R&D is 70% in the US, 57% in the EU, and 79% in China. In India, the share is just 36% of national R&D spending. Amusingly, it also becomes a convenient punching bag.

At the CII Annual Business Summit in late May, the telecom tycoon Sunil Bharti Mittal said https://www.business-standard.com/companies/people/sunil-mittal-calls-for-tax-dispute-scheme-simplified-rate-ftas-elections-125052901577_1.html India’s R&D spend is “woefully low, at less than 1% of GDP”. 

I couldn’t agree more. But Mittal also called for “import substitution”, which I don’t agree with, even though India practiced this for many years after independence and built up a few domestic industries like cement and steel. Columbia University professor and current chairman of the 16th Finance Commission, Arvind Panagariya, has reasonably argued https://eastasiaforum.org/2023/12/06/the-false-promise-of-import-substitution-industrialisation-in-india-2/ against this:

The true success of import substitution must be judged not by its ability to create and sustain protected industries, but by its capacity to accelerate the entire economy’s growth […] The case for import substitution crumbles along this metric.

The false promise of import substitution industrialisation in India, East Asia Forum

But given the volatile geopolitical times we are in, selective protection (i.e. substitution) that allows Indian businesses to integrate into global markets may not seem old-fashioned. There’s a global race to shore up new technologies and associated materials—glocalisation, as it were.

Earlier in June, Harvard University released a new Critical and Emerging Technologies Index https://www.belfercenter.org/critical-emerging-tech-index that ranks 100 geographies on advanced technologies. It’s an indicative ranking, not a definitive one, aimed at facilitating comparative, cross-sector technology analysis. India ranks #10 overall, but rises to #7 in the artificial-intelligence sector, propped up by human capital and data.

The pole position that the US enjoys—it contributes 25% to global GDP but has over 60% of the global market capitalisation—owes much to its dynamic private sector, which has consistently invested in R&D. It hit particularly high notes in the last decade.

Indian industry has come a long way, going from a licensing and import substitution regime to a freer environment with better corporate and goods and services tax regimes, as well as a growing domestic market. But R&D remains anathema. Why research when you can trade? Why invent when you can copy? 

A medtech founder who had a super-successful exit to private equity and is building a second company in the same space recently told me, “We don’t do R [of R&D], we do D. We copy global products, do some packaging, and sell them globally.”

As it turns out, when countries are rewriting their industrial playbooks, India can’t keep up by borrowing ideas or patching up its rickety innovation system.

Mindset, mindset, mindset

Not every business needs to invest intensely in R&D. Sectors like pharma and automotive depend on it, while others such as insurance and banking can thrive just by investing in tech. 

There’s a nearly total overlap between India’s top 10 companies by market cap and by revenue. Clearly, their innovation strategies have significant downstream effects in the economy. 

Let’s take Reliance, for instance. The group’s R&D spending has remained flat at around 0.4% of total revenue for the past four years (Rs 3,643 crore in FY24, the latest data available). Interestingly, its annual reports have consistently indicated that it has “1,000+” R&D employees for the last three years—a static figure despite highlighting 232,822 “new jobs” in FY22 and 171,116 new hires in FY24—two precise numbers. 

The same rigour is found elsewhere: Reliance filed 1,301 patents in FY24, for instance. Curious, isn’t it?

During my research, I found ITC Ltd., whose non-tobacco or FMCG business is about 24 years old (compared to its 115-year-old parent), reported it had 400 scientists and 800 patents in FY24, and added that it had reached this state “in a relatively short period of time”. It was good to see the company list several research heads by name and designation. 

The telecom giant Bharti Airtel doesn’t report R&D, but news reports https://www.techcircle.in/2024/09/13/spend-it-airtel-s-it-expenditure-growth-sees-significant-drop-in-fy24 say its IT spending was Rs 700 crore in FY24, up 6% from the previous year. (Gartner estimates $5.6 trillion will be spent worldwide on IT in 2025.)

The takeaway? Mindset, organisational DNA, and culture must evolve—not just in industry but also government and investor circles. Simply investing more in R&D or security is not enough. Everyone must think of structural reforms too. 

For instance, as a US-returned researcher in advanced technologies noted, the Department of Science and Technology (DST) offered Rs 60 lakh as a starter grant 12 years ago. Today, the amount remains unchanged. “The moment you talk of a few crores, the opportunities vanish. How can you set up a lab, get equipment, hire, and plan for anything? The mindset is still the same—we don’t want to take risks,” he quipped. 

Another accomplished scientist listed the no-go areas more succinctly: 

⁠Anything that takes more than five years to get into high technology readiness levels (TRL), or which you can’t commit to attain TRL from the beginning. 

⁠Anything that is expensive, say a few crores to tens of crores, and is not technology development. 

Anything that does not have a released product in the US, such as next-generation semiconductors or new sensing materials, and would take another 5–10 years to develop. 

“In short, no moon-shot proposals. No opportunity to think big. No opportunity to target areas where the chances of success are less than 10%,” he added.

None of this is new to me, or to anyone following this space. But with the heightened sense of decoupling in global trade and “reindustrialisation”, the need for in-house tech competence cannot be overstated. 

India cut corporate taxes, but money did not flow https://www.thehindu.com/opinion/editorial/capex-quandary-on-the-economic-data-policy/article69120257.ece into capex; the Indian government is sore about this. The capital went in a different direction: Indian companies increased https://www.business-standard.com/opinion/columns/india-s-fdi-puzzle-why-india-inc-is-investing-more-abroad-than-at-home-125052701889_1.html their outward foreign direct investment from $14 billion in 2022–23 to $29 billion in 2024–25. So merely increasing the country’s annual R&D budget won’t cut it. 

If the largest Indian companies don’t believe in R&D or sufficiently invest in technology, it’s unlikely that they’ll become the backers of deep tech innovation which is just taking roots in India. This means India won’t undergo the same virtuous cycle that other geographies’ business sectors have experienced. When there’s a need for large pools of domestic capital, there might not be any, even in the form of enthusiastic enterprise customers.

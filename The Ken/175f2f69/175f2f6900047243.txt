Ashok Varma, the protagonist of todayвҖҷs story, is no more. He died by suicide almost exactly two months ago. He was 32.

TodayвҖҷs story wasnвҖҷt an easy one for Sumanth or The Ken to tell, but it is one that still needed to be told. Not just because it chronicles the posthumous life of a young and promising founder, one perhaps destined for much greater heights than what he had accomplished.

But also because it reveals the everyday reality of entrepreneurship, which is often replaced with glorious stories of funding and valuation and exits in the tech press. And most importantly, because it subtly highlights the two words that Indian entrepreneurs increasingly dread: вҖңregulatory approvals.вҖқ

Varma wasnвҖҷt 30 yet when, in mid-2018, he signed a deal to sell his SaaS startup, Report Garden, to a US acquirer for millions of dollars. If that sounds small in the age of loss-making unicorns and $100 million rounds, consider the fact that Varma had built the company to many millions of dollars in annualised revenue and thousands of customers around the world without taking a single dollar of venture funding.

Anyhow, he inked the deal to sell his company around September 2018 and then had to wait for two years for the deal to pass through due diligence and regulatory approvals. But even after this long wait, Varma wasn't able to enjoy this victory.

Varma died on 24 September. Because in the long period between agreeing to sell his company and legally being able to, someone obsessively and persistently tried to derail the deal. And that someone succeeded.

Varma was young and talented. He had a young kid and a loving wife. He had ideas for an even more ambitious second startup.

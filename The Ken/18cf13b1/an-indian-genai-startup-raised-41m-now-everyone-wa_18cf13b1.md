Good Morning Ishan,
Elon Musk recently [declared](https://twitter.com/elonmusk/status/1742983369296916762) that AI is growing faster than anything he’s ever seen. Maybe he hasn’t watched a toddler grow up; those little humans can surprise you. But I digress. Let’s talk about this “AI” buzz.
In the past year, if a CEO tosses around the term like confetti, chances are they’re part of the “generative AI (GenAI)” parade. All thanks to ChatGPT—the reigning humanlike conversationalist.
Businesses worldwide are gripped by AI FOMO, desperately trying to jump on the GenAI bandwagon. Microsoft-backed OpenAI and the Google-Amazon-backed Anthropic are throwing large language models (LLMs)—which comprehend, generate, and transform text—at us like candy. Countries are pouring resources into the AI race, hoping not to be the last one standing.
But guess who’s the latest to take a plunge into the GenAI pool? Indian startups, building LLMs.
Sarvam AI, just six-month-old, recently snagged $41 million in funding—the highest ever for a GenAI company in India. Even Bhavish Aggarwal, the Ola Cabs supremo, is riding the LLM wave with a brand-new startup.
But here’s the thing, though—if AI is like a Swiss Army Knife, tackling multiple tasks, GenAI is its creative attachment (arguably one of the most vital and intricate ones).
Most GenAI ventures, globally and in India, aren’t reinventing the wheel. They’re tweaking existing open-source models because, as one founder eloquently puts it, “Building from scratch is a race you cannot win.” Touche! Also, the hiring cost of machine-learning engineers is nothing to sneeze at.
So, what precisely are these firms up to? What’s the endgame—earn revenue or get acquired? And can they really pull it off, at least in some sectors?

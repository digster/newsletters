Tiger Global’s downturn hits Bain India. One in every five consultants loses job
Any management consultant worth their salt would caution clients against taking on heavy “concentration-risk”—the danger from over-dependence on a single customer. Sure, the said customer might bring in the big business and lucrative profits in the good times. But if and when they call it quits, the hole might just be too big to fill—with all the attendant consequences. Diversifying to hedge risk never goes out of fashion.
But what if the consultant themself gave in to temptation and piled on big concentration-risk in its own business? What if the consultant were no run-of-the-mill advisor but one from the exalted “Big 3″—MBB (Mckinsey, BCG, Bain)—cohort? And what if the chickens came home to roost?
Abhirami’s Big Story today has a key news break about the India office of Bain—the smallest of the Big 3. One fine day earlier this month, it laid off 15–20% of its consulting wing’s workforce across the hierarchy. Ironically, Bain has a reputation of being more employee-friendly than its larger rivals. The genesis of this deep cull lies in Bain India not practising what it would have undoubtedly preached. The irony is hard to miss. The pain for Bain and its workforce lies in other sins, too. But then, the tide turned—as it always does. Nevertheless, never let a good crisis go to waste. Click [here](https://the-ken.com/story/tiger-globals-downturn-hits-bain-india-one-in-every-five-consultants-loses-job/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter) for a deep-dive into Bain India’s crazy ride to crisis point and its drive back now to make the best of a bad situation.
If wishes were horses
It’s not just Bain India that’s dealing with crises. India’s new government that had just started chugging along has had to firefight on multiple fronts—a rail accident, exam scams, and terrorist attacks—all within a fortnight.
Then, with the Union Budget to be presented next month, wish-lists from all and sundry have started landing at the Finance Ministry’s door. The chairman of India’s largest bank, SBI, wants tax relief on interest earnings to incentivise bank deposits (not surprising, given that deposits are becoming a scarce commodity for banks these days). The industry bodies of corporate India want a simplified capital gains tax regime (likely a cue to lower taxes on debt investment gains), relief on personal taxes to boost consumption, and no change in corporate tax rates that were eased a few years back. Expect many more requests and demands from various lobby groups in the coming days.
All said, the recent election results that have forced a coalition government at the Centre and key state elections in the coming months will exert a big influence on who gets relief and who doesn’t in the upcoming Budget. A tilt towards populism is what many expect. That might well mean that the long-pending relief for the middle-class could finally arrive, in some form or the other.

Now, onto Phonepe.

Phonepe is going public and wants to raise about $1.3 billion. If you’re a potential investor wondering whether to put your money into the company, you’re not investing for what it has already done over the last 10 years, but for what it could do in the next 10.

And that’s what this episode is about.

Phonepe holds a 49% market share in UPI transactions, has 600 million users, and processes Rs 13 lakh crore worth of payments annually. It’s a behemoth owned by Walmart, but it doesn’t make money from UPI transactions.

In fact, regulatory changes in just the last 12 months have shaved off 21% of its revenue. So what’s next?

Rohin Dharmakumar, Praveen Gopal Krishnan, and Rahel Philipose sat down with Arundhati Ramanathan https://www.linkedin.com/in/arundhati-ramanathan-3ba24810/, The Ken’s deputy editor—who wrote about Phonepe’s financials https://the-ken.com/story/should-phonepe-be-worth-more-than-paytms-7-9b/ this Monday—and spent an hour debating this. They disagreed on almost everything. It felt like sitting at a dinner party where everyone came with very strong views and refused to budge.

Here’s a peek into the conversation.

The woes and wins of a market leader

Rohin had a thesis: Phonepe’s market leadership is both its strength and its weakness.

Rohin Dharmakumar: Phonepe’s immense leadership is actually a blinder, or it’s actually an obstacle, because every hair in its organisation, every part of its being, is designed to capture and protect that market share.

Arundhati Ramanathan: Anything that Phonepe does has the potential to scale things dramatically. And we know how the regulator gets wary about things that scale very, very quickly.

She gives an example of Google Pay that tried launching a feature, and the RBI started probing and asking questions. Amazon Pay launched the exact same feature https://the-ken.com/kaching/amazon-pay-is-treading-the-road-not-taken-by-google-pay/ a couple of weeks ago, and it’s still live. Perhaps it’s because its market share is small enough to be ignored for now. But Phonepe cannot do that because it is too big and affects the market too much.

The culture problem

There were some disagreements on this. 

Rohin Dharmakumar: Phonepe is very regulations- and regulator-sensitive. It always thinks twice, thrice, 300, 400 times before doing things. It is not the kind of organisation which is ‘shoot first, ask questions later’.

Praveen Gopal Krishnan: If you want to succeed in fintech in India, you need to be this kind of a company. If anybody goes way out of line, they get punished severely for it.

Rahel Philipose: We kept talking about culture and the great team behind Phonepe. I think that possibly after this is when we will see some great swings, probably going forward.

The episode also dissects Phonepe’s failed experiments like Pincode https://the-ken.com/kaching/phonepe-shows-why-payments-companies-cant-keep-up-with-the-e-commerce-act/, their grocery delivery service; or Indus Appstore, an Indian alternative to global app marketplaces. Then there’s rent payments, which were 22% of their revenue before the RBI shut it down.

There was also a fascinating discussion about whether Phonepe should become a merchant-focused company like Paytm, or stay consumer-focused. About whether advertising revenue matters, or lending and insurance.

Is there a bold acquisition on the horizon for Phonepe that could change everything?

As Rohin pointed out, “Phonepe has [Rs] 6,000 crores of cash and Walmart as a parent. If there was one company uniquely positioned to make acquisitions at scale and make them work, it’s Phonepe.”

I don’t know who’s right. But I do know this: Phonepe can’t just coast on its UPI market share for the next 10 years. Something has to change, and watching that change happen, or not happen, is going to be fascinating.

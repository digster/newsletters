Good Morning Ishan,

Facial recognition systems (FRS) are already here, and they are going to be more evenly distributed than desired.

(Allow me to riff on William Gibson’s famous quote: The future is already here—it’s just not evenly distributed.)

From boutique uses of facial recognition, in closed institutions or community uses, the tech is racing towards massive surveillance systems. Every other week some city or state body in some country chooses to use it in governance.

If it was Delhi Police relying heavily on FRS—originally set up to track missing children—to surveil a political rally during anti-citizenship amendment Act protests, expect more agencies to make this a default tool.

All this had no legal basis until now. But a new draft Bill, the Identification of Prisoners and Arrested Persons Bill, 2020, to be tabled in the Monsoon session of Parliament in July, will legalise the surveillance state. 

If passed in its current form, the Bill will provide legislative backing far beyond the mere collection of fingerprints. The biometrics will include foot and palm prints to photos, iris and retina images, voice samples, and even vein patterns.

If that’s not bizarre, know that even individuals summoned for interrogation would have to furnish all such data.

So far, the authorities collected structured data such as mobile call records and financial transactions. They are now fixated on unstructured data—social media posts, speeches, photos, etc. All to be linked with massive databases such as passports, driving licenses, voter ids, and so on.

“In the future, facial recognition will become the biggest identifier,” according to an official involved in the Home Ministry’s technology projects. 

Who understands these algorithms? How accurate are they? How fairly do they label individuals as the latter move through the criminal justice system?

China has had wide-scale FRS for a few years now; people are more accepting, perhaps because they trust algorithms more than the state. Contrastingly, the public opposition in the US and the UK has been more pronounced. 

How are we placed in India?

Questions are swirling.

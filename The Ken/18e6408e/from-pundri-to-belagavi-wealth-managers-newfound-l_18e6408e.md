Good Morning Ishan,
In 2023, there were over 1,300 individuals in India with a net worth of over Rs 1,000 crore (US$120 million) each––up from a mere 100 a decade earlier––according to one estimate. If those 100 lived in just 10 cities, the current lot is based in more than 75.
This is just the ultra-rich. The merely wealthy will be far greater in number and have a wider geographical footprint. They are increasingly investing in assets other than real estate, gold, and mutual funds.
That’s partly because wealth managers––used to pitching to the well-heeled in large cities––have recently been making a beeline for high-net-worth individuals in places like Pundri in Haryana and Yavatmal in Maharashtra.
But what does it take to convince folks not used to paying a hefty fee to have their wealth managed? That’s Narayanan’s [story](https://the-ken.com/story/from-pundri-to-belagavi-wealth-managers-newfound-love-beyond-mumbai-and-bluru/?utm_source=daily&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter) today.
Thanks to a story in The Financial Times, I realised the mutual fund is turning 100. “Is it becoming obsolete?” the headline read.
Most people understand mutual funds as having a manager making stock picks. But these “active” funds have been on the decline globally as investors choose lower-fee and, often, higher-return funds that passively mirror an index.
In 2023, passive funds’ net assets eclipsed those of active funds for the first time. Investors pulled out US$576 billion from actively managed funds while pumping US$466 billion into index funds and exchange-traded funds.
Passive funds are gaining steam in India, too, accounting for nearly one-fifth of the mutual-fund assets under management––up from less than one-tenth three years ago. But in December, active funds, despite their large base, grew at almost the same pace as passive funds.
It’s not that Indian fund managers are particularly good at beating their benchmark index. Distributors, a vital cog in the mutual-fund machine, get paid more for pushing active funds. That’s one reason why these funds manage to find new investors even when their returns are relatively nothing to write home about.

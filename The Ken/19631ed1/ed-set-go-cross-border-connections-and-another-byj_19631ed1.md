In the latest Byju’s case to join an impressive line-up of court proceedings, Byju’s Alpha Inc., the edtech’s US-based special purpose finance vehicle, has sued its parent company, founders Byju Raveendran and wife Divya Gokulnath, brother Riju Ravindran, and others for the alleged theft of $533 million—part of a term loan extended to the group by a consortium of lenders.
Filed in the bankruptcy court in Delaware, US, the suit follows recent adverse rulings by US courts against Byju’s entities and alleges that the founders and others orchestrated the theft through a “web of deception designed and executed to defraud lenders”.
“On the heels of the Delaware Bankruptcy Court’s recent judgment against his brother and companies, this action is being brought to now hold Byju Raveendran, the former CEO of BYJU’s Alpha, and two more of his co-conspirators – namely his co-founder and close business associate – accountable for their roles in masterminding the theft of more than half a billion dollars,” said the ad hoc group of term loan lenders of Byju’s Alpha’s Term Loans.
They further stated that “it is clear that Byju, Divya, and Anita deliberately hid the assets of BYJU’s Alpha and repeatedly were deceptive about the location of the money in order to steal funds rightfully owed to the Lenders”.

Political turmoil, still escalating, in some of the most popular destinations for Indian students is now reflecting starkly in official numbers.
According to figures released by the Indian government, the number of Indians studying abroad fell by nearly 15% in 2024, with Canada, one of the most popular university destinations thus far, seeing a steep 41% drop. Other countries such as the United States and the United Kingdom have also seen significant falls.
Most of this, as we’ve written about multiple times in this newsletter, is the direct result of tightening visa and immigration norms in the West. For instance, just this week, a bill was introduced in the US that seeks to cancel the OPT programme, or Optional Practical Training.
The OPT program allows students (who are in the US on F1 visas) to work for up to 12 months in a field related to their area of study. This can be a ‘pre-completion OPT’ – before the student completes their studies – or a ‘post-completion OPT’, which allows the student to work after graduating. For the pre-completion OPT, international students must be enrolled in a full-time course in the US for at least one academic year.
Students in STEM fields can extend their OPTs by another 24 months. This effectively means that a student in a STEM field can work in the US for three years after graduation.

Other restrictions and policies in the US, UK, and Canada have made life increasingly difficult for Indian students, especially if they were hoping to immigrate. This has made countries with less onerous regulations seem far more attractive, as the shift in numbers shows.
Canada, once a preferred destination, saw a dramatic 41% drop, to 1,37,608 in 2024 from 2,33,532 students in 2023. The country tightened student visa rules last year, leading to higher rejection rates and cancellations of [study permits](https://scroll.in/latest/1062279/canadian-permits-for-indian-students-dropped-by-86-amid-diplomatic-row).
In the US, figures declined to 2,04,058 in 2024 from 2,34,473 in 2023, a drop of nearly 13%. Indian students in the UK decreased to 98,890 from 1,36,921, down by nearly 28%. Australia saw a fall to 68,572 in 2024 from 78,093 students in 2023.
However, the number of Indian students going to Russia rose significantly, to 31,444 in 2024 from 23,503 in 2023, an increase of nearly 34%. France also saw a rise, to 8,536 in 2024 from 7,484 students in 2023. Germany had 34,702 Indian students in 2024, up from 20,684 in 2022. New Zealand saw a jump from 1,605 Indian students in 2022 to 7,297 in 2024.

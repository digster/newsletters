Good Morning Ishan,
Today’s story is a perfect Friday read. It’s intriguing, disquisitive, and cliff-hanging. Let me offer you a quick snapshot of it and explain why I chose those words to describe Anand’s piece.
But before that, here’s an unpopular opinion: I find earnings-call transcripts boring to the extent of seeming choreographed. However, on 21 July, during the earnings call for the quarter ended June, something made Vijay Chandok, chief executive officer of ICICI Securities (I-Sec)—India’s fifth-largest broking firm by active clients—sound helpless and maybe even a little angry.
It was an analyst’s question on a prickly matter. What’s that matter, you ask? Delisting of I-Sec. That’s the central plot of today’s story.
Here’s why it’s intriguing. The decision to delist came when I-Sec was gaining market share. Some minority shareholders find the valuation in the proposed delisting to be unfair. They think that the timing of the proposal—in terms of ICICI Bank and I-Sec stock values—makes this look like a case of use and throw.
Now, let’s get to the disquisitive bit: The proposal refers to something almost unheard of in India. The delisting will be carried out via share swap—under a special provision by the markets regulator, the Securities and Exchange Board of India. But there’s a catch, argues Anand. ICICI Bank and I-Sec aren’t in the same line of business—thus not meeting a prerequisite to use the special provision. So, how is this delisting proposed under the special provision? The answer is tucked away in a couple of documents put up by ICICI Securities on its website a few days back.
And here’s the cliffhanger: For the delisting to succeed, two-thirds of I-Sec’s public shareholders who cast their votes must approve the deal. And Anand finds several reasons for them to feel shortchanged. What are they?

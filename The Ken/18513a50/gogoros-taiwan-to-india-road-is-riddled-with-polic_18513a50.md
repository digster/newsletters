It’s rare in business that the entry of the most formidable competitor is truly welcomed.
But when you expect that competitor with heft and experience to either steamroll or sort out thorny issues, the welcome starts to make sense.
We are talking about the highly fragmented business of electric two-wheelers with swappable batteries. Taiwan’s Gogoro is revving up its pilot in the northern city of Gurugram, where it promises to swap a two-wheeler battery in six seconds.
A few seconds here and there don’t matter. What matters is whether Gogoro will be able to crack this uniquely difficult market, which first debated whether to promote charging or swapping and then put its policy weight behind the former.
Swapping is finally getting attention, and it’d be fair to lay some credit at Gogoro’s door. It formed the Indian Battery Swapping Association (IBSA) eight months ago with some Indian companies. Since then, the IBSA has lobbied against standardisation—an idea mooted by the government—and worked with agencies to offer alternatives.
Battery standardisation, like LPG cylinders, was never going to work. After all, apart from physical dimensions, there are several variables, including chemistries, compositions, and safety issues between brands. The Ken has learnt Honda was in favour of battery standardisation and had lobbied for the same.
The industry is evidently divided. So has been the policy-thinking, all this while.
But the US-listed Gogoro, which has a network of 1.1 million batteries across Taiwan and handles over 370,000 swaps daily, has a plan. It has been eyeing the world’s second-largest two-wheeler market, the “holy grail”, for some time. With its stock price down 65% since its April listing, Gogoro is badly in need of another large market. It also needs to convince India to clear the air on its subsidy in swapping.

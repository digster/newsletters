Good Morning Ishan,

Yet another season of the Indian Premier League has wrapped up. But did you catch the sudden buzz in the energy drinks market? Those Red Bull and Hurricane logos on jerseys? The Charged ads on Star Sports? And Jio Cinema’s shout-out to Sting?

If yes, you are already on the pulse of our Big Story today (its protagonist is in the last question).

What’s the deal with Sting, the energy drink Pepsico launched seven years ago, you ask?

  • It’s made energy drinks accessible to everyone in India, sparking a 30X market surge from 2018 to 2023.
  • It’s hooked teens and rural folks, tapping into a 126 million-strong student and low-income consumer base.
  • It’s been a game-changer for Pepsico’s bottler Varun Beverages, making its stock skyrocket from about Rs 150 (US$1.8) to about Rs 1,500 (US$18) in five years and turning its founder into a multibillionaire.
  • And it has taken over a 90% share of the market, a remarkable shift from six years ago when Red Bull was synonymous with energy drinks.

But the drink that makes Red Bull taste like pinot noir, as Aakriti writes, has also done something more. It has raised red flags due to caffeine and sugar overload.

So, what makes Sting tick? Sure, it’s easy on the pocket, priced at a cool one-sixth of Red Bull’s. And yes, its distribution game is great. (Coca-Cola and the ilk have done all they can to make their space.) But is it merely the first-mover advantage? Or perhaps it’s the seductive allure of its red and blue branding?

Dive into today’s story to uncover the highs, lows, and everything in between of India’s energy drink obsession. It’s as eye-opening as it is exhilarating, and trust me, you won’t want to miss a single sip.

RBI’s dividend: Beyond the windfall

The Reserve Bank of India (RBI) last week declared a US$25.5 billion dividend—a record-breaker that’s not just about the windfall. It’s a snapshot of the larger economic context. Suddenly, in an election year, the government has a chunk of money that wasn’t there before.

India’s economy has been through the wringer: stubborn inflation, sluggish growth, and heavy spending. So, the dividend is a lifeline. But the question is: what to do with it?

Now, the government has options aplenty. It could trim its borrowing, reduce its fiscal deficit, and improve its sovereign rating. Or, it could channel these funds into critical infrastructure—roads, bridges, digital networks—luring private investment. Then, there’s the idea of tax cuts (though it’s not the favourite play in the government’s book), which might just stir up consumer spending. Finally, India could use the cushion to slow down the rush to sell off public assets.

But let’s not overlook the fact that central banks stash reserves for proverbial rainy days. They don’t stumble upon it; it’s the result of astute investments in government securities, adept management of hefty foreign reserves, and prudent lending to banks.

The RBI may have tapped its reserves, and not its income, to dole out the dividend, according to an economist. Politically, yes, it’s a boon for the new government’s budget. But the central bank’s independence is still paramount.

Good Morning Ishan,
Shortly before leaving his job, the vice president of sales at one of India’s largest banks downloaded sensitive data of around 20,000 customers. Names and phone numbers. Email IDs and financial details. He converted the database into a PDF and sent it out over Whatsapp. There was no breach in the conventional sense. There was no external attack either. The leak was detected and contained before real damage was done.
Under India’s newly operational Digital Personal Data Protection Act, that same sequence would have played out very differently. The bank would have had 72 hours to inform regulators. Customers would need to be notified. Penalties could have climbed as high as Rs 250 crore. At that point, intent would no longer matter.
That is the practical reality the new law is forcing companies to confront.
Since November, any organisation that collects or stores personal data has been pushed into unfamiliar territory. Consent that once sat in a checkbox now has to be logged and refreshed. Data needs to be tracked as it moves through vendors or lands on employee devices. Deletion, too, has to be demonstrable.
Naturally, then, a new market has emerged to manage that shift. Startups are building consent tools. Security firms are scanning internal systems for forgotten access. Established KYC companies are repositioning themselves as compliance providers. Demand is rising fast, but clarity is not. Who qualifies as a consent manager? Can firms that process data also govern it? Can companies headquartered abroad really participate?
Europe once tried to drive compliance through fines. The results were uneven. India is now attempting its own version, backed by penalties large enough to warrant attention.

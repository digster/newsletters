Last week, the Central Board of Trustees (CBT) of the Employee Provident Fund Organisation (EPFO)—the statutory body established by the Union government to govern the social security agency—released a set of new guidelines that overhauls how and when members can withdraw their accumulated corpuses.
The move, aimed at simplifying numerous complex withdrawal conditions in force earlier, ended up sending many subscribers into a tailspin.
At the heart of the outrage were new provisions that extend the time that a person has to wait after losing or leaving a job before being able to withdraw their full provident fund and pension corpuses.
Earlier, subscribers could withdraw their full PF and pension balances just two months after losing or leaving a job. From 13 October, when the new rules took effect, that wait time has become 12 months for PF. And even longer for pensions under the Employee Pension Scheme (EPS)—36 months, or a full three years.
Put like that, no wonder many EPFO subscribers caught a case of the rages.
Imagine being told you will have to wait a year or more to access your full PF or pension corpus after being laid off.
But, as it can often turn out, the devil is in the details.
And in this case, that detail is the word “full”.
As the government hastened to [clarify](https://www.pib.gov.in/PressReleasePage.aspx?PRID=2179689) after public opposition started building to the new rules, the new lock-in periods for withdrawals only apply to 25% of a subscriber’s PF balance. The rest of the 75% can be withdrawn immediately after the loss of employment. So while a full withdrawal will take longer (two months earlier versus one year under the new rules), 75% can be withdrawn with no wait time, unlike the one-month lock-in period in effect before.
Also, for partial withdrawals:
Prior to the simplification of norms, the member was allowed to withdraw only the employee contribution and interest ranging from 50-100%. Now, the withdrawable amount will also include employer contribution besides employee contribution and interest. As a result, 75% of the eligible amount that can now be withdrawn will be much higher than the amount he/she could withdraw under the previous provisions.
Government of India clarification on new PF norms
In fact, the new provisions make several other aspects of withdrawal easier and less onerous.
Currently, there are around 13 separate reasons one can make a premature withdrawal from the PF corpus—such as education, marriages, investing in a home, etc—with different rules and conditions to be met for each. The new guidelines consolidate these categories into just three: essential needs, housing needs, and special circumstances. The number of times one can make a partial withdrawal from their PF over the course of their employment have also been increased. For education and wedding requirements, this has been increased to 10 times and five times, respectively, from just three times earlier.
I spoke to a few investment experts to understand whether the government’s move to extend the waiting periods for full withdrawals make sense, and they say the angst amongst subscribers arises from not seeing their PF and pension corpuses the way they should ideally be seen: namely, as a long-term investment that takes advantage of compounding effects. Instead, for many, PF ends up becoming an emergency fund.
To quote from the government’s statement:
Repeated withdrawals routinely led to insufficient PF balance at the time of retirement. 50% of PF Members had less than Rs 20,000 in the PF balance and 75% had less than Rs 50,000 at the time of final settlement. Due to repeated withdrawal, the workers with lower salaries did not realize the benefits of compounding @8.25% and thereby losing out on higher social security at the end of their working life.
That is why, as per CBT’s decision, 25% of the contribution needs to be retained to ensure respectable corpus at retirement as a safety net and to provide long-term social security.
“Are these (rules) paternalistic? Yes. But, they are also not outright illogical,” Deepesh Raghaw, a registered investment advisor told me. “If you do not have funds to tide over for a few months (in case of job loss or other emergencies), it could only mean that you have not been planning your finances properly, and there is nothing much that the government can do about it.” That may sound tone-deaf to many, but truth is often bitter.
Raghaw says it is best to have a well-diversified portfolio to hedge against risks—both in the short and long terms. And a critical component of this portfolio needs to be an emergency corpus—which can be a liquid fund, arbitrage fund, or even a plain bank fixed deposit.
“The EPFO allows you to withdraw for various reasons. The purposes of withdrawal are available to you. But just because they are available does not mean you should immediately use it,” Raghaw noted.
Abhishek Kumar, another registered investment advisor, agreed. “The provident fund should not be considered an emergency corpus to manage unforeseen events. It should always be part of your long-term planning for retirement or other goals.”
In any long-term savings product, the point is to allow your money to work for you—i.e., let the capital experience the power of compounding. Which is where the “provident” in the provident fund comes in. “Once you take out the money from PF, there is no way we can credit it back to the account, so you lose out on all the future gains from compounding. (The extended wait time) is a way of saying—don’t just exhaust your retirement corpus, think it through,” Kumar said.
The government’s nanny act may come across as a bit patronising and ‘big brother’-y. But not every corpus needs to be an immediately liquidatable one. Especially if it’s meant for the long haul.

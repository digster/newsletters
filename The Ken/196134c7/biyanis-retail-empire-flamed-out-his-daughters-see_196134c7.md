Good Morning Ishan,
What happens when the redemption-arc meets ghosts of the past? Ask Ashni and Avni, daughters of erstwhile retail czar Kishore Biyani. After Future Group, the retail empire set up by their father, got crushed by debt during the pandemic, the Biyani sisters made a fresh start. A shot at rebuilding a legacy. Tapping into their retail chops—honed during their time with Future—they launched Think9 Consumer in 2021. But unlike the mass-market focused Future, Think9 doesn’t really do “dukandari” (shop-keeping)—a term made infamous by commerce minister Piyush Goyal last week, when he chided Indian startups for not being ambitious enough. At least not “dukandari” in the traditional sense.
Instead, Think9 has taken a leaf out of the roll-up book, and is incubating new brands for the young and the rich spanning consumer themes like apparel, beauty, food, and health. The plan? Found jazzy online-first brands, grow them to scale, and exit in 5–7 years. As many as 13 brands have got rolling so far. On paper, it all sounds nice, looks neat.
But reality is no respecter of reputations and legacies. When the rubber hit the road, the cracks surfaced, the contradictions showed. Then, there are the ghosts of the past that are coming calling for Think9. The market doesn’t easily forget or forgive. Can the Biyani daughters make a clean, profitable break, and resurrect the dynasty’s name? It’s a long haul, writes Aakriti in today’s well-reported [story](https://the-ken.com/story/biyanis-retail-empire-flamed-out-his-daughters-seek-redemption-in-birthing-new-brands/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter).

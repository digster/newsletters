Physics Wallah raises money. What’s next? The new funding has set off rumours of an edtech revival 
 By Olina Banerji https://the-ken.com/writers/oli-banerjigmail-com/

Good Morning Ishan,

I’m writing one part of this edition from a Death Cab for Cutie concert, so I hope you excuse the levity. The theme of the concert is second acts: older millennial bands relevant to a generation that doesn’t decide the top ten hits anymore. Our playlists are mostly nostalgic.

If Byju’s is the elder millennial of all Indian edtech, where would you place Physics Wallah (PW)? Since its launch in 2016, PW has embodied a yolo-ness in its approach to business: upload everything, charge nothing, and watch the sector burn. It was the uncontrollable GenZ urge to democratise everything through the internet. 

And when the rest of the sector took a downward plunge—with shrinking revenue and runways—PW seemed to be doing the opposite. Its subscribers grew multiple-fold, as did its cult following. 

And this Friday, PW announced that it has raised a sizable US$210 million at a valuation of US$2.8 billion, doubling what it was valued at when it first received funding in 2022. In a struggling sector, that’s nothing to sneeze at. 

The real question in my mind, though, hasn’t ever been whether PW would raise funds. But rather, what it would do with the money. It can either learn from the mistakes of other edtechs that came before it and spend on things that are totally different, or go down the same route. 

Turns out, PW’s going to have it both ways. Here’s how.

Familiar plans

In the previous generation of edtech, whenever a company raised a significant second or third round, talks of an IPO would inevitably come up. It’s no different for PW, though people now are more cautious about throwing the word around loosely.

Will PW go in for an IPO?

My guess is that PW’s founders knew this question would come. So, they had an answer ready. In an interview https://www.cnbc.com/2024/09/20/indian-ed-tech-startup-physics-wallah-bags-2point8-billion-valuation-with-funding.html with CNBC, co-founder Alakh Pandey said:

“An IPO is something that we will do. We want to have a strong governance in the company, we are working on that, forming a board of independent directors … it’s not that important for us when the IPO will happen, we are running the company like a public company,” Pandey said.

Notice what’s happening here. PW isn’t ruling out the possibility of an IPO. But it isn’t overpromising either. Pandey is trying to avoid the pressure that Byju’s took up when it promised to deliver one of the most successful stock-market outings by an Indian firm on a global stage. I remember everyone discussing whether Byju’s would list on the NSE or internationally.

PW isn’t making any plans it won’t stick to—at least for now. Pandey’s statement on governance is also a subtle nod to doing things differently than his predecessors. It’s unclear, though, why Pandey thinks his company is as transparent as a listed one. 

Will PW go on an acquisition spree?

Here again, PW may take a path well trodden. PW isn’t shy of racking up its acquisition count, but it doesn’t do it with much fanfare. Which is probably a great thing when compared to the media circus we had when Byju’s acquired WhiteHat Jr. PW has so far acquired six other startups, which seem to have boosted its user base and added products for its college-to-career pipeline. Like other edtechs before it, PW’s also worked to bring younger students into the fold now by offering products up to class 8.

Now, Byju’s did this too, and at the time, it made sense that it would buy startups like Osmo, or take over a struggling asset like Toppr. But Byju’s also tried a lot of random tie-ups, like its whole thing with Disney, or messed up big-ticket mergers, like with Aakash, where the handover of power was rough.

In contrast, PW is likely to keep its acquisitions on the DL, because it doesn’t want to attract attention in case things go sideways.

Will PW let its valuation dictate its business?

This is a more polite way of asking, ‘Will investors meddle in PW’s growth plans?’ 

By now, we know what the drill is: with more money comes more inorganic acquisitions, new business models, and pressure to deliver returns.

There’s some of that with PW surely: a new startup school, a coding-focused vertical, an English-prep program. PW also seems as thinly stretched out as its predecessors did at their height. 

PW, and its investors, have the benefit of hindsight, and can avoid putting it through the kind of growth pressure older edtechs faced. PW’s success is still contingent on the price arbitration it can achieve because of its massive Youtube lead-generation funnel. It needs this kind of guerilla marketing to work in order to keep its conversions high. It may replace this business model with another in the future, but it will have to find another medium that works for it as well as Youtube.

The advantage is that PW doesn’t have to choose between an online and offline model. Post-Covid, the hybrid model is more acceptable than it was earlier. Older edtechs felt like they had to choose between one or the other, and the whole thesis of growth was to shun the offline-centre model since it was slow to grow. In PW’s era, everything is possible—study free courses online, attend a class in person, solve doubts by asking a bot.

PW may walk the same roads to an IPO, like the earlier cohort did. But everything it does will have the benefit of hindsight. Every decision likely has a precedent to fall back on. It’s PW’s game to lose.

What else caught our eye

Canada to restrict inflow of international students

Canadian Prime Minister Justin Trudeau said https://www.business-standard.com/finance/personal-finance/canada-to-cut-student-visas-by-10-in-2025-another-blow-for-indians-124091900447_1.html this week on social media platform X (formerly Twitter) that the country would cut international student permits by 35% this year, with an additional 10% curtailment the following year. Indian students, who make up a large share of Canada’s foreign students, ought to be affected significantly. The move is a result of rising immigration contributing to a population surge in Canada in recent years, driving up the cost of living and affecting the availability of low-wage jobs for Canadians.

University of Southampton to establish campus in Gurugram

On Wednesday, the University of Southampton announced https://www.hindustantimes.com/education/news/university-of-southampton-set-to-establish-new-campus-in-delhi-ncr-aims-to-strengthen-india-uk-education-ties-101726662880568.html that it plans to set up a campus in India, and take in undergraduate and postgraduate students for courses ranging from finance to computer science. The University of Melbourne had also recently opened https://www.thehindu.com/education/university-of-melbourne-launches-centre-in-delhi/article68654945.ece a centre (not an offshore campus) in Delhi to promote intercultural relations in academia. 

ISB to take in students with no work experience for 20-month long PGP 

The Indian School of Business (ISB) has launched https://indianexpress.com/article/education/isb-introduces-pgp-in-management-for-early-career-professionals-fee-over-rs-21-lakh-9574168/ a post-graduate programme for young leaders (PGP YL), specifically targeted at the younger cohort of graduates with no work experience. In contrast, the business school’s flagship PGP only admits students with a minimum of two years of work experience. The new PGP YL will also accept CAT scores in addition to the GRE and GMAT, unlike the flagship PGP course, which only accepts the last two.

ADB asks Pakistan to draw on India’s ULLAS scheme to improve education sector

The Asian Development Bank (ADB) has advised https://tribune.com.pk/story/2496268/pakistan-asked-to-adopt-indian-plan Pakistan to take note of India’s New India Literacy programme (aka the ULLAS scheme). The scheme is a programme introduced in alignment with the National Education Policy 2020 to enhance literacy among those aged 15 or above who are outside the formal education system. The ADB’s recommendation was followed by the ADB approving https://www.business-standard.com/world-news/adb-assures-cash-strapped-pakistan-2-billion-in-new-loans-annually-report-124091700343_1.html a loan of US$8 billion for the cash-strapped country a few days later, with US$2 billion to be disbursed annually over four years.

Amazon’s Jeff Bezos famously banned Powerpoint presentations within meetings in 2004 and moved the company to document writing. The purpose was clear: to think through problems in a meticulous way.
Since then, Amazon’s meeting ritual goes like this: employees walk into the meeting room, grab a printed copy of the document, and spend the next 30–40 minutes just reading. Then the discussion starts.
“I like a crisp document and a messy meeting,” said Bezos in a 2023 Lex Fridman podcast. “The document should be written with such clarity that it’s like angels singing from on high…And the meeting is about asking questions that nobody knows the answer to and trying to wander your way to a solution.”
This Amazonian document-writing culture became so legendary that companies from social-media platform Twitter under founder Jack Dorsey to cloud-based storage and service provider Dropbox embraced it.
But things are changing. Weeks of agonising over these six-page memos is giving way to instantaneous prompts on an AI tool.
This recent turn towards AI is company induced. Amazon has enabled internal AI tools such as Amazon Q, a generative virtual assistant, and Cedric, an AI chatbot “that is safer than ChatGPT” and trained in the Amazonian way of writing—and is encouraging employees to use them wherever possible.
But can an AI-assisted document have the same effect, or result? What happens to Bezos’ idea of “truth seeking”?

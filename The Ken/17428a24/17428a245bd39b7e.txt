No one can blame Ola, India’s number one ride-hailing platform*, for not being nimble, ambitious or aggressive. Against all odds, it staved off years of challenges from its much better-funded rival Uber, drawing on their epic fight and bleeding its opponent out till public investors and a new CEO balked at the prospect of continued losses.

Alas, the victory was pyrrhic. Because, who could ever plan for a scenario where people would be locked down in their homes for the better part of an entire year, and possibly more?

Not many. Perhaps not Bhavish Aggarwal, Ola’s co-founder and CEO.

But what Aggarwal did do was to take out a few of the eggs from his then overflowing Ola basket and place them in smaller ones. Like cloud kitchens. Electric vehicles. Grocery delivery. Meal delivery. And fintech.

Whether those bets were strategic diversification ones or tactical ones driven by FOMO, we’ll never know. Aggarwal does not give out interviews in general, and to The Ken in specific.

Many of those bets flamed out, sometimes spectacularly and sometimes quietly. Some, like Ola Electric, are still ambitious but unproven.

Then, there was fintech. Born of Ola’s desire to replicate the Uber-Paytm (digital wallet) combo that was so potent in India’s early ride-hailing years, it created OlaMoney, its own wallet. Unfortunately, OlaMoney never really took off in a meaningful way within India’s intensely competitive fintech industry. It accounts for roughly 5% of digital wallet transactions for one of India’s largest payment aggregators. Even Ola’s ride-hailing customers weren’t enthused by it, with just 20% using OlaMoney.

But it was still a good bet. Because, in 2020, with the pandemic having decimated ride-hailing for the near-term, OlaMoney is coming out on its own.

Ola Financial Services, a newly spun out entity from Ola, houses OlaMoney, postpaid payments, co-branded cards, and insurance. And it just raised $27 million in venture capital at a valuation of $240 million. In May 2020.

In fact, when Ola laid off almost 1,400 people out of a 6,000-strong workforce in May, it did not include a single employee from OlaMoney’s team of about 100-120, said an OlaMoney executive.

But can a platform whose primary purpose wasn’t already digital payments make fintech work? It’s not easy. Which is why Amazon India, Flipkart, and Ola have all struggled to match the successes of Google Pay, Paytm and PhonePe.

By spinning out Ola Financial Services, it looks like Ola is starting to learn from its earlier mistakes, write Arundhati and Pranav in today's story: https://the-ken.com/story/ola-amazon-and-the-flaw-in-faafing-for-fintech-dominance/ https://the-ken.com/story/ola-amazon-and-the-flaw-in-faafing-for-fintech-dominance/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter (10-minute read)

There are few things more Indian than complaining about the heat. Unless, of course, you are an air-conditioner company complaining about the lack of it.

Havells, the electricals giant that sells everything from cables to ceiling fans, is in one such position. It spent years trying to turn its AC brand, Lloyd, into the next big consumer-durables story—new factories, fancy ads, celebrity endorsements, the works. It even won awards for keeping its dealers happy. Very happy.

And yet, here they are: warehouses stuffed with unsold ACs, new energy-efficiency norms looming in January, and a balance sheet sweating harder than customers. Even the stock, once buoyed by the company’s consumer push, hasn’t felt the breeze.

It’s not that Havells did anything wrong, exactly. It just did everything—at once, and expensively. It built capacity when summers were long and demand was hot. It showered dealers with incentives, sponsored cricket tournaments, and positioned Lloyd as an affordable, mid-premium brand. All of which worked beautifully… until it didn’t.

Now, Havells has to offload a mountain of soon-to-be-obsolete inventory without burning too much cash (a tricky feat when over Rs 3,000 crore of capital is already tied up chasing growth). Priyal, in her story today, looks at Havells’ big bet on Lloyd: how it grew fast, spent faster, and ended up running hard to stay in the same place.

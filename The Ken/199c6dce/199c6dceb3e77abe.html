As part of its ambitious National Education Policy 2020, India said it would allow select universities ranked among the top 100 globally to set up campuses here.

For a country plagued by sub-part collegiate education, the idea was a bit of a stretch. Predictably, by the time the policy was codified three years later, India had moved the goalposts and began targeting the top 500 universities.

Two Australian universities, Wollongong and Deakin, have been in Gujarat’s Gift City for over a year. And eight global counterparts, mostly from the UK, are on their way. Of these 10, only two—the universities of Southampton and Bristol—feature in the top 100 of QS World University Rankings.

States such as Maharashtra are hard-selling their prospective “educities”, or clusters of foreign universities.

Middling institutions in higher education are a dime a dozen in India. So the government’s desire to expand their ranks with yet more undistinguished universities is befuddling. Especially when the labour market is in turmoil.

But, as Atul argues in a compelling story, India’s hand has been forced.

I find it intriguing that many leaders across various GCCs (Global Capability Centers) are expecting decision-making authority to be delegated to them. Since when has power been shared with nodes so distant from the central hub (or power center)?

While it’s trendy to label GCCs as emerging innovation hubs, we are far from realising that vision. Let’s be honest. Most GCCs are still primarily back-office operations. Critical decisions, especially those that can significantly affect the core of the business, cannot simply be handed off to far-off locations. Most parent companies are publicly listed and accountable to their shareholders.

In a world obsessed with customer experience and “zero distance,” decisions often require a deep, nuanced understanding of local markets, specific regulations, diverse consumer segments, and other complex variables. The reality is that many people in BFSI GCCs have never set foot in a Bank of America or Wells Fargo branch. The same applies to retail GCCs like Giant Eagle or JC Penney. We need to be realistic and grounded in our expectations, despite the hype surrounding GCCs.

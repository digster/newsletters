Good Morning Ishan,
By now we know vegetarianism is a touchy subject in India.
Mankind Pharma, the country’s fourth-largest pharmaceutical company by domestic sales, learnt it the hard way when it marketed its multivitamin tablet HealthOK as a vitamin for vegetarians.
Mankind is one of the few pharmaceuticals that sell over-the-counter (OTC) products and relies on edgy marketing to do so.
Thanks to its marketing, the company’s OTC range of products is so successful that it’s easy to forget that it is a pharmaceutical that makes a billion dollars in total revenue, even as its brands like Manforce (condoms), Prega News (pregnancy detection kits), and Unwanted-72 (contraceptive) are market leaders in their categories.
So, when it saw that post-Covid, there was a rush among people to load up on vitamins, it took the call to sell HealthOK, a prescription drug until then, as an over-the-counter one. And it relied on marketing it as a 100% vegetarian product.
Let’s just say things didn’t go as planned. [Shivani’s story today](https://the-ken.com/story/mankind-pharmas-canny-marketing-worked-wonders-until-it-began-selling-supplements/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter) looks at just what Mankind had riding on this gamble.
Parents would give an arm and leg to make sure their babies eat. And even though ads and marketing and discounts are directed at adults, it is important for companies to hook the babies to the brand. After all, what are you going to do, if your baby refuses to eat anything else?
Consumer food companies know this well.
Nestlé, the world’s largest consumer goods firm, was found to be adding 2.7g of sugar for every serving of baby products like Cerelac that are sold in India. That is a little more than half a teaspoon of sugar (1 teaspoon = 4.2g) in every serving.
A Swiss non-profit found this when it sent samples of the multinational’s baby food products sold in Asia, Africa, and Latin America to a Belgian laboratory. This while it adds no sugar to the products it sells in Europe.
Nestlé India went into crisis containment mode and said that it has been reducing sugar content over the last five years. It reduced sugar content across its products by 30%.
So if the half teaspoon per serving is the effort of a 30% reduction, imagine the amount of sugar in the products sold five years ago.
The number of years it will take for Nestlé to go the zero-sugar way in India, too, will directly depend on how loud Indian regulators cry.

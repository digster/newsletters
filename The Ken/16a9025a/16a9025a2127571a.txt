Good Morning Ishan,

If ever there’s a book written on how pharma company Ranbaxy Laboratories sold out for $4.6 billion in 2008 and what promoters did with that money, there will be a solid chapter on SRL Diagnostics. Founded in 1995 as a faction of Ranbaxy, SRL, the largest diagnostic chain in India, has charted multiple courses but none ended at the stock exchange which it so dearly wanted. It’s been a 10-year itch.

Now, bankers have a mandate to find buyers for the 43% stake the private equity investors and other shareholders hold.

Last year when Malaysia’s IHH Healthcare bought Fortis Healthcare—the hospital chain which at some point was the major contributor to SRL’s revenues—it did not buy all of SRL. Whereas the Manipal Group, which lost the bid for Fortis, wanted to buy Fortis with SRL in tow. Apparently, Manipal valued it at Rs 3600 crore. Today, few would find SRL that valuable.

If the germs were SRL’s, accumulated over the years, promoters’ legacy was the wind.

Pathology may be a commoditised service where it is hard to differentiate because one lab can’t displace another lab, yet it is a central piece in the healthcare whole. It can bring huge margins if done right. Naturally, SRL’s competitors have sped to the public markets, leaving it behind as an onlooker.

The new buyers will surely weigh IHH’s ability to unlock SRL’s potential as a market leader. IHH, of course, has a bigger problem at hand—to fix Fortis. Where and how SRL dropped the ball is Ruhi’s story today. She dissects it down to three major issues and in so doing, tells us what the others are getting right.

“Open-sourcing big models is like being able to buy nuclear weapons at Radio Shack.”

That’s what Geoffrey Hinton, one of the godfathers of AI, said a while back. The idea, perhaps dramatic, was simple: AI is too powerful, too unpredictable to be handed out like coupons. If every enterprise could run its own LLM, who would control it?

Well, turns out, nobody. Because in January, Deepseek R1 happened.

A China-built, open-source model that slashes costs by 98% while keeping up with OpenAI’s best? That’s not just another AI model—it’s a detonation of different proportions. The moment enterprises saw they could host AI themselves, the old rules stopped mattering. Banks, insurers, and corporations that once treated OpenAI’s API pricing like a tax are suddenly asking: why pay for a black box when we can have/build our own?

And that’s where the real shift is happening—not in the models, but in who’s making AI work behind the scenes.

For years, LLMops companies—the ones that actually deploy, optimise, and glue AI models together—were ignored. Too technical. Too backend. Too boring. But suddenly, BFSI firms are knocking on their doors, desperate to integrate Deepseek into their workflows.

So now, the stuff that used to make investors’ eyes glaze over is making them write cheques. Because AI isn’t a one-model world anymore. The smartest companies aren’t choosing between OpenAI or Deepseek. They’re stacking models like Legos:

  • Deepseek for cost efficiency and logic-heavy work
  • Claude for calculations
  • Gemini for content
  • OpenAI (when they can justify the price tag)

But AI models don’t naturally play nice together, which is why the real money is in making them work seamlessly. Enter Simplismart, Pipeshift, Lyzr AI, and other LLMops firms quietly taking over AI infra.

Google, of course, recently said Deepseek is overhyped. OpenAI might have to actually compete now. But enterprises? They’re already moving on.

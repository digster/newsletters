Good Morning Ishan,
If data is the new oil, Netradyne might well qualify as an oil baron. A mighty one at that. The name might not immediately ring a bell. But this relatively unknown Indian fleet-safety-solution provider claims to have collected a cool 18 billion miles of road data and counting. Across multiple countries—the US, Canada, Germany, the UK, Mexico, Australia, and, of course, India. Really.
The 10-year-old company is oiling, okay milking, all this data—and its understanding of the roads—pretty well. It’s clocked big revenue by Indian startup standards, and earlier this month, it raised $90 million in a Series D round to become India’s first unicorn of 2025. Its backers in earlier rounds include biggies like India’s original oil baron Reliance Industries and global moneybags like Softbank. Netradyne must be doing something right.
Here’s what it does. It claims to make roads safer for truckers by keeping a close watch on both the driver and the road. This, it does, with high-tech cameras and related paraphernalia that ping alerts when something’s amiss. Big brother? Privacy intrusion? Maybe, but all’s good for the greater good, maybe. Anyway, in the process, Netradyne collects truckloads of data—from 3,000 customers including the likes of Amazon, Shell, and Indian Oil, and 450,000 vehicles. Nice.
But, and there’s always a but, what if a future cometh where trucks don’t need drivers? That future—of autonomous vehicles—is coming slowly but surely. Will Netradyne die, then? Or can it pull a rabbit out of all its data hat? Also, pray, how does Netradyne deal with the great Indian road? Is it on a ping and a prayer in this epitome of chaos?
Today, Abhirami’s fine story has the answers. It’s one for the road. [Read it here](https://the-ken.com/story/netradyne-made-a-1-3b-business-out-of-surveilling-drivers-now-it-must-focus-on-driver-less-cars/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter).

While fewer businesses are enrolling themselves on the platform, Indiamart is also proving to be not that valuable for a chunk of its subscribers.

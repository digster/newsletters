Good morning fellow Sunday dwellers, which includes you Ishan. Isn’t that what we are? Hundreds of thousands of eclectic, diverse, and unique individuals living all over the world, united only by our unhurried Sunday life.

This edition leans into that vibe.

I often wonder if this newsletter is meant to be read in a reflective mood for the week gone by, or in a contemplative one for the week starting up?

I’m torn. Sundays are relaxed and reflective for me, but I also find it hard to not spend much of it thinking about the week that lies ahead. And much as I love Monday mornings (probably my next favourite morning of the week, after Sundays), it is also when I often feel that dreaded Imposter Syndrome.

“There is so much to do this week and not enough time. Have you finally bitten off more than you can chew, Rohin?”

“This time you’ve done it. You’ve been playing a game way beyond your skills and capabilities. And boy, the crash is going to be glorious.”

And then, after the first sip of a freshly pressed coffee, it passes.

It’s surprising how long this newsletter has run without me talking about coffee.

This week, our espresso grinder broke down, so we are surviving with pre-ground coffee till it gets repaired.

The coffee rabbit hole is very much like the hi-fi music rabbit hole. You can always spend more money to go deeper, convincing yourself that pure gold cables or third wave water make your music or coffee so much better.

I won’t argue with either. To each their own. We all need our vices or passions.

We “invested” in an espresso machine within a few months of the pandemic of 2020. Hands-down, it was one of our best investments, reliably delivering impeccable coffee every morning (and through the day).

Honestly, I think the best test of what a great investment (not monetary) is, is how often you use it in a day or week, and how much joy it gives you.

What is it for you? I’d love to know.

Sundays are also bath-ritual days for me and Gabru. As soon as he realises he’s going to have a bath (either because I use the word “bath”, or because he sees me filling a bucket with hot water), he slinks off to some faraway corner, tail between his legs, ears folded back.

Then, when I go to get him, he turns turtle or goes weightless. I have no option but to lift him up and carry him to the bathroom.

After the bath and the towelling, we go for a brief walk. He knows his meal will be waiting for him when we get back, so I’m practically dragged back, like Ben-Hur, during the latter half of the walk.

After the meal he does his “happy dance” on a nearby bed or floor. Dragging his snout, throwing his body to all sides, “digging” with his paws. That’s him telling us he’s happy and full.

These last few Sundays, the late evening sun is just the right warmth for us both to sit in the balcony, enjoying the golden rays of the golden hour. Me reading a book, him watching the world and barking at other dogs passing by.

2. Decisions, decisions, decisions

Welcome, dear reader, to this edition’s books section. I’m Anushka, and every week, I ask you to take a quick look at our book recommendations with the hope that you’ll find your next read. It’s a big ask though, I realise that. Are the reviews convincing enough? What is the opportunity cost of reading this book versus the one hanging around—waiting, longing to be picked up—on your TBR list? You better get to that one first, right? After all, you put that there for a reason. 

I don’t know about you, but even small decisions like this push me into a spiral of overthinking. So many choices, so much to read, so little time. Very recently, I learnt from a friend that there’s a term for this: analysis paralysis. “An individual or group process where overanalyzing or overthinking a situation can cause forward motion or decision-making to become ‘paralyzed’, meaning that no solution or course of action is decided upon within a natural time frame.” 

Analysis Paralysis basically says that your fear of not making a superior decision grows so large, takes up so much time, that it overshadows any potential success you could have if you acted in a timely manner.

But perhaps there are solutions, or experiments, that could help. I have three suggestions. 

The first is something already familiar to those in behavioural sciences: limit choices. This paper lays out a simple observation—consumers are quicker and surer of their decisions to purchase when then there are fewer options to choose from. This reminds me of something that my colleague Seetharaman wrote in an edition of Trade Tricks (The Ken’s weekly newsletter on retail and commerce). Quick-commerce companies like Blinkit and Zepto present so few choices for non-grocery items that you just buy whatever looks good enough. You wouldn’t do the same on a Myntra or Amazon. 

I suppose it’s only helpful that we have a limited list of four books for you to choose from this week. 

The second comes from an older edition of First Principles: place yourself at the centre of your decision, instead of observing as a third person. Ask yourself why you’re making this decision, and what you believe in. Go meta, as Rohin says. 

And the final one comes from one of our book recommendations this week! 

“Annie says life is like poker, but most of us play it like it’s a game of chess,” says Akshaya Chandrasekaran, my colleague in The Ken’s podcasts team. She recommends Thinking in Bets by Annie Duke.

View in Browser

“Chess contains no hidden information. If you lose at a game of chess, it must be because there were better moves that you didn’t make or see. Poker, in contrast, is a game of incomplete information. You could make the best possible decision at every point and still lose the hand, because you don’t know what new cards will be dealt or revealed. Much like life. You could run a red light and get through the intersection safely. Or follow all the traffic rules and still end up in an accident. That doesn’t mean running a red light is a good decision and following rules is a bad decision.” 

Here’s an excerpt from the book itself:

Every decision is a bet. A bet against all the future versions of ourselves that we are not choosing. We are constantly deciding among alternative futures. Futures where we take a job in a different city, stay at our current job, or take some time away from work. Whenever we make a choice, we are betting on a potential future. We are betting that the future version of us that results from the decisions we make will be better off. At stake in a decision is that the return to us (measured in money, time, happiness, health or whatever we value in that circumstance) will be greater than what we are giving up by betting against the other alternative future version of us.

Not convinced about picking up Thinking in Bets? Perhaps you’d like stories that you can learn from. 

Ashwini Rao, a subscriber, recommends Loonshots by Safi Bahcall, “a book of crazy ideas which when pursued becomes really big.”

And if you’re in the mood to read fiction, we have a wonderful recommendation from Rajan Kalia, who says that Cutting For Stone describes rain lovingly. You may have seen the author Abraham Varghese’s new book, The Covenant of Water, making headlines for the awards it’s been winning.

And our last recommendation, from Karan Rajpal, is Butter by Asako Yuzuki. This book has been making the rounds on the internet, and is my favourite recommendation yet simply because it’s what I’m reading right now!

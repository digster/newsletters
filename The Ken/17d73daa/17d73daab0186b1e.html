Good Morning Ishan,
 
Over a year ago, I moved houses. This meant deep cleaning, pest control, carpentry, plumbing, balcony netting for my cats… and finally, when all this and more was done, full-body massages for my partner and me.
 
All of these services were handled by a single company—the protagonist of our story today, Urban Company. While the $2.1 billion valued one-stop app is super convenient (and largely affordable) for its myriad services, it can't always satisfy all its stakeholders.
 
Urban Company partners—skilled gig workers, often trained and supported by the company—have held protests and expressed a fair few grievances of late. Some have even alleged that the company had hiked commissions to 30% and assigned late-night bookings for its women partners.
 
Unlike Uber, Ola, Swiggy and scores of other tech companies resting (relatively) easy on their gig workers, UC goes into people's homes. Its partners need to be better than the best—skilled, friendly, hygienic, to name a few basic requirements—and therefore, it cannot afford to lose its investment in them.
 
So what has the company done? It introduced insurance benefits, opportunities for better earnings, vaccinated its staff, etc. But philanthropy does not a profitable company make.
 
The company's growth is slowing, and its losses are mounting. And UC’s promised measures will only dent its numbers further. Even if Urban Company manages to satisfy both its customer and partner cohorts, though, there's a third cohort it could upset. Potential investors.

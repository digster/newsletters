In spite of its seven-minute loans, agri-tech startup Arya.ag—by co-founders Anand Chandra, Prasanna Rao, and Chattanathan Devarajan—has been unable to stop farmer savings from shrinking to zero
Arya.ag’s collateral-based lending model is facing its biggest test as agricultural commodity prices crash across the board. The company has disrupted rural lending by offering farmers quick, digital loans at significantly lower rates than local moneylenders, using AI-enabled warehouse systems that process everything in minutes. The company targets the two-thirds of agricultural produce that never reaches urban markets—which small farmers and cooperatives sell in primary markets largely ignored by banks. Though the platform offers farmers much-needed agency, it can’t fix a broken system that keeps prices in freefall. [Big AI shows up in India. The antitrust regulator hasn’t got the memo](https://the-ken.com/story/big-ai-shows-up-in-india-the-antitrust-regulator-hasnt-got-the-memo/?utm_source=weekly_email&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=weekly_recap_card)
India’s AI boom depends on global models. Its competition watchdog is still figuring out how to regulate them
Most AI startups run on foreign foundation models from OpenAI, Google, Anthropic, or Meta, outsourcing their infrastructure, pricing, and control. Algorithms compete and can collude. When code starts coordinating prices or behaviour, you can’t fine it or jail it, making antitrust enforcement a new kind of puzzle. The CCI’s fix is “self-audit”. Its first market study on AI urges firms to trace their data and models, but that’s a light-touch remedy for a heavyweight problem. The watchdog itself is short on muscle. Too few staff—and none with data science expertise—leaves it ill-equipped for the market it’s meant to monitor.

Dear Ishan, hello and welcome back. Have you ever observed yourself immersed in “return trip energy”?

Ha ha, sorry for that rather abrupt opening. But yesterday, I caught myself observing something I’ve known and felt for virtually most of life. I was walking back home with Gabru after our morning walk. I like watching him as he’s walking back home because I know he’s satisfied and content. He’s a very different dog than the one who drags me out of the door as we’re leaving for the walk.

While leaving, he’s impatient and excited. During the walk, he’s curious, sniffing, and zigzagging at every tree stump, bush, or wet spot on the path. He’s easily triggered by every passing dog and could jump or lunge at anything that catches his fancy, forcing me to stay on guard all the time to avoid faceplanting myself.

On the way back, though, he’s calm and prim, trotting along in about as close to a straight path he can manage. He ignores most distractions. He simply wants to be home.

I’ve seen him in this mood many hundreds of times over the years. But it was only this week that I realised his “return trip energy” was infectious. I felt happy, calm, and content.

Upon reflecting a bit deeper, I realised we exhibit some versions of these energies when we’re going on vacations and coming back from them.

As we’re leaving for vacations, we’re excited, anxious, and edgy. The future is full of possibilities. The journey is what matters.

When we’re returning though, it’s the destination that matters. We’re tired and content, and all we want is to get back in the straightest possible path. We want to go home.

Both energies are infectious too.

Virtually all of us have experienced these. Many of us may even have consciously become aware of it. I only managed to do it this week. Perhaps it’s because we’re nearing summer vacation season!

It’s also summer, meaning flowers and fruits!

Now that the Tabebuias have all receded into the background, others are stepping up into the spotlight. For instance, this riotous Gulmohar a few lanes down from The Ken’s offices.

1. Learning to time travel

Imagine you’re an actuary with an insurance firm. You spend months thinking of a new way to model premiums with customer profiles and claim ratios. Finally, late one night, you come up with a new model to price premiums much lower even while improving profits. Sleepy-eyed and excited the next day, you propose to your boss that your company implement your new plan.

Will they?

Okay, let’s imagine you’re an up-and-coming quant at an algorithmic trading company. You have just found a new way to simultaneously hedge against sudden swings in the stock-market indexes, while betting on companies that are impacted by US King President Donald Trump’s “policies”. Your strategy is going to lead to an 11% increase in trading profits in just one month. You go to your boss and tell them to bet 50% of the company’s funds on your model.

Will they?

Okay last example.

You’ve been renewing your gym subscriptions every January for the last two years, but you barely use it. After some introspection, you realise the reason was because you did not have good workout shoes and a smart watch and a subscription to a workout log service. Oh, and moving your workout timings from the mornings to the evenings. You’re convinced that now you’re going to be a regular at the gym.

Will you?

Of course, you don’t have to answer me. But think about how often we make strategies and plans, telling ourselves, “this time it’s going to work”.

Do they always? Of course they don’t.

But that’s not the point. The point is, what can you do to progressively improve your chances of success as time goes by.

You can test your plan backwards and forwards in time.

“Backtesting” is used extensively in financial or trading models to “predict” if the new model would have worked in the past.

Backtesting assesses the viability of a trading strategy or pricing model by discovering how it would have played out retrospectively using historical data.

The underlying theory is that any strategy that worked well in the past is likely to work well in the future, and conversely, any strategy that performed poorly in the past is likely to perform poorly in the future.

The climate research sector has an even better name for this: “hindcasting” (the opposite of forecasting).

When you come up with a new or improved way to solve a long-running problem (or capture a long-running opportunity), you should force yourself to time travel to all the past instances when earlier solutions to the problem failed. If you had your new solution back then, would the outcome have been different? Would you have been successful back then?

But what fun is it to only test for success in the past? Enter “forward-testing”, also known as “walk forward analysis”.

“Walk forward analysis is a sophisticated technique used to test and optimize trading strategies. Unlike traditional backtesting, which evaluates a strategy over a fixed historical period, walk forward analysis continuously updates and refines the strategy by moving through the data incrementally. This dynamic approach aims to simulate real-world trading conditions more accurately.”

If you’re thinking that these are statistical or financial methods, and thus not appropriate for your “regular” decision-making, you’re wrong.

The powerful concept here isn’t the mathematical bit, instead it’s the simulation and testing against actual events from the past and hypothetical events in the future.

In the third example I gave, can you imagine yourself two years ago, armed with a pair of shoes, the latest smartwatch, and an evening schedule. Were those sufficient for you to have become regular?

Conversely, peer into the future. Do you reckon you will be a regular after a month? Are you committed enough to let a month pass and then check if that was true?

Mental time travel, or MTT, is one of the true “superpowers” that human beings possess.

“You have a remarkable talent – the ability to step outside the present, and imagine the past and future in your mind’s eye. Known as ‘mental time-travel’, some psychologists propose it’s a trait that allowed our species to thrive.

If I ask you to imagine what you did yesterday, or what you’re planning for tomorrow, you can conjure up rich scenes in the theatre of your mind. Not only that, you can turn back the clock to picture past eras – Shakespeare’s London, ancient Greece, the dinosaurs, the Big Bang – before spinning the dial to imagine deep futures, from our grandchildren’s lives in the next century all the way to the day the Sun becomes a red giant, billions of years from now.

Yet many people don’t make as much of this talent as they could. In the accelerating, information-rich, target-driven culture of the early 21st century, the present often dominates thoughts and priorities instead.”

It is also why some of the most frequent questions my colleagues get from me are, “Why will this not fail the next time too?”. And, “If we had done what you say, would we have succeeded the last time?”

“Our ability to imagine the future is essential to our humanity”, writes Maria Popova.

“[The brain is] a fundamentally prospective organ that is designed to use information from the past and the present to generate predictions about the future. Memory can be thought of as a tool used by the prospective brain to generate simulations of possible future events… We tend to think of memory as having primarily to do with the past… And maybe one reason we have it is so that we can have a warm feeling when we reminisce, and so on. But I think the thing that has been neglected is its role in allowing us to predict and simulate the future.

In order to mentally time-travel into the future, the brain has to accomplish a couple of things at once — we activate our “semantic memory,” which encompasses our basic knowledge of facts about the world and thus helps paint a backdrop for the imagined scene, and we call on our episodic memory, which pulls on our autobiographical library of remembered experiences to fill in specific details for this general scene. Curiously, episodic memory tends to be rather flawed but, according to two scientists Falk quotes, that’s okay since its core purpose is to provide “a more general toolbox that allowed us to escape from the present and develop foresight, and perhaps create a sense of personal identity.”

I will add that it is also essential to the growth of our selves.

3. Friction follow-ups

Community responses to last week’s Friction

“Just like any Indian mom and daughter duo, mummy and I have major differences in life ideologies driven by societal and generational norms. But it’s important to me, to ensure she’s onboard with each of my life decisions even if it means getting through a lot of friction, like who to marry, whether to quit ‘her dream job for me’ at Google, solo trips as a woman and the list goes on. As we work through our friction each time despite it feeling world-ending at the time, I get so much clarity and conviction to double down on my decision or see sides of it I didn’t think about, eventually walking away from it (usually my ex-es who she made me see the problematic sides of, while I was madly in love 😀). In our family, my infantry officer dad has always said ‘conflict is good’ and we embrace it wholeheartedly. I recorded an episode (and an entire season) on my podcast about mother daughter conflicts in Indian society, if you are keen.”

—Nalini Singh, Global head of BD and partnerships, Workvivo by zoom, Dublin, Ireland

“I’ve had at least a dozen heated debates—sometimes more—over the years with colleagues who: skipped or rushed through design thinking, leading to clunky user experiences. Ignored market research, preferring gut instinct or copying competitors. Treated marketing as a sales support function, not as a driver of long-term brand value. Each conflict felt draining in the moment. But here’s where friction became fuel: After one particularly tough argument about skipping user research, I insisted on running a quick guerrilla usability test. The results were so clear that even the skeptics became advocates for design sprints. The friction forced the team to confront uncomfortable truths. In another case, a product manager insisted that “marketing equals lead gen”. I pushed back, using data to show how brand perception was eroding and leads were getting harder to convert. The debate led us to pilot a content strategy that eventually doubled inbound leads. When a senior stakeholder wanted to launch without competitive analysis, I refused to sign off. The ensuing tension led to a workshop where we mapped gaps and opportunities. We delayed the launch, but the product landed stronger and with a clearer story.”

—Sowmya Moni, Strategic Advisor, Social Impact Investor, Bengaluru

“When one wants to go on a diet, you want unhealthy food to be made difficult to access (before being removed completely)—so keep it on the upper shelf, behind big boxes, so it’s difficult to reach and not very obvious to notice. When I wanted to cut down on my social media usage, it was easy to delete the Facebook app, as it was pure leisure. But with Linkedin, I had to be a bit more kind, as I needed to use it for work also. So I did not delete the app, but moved it from the home screen, introducing a small amount of friction every time I want to use it.”

—Hetal Sonpal, Independent Consultant, Coimbatore

“It happened to me 20 years ago when I switched jobs from academics to business consulting. The writing methodology for preparing reports was significantly different. Initially, every page of my reports had red marks after evaluation by my manager. I overcame it by attending internal training programs and reading the Pyramid Principle book. Though it took quite some time. But the friction helped me to become a better writer eventually.”

—Anindya Roy, Director, Marketing and Sales, Gurugram

4. Breaking through panels

Hello, Ishan, it’s Hari here back after a week of sickness. I hope everyone’s having a good laidback Sunday.

I don’t know how many of you remember me talking about moving house a couple of weeks ago, but well, I finally managed to unpack my carton of books late this Friday night. As I was putting them up in the makeshift study room that came with my new place, I noticed three books in my possession I hadn’t picked up in a while. 

Art Spiegel’s deeply personal memoir Maus, and a copy each of Sarnath Banerjee’s All Quiet in Vikaspuri (which we’ve featured before) and The Barn Owl’s Wondrous Capers. If you didn’t already know, all of these are graphic novels.

View in Browser

I don’t read many graphic novels, to be honest. I grew up reading Chacha Chaudhary in my early years, and Balarama, the Malayalam children’s magazine published by Malayala Manorama, once I learned to read well in Malayalam. Marvel and DC Comics weren’t really part of my formative years. 

But that doesn’t mean I dislike the format. As the three books on my shelf show, I do enjoy the occasional graphic novel. I’m also a firm believer in the fact that they are best enjoyed as physical books. I don’t think Kindles, if they ever come back in stock, make the cut. There have been entire days I’ve spent with copies of cartoonist and journalist Joe Sacco’s graphic novels during my visits to bookstores. His Journalism is a particularly striking example of the art.

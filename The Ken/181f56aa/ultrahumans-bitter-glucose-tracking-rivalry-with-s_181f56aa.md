Ultrahuman is not the first startup to be inspired by Apple’s iconic product launch, but it arguably has some arguments to stake that claim. It starts with its device.
Ultrahuman’s flagship product is a continuous glucose monitoring (CGM) sensor (typically used in diabetes) pierced onto the user’s arm, to show the real-time movement of a person’s sugar levels. It’s also expensive. A year-long subscription costs upward of Rs 1 lakh (US$1,250).
And just like Apple, it appears to have a strong brand, particularly among prominent influencers in the technology, fitness, and startup community in India, who simply can’t stop talking about how they’ve ‘bio-hacked their body’.
That’s where the parallels end.
Because Ultrahuman’s sources of inspiration extend beyond just the iPhone’s product launch. Its latest product, a finger ring that claims to give deep insights into your sleep patterns and stress is modelled closely on the already available Oura or the Whoop ring, both of which have been in the US for a while.
Even in India, Ultrahuman isn’t fundamentally unique. A host of older Indian companies such as fitness and healthtech platform HealthifyMe, and sugar.fit—a spin-off of rival fitness and healthtech platform cult.fit—have all launched competing products. And unlike Ultrahuman, they seem to be in a better position to connect it to their existing ecosystems.
All of this leads us to two key questions:
The first one is related to medical health—does simply calculating the presence of blood sugars even provide precise insights into one’s metabolic health?
The second is about financial health—how is Ultrahuman doing as a business? After raising millions in venture capital, how many users does it have? How much is it paying all those influencers? How are its competitors doing?
To answer both of these questions, our ace healthcare reporter Maitri tag-teams with resident financial data wiz Gaurav Tyagi, to give us a gem of a story about the hottest new startup that’s making waves in India’s wearable device market.

Hyundai India’s #1 IPO tag may not save its #2 carmaker status. Mahindra & Mahindra, in fourth place earlier, has moved to #3 now and could soon zoom past Hyundai. That has implications for the IPO as well

India’s new IPO king is on its way.

Hyundai Motor India is now cruising with the country’s largest public issue—a neat Rs 27,870 crore (US$3.3 billion) that will entirely go to its Korean parent, Hyundai Motor Company.

Anchor investors lapped up nearly a third of the mega issue on Monday, and public investors scooped up about 18% of their portion on Tuesday. With two more days to go, the issue should likely sail through.

Many investors are betting on “the second largest auto original-equipment manufacturer in the Indian passenger-vehicles market,” as Hyundai has positioned itself. It’s a rank Hyundai has held for over 15 years, albeit a distant second to market leader Maruti Suzuki. That’s not a mean feat in a car-eat-car market where many of Hyundai’s foreign peers like Ford and General Motors have called it quits.

Just that Hyundai’s #2 sticker could soon be a thing of the past—even within the next fortnight.

Something’s flipped. It’s not showing in its IPO documents and press releases, but it could have a bearing on IPO subscriptions and on how the stock lists next week.

While the sales gap between Hyundai and its nearest competitor was over 6,500 cars in June, it’s shrunk to just 39 cars in the three months since. The automaker snapping at Hyundai’s heels—Mahindra and Mahindra (M&M)—moved up a position to #3 in September by bucking the slowdown in the sector and rolling out a few new launches. Soon, it could beat Hyundai for the #2 spot.

And M&M’s growth isn’t on a small base. The top four players in the Indian auto space (Maruti Suzuki, Hyundai, M&M, and Tata Motors) account for nearly four out of every five cars plying on the roads. That makes M&M’s performance even more impressive, especially with the other three companies slowing down in each of the three months ending September.

What gives?

View in Browser

M&M has been able to make the most of the structural shift in India’s passenger-car market towards premiumisation and sport utility vehicles (SUVs), which now account for over half the cars sold in the country. It is the leader in this space, with its SUVs such as XUV700, Thar, Scorpio, and XUV3XO among the most popular. The recently launched Thar Roxx is also likely to add to M&M’s momentum.

That said, Hyundai India is no pushover, and its bestseller, Creta SUV, continues to see growth in terms of volumes. But that doesn’t seem enough to keep M&M at bay—at least, not yet.

For its part, the company’s management is putting on a brave face. A senior company executive said Hyundai is “confident of maintaining its second position.” The company has always been ahead of the curve in technology and design, and has the strong support of its parent, they told The Ken at the company’s IPO press conference last week. The coming months will show if Hyundai is able to walk the talk.

Meanwhile, the grey-market premium (GMP)—the extra price speculators are willing to pay above the IPO price in unofficial markets—is reported to have crashed sharply over the past few days for the Hyundai IPO. The drop is said to be over 90%, from Rs 370 on 4 October—when companies announced their September sales numbers—to just Rs 30 above the upper end of the Rs 1,865–1,960 per share IPO price band.

This just might dampen the participation of those looking to flip their IPO shares soon after listing for some quick gains. A recent Sebi study on 144 mainboard IPOs between April 2021 and December 2023 showed that more than half of the IPO shares allotted to investors were sold within a week of their listing.

Market observers cite increased competition eroding Hyundai’s market share as one of the reasons for the fall in GMP—a reference, seemingly, to M&M inching up on Hyundai.

In fact, at 14%, M&M’s market share in September 2024 was nearly triple its market share in the year ended March 2021. Hyundai India’s share shrank to 14% from over 17% in the same period.

Besides, as The Ken wrote in June, there’s more than meets the eye to what the Hyundai India IPO is paying the automaker’s Korean parent, thanks to a hefty dividend payout before the public issue and an increase in royalties.

On 10 June, just about a week before the draft prospectus filing, Hyundai India entered into a new royalty agreement with its Korean parent for the right to manufacture and sell passenger vehicles and parts.

Previously, the Indian arm had separate technology and royalty agreements with the parent for each passenger vehicle model it manufactured and sold. Now, it is required to pay the parent company 3.5% of its sales revenue.

[…]

Ramping up the royalty rate before the IPO launch could be a preemptive move to sidestep potential resistance from public shareholders once the company goes public. 

Hyundai India’s IPO pays its Korean parent way more than meets the eye, The Ken

Factoring all this in, Hyundai India’s IPO valuation doesn’t seem as cheap as it initially did. It’s above the 26X trailing price-to-earnings (PE) that the Maruti Suzuki stock trades at, and approaching the 35X trailing PE of M&M.

But then, M&M’s volumes are growing while Hyundai’s and Maruti Suzuki’s aren’t. And growth commands a premium on the bourses.

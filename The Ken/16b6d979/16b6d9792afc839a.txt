India produces more biomedical waste than it can manage. This ranges from hospital waste like used needles and syringes to everyday products like sanitary napkins, diapers and earbuds. Each year, about 15,000 tonnes of this goes untreated. It's either mixed in with regular waste, buried, or just dumped.

To put that number in perspective for you, one could fill almost thirty Olympic-size swimming pools with that amount of waste. That's thirty gigantic swimming pools of potentially infectious waste. A danger to both people and the environment.

And it gets worse. Because it always does. By 2022, India's biomedical waste output is set to go up 40% even as the number of treatment plants has remained largely stagnant in the last decade. As India sits on this ticking biohazardous bomb, today's story by Suraksha asks a pertinent question—why haven't the number of treatment plants increased in lockstep with the country's requirements?

The answer is an interesting mix of bureaucracy, business imperatives and good ol' fashioned rule-breaking.

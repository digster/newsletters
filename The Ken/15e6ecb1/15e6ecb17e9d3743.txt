Good Morning Ishan,

Imagine a virus that can stay dormant in a human body for years, even decades. Only to surface one day and start attacking the liver and lead to cirrhosis or liver cancer. A virus spread primarily through unsafe medical procedures or needle use, which means people who underwent surgeries between the 1980s and 2000s might only now showing full-blown symptoms. 

That virus is Hepatitis C. 

Imagine a drug that could ‘cure’ people of this deadly viral infection. A drug so valuable that Gilead Sciences, the US pharma major valued at over $100 billion, would pay $11 billion to acquire the company that made it. And price each resulting pill at $1000 a pop. And a full course of treatment at $90,000.

That drug was sofosbuvir.

Imagine a country that’s the world’s cradle for producing low-cost and affordable medicines. But also a country that doesn’t know how many of its people have Hepatitis C. “Guesstimates” place it at 8-10 million. How many more are contracting it each year? No one knows for sure.

That country is India.

Now imagine a complicated game theory example involving a $1000 pills, powerful US corporations, canny Indian pharma companies, compulsory licensing, medical tourism and a government that is still figuring out a gameplay to eradicate a deadly virus.

When Amazon-JP Morgan-Berkshire Hathaway joint venture Haven said last week that it will shut down next month, the news disappointed healthcare professionals around the world. An ambitious project to disrupt healthcare had failed.

But the share prices of pharmacy chains were up. After all, which business wants to be Amazon-ed?

In India, though, pharmacy chains and retailers canвҖҷt escape being bulldozed by Amazon, Reliance, and now the Tatas. The Ken has learnt that in the next few weeks, the Tata Group will close its $200 million investment in 1mg, one of the earliest e-pharmacies in India. Amazon, despite its own e-pharmacy launch last year, is reportedly investing $100 million in IndiaвҖҷs largest chain Apollo Pharmacy. Reliance scooped up Netmeds last year.

The battle lines for online pharmacy are clearly drawn with a million or so offline pharmacists and distributors on the other side trying everything under the sun to stay relevant. For now, the balance is in their favour because online pharmacies account for only 3% of the $16 billion domestic market.

For her story today, Maitri went on an offline pharmacy trail to figure out their survival plans. They are consolidating, reducing the bounce rate, improving efficiencies, adopting tech, loyalty cardsвҖ”even the discounting lingo is new. Their tech partners are redoing the math for them. вҖңEach family, on average, spends between Rs 3,000 to Rs 5,000 a month on drugs and health supplements. To run a successful business, each retailer will need to acquire 300 to 500 such unique customers. If there are six retailers in a neighbourhood, they need 1,800 to 3,000 such families in all for each oneвҖҷs business to run comfortably.вҖқ

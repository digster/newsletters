Long and Short
Wed, 03 Sep 25
The story behind the numbers and motivations driving India's stock market.
Good Morning Ishan,
“I can’t recall the last time this happened,” said an IPO lawyer to The Ken.
He was talking about fintech Mobikwik taking its book running lead manager (BRLM), Dam Capital, to court for not releasing payments to all the professionals who worked on its initial public offering (IPO) even eight months after the company [went](https://www.ndtvprofit.com/ipos/missing-invoices-and-threats-from-ipo-vendors-why-mobikwik-dragged-dam-capital-to-court-profit-exclusive) public.
In December, when Mobikwik raised Rs 572 crore from its IPO, it had set aside nearly Rs 42 crore as payment to the various vendors involved in the process. However, Dam Capital, which oversaw how the IPO proceeds were spent, did not release the payments.
So after eight months, and paying Rs 13 crore out of its own pocket, the fintech firm has gone to court to resolve this.
Around a dozen different types of professionals work on an IPO—from merchant bankers, lawyers, and auditors to advertising executives, prospectus printers, and registrars. And all of their payment is drawn from the IPO proceeds. How much this “IPO baraat (procession),” as the lawyer called it, receives is all laid down to the last detail in the IPO offer document.
All of these proceeds are parked in a public issue escrow account, and the payments are managed by the lead bankers—in this case, Dam Capital and SBI Capital Markets.
When the company raises money, the proceeds are first parked in the public offer account, and then later moved into the issuer’s account. That happens only after a nod from the BRLMs, after they get an instruction from the company. They act as gatekeepers of the fund, explained one lawyer. “This is also a tool in the hands of BRLM to make sure they also get paid.”
And Dam Capital is making full use of the power it holds.
One of the core duties of the banker is to bring interested investors who then subscribe to the IPO. When a company goes public, it earmarks the total fee it wants to pay the bankers. How much each bank gets, however, is determined by their performance.
As a result, most of what bankers earn from IPOs is variable. If the bankers are able to convert the investors they said they would bring, they get the fee. Most IPOs do end up with companies and bankers haggling over their fees, but it is an issue that is easily resolved because investors themselves disclose who was responsible for securing their investment.
In the case of Mobikwik, the bankers put in the work and helped pull off a miracle. The company initially postponed its Rs 1,900 crore IPO in 2021, and then cut its IPO size [twice](https://www.business-standard.com/companies/news/mobikwik-ipo-price-range-set-at-rs-265-279-for-december-11-launch-124120601232_1.html) after that. The result: when it finally did launch the offer, it was oversubscribed 120X.
Now, Mobikwik and Dam Capital can’t seem to agree on who gets credit for some of the investors who invested in the IPO, and as a result, who will get the Rs 1.5 crore variable fee. That’s the root of the dispute here.
But by holding the Rs entire Rs 42 crore that Mobikwik owed as expenses to various vendors, Dam Capital has shown who has the leverage.
The company argued that such a small disagreement should not stop the full release. It asked the Court for urgent directions so that pending vendor payments could be settled. DAM Capital countered this claim and said the disputed amount could be higher than Mobikwik’s estimate.
On August 14, Justice Bansal issued notice and noted the submissions. Mobikwik pressed for immediate release while DAM Capital sought more time. A week later both parties informed the Court that they worked out a temporary arrangement. DAM Capital agreed to release the escrowed funds within 24 hours if Mobikwik supplied the necessary details. It was also decided that undisputed dues would be paid from this pool while Rs 1.49 crore would remain in escrow until the dispute is settled.
Delhi High Court Refers Mobikwik And DAM Capital IPO Dispute To Mediation, [BW Legal](https://www.bwlegalworld.com/article/delhi-high-court-refers-mobikwik-and-dam-capital-ipo-dispute-to-mediation-568951)
At the most recent hearing of this matter on 21 August, the court said the parties should resolve the outstanding issues through a court-appointed mediation.
All this is also playing out at a time when companies feel like the balance of power is shifting more in favor of IPO service providers than the companies, thanks to heightened IPO activity. 2024 was a [record](https://www.goldmansachs.com/insights/articles/will-indias-ipo-volumes-set-another-record-in-2025) year with 268 companies going[ public](https://www.moneycontrol.com/news/business/ipo/nse-leads-asia-in-ipo-count-in-2024-sets-global-record-for-equity-capital-raised-in-primary-market-12902822.html) and another 108 listing in the first half of 2025.
That means, according to a lawyer who works at a listed company, “the pool of bankers, lawyers, and other service providers is small, and their bands and rates are set. So companies don’t have so much bargaining power in this kind of a cycle.”
As it is, bankers’ fees have been on the rise. As Dam Capital itself wrote in its note when it was going public: “Since FY20, the BRLM fee percentage has seen a marked increase, particularly in the mid-sized issue bracket of Rs 500 crore to Rs 1,000 crore, where the average fee percentage has nearly doubled from 1.6% in FY20 to 3.1% across most issue size brackets.”
Now, Rs 1.5 crore may not seem like much in the larger scheme of things. But for bankers like Dam Capital, it will make a world of difference.
Firstly, their business is cyclical, so you have to make hay when the IPO sun shines. But in the first quarter of FY26, Dam Capital’s revenue fell 33% to Rs 31 crore from a year ago. It saw a steep 58% fall, especially in its merchant banking revenue, to Rs 9 crore due to limited market activity that quarter stemming from geopolitical uncertainties.
Like Dam Capital, Mobikwik, too, is under immense pressure.
The Gurugram-based fintech also had a terrible first quarter. Its net loss ballooned over 6X from a year ago to Rs 42 crore (incidentally, the same value as its IPO expense) even as revenue fell 20% to about Rs 270 crore.
In a way, the reason this Rs 1.5 crore matters so much is because both companies are in the same boat. Both listed in December 2024, and both their stocks are down over 40% since they listed.
According to the IPO lawyer, this incident has led lawyers like him to tighten deals they draft between bankers and clients more than ever before, leaving nothing to chance. As it is, lawyers and bankers believe that companies will walk away from their promises if they could.
This incident has just dialled up this distrust between bankers and clients by a couple of notches.

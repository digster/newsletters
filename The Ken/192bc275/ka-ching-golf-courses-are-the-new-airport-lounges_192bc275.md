Ka-Ching!
Thu, 24 Oct 24
A weekly newsletter about how finance is getting supercharged by tech in India, and how you can make money work for you..
Good Morning Ishan,
I received my shiny new Swiggy HDFC Bank credit card in the post on the day I sat down to write this newsletter.
You might think I applied for it because of all the rewards on food-delivery and dine-out spending, and you’d be right. But it also offers another perk that piqued my interest.
Free golf lessons.
Humour me for a minute here. Close your eyes. Imagine the kind of person who’s a frequent user of a food-delivery service. Then, picture a golf aficionado.
In the first case, you probably conjured up a young professional, living alone in a metro city, and maybe not too much of a cook; in the second instance, perhaps someone more senior with a settled career, a business leader with the salary to afford the finer things in life.
To be clear, I’ve never really been enticed by golf. But it struck me as interesting that a card emblazoned with branding designed to appeal to the former archetype is trying to sell something so clearly suited to the lifestyle of the latter.
So I made calls to a couple of people in the fintech and banking space.
Apparently, my surprise had been misplaced, because they told me golf rewards are now becoming increasingly common—especially for more high-end cards like the IndusInd Pinnacle, Axis Reserve, and HDFC Diners Club Black.
This was Ashish Lath, founder and CEO of Savesage Club, a platform for tracking and optimising your credit-card spending for the maximum rewards:
The “lounge-access playbook”.
If you’ve followed The Ken’s coverage of financial services, you’ll know exactly what he means:
From being an exclusive service offered only to premium credit-card holders, free access to airport lounges became the top selling point for every product in a bank’s credit-card pack; it was seen as an aspirational benefit to most.
When credit cards made airport lounges too much of a good thing, [The Ken](https://the-ken.com/story/when-credit-cards-made-airport-lounges-too-much-of-a-good-thing/)
And just like airport lounges once upon a time, golf courses are now being seen as an exclusive club for high rollers. A place where you can hobnob with founders and CXOs of unicorn startups, or distinguished bureaucrats. Essentially, a convenient gathering of potential friends in high places.
But let’s spend a minute thinking about what happened when banks went all-in on airport lounges. Before very long, the number of visitors had ballooned massively, which just ended up diluting the prestige of the airport lounge.
After all, as 20-century comedian Groucho Marx would say: “I refuse to join any club that would have me as a member.”
Plus, banks—who were footing the cost for this lounge access—felt their bottom lines starting to hurt. So they started imposing all kinds of terms and conditions, like introducing spending milestones to unlock free lounge access or reducing the number of free entries.
And after lounges, the same thing happened with airport spa visits too, Lath added.
Golf benefits depend on a similar model; issuers are betting that not too many people will actually use the perk. But banks have also wisened up, which is why this story is playing out slightly differently.
“Nowadays, even an entry-level card is giving you one golf round in a month,” said Kashif Ansari, an assistant professor of finance at O P Jindal Global University. “But 95% of people are not going to use it. That makes it an aspirational new feature at nearly zero cost.” (If Ansari’s name sounds familiar, you may recall [reading](https://the-ken.com/kaching/i-just-got-my-30th-credit-card-and-im-planning-to-get-more/) about his whopping collection of 30-plus credit cards in an earlier Ka-Ching! edition.)
Ansari has played golf a few times, but surprisingly, never by utilising any credit-card perks. And he shared a few insights on why it may not be so easy for cardholders to just get out there and play golf:
Not to mention, that “free golf lesson” I mentioned in the beginning of this edition isn’t really free. All it means is that you don’t have to pay for access to the golf course—you’ll still need to rent or own a golf club and pay for the “caddy” who assists you with your technique, your equipment, and navigating the course.
There’s another catch—limited slots. Take Delhi, for example. There are maybe six or seven golf courses in all of NCR, estimates Ansari. And if a golf course has a certain number of slots available in a day, the card issuer would typically have booked only a small number of them. And this varies from course to course and day to day—a further measure to cap their losses, Lath pointed out. This is also why one has to first contact the concierge if they want to use any golf perks.
At the end of the day, card issuers are always walking a tightrope between offering perks that are attractive enough to entice customers, and controlling their expenses by introducing friction. And the way these new golf perks have been designed, they appear to have taken very deliberate cues from the airport lounge saga.
But could issuers have erred by swinging too far the other way?
The utilisation of golf rewards has gone up recently, but that’s largely driven by those using the most elite cards like HDFC Infinia Metal or SBI Aurum, say Lath and Ansari both. These are cards that offer a higher, or even unlimited, number of free rounds and waived fees. But the bank still ends up on top here because people holding these cards are generally heavy spenders. For instance, you need a salary of Rs 24 lakh (US$28,500) a year to be eligible for Axis Reserve, and that goes up to Rs 50 lakh a year for HDFC Infinia.
As for people like me, who might find the idea of golf rewards appealing but rarely (if ever) avail them, we’re getting a swanky feature we’ll probably never use (and some bragging rights that we probably will use), at no cost to the bank. Which, honestly, doesn’t sound that bad.
But maybe I should give golf a chance? If you’ve tried the golf perks on your credit card, write to kaching@the-ken.com and tell me about your experience. Who knows? I might just change my mind.
For now, take care. We’ll see you again next Thursday morning!

Good Morning Ishan,
 
Chances are you haven’t a clue about who or what Skult, Nush and Rheson are. (No, they’re not grunge bands.)
 
But perhaps you may have vaguely heard of Just F, Prowl, YWC, One8, Seven or True Blue. (No, they’re not the must-have iPhone-killer Android smartphone models for September.)
 
Which brings me to Wrogn, Being Human and HRX. Unless you live outside India, you might have recognised at least 2, possibly even all three of them.
 
Each of the names I listed is a “celeb brand” floated by Bollywood actors or cricketers in the hope to literally cash in on their brand. The big daddy of the lot is Bollywood actor Salman Khan’s Being Human, but the bright star is clearly HRX, the brand extension of Hrithik Roshan, also a Bollywood star.
 
Through a mix of hard work, canny strategy and sheer luck, HRX rode a series of waves to success - being one of first movers among celeb brands; the rapid rise of “athleisure” as an apparel category; and an early and exclusive partnership with an e-commerce category leader (Myntra). 
 
So rapid was HRX’s rise that Myntra, its e-commerce partner, acquired a majority stake of 51% in it in 2016. It claims to have done close to $20 million in sales for the year that ended this March, and claims to be on track to double that by next year.
 
Naturally, Skult, Nush, Rheson, Just F, Prowl, YWC, One8, Seven, True Blue and many others think they too can replicate HRX’s success. 
 
Easier said than done, say Harveen and Gayathri in their story today. In many ways, the rise of celeb brands was inevitable once social media and e-commerce platforms allowed them to first reach millions of fans directly, and then sell to them. The Kardashians, Rihanna, Gwyneth Paltrow and Jessica Alba have all built brands worth hundreds of millions or billions in value.
 
But that doesn’t mean its happening yet in India. Read our story today to understand why: https://the-ken.com/story/hrx-celebrity-brand-phenomenon/

Free code can build India’s AI fortune. The narrative that India must build the next GPT‑5 or Gemini‑X to compete in AI misses the real lever

A “research-only” licence https://huggingface.co/sarvamai/sarvam-0.5/blob/main/LICENSE.md—like the one generative-AI company Sarvam used to have until last month—“can’t be classified as genuinely open-source”, said Sai Rahul Poruri in a recent conversation with The Ken.

Rahul isn’t just a critic or advocate on Mastodon, he’s CEO of FOSS United, a nonprofit that’s basically the organised religion of free and open-source software (FOSS, duh!) in India. Its founders include Zerodha* CTO Kailash Nadh and Rushabh Mehta, the founder of ERPNext, a free, open-source resource planning platform for enterprises. 

Rahul’s statement might sound like a quibble about software licensing. But in India’s fast-heating up AI moment, it was a shot fired. Software is only truly open source if it’s free to access, modify, and redistribute—per the criteria set by the US-based Open Source Initiative (OSI). Anything else is mostly off-limits.

This distinction matters because Sarvam wasn’t genuinely open earlier. Until mid-July, the company operated under a restrictive licence. In AI-speak, that means not releasing the weights—the learned parameters that make the model “smart”. Without them, all users have is a locked box: something you can look at, maybe poke, but not actually tinker with or improve.

But just as Rahul was making his point, the Indian government made its biggest open-source AI bet yet. In July, under its Rs 10,000 crore IndiaAI Mission, it handed over Rs 98 crore worth of compute subsidies to… Sarvam. 

More importantly, it said that the company would use a “permissive” open-source licence.

If you’re keeping score, Sarvam is one of eight startups selected to access India’s new compute cluster, featuring more than 10,000 high-end GPUs like the Nvidia H100 and H200. From a licensing perspective, the new promise actually puts it ahead of Meta’s Llama, which gave away model weights but kept a commercial-use lock on them.

As long as valid copyleft (which allows reuse as long as modifications are also open) or permissive (which allows reuse with fewer conditions) licenses are used, open-sourcing state-funded foundational AI models aligns with the definition of FOSS.

Sai Rahul Poruri, CEO, FOSS United

Of course, there’s a homegrown economic case for FOSS here. Kerala’s IT@School project (now KITE) saved an estimated Rs 3,000 crore by ditching proprietary software for FOSS. 

And globally, the race is heating up. 

In China, Alibaba Group recently released its Qwen models into the wild. Earlier, Deepseek released R1, a powerful open-weight model trained for just $6 million. Even startups like Moonshot are sprinting ahead, setting alarm bells ringing in Silicon Valley. In response, OpenAI—once known for keeping its models… well, not open—recently released https://www.nytimes.com/2025/08/05/technology/openai-artificial-intelligence-chatgpt.html GPT‑OSS, a pair of LLMs trained entirely on public data. Though this licence, too, restricts commercial use.

But free code, it turns out, isn’t just a nice academic gesture, or only about economics. It’s a geopolitical tool.

China understands this. Models like Deepseek and Qwen are instruments of soft power. These models, open in weights and widely adoptable, win global developer mindshare, define the AI stack, and make it harder for closed systems to charge rent. 

Which is why the choice of licence in India is not some obscure legalism. In the nation’s increasingly https://timesofindia.indiatimes.com/city/bengaluru/under-fire-sarvam-ai-co-founder-says-worries-about-indic-genai-model-premature/articleshow/121554967.cms politicised https://analyticsindiamag.com/ai-news-updates/open-source-ai-models-may-cease-to-exist-it-minister/ AI discourse https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/tech/artificial-intelligence/re-skilling-techies-compute-capacity-geopolitics-in-focus-for-second-indiaai-report/articleshow/123101231.cms, it’s the difference between models you can build on, fine-tune, commercialise, and reuse—and models that are walled gardens, with maybe a nice public park out front.

Open in name

One would think that a Rs 10,000 crore mission to build foundational AI models would have something—anything—to say about licensing. But the IndiaAI Mission, despite its ambition, compute firepower, and funding, is remarkably quiet on the matter. 

The original press release? No mention. The official IndiaAI homepage? Nothing. Even the detailed “Foundation Model Development Guidelines” issued in June discuss compute allocation, access rules, and evaluation benchmarks, but somehow skip past how the resulting models are to be licensed—or governed.

Rahul believes this ambiguity hurts both innovation and transparency. “Even if the intellectual property rights (IPR) don’t belong to the government, we believe that it is still important to adopt permissive or copyleft licenses,” he said. “Public money, public code.”

In fact, India already has a policy precedent for this. The 2015 Policy on Collaborative Application Development (from the electronics and information technology ministry) requires government-owned software to be open-sourced. But there’s a caveat: it only applies when the IPR resides with the government. If a third-party vendor or grant-funded startup retains the IPR—as Sarvam does—there’s no clear licensing mandate. That legal vacuum is increasingly being filled by “research-only” licences, which allow viewing or academic evaluation, but no commercial application.

In the world of AI, where fine-tuning and transfer learning are core to value creation, such licences act as deadends.

Globally, governments are grappling with the same tensions. In the US, open-source AI has become a priority. The White House has publicly endorsed https://venturebeat.com/ai/why-open-source-ai-became-an-american-national-priority/ open-weight foundational models as a way to democratise innovation and to counter China’s accelerating AI capabilities. 

The Linux Foundation even has a little taxonomy—Class I is open science, Class II is open tooling, and Class III is the actual open model. Sarvam’s latest models would score decently well; they’re not Claude- or Gemini-level locked-up, at least. 

And developers really care about this. No wonder Deepseek’s R1 has quickly topped Hugging Face rankings and gained global developer adoption. Even Zerodha’s Nadh didn’t even start playing with generative AI until Deepseek came out, he said at a recent The Ken event https://the-ken.com/event/bfo/how-ai-is-breaking-and-remaking-the-way-products-are-built/.

OpenAI’s response to all of this, by releasing some fully open-weight models, shows a partial return to its founding ethos. For the US, it was a statement of intent.

India risks missing that moment if permissive licensing doesn’t become the default. Without formal licensing norms, many startups may opt for restrictive models, fragmenting the ecosystem. Opportunities to capture developer mindshare, shape standards, and scale fast could be lost. “Public money, public code” is becoming policy logic globally. If India continues toˀ treat open licensing as optional, it may find itself outpaced—not just by US labs, but by agile, community-led ecosystems like Hugging Face or Stability AI.

In practice, India is trying to walk the middle path—borrowing Meta’s playbook without its reach. Llama, for example, is open in name, but not under OSI standards. Sarvam’s original “research-only” license followed the same approach. But without Meta’s distribution or community scale, India’s models could end up in an awkward no man’s land. Neither commercially viable nor community-powered.

Of course, there’s a reason for all this hedging: startups need to make money. Even Sarvam, flush with compute credits, faces commercial pressure. True openness doesn’t always fit the Series A pitch deck.

But openness is shaping the market itself.

Look at inference costs (the price of running a model). In the past year, GPT-4’s API fees have fallen from $0.06 to $0.006 per 1,000 tokens—a 90% drop. The reason? Competition from open-source models like Mistral, Qwen, and Llama. They’ve set a low-price benchmark that closed models have to follow. Today, running a million sentences on an open-source RoBERTa model costs just $2.70. On GPT-4, that would be over $3,000. That’s 99.9% cheaper.

The problem is that open-source models don’t monetise easily unless bundled with services, hosting, or enterprise tooling. As the Linux Foundation notes, Class II and III openness may boost academic citations or public goodwill, but they do little to stimulate real community development. But by just importing the licensing models of large, well-capitalised Western labs, India risks missing the bus on building surrounding ecosystems that makes such choices viable. 

At the other end of the spectrum is China, whose model is radically different. There, sovereign AI is vertically integrated—state-funded labs, proprietary stacks, and controlled access. 

India wants sovereignty, but with openness. The risk, of course, is that it ends up with neither: insufficient community engagement for openness, and insufficient coordination for control.

India is throwing real money at the AI problem. The IndiaAI compute platform is arguably the most significant public investment in AI hardware outside of China and the US. Over 17,300 high-performance GPUs have been provisioned through eight cloud providers, including Nvidia’s top-tier H100s and H200s. Sarvam alone was granted 4,096 H100s. E2E Networks, another beneficiary, is deploying 2,048 H200s. They are enough to train models on the scale of Llama 2, or even 3.

But compute isn’t the bottleneck anymore. People are.

Rahul put it bluntly:

“The people in this community are usually supported by full-time jobs in the for-profit or not-for-profit ecosystems that incentivise them to create and/or maintain open-source AI. But the organisations that value such work are still the exception and not the norm.”

The 2025 FOSS Report https://www.nls.ac.in/wp-content/uploads/2025/07/FOSS_Report_July-2025.pdf—authored by NLSIU researchers with informal support from Venkatesh Hariharan, an open-source evangelist—paints a stark picture. 

While Indian developers are active consumers of open-source AI tools—using libraries like Hugging Face Transformers, Langchain, Pytorch, and others—they rarely contribute upstream. Only 15% of contributors to India-linked AI projects on Github are actually based in India. Few of India’s premier institutions—barring outliers like AI4Bharat (IIT-M) and IIIT-H—have active teams building or maintaining AI infrastructure. Public-sector institutions, which could lead here, are mostly absent.

You can’t fork what you can’t fund

It’s a structural issue, too. 

Maintainers often go unpaid. Reviewers burn out. Contributors stop contributing once the academic incentive dries up (or the fellowship ends). In many cases, the most important open-source projects have no long-term support, no dedicated bug-fix teams, and no institutional backers. 

“Maintainers broadly don’t receive the recognition that they deserve,” Rahul said. “They aren’t financially supported to perform continued maintenance, and their long-term career prospects are negatively impacted.”

So what would a truly open-source AI ecosystem in India look like?

Aaditeshwar Seth, a computer-science professor at IIT Delhi and co-founder of social-tech firm Gram Vaani, argues https://www.theindiaforum.in/forum/bringing-back-public-digital-public-goods for “bringing the ‘public’ back in digital public goods.” That means thinking of software not just as a delivery mechanism from the state, but as a participatory tool that communities help shape.

For instance, IIIT-Hyderabad could partner with local linguistic academies—like the Telugu Akademi—to build fine-tuned models for Indic languages. These could then be shared under open-source licences, allowing other state departments to replicate or customise them. 

The Linux Foundation’s standard provides a template: publish model weights, training code, preprocessing scripts, and evaluation benchmarks. Leave out sensitive data if needed, but make everything else replicable.

There are already precedents. The AI4Bharat team has released IndicTrans2, a multilingual model trained on over 230 million parallel sentences across 22 Indian languages. It powers projects like Bhashini, the Supreme Court’s SUVAS translation system, and agri-tech voice bots. More importantly, it’s been released under a permissive license. With backing from Nilekani Philanthropies and the National Translation Mission, IndicTrans2 shows what public-private open-source AI can look like when done right.

But scale needs culture. And here, too, India faces headwinds. Most CS and IT programmes don’t formally teach FOSS. Students learn how to use open-source libraries, but rarely how to contribute to them. FOSS United is trying to fix this by:

piloting credit-for-contribution programmes in colleges
recognising FOSS contributions as valid internships
hosting faculty workshops on integrating open-source tools in classrooms

But Rahul wants a deeper shift: “Embedding FOSS in education means changing the default software tools used across the education sector from closed-source to FOSS wherever possible.” That means replacing proprietary IDEs https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integrated_development_environment, simulators, and toolchains with open ones—and then teaching students how to look under the hood.

The economic case is strong globally. Studies show that open-source contributions add 2.2% to the European Union’s GDP annually. Even a 10% rise in FOSS activity can boost GDP growth by 0.4–0.6%. Github’s research shows that if deployed widely, Copilot-style tools could add $1.5 trillion to global GDP by 2030. For India, which is still figuring out how to digitise its long tail of MSMEs, that productivity boost could be transformative. 

But only if the stack is cheap, local, and open.

The National Payments Corporation of India (which operates UPI) uses open-core stacks to process more than 10 billion API calls per day. Discount broker Zerodha saves over “thousands of dollars” per month by replacing Mailchimp with Listmonk, an open-source mailing system it now maintains. 

What’s missing is financial support. 

There’s little earmarked for long-term maintenance of open-source projects. Zerodha’s $1 million-a-year FLOSS fund is a rare exception—one that works because the company has the internal technical capacity to evaluate and engage with FOSS projects. Rahul believes similar grant mechanisms could be set up nationally, but only if contributors—not just policy experts—are part of the decision-making body. “It is important to ensure that the decision-makers include representatives from the larger open-source AI community… not just lawmakers and industry bodies,” he said.

At the moment, even India’s central FOSS platform—Openforge—is outdated. That says something about how the government views FOSS: as a side quest.

That mindset needs to change. India already relies heavily on open-source AI infrastructure. But it has not yet institutionalised how to give back. Without policy clarity, licensing standards, contributor pipelines, and sustainable funding, India’s open-AI project risks becoming just a showcase, not a powerhouse. 

Open-source AI, after all, won’t succeed just because we train big models. It’ll succeed when those models become something communities can build with, build on, and build for.

If you’re a B2B AI startup in India, your biggest problem isn’t revenue, costs, profit, or even talent—it’s time-wasters. 

Like most things in life, this seems to be a uniquely Indian problem.

Here’s Vaibhav Domkundwar, CEO of Better Capital, a pre-seed VC fund explaining why some AI startups have adopted a “Skip India” movement.

View in Browser

This isn’t a one-off rant. Paras Chopra, founder of Lossfunk, an AI startup, replied to the original post with, “I have banned builders at Lossfunk to talking [sic] to Indian customers.” The thread quickly grew, as other founders added their thoughts and breakdowns. Most of it is predictable.

Suppose you are a new startup who makes a shiny new piece of software—say a tool that helps a company’s HR team do hiring more smartly. You then put up a website that describes in detail why your software is amazing. You add screenshots, videos, and explain your features in detail. Finally, at the bottom of the page, you put a button that says something like, “I’m interested in this”, which takes the user to a form that they fill out with some basic details. 

One day, you get an email informing you that someone has filled out the form. Naturally, you assume the next step is a brief call where you ask, “How many licenses?” and send the invoice—because clearly, they already know what they’re buying.

Oh my poor, sweet, innocent child.

What actually happens is that you find out during the call that: 

1. The lead you received is from an intern at the company, or worse, someone from middle management with an impressive sounding but ultimately useless designation, like a Director of Global Solutions and Strategic Initiatives or something like that.

2. This person then asks you to walk them through the product features. So, you spend half an hour reading out the same thing you had written on your website.

3. They look suitably impressed and then say something like, “We are really interested in your product. Can you do a demo for our SVP? We’ll schedule another call.” At this point, you’re thinking of closing the sale, so you agree.

4. You don’t hear back from them for a week. All your emails go unanswered.

5. Suddenly, the SVP shows up in your inbox a month later asking for a demo. You schedule another call with this person who obviously has no clue about the past conversation and now needs another walkthrough about your product all over again.

6. The SVP looks suitably impressed and asks for a PoC—a proof of concept. They say something like, “We want to be sure we’re picking the right product, so why don’t you take a month and show us how this software would work for our business processes?” They insist that this is a mandatory step to justify this business investment. They hint that they have a huge budget. They ask for a trial period to use the product after you’ve done this.

7. You think the SVP is an idiot, but you agree. Big budget means big bucks. This is a lot of work for you, but if this is what it takes to convince them, so be it. You fight with your boss.

8. You finish doing the PoC and send it over to them. In your email, you gently ask them for another meeting to close the deal.

9. This time, they ghost you for another two months.

10. One afternoon, someone else with an equally impressive sounding designation shows up in your inbox and says that they love your product and are on the verge of making a decision. They ask to use your product for another quarter to make sure that there’s “adoption” in the team. They insist this is the last step and they are evaluating two products simultaneously, and want to pick one.

11. You think, “Let me extend their trial one last time and get them hooked.” They’ve spent as much time as you going back and forth, so they seem serious about this.

12. They aren’t.

13. Two years later, you’ve quit B2B sales and moved on to other fulfilling things in life. And then, suddenly out of nowhere, you get a phone call from an unknown number. It’s a procurement guy from the same company. They want to buy one license for $10. They also want a 50% discount.

14. Before you can say anything, the procurement guy tells you he’s sent you a form for “vendor registration” which asks you to upload the last ten years of your company’s IT returns, seventeen GST certificates, the latest organisational chart, and Aadhaar cards of every employee in your company.

It’s always been somewhat of a mystery to me why selling software (AI or otherwise) to Indian companies is so cumbersome. One theory I have is that a lot of people exist in organisations with no real decision-making powers, but pretend that they do. In some ways, the bureaucracies at Indian private companies rival that of government organisations. When paired with a thin domestic market, this feature all but guarantees that Indian SaaS companies end up selling to the West.

The startups who sell software are right to be frustrated by this. 

However, they also believe that this is a problem only faced by them. 

This is where they are wrong.

As usual with AI disruption, it was creative workers who bore the brunt first.

The original victims of the tyranny of these endless pitches with little commitment were people who sold creative services—these are typically offered by agencies, freelancers, or studios, and span a wide range of industries from design to strategy to content. Think stuff like UX design, branding, social media marketing, video production, or content production and optimisation. They experience the same annoyances—make us one video and show us what you can do, or do this design for free first so we can evaluate you, or run this campaign for us as a PoC. Endless cycles of free work, with the bait of offering payments later. Creatives are constantly pitching.

However, creatives also discovered a solution to this. 

If you are in the business of making software for or selling it to Indian customers, you should pay attention. 

I’ll tell this using a personal story.

A couple of years ago, at The Ken, my colleague Ruhi launched a learning vertical. The offering was simple: it helped companies learn how to use narratives effectively in their own business context. Maybe you’re a tech startup crafting a story to pitch to VCs. Or you want your product managers to communicate better with stakeholders through compelling storytelling. Perhaps you’re preparing for a strategic launch or pivot and need to stand out in a crowded market—but are unsure how to shape the story that will make people care. That’s where we came in. Our pitch to customers was pretty much three things: We understood business. We understood how to create narratives. And we were actual practitioners—this is stuff we did everyday ourselves.

Some companies were convinced that we knew what we were talking about and decided to work with us. We got to work with founders, CEOs, and leaders at multiple firms—from startups to listed companies to tech giants. We ran several focus group discussions with their executives and taught business storytelling using frameworks and real-life examples. Some clients asked us to teach them things we hadn’t done before, so we had to learn them ourselves first. It was fun.

But here was the catch. We knew how to build a great curriculum, design impactful workshops, and train people effectively. What we didn’t know was how to sell creative services. So, for a while, we made the same mistakes many B2B startups make: chasing leads that went nowhere and wasting too much time on people who expected free work.

One day, someone passed along a book with a title that immediately caught our attention: Win Without Pitching by Blair Enns. It was a guide with a clear mission—how to sell a creative product. Notably, in the book, a pitch is defined as: “an attempt to sell or win approval for one’s ideas by giving them away for free, usually within a competitive, buyer-driven process.”

This is Enns’ main message.

“Today, we in the creative professions find ourselves standing at a crossroads. On the one side, the process of design is finally being seen as the last great differentiator of businesses and economies; while on the other, the outputs are increasingly seen as commodities.

Technology and oversupply are combining to rapidly widen the gulf between the commoditized tacticians who now bid their services against each other online, and the expert practitioners who command significant fees for leading their clients to novel solutions to meaningful business challenges. The middle is disappearing. The need to choose a path is being forced upon us. If we continue to choose not to choose, the decision will be made for us, and we will be pushed down the commodity road where we will reside with thousands of other order-taker suppliers who will never be free of the pitch”

Win Without Pitching by Blair Enns

You be the judge of whether this is true for software as well.

The manifesto is a collection of twelve proclamations. You could ask an AI to summarise it, but it won’t hit you the same way. Just read the book. It’s worth it. Some of the rules are quite provocative, like “We will specialise” or “We will diagnose before we prescribe” or “We will address issues of money early”. In essence, the book is a way for creative professionals to move away from offering a service to positioning themselves as experts—much like elite lawyers, doctors, or consultants. 

I’m not going to claim that one book was responsible for changing everything, but I will say that it helped us understand the problem better and prevented us from making mistakes. We became much more comfortable saying no to people who wanted us to do a diagnosis. We stopped making pitch decks and switched to conversations. It made us much better at filtering out people who we knew could never make up their mind. We became much more unapologetic about why we priced our product at twice or even thrice what others charged. And to this day, we’ve never done a proof of concept for anyone for free. 

As a result, we became selective… in a good way. The sales process got much more enjoyable. We worked with companies and leaders who knew what they wanted, and understood we would be committed once they demonstrated some commitment first. It was fun. 

I’m not sure if AI startups can adopt the same rules and even win without pitching, especially because some of the things that creative professionals understand about selling don’t apply to AI in its current state. Do companies buying AI even know what they want? And what exactly makes one startup selling AI software different from another? Unless a company has access to some really proprietary data or some specific technology, I don’t see how an AI startup can “specialise” in solving a problem, or can position itself as an expert. Can someone doing sales at an AI startup really replace pitches with “conversations”? 

Probably not. 

Maybe someday they will, but not today. 

And until that day comes, they’ll keep churning out free work for clients still figuring out what they even need.

Sure, AI startups might eventually figure out how to win without pitching, but they’ll still lack what creative shops take for granted: a sense of purpose bigger than the next deal. Blair Enns puts this into words in the book’s closing proclamation, “We Will Hold Our Heads High.”

The cause of our revolution is not to rid the world of free pitching; it is to build a business that allows us to rise to the highest heights possible and make an impact on the world that is larger than us.

We will master the twelve proclamations so that our enterprise may sustain us and nourish our creativity. We will do great work for those who respect us and pay us our worth. From our rewards we will use our gifts to lift our families, inspire our communities and influence all of humankind. Focus, selectivity, respectful selling, continuous learning and conquering money – these are but steps on our path.

These are words that AI cannot, and will never write.

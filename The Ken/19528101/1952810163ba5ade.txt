A well-crafted mission is the secret to alignment and trust. Great mission statements can help companies break into the league of heroes 
 By Ruhi Kandhari https://the-ken.com/writers/ruhikandharigmail-com/

Good Evening Ishan,

You are reading our subscriber-only newsletter Inciting Incident—a weekly commentary on brand narratives and storytelling 

Two short stories can tell a company’s whole story. The vision statement is the more creative endeavour, painting an image of the promised land. But it is the mission statement that articulates how things are going to get done in the real world—the connecting thread between what is and what will be. Mission statements are supposed to show that companies are willing, capable, and committed to achieving their visions. A big duty to place on a few sentences. 

Since I wrote about the three keys to a differentiated vision statement https://the-ken.com/incitingincident/three-keys-to-a-differentiated-vision/ last week, I thought it would be nice if we looked at how to build a great mission statement this week. 

Historically, ‘missions’ have been associated with heroic ventures that demand big sacrifices or are in the service of noble causes. Think of all the war movies where the protagonists go on a mission to save the day—for whoever that may be. Even humanitarian, charity, and diplomatic missions have a similar sense of urgency, with serious obstacles and high stakes involved. 

This is why even professionals are drawn to tours of duty- or mission-style career paths, as my colleague Rohin Dharmakumar wrote in the First Principles newsletter earlier this month https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/careers-as-tours-of-duty/, with several subscribers subsequently sharing their own thoughts https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/is-writing-thinking/ on the matter. 

By writing mission statements, companies can break into the same league, rubbing shoulders with ‘heroes’ trying to make the world a better place.

But most businesses don’t do a very good job of it.

The Harvard Business Review wrote https://hbr.org/2024/03/what-successful-purpose-statements-do-differently about these failures last year:

Using generative AI tools, our firm, Bain & Company, recently analyzed nearly 150,000 Glassdoor reviews for 81 of the largest U.S. retail and consumer products companies. We were looking for evidence that employees find their company’s purpose both clear and meaningful to their work. But we found little. Less than 3% of Glassdoor respondents even mentioned the company’s purpose as a factor in their reviews. Reviews that did touch on company purpose panned it nearly as often as they praised it.

The irony is that purpose statements really can inspire and motivate employees and other stakeholders, shaping their behavior and building teamwork — but only when companies take their purposes seriously.

But some companies do succeed in making their mission a core part of their identity, even after they hit the big leagues. And they use it to successfully form deeper bonds with their employees, customers, and investors. What is it that makes them different?

A tightrope walk

All good mission statements have to tread a fine line between audaciousness and believability. They need to offer a future that is entirely out of the ordinary, but also be very specific about how they are going to create it. Missions also need to speak to the needs of many rather than the few, and perhaps most importantly, offer the audience a role in the story. 

TED learned this lesson when it evolved its earlier mission—‘Spread Ideas’—into something that is still focussed on the big picture, but is more detailed in the impact it wants to create: “Discover and spread ideas that spark conversation, deepen understanding, and drive meaningful change.” What TED did was bring its audiences into its mission.

Most mission statements traverse this journey between a universal mission that affects everybody, and how this mission affects every single person who uses the product or service. 

Here are a few examples of mission statements that are universal, but do not involve the user/consumer/audience at all:

Tesla: “To accelerate the world’s transition to sustainable energy.”

Medtronic’s past mission statement https://www.inc.com/jim-schleckser/apple-s-boring-mission-statement-and-what-we-can-learn-from-it.html: “To extend human life.” 

Starbucks: “To be the premier purveyor of the finest coffee in the world, inspiring and nurturing the human spirit — one person, one cup and one neighborhood at a time.”

Greenpeace: “Our goal is to ensure the ability of the earth to nurture life in all its diversity. That means we want to: protect biodiversity in all its forms, prevent pollution and abuse of the earth’s ocean, land, air and fresh water, end all nuclear threats, promote peace, global disarmament and non-violence”

Now look at the mission statements that involve users and keep them at the centre, while building something that meets a universal need.

Linkedin: “Connect the world’s professionals to make them more productive and successful.”

Airbnb: “To create a world where anyone can belong anywhere and we are focused on creating an end-to-end travel platform that will handle every part of your trip.”

Nike:“To bring inspiration and innovation to every athlete in the world.”

Philips: “Improving people’s lives through meaningful innovation.”

Whole Foods Market: “Our deepest purpose as an organization is helping support the health, well-being, and healing of both people — customers, Team Members, and business organizations in general — and the planet.”

L’Oreal: “Offering all women and men worldwide the best of cosmetics innovation in terms of quality, efficacy and safety”

And the payoff from getting your mission statement right can be huge for companies—because that’s how you create strong buy-ins and build trust among your customers. Forbes argues precisely this point in this article https://www.forbes.com/councils/forbesbusinesscouncil/2022/08/26/3-steps-for-creating-company-buy-in-with-a-strong-mission/:

To create ultimate buy-in, you must be able to bring the mission down to the individual level with your team. As leaders of organizations, we must help our employees answer the question “How do I fit in that?” by communicating how their career goals and dreams can be attained through focusing on the company mission.

In another article from 2018, Forbes notes https://www.forbes.com/sites/williamcraig/2018/05/15/the-importance-of-having-a-mission-driven-company/ that mission-driven workers stay longer with companies and are likelier to grow into high performers compared to those who look at their jobs as just a transaction. Harvard Business Review agrees https://hbr.org/2024/03/what-successful-purpose-statements-do-differently, writing last year that when companies are truthful and build teams around their purpose, put money behind that purpose, and turn purpose statements into standard operating behaviors, they are able to build a deeper bond with both internal teams and consumers.

Marriott International’s purpose statement highlights five core values: putting people first, pursuing excellence for customers, embracing change, acting with integrity, and serving our world.

Many companies say similar things, but in our study, Marriott scored in the top 10% of companies whose employees, unprompted, mentioned purpose clarity and impact in open-ended descriptions of their experiences. Positive comments about the company outnumbered criticisms three to one. “Marriott is a fantastic organization that deeply cares about its people and mission from top to bottom,” one person wrote. “People-first mission statement and they stand by that,” said another.

Finally, many leaders have by now come to the conclusion that instead of crafting a mission for the sake of crafting one, with no strong belief or connection with audiences, it would be better https://www.fastcompany.com/91169371/mission-statement-origin-story-leader-company-danny-brooks-starbucks-ideo-patagonia to just share a simple origin story that showcases the values of their company.

Brooks—who worked as Starbucks’ VP of innovation culture and as a designer at IDEO before cofounding boutique strategy consultancy Penrose three years ago—knows something about mission statements. 

[…]

“The majority of mission statements simply don’t work,” he says. “They’re just not as effective as people think they are, and they can be a massive waste of time.” The smarter move, he believes, is to focus on your origin story. In fact, Brooks says, if he had to choose whether to put a mission statement or an origin story on a company’s website, “I’d go origin story 100 times out of 100.”  An origin story explains how your company came to be. If executed correctly, it does something a mission statement rarely does: It communicates a company’s values and fosters a bond with the consumer.

And of course, the worst mistake one can make is to think arriving at a great mission statement is a one time thing. Because missions should and will evolve with time, and mission statements should be flexible https://sifted.eu/articles/how-important-is-a-startup-founders-mission-statement. There is a fine line, however, between being too dogmatic and changing it too often, because while the former does not respond to evolving situations, the latter can create mistrust. 

The best way to do this, at least according to me, is to retain a strong core, but not be afraid of tweaking it a few times. The BBC, for instance, was founded in 1920, but only arrived at its mission statement a whole decade later.

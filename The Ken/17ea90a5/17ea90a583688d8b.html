Good Morning Ishan,
 
As we inch closer to the Lunar New Year on 1 February, we have another chance at a new beginning. And once again, hope for a fresh future. It feels a little better in Delhi, at least, as the weekend and night curfews in place in December were lifted this week. The winters are gradually becoming kinder. It feels like the worst of the pandemic might be over, and it might be the time to come out of hibernation.
 
This week at The Ken, too, we told stories about evolution. Letting go of the old, preparing for the new, and in the process, accepting the present for all that it has to offer.
 
Jaspreet told the story of six-year-old company Slice, which offers card-based credit. What does that mean, you ask? It isn’t really a credit card company but a fintech with a valuation of over $1 billion offering buy-now-pay-later services disguised as a credit card service. Disguise is an effective technique to evolve in the short term, but how will Slice control risk while growing at pace in the long term?
 
From the world of financial technology to greentech and edtech, companies are confronted with challenges in their evolutionary journey.
 
Confronted with sliding share prices and unhappy investors, two of India’s largest lead-acid battery makers, Exide and Amara Raja, want to make lithium-ion batteries. They have the will, but they also have supply chain problems. There’s great demand—India does not have even a single manufacturer of Li-ion cells. However, the time to meaningfully build the product, Shruti argued, may be slipping away.
 
There is the challenge of time, and then there is the challenge of space.
 
Edtech company Quizizz found resounding success on US shores in its short two-year-old life. The company, which garnered 75 million users globally, has now turned east to its home ground—India. However, wrote Olina, replicating its success in India might be its toughest challenge yet—what worked in the US for Quizizz could work against the company in the Indian market. 
 
The story of evolution, after all, is neither simple nor straightforward.
 
And even if you are willing and able, the context is constantly evolving as well.
 
The year 2021 was a fantastic one for fundraising in India. Over $50 billion in venture capital and private equity investments went to Indian companies, the largest ever. Among the investors is the tech fund run by IIFL Asset Management Company, an arm of financial services group India Infoline (IIFL). The company made a splash in 2021 with its Rs 5,000 crore ($670 million) late-stage and pre- IPO tech fund. But the real test will come when the rising tide ebbs. More in Seetharaman’s story here.
 
As for our newsletters this week, Seema wrote in Green Margins about how, as the electric vehicle hype cycle peaks, more and more legacy companies are getting charged up. Olina, in Ed Set Go, took stock—can tech really help students learn? Not sure. But can tech help with distribution? Absolutely. 
 
You know what always smells like a sign of personal evolution? A brand new best friend who pushes out the old and brings in the new. In The Nutgraf, Praveen asked: why Airtel is Google’s new best friend?
 
Over the last few months, The Ken has evolved, as well.  We began as a one-email-one-story-a-day publication. That has expanded into newsletters and podcasts, and in the coming days, hopefully, more—shiny new products in the journey of our evolution.

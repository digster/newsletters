Dear Ishan, good morning. It’s nice to be back in your inbox after last Sunday’s somewhat curtailed edition.

Well, that edition was curtailed because I was busy preparing for The Ken’s very first live subscriber event, which was scheduled for 5 pm on Monday. It did not happen, as we had to cancel it literally at the last hour (note to my millennial editor Jonathan: we cancelled it with less than 60 minutes remaining for the event to start, hence my use of the word, “literally”). One of our guest speakers became unavailable because of some unforeseen event he had to attend to.

So, about a dozen of my colleagues set themselves up into a crisis response unit over the next hour to individually call and email everyone who had bought a ticket. We had nearly sold out the beautiful 180-seater BIC auditorium, so it took a while. Some of us stood near the entrance to personally meet attendees who had already started arriving by 4.30 pm.

To our pleasant surprise, almost all of them were not just very understanding of our cancellation, they were empathetic.

“This was your first event. It must feel really bad?”

“Please don’t apologize. This couldn’t have been in your control. It happens.”

“Wow. 30 minutes to go. How are all of you handling it?”

From being defensive and disappointed, we started to get energised.

“Thanks for calling, but I’m already on my way. If you don’t mind, I’ll drop in any way and meet you folks,” said a few of the folks we called.

Divyashree Bhat, one of the many attendees who had already arrived by then (and also a subscriber), suggested we make some lemonade from the lemons we had ended up with. “Why don’t you encourage those who arrive at the venue to hang around for a while, since you already have the BIC space? I am sure subscribers would love to talk to each other and to folks from The Ken.”

So, for the next hour or so, a few dozen attendees (most also happened to be subscribers, and quite a few First Principles readers!) and all of us from The Ken hung about in BIC’s wonderful and open reception, accompanied by tea, coffee, and kombuchas from the in-house café. It was quite fantastic, really.

Nothing that happened was something we had foreseen, much less planned for. But there was a line that traces back from us launching The Ken in 2016 to us running a pandemic-themed newsletter called “Beyond the First Order https://the-ken.com/bfo/” to me starting the First Principles newsletter to last Monday evening at BIC.

But that line isn’t straight. Instead, it’s “emergence https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/emergence/”.

“In 2022, when I launched the First Principles podcast I did not know I would also launch a First Principles newsletter.

I did not know in May 2023 when I wrote the first edition of this newsletter that we would also end up having sections on Silent Sunday photos, books, music playlists, or habits.

I did not know that through the simple act of exchanging emails with many of you, a community would evolve.

I did not know that we’d start using the scale and diversity of our community to make this a truly many-to-many learning and discovery project on Sundays, instead of just one-to-many. Quests is just one aspect of that.

And I admit, I do not know what we will be doing six months from now, or in two years. I’m happy to let that reality “emerge”.

But if emergence is unpredictable and random, what is even the point of discussing it, you might be wondering. You have a point.

Emergence, by its very nature, cannot be planned and created. But we can create systems that are conducive to emergence. Conversely, when you’re planning something new and ambitious, place it in the context of the larger systems it operates in.”

Mango season is now on in South India. Quality Imampasands, my absolute favourite, are now available. Last year, we froze many kilos of peeled and sliced mangoes to enjoy during the 9–10 months that we don’t get the king of fruits. I must say they held up quite well.

1. “Empathy is a superpower”

“The fundamental weakness of Western civilization is empathy,” said https://edition.cnn.com/2025/03/05/politics/elon-musk-rogan-interview-empathy-doge/index.html Elon Musk recently. It is amazing how many lies he managed to fill into such a short sentence.

Empathy—“the action of understanding, being aware of, being sensitive to, and vicariously experiencing the feelings, thoughts, and experience of another”—is not a feature of Western civilization (RIP). It is a feature of human civilizations, including all that predate Western ones.

Empathy is also far from a weakness. Instead, in virtually all research related to workplaces and organisational leadership, empathy ends up as one of the most important traits https://hbr.org/2024/01/how-to-sustain-your-empathy-in-difficult-times.

“Empathy is not a weakness but something of a workplace superpower. Employees are more satisfied in their jobs, more willing to take creative risks, and more likely to help their colleagues if they work in empathic organizations. They are far less likely to report severe burnout or to develop physical symptoms of stress, and are more resilient in the face of adversity. They also tend to stay: A 2022 Gallup survey of more than 15,000 U.S. employees found that those with caring employers were far less likely than others to actively search for a new job. In 2021 Ernst & Young surveyed more than 1,000 workers who had left their jobs during the Great Resignation and found that 58% cited a lack of empathy from their managers as a central cause of their departure. Increasingly employees, especially Millennials and Gen Zers, don’t merely hope for empathy from their leaders—they demand it.”

Empathy is really about flipping our points of view and learning to see and understand the world from those of others. “Perspective shift” is one of the most powerful techniques in modern journalism https://oer.pressbooks.pub/narrativejournalism/chapter/chapter-8-point-of-view/ and writing.

“We are trained as journalists to describe action secondhand, through quotes and observation. Skilled narrative writers put the reader there and let her witness it, have the experience, feel it. That’s much more powerful than a secondhand version of reality.”—Jack Hart

Flipping the POV allows us to imagine how significant our impact might be from the other side. For instance, in a discussion with a close friend about our pets, we ended up talking about our pets’ POVs. “Could that be because Gabru will be one chapter in your book, but you, his entire book?” he asked me.

I’d never really thought of it like that. His analogy hit me the way Tim Urban’s series on the time we have left https://waitbutwhy.com/2015/12/the-tail-end.html with our parents (or children, before they leave home to live their own lives) did once.

Coming back to last Monday evening, we left the venue around 6 pm. Instead of being downcast and weary, we were all energised and chirpy. We were now even more excited and confident about doing subscriber events. The conversations we had, the laughs we shared, and the feedback we received made it amply clear that our subscriber community was a deep source of support for us. We’re already planning not just a rescheduling of that first event, but a series of events where we hope for much more emergent magic.

Our subscribers’ superpower, empathy, had become our supercatalyst.

4. Misplaced

Hello Ishan. It’s Hari here.

Serendipity is a wondrous thing. Say you invite a guest for the third time on your podcast—on Two by Two, we make it a habit of gifting all our guests books we think they’d enjoy. With too little time left, you realise your stock of ready books has run out, and there’s no way any online order is reaching you on time. So you stare at your antilibrary https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antilibrary for a few moments and narrow down books you think would make for good, unusual reads. 

I found myself in exactly this position last week, and just to make sure I would have a good number of options, I picked four—Misbehaving https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/26530355-misbehaving, Born to Run https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/20998547, On Photography https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/52372.On_Photography, and Open https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/6480781-open—before rushing to the recording session. If you haven’t checked them out yet, you should.

The only problem was, the guest had already read the book we finally chose to present him with. Sigh ☹️. 

Anyway, once I was back in office, I wasted no time placing that book order for the next guest, and it arrived exactly as scheduled. Sooner even; the new book smell hit all the right spots. 

But… they sent me the wrong book!

It seemed the week was determined to be one of disappointments, but when life gives you lemons…

I read Man’s War Against Nature https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/56808147-man-s-war-against-nature, a mid-20th century book written by American marine biologist and conservationist Rachel Carson, through Friday and Saturday. It’s short—90 pages or so. Just one long essay about the terrible effects of pesticides and insecticides on the environment, and the humans who keep creating more potent formulations in the name of ‘protecting’ crops. 

Maybe it was a bit dated, but I enjoyed the fluidity with it broke down the harmful effects of synthetic pesticides. At one point, I even found myself musing about how something like this would have made my 9th-grade Chemistry class so much more interesting. 

I hope you find one of the books here this week interesting enough to pick up. I would be delighted if you sent in your own recommendations for books that the First Principles community would enjoy. You can send them to us using this link https://theken.typeform.com/FPBooks.

Mergers and acquisitions are notorious for their failure rates. But that didn’t stop Axis Bank from spending $1.6 billion to buy Citigroup’s retail-banking arm in India.

After all, it made full sense for Axis to take the leap of faith. Citi’s high net worth clients and high spending users would help the Indian lender boost its margins and catapult it into the top three credit card issuers in the country.

However, to make that happen, Citi’s employees are crucial. Axis needs them to not only help retain but also smoothly integrate Citi’s customers into the new bank. So as part of the deal, Axis Bank made offers to 3,600 Citi employees, but 400 rejected the offer.

Those who stayed back are rattled by Axis Bank’s “aggressive” work culture. While the foreign bank prioritised customer and employee satisfaction, along with running a profitable show, Axis is all about achieving business targets at any cost, say employees.

As it is, Axis Bank has the third-highest attrition rate among private banks. If it were just that, it may not have been such an issue as banks have generally been experiencing high attrition since 2022.

But Axis bank has also progressively worsened on the customer-complaints front. In March 2022, it became the private bank with the most complaints.

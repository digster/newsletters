1. Omission biases and imagination deficits

Last week, I met Manish Sabharwal, the co-founder of Teamlease, who’s now waging a multipronged battle against “regulatory cholesterol” that he believes is holding back India from its true potential. We were recording the latest episode of the First Principles podcast. It’s a must listen, if I may say so myself.

Manish made a lot of interesting and contrarian points during our 1.5-hour conversation. But this part spoke to me as an entrepreneur, manager, and parent.

Manish: I want my kids to learn three concepts. I want them to learn compounding. I want them to learn probability. Then I want them to learn that the sins of omission are more expensive than the sins of commission.

Let’s start with that last one.

What you don’t do costs you more than what you do wrong. Because what you do wrong, you will get some feedback. But what you don’t do is a counterfactual.

Rohin: That’s the hardest thing to get feedback on, or for anyone else to be able to observe.

Manish: Yeah, but the most dangerous lies are the lies we tell ourselves, right? So how do you figure it out? See, compounding is probably the most important one, right?

And this is something that I bring up again and again to kids: will you think hard about one paisa doubling a day for 31 days or 10 lakhs right now? And most people will take 10 lakhs, right?

But one paisa doubling a day for 31 days is one crore six lakhs, right? The catch is that till the 24th day, you’re better off taking 10 lakhs, right? So, dessert is at the end of the meal in almost every institution, in almost every career that I respect.

It takes a really long time. Overnight success takes about 20, 30 years.

First Principles Podcast: Manish Sabharwal of Teamlease on creating great ancestors, India’s development journey and ‘regulatory cholesterol’

“Errors of omission”—the impact of decisions we don’t make—are a really hard thing for even most adults to comprehend.

My first thought when Manish mentioned errors of omission was, yeah, how would you even know you had made an error if no one or nothing told you?

“One complication in learning from errors is that judgment errors take two different forms errors of commission, where the decision maker does something that produces a negative result, and errors of omission, where the decision maker declines to follow an action that would have improved performance. Commission errors lead to specific penalties for behavior, while omission errors lead to foregone rewards.”

[…]

“While inferring the correct action to take in response to an error is always a challenge, the opportunity to learn from commission errors is always present – experiential learning processes provide feedback that directly affect future choices. Learning from omission errors, meanwhile, is more complex and challenging. The primary difficulty is that information on errors of omission is not always available to decision makers.”

Organization Science—Learning from Omission Errors

Omission errors are such a complex thing for us to deal with that we consider it a subconscious bias. Remember the famous (and much remixed) “trolley problem” thought experiment? Omission bias.

In the world of professional careers and business, errors of omission are usually recognisable when we tend to opt for the status quo over the unknown.

“The source of the status-quo trap lies deep within our psyches, in our desire to protect our egos from damage. Breaking from the status quo means taking action, and when we take action, we take responsibility, thus opening ourselves to criticism and to regret. Not surprisingly, we naturally look for reasons to do nothing. Sticking with the status quo represents, in most cases, the safer course because it puts us at less psychological risk.”

[…]

“In business, where sins of commission (doing something) tend to be punished much more severely than sins of omission (doing nothing), the status quo holds a particularly strong attraction. Many mergers, for example, founder because the acquiring company avoids taking swift action to impose a new, more appropriate management structure on the acquired company. “Let’s not rock the boat right now,” the typical reasoning goes. “Let’s wait until the situation stabilizes.” But as time passes, the existing structure becomes more entrenched, and altering it becomes harder, not easier. Having failed to seize the occasion when change would have been expected, management finds itself stuck with the status quo.”

The Hidden Traps in Decision Making by John S. Hammond, Ralph L. Keeney and Howard Raiffa, Harvard Business Review

Our unwillingness to imagine an unknown and uncertain future is something I have observed firsthand during hiring interviews. Time after time, I come across intelligent, smart, and articulate professionals with impeccably curated resumes and experiences who are unable or unwilling to glimpse into even a near-term future (6-12 months) and tell me what they would like to do differently if there were no constraints.

Ambition without imagination is pointless today. The old corporate ladder that you could reliably climb through sheer dint of hard work is on its last legs, if not dead. Professionals, young or old, who can situate their ambition at the intersection of imagination, possibility, and hope are what modern organisations seek.

But it’s easier to opt for the status quo. To tell potential employers what you have done, instead of what you yearn to do. To give them examples of what they might be doing merely inefficiently, instead of what they are not even thinking of.

If you cannot excite yourself about a future you want to create—but one that you cannot see or be sure of—then why would someone else? That “imagination deficit” eventually needs to be balanced in your workplace or company. If you aren’t a net provider of imagination and possibility in your role or career, then you are a net consumer. Ask yourself: would you want to be in charge of being a constant provider of imagination and possibility for others in your team or organisation?

What can you do to shake yourself out of your status-quo trap? The HBR article on decision-making I quoted above has some suggestions.

First of all, remember that in any given decision, maintaining the status quo may indeed be the best choice, but you don’t want to choose it just because it is comfortable. Once you become aware of the status-quo trap, you can use these techniques to lessen its pull:

– Always remind yourself of your objectives and examine how they would be served by the status quo. You may find that elements of the current situation act as barriers to your goals.

– Never think of the status quo as your only alternative. Identify other options and use them as counterbalances, carefully evaluating all the pluses and minuses.

– Ask yourself whether you would choose the status-quo alternative if, in fact, it weren’t the status quo.

– Avoid exaggerating the effort or cost involved in switching from the status quo.

– Remember that the desirability of the status quo will change over time. When comparing alternatives, always evaluate them in terms of the future as well as the present.

– If you have several alternatives that are superior to the status quo, don’t default to the status quo just because you’re having a hard time picking the best alternative. Force yourself to choose.

When two roads diverge in a wood, force yourself to choose consciously.

Oh, and don’t be a net imagination consumer.

3. Interesting reads

Waking from the Meme Dream—Susan Blackmore

Ideas that go round and round in your head will be successful. Not only will they be well remembered, but when you are next talking to someone they will be the ideas “on your mind” and so will get passed on. They may get to this position by being emotionally charged, exciting, easily memorable or relevant to your current concerns. It does not matter how they do it. The point is that memes that get themselves repeated will generally win out over ones that don’t. The obvious consequence of this fact is that your head will soon fill up with ideas. Any attempt to clear the mind just creates spare processing capacity for other memes to grab.

This simple logic explains why it is so hard for us to sit down and “not think”; why the battle to subdue “our” thoughts is doomed. In a very real sense they are not “our” thoughts at all. They are simply the memes that happen to be successfully exploiting our brain-ware at the moment.

This raises the tricky question of who is thinking or not thinking. Who is to do battle with the selfish memes? In other words, who am I?

*******

Gen Z and the End of Predictable Progress—Kyla Scanlon

When a main path to financial security comes through the algorithmic gods rather than institutional advancement (like when a single viral TikTok can generate more income than a year of professional work) it fundamentally changes how people view everything from education to social structures to political systems that they’re a part of.

*******

From Private Profit to Public Power: Financing Development, Not Oligarchy—Oxfam International

While the world failed to eradicate poverty over the last decade, it succeeded in minting 1202 new billionaires and is currently on track to have five trillionaires within a decade? Since 2015, the richest 1 percent have gained least $33.9 trillion in wealth in real terms, enough to end annual global poverty 22 times over.

They now own more wealth than the bottom 95% of the world combined.

Billionaires alone-roughly just 3000 people according to Forbes, overwhelmingly men- have gained $6.5 trillion since 2015, more than enough to cover the entire annual SDG financing gap and enough to end annual global poverty four times over. This wealth is also vastly disproportionately concentrated in the Global North, home to the majority of the world’s billionaires and wealth, despite being home to just one-fifth of global the population.” This immense concentration of wealth has translated to political power, in a movement towards oligarchy that sees ultra-wealthy individuals able to shape political and economic decision-making in ways that increase their wealth and impede efforts to create a more equitable society.

5. Telling stories that matter

Hello Ishan, it’s Hari here with this week’s book recommendations. Rohin has already talked about our latest First Principles podcast with Manish Sabharwal. Well, apart from being a great guest, Manish is also a voracious reader, and he told us during our discussion that he has been reading a book a week for the last 42 years! 

It still boggles my mind to think about it. Anyway, this week, I thought I’d talk about a cause Manish is close to—The New India Foundation, which aims to document India after Independence through research and translation fellowships. I came across many of the books the foundation has supported while researching for the episode, and thought I’d share a few with you today. 

Of course, you can always send us your own recommendations using this link.

One of the first books I came across while looking into the foundation was one we’ve already featured here: Neha Dixit’s The Many Lives of Syeda X. It’s a lovely book, which you should consider picking up if you haven’t already. 

But I’ve also added two more to my reading list. 

Both are memoirs. Both written by journalists. 

The first is Sohini Chattopadhyay’s The Day I Became a Runner. Both the looks of it, and whatever I’ve read about it, meant it didn’t take very long for me to hit the “Buy now” button.

The second is Pradeep Magazine’s Not Just Cricket, which, as the title suggests, is not just a story about cricket.

I’m pretty excited to dig into both. If you do (or already have) read them, write to us and let me know what you think.

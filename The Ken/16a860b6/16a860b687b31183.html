Good Morning Ishan,
 
I don't like dwelling on the past. Sometimes people get so hung up on what you were in the past that they forget how far you've come since then. 
 
There's a reason why companies have quarterly evaluation. Because hey, if you were to be judged on your performance in the first quarter alone, why bother even trying the rest of the year. Right?
 
But if you do decide to reflect on the past, look at growth. And that too relative growth. Unless you realise how you started out in the first place, you can never know how much you've grown.
 
Yesterday, Pranav and Prajakta had some pertinent reflections on the digital payments systems in India in the past five years. They found that the past five years in India have been a rollercoaster ride for companies from card networks like Visa and Mastercard to Paytm and MobiKwik. And they broke it down in eight graphics. Read it here (also, it's free).
 
Roshni, in her Friday story, takes a leaf from the past and says the wine industry that was once dependent on on-trade sales is now bolstered by retail formats such as Foodhall and Nature’s Basket, which offer experiential, rather than transactional wine-buying. And as our CEO remarked, this story is simultaneously about wine and about millennial thinking. 
 
On Thursday, Vandana looked at Xiaomi's past. Particularly how it started out in 2014. Since entering India in July of that year, the Chinese smartphone maker has grown to control 29% of the Indian smartphone market, boasts of 75-80 million users, and accounts for the bulk (57%) of online smartphone sales. But it is only aspiring for more—to get India addicted to its internet services too. 
 
On Wednesday, our editor Seema looked at Manipal Hospital's past. Since 2013, the company hasn’t acquired any hospital despite needing it to grow and give its investors exits. And not for lack of trying. In an insightful interview with Ranjan Pai, Manipal group's chief, you will get to know everything about the challenges of running a hospital to acquiring one. The story's also free. So please share widely. 
 
On Tuesday, Sumanth wrote about Reverie rising like a Phoenix from its ashes. The Bengaluru-based startup that offers local language technology solutions had a long and eventful journey before it was recently snapped by Reliance Industries for a sum of $37 million. See? The past can never judge what you're capable of. Folks in the startup ecosystem saw Reverie as a zombie startup—with little prospect of breaking out. But come back it did.
 
And in our last story from the week (technically the first, since it was published on Monday), Harveen traced the journey of sports retailer Decathlon, which built an empire in the shadows in India. From just four stores in 2012, it closed 2018 with about 65 stores—but hardly anyone knows anything about the company beyond the financials. Well, until Harveen's story, that is.
 
Go ahead, read our past week's stories folks and have a great Sunday! Though a little prescriptive—remember, if you judged yourself by your past, you'd never manage to even get out bed every morning.

Good Morning Ishan,

In an age of on-the-sleeves nationalism mixed with mass scale distrust of the media, it isn’t easy being a journalist. And nothing demonstrates the quandary of being one than today’s article.
 
TeamIndus, the highly-feted Indian competitor in the Google Lunar XPRIZE (GLXP) competition, is learned to have cancelled its launch contract with India’s space research organisation, Isro. Seema Singh, our editor, breaks the story. In case you’ve forgotten, the GLXP challenge was to launch a privately-funded robotic craft on the Moon, travel a distance of 500 meters, and transmit HD video and images back to earth.
 
It’s a tough story for us to break. Because on the one hand, you wanted TeamIndus to succeed, given how far they have managed to come. Of the original 16 teams, TeamIndus was one of the remaining five. India’s space program is widely held up as an example of frugal but cutting-edge, ambitious and innovative the world over. And having a privately-funded Indian startup join it on the world stage would’ve been truly remarkable.
 
But as Seema’s multiple deeply-reported stories (four) on TeamIndus during the last six months have shown, the writing has been on the wall for a while. Lack of funds, differences between the founders and senior management, delayed technical milestones and the dual-edged blade of PR coverage have all contributed to its current predicament.
 
Where does it go from here? What does this mean for Japan’s Team Hakuto that too was hitching a ride on the same Isro rocket? Is there still a chance for TeamIndus to land its craft on the moon in 2018? You’ll have to read Seema’s story for that: https://the-ken.com/teamindus-isro-call-off-glxp/

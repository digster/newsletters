Good Morning Ishan,

Getting a grip on the growth pangs of a company that has largely put people before growth-at-all-cost is an exercise in itself. Even for the founder.

It was quite telling then that Girish Mathrubootham*, the founder and CEO of Freshworks, said at a recent all-hands that the Nasdaq-listed company was going through a teenage-like phase of growth—"it is harder to manage a teenager than a toddler”.

As personal an analogy as it was for the father of two teenagers, it was also quite apt. The company that grew at a scorching pace of 40-50% every year for over a decade until it got listed on Nasdaq in 2021 now expects to grow 18-20% in 2023.

It's still good growth given the macroeconomic conditions, I’d say. But it’s not good enough for the company, which is only halfway through its earlier stated goal of achieving $1 billion in annual recurring revenue by 2023-24.

Over 60,000 companies—mostly small and medium businesses—across the world use Freshworks’ suite of over a dozen products. But it’s largely two flagship products that are driving its growth. People say Mathrubootham’s intuitive product-thinking magic is yet to rub off on other products.

Moreover, the growth drivers themselves need a recharge.

Freshworks has to dial up its growth while lowering its expenses. A company where nearly 75% of its 5,000+ employees own stocks, a company that has almost pampered its employees all these years, is now having to recalibrate its culture. To one that is more performance-driven.

“Even if I did 25% of my numbers, no one bothered me with it [earlier],” said one employee.

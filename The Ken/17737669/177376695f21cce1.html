It’s not often a country’s central bank tells its number 1 private bank something to the effect, “You’re a mess. Please go fix yourself. Take a while. Till then, go easy on growth.”
 
But that’s exactly what the Reserve Bank of India ordered HDFC Bank, the country's largest private bank, to do on 2 December. RBI stopped HDFC from rolling out any new digital initiatives or adding new credit card customers till it fixed its recurring network outages, of which the most recent one was on 21 November. That's when its internet banking and payment networks went down because of a power failure at its data centre.
 
What happened with HDFC on 21 November was in many ways both the tip of the iceberg and the bottom of the pit. Over the years, as India’s banks have grown at breakneck speed, their data centres and technical infrastructure haven’t kept up.
 
An hour of failure in a large bank can stall over 400,000 UPI-powered payment transactions. And it's not just HDFC. Its closest rival, ICICI Bank, and India’s largest bank, the State Bank of India (SBI), have also seen failing digital transactions. Between the three banks, there have been 85 instances of outages in 2020, according to a service that tracks this.
 
With all banks keeping quiet about what’s going on, Arundhati had to speak to numerous current and past officials to piece together a picture of not only what happened on 21 November, but how it has been building up, too.

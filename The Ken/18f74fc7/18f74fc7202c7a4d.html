The insurtech unicorn’s upcoming stock-market listing rides on zagging into the lucrative but challenging world of group health insurance while the industry mostly zigs in retail

Good Morning Ishan,

Go Digit’s will launch its IPO tomorrow. It’s great timing—the company became profitable for the first time in 2023, and there are indications that this might continue this year.

It achieved this by aggressively underwriting more group health-insurance business, a vertical that’s notoriously difficult for insurers. Corporate clients tend to ask for lower premiums if their employees’ claims fall short, or they’ll move to other providers that offer better deals.

But it’s a big enough pie: this type of coverage accounts for 50% of all health-insurance premiums in India. In Go Digit’s case, group plans account for nearly 95% of its health-insurance premiums, and it’s using this to grow its book.

That’s a tight line to walk, Narayanan writes in today’s story from India. Check out his coverage to find out how Go Digit is managing this, and how this direction may affect its profitability in the future.

Meanwhile, over in Vietnam, Coby looked at electric vehicle maker Vinfast’s sales performance. Its vehicles are already on Vietnam’s roads—mostly as taxis. Vinfast has been selling its cars to GreenSM, an electric-only taxi firm that was established by none other than Pham Nhat Vuong, Vinfast’s own CEO and the founder of its parent company Vingroup. As a client of Vinfast, GreenSM ordered US$839 million worth of cars in 2023, with more to come this year.

Sure, it makes sense to keep in the family. But the way Vinfast relies on companies within the orbit of Vingroup and Vuong makes its prospects look less enticing than it’s trying to project, particularly at a time when overseas sales and expansion plans have been spotty.

Vuong is eager to frame Vinfast as more than just a car company. He describes it as the pride and joy of Vietnam. If that’s the case, there’s a lot riding on the EV maker’s success. Coby’s story covers the “Vin-family” sales channels that Vinfast depends on and how the company is trying to project the illusion of strength.

You’ve seen the headlines about Tiktok facing an ultimatum in the United States: divest or leave.

It’s a situation that rhymes with recent history. Donald Trump tried to ban Tiktok and Wechat, which are used by many Chinese-Americans to stay connected with extended family in China, and by businesses that have dealings in East Asia. That was in 2020. Neither executive order worked out.

But the stakes feel different this time. And there could be collateral damage.

E-commerce upstart Temu, which was created by PDD Holdings to sell cheap goods from China-based merchants to customers located everywhere else in the world, has been a hit with American users. If it could do well on Amazon’s home turf, the logic went, then it could succeed anywhere.

With the US government putting Tiktok in its crosshairs, Temu is compelled to change its priorities, hastening its shift towards Europe and other countries. The homegrown e-commerce platforms in those markets are about to feel squeezed.

Like Tiktok, Temu has been plunged into American politicking. Some lawmakers want to make rules to stop Temu from using tax exemptions to undercut competitors in the US, and the company has been linked to goods that were made by forced labour. Additionally, again like Tiktok, Temu is being accused of collecting excessive personal information about its users in the US. All of these signals suggest that it’s time for Temu to curb its dependence on customers on American soil.

There’s something to be said about sneezes and colds here. Two economic superpowers are in sternutation fits and everyone else is holding their breath.

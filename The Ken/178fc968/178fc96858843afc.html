An investor recently asked on Twitter which Nifty stock never moves more than Rs 5-10 even if investors are bullish on its business.

A few named engineering and infrastructure conglomerate Larsen & Toubro. Some pointed to the telco Bharti Airtel. But the overwhelming majority of the 500-odd responses went with a different company: ITC Ltd.

The $34 billion cigarette and FMCG major is often called "Indian Tortoise Company" on Twitter.

Not without reason, either. The stock has fallen 6% in the past five years. In the same period, its larger FMCG rival Hindustan Unilever's shares have returned 170% and the benchmark Sensex has seen an 85% surge.

This is despite ITC being highly profitable, cash-rich, and debt-free. So what's the catch?

The investor skepticism is often chalked up to the company's reliance on cigarettes, which brought in 85% of ITC's operating profit in FY20. But the company is making good on its commitment to move away from tobacco, with its other businesses contributing more than half its revenue in FY20.

What if the problem lies elsewhere? That's what our contributor, strategy consultant Daljit S Kochhar, asks in his analysis today. More precisely, with how ITC is owned.

It's not controlled by a family, as most Indian companies are. Instead, British American Tobacco and the Indian government—who don't see eye to eye—hold 29.4% and 28.5%, respectively, in ITC.

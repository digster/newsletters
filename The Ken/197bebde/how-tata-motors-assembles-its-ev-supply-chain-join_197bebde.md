Good Morning Ishan,
Last week, Tata Motors launched the Harrier EV—a shiny, feature-stuffed, subsidy-friendly electric SUV. It looks great. It probably drives great. And thanks to some strategic localisation (over 50%, right on the money), it checks the government’s “Made in India” box.
But behind the localisation numbers, behind the company’s sales metrics, sits a company that didn’t even share the spotlight.
Tata Autocomp isn’t the brand on the grille—it’s the one soldering what’s behind it. The drivetrains, the battery packs, the aluminium radiators, even the seats. Built not just in-house, but often through a maze of partnerships.
Ask how any component got made, and the answer, more often than not, is: “We have a JV for that.”
It’s a deliberate playbook: localise just enough to unlock subsidies, borrow just enough to avoid expensive R&D, and scale fast. It’s working. Tata Autocomp clocked a profit of Rs 1,400 crore last year. And now, after 15 years of radio silence, the IPO chatter is back.
The catch? It’s not a Tata Motors subsidiary. It’s controlled by a couple of other Tata companies. Which means when parts run late or platforms don’t align, no one’s technically out of order. Just… misaligned.

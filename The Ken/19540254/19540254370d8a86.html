As I write this, India’s IT industry is engaged in a much-publicised event in Mumbai, soul-searching for the next growth opportunity as the gains of 30-odd years of outsourcing dry up fast.

But what if we argued that an adjacent industry—the consumer internet businesses relying on gig workers—has been growing because Indians are choosing to outsource their everyday lives? These platforms alone have attracted nearly $20 billion—13% of all tech funding—since 2010.

From getting someone to “soak rice and pulses”, “knead dough”, “stand in a queue”, “take a holy dip” in the Ganges holding your photo, walk your dog, or do the laundry—urban India is happy outsourcing anything that looks like a chore.

We may believe that our time is better utilised elsewhere, or that instant gratification at a small price is helping someone earn money. What’s there to question? In the acute jobs crisis facing India, e-commerce and hyperlocal companies employ 20–30% of 11.2 million gig workers. The government think tank, Niti Aayog, calls internet platforms “an engine of job creation” and gig work “a catalyst for occupational mobility”.

Occupational mobility?

Report after report from consulting firms of all pedigrees cite the dire need to create productive and high-paying jobs—India has serious productivity gaps in virtually every sector relative to Asian and developed countries.

But in a country where college degrees can, at best, employ four out of every 10 graduates, what options do the young have?

In a provocatively argued piece today, Seetharaman writes: “The gig economy’s appetite for speed is insatiable. First, it was instant groceries. Then it was sandals. Then, phones, laptops, Playstation, microwaves. Now it’s 10-minute food. Who knows what instant craving will be next?”

Let’s get to the story.

Jio Financial Services is a big stock. No two ways about it. Its huge free-float market cap is why the stock is finding its way into the benchmark Nifty 50 later next month.

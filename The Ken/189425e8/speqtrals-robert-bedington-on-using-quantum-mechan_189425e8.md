Good Morning Ishan,
Think of a secret that you guard closely, or information that you generally don’t want to share with anyone. There’s a chance you have it recorded somewhere in digital form. Maybe it’s on a hard disk, or you carry it around in your pocket, on your phone.
We take for granted the cryptographical processes that keep our secrets and data under “lock and key.” It’s assumed that our financial data, personal information, cat photos, and stored secrets are for our own eyes only, and it would take far too long for someone to brute-force a reasonably strong password.
But what if an advanced computer emerges, and it’s strong enough to crack the encryption that’s currently used? This may sound far-fetched (but not that far-fetched if you read our [earlier edition](https://the-ken.com/sea/present_future/entropica-labs-tommaso-demarie-on-making-quantum-computers-easy-to-use/) on quantum computing), but there are scientists who are already developing ways to prevent this catastrophe.
To find out more, I spoke with Robert Bedington, the CTO and co-founder of Speqtral, a Singapore-based company that is manufacturing commercial quantum communication systems. Speqtral’s first commercial offering is a cryptographic standard that will prevent bad actors from using quantum computers to access our personal data.

It wasn’t long before businesses came knocking. There was commercial demand for satellites able to deliver quantum key distribution (QKD) from space, a communication method that is built upon the laws of quantum mechanics.
This led Lum and Bedington to form Speqtral. They eventually launched a satellite called Spooqy-1, whose name was a humorous nod at Albert Einstein’s quip that quantum entanglement is “spooky action at a distance.” Their[ mission patch](https://sites.google.com/site/phylej/projects-1/spooqy-1) shows a frightened Einstein gripping its outer ring, scared by a pair of ghosts.

So how does Speqtral’s satellite launch relate to keeping information secure? Here’s how Bedington explained it:
|
The problem is real. Over the past few years, hackers have been collecting encrypted data such as personal financial information and proprietary IP like drug blueprints, operating under the assumption that they will eventually have access to computing power great enough to decrypt that data. The gambit is that this information will still be valuable in the future, and that “harvest and decrypt” attacks will yield payoffs when unfettered access becomes a possibility.
To secure data and guard against these bad actors, it’s necessary to develop QKD. Whereas conventional key distribution works because the mathematical calculations behind it form a layer of security that requires immense processing power to break, QKD relies on laws of nature. With QKD, an eavesdropper that attempts to look at the system would change it in a way that would notify the senders and recipients. More importantly, since it is impossible to clone an identical copy of an unknown quantum state, attackers cannot copy data the same way as they would with network traffic as we know it today.
|
|
Network effects
While Speqtral’s current work is related to securing information against bad actors, the company’s end goal is to develop the quantum internet, a global quantum network for sharing immense processing power around the world. The applications go well beyond encryption.
Bedington described this as a parallel to how the internet was originally built for defence institutes and scientific research organisations as a medium to exchange information, but has since come to be used by people everywhere.

If you’ve been a reader of Present/Future since its launch earlier this year, you may have noticed that Speqtral has some of the same DNA that is in other companies previously featured in this newsletter. Bedington said Speqtral shares some similarities with Transcelestial, which is developing and improving its wireless laser communications network, although Speqtral is “even more niche.”
Whereas Transcelestial is making it possible to develop[ data communications with lasers](https://the-ken.com/sea/present_future/transcelestials-rohit-jha-on-using-lasers-to-give-a-billion-people-an-internet-power-up/) in open space, Speqtral performs quantum communications using weak lasers in the same setting. One key difference is Transcelestial sends billions of photons in each pulse that runs through their network, while Speqtral would send just a single photon.
That makes communications extremely secure for Speqtral, but the rate of information exchange is also much lower—just enough to derive an encryption key.
Regardless, there’s an interesting network effect at work in Southeast Asia’s deep tech companies. It’s reasonable to imagine a world where their inventions work in tandem a decade from now, improving the infrastructure that keeps people connected. With Speqtral’s demo satellite scheduled to launch in 2024, we might just be on the precipice of major changes that will reveal themselves in the years to come.

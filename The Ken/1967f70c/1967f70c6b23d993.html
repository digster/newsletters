No chief executive around the world had any doubts about Donald Trump’s intentions when he won the US presidential election last year. Even so, his tariffs earlier this month caught most companies unawares.

Not Zetwerk, though.

Born smack in the middle of Trump’s first term, Zetwerk’s introduction to manufacturing was a baptism by fire. Its business model was simple: aggregate small manufacturers’ spare capacity and set up its own facilities to make everything from headphones and televisions to auto and solar components.

But Zetwerk knew that servicing its global clientele by producing in a single country or region would be suicidal.

“Devices like hearables and wearables are labour-intensive and need skill. You can’t do that in the US,” Josh Foulger, president of Zetwerk’s electronics business, told us. “But last-mile assembly, like testing, can be done onshore.”

But Zetwerk, last valued at $3 billion, didn’t have to reinvent the wheel for its “blended supply chain”. Nokia, Foulger’s employer for nearly two decades, had perfected it.

Shristi and Seema’s story today is about how Zetwerk chose the Finnish company’s armour for the trade war Trump has unleashed.

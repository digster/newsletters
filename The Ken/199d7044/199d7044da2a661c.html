Good morning Ishan, and welcome to the weekend—an entirely made-up construct that we look forward to, luxuriate in, and miss terribly. 

I couldn’t write last week because I was in Goa on Saturday. We spent four days doing so little, and yet so much. 

I read four books in four days. It’s amazing how fast you can read books when you don’t have distractions. 

1. Breath, by James Nestor. A brilliant treatise on not just breathing, but also the human body, our brains, our mouths, and our developmental regression after thousands of years of evolution. I place it on the same pedestal as Christopher McDougall’s Born to Run. Both are incredibly well written, Gonzo-ish, and turn conventional wisdom on its head. A must read!

2. Advice on Upskilling, by Justin Skycak. A really thorough, no-nonsense approach to professional self-improvement via upskilling. Skycak manages to do the impossible—talk about upskilling at a non-specific level (thus, it could be applicable to math, gym, coding, writing) while also managing to be incredibly precise and detailed in his recommendations. He makes it available as both a free PDF or a free Google Sheet that is frequently updated.

3. Get Out of Your Own Way, by Mark Goulston and Philip Goldberg. The book’s description is pretty solid: “Practical, proven self help steps show how to transform 40 common self-defeating behaviors, including procrastination, envy, obsession, anger, self-pity, compulsion, neediness, guilt, rebellion, inaction, and more.”

4. Blue Moon, by Lee Child. Sigh, I think I am done with my Reacher love. I guess I must have read about ten Reacher novels by now. I’ve seen all three seasons of the TV series too. With Blue Moon, I feel like I’ve reached the limits of my cliche tolerance. It is just too formulaic, lazy and, well, cliched to enjoy. I still slogged through the book, but don’t think I’ll be buying or reading any more for a while.

I also managed two barefoot runs on the beach for about 3.5 kilometres each. The last time I tried it was back in 2022. My calves ached like hell for the next two days. This time, they (and I) fared much better. I credit that to my decision to adopt mostly “barefoot” shoes for the last three years (inspired by Christopher McDougall’s Born to Run!). 

I made friends with at least seven or eight dogs. Goa’s beach dogs are really quite cool. I had a few join me on my runs, really! Bengaluru might love its dogs, but Goa and Goans are on a different level altogether! We even saw a massive bull doing beach runs each evening, being both pulled and pushed by two men. I hope he and they had nothing to do with sad and cruel illegal bullfights, which I came to know is an ongoing Goan issue after reading through a few news sites.

It rained virtually every day. The heat and humidity (relative to Bengaluru’s cool) were something we had to endure (the place we were staying at had no AC). We saw a monitor lizard. Of course I drank Goan beer. 

A lot. But also, quite nothing at all.

1. Why we gift, and what we gift

A few weeks ago, we ran our annual Gifting survey. Over 250 of you gave us a bunch of ideas, philosophies, formulas, and methods around your gifting. 

Today, I want to publish the insights from the survey. Next Sunday, we’ll publish the recommendations as our 2025 Gifting Guide!

My creative colleagues from The Ken‘s editorial design team (Sakshi, Kashvi, and Kavipriya) helped me visualise the results. 

Our first question was this: “Let’s admit it, we want great gifts, too! So, let’s start with that. What is a practical (as in, you can imagine someone getting it for you) gift you’d love to get in 2025?” 

Here are the most popular answers. (I’ll do my best to move all the interesting items from the long tail into the actual guide next week, promise!)

We didn’t ask any personal details in the survey, except for ages.

View in Browser

Which brought us to my favourite question. “Do you have a personal philosophy about gifting? Something that broadly guides your gifting choices? A common theme that connects together gifts, people, and occasions?”

View in Browser

I went through all the answers you gave, and categorised them into the following buckets, based on what the philosophy pertains to. 

I like to view these as circles in a giant gifting venn diagram. Pick the few that appeal to you, then wonder what gift will work at their intersection.

A. About the recipient

A recurring theme was gifts that recipients might not want or be able to buy themselves.

“Something that would be meaningful to the other person, but not necessarily one that they’ll buy for themselves.”

“Buy them something they want but would never get for themselves.”

“I will gift something that people hesitate to buy on their own.”

“Something that they wouldn’t buy on their own.”

“I am trying to buy people things that they would delay buying themselves.”

“Someone would love an item, but feels it is an indulgence enough that they won’t buy it themselves without having guilt of overspending.”

“Balance between need and want.”

“Give gifts that you think the other person needs, rather than a good to have (talk of the town).”

Many of you said it was important to *really* think about the person receiving the gift (it isn’t that common or obvious, trust me).

“My only philosophy behind gifting is that it should be thoughtful and that too from the ‘what will bring a smile to this person’s face when they see it’. And then the gift is no longer restricted to just the item, but I add a letter or a note or a bouquet of flowers which hold some emotional value in them.”

“What would they like? What’s their personality like? My friend who loves stationary and art always gets different kinds of journals with contemporary art.”

“I gift people based on their interests. For example, if my friend is crazy about Friends TV series, I will find out something unique that would be from that franchise and will give immense joy to my friend. Most likely, it will be some daily use or display merchandise, not something dumb.”

“Think about the person before you buy them something.”

“I want to buy something that they love, not something I love (sounds obvious, but have many family members that believe the opposite).”

“I keep a note of what people have mentioned in retrospect as something they might have wanted.”

B. About the gift’s attributes

“Sustainable.”

“I feel gift things people will talk about.”

“Understated brand, craftsmanship, functionality, premium.”

“Functionality over form.”

“Gifts tell how much you value others in your life. The best part about the gift is the message card.”

“Spiritual items or donate to a cause.”

Utility, value and usability were very important too.

“It should be usable.”

“All gifts should have high utility in the lives of the receiver. I do not care much about gifts that do not have much use other than to show-off.”

“A gift that is used daily/weekly or provides happiness.”

“Should be helpful in day-to-day working.”

“Useful, can be consumed or worn but not displayed.”

“Something that can be used by the receiver, healthy, environmentally friendly.”

“I would love to give/receive gift items that can be used and not get passed on or get thrown away. On occasions to gift, we choose home/daily utility items, fruits, nuts, chocolates (the better ones). Keeping things *simple is enjoyable* than fancy things as gifts we don’t get to use regularly.”

C. About what the gift spurs

“I like to introduce a new experience.”

“Supporting new hobbies or helping find new ones.”

“Information-enriching and not finding itself just in a bookshelf or showpiece collecting dust over a period of time.”

“Something which raises your intellect, something which stays with yourself.”

“Something that is creative and informative.”

“Gift consumables or digital subscription. Chance of utilisation is higher. With perfumes or show pieces etc, it could land in a storage box or be recycled.”

D. About the relationship connected through gifting

“For really close friends, you know their likes and dislikes and the gift would address that. But in general, for a new person, I’d go with culturally rich shared experiences like food, good music, theatre, cinema, travel… These have always got people together. So I think it’s a great ice breaker.”

“Gift needs to align with expectations, not overwhelm or underwhelm. When it matters, it needs to show you have thought about it.”

E. About the person gifting

“Gift something that you would really love to keep if the other person is out of station :)”

“Gift something which I would love to receive as a gift.”

“The first question I ask is ‘Would it be cool to have this?’ followed by ‘Would I use it myself?’ and then ‘Would it relevant to the person I am gifting it to?’”

F. About memories

“Gift should remind you of the person who gifted it to you. Gift is a manifestation of the specific emotions that led to it.”

“Gift should remind the person who gifted it every time it’s used.”

“I feel people should gift others as it helps in creating happy memories and whenever the person sees or uses the gift, it reminds of the moments and connections with gifter.”

G. About what it should not be about

The most important rules are the ones that tell us what not to do.

“I don’t gift friends because it sets unrealistic expectations—gifting small items like books are fine. Buying a beer is where I stop.”

“Shall not be overly pricey. Puts other person in the spot. Anything that can just get happiness.”

“I’m a minimalist. Most of my friends are too. So if you can’t think of anything useful, don’t gift for the sake of gifting.”

“Prefer to gift a book based on what I know of the individual. Never gift expensive electronics, especially to those who are overtly material.”

“Less consumerism; a gift they can share or pass on.”

“No cash. Has to be something utilitarian/functional. Preferably something one would not be very keen on ‘splurging’ for self, but fine with gifting another.”

“I avoid gift cards as they are very impersonal. I try to pick up cues on what I could possibly gift others.”

“I don’t believe in gifts as formalities. And I hate recycled gifts—never give them and sadly, receive them occasionally.”

The next question was about “extravagant gifts (as in, fat chance of anyone getting it for you, but you just might!)” we’d love to get in 2025?

I did ask if we’d missed gifting subjects, and got a bunch of thoughtful answers.

“I do donations for underprivileged and underserved communities once in a while, but I don’t think it’s appropriate to call that gifting.”

“Household help, nanny.”

“House help, office security people, municipal corporation workers.”

“The underserved—the ones where the gifts you give no matter something small/cheap will be more valued.”

“People that I know who struggle financially and can’t afford something useful.”

“Children of friends.”

“Kid’s friends.”

“Clients/customers.”

“Mentors.”

“Teachers.”

“Partners. They aren’t family yet, definitely get more gifts than closest friends.”

“Neighbours.”

“Pets.”

“Spouse (she can break any formula that I create).”

I also asked if there were any other “rules” that guided our gifting decisions, and got a few.

“How much is the cost of gifts they gave you in the past.”

“The gift shouldn’t be so over the top that a person feels burdened by it.”

An unsurprising set of toppers for the gifts we default to when we’re lazy.

2. Anthologies, and cooking something new

Hello Ishan, it’s Tanim with your books for the week. 

You know what’s better than one great story? A whole collection of great stories. 

That’s the magic of an anthology. I love the freedom of picking a story at random and finishing it in five to ten minutes. It’s like a box of assorted desserts. 

When it features different authors, you get a tour of diverse writing styles and perspectives, all in one book. It’s a fantastic way to appreciate the unique beauty that different voices bring to storytelling.

Do you have any anthology recommendations that you think our community would like to read? Please share it with us here. 

To start us off, here are two picks from The Ken’s newsroom: 

Sumit, our head of desk, writes: 

“Caste anthologies are usually like mandatory office training. Well-intentioned, important, and often painfully dull. The Blaft Book of Anti-Caste SF is the exact opposite. It takes the weight of caste politics and hurls it into speculative universes full of cyborgs, aliens, dystopias, and alternate mythologies.

Instead of trudging through another moral lecture, it pulls the reader into stories that are inventive, unruly, and often unsettling in the best way. The brilliance here isn’t just in the subject matter, but in how it weaponises science fiction to dismantle old hierarchies and imagine something radically different. It refused to let me stay comfortable.”

View in Browser

Archishma, our staff writer, writes: 

“I read this book called The Blaft Anthology of Tamil Pulp Fiction. It is a collection of eclectic short stories, originally written in Tamil by some famous Tamil authors translated into English. There are three volumes to this series. The book I’m referring to is the first volume. The short stories range from how robots can take control of human lives, to a lawyer (along with a secretive group formed underground) meting out justice to wronged victims, and drug-driven love affairs, etc. It is a mix of science fiction, crime, romance, and more genres.”

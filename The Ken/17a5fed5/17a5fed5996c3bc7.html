Two doctors go on vacation and a 260-bed hospital's patient volumes are hit.

This is what happened to a facility run by Apollo Hospitals, India's largest healthcare chain, in Chennai in the three months ended June 2017.

This wouldn't be absurd if you knew how important "star doctors" are to such hospitals. These doctors have over two decades of experience, attract hundreds of patients a week, and conduct multiple surgeries a day. They have the potential to earn over $1 million a year.

But the pandemic has been bad news for this breed. With hospitals overrun by Covid patients and elective surgeries postponed, these doctors are no longer as prized as they once were.

Furthermore, the rise of telemedicine has levelled the playing field for their younger and less popular counterparts. For instance, the average experience of the 10,000 doctors available for online consultations on MediBuddy, a healthtech platform, is 10 years. And the portal has seen teleconsults surge more than 3X to 6.5 million in the year ended March 2021.

Making the transition to telemedicine has not been easy for senior doctors, who are not as tech-savvy. But, as Ruhi argues in her story today, there is a silver lining here: online consultation brings with it the risk of criticism on social media by disgruntled patients. There's more to the shifts in the balance of power between the two classes of doctors.

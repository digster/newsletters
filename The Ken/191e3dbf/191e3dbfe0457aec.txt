‘Highly secure’ Apple has a fake-loan-app infestation too. The electronics giant touts security and privacy as one of its strongest USPs, but seems to have missed the potential for misuse inside its own ecosystem 
 By D V L S Pranathi https://the-ken.com/writers/pranathi_4/

11 min read

Good Morning Ishan,

Last week, I received this text message from a random number on my phone.

https://the-ken.com/?post_type=ka_ching&p=1467893?utm_source=ro_hdr&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=View in Browser https://the-ken.com/?post_type=ka_ching&p=1467893?utm_source=ro_hdr&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=

Considering my line of work, and how pervasive such things have become these days, it was quite obvious that this was some kind of scam. But I clicked on the link anyway (also because of my line of work; journalists are an irrepressibly curious bunch, after all) and was taken to a website that had nothing to do with the financial services provider mentioned in the text message. 

Instead, it was called NSC Credit Rupee, and showed different categories of credit offerings such as Mahila loans, personal loans, student loans, etc. NSC Credit Rupee is an unauthorised lender, but aside from the obvious difference in the name, the website looked absolutely legit. 

Fake loan offerings by unscrupulous players flying under the RBI’s radar are not a new problem, nor one unique to me. The Ken has already written about the rise of malicious instant-loan apps https://the-ken.com/story/indias-instant-loan-app-crisis-is-made-in-china/ and their impact several times since 2020. Here’s a little excerpt from one of Arundhati’s stories from way back in 2021. 

The lockdown had made things perilous for Abhishek Makwana, a writer who freelanced for a popular Hindi sitcom. And these small loans with their short repayment cycles—between seven and 15 days—kept popping up on his radar. They required no documentation; money would be instantly deposited in his bank account.

The flipsides, however, were the high interest rates ranging from 800% to 1,000% on an annual basis and the pack of collection agents that hounded him if the repayments were even a day late. Abhishek Makwana died of suicide in December 2020, but ironically, he had repaid most of his loans.

“No one is going to commit suicide for dues of Rs 1 lakh ($1,360). It was the constant debt cycle and the harassment of collection agents that killed him,” Makwana [Abhishek’s brother] tells The Ken over the phone. “Even after his death the collection agents harassed me, my family and my brother’s friends,” he says.

It’s been nearly four years since that story was written, and during that period, many regulatory actions have been bandied about by the Reserve Bank of India (RBI), and thousands of fake apps have been taken down from app stores. The latest is a plan to make a repository of “authentic” loan apps https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/wealth/borrow/rbi-to-make-repository-of-authorised-digital-lending-apps-to-prevent-borrowers-being-scammed/articleshow/112363964.cms?from=mdr, which is supposed to serve as a guide for people seeking online credit. 

But the proposed solution only underscores how prolific the problem remains. Debt traps, harassment, abuse, and outright theft… the cases continue to pile up. And one of the most common ways in which such apps are distributed remains the world’s most popular app stores. 

Babu Lal, a 32-year-old software engineer-turned-consultant based out of Rajasthan, has made it his business to track and identify these fake apps. And a database he maintains has flagged over 5,700 of them across platforms as of 11 September. Google and its app store account for the large majority of these (given Google’s Android is by far the most-used mobile OS in India), but nearly a fifth of them—1,087 apps—came from a platform that’s touted as the world’s most secure mobile ecosystem available to consumers right now. 

But if thousands of fake loan apps keep popping up on the Apple App Store, is Apple really as secure as it likes to claim.

External security, internal vulnerability?

In July, Apple launched a pretty big marketing campaign https://www.moneycontrol.com/technology/apple-launches-new-campaign-pitches-safaris-privacy-and-security-features-article-12771152.html for its Safari browser, and front and centre in all communications were its much vaunted security features. Some of the ads in the campaign even took digs at other browsers for tracking user data and browsing habits—Safari, instead, would protect you from being tracked by nosy websites. 

This assurance of privacy and security is a cornerstone of Apple’s product strategy. And it works. Apple’s annual sales in India https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2024-07-15/apple-sales-in-india-surge-33-to-8-billion-as-chinese-market-slows hit almost US$8 billion in the year ended March 2024, and a significant chunk of its customers swear by its security features. When it comes to protecting customers from external threats, Apple is numero uno in public perception. But what about threats from within its own ecosystem? 

Pranav Bidare, a researcher at the Centre for Internet and Society, says Apple seems to have developed a bit of a blind spot.

“Apple’s focus has been on protecting user data from external parties, like advertisers and meta. But they seem to have overlooked the potential for misuse within their own app ecosystem.”

Babu Lal is far more strident in his criticism. 

“These fake loan apps are not only getting through Apple’s vetting process, they’re thriving. Most of them start trending in the App Store within 24 hours of being published.” As an example, he pointed me towards KCPL Rupee, an unauthorised app that was launched on 30 August and was trending at #11 with almost 500 reviews on the App Store as of 3 September, which is when I spoke to Lal.

https://the-ken.com/?post_type=ka_ching&p=1467893?utm_source=ro_hdr&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=View in Browser https://the-ken.com/?post_type=ka_ching&p=1467893?utm_source=ro_hdr&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=

There are regulations from both the RBI and Apple on what loan apps can and can’t do. The RBI issues every registered loan app with a registration number, and requires lenders to maintain several standards, including not using unethical means to force repayment, transparently declaring interest rates, etc. Apple’s guidelines for these apps also says they “must not charge an APR [annual percentage rate] exceeding 36%, inclusive of all costs and fees, and they must not demand full repayment within 60 days or less.” 

Well, according to Lal, and the few apps I managed to check, many of these lenders offer small loans that require very little paperwork but charge massively high interest rates—ranging anywhere between 25%–100% for loans ranging from seven days to six months.

Here’s the app store description for the KCPL app:

https://the-ken.com/?post_type=ka_ching&p=1467893?utm_source=ro_hdr&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=View in Browser https://the-ken.com/?post_type=ka_ching&p=1467893?utm_source=ro_hdr&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=

Many of these apps are similarly named (Pocket Cash, White Cash, Golden Cash, Love Finance), have millions of downloads, and ratings of 4 stars and above. 

On 3 September, when I searched for ‘instant loan online app’ on the App Store, the first five results were KCPL Rupee, Credit+, Get Rupee, CASHe, and Buddy Loan, all of which were flagged between 25 August and 2 September as fraud loan apps by Lal. What is even more bizarre is that many of them also run ads inside both Google and Apple’s app stores.

“I’ve created an automated process that scans through the top 500 apps in India,” Lal told me, “and it helps me identify and investigate newly launched apps within four–five hours of launch, and report them if necessary.

“If I can identify these fakes with some code and manual checking, why can’t a company like Apple do the same?” 

Digital deception

For its part, a May 2024 update https://www.apple.com/newsroom/2024/05/app-store-stopped-over-7-billion-usd-in-potentially-fraudulent-transactions/ on Apple’s website says the company is committed to maintaining the integrity of its App Store; that it has rejected over 1.7 million app submissions that failed to meet its standards; and closed 374 million developer and customer accounts due to concerns over frauds and fake reviews worldwide. It also says that it has made efforts to enhance its review process. 

But my conversations with people with knowledge of the vetting process reveals a few loopholes and blindspots that unscrupulous actors exploit.

One way is by registering under the names of known businesses, which helps them bypass some parts of the vetting process, including the requirement for a DUNS (Data Universal Numbering System) number—a standard identifier for businesses issued by Dun and Bradstreet. Somewhat similar to a KYC for apps. 

Lal pointed to Pocket Cash (once trending at #11 in its category on the App Store, now banned) which was registered under the developer name of PMI Global Technologies, a US-based aerospace, defence, and industrial manufacturer that has nothing to do with the app. Pocket Cash was offering an interest rate of 25% for a six-month loan, targeted especially at people with bad credit scores. 

In many cases, the scammers use a “bait-and-switch” technique, where they submit outwardly harmless products for the initial review process (such as photo editors or puzzle games) and then later change them into gambling or instant-loan apps. People who have taken apps through Apple’s review process tell me that while Apple reviews aspects such as the name, description, logo, etc., for app updates, it cannot review changes to internal app features made through APIs (Application Program Interfaces are essentially tools and protocols that allow developers to easily integrate their software systems with others.) 

But given many such apps prominently mention loans and credit in their names and descriptions, the vetting process is clearly not as foolproof as one would like. 

And even removal from the app stores isn’t a complete solution, as Bidare notes.

“Even if an app is banned or delisted, users who have already downloaded it can still continue using it. The damage is often already done by the time these apps are discovered and removed.”

What we need instead is a multi-pronged approach, he says, including significant efforts to raise consumer education and awareness. 

“Red flags like quick timelines of five minutes, in which it is practically impossible to read important documents, lack of contact information, or unusual brand names should set off alarm bells. Thorough research and cross-checking with legitimate sources are essential to avoid falling victim to these scams.”

But with even legit loan apps gunning for faster disbursals in minutes, it has only become harder to distinguish the real from the fake. The RBI’s plan for a repository of authentic apps is a good start, but it’s a list that’s not easily available, according to Lal. And as Bidare points out, there is no comprehensive list or registry for the fake apps either. 

In a digital landscape where trust is the most valuable currency, it won’t be long before Apple’s failure to keep its app store clean could start losing it dollars.

What else caught our eye

RBI declines Canara Bank’s bid to establish a credit-card subsidiary

Canara Bank wants https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/industry/banking/finance/banking/rbi-rejects-canara-banks-credit-card-subsidiary-plan/articleshow/113202669.cms?from=mdr to transform its IT services subsidiary, Canbank Computer Services, into a standalone credit card subsidiary, much like State Bank of India’s SBI Card. But the Reserve Bank of India doesn’t seem very keen on the idea given concerns about rising unsecured loans. It doesn’t help that credit cards issued against fixed deposits as collateral are a minority of the total cards issued, making most credit cards unsecured loans.

Government to boost credit guarantees to small business by Rs 5 lakh crore in two years

The Indian government plans https://www.business-standard.com/finance/news/govt-aims-to-extend-additional-rs-5-trn-via-credit-guarantees-in-2-years-124091000878_1.html to extend credit guarantees to micro and small enterprises by an additional Rs 5 lakh crore (US$60 billion) through the Credit Guarantee Fund Trust for Micro and Small Enterprises (CGTMSE). According to the Additional Secretary and Development Commissioner for MSME, CGTMSE has already extended credit guarantees worth Rs 4 lakh crore (US$47 billion) over the past two years against just Rs 2.6 lakh crore (US$30 billion) over the previous twenty-two years.

RBI imposes penalty on Axis Bank and HDFC Bank

On Tuesday, the central bank put out a statement announcing https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/industry/banking/finance/banking/rbi-slaps-penalty-on-axis-bank-hdfc-bank/articleshow/113233844.cms?from=mdr a penalty of Rs 1.91 crore (US$227,000) on Axis Bank for multiple shortcomings, ranging from violations of the Banking Regulation Act to non-compliance with guidelines on deposit interest rates, unsecured agricultural loans, and KYC. HDFC Bank has also been charged a penalty amounting to Rs 1 crore (US$120,000) for non-compliance with guidelines concerning customer service, recovery agents deployed, and deposit interest rates.

CCI gives go-ahead for merger of Tata Motors Finance with Tata Capital

The Competition Commission of India (CCI) has given its approval https://www.business-standard.com/companies/news/cci-clears-proposed-merger-of-tata-motors-finance-into-tata-capital-124091001168_1.html for a merger between Tata Motors Finance Ltd, which finances automobile purchases, and Tata Capital, which is engaged in a wide range of financial services. The move is reportedly part of Tata Motors’ plans to divest from non-core businesses and allocate its resources towards automobile development and innovation.

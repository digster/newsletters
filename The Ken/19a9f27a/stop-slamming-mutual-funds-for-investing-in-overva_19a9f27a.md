Good Morning Ishan,
Shock! Outrage! And a general sense of sharp-tongued acrimony!
That was the general sentiment after more than 20 of India’s mutual-fund houses, including its biggest ones, joined the long list of anchor investors in Lenskart’s Rs 7,300 crore initial public offering—meaning these funds pledged to buy a significant portion of shares in the IPO before it opened to the public.
The public reaction was strong, to the extent that there were calls for Sebi to ban these entities from taking part in IPOs in the future. People who hold this belief figure that the companies going public in India this season have exorbitant valuations, so investors who use other people’s money to bet on them are breaching their fiduciary duty to their clients, the real investors.
They are, however, wrong.
Anand examined IPO data and fund performances from the beginning of 2021 until now, and found that some mutual funds that were anchor investors have managed to reap decent returns for their customers, even if the IPOs they invested in were costly.
Sure, some bets have backfired, but the numbers don’t lie. Anand unpacks his findings in detail in today’s story. [Check it out](https://the-ken.com/story/stop-slamming-mutual-funds-for-investing-in-overvalued-ipos/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter).

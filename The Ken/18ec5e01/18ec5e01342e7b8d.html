By demerging Madura Fashion, the retailer makes itself far less appealing to investors

Delivered to Ishan, Subscriber to The Ken.

Wed, 10 Apr 2024

The Spotlight by Anand Kalyanaraman
Finance Editor

Good Morning Ishan,

The “sacrifice” is a classic manoeuvre in chess—players sometimes intentionally give up their pieces to try and achieve a different or larger goal. Seetharaman’s sharp story today on apparel retailer Aditya Birla Fashion and Retail Ltd (ABFRL) reminded me of this bold but risky move.

Things don’t always go as planned and such gambits often fail. ABFRL must be hoping hard that its supreme sacrifice—of itself—does not go in vain. After all, this is its only shot at redemption.

Last week, ABFRL announced that it would demerge and list Madura Fashion & Lifestyle—the business that houses its best-selling brands like Allen Solly and Peter England. Essentially, the retailer is making itself even less appealing to investors than it already has been, for years now. But surprise surprise, the market loved the announcement—taking the ABFRL stock up 11% in a single day, compared with its mere 6% rise over the past five years. In contrast, the stock of competitor Trent, a Tata group company—had shot up 1,000% in this period. What gives?

ABFRL is doing what it is doing to fix an identity crisis that has been crippling it for a decade now. A string of not-so-great acquisitions, puzzling sales strategies, complex structures, and ghosts from the past have been haunting it. But will the bitter medicine now—letting go of its cash cow—be worse than the disease? Why is the market rooting for this? Find the answers here.

Desperate times, desperate measures

It’s not just ABFRL. In another striking example of desperate times calling for desperate measures, Wipro CEO Thierry Delaporte put in his papers last weekend. Frankly, many saw it coming.

The IT services major has been struggling for several quarters now—with weak financial metrics lagging peers, big attrition among senior staff, and a lacklustre stock performance. Wipro’s promoters—the Premjis—haven’t been happy, or so say news reports.

With this, Delaporte who took charge in July 2020 joins the list of many Wipro CEOs whose five-year tenures were cut short. Also, it is a setback to Wipro 2.0’s carefully engineered comeback trail, that The Ken had written about in May 2021.

“I would rather have a general who was lucky than one who was good,” famous Frenchman Napoleon Bonaparte once quipped. The stars weren’t on fellow Frenchman Delaporte’s side. Will the new CEO-designate Srinivas Pallia, a long-term company insider, have better luck? The Premjis and other Wipro investors will be watching closely.

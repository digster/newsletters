Good Morning Ishan,
 
In early March, well before India went into lockdown, I went hunting for hand sanitisers. 
 
At pharmacy after pharmacy, I was met with apologetic glances. Most had sold out over a week earlier, if not more. Incidentally, on 1 March, India only had a handful of Covid-19 cases. All in Kerala and many—if not all—were well on their way to recovery. Yet in Delhi, at the other end of the country, people had cleaned pharmacies out.
 
So scarce was sanitiser that WhatsApp forwards on how liquor could be used instead began doing the rounds. These messages have aged poorly, of course, as the subsequent nationwide lockdown put paid to access to alcohol altogether.
 
Since then, Covid-19 has washed over India. In city after city, the cases continue to pile up. Over 2,200 have died, some 70,000-odd cases have been documented. 
 
These are terrifying times. However, go to any pharmacy now and you will probably find sanitiser. In multiple colours. From multiple brands. Some with aloe vera extract, even.
 
Because, like selling shovels in a gold rush, Indian businesses realised the opportunity the sanitiser business presented during the pandemic. The government, for its part, expedited licensing for would-be manufacturers. But though this has made for an abundance of sanitiser, not all sanitisers are created equal. Some brands embrace quality standards and cutting-edge tech; others cut corners in the quest for a quick buck.
 
India's authorities, notoriously inept at regulating pharmaceutical quality at the best of times, are not able to identify the good from the bad in the sanitiser deluge.

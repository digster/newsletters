“It’s like you are building a house [and] your neighbour comes and says, ‘keep aside a room for me,’ even though he hasn’t contributed a single rupee towards building it,” says a senior executive of one of the global telecom equipment vendors.
The “room” he refers to are a few bits that the TSDSI, or Telecommunications Standards Development Society, India, wants global telecom standards body 3GPP to reserve for a new standard it has developed. Called Low Mobility Large Cell (LMLC), the standard aims to maximise the transmission range of telecom radio signals over large distances, even if the speeds aren’t very high. This is ideal for large parts of rural India where low-speed, low-cost coverage is much better than high-speed, high-cost coverage.
But 3GPP is dominated by telecoms vendors. And it is refusing to build and reserve an empty room for India as part of the brand new 5G mansion that will be rolled out later this year. “You have worked in isolation, outside of our framework’,” says 3GPP, in the words of the executive quoted earlier. Of course, he refuses to go on the record. Nobody wants to mess with governments around telecom standards.
Nokia, Ericsson, and Qualcomm are the ones quietly opposing the inclusion of India’s proposed standard. Because there’s no Indian equipment company big enough to wield commercial clout internationally, it's largely the Indian government and TSDSI on the defending side.
But, as Seema reports in her behind-the-scenes story today, some vendors are already hedging their bets by quietly including support for India’s new standard into their new 5G chipsets. Nobody will confirm or deny anything, afraid to give away their cards. Who are these vendors? What chances does LMLC stand? Will India prevail?

There’s a game I play with my friends called “monkey’s paw”, inspired by WW Jacobs’ suspenseful short story of the same name. Here’s how it goes: one of us makes a wish, and then the next person assumes the role of a devious genie, granting the wish in such a way that it backfires.

When I read today’s story, I couldn’t help but think that brands getting onto platforms like Blinkit, Zepto, and Instamart seem to be trapped in exactly such a game.

After all, it’s an obvious wish for any brand to want a piece of the quick-commerce pie. But once they’re on board, the unit economics turn out to be brutal. Platform commissions, advertising expenditure, and merchandising—it all adds up to nearly zero margins.

“In the end, you realise you’re running a business to pay commissions to aggregators, not to make money for yourself,” an executive from a beverage brand told Nuha, the author of today’s story.

Of course, these platforms unlock a massive pool of customers. Then again, red-hot demand means nothing without the capacity to provide supply, as some have discovered.

That’s all to say brands are discovering that walking the tightrope of opportunity isn’t exactly sustainable. Nuha has a story that’s all about this double-edged sword and just as sharp.

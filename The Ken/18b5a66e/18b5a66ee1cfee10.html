When KPMG recently asked over 1,300 global CEOs about their post-pandemic work policy, only one in four said hybrid work would continue.

I don’t know if Tata Consultancy Services (TCS) chief executive K Krithivasan was among the respondents. But if he had been, he would certainly not be part of that 25%.

Earlier in October, the IT-services giant asked its 615,000 employees to show up to the office on all weekdays by the end of the year.

After being told in mid-2020 that they would only be expected to spend a quarter of their time in the office by 2025, this is not what a lot of them wanted to hear.

Unsurprisingly, a hiring agency has seen a 4X spike in the number of TCS employees looking to find a new gig in just the past month.

Companies around the world have bemoaned the transactional nature of hybrid work. TCS, in its latest annual report, said such workplace practices “badly impacted” employee engagement.

So Nathan’s timely and well-argued story is not about why TCS did this. Rather, it’s about what this policy means for India’s largest private employer.

Like most big social issues of our times, education calls for simple solutions. Ones that yield meaningful improvements. Because far too many edtech companies have burnt far too much money doing far too many things, and far too soon. It’s a mouthful, but true.
So when Byju’s, India’s largest edtech company—valued at $3.6B, projected revenue at $200 million by March 2019—came brandishing the ‘fun’ factor in their learning apps, students and parents paid attention. Unsurprisingly, it has 2 million paid and 33 million total registered users till date.
In January, Byju’s acquired Palo Alto-based edtech company Osmo, for $120 million. Now, with a multi-product play, Byju’s is looking at a global launch later this year. And, of course, is raising more money. The company is on a high and it seems nothing can burst this VC-funded bubble.
Except real and long-term value, says Olina in today’s story. Because public chatrooms, WhatsApp and Facebook groups are agog with irate parents demanding a refund for their subscription fees. Why?
This question is worth asking because the stated objective of Byju’s is to make learning fun; deeply conceptual even. Marks is not its preferred metric. But this is at odds with what experts have found to be at the heart of the slick editing and 3D visuals in Byju’s modules. It’s the tired and tested DNA of exam prep coaching. Learning, for all we know, goes out the window.
Olina has done some solid homework in analysing how the learning app is created and if the “hacky” way of teaching works. “10% of students are motivated to study on their own. It’s the other 90% we need to focus on. Edtech apps don’t understand issues of boredom, focus and attention. Without addressing those, fear or fun as inputs won’t work,” says one expert who studied the app for The Ken.

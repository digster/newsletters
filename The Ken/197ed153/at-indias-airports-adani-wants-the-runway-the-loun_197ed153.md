Good Morning Ishan,
The Adani Group first came for India’s airports. Now it’s coming for the stores that dot them.
In October 2024, we [said](https://the-ken.com/long_and_short/the-retailer-taking-shape-in-adani-enterprises-airports-business/) there’s a bona fide retailer lurking within Adani’s airports business. Aakriti’s story today only proves that further.
The latest trigger may be airport-lounge aggregator Dreamfolks accusing Adani and GMR, without naming them, of pressuring credit-card issuers like ICICI and Axis to abandon aggregators in favour of themselves. But Adani has been shaking up the way flyers spend their time and money for a while now. (Adani and GMR, between them, operate five of India’s 10 busiest airports.)
At the top privately run airports, revenue from retail stores, eateries, parking, advertising, and forex is almost two-thirds of the total.
What’s increasingly apparent is that the available real estate inside India’s airports—once teeming with duty-free, duty-paid kiosks, pop-up stores, and franchise outlets—is being slowly “squeezed out”.
“If it’s not owned by the airport operator, it’s being replaced by something that is. Sometimes, it’s a takeover. Sometimes, a replica,” writes Aakriti. Don’t miss [this important story](https://the-ken.com/story/at-indias-airports-adani-wants-the-runway-the-lounge-the-latte-and-the-lipstick/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter).
Headlines have been abuzz the last few days with news of Jane Street, the US-based hedge fund giant, being banned by Sebi for its massive manipulation of India’s stock market and derivatives trades. The Ken [broke this story](https://the-ken.com/story/the-mystery-fund-playing-god-and-wreaking-havoc-on-the-stock-market/) in January, with [a follow-up](https://the-ken.com/story/is-jane-street-the-all-powerful-hidden-hand-in-indias-stock-market/) in June. Reading between the lines of Sebi’s report has thrown up the role—or lack thereof—of the NSE as the first-line regulator in this whole saga. The NSE consistently failed to flag the big positions being taken by Jane Street as far back as 2023.
In this week’s Long and Short, Anand explores why the stock exchange became the watchdog that didn’t bark.

Good Morning Ishan,

When Phonepe decided to get into the business of helping online merchants accept different payment options last year, its reputation preceded it.

Unified payments interface (UPI) is India’s most popular payment method, and almost every second UPI transaction is carried out through Phonepe.

Naturally, the company—owned by Walmart—is making the incumbents in payment aggregation quake in their boots. Phonepe now processes nearly US$600 million every month. Razorpay, among the three largest aggregators, took three years to get close to that number.

With large customers such as telecom giant Bharti Airtel and train-ticket-booking platform IRCTC, Phonepe is undoubtedly a force to be reckoned with in payment aggregation. But, as Arundhati’s story makes clear, there’s much more to Phonepe’s incredible run than its UPI dominance.

Air India Express fires and reinstates striking staff—all in two days

In the constant tug-of-war between airlines and their employees, you can never be sure who has the upper hand.

Last year, Akasa Air sued over 40 pilots who had quit without serving their six-to-12-month notice period, some to join rival Air India Express. Air India’s chief executive then said the government’s rules on the notice period were not enforceable.

This week, it was Air India Express’s turn to crack the whip. After around 100 members of its cabin crew called in sick, leading to over 90 flights being cancelled, the airline reportedly fired a quarter of them on 8 May.

Air India Express employees are said to be unhappy about the carrier’s merger with AIX Connect, formerly Air Asia India. The Air India management would have hoped that its latest move would cure its staff of their illness.

In fact, it did, with the striking employees agreeing to return to work late last evening after the airline agreed to reinstate the terminated workers. It may appear that Air India Express has got its desired outcome. But its employees have forced its hand.

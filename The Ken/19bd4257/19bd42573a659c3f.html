Who doesn’t love Esops? Well, turns out, shareholders of publicly listed companies are not fans. Especially if the strike price—or the fixed price an employee pays to buy the company’s shares—is low.

Understandable, since a low strike price means employees are getting the shares for cheaper than investors are.

Consider Eternal, which has chosen to maintain a strike price of Re 1, even four years after listing. This means that employees with vested shares get to buy them at Re 1, while all other investors have to defer to the price the stock is currently trading at, which is around Rs 290.

Naturally, employees love this. But, a lot of times, investors end up pushing back.

And a lot of times, these investors end up pushing back. For instance, the blockbuster stock-market debutant of 2025, Groww, is encountering its first reckoning as a listed company. Proxy-advisory firm IiAS is recommending shareholders to vote against the company’s Esop policy for “lack of clarity” with respect to its exercise price and long vesting periods of 10–20 years.

This is the dilemma that listed companies find themselves in. Should they keep their strike price low and maintain their image as an “employee-friendly” company, or should they bow to investors’ demands?

In today’s story, Arundhati takes a long, hard look at how companies resolve this catch-22 and the effects it ends up having.

There is no revelation here barring the fact that what is well known in education, banking and investment circles has now been made public for everyone to read. It’s a bold move and K-12 businesses were considered not fit for an IPO for the longest time owing to the concern that the profits will be there for everyone to see and invite potential govt and regulatory action given that fee levels and raises are theoretically capped in most states (education being a state subject).

Contrary to the notion that schools prior to entry of PEs were operating on similar model but with a lot of hanky-panky, a number of larger school chains had exactly this same set-up with exactly the same mechanics and many of them were clean with nearly 100% white money. I evaluated an LBO financing 8 years back for one of these chains which was even better – a completely asset-light model with the entire real estate financed and owned by independent 3rd parties – lending higher scalability to the ManCo from an investor’s perspective.

There is one aspect here that puzzles me. The OpCo here is a trust/society which has trustees/members whose appointment and removal cannot be at-will and no company can “own” a trust or society – the way it is mentioned here. For a trust, trustee replacement needs approval from Charity Commissioner of the relevant jurisdiction. As an investor, you need to replace existing trustees with your own guys and ensure they take instructions from you rather than act independently. The quantum and mode of payment of their remuneration could be a tricky affair. Here Marian society’s heads are essentially the erstwhile promoters – what happens if there is a fallout between them and the PE at some stage in the future?

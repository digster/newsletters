Insurance is supposed to be boring. The realm of risk-averse, number-crunching actuaries. At least, that’s what customers have been led to believe. 
 
Then there is LIC, or The Life Insurance Corporation of India, India’s government-owned insurer that dominates the insurance sector by virtue of its 70% market share. It’s huge - earning close to $44 billion in premium income in just the last year and with total investments totalling $365 billion.
 
Given that millions of Indians trust LIC to protect themselves and their families against disease, disability and death, you’d expect LIC to be akin to a vault. Unbreakable, secure, dependable.
 
In reality, it is increasingly looking like a cookie jar. And the greedy child with impulse-control issues? The Indian government.
 
On 1 August, LIC acquired a 51% stake in one of the worst-performing banks in India, IDBI. Why? The government wanted it to. 
 
During the past 15 years, various governments have dipped into it to for a few cookies (cookies being a few dozens to a few hundreds of millions of dollars) every time they had sugar pangs. An initial public offering of a government-owned company not going well? Cookie jar. Not enough takers for the government's stake when it divests its shares in companies? Cookie jar. 
 
LIC today owns a stake higher than 1% in 400 Indian companies. This includes stakes in 31 banks, of which 11 are on the central bank’s radar for breach of regulatory requirements. 
 
The cookies are everywhere.

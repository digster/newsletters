Payments and e-commerce companies have for some time been engaged in an elaborate digital roleplay. 

Over the last few years, Amazon, Flipkart, and Swiggy have all launched their own versions of a payments play. And payments companies like Phonepe and Paytm* ventured into hyperlocal commerce. These are two very different sets of companies with very different business realities, yet united by one sentiment—how to make their millions of users count in more ways than one. 

So far, the jury has been out on who has had better luck in building out their alter egos. 

Last week, however, Phonepe’s own verdict arrived. India’s largest payments company announced it was shutting down its nearly two-year-old quick-commerce bet, Pincode.

The IPO-bound company’s CEO, Sameer Nigam, said that operating yet another B2C quick-commerce app was distracting from its core mission of helping its offline merchants improve growth and operational efficiency in their existing business. It will instead focus on B2B tools for merchants.  

For Phonepe, it became apparent that pursuing quick commerce was a wasteful endeavour at a time when the likes of Blinkit, Swiggy, and Zepto were gobbling up the market. 

Pincode’s revenue was merely about Rs 10 crore but suffered losses of Rs 280 crore from this business, according to its annual report for the 2025 financial year. 

“You need nothing less than $2–3 billion in investment if you need to crack a large-scale business in the country,” said a quick-commerce executive.

Phonepe is no stranger to cash burn, but the question is: what will it come at the cost of? 

Merchants of India

When Phonepe launched Pincode in April 2023, it was its big push into e-commerce. It was a natural extension. The company already had millions of merchants—what if it could do more than just enable payments? It could be a winning proposition both for itself and for its merchants.

Phonepe will know that you are buying medicines every month. And Phonepe will know you are buying Rs 314 of medicines every month from the same pharmacy. Phonepe will get Pincode and say, “Pincode, go get this pharmacy on board. Here is a customer I have who is buying Rs 314 of medicines every month from this store.”

Will Flipkart become Phonepe before Phonepe becomes Flipkart? The Ken

Moreover, the digital infrastructure for e-commerce was all laid out. 

Phonepe became the payments leader by building on government-backed digital infrastructure like UPI. With Pincode, it hoped to undertake hyperlocal commerce by building once again on the government-backed Open Network for Digital Commerce (ONDC)—an initiative aimed at democratising e-commerce.  

Phonepe didn’t leave anything to chance with this venture. Pincode was given full independence, operating like a startup within a startup, as The Ken wrote earlier. 

Having been in the right place at the right time around the launch of UPI, it wanted to make sure it could capture lightning a second time, too. It put one of its top leaders on the job. Vivek Lohcheb—who earlier headed the merchants business for Phonepe—was made the CEO of Pincode, and it was up and running in just six months. 

The initial results were encouraging. It was seeing 5,000 orders a day in its pilot in Bengaluru, so it doubled down on enabling more categories like medicines, food, electronics, and home décor from neighbourhood shops, across more cities.

But within a year, it saw that orders were not rolling in. ONDC’s promise of democratising e-commerce did not have legs. 

By 2024, Pincode cut down categories it offered via ONDC and earlier this year started 10-minute deliveries. Somewhere, it may have believed that its inability to nail e-commerce was an ONDC problem. 

On paper, cracking quick-commerce could have potentially unlocked double the revenue for Phonepe, estimates the executive quoted above, as it would allow the company to earn 5–10% of a transaction as commission. “There are few categories that can offer such a prospect,” he added.

But if it believed it could solve quick-commerce by relying on its merchants, it’s also those same merchants who stand in the way of a payments company solving it. 

Companies like Blinkit have shown that the only efficient way to run hyperlocal commerce is by building out their own dark stores. Only then can platforms have tight control on selection, quality, availability—the holy trinity of 10-minute deliveries 

But going down the dark-store path was never an option for Pincode.

Dark stores would have been seen as competitors to the local kirana stores that are Phonepe’s merchants. Building them would have created massive conflict with its existing merchant network, endangering its core Rs 6,300 crore payments business. 

That meant Pincode was reliant simply on facilitating online deliveries from offline stores—stores for whom facilitating online sales was never as big a priority. They don’t care about things like fill rates or ensuring the availability of products at a certain time of day.

“It’s impossible to get a local retailer to stock items at 7 p.m., even though that’s the time someone comes back home and wants to place an order for groceries.” 

Hyperlocal executive

Pincode’s closure couldn’t have been timed better, as the quick-commerce battle is only intensifying. 

A model that has so far relied on relentless fundraising is nearing its limits and companies will soon have to decide how long they can keep absorbing steep losses, Blinkit Chief Executive Officer Albinder Dhindsa said in an interview.

Blinkit CEO Warns India’s Quick Commerce Bubble May Be Close to Bursting, Bloomberg

But for now, it seems like e-commerce companies have had better luck sticking with their fintech bets. 

Flipkart, after all, did incubate Phonepe, which now holds a 45% market share in UPI. Now its up-and-coming, one-year-old UPI app, Super.money has climbed its way to be among the top five UPI apps with 250 million transactions—even though that’s still just 1% of total UPI volume. 

What also helps e-commerce companies is their realistic expectations of what they want out of their fintech bet. For the likes of Amazon and Flipkart, the reason they get into payments is more strategic. “If I’m able to control a bunch of payments, I’ll be able to control my costs as well as make sure that more of the money is flowing through me for my purchase,” explained the quick-commerce executive.

That’s why companies like Amazon keep investing in their payments arm, even though their miniscule 0.4% UPI market share after six years of launch would, for many, be a sign to give up.

For now, the incumbent will have to accept that—just as no other payments company has been able to make a dent in its UPI market share—when it ventures into newer territories, it is dealing with the ‘Phonepe’ of that category.

Good Morning Ishan,
I’ve lost count of the stories discussing Tesla’s imminent entry into India’s nascent electric-car market. Now, Tesla is reportedly keen on finding a local partner to make rooftop solar panels, too.
The news was out on the same day as the Indian government’s announcement that it would spend US$9 billion to power 10 million homes with subsidised rooftop solar panels.
At first glance, it looks like Tesla has timed its India move very well. But clean energy is not the red-hot business it once was in the country. The shares of Renew Energy Global, one of India’s largest renewables companies, have been languishing on the Nasdaq.
Scaling up has been particularly hard in the rooftop-solar segment, which accounts for just 15% of India’s solar-power capacity. And within rooftop solar, residences contribute just one-fourth. Developers are eschewing terraces of homes and offices in favour of large expanses of land where they can install hundreds of thousands of panels. This allows them to cater exclusively to large, industrial customers.
That’s why I find Tesla’s supposed eagerness to venture into the country’s rooftop-solar sector quite counterintuitive.
Much like my colleague Vanita’s story about why Indian tech startups are not fretting over losing people in droves.
When Paytm, Nykaa, and Zomato went public in 2021, their attrition rate was roughly 20–35%. But last year, it went up to 30–65%. Mature startups like them are not just learning to live with it but are actually leaning into it. If you want to know how, head right to the [story](https://the-ken.com/story/how-startups-stopped-worrying-about-high-attrition-and-learned-to-live-with-it/?utm_source=daily&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter).
Or if you’re in the mood for a classic underdog-takes-on-powerful-incumbent tale, we have that too. In fact, Katrina’s [story](https://the-ken.com/story/indrive-and-angkas-threaten-grabs-grip-on-philippine-roads/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter) from the Philippines has not one but two underdogs: a local bike-taki company, Angkas, and American cab aggregator Indrive.
They want to chip away at Grab’s 90% market share in ride-hailing in the Philippines. Five other companies have tried taking on Grab and bitten the dust. But Angkas and Indrive are looking for a different result by targeting the blind spots common in a near-monopoly.

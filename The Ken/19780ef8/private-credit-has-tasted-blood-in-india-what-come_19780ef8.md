Good Morning Ishan,
If you’ve been keeping your ear to the ground, you would have noticed major private-credit deals getting sealed week after week.
Here are a couple of cases: Deutsche Bank led India’s largest ever deal of this sort in May, funneling $3.4 billion to Shapoorji Pallonji Group to refinance existing debt. And then there was Manipal Education and Medical Group, which took on $600 million in debt from KKR just days later.
India’s private-credit industry has $25 billion in assets under management, equivalent to 0.6% of the country’s GDP. It was a lucrative line of business, but conditions have changed quickly over the past 12 months.
Now, there’s tighter competition—the Reserve Bank of India is encouraging commercial banks to lend more to companies. At the same time, interest rates are falling. In the first three months of 2024, three in four private-credit deals yielded returns of over 18%. Only one in four hit that mark a year later.
Even though there are founders seeking to borrow so they can raise their stakes—those of Inmobi, Zepto, and Zetwerk are reportedly looking for capital—they aren’t the best fit for private credit given the absence of suitable collateral.
All of these point to a challenging future for private-credit lenders in India. Seetharaman dived into the data to explain the developments in today’s [story](https://the-ken.com/story/private-credit-has-tasted-blood-in-india-what-comes-next/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter).
Didn’t know Bata was not an Indian company? Join the club. The Switzerland-based company has made a name for itself in India over almost a century, selling value-for-money shoes and sandals squarely targeted at the middle class. But in the last five years, it has been struggling, its stock down 10% while the Nifty 500 index has jumped 170%. Unable to successfully expand into premium products like its rival Metro Brands, and squeezed from the bottom by cut-price retailers like Zudio, Bata is caught in a tight spot. And now Sandeep Kataria, Bata Group’s CEO, has resigned. Coincidence?
In this week’s Long and Short, Seetharaman delves into the footwear retailer’s unique plight.

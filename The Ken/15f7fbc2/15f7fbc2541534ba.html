Fortune favours the bold. But if you’re a startup, you’d be forgiven for thinking it favours the funded. Such is the sheer power venture capital and venture capitalists are thought to hold over startup fortunes.
 
It’s easy to forget sometimes that venture capital, for all of its cool confidence and know-it-all cockiness, is governed by the same laws of success and failure that startups are. Take Orios Venture Partners for instance.
 
One of India’s first domestically-raised venture capital funds, Orios has had a rotten streak of luck. In just the first three years of its existence, it has had to write off over 25% of its entire corpus, leaving it with rather daunting mathematical odds to even be considered an average performer. Over half of that write-off is on account of a single investment—budget hotel rooms startup Zo Rooms.
 
Ashish’s story today focuses on how the unravelling of just one “exit”—Zo Rooms’ acquisition by industry leader Oyo Rooms—has left a massive crater in VC fund Orios’ fortunes. One that won’t be easy to recover from.
 
Venture capital is a game of perception as much as it is of perseverance, luck or conviction. Which is probably why the last time we’d written about Orios Ventures, we got sued for defamation by Rehan Yar Khan, its co-founder. We’re still fighting that case in the Honourable Bombay High Court.
 
I wish Mr Khan the best of luck for the remaining life of his fund and sincerely hope that he doesn’t see independent journalism as the enemy of his business the second time around too.

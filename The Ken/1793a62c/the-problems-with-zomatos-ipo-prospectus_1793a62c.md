Good Morning Ishan,
If a public listing is the moment a startup comes of age, the filing of a draft red herring prospectus (DRHP) serves as the proverbial coming-of-age ritual. The DRHP signifies the shedding of a startup's cloak of private-market secrecy, showing the wider public what lies under its hood.
These disclosures can prime a company for a mammoth IPO or doom it to crash and burn, as WeWork found out the hard way in 2019. Once WeWork allowed the world an insight into its inner workings—making public its mammoth losses—its IPO was scrapped. Almost overnight, the co-working startup's valuation was slashed from $47 billion to just $8 billion.
With the stakes this high, the draft red herring prospectus is as much an art as it is a science. It is about presenting the best possible picture of a company, while also carefully obscuring its rougher edges. It's akin to a magic trick, with a heavy dose of misdirection aimed at concealing realities that could scare public market investors away.
Last week, Zomato crossed this final hurdle in anticipation of a $1.1 billion IPO in India later this year. It, too, was careful about the metrics it wanted to highlight. Average order value, for instance, had spiked to almost Rs 400 ($5.4) from Rs 278 ($3.7) at the start of the Covid-19 pandemic last March. Its contribution margin—the difference between the revenue and the variable costs for each delivery order—has also turned positive.
Zomato was keen to sell the narrative that, like its US peer DoorDash, which had a successful listing in December 2020, the pandemic had served as rocket fuel for its business. But as with any good magic trick, there's a fair amount of sleight of hand involved, as a closer look at the company's numbers bears out.

India wants to build a sovereign AI stack. But it turns out sovereignty is expensive. 

So last week, the government reportedly shortlisted seven companies—including foreign majors like Amazon Web Services, Oracle, and Google Cloud—for the second round of GPU tenders under its IndiaAI Mission. The idea is to scale up the country’s compute infrastructure, one hyperscaler at a time. Which is a little ironic, but also, you know, practical.

Anyway, while Big Tech is jumping through formal procurement hoops to rent India some sovereignty, the actual job of building the sovereign large language model (LLM) was handed out a bit more informally.

To Sarvam AI.

The original plan was to fund multiple startups, encourage competition, and avoid picking winners. The government would diversify its bets, hedge against failure, and support an ecosystem. But… it’s just ended up picking Sarvam. 

One startup. One model. 

Five other shortlisted companies—Gan AI, Gnani AI, and Soket AI Labs, Corover, and Krutrim—are still waiting for a call even though Sarvam’s selection was announced last month, according to two people familiar with the developments. 

Sarvam, for its part, is making it work. It received Rs 220 crore worth of GPU credits—not exactly cash, but for which the government somehow gets an equity stake. And it’s now using that as an endorsement to raise another $50–100 million in venture funding, three other people in the AI ecosystem tell me. 

After China unveiled Deepseek, India needed to show it was also “AI-capable”. It needed a hero of its own, one that could maybe even export some AI swagger. For its part, Sarvam seems to have wanted some sovereign glow. The Mission had some to spare. 

So now, they’re in this together—India’s flagship AI initiative and a two-year-old startup with venture capital from Lightspeed, Peak XV, and Khosla Ventures. It’s a nice story, on the surface anyway. Until someone asks whether a sovereign AI model should be built by a company backed by non-sovereign capital and running closed-source code.

Marriage of convenience

The usual rationale that’s given for any sovereign AI model is this: protect a country’s national interests by securing the technology behind the model, as well as the data it’s trained on. That way, the government doesn’t have to rely on systems that come from other countries. Above all, it shows that a nation is “AI-capable”, and can hold its own against other countries and their LLMs.

It’s hard to tell how far the India AI Mission has imbibed this definition, though.

“The call for proposals was announced very reactively after [Chinese LLM] Deepseek was announced,” said one AI policy professional. “And the timelines were so short that their best selection would be someone who’d already built up those capabilities. Sarvam is the natural top choice.” 

For context, Deepseek was announced on 20 January and the call for foundation model proposals went out on 31 January.

This is in contrast to the India AI Mission’s Rs 10,000 crore GPU grant project, for which tenders were opened last year. The process there has been much slower and has given room for a lot more companies to show their capabilities.

But since the call for foundation model proposals wasn’t an official tender-submission process, it’s been a lot easier for the IndiaAI Mission to avoid transparency on what’s going on behind the scenes—hence the applications of the other shortlisted companies still being in limbo.

Officially, the Mission’s stance is that more companies will be selected in due time. Indeed, in an interview with Economic Times, IT Minister Ashwini Vaishnaw said that the government would have three foundation models by the end of the year.

But for now, in practice at least, critics say Sarvam appears to have become the “preferred” partner of the Indian government for generative AI applications. Whatever that may portend for the future of India’s AI efforts, there are one or two complications for Sarvam.

Walking a fine line

Right from its inception in 2023, Sarvam has based its credibility on its tech chops—that is, its ability to undertake various parts of the model-building process. In a country where AI talent is rare and dear, they’ve managed to put together a team that’s capable of not just fine-tuning models, but also pre-training and post-training them—the three most important steps involved in building models from the ground up.

In fact, Sarvam is one of the few AI startups in India with the capability to pre-train its own models. They released their first model built from the ground up, Sarvam 1, in October 2024.

But the company hasn’t always been consistent in terms of its business model and priorities.

It started out as a pure-play model builder, then switched to more services-oriented operations. Now, it’s somewhere in between. Its first models were open-source, but it then started building closed-source models for both its clients and itself. As one AI professional put it, “it’s a VC-funded research lab.”

And when it announced its partnership with the government to build a closed-source model, many were miffed.

“There’s no clarity on why the company was selected,” said an AI founder. “It’s not possible to tell if they just showed a fancy demo or if due diligence was actually done. There’s no transparency.” They also claim that Sarvam may be chosen by the government more or less exclusively for future contracts.

But Sarvam is actually in no place to bid for government contracts. 

As a pre-revenue company that was established in 2023, it fulfills neither the revenue criteria nor the age requirement that most government tenders ask for. Of course, this does not preclude it from being subcontracted by an official contractor who gets the contract.

More importantly, though, the company is actively trying to build revenue, and this requires it to be as widely acceptable as possible to potential customers. But tying up with the government comes with its own set of perception problems, note people in the segment, and prospective technology partners may fear government strings that impact Sarvam’s autonomy. Being funded by the government may also make it difficult for the company to find an exit outside of India.

Big fish, small pond

Some in the AI community see all the limelight around Sarvam as a distraction from the main issue facing AI startups in India—a lack of good-quality data. They also note that how good Sarvam’s models are is a question still up for debate.

But there are also many who believe that Sarvam is the only one that could’ve possibly gotten funding among all of India’s AI startups. 

“India is simply not an innovative startup ecosystem—there’s barely any technical innovations that have come out of this country in the last 15 years,” said an AI professional familiar with the landscape. This is reflected in the kind of AI investments that VCs make in the country too, they added.

“They’ll fund you if you’re an application-based startup, but not if you want to build the next OpenAI.”

It’s part of the reason why the government wants to put money into Indian AI startups as well, said two people familiar with the industry. Given private investors are unwilling to bet on Indian deeptech or AI startups, the government has decided to put its money where its mouth is.

But if it’s the government’s aim to strengthen the startup ecosystem of India, Sarvam’s selection doesn’t make sense—it already has $53 million in funding. Unless the decision has as much to do with bolstering the Mission’s own credibility as it is to fund the AI startup.

Whatever the tangled intentions involved, though, Sarvam is doing its best to stay relevant to the government. In March, it tied up with the Unique Identification Authority of India (UIDAI), which manages the Aadhaar identification service. And it’s currently hiring for a go-to market strategy role that prioritises military experience—a sign that it may be eyeing the defense market.

But the sole selection of the company by the Mission (thus far, at least) is also being seen in the industry as an anticompetitive move for other developers and companies, with critics claiming it will effectively lock Sarvam in as the partner of choice for the government. And that’s impacted Sarvam’s credibility among a few in the AI tech ecosystem.

For now, though, the IndiaAI Mission is getting its model—or three, or five; we’ll have to see. And Sarvam’s getting a shot in the arm. But the rest of India’s AI space is still waiting for the Mission’s promises to materialise.

Good Morning Ishan,
“Flipkart eyes more acquisitions, in talks with Swiggy...
...and UrbanClap...
...and UrbanLadder…
...and Zomato”
Thus ran headlines in India’s two largest business dailies last week. I was left scratching my head. These are seemingly unrelated sectors, and the proposed investments/acquisitions did not immediately make strategic sense to Flipkart.
But e-commerce is evolving faster than most can keep pace with. It doesn’t just mean retail anymore—insurance and financial services, auto sales, government services, travel… the list is growing. It’s certainly not an easy business to be in if one wants to grow, remain trendy and be profitable.
(I remember having a conversation on this last year with India’s biotech czarina, Kiran Mazumdar-Shaw. She'd said that she didn’t quite understand e-commerce companies and their sharply rising valuations. Perhaps she was being modest, but it was good to learn that someone like her scratches her head too, even if occasionally.)
Anyhow, back to Flipkart. Why is it devoting so much time focusing on peripheral acquisitions when its own core business is arguably yet to reach product-market fit?
Who better than my colleague Sumanth to break it down for us. He has a striking analogy: zugzwang. If you are not a chess player, it’s a position where one is compelled to make a move even though any possible move worsens one’s position.
His line of argument: “For many years now, Flipkart’s public narrative has been framed as a battle—a battle of attrition, a battle for market share, a battle against Amazon… It is tempting to believe that Flipkart’s current position is a result of this battle royale with Amazon, but the fact of the matter is that it has little do with a direct competitor. Flipkart’s predicament is almost entirely its own doing and is a result of what can be termed the 'Curse of Capital'.”

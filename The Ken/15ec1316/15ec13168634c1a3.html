Good Morning Ishan,
 
I’ve not been to China, but they say you can’t get through a day in the country without using an Alibaba or a Tencent app.
 
Such is the hold of the two mightiest companies over the Chinese, who, it turns out, have cut down on several things in their daily life to spend time online. Add Baidu, and the trio, BAT, generated $30 billion in cash in FY2017 alone.
 
That’s a lot of money. After decidedly changing behaviour in the domestic market—the mobile share of e-commerce in China grew from 12% to 75% in last 3 years—the cash is now flowing into other markets. A lot of that has already come into India with more on its way. Both Alibaba and Tencent are doing well and their money has staying power, even as a new acronym is emerging in China—ATM (M for Xiaomi).
 
Already, Tencent and Alibaba have put their stakes in the ground in India; any internet business deal worth its name has to cross their doors. But are they just writing cheques? How is Alibaba different from Tencent in India? What has Baidu been up to after setting its office in Gurugram?
 
We don’t hear from these Chinese companies so often because they have a rule—to not talk to the media, and rather let their investee companies do all the talking. Which didn’t surprise Ashish when he set out to report the story, though it made his task a tad harder. To tell a story of what BAT looks like in a time when all are racing to grab that fundamental raw material of the internet: user attention.
 
In pitching the story, Ashish’s point was, “These companies are not sufficiently understood in India.” After reading his account, you’ll agree with him. He’s packed in quite a lot for you.

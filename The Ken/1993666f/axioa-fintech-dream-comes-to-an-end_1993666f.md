A weekly newsletter about how finance is getting supercharged by tech in India, and how you can make money work for you.
Good Morning Ishan,
Last week, Amazon acquired a lender that was once a fintech darling for $200 million in cash—one of the e-commerce giant’s largest buys. Amazon had already invested $20 million in Axio (formerly called Capital Float), but it’s now doubled down on its bet by outright buying the fintech firm.
At a time when there is radio silence among fintech lenders in India, the deal may seem like a good outcome. But this is a company that had raised nearly $200 million in equity from the likes of Peak XV (which owned a 10.7% stake), Elevation (13.8%), and Ribbit Capital (8.9%), with about $670 million in debt. Last valued at $200 million, Amazon’s acquisition of the firm at this price is an unmemorable exit for its venture-capital investors and employees, especially since this was a bet that investors had pinned a lot of hopes on.
When it was set up in 2013, Capital Float was among the first handful of fintech lenders that wanted to use alternative data to lend, painting an efficient picture of its in-house models through which credit decisions would be taken with minimal human intervention. VCs believed this was a bet that would earn them a handsome multiple on their investment.
Fast forward a decade and hardly any of these hopes have become a reality. Zest Money, fast running out of money, was sold to DMI Finance in a fire sale. Similarly hard pressed for cash, Lendingkart was sold to Fullerton at Rs 252 crore ($28.5 million). And now, there’s Axio.
Some of their troubles, at least, began with a regulatory crackdown by the banking regulator. Alarmed at the pace at which unsecured lending was growing (a trend at least partly driven by fintech), the Reserve Bank of India came up with successive rules starting 2021 that clamped down on some of fintech’s favourite levers, like the FLDG (or [first-loss default guarantee](https://blog.setu.co/articles/what-on-earth-is-an-fldg)).
These were arrangements where fintechs were trusted to find good, credit-worthy customers for their lender partners, but in the case of defaults by such borrowers, the fintechs would cover at least some of the loss, thus protecting the actual lender.
Thanks to the equity capital they had raised, many fintechs showed great capacity to stomach losses, and so they eagerly entered into such deals—guaranteeing anywhere from 20% to even the entire amount of the loan.
In 2023, though, the RBI came down hard on FLDG, mandating that only 5% of losses could be covered like this.
And sure, you can perhaps blame the crackdown for their misfortunes, but it was the thirst for massive growth that led fintechs to chase partners at such cost.
Axio burst on to the scene when e-commerce was on a tear, so it targeted online sellers on these marketplaces, and also Ola and Uber drivers. These were well-funded companies which gave attractive incentives and commissions to their partners, so it seemed like a sound plan. By 2018, Axio had also launched a consumer finance business, seeking to replicate what Bajaj Finance had so profitably done.
Back then, it was fashionable for every fintech to think of themselves as a competitor to Bajaj Finance. But in its headlong rush for scale, the company made some mistakes.
One of them was Byju’s.
One of the first and largest lenders to Byju’s was Capital Float in 2017, which helped the six-year-old fintech company expand into consumer lending. It was a mutual alignment of interests.
Sensing opportunity, Capital Float bet big on Byju’s. In 2017, over 80% of its consumer finance portfolio comprised of Byju’s customers, said a second former Capital Float executive. Of the Rs 500 crore ($71.9 million) consumer finance book that Capital Float built, nearly Rs 400 crore ($57.5 million) came from Byju’s.

But when customers, unhappy with the product, decided not to pay, those like Capital Float paid the price, leading to the write-offs of large portions of such loans.
Similarly, when incentives for Uber drivers were reduced, they were no longer as attractive a class of borrowers as they were before.
Besides the choice of growth-partners going wrong, successive other challenges also took the wind out of most fintech lenders’ sails—from the IL&FS crisis drying up liquidity to the eventual regulatory tightening of unsecured lending.
All this took a toll on Axio’s asset quality. Its gross non-performing assets (GNPAs) in March 2021—in addition to annual write-offs—were 12.9% of the total assets under management (AUM). By September 2024, this had improved to 9.2% out of a total AUM of Rs 2,300 crore, but was still high enough to keep Axio recording losses.
Its revenue in FY24 rose 50% to Rs 351 crore, and losses fell to Rs 18 crore from Rs 137 crore in the previous financial year. But according to credit rating reports, all that came undone in the first half of FY25, with losses rising again to reach Rs 75 crore.

As for Amazon, its move to buy a lender like Axio puts it on equal footing with Flipkart, which got itself an NBFC license in June 2025.
In its [blogpost](https://www.axio.co.in/blogs/paddling-ashore) about the acquisition, Axio said: “We are ready to make this business BIG. Lending is a business that works best at scale. It is also one that can scale, in India and universally. As a startup, we flexed on tech and risk management, but distribution and capital are also crucial ingredients for the lending engine to truly hum.”
Axio is expected to operate as a separate subsidiary under Amazon. But it’s unlikely e-commerce companies will have the stomach to keep up with RBI’s demands and get deep into lending themselves. So, as a former employee of Axio said, the fintech may end up becoming nothing more than a tool for Amazon to learn how to lend in developing markets.
But, at least now, Axio finally has some closure.

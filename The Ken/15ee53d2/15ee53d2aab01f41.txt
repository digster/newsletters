Good Morning Ishan,

What a time to be alive. Yeah, you can dissect the previous line in many different ways, but at the bottom of it, it just feels good to be alive. To be here. To be writing to you. 

Now, I’ve said this before. There’s a lot happening in the payments business in India and too many folks want in on it. And when that happens, you have to make a choice. Either you can amble your way through and become just like everyone else (because everyone is chasing the same number of people). Or you can hunker down and say, 'No, we need to think this through and see where our real competency lies'. Then, execute.  

I think both Instamojo and Razorpay have arrived at this moment of reckoning. Both are payment startups in India and their existing business is no longer enough for them to grow. To quote from Arundhati’s story: “There are 80-90 million people that buy online, spending close to $12-14 billion a year. But most of this spending is happening across the large retailers. The share of SMEs in this is only about 5%.”

That is not good enough. 

Not when you have too many people chasing it. Unified Payments Interface has unleashed a whole host of companies into this space—WhatsApp, Google’s Tez, Flipkart’s PhonePe, in addition to entrenched wallets like Paytm. For all of them, getting the SMEs is the top priority. Think about this: Instamojo called itself a payment solution company and Razorpay, a payment gateway. Today, both don’t want to be identified by those labels. So what do they want to become? That’s Arundhati’s story for today.

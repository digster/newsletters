Good Morning Ishan,
 
“No place on the internet is safe for your personal data.”

It was the pitch of a Singapore tech company to our reporters this week. Whatever its product, the fact wasn’t off the mark, given how high India ranks in cybercrime incidents—#3 in the world, after US and China.

If you’re Government of India, you should worry. Maybe you do. Because the ministry of IT (MeitY) has asked all ministries to allocate 10% of their tech budgets to cybersecurity and appoint chief information security officers as well. That’s the good news.

The sad news, though, is that most new, upcoming tech companies won’t be touched by this public sector opportunity. Because it’s the market research firm Gartner’s Magic Quadrant report which makes or breaks a cybersecurity company in India. An annual report that evaluates cybersecurity products but only of companies with “global visibility” and with a certain revenue threshold. This automatically excludes Indian companies from even offering their tech for a review.

Public procurement in India rarely works for small companies, however good their technology may be. Earlier this month, a new order came into effect. It allows the government marketplace, the GeM portal, to list cybersecurity products and doesn’t impose “foreign certification” as an eligibility criterion.

It still doesn’t address the elephant in the room: the Magic Quadrant. The irony is, even Gartner believes it is only a guiding tool, not the benchmark.

There’s one rare, shining example though. Of Sebi, the Indian regulator for securities market, which worked with small companies to change the rule of the game.

Why don’t we have more change agents? Let the best tech win.

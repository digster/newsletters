A walk through an AI-focused conference in Singapore last June yielded a cornucopia of buzz phrases. Generative AI would increase efficiency in the workplace. It’ll level the playing field. Content will be personalised. Customers will feel more engaged. And there will be so, so, so many applications—one for each thing you do in a day!

There was some truth to those claims. A few booths ran demos of their AI products, and some truly made me feel like I had an experienced colleague sitting next to me, making useful suggestions with each step I took to complete a task or project. Many others, however, were duds that trapped the user in loops with no resolution in sight.

The business consultants who Abhirami spoke with for today’s story now use AI assistance every day. Junior staffers at firms like McKinsey, Boston Consulting Group, and Bain (MBB) deploy it for research and number crunching. And since this setup is meant to increase efficiency in the workplace, deadlines are nigh impossible—unless corners are cut and reports are generated without thorough checks.

The bosses don’t seem to mind. “A lot of senior partners and partners think of AI as a magic bullet,” said a former Bain manager. Even though there’s a mismatch between expectations from people who call the shots and the results seen by those who need to execute, AI assistance is here to stay. MBB and smaller firms know that continued relevance to clients depends on their mastery over AI consulting.

Abhirami writes how the nature of business consulting work is changing. Read her article to find out how that’s sparking contention in those firms’ offices.

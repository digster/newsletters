If there was a hall of fame for digital exports, the United States would count FAANG (Facebook, Amazon, Apple, Netflix and Google) and the EU its General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) as their prized exports. India would come close with its digital ID system, Aadhaar.
From the time it was launched in 2009, Aadhaar has not only triggered major shifts in India’s digital economy, but has been systematically evangelised and promoted in several countries, including some in Southeast Asia.
On Monday, Nandan Nilekani, the Indian tech billionaire who conceptualised Aadhaar, tweeted: "Made in India. Made for the World. Ready for Prime Time."
This tweet was about an education platform, another product from the same stable, and in the same mould: Build tech—get World Bank—financialise it.
When Aadhaar was being built, the theme of digital identity was also getting a push in development circles worldwide. Nilekani positioned Aadhaar as a development tool; and within the World Bank, the ID4D (Identity for development) programme was initiated.
Nilekani joined ID4D’s global advisory committee. Gradually, digital identity became a financing theme for the World Bank. According to one of its own reports, the World Bank channelled more than US$1 billion into supporting ID-related projects in more than 45 countries.
The unstoppable Aadhaar juggernaut has now landed in Indonesia. In the world’s fourth-most populous country, reports of entire villages being duped by identity thieves are common. The Indonesian government believes an Aadhaar-like system could not just end fraud, but bring about an India-like digital economy acceleration.
In the pandemic year, as the World Bank-Indonesian government plan slowed down, voices from within and outside have begun to question if a 10-year-old centralised system can still be billed as the blueprint for digital ID. Surprisingly, one of its earliest proponents from the World Bank believes this model is obsolete and the tech would be vulnerable in the face of state-backed quantum decryption.

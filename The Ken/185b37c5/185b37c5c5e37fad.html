Good Morning Ishan,
 
If you come from the same part of the country as I do, you are likely celebrating Pongal, the Tamil harvest festival, today. One image that comes to mind when I think of Pongal is families dressed in their finest attire, lining up for tickets to the latest movie—the "Pongal release".
 
For brevity's sake, I won't go into the details of this year's movies. However, I'll tell you about the criticism I hear most often—the predictability of plot lines, the formulaic treatment of conflict, stock characters and so on. Strangely, I find something comforting and familiar in the repetition of these themes. Events happen as you expect them to. There's no story here; just pure entertainment and passionate discussions on the ride home. The moral? Don't go looking for a story in familiarity. After all, it's the disruption of familiarity that gives us a story.
 
Arundhati's stellar story on Monday—a cautionary tale about a venture capital firm's unusual investment terms—captures a disruption in familiarity. US-based Guild Capital has been setting some strange conditions for the companies it invests in, including paying board fees to investors and hiring consultants at high salaries. This has led to legal troubles for young startups, with founders quitting and eventually shutting down businesses.
 
We then read on Tuesday about how a recent trend of employees engaging in multiple jobs (moonlighting) has intensified what used to be a one-time due diligence during the interview process—background checks. Vanita writes about this familiar topic made unfamiliar by the extent, frequency, and manner in which they occur.
 
In Southeast Asia, climate tech has begun to enjoy a growing interest from investors. Two venture-builders are trying to kickstart the nascent ecosystem with contrasting approaches. Nadine's story reveals the challenges of the venture-building model, particularly as it applies to climate tech in Southeast Asia.
 
"Invest in [insert advice here], you will get tax breaks!"—a familiar refrain. However, many argue that this logic behind investment decisions needs to be discouraged, even disrupted. Anand dives deep into the world of capital-gains tax—a complex, chaotic world that could do with simplifying.
 
We ended the week with a story on India's insolvency law. The law ensures banks can recover debts and creditors avoid losses when a company goes bankrupt. The conflict? Retail investors are left with nothing. Our contributors tell a gripping story of five bankrupt companies where retail investors' shares were completely written off.
 
We also had some excellent newsletters this week. In Strait Up, Isa investigates a case of missing onions in the Philippines. Ed Set Go returns with an edition of YouTube's latest offering, Courses, an initiative to scare India's edtechs just a little, but not enough. Finally, Arundhati's piece in Ka-Ching! addresses NPCI's latest win and payment companies' latest nightmare—making UPI accessible for NRIs.
 
That was it from us this week. Do write in if you treated yourself to a Pongal release this weekend. However, if you're looking for a story, we'll bring one to you tomorrow.

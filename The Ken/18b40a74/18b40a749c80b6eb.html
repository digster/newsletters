The money-world’s favourite disclaimer “past performance is no guarantee of future results” is no respecter of reputations. It applies even to the Reserve Bank of India (RBI), the country’s storied central bank.

Illustrating this best is perhaps the UPI (Unified Payments Interface) and the CBDC (Central Bank Digital Currency), both of which have the RBI as their key driver.

While the seven-year-old UPI—an instant payments system—has had wild success and gone places, the CBDC—a digital form of physical currency—has struggled, to put it mildly.

Launched about a year back, the goal was to have 1 million daily retail transactions in CBDC by December 2023. Two months to the deadline, the e-rupee as it is popularly known is way behind—clocking just 10,000–18,000 retail transactions a day.

The RBI is rolling out growth hacks to crank up the numbers—including leaning on the UPI. But the needle’s not moving. And it’s unlikely to, due to its many contradictions and constraints, write ex-head of merchant services Ateesh Tankha, ex-central banker Ganga Narayan Rath, and banking expert Maulik Gawarepatil, the authors of today’s sharply argued opinion piece.

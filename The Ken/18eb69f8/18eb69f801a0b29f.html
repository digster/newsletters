Good Morning Ishan,

Where I’m from, people generally aren’t very interested in international affairs, except when it comes to the United States. As of 2024, the Philippines ranks third globally in terms of affection for the world’s largest economy.

During a recent dinner with friends, we pondered what the average Filipino knows about India. We speculated that it narrows down to three things. First, the presence of Indian informal lenders who take risks by lending to unbanked Filipinos and are often seen traversing the streets on motorcycles to find clients and collect payments.

Next was the crowning of India’s Sushmita Sen as Miss Universe in Manila three decades ago. She became so popular in the country that many Filipinos born that year were named after her. Lastly, India’s role as the origin of the trendy snack panipuri, which now attracts long queues in local Filipino street markets in the capital.

This level of familiarity, however, is taking on a new dimension. In recent months, Philippine news has been buzzing with headlines about the strengthening of bilateral relations between the two countries. 

About a week ago, Indian Minister of External Affairs S. Jaishankar visited the country and reaffirmed its alliance with the Philippines amid geopolitical issues in the South China Sea. The Philippines, in turn, publicly expressed gratitude for India during the high-profile visit for the Indian Navy’s “swift and decisive action” in rescuing Filipino crew members of a merchant ship attacked by rebels in the Indian Ocean in March.

But it’s not just conflict that binds India and the Philippines. Both countries are similar in a lot of ways and can share notes on democracy, best practices in their respective huge outsourcing industries, finding opportunities around big remittance movements, and ways to deal with bad traffic. 

The tides are shifting, and whether fueled by conflict or opportunity, India and Philippine bilateral relations are moving forward regardless.

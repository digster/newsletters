“In his blue gardens, men and girls came and went like moths among the whisperings and the champagne and the stars.” If F. Scott Fitzgerald were describing the ‘great’ Jay Gatsby’s parties (in India, not Long Island) today, he’d probably add: “…and portfolios”.

Because in 2024, the Great Indian Wedding isn’t just about family heirlooms or a lifetime of savings—it’s about liquidating stock holdings.

Yes, that’s right—weddings funded by demat accounts. Thanks to a 35% bull run since 2023, budgets have effectively become tied to the market. Expect five-star venues, celebrity performances, and an absurd amount of exotic meats that require high-level vendor negotiations. Because stock gains = salmon canapés.

To put it into perspective, nearly 140,000 weddings are taking place every day this season, collectively totalling Rs 6 lakh crore—more than Bangladesh’s GDP, as Rounak points out.

Real-estate investors are getting in on the action, too. In Delhi, property flippers are selling homes, converting that liquidity into wedding budgets big enough to fund fireworks, Bollywood A-listers, and whatever else might make you question reality.

For everyone else, there are the “Marry Now, Pay Later” loans. With interest rates going up to 24%, these schemes offer a lifeline to couples willing to go deep into debt for extravagant décor and whatnot. The fine print: newlyweds, freshly burdened with debt, might soon get a visit from the debt collector, asking, “Was the farmhouse really necessary?”

And while retail investors anxiously watch their portfolios shrink, one thing is certain: that wedding will be unforgettable… and so will the bill.

But there’s more to this story than just lavish/reckless spending. Rounak goes deep into an ecosystem of investors, wedding planners, wealth managers, and bankers… hold on, it’s getting crowded… retailers, hoteliers, caterers, and gold companies… basically, everyone fuelling one of the most extravagant wedding seasons we’ve ever seen. It’s a fun read.

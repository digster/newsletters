The Big Story

The death of the independent doctor

Prohibitive costs, competition from hospital chains, and the pressure of online ratings have punctured the dreams of young medics

The rising cost of medical degrees and costs to set up independent practice are leaving half of India’s doctors drowning in debt. Despite super-specialty degrees costing Rs 2–3 crore, the corporate job market demands more—a good patient base for starters. While struggling to find their footing, young doctors also have to deal with rising rents, competition from corporate clinics, and acing the social-media game. Medical careers can be rewarding, but only for those who can endure. For the others, there are side-gigs or jobs overseas. [Read the full story]

UPI was the scapegoat. So, who really was at fault for the Bajaj Housing IPO mess?

India’s second-largest home financier went public, retail demand surged, and sponsor banks were swamped. The result: UPI-app-mandate notifications got stuck in the backlog

Bajaj Housing Finance’s IPO stands out as one of the largest offerings in recent history. But, the IPO faced a 16% rejection rate for applications. Investors blamed brokerages, stock exchanges, and the NPCI—the UPI operator—for the technical glitches. In reality, the responsibility lay with the sponsor banks appointed by the financier. Kopi Kenangan wants to keep its coffee within everyone’s pay range

Stories you don’t want to miss

The $10B Parag Parikh Mutual Fund is making a virtue of not investing in a raging bull market

The star fund house is growing at breakneck speed and so is the cash in its portfolio

Parag Parikh Flexi Cap’s staggering Rs 14,000-crore cash hoard is now about a fifth of its portfolio, much higher than most peers’. A runaway bull market has elevated stock prices to levels where the fund house does not find comfort in entry valuations for the most part. The fund’s cash-calls have done well in the past by limiting losses in bad markets even at the cost of some gains in good ones. But can past performance be an indicator of future returns? Besides, are equity funds meant to invest fully in stocks or be big on cash? [Read the full story]

He steered Puma’s rise. Now, he’s on the anti-Puma path for his sportswear startup

Abhishek Ganguly’s one-year-old company wants to defy the industry norm by manufacturing shoes in-house—a move that most Indian startups would shy away from

Agilitas, fresh off its Mochiko acquisition, notched up Rs 740 crore in annual revenue. And this was before it even dipped its toes into the consumer market. Mochiko manufactures shoes for international brands such as Adidas, Puma, New Balance, Skechers, Reebok, Asics, Crocs, Decathlon, Clarks, and US Polo. Agilitas also got an exclusive license to bring Italy’s Lotto to India, aiming to cater to both the premium- and the mass-market. In a departure from the sportswear playbook, the company operates a vertically integrated model, overseeing the entire lifecycle from manufacturing to retail.

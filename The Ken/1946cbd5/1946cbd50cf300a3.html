Indians want an income-tax cut. They probably won’t get it. Major tax cuts will likely remain elusive
By Rounak Kumar Gunjan
5 min read
View in browser

Good Morning Ishan,

You are reading our subscriber-only newsletter Ka-Ching!—a weekly commentary on fintech and personal finance

The Union Budget is less than two weeks away and the usual list of expectations, wishlists, and predictions have started pouring in. In just the last handful of days, I’ve gone through dozens of press releases and pre-budget quotes from industry associations, analysts, consumer groups, and corporate executives. 

Almost all of them contain one common thread. I think late poet and novelist Khalil Dhantejvi said it best: 

Itni mehengai ki bazaar se kuchh lata hoon,
apne bachhon mein use baant ke sharmaata hoon

(Loosely translated: “the prices are so high that whatever I bring home from the market, I’m embarrassed to distribute it among my children.”)

Unaffordability and price rise feature in most complaints from the salaried class, many of whom feel their incomes are becoming more insufficient by the day. They want immediate relief, and if it comes in the form of that long-wished-for income-tax cut, so much the better. 

The conversations I’ve had with finance ministry officials and analysts, however, indicate they are unlikely to get much.

More disposable income, please

For almost a month, various trade bodies have been asking the government to raise India’s income-tax exemption limit to Rs 10 lakh (US$11,600) per annum. Presently, the basic exemption limit in the new tax regime is Rs 3 lakh. Salaried employees get a standard deduction of Rs 75,000, alongside a rebate under section 87A which could make incomes tax-free up to Rs 7.75 lakh a year. The demand is to raise this tax-free amount to Rs 10 lakh. 

Two tax experts I spoke to, both of whom have been on government committees, believe this isn’t a very difficult demand to grant, and that “some tweaks here and there can make it easily achievable.”

“Just by raising standard reduction and rebates, the math can be done.”

Taxpayers have good reason to push for a cut; there’s been negligible growth in incomes vis-a-vis retail inflation.

For instance, food inflation stood at 8.39% in December and 9.04% in November, per data sourced from the statistics ministry. Vegetables have been particularly hard on the pocket, with prices rising 26.56% year-on-year last month. 

In comparison, annual income growth has been slow. According to an estimate by research firm Avendus Spark, growth in average household income was only around 1.5% at the end of FY24. Housing and automobile prices have outpaced income too. 

Even for people with investments in equity, last year’s hikes in long-term capital gains tax (from 10% to 12.5%) and short-term capital gains tax (from 15% to 20%) are starting to hurt. According to one of the tax experts:

“Markets were in a bull run till almost the beginning of 2024. So investors were making more money and the tax deduction didn’t really hurt. But now markets are past their peak and incomes are smaller. Paying taxes on this income hurts even more.”

“Taxbreak not solution”

But if you ask people in the finance ministry whether the government is under any pressure to cut taxes, they answer in the negative. Even all the memes criticising Finance Minister Nirmala Sitharaman “haven’t moved a feather inside the ministry,” a senior North Block official told The Ken on Wednesday. “The government is aware of the gap in income growth, but a tax break is not the solution.”

They may be right, of course. Such tax reliefs don’t do much for long-term growth in incomes.

“It’s about creating better quality sources of income. Be it jobs or through entrepreneurship. Only that can solve the gap,” the official added, stating that the upcoming budget will focus on job creation, direct benefit transfer schemes, and improving overall infrastructure that would address logistical problems for sectors like manufacturing.

“A holistic approach will be put in. There may be a few tax reliefs here and there but the government is under no pressure at all.”

And if you still find that hard to believe, perhaps you should take a look at India’s electoral calendar, which shows only two states going to the polls this year.

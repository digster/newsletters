All of us need magic. The quality of ad campaigns, ad spending, and quality roles for marketers all seem to be on the decline

I picked up a book this week that has been on my to-be-read pile for a long time—Autobiography of a Yogi by Paramahansa Yogananda. 

I am interested in and have practiced yoga on and off for several years, but one of the reasons I wanted to read this book was because Apple’s co-founder Steve Jobs was devoted to it.

Ralph Waldo Emerson, for example, wrote poetry based on the Bhagavad Gita and paid tribute in person to Coleridge and Carlyle. Autobiography of a Yogi, a book Jobs claimed to have read annually since he was in college, quotes Emerson several times. Values regularly celebrated in Romantic texts — passion, spontaneity, authenticity — were counterculture touchstones as well.

Jobs’ philosophy, then, overlapped with the Romantics’, whether he knew it or not.

Steve Jobs, Romanticism, and the Marriage of Technology and Humanities, Forbes https://www.forbes.com/sites/oreillymedia/2012/10/02/steve-jobs-romanticism-and-the-marriage-of-technology-and-humanities/

In this, the second in a trilogy of December-themed editions we’re running on Inciting Incident, we explore a subject that shows up in countless stories and traditions from across the world. In Santa Claus, the Christmas story, tales of miracles and the supernatural. In Autobiography of a Yogi, christmas themed movies on streaming services, and numerous religious tales. Even in marketing campaigns. 

Magic.

The tactical uses of magic

Last month, one of my colleagues wrote about how the role of Chief Marketing Officer, once a coveted position for business professionals, is dying out, especially in younger companies. Apparently, businesses are increasingly building their marketing tactics and strategies on consumer data—reacting to passing trends rather than creating long-term auras around their brands. And that’s a job that can be done by a few simple algorithms.

For decades, the CMO was the charming, confident face with a knack for brand-building. They were capable of turning a catchy jingle into cultural currency or hitching their brand to a massive sports franchise, like when alcohol brand Kingfisher beer’s “Divided by Teams. United by Kingfisher” became synonymous with cricket fandom. But the days when a CMO could boast a repertoire stretching across ad campaigns, brand strategy, and celebrity endorsements are gone.

CMOs are a dying breed. Nykaa, Nestlé India, and others are living proof, The Ken https://the-ken.com/story/cmos-are-a-dying-breed-nykaa-nestle-india-and-others-are-living-proof/

But by downgrading their brand building to something so shallow, what organisations lose is the ability to create an identity that goes beyond just the products and services they sell. And if that story has some elements of magic, something that hints at the possibility of breaking free of the drudgery of daily reality, then so much the better. 

What’s happening with marketing strategies now, though, is quite the opposite. And it’s a trend not just evident in staffing decisions but also in ad spending, and the quality of ad campaigns. 

Earlier this year, the Hindustan Times quoted Lloyd Mathias, angel investor and former marketer at Pepsico, Motorola and HP, saying Indian ad campaigns were underwhelming. In the same article—titled Indian ad campaigns are losing their magic https://www.hindustantimes.com/cities/mumbai-news/indian-ad-campaigns-are-losing-their-magic-101714678027083.html—the paper quoted another marketing executive saying that recent ads were too focused on the here and now, lacked imagination and creativity, and were designed to just increase transactions.

“The advertising landscape is evolving with the fragmentation of search, the rapid rise of influencer marketing and retail media,” said Ashwin Padmanabhan, president-investments, trading, and partnerships, at GroupM India. “Within digital ad revenue, search contributes 22%, retail media 18%, and the rest 60%. Sectors like auto, realty and offline retail are expected to power the overall advertising growth,” he said.

Ad spending in India to grow at a slower pace in 2024, says GroupM, Livemint https://www.livemint.com/industry/ad-spending-in-india-to-grow-at-a-slower-pace-in-2024-says-groupm-11707829330565.html

What many companies aren’t accounting for is that stories that transport audiences away from boring reality and mere functionality can be extremely effective at attracting and holding customer attention. A realisation that Jobs, for instance, came to early and applied consistently with his storytelling during Apple launches. 

Not everyone is abandoning the magical for the mundane, though. Hettich, a furniture manufacturer, released an ad recently https://brandequity.economictimes.indiatimes.com/news/advertising/hettich-transports-viewers-to-a-magical-place/113230145 that places an eight-year-old magician as the protagonist, even if that’s using the concept very literally. It’s a story that goes beyond just the functional experience of the brand’s products, and tries to spark the imagination (and aspirations) of its audience. “Magical Interior Experiences” is how Hettich positions its identity globally. Audio streaming service Spotify, for its part, had a campaign earlier this year that sought to highlight the magic of music https://www.afaqs.com/news/advertising/spotify-launches-music-magic-hai-campaign-in-four-languages.

In an effort to highlight the role of music in listeners’ lives, across moments and relationships, Spotify launches a fresh campaign in four languages – Hindi, Tamil, Telugu, and Malayalam. Reflecting the sentiment of “music, magic hai”, the new advertisements include four short films, which showcase, through simple relationships, how music unites, evokes nostalgia, bridges generational gaps, and is an enjoyable, everyday companion.

Perhaps more brands could take the cue and go beyond the literal.

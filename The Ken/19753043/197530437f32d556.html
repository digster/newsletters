In my day job, I work with a global management consulting firm. But I aspire to become an investment banker, which means I can’t help diving headfirst into the fine print of deals I come across and tracking how they actually play out over time. 

Some would call it compulsion. I tell myself it is entirely reasonable preparation for the future, given how convoluted some of these deals can get and the magnitude of the stakes riding on them. 

Mergers and acquisitions (M&A), in particular, have been on a tear in India recently. 2024 was a record-breaking year for M&As, according to Grant Thornton’s annual deal tracker, clocking a massive $44.5 billion in cumulative value across 683 deals. Over half of the value, and 70% of the volume, was driven by domestic consolidation. 

What cross-border activity there was was largely outbound, with three huge deals, together valued at $17.5 billion, propping up the numbers—Viacom18’s merger with Disney, AsterDM Healthcare’s merger with Quality Care, and Bharti Enterprises’ acquisition of a 25% stake in British Telecom. The number of inbound deals actually fell 52%, which the firm attributed to “cautious investor sentiment” and “robust performance of the US economy leading to a significant redirection of investments towards the US”. 

Regardless of that last factoid, it is clear that the Indian market is in the throes of a major fit of consolidation.

The government, perhaps in search of more statistical fodder to flaunt, seems to want to speed up this frenzy even more. 

In this year’s Union Budget, one of the proposals made by the finance minister was an overhaul of M&A regulations—including the introduction of fast-track mergers (FTM) for a wide variety of companies (including unlisted firms and fellow subsidiaries) and measures to remove bottlenecks in deal pipelines.

That sounds great, sure. 

But is a lack of haste really the biggest problem afflicting Indian M&As? 

I would say it does not even make the top five. 

In fact, from my tracking of several major M&As in recent years, more speed can make many of the real problems quite a bit worse.

Speed drowns out scrutiny

In March, logistics major Delhivery acquired Ecom Express for Rs 1,407 crore in an all-cash deal. It was a firesale, valuing Ecom Express at less than 80% of its 2022 peak and forcing its investors to take major haircuts. (The Ken had a detailed piece last week on how the company went from mulling an IPO to a firesale in a matter of months.)

Leaving aside the series of operational missteps and erosion of trust that led to key clients like Meesho, Amazon, and Reliance cutting ties with the company, there were also accusations that Ecom Express was misrepresenting certain numbers in its DRHP. 

Accusations that were levelled by—surprise, surprise!—Delhivery.

Ecom Express said it had shipped 514.41 million shipments in FY24, while Delhivery handled 740 million packages during the period.

However, in a filing to the stock exchanges on Friday, Delhivery alleged that this comparison was flawed. For example, Delhivery counts a shipment — even if it is not delivered to the destination and returned to origin — only a single shipment. But the peer (Ecom Express) counts it as two shipments as the to and fro transportation are billed separately.

Ecom Express claimed that it offers its services in 27,000 pin codes. However, Delhivery highlighted that as per Government of India, there are 19,300 unique pin codes in the country.

IPO-bound Ecom Express used incorrect numbers in DRHP, says Delhivery, Business Standard

So, here’s my question: why does Delhivery, which is still grappling with the integration of its 2021 acquisition Spoton, want to buy this sinking ship? One that, in the words of Delhivery’s own CEO Sahil Barua, has a nearly 100% overlap of customers with Delhivery?

One answer is securing more market share and additional infrastructure at bargain-basement prices. But that indicates a lack of sufficient concern over the company’s poor governance practices, and hence, a large risk appetite for deals that deliver growth and scale—even if they come at the cost of further trouble down the road.  

Enabling more speed in such cases only makes it easier to shrug off such concerns before the signatories put ink to paper. Jet Airways’ failed revival under the Jalan Kalrock consortium is an example where relatively fast clearance for acquisition from the bankruptcy courts ended in the eventual liquidation of the company because of ownership disputes and unpaid dues. 

Streamlining M&As through an expanded FTM policy is good, but only if diligence on crucial aspects of the deal and successor liabilities keeps pace. 

After all, every skipped checkpoint during diligence raises the risk of a later crash.

Earnouts, misaligned incentives, and the SPAC illusion

Haste is just one problem. There are many others. 

Earnouts, for instance, can be a powerful tool to ensure that M&As deliver the best possible results. Such deals tie the amount that sellers receive to the future business performance of the acquired company. You see this strategy used in the pharmaceutical sector quite frequently, linking payouts to regulatory approvals, product milestones, or revenue targets.

A great example of a successful earnout is IT major Wipro’s acquisition of cMango, a US-based tech consultancy firm. The deal was structured such that cMango’s shareholders would get $6 million if the unit’s revenues reached at least $17 million in the first year, with a further payout of $6 million if they reached $34 million in the second year. The deal also provided for lower, graduated payments if revenue fell short of the mark. 

But such deals only work if the founders of the acquired enterprise want to continue in leadership positions and there is close alignment on business goals and operations. In Wipro’s case, the company decided to let cMango’s primary shareholder continue to manage the company after acquisition and gave it significant flexibility in budget allocations.

Many M&A deals aren’t as well-thought-out, though. Most Indian buyers seem to prefer gaining full control upfront, and multi-year payouts are seen as risky and difficult to monitor. This needs to change, especially as India’s tech and consumer sectors witness increasing consolidation through mergers and the acquisition of startups. 

Entrepreneurial energy and zeal can’t be sustained with a one-time cheque.

There is also rising enthusiasm for taking the SPAC route to raise public capital without having to make all the disclosures an IPO would entail—at least in markets that allow such things. SPACs, or Special Purpose Acquisition Companies, are shell entities that are listed on the stock markets, raise capital, and then acquire or merge with an unlisted company, effectively making it a listed entity in the process. 

But there can be big costs to this. Sponsors typically receive 20% of shares for free, leading to high dilution for other investors. And the overall cost of a SPAC deal can exceed that of a traditional IPO. Many startups which take the SPAC route to get listed, often with minimal revenue, also struggle post-merger due to the rapid depletion of cash. 

If a SPAC doesn’t find a target within two years, it must return funds and dissolve, misleading some investors into believing it’s a risk-free investment. But SPACs continue to see high redemption rates and poor long-term performance. Take the case of US-based genomics firm 23andme, which went public via a SPAC in 2021 at a $3.5 billion valuation. It quickly burned through capital, revenue stagnated, share prices fell from $266 to under $1, and the company is now close to bankruptcy.

India’s own Renew Power listed on NASDAQ via a SPAC too, which allowed it to raise some capital. But the stock has since underperformed and investor sentiment remains cautious. In December 2024, The Ken published an edition of the Long and Short newsletter about Renew’s troubles after listing and why its shareholders want to reverse course and take it private.

In early December, four of its shareholders—including Sinha and the Canada Pension Plan Investment Board—said they plan to take the Nasdaq-listed firm private by buying one-third of the company they don’t own. They are offering a little over US$7 a share, which is more than what the Renew stock was trading at, at the time of the announcement.

But it’s less than the US$9.55 it closed at on the day of its listing in August 2021, after merging with a blank-cheque company.

In short, SPACs come with high systematic risks like model overvaluation, unsustainable growth expectations, and misaligned investor incentives—all reasons why India’s market regulator Sebi has resisted allowing them in India until now. 

And compounding all the above problems is the behaviour of some investment advisors and bankers, whose incentives aren’t always aligned with ensuring the long-term success of deals and often lean towards bagging the biggest one possible, regardless of which direction the underlying fundamentals point to. Advisory fees, after all, typically range from a cool 1% to 6% of the deal size. 

So, how do we straighten out all these tangles in India’s M&A landscape? 

Much of it is a matter of changing corporate and business culture—a collective realisation that competitiveness isn’t just about speedy deals and rapid scaling. That dealmaking needs to be preceded by strategy, not vice versa. That post-merger integration matters as much as pre-deal valuation. 

But if you ask me, a good dose of regulatory reform would help too.  Especially if it’s aimed at balancing deal velocity with increased accountability and fixing counterproductive incentive structures.

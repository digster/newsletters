Workplaces are more than desks, chairs, coffee machines, and computers. They’re made of and defined by the people who inhibit them. People you share a meal with, people you watch mid-day cricket matches with, people you share your deep secrets with. 

But people aren’t always at their best behaviour. 

People sometimes make other people feel uncomfortable. People sometimes turn the workplace, an almost indispensable place of dwelling and thriving, into someone’s least favourite place to be at. And the victims are often, women. Young, vulnerable, powerless, and most prone to unsolicited sexual advances. 

Enter the Vishakha guidelines. First laid down by the Supreme Court over two decades ago in 1995, they were brought in with the intent of creating a safe and supportive workplace for women; one that respects their right to equal opportunity and status at work. 

A lot has happened since. In the backdrop of a legislation passed in 2013, and the MeToo movement of 2019, Sneha Vakharia takes a look at the progress we've made, the problems that persist, and the way forward.

In this 13th episode of Cost to Company we have:

A conversation with Meghna Srinivas, CEO of Trustin, and Pooja Chandra, a partner at a corporate law firm, about how the PoSH policy unfolds inside workplaces.
	The story of Snehal, a marketing executive for a luxury brand who explains what it’s like when you exercise the law as a victim, the challenges she faced, and how the teeth of the law might be hurting those it was made to protect.
	A deep pondering on how culture, and not only legislation, stands in the way of creating a workplace we once dreamt of.

Good Morning Ishan,

Something happened in November 2017 in Bhiwandi, a small city 20 km from Mumbai, that foreshadows the biggest bet that Indian e-commerce will make over the next few years—Walmart opened its first “dark store” in India. Spanning 45,000 square feet, the “store” wasn’t really a store, in that customers couldn’t just walk in and shop. Instead, the “store” was really a large warehouse that would fulfil orders placed by small mom-and-pop stores or other B2B businesses.

Walmart didn’t invent the concept of dark stores, but UK retailers like Sainsbury’s and Tesco did. Back in India, BigBasket has been using dark stores for some years now to ship online orders fast from “stores” closest to a customer. Except that its pickup and delivery staff are the “customers” in these stores. That’s how BigBasket is able to get their express deliveries done in just a few hours. Amazon has been doing that too, under its Amazon Now umbrella.

Last week, Sumanth laid out his theory behind why Walmart took a "$20 billion punt" on Flipkart. He posited that the nosebleed valuation was on account of two key premiums - a policy premium and an “Amazon-beater” premium. 

Today, he changes his POV to explain how Walmart might meld its own formidable experience and expertise with that of Flipkart. By going “dark.”

"In Walmart-Flipkart’s omnichannel strategy, orders are placed online and they are fulfilled from the closest dark stores in the shortest amount of time possible. The convenience of online ordering meets the speed and near real-time fulfilment of offline stores,” says Sumanth.

Good Morning Ishan,

I am sure that by now you’ve heard a lot of people talking about blockchain. From the Prime Minister of India to Finance Minister Arun Jaitley to, well, random folks gathered around in pubs and bright smoking corners. 

The chatter is mostly euphoria. How blockchain is going to revolutionise one industry after another. And you must have heard this. India is building its biggest blockchain network called IndiaChain. And in the words of Niti Aayog’s CEO Amitabh Kant, “Niti will run pilots on electronic health records, land records and digital certification. Wherever people need to establish a trusted, shared, unique record, blockchain technology can help by simplifying processes and removing intermediaries.” 

The first proposal there is to issue blockchain-based academic certificates from Indian Institute of Technology (IIT) Delhi.  

When my colleague Sumanth heard this, he went, well, hold on a second. Important questions aren’t being asked. Is blockchain really the panacea it is being seen as by the powers-that-be? Can it really “establish trust”, “simplify processes” and “remove intermediaries”? Can the government really deliver on these lofty goals? Plus, by its very design, blockchain is saddled with several generic disadvantages around efficiency, scalability and costs. What happens to that during the implementation phase?

Sumanth delved deeper into the issue. The result is today’s story.

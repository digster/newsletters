Trade Tricks
Tue, 01 Apr 25
A weekly newsletter about the biggest changes in commerce—focusing on shifts that matter to you..
Good Morning Ishan,
Since the pandemic, investors have worshipped at the altar of Trent, the Tata Group’s apparel retailer. The stock is up a stonking 1,000% over the past five years.
The breathless rally vaulted Trent into the benchmark Nifty 50 index in September, where it joined another Tata retailer, Titan Company.
But something’s gotta give.
At 128X trailing earnings, Trent is considerably pricier than the parent of Dmart, which is India’s most valuable listed retailer. And Dmart is already very expensive.
So, finally, investors are fleeing Trent. The stock is down by a quarter so far this year, while the Nifty 50 has been flat.
In February, I argued that Zudio, the fast-fashion chain singlehandedly responsible for Trent’s rise, was heading towards an age-old [retail trap](https://the-ken.com/tradetricks/is-zudio-walking-into-the-age-old-retail-trap/).
Just four years ago, Zudio, Trent’s wildly successful value-clothing chain, was one-third of Westside—the company’s flagship brand—in terms of average store size.
Now, Zudio is almost half as big.
The average Zudio outlet is 9,800 sq ft. Westside stores measure over 21,400 sq ft.
Should this be a problem? Zudio is the reason Trent, once a lightweight in the Tata Group, is now its fourth-most-valuable company. In just three years, Zudio’s footprint has quadrupled to 635 outlets in 190 cities.
Trent was able to take Zudio far and wide because it sold everything under Rs 1,000, and also because the stores were compact. Delhi, for instance, has 21 Zudio stores and a mere eight Westside outlets.
But few retailers can resist the temptation to make their stores roomier, especially when things are going swimmingly.
Even so, Trent outperforms most other apparel retailers around the world, and will continue to do so, according to a recent report.
If you’re a regular reader of this newsletter, you may remember an [edition](https://the-ken.com/tradetricks/dmart-and-investors-rekindle-their-love/) from a year ago about how Dmart, despite its recent struggles, runs circles around global giants such as Walmart and Carrefour. It had this telling chart.
It won’t be surprising if Trent, even if it can’t maintain its breakneck growth of recent years, could expand its sales by over 25% annually over the next two years, according to broker Macquarie. That’s three times the projected growth of Japanese retailers Muji and Uniqlo, and four times that of Inditex, the Spanish owner of Zara and a joint-venture partner of Trent’s in India.
But what caught my eye was how much more efficient Trent is—a point two key metrics drive home.
No retailer wants to have unsold stock, and the longer it remains on store shelves or in warehouses, the worse it is for the retailer’s financials. It means the company has to spend more on displaying or storing its wares; reduced cash flows; and the unappetising prospect of selling the products at steep discounts, which impacts profits.
In the five years to FY24, Trent slashed its inventory days by a third, and its global rivals don’t even come close. Swedish fast-fashion chain Hennes & Mauritz (H&M), for instance, takes almost twice as long to sell its stock.
And Trent scores high on another measure of efficiency too.
Trent is second only to Inditex in how well it uses its capital to turn a profit, and is far ahead of Asian peers, including China’s HLA Group.
Zudio, thanks to its bargain-basement prices, is not as profitable as Westside, which was until recently Trent’s flagship chain. But Zudio’s unparalleled growth quintupled Trent’s operating profits to almost Rs 2,000 crore between FY20 and FY24.
Plus, Zudio’s impressive inventory turnover has allowed Trent the luxury of not having too much working capital tied up in dead stock, which has in turn vastly improved its return ratios.
It seems even when Trent is down (at least from the point of view of its investors), it still stands tall.

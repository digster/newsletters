Good Morning Ishan,
 
India is an advertiser's heaven. A population of 1.3 billion, a growing economy, a raucous cacophony of media and an aspirational consumption ladder with consumer brands at every rung. Advertising is the grease that lubricates a vast majority of its media industry.
 
Newspapers are still growing and profitable, because advertising. There are close to 900 TV channels, because advertising. YouTube and Facebook are massive, because advertising. Music streaming services are popular, because advertising.
 
Newspapers are so full of advertising that local businesses pay vendors to insert pirated ads by hand. The backs of airline boarding cards and seats have advertising. Even taxpayer-funded services from the government, like buying a railway ticket or applying for a passport, have some advertising embedded. Primetime TV had so many minutes of advertising that, at times, they overshadowed the actual time allotted for programming. 
 
But if you pull back the curtain that covers all of this, things are rotten. And there’s no entity that better represents the state of rot than “media agencies”.
 
These are behemoths that aggregate advertising spends across many clients and then match it with ad inventory they buy from publishers. Which means, an ad you saw on a TV channel or newspaper for, say, Samsung or P&G, wouldn’t actually have been bought by those brands directly. But instead, by their media agencies.
 
For our Sunday story, Ashish goes cerebral, by pointing out how media agencies are stuck in a hateful tango with their clients (brand advertisers) and publishers. Neither of them likes media agencies. And thus, media agencies end up engaging in all sorts of shady and questionable practices.
 
“This is a serious issue,” says Chintamani Rao, an independent media consultant. “Nobody is asking the fundamental question. Are we still in an advertiser-agency business? I don’t think anybody is even sure anymore, and the answer is no.” Speaking on principle, the relationship between an agency and an advertiser is based on the idea that the agency will work in the best interest of the client. But with an agency making money from both sides of the table, it would be fair to say that the agency is actually working in its own interest. Not the client’s.
 
How long can this rot continue, before it starts eating into the very rationale of mass advertising in India? I’m not keeping my hopes up.

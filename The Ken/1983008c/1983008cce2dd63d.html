The door is revolving at EY.

In the Big Four universe, partners leave routinely, but the frequency at which it’s happening at the audit-and-consulting major, it’s nothing close to routine.

Dubbing it normal “attrition”, even “healthy” to an extent, an EY spokesperson said the firm hired 50 partners in just one year, and it has overall hired more than it has lost.

That may be true, but to arrest the departures and their impact, EY increased the notice period for partners leaving the firm from six to nine months earlier this year.

As the lines between the Big Four, MBB, IT-services (and now BPO with Capgemini acquiring WNS), and boutique tech-consulting firms blur, there’s a scramble to become a one-stop shop and deliver everything in the shortest possible time.

In all this, tech consulting becomes a pressure point, and at EY, the highest revenue-earner division, it is also the fastest revolving door. Senior partners are leaving structural mismatches behind them. As one former partner summarised to Debanjali, writer of today’s story, “You need to play chess with these industry veterans. Instead, they [EY] are playing Chinese checkers.”

Good Morning Ishan,
 
My favourite opening line in literature is from Richard Yates' novel Easter Parade: "Neither of the Grimes sisters would have a happy life, and looking back, it always seemed that the trouble began with their parents' divorce."
 
This is an incredible sentence, not just because it’s beautiful in and of itself. It stands out because Yates doesn't waste a second to tell you what the book is all about, which is very uncharacteristic of a work of fiction, particularly a novel. If 'story' is all you care for, there is no reason for you to read any further. Viewed journalistically, the line tells you the "what" and "why" of the Grimes sisters' doomed lives.
 
But it derives its power from making you very curious about the "how".
 
The reason I bring this up is because the headlines for The Ken's stories are not unlike this line. There is a tendency among journalists to be cryptic or cute with headlines to pique the reader's interest. In that regard, headlines can sometimes read like book titles.
 
But at The Ken, though, we want to tell you right upfront what you’re getting into while also trying to make you want to read the story. We don't sacrifice clarity at the altar of cleverness. And this week's headlines were no different.
 
"Fatigue and FOMO: The span of Indian edtech VC emotions": We began with Olina's look into the evolution of the kinds of edtechs investors want to back. Yes, there is a whole world beyond Byju's and Unacademy.
 
"The Interview: Mapping the world with NextBillion.ai’s Ajay Bulusu": On Tuesday, Ka Kay had a fascinating chat with the co-founder of a Singapore-based startup that provides custom navigation data to e-tailers and ride-hailing companies, among others.
 
"Corporates on notice as mutual funds find their voting voice": In our Wednesday story, I looked at how the Indian capital markets regulator's mandate forcing mutual funds to vote on shareholder resolutions could finally hold companies accountable. But how far can mutual funds go when these very companies are also their clients?
 
"Xiaomi’s plans to gatecrash Apple, OnePlus’ premium party": The Chinese handset maker has been synonymous with cheap handsets. But, as Aayush wrote on Thursday, Xiaomi now wants to be an aspirational brand. And it's overhauling how its phones are sold and how its brand is perceived. But Xiaomi is still in for an uphill climb.  
 
"Insurtech 2.0 wants to fix the loopy claims and complaints process. Can it?": Mis-sold policies and rejected claims are partly why insurance policies worth over $3 billion were not renewed in the year ended March 2020. It's no surprise, then, that a new crop of startups wants to bring some order to insurance grievance redressal, Maitri reported in her Friday piece.
 
Besides these, we had some fascinating pieces in our nine weekly newsletters. My pick is Jaideep's Moneyball piece on the probable provenance of tennis legend Novak Djokovic's anti-vaccine stance.
 
That's all from me. Have a good Sunday!

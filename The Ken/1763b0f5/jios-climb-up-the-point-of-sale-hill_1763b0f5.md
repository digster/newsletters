Venture capitalists supposedly have a favourite question: "What would you do if Google decides to compete with your startup?"
Now, you can replace Google with any of the internet behemoths around— Facebook, Amazon, Apple—but what about wannabe internet behemoth Jio Platforms?
Two years ago is when we first saw the signs of Jio's intent to disrupt the way we shop. It wanted to digitise the humble kirana (mom-and-pop) store. And its weapon was an all-powerful Point-of-Sale (PoS) terminal. Besides accepting payments, Jio's PoS machines would also help retailers with invoicing, billing, and managing inventory.
Now, digitising kiranas via the PoS is a hill many startups have chosen to die on in the past. It has some stubborn patterns that startups, time and again, hoped to change but barely could—from reluctant retailers and maintenance costs to keeping things off the books, among the others. Moreover, QR code-based payments, meant to take payments to the long tail of merchants, were growing.
But Jio was no ordinary startup. It had the might of its telco network to take the PoS to as many cities and villages as it did with its telco network. Plus, it followed the telco playbook of freebies to lure in the kiranas. Similarly unsurprising was its ambitious goal of deploying 150,000 machines in six months.
As we head into 2021, however, there are barely… 20,000 of Jio’s PoS deployed. “The software was buggy, and Jio’s network was also bad here, making the entire process time-consuming,” a retailer, who returned his Jio PoS, said.
In today’s well-reported and insightful story, Pranav finds out what ails Jio PoS, what its chances are, and what kind of a future it has. In a way, it also answers our earlier question: "What would you do if Jio decides to compete with your startup?"

Good Morning Ishan,
There are IPOs and then there are IPOs.
Who can forget Resourceful Automobile, a motorcycle dealer with two showrooms and eight employees whose share sale made jaws drop last year?
The company wanted to raise Rs 12 crore but got bids for Rs 4,800 crore. SME flotations like this are often get-rich-quick schemes for investors.
Unlike larger companies whose IPOs have to be greenlit by the stock-market regulator, SMEs approach the National Stock Exchange and BSE (formerly the Bombay Stock Exchange) directly.
But the bourses cannot possibly vet all the SMEs eager to go public. In FY25 alone, nearly 240 SMEs listed on the exchanges.
Unsurprisingly, artificial-intelligence screening tools are the natural next step. And Onfinance, a Kunal Shah-backed company that builds AI agents for compliance, wants to be the first port of call. BSE uses its agents, and NSE had a partnership with it in late 2024. But after The Ken asked NSE about it, all records of the association disappeared from the internet, including on Onfinance’s blog and Linkedin page.
I can’t for the life of me understand the secrecy. But BSE claims the process of verifying IPO documents now takes half an hour, compared with three weeks earlier.
Even so, how far can AI agents truly go in the stock exchanges’ quest for investor protection? Abhirami has the [story](https://the-ken.com/story/how-bse-is-using-ai-to-sniff-out-rotten-ipos/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter).

Bigbasket, Blinkit, Instamart, and Zepto are all fighting to capture more and more share of the quick-commerce market. But as they scale, a new set of players fulfilling one use case in minutes is entering the market with hype and funding.
Does this mean we will see an unbundling of quick commerce from being an everything store to having separate apps for everything? Or is it just another cycle of unbundling and bundling?

Most large Indian software (and services) companies earn the bulk of their revenue from outside India. Beyond Tally, it’s hard to think of a product company that scaled by selling primarily to Indian businesses. Even SaaS success stories like Zoho, Freshworks, and Chargebee follow the same playbook. Tech-led consumer platforms like Zomato, Swiggy, Ola thrive, but not by selling software to Indian enterprises. The economics don’t favour it. Our cost structures — developer salaries, tools, infrastructure — are built around getting paid in USD. But Indian clients often expect world-class solutions at throwaway prices. In one project I recently advised, the IT service company delivered improvements to the existing system and produced a detailed BRD for the next phase. After months of work, the business simply walked away — no complaints, no disputes, just a refusal to pay. This isn’t about funding. It’s a mindset. Until we value what’s being built and pay for the effort, “Skip India” will remain the default, not the exception. |

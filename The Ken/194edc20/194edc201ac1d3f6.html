A disruptor finally becoming what it disrupted.

Wework’s investors will hope for just that when the Indian franchisee of the beleaguered shared-workspace company goes public in the coming months.

The market leader, majority owned by property developer Embassy Group, recently filed documents for the share sale, which could reportedly see it being valued at $2–2.5 billion.

When Wework launched its India operations in 2017, it set the tone for plush, exquisitely designed co-working spaces aimed at early-stage startups and their investors. The kind that wouldn’t go for more than 50 desks.

But Wework’s competitors soon realised the only way they could scale was by targeting larger clients who would want entire floors custom-made for them instead of sharing space with other companies.

So, the flexi-office providers turned out to be not all that different from the landlords in commercial districts they took aim at.

That 60% of Wework rival Awfis’ total capacity is managed offices is partly why its shares are up by three-quarters since they began trading in May 2024. Enterprise clients can, and want to, hold on to their offices longer than startups—a fact not lost on Wework or its potential investors.

But can it ditch what made it a pioneer and instead join those that it once inspired?

Gaurav set out in search of the answer in this sharp take.

Conquering the friendly, neighbourhood kirana (mom-and-pop store) and tapping their stocks to deliver orders placed online is a sword multiple startups have fallen on. But somehow, wave after wave of companies want to tame that beast.
When Reliance Industries walked into the room with $27 billion, everyone knew that if there was one entity that could win over the indomitable kiranas, it is RIL, with its digital commerce platform JioMart. It was going to be the one-stop shop, both for the kirana and the customer. Meaning, users could order online via JioMart and get their orders delivered from the closest kirana. And it would make itself indispensable to the kiranas by supplying inventory. They could even order their inventory online via JioMart's point of sale device.
JioMart had all the pieces in place to take this segment by storm—a network of 800 Reliance supermarkets to supply from, a supposedly close integration with Facebook-owned WhatsApp (Facebook is an investor in a Reliance arm) to let users order on, and high-margin private labels that it wanted kiranas to stock up on.
Now, one year into JioMart's journey, Seetharaman and Pranav look at what the oil-to-telecom giant has been able to achieve and ask some hard questions.
The big news is that JioMart beat India’s largest online grocer, BigBasket, on the number of orders. It had 330,000 orders a day in December, while JioMart had an average of 500,000. But when it comes to average order value and gross merchandise value, JioMart has a lot of catching up to do.
The complexities of handling online deliveries via kiranas are also catching up with JioMart. Even as it delivers grocery in over 200 cities, it works with kiranas in only 33. Distributors, who have a close relationship with kiranas, aren’t impressed with the way it is supplying inventory to them. For instance, a distributor added that while JioMart onboarded around 9,500 stores in Chennai, it does active billing only with a third of those.

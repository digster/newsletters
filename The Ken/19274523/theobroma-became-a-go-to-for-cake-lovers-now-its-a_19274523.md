Good Morning Ishan,
A great croissant is airy and decadent but not filling. A well-made brownie can be fudgy, chewy, or cakey, depending on your preference.
Theobroma is feeding Indians who have a sweet tooth for pastries, cakes, and other desserts. Its 225 branches’ decor looks like something out of a Wes Anderson movie, and the chain commands a valuation of Rs 2,800–3,500 crore, or US$330–410 million, up to 9X its revenue for the year ended March 2023.
Those figures matter because both Theobroma’s founder Kainaz Messman and major backer, ICICI Ventures, are likely to sell their stakes to a new investor. Chrys Capital, Bain Capital, and Carlyle Group are already in line to seal a deal and take over the two-decade-old patisserie.
Long-time customers, meanwhile, have noticed that Theobroma is shrinking its portion sizes and hiking prices. It’s also stocking fewer perishable items, like the baked goods that made the chain stand out in the first place. All of that is to improve its margins. Besides, many of Theobroma’s branches don’t have in-house kitchens, to the detriment of the experience of being in a pastry shop, some visitors say.
Even so, Theobroma has written a recipe for success where others have overbaked their strategies. Au Bon Pain’s muffins and bagels didn’t impress, so the American bakery chain had to leave India in 2017. Dunkin Donuts’ glazed goods brought on a sugar crash, and it has since rebranded to focus on brewing coffee.

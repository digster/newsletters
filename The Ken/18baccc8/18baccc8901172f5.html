Good Morning Ishan,

“We didn’t have a choice, so we had to take their terms.”

“If we bend this time, this will become the new normal.”

These seemingly contradictory quotes, courtesy of two venture capital (VC) partners, are enough to give you a glimpse into how a state-run fund of funds is turning the VC world on its head.

For those not glued to business newsfeeds, the Small Industries Development Bank of India (Sidbi) received a billion-dollar infusion from the government seven years ago to sprinkle among over 100 VC funds, benefiting nearly 1,000 startups in the process.

Things were going smoothly until last April when it made it amply clear where it stood on the management fee that it pays to the VC funds.

Sidbi’s logic: “It’s time for VC funds to improve efficiency.” It presents compelling arguments, but the impacts are far-reaching.

For one, VC funds raise the question, “How do we keep the lights on and draw the brightest minds if we get a reduced fee?” Limited partners join the chorus, staking their claim. “If any of our funds take a 1.5% fee from Sidbi, we’ll be seeking a similar deal,” insists one.

And it’s not just about the fees; despite some issues, VC funds trust their largest domestic investor for its “integrity” and “transparency”—making it tough to sever ties.

In a story that transcends boardrooms and investor meetings, Arundhati gives voice to both sides of the debate and unravels the potential impact on India’s startup ecosystem.

Good Morning Ishan,

We do not usually go into the machinations of policymaking at The Ken, but this was too important to ignore. The backstory of how one of the most controversial legislations of Modi government got written.

If clubbed with the ModiCare announcement last week, of insuring 500 million people in the country, healthcare in India won’t be the same.

The National Medical Commission Bill unveiled in late December provoked doctors across the country to take to the street in protest on 2 January. It's now under the review of a Parliamentary Standing Committee. The report is likely to be out this week and sources tell Ruhi, the writer of today’s story, that the Committee will back the Bill. Because the backlash from the 200,000-strong doctor community and many others was well accounted for when the legislation was being written by the think tank NITI Aayog. 

Apparently, the Prime Minister, who is also the chairman of the think tank, said: “Take the bitter pill”.

The bitter pill being: lowering the quality of doctors, deregulating the medical education industry (worth $8.72 billion in two years), and letting Ayurveda or Homeopathy doctors prescribe allopathic medicines after a bridge course. In one fell swoop, it purports to fix one of the biggest problems—doctor shortage.

For years, different governments have tried to address this. But without any luck. How did Modi government manage to sneak in (or bulldoze, if you will) its ideas?

The stakes are high. Healthcare is finally getting centre stage in Indian politics.

Good Morning Ishan,
The name Shaik Salauddin may not mean much to you. But this 38-year-old cab driver is highly regarded among India’s 8 million gig workers, and, in turn, a thorn in the side of platforms like Zomato, Amazon, and Uber.
The Hyderabad-based driver heads the Indian Federation of App-based Transport Workers (IFAT), which has over 25,000 members. And this five-year-old entity has an approach to worker welfare that is unlike the trade unions of the past, known for their political ties and strikes.
Instead, it uses consultations and a data-based approach to find solutions to problems—from workers’ social security to companies tampering with EV scooters’ odometers.
This change in the way unions operate couldn’t have come at a better time. India needs its labour unions now more than ever, especially since workers don’t have many opportunities to present their issues.
Also, the country’s unemployment rate has jumped to 9.2% in 2024 from about 6% three years ago, and India’s labour-participation rate stands still at about 40% for the last three years. All this, when the labour budget is falling with every passing year.

"Make something that people want."
This aphorism by Y Combinator's Paul Graham has long been a Northstar credo for startups the world over. The essence of this statement is that a startup's product won't see demand unless it is something that the market "wants".
By this measure and several more, online furniture startup, Urban Ladder nailed the brief.
The company's products were not just well-regarded and well-liked, they were loved by the market. Customers raved about product quality and swore undying loyalty.
Equally importantly, unlike most other startups, its unit economics also seemed to make sense with the company turning in a healthy Rs 50 crore profit for 2019.
Yet, Urban Ladder was recently acquired by Reliance in what can only be regarded as a firesale—a ticket price of just over $25 million—equivalent to 61% of its 2019 topline and just 23% of the total VC funding.
How and why did this happen?
In today's story, Rohin goes beyond the cosmetic headlines and unpacks the market, competition, and investor dynamics that led to Urban Ladder's unravelling.

Good Morning Ishan,
 
Karnataka elections are over; the BJP has won. Let’s move on.
 
For doctors or lawyers, years of experience is a badge. In tech, it is a deadweight.
 
Suddenly, professionals with 15-20 years of work experience, who thought they had neatly laid out their career paths, are faced with a turn of events that speaks as much about the industry’s ability to foresee the dislocation as it does about the profound societal change that lies ahead.
 
Today’s story is about the ‘invisible class’, borrowing the term from the sociologist Shiv Vishwanathan. It’s a story we’ve discussed for weeks at The Ken, getting enraged, gloomy, and hopeful in the process.
 
As layoffs in tech become common, so does the bias. In the age of savvy HR, companies are loath to admit this, but the proxy of age is years of experience, which, search and recruitment firms admit, few companies want today.
 
This bit from the story is revelatory:
 
“Fifty,” said Maiya. “Fifty a day,” said Imran. “Seventy,” said Arulselvan, “Eighty,” said Singh.
 
These are the number of companies they applied to for jobs. And here is how many interviews they got called back to. “Four.” “Two.” “Two.” “Two”….
 
Tech’s middle-aged crisis is not an Indian phenomenon. In March, a ProPublica investigation showed how IBM systematically reduced its fatty layer by laying off more than 20K people. In China, it starts early, at 30. However, each region is leaving its own footprint in the crisis. How is tech in India, that employs more than 4 million, faring?
 
To be fair, this is playing out in other industries as well. The editor of a large news network sent out an email on 1 January saying they must replace outgoing 40-somethings with 20-somethings.
 
Is 40 the new 60?

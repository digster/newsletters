Meesho Mall isn’t doing much to change Meesho’s image. But the e-commerce player is growing it anyway

Meesho cut its teeth as an online marketplace for small sellers—the teeming thousands left out of the glitz and glamour of branded commerce, and yet, the force behind a large majority of India’s market. 

It worked. Meesho is a veritable giant in the country’s e-commerce landscape, last valued at $3.9 billion and gearing up for a $1 billion initial public offering (IPO) that could value it at $10 billion. 

But Meesho has, for a while now, also wanted its share of the branded pie. 

And Meesho Mall was supposed to be the knife that cut out a nice, hefty slice.

Except, it’s not doing it very well. 

There are good reasons for Meesho wanting more “big brand” presence in its marketplace—they’re easier to monetise; searches on the platform indicate there’s quite a bit of interest, if not significant demand, for branded goods in smaller towns; and they can be more profitable in some cases. 

But even just months after Mall’s 2023 launch, it became clear that this wouldn’t be easy. You should read our January 2024 story for a detailed breakdown, but this is the gist: 

With Mall, Meesho envisioned a model along the lines of other global players like China’s Taobao and Singapore’s Shopee, which took similar steps into the branded segment with Tmall and Shopee Mall, respectively, said the company spokesperson. Mall would ideally drive a larger share of Meesho’s profits as brands can be monetised more effectively than small businesses, they added.

But brands have expectations higher than smaller sellers. And it’s something that Meesho isn’t fully equipped to handle—just yet. The platform is missing the mark on good returns on ads, reliable analytics, and checks on fake products.

Recent conversations I’ve had with people at Meesho suggest things haven’t changed very much since. 

The company claims that in the last six months, Meesho Mall drew 3.2 crore shoppers and onboarded 1,000-plus brands. A couple of weeks ago, it announced a partnership that would add leading personal care brands like Pampers, Head & Shoulders, Pantene, Ponds, Vaseline, Tresemmé, and Himalaya to this list. According to one current and one former Mall manager that The Ken spoke to, Meesho is determined to grow Mall further, especially given its planned IPO. 

But Mall has had little impact on Meesho’s IPO talks or valuation calculations. A person close to the company who did not want to be named said: 

“It’s an important vertical because we want to capture this 20% branded share, but it’s a long-term thing rather than short-term.”

Dhiresh Bansal, CFO, Meesho

The pricing challenge

“Meesho’s platform is very price-sensitive, so it cannot be designed for something like Mall, where you have branded goods,” the current manager told me. 

Most people shopping on Meesho don’t really care much about branding—after all, Meesho’s sellers are known for their cheaper dupes of a lot of branded products. So price remains king. 

Meesho, for its part, has tried to get big brands to tinker with their pricing. A few months ago, Megha Agarwal, Meesho’s CXO of Business, told ET Retail: 

“Our approach is centered around the user. Tier-2 and beyond markets continue to be our key focus, representing over 75 per cent of the demand for Meesho Mall. Brands that truly understood Meesho’s value proposition and adapted their offerings to focus on value experienced remarkable growth.”

But not all brands are open to adapting.

“Brands would prefer to have the same pricing across platforms, so that can’t be the moat for a brand to grow on Meesho,” said the current manager. “Either they will sell new kinds of SKUs, which they don’t sell anywhere online, or they will do something else. If I were them, I would not have negative margins on one channel versus the other,” said another source close to the company.

Which means that if it wants the price of branded goods to drop on its platform, it’s Meesho that will have to burn a lot of cash. And the company “doesn’t want to do that”. 

Meesho’s plan for Mall was to start making inroads category by category, to ensure that at least some portion of consumer spending would also happen on branded products. For instance, in beauty and personal care currently, branded products currently make up the majority of sales, a person close to the company said. 

But things may not work as well in many other segments. 

Mall doesn’t necessarily have to show comparable growth with the unbranded business, said the former manager, because it is a good add-on for the platform, widening its customer base and increasing its gross merchandise value (GMV). 

But if Meesho was hoping Mall would turn it into a branded-goods powerhouse, there’s a long way to go yet.

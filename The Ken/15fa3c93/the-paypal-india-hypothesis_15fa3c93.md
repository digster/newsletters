Good Morning Ishan,
I’ve two questions for you, the answer to both of which is the same.
A. Say you’re an Indian business. An online seller. How much additional order conversion do you reckon you could get simply by switching to a new payment provider?
B. How much inefficiency do you think exists within the hugely competitive payment processing market in India?
Answer: 35%. And that is the PayPal India Hypothesis.
“We can deliver 35% higher conversion for merchants by helping simplify the checkout process and also by making sure we reduce the failure rates,” says Anupam Pahuja, head of PayPal India.
PayPal launched its domestic payments business in the crowded, overfunded and competitive Indian market on Wednesday (it must have won some brownie points for picking the first anniversary of the Indian government’s decision to “demonetise” 86% of its currency).
The launch was the easy part though. PayPal is now up against a host of formidable, nimble and well-funded opponents, ranging from payment providers like PayU and Razorpay; wallet companies like Paytm and Mobikwik; and Flipkart-owned PhonePe. Then there’s the joker in the pack—the runaway hit, UPI, an instant and near-free money transfer protocol promoted by the government itself.
It takes chutzpah to stick your neck out in such a market and promise up to 35% additional business, even if you say “up to*” instead of “up to”.
PayPal says it will bring its experience of handling payments from over 200 countries to bear in India. Its competitors say India is a rather different beast than all else. Only one may end up right.

The demand dead-cat bounce
 
Savio
 
Parle-G, India’s top-selling biscuit maker, just reported stellar sales for March, April, and May. It is quite clearly in the minority. Still, after a turbulent three months, some companies are noticing a revival of demand in the past week or so. E-commerce firms like Flipkart and Amazon, phone and electronics makers, shoe companies, and even those in the hapless auto industry all expect this demand to pick up as India opens its shutters.
 
How is this happening when a host of economic indicators are pointing south? The most common explanation is this: the unleashing of the demand that built up during the long and severe lockdown. Remember, the lack of commerce in the past few months was not due to low demand.
The pandemic began by crushing the economy’s productive capacity—a shock to the supply side of the economy, as many types of business activity were shut down for public health concerns.
 
In normal times, when there is a negative supply shock (say, a year of drought that reduces agricultural crops, or new tariffs that make imports more expensive), the pain can be intense for people in sectors directly affected, yet the economy as a whole adjusts.
Don’t Lose the Thread. The Economy Is Experiencing an Epic Collapse of Demand, The New York Times
But this time is different because the supply-side shock has trickled down into the demand side as well. The work-from-home movement is no longer just lip service, so why would one buy formals or even cars? There are job losses and furloughs galore, while whole industries—like hospitality and aviation—face an existential crises. This has a ripple effect on other sectors as well.
 
The result is that the disruption to the supply side has transformed into a collapse of demand, three economists argue in a recent paper. The authors present a theory of Keynesian supply shocks: supply shocks that trigger changes in aggregate demand larger than the shocks themselves.
We then turn to incomplete markets and show that the condition for a contraction in employment in unaffected sectors becomes less stringent.  Intuitively, if workers in the affected sectors lose their jobs and income, their consumption drops significantly if they are credit constrained and have high MPCs. To make up for this, workers in the unaffected sectors would have to increase their consumption of the remaining goods sufficiently. This Requires a higher degree of substitution across sectors. If goods are not too close substitutes, aggregate demand contracts more than supply and employment in the unaffected sectors falls.
 
[...]
 
As some initial set of businesses is shut down (e.g. restaurants),e.g. due to health concerns, Keynesian forces reduce demand for other businesses (e.g. car dealers) as well.  This, however, might mean that some of the other businesses become unable or unwilling to remain open. When they close, however, laying off their workers, a new, endogenous, Keynesian supply shock is born, that amplifies the existing exogenous one, creating a multiplier effect that may be sufficiently strong to shut down most of the economy.
This is the graphical representation of their argument.
This demand shock will have a lag effect and will show up gradually over the coming months. There are already signs of it in the economy. For example, the lockdown has little to no effect on demand for second-hand products, while e-commerce sales and home-services aggregators demand show people are gearing up to stay at home longer.
 
Consumer behaviour is changing, with more people trading down to cheaper brands in discretionary categories and trading up for staples and household care. I would argue that the definitions of discretionary and necessary have changed. Impulse purchases are falling, and it doesn’t augur well for demand down the line.
 
In financial markets, there is something called the “dead-cat bounce.” It refers to a small, short-lived recovery in prices after a steep and prolonged decline. It is derived from the idea that even a dead cat will bounce if it falls from a great height.
 
I wonder if that is what is happening now with the current demand uptick.
Revisionist (GDP) history
 
Rohin

Last Friday, India released GDP growth estimates for the January-March 2020 quarter. At 3.1%, it was the slowest the country has grown at for the last 40 quarters.
 
But 3.1% is still pretty good, considering the state the world and India is in. Some economists were expecting it to be -1.5%. Or is it?
 
You might be tempted to ask, “Was that really India’s GDP growth rate?”
 
And the relevant authorities would answer, “Well, that’s hard to say conclusively in the midst of what is probably the worst economic and societal disruption the country has seen since independence in 1947.”
 
“Okay. What was its growth rate in the three quarters between April 2019 and December 2019, before Covid-19 hit? Surely, that should be known?”
 
“Of course. Those were 5.2%, 4.4% and 4.1% respectively.”
 
“Wait, what? But you’d said those were 5.6%, 5.1% and 4.7%? How did they fall further?”
 
“It’s perfectly normal. What we’re now giving you are revised estimates.”
 
“So you won’t revise them again?”
 
“On the contrary, we’ll have to revise them a few more times.”
 
“Why?”
 
“Because we’re unable to get proper data from economic entities due to the Covid-19 pandemic and the lockdown. When they do submit their data, we’ll issue another set of revised estimates.”
 
“Fine! I guess Covid-19 is a valid reason. Thankfully we can rely on historical GDP growth rates for earlier years like 2017 and 2018. They were 7.1% and 6.5% right?”
 
“About that…”
 
“What?”
 
“Yeah, well we revised those too. They’re now 8.3% and 7%.”
 
“But, but…how can you revise GDP growth that’s 2-3 years old?”
 
“Oh, we’re allowed to keep revising past GDP data up to six times and over 36 months.”
 
“That. Doesn’t. Make. Sense!”
 
A.K Bhattacharya in Business Standard tries, in vain, to unravel India’s revisionist GDP history.
Few other countries follow such a long-winded process of revising economic output data. The US, for instance, has only three iterations of the GDP data for a period and these are released wi­thin just three months.

But in India, it is this long period of data release with six iterations that results in mind-boggling confusion for policymakers. Which GDP data should they use for framing their policies? For instance, in January 2017, the GDP data for 2016-17 was pegged at 7.1 per cent. Three years later, the GDP growth for that year was revised upwards to 8.3 per cent. Something similar happened with the GDP data for 2017-18. In January 2018, the growth was estimated at 6.5 per cent, but two years later in January 2020, the Second Revised Estimates put the growth at 7 per cent. It is unclear if the Third Revised Estimates, to be out in January 2021, will revise this number again.
Immunity boosters or sales boosters?

Vandana

Immunity-boosting food is a thing these days. If you haven't read multiple WhatsApp forwards on how the secret to good immunity lies in your kitchen, fret not. Mother Dairy has you sorted.
 
The Delhi-based dairy company has launched turmeric milk that claims to deliver the same benefit as taking one teaspoon of turmeric powder in milk. And it's not just Mother Dairy, everyone from restaurants to companies like Dabur and ITC are trying out the season’s new flavour.
 
In Mumbai, the corporation body BMC went a step ahead and distributed Arsenicum Album 30, a homeopathy drug that supposedly boosts immunity to people residing in containment and red zones. Various state governments have also taken up the mantle of boosting their citizens' immunity, either by distributing so-called "immunity boosting drugs" or the informally advised ayurvedic health supplements such as chyavanprash and kadhas.
 
The problem with all the food, drugs, and concoctions is that effectiveness has not been medically proven. These fads aren’t doing the rounds only in India, however. Probiotics and multi-vitamins have become all the rage in the US as well.
 
"The medical profession still doesn’t know exactly how to influence the immune system despite what supplement products may claim," says Julie Stefanski, a registered dietitian nutritionist and spokeswoman for the Academy of Nutrition & Dietetics in an article in The Washington Post.
 
In India too, the scientific fraternity has been watching this clamour around immunity boosters. And in April, a group of scientists released a statement against the promotion of such products.
"Citing lack of scientific evidence, scientists said consuming mustard oil or tea, homeopathic solutions or ayurvedic preparations, or modern medicines like hydroxychloroquine that are claimed to provide either immunity or cure “do not provide any known and/or validated protection against Covid-19”
Scientists bust myths about "immunity- boosting" substances in fighting Covid-19, The Hindustan Times
These products encourage a false sense of safety, but the lack of regulations governing them is a bigger problem. The FSSAI (Food Safety and Standards Authority of India) has remained silent on all these claims.
"The Food Safety and Standards Authority of India (FSSAI) regulates dietary supplements and other food products but its various regulations don’t touch on claims about enhancing immunity. Instead, the authority simply allows manufacturers to make claims about effects on the immune system, and manufacturers are not required to report what they say unless the FSSAI specifically asks for them. Finally, the FSSAI imposes no preconditions to making such claims either."
 
"On the other hand, the US Food and Drug Administration has issued multiple advisories and warning letters against advertising claims about boosting immunity vis-à-vis COVID-19"
As products pose as "immunity boosters", the government looks on, The Wire Science
However, in a country where a government ministry authorises Ayurveda-based immunity-boosting products, there can hardly be any expectations. Or maybe there should be. What better time than now to update the rules and regulations and definitions?

Data: Economies are reopening

Jon
 
China, Australia, and Germany are among the first countries to return to ‘normal’ following the removal of lockdown measures, according to mapping company TomTom.
 
Digital mapping services are integral to modern life and therefore an ideal (and qualitative) lens to view the reopening of economies. TomTom’s congestion data for G20 countries—tweeted by Bloomberg’s chief economist Tom Orlik—paints a vivid picture of the impact of lockdowns across the world from the initial implementation of restrictions on movement to the lifting in recent times.

Insider trading gets more complicated

Shreedhar
 
Executives higher up at listed companies have to be very careful about who they speak to about the goings-on at their firm. Speak the wrong thing, at the wrong time, and they might just be guilty of insider trading. At its simplest, insider trading refers to using privileged information to make decisions of profit, such as selling (or buying) stock.
 
Working from home has changed the dynamics of insider trading. What would have been a closed-room meeting earlier is now a video meeting with participants spread across the country or even the world.
 
This creates a whole new third participant in the meetings that can be privy to sensitive information—the team running the IT infrastructure.
Office IT departments have access to content of video meetings run on company infrastructure. And companies are waking up to this, asking their IT employees to comply with insider trading prevention rules.
 
A leading paint company recently asked 35 employees from its IT team to disclose market investments made by them and their immediate family members.
“Our compliance team indicated that IT department employees who have access to sensitive information need to be treated as designated persons under insider trading rules,” said an official of the company. “It’s not just price sensitive information, during our internal meetings we also tend to discuss our vendors, suppliers, which themselves are listed companies.”
Covid creates new class of insiders, The Economic Times
Insider trading was always murky. What constitutes insider trading is difficult to deduce. Last year, the Securities and Exchange Board of India, even formalised a mechanism to reward informants that helped in getting convictions.
 
And things don’t get better now that the lockdown is being eased around the world.
Ironically, the easing of lockdowns may make some of these problems worse. Many corporate workers will start having face-to-face contact with more people, but won’t yet be returning to their secure offices. That increases the opportunity for mischief—and inadvertent leaks.
 
“The immediate market volatility is passed, but this insider dealing issue is not going away,” says Nick Bayley, a managing director at consultancy Duff & Phelps. “At the moment, it is the traders who are going back to working in the office, but banks may well want to prioritise their deal teams because there is nothing safer than sticking them in a room behind a locked door.”
Home working makes spilling corporate secrets all the easier, The Financial Times
How to hug—a step by step guide

Arundhati
 
“do it quickly.” ..“When you are done, don’t linger.”..  “Wash your hands afterward”..
 
If you were wondering what those instructions were for, it is for the most underrated, feel-good human connection that the coronavirus put the kibosh on with its social distancing ways.
 
But if it's some hug therapy you are really yearning for, an aerosol scientist The New York Times spoke to has a way.
 
Wear a mask. Hug outdoors. Try to avoid touching the other person’s body or clothes with your face and your mask. Don’t hug someone who is coughing or has other symptoms.

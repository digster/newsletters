"A barren hellscape."
That's how a friend's precocious daughter recently described her online school classes. Turns out, the pandemic has achieved what educators across the world have struggled with for centuries; having experienced the isolation of a nationwide lockdown, India's children cannot wait to go back to school.
And, as Olina writes in today's story, schools are equally desperate for this. Perhaps even more so. Especially the 270,000 affordable private schools (APS) that cater to around 1 in 3 Indian students. Because while more affluent schools have been able to transition online fairly seamlessly, APSs—which generally cater to lower-income families and do so with meagre resources—have been hamstrung by the pandemic.
Teachers still have to be paid. The same goes for rent and other overheads. Fees, however, are a whole other matter. While state governments have allowed APSs to continue collecting fees, this comes with a caveat—they must continue providing services. But how do schools with such limited resources do this? And do lower-income families even have the means to avail online lessons?
These questions may be moot, though. Because parents, even those who may still be able to afford school fees, are hesitant to pay in these uncertain times. Some are shifting their children to government schools.

Good Morning Ishan,
 
It’s the start of a new week, and I can’t help but remember what Adam Smith once said about “managers of other people’s money”. They couldn’t be trusted to be as prudent with it as they were with their own. His words never rang truer than at the turn of the millennium when financial crises hit the roof and sound accounting was in short supply.
 
The tide is turning in India. There’s a spate of resignations from accounting firms. Since January this year, more than 30 firms have resigned as auditors of companies midterm, compared with seven such resignations in the same period last year. On Saturday, auditors of the renewable energy company INOX Wind resigned, citing ‘time constraint’ as the reason.
 
 
Most of the news reports have also been like auditors’ resignations, without much explanation. Meanwhile, shareholders are seeking answers from auditors on the reasons for abrupt resignation.
 
Are these resignations a sign that India’s auditing firms have suddenly developed a conscience? Is it fear? If the Satyam Computers scam (of cooked books) nine years ago was a watershed moment in Indian accounting, demonetisation of 2016 was another, when scores of CAs were arrested.
 
So things had to change. On 1 July, 2017, PM Modi put the accounting fraternity of notice. Many CAs believe there’s a political angle to the harsher regulatory approach.
 
Are the resignations now the CAs way of giving it back to the government?
 
It’s a complex situation. Holding capitalism to account, reporting tax evasion or exposing risks to the economy are not exactly an audit firm’s stated KRAs. However, the resignations are a good starting point because even in major scandals, the media spotlight is rarely on auditors. But Vivek Ananth, our newest writer, doesn’t shy away from it. A qualified chartered accountant, Vivek puts the current resignation phenomenon in perspective.

Poor Gaana. Until a few years ago, it looked as if it had the Indian music streaming space stitched up. Together with arch-rival Saavn, they had the lion’s share of the market. Then the earth caved in as player after player joined the party like a [Marshmello show inside Fortnite](https://www.bbc.com/news/blogs-trending-47116429). Airtel, Apple, Google, Amazon, Jio…and Spotify. All battling for a share of India’s music streaming market that was last estimated at less than $90 million in 2017 (Jio’s data jamboree would’ve made it jump by now).
From being the #1 player in a 2.5-player market, it became the poorest in a 7-player battle. So it started looking for newer battles to fight. Ones preferably with larger markets and a smaller number of competitors, thus ending up with YouTube in its crosshairs.
Because, first, video is booming. An Ernst & Young report estimates 500 million online video users by 2020. Times Internet’s own MX Player claims usage of 2 million video minutes every minute on its platform.
And then, YouTube was having it too good. Even at its size, it said it was doubling its monthly active users annually. To make matters worse, even though it wasn’t a music service, it had captured 14% of the music listening market without even trying. Heck, Gaana’s own users were spending more time when they had access to YouTube’s music videos.
So, Gaana wants to take the fight to YouTube. Harveen tells us that it is close to launching a music video offering of its own, in an attempt to both stand out in the crowded music streaming market and to try and extract better ad rates from advertisers (video pays better than audio). It’s either an audacious or a desperate plan. What do you think?

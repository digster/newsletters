In fact, Home Credit isn’t many things. It isn’t Indian, because it's a subsidiary of a Czech Republic-based lending giant that was set to raise $1 billion via a Hong Kong IPO last November, before the pro-democracy movement there put the kibosh on those plans. The valuation it was seeking was $10 billion.

In India, the largely unreported company has been following a super contrarian strategy for the last 8 years and racking up 10 million customers. A strategy that seems like a borderline death wish for a lender.

Because Home Credit lends either to millions of Indians with no credit history or to those whose credit histories are “subprime”, meaning their CIBIL credit scores are less than 600 (good borrowers have scores of 750-900). As though that wasn’t enough, it charges 0% to lend to people who want to buy mobile phones, losing money on each such loan in the process. Its bad loans ratio is 8%, compared to its peers' 3-5%.

And yet, with all that baggage, Home Credit has built a loan book of nearly $1.2 billion, and after 8 years of losses, it started making profits in 2019!

It. Doesn’t. Make. Sense.

And yet, it does somehow.

In a wonderful story, Arundhati reports on one of Indian fintech’s biggest secrets - the contrarian that wilfully defies every single rule in the rulebook, instead preferring to write its own. Including its own credit scores.

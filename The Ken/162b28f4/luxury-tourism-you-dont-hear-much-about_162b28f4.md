Good Morning Ishan,
Last week, Salman Khan, the 52-year old Bollywood actor who’s arguably its biggest “superstar”, was jailed briefly for two days for illegally hunting a protected species of deer in 1998. (Yes, that’s how fast the wheels of justice turn). But in May 2013, he was inaugurating an ultra luxury 5-star hospital in Delhi—the Fortis Memorial Research Institute—complete with a theatre, swimming pool, gym, spa, swanky shops and chefs baking fresh bread every day. (C’est la vie, eh?)
Unlike most (corporate) hospitals which take roughly 18 months to break even, FMRI broke even in a mere four months. Its secret? Learning to say no.
FMRI decided to say no to poorer patients subsidised by government-funded health or insurance schemes. It also said no to discounted corporate clients. Instead, right from day 1, it turned its attention to foreign patients. FMRI has turned conventional hospital wisdom on its head. From Ruhi’s story today, "A regular hospital starts to fill up beds with discounted business and then replaces them with medical tourists. Even then, it would hover at around 70% occupancy. FMRI attained 100% occupancy within four months; a third of the beds were occupied by international patients.”
Fascinating, isn’t it? Most big Indian hospitals reserve 10-20% of their beds for foreign patients, even when there’s no visibility. Why? Because they can show up any time.
Anyhow, today’s story isn’t on FMRI, but instead on the exploding world of medical tourism in India. And on the efforts of companies like medECUBE, a “care coordination concierge” or “medical tourism service provider”, depending on your POV. Its founder is the very same person who started and led FMRI to success. Can she repeat it on the other side of the equation? It’s not easy.

India’s logistics sector has never been as hot as it is now. 

A space that once whispered with the quiet strides of legacy names like Blue Dart, DTDC, Gati, Fedex, ABC, and Concor now thunders with the racket of a new breed of players. M&As here, market debuts there, sheer startup energy, and investor attention. 

In the last five years alone, Indian logistics startups have attracted over $4 billion in investor money. The advance of first D2C e-commerce, and then quick commerce, has pushed names like Delhivery, Ecom Express, Blackbuck, Xpressbees, Shiprocket, and Shadowfax into the limelight—startups that promise tech-first logistics, national networks, and lightning-fast deliveries.

Delhivery led this new pack into the stock markets in 2022, and though the stock hasn’t done particularly well since (it’s trading about 20% below its issue price), more are starting to follow. 

Last month, Shadowfax, a third-party logistics (3PL) company, filed confidential IPO papers with Sebi. Reports say it is aiming to raise between Rs 2,000–2,500 crore at a likely valuation of Rs 8,500 crore. Just five months earlier, it had raised fresh capital at a Rs 6,000 crore valuation.

Shiprocket, a fourth-party logistics (4PL) company, has also filed a confidential draft red herring prospectus. Unlike 3PL operations, which usually take care of functions such as warehousing and transport, 4PL companies manage the entire supply chain. Shiprocket plans to raise a similar Rs 2,000–2,500 crore, but Sebi has asked for clarifications, which could delay things. The company was last valued at over Rs 10,000 crore in December 2024, when it had raised Rs 222 crore. 

Another 3PL player—Xpressbees—is also said to have appointed investment banks such as JP Morgan and Barclays to begin IPO preparations. 

It’s understandable why these companies may fancy their chances now. Quick commerce and D2C have opened up large new markets. Even as early as FY24, industry tracker Redseer placed quick commerce gross merchandise value (GMV) at $4 billion, with over 600 million shipments. Over the next five years, shipments are expected to grow by 50–60%, with average order values increasing due to an expansion into non-grocery categories and higher-price products. 

And thus, a flush of new IPOs and heady optimism. 

But what’s missing—these IPO listings are “confidential” after all—are signs of sustained profitability.

Profits versus parcels

Older players in the logistics sector stand in stark contrast here. 

Delhivery, which claims to be India’s largest 3PL, finally recorded its first full year of profitability in FY25—making Rs 162 crore, to be exact, from a total revenue of around Rs 9,000 crore. But that profit came after years of losses and hundreds of millions of dollars in venture capital investment. 

Meanwhile, legacy players such as DTDC and Blue Dart have shown how advantageous “slow and steady” can be. DTDC has been largely profitable over the last decade. Its revenue for FY25 is estimated to have hit Rs 2,500 crore, recording a year-on-year growth of 21%. 

That may not sound flashy, but it reflects measured, cash-driven growth, much like the company has seen over its three decades of existence.

“In all these 35 years of growth that we’ve done, we’ve never raised any capital. We’ve continued to run the company very strongly, pumping all our profits back into the business and keep finding growth.”

Abhishek Chakraborty, CEO, DTDC

Blue Dart, for its part, recorded Rs 5,700 crore in revenue in FY25 and posted a profit of Rs 244 crore—50% more than Delhivery.

So their scale may be smaller, but the economics are solid, even if profits have moderated over the last few years. And now, as capital starts to become more expensive and scrutiny more intense, the market is beginning to favour such discipline. 

Chakraborty puts it simply: in logistics, where margins are razor-thin, managing cash burn is the first job. And it means choosing your plays carefully. 

“It makes little sense to chase rapid commerce in categories like groceries, where the margins barely justify the cost,” he says.

His point, essentially, is that you can afford to price-in the cost of quick commerce for high-value items such as phones. After all, if you are spending Rs 10,000 on a phone, you probably wouldn’t hesitate to shell out another Rs 200 for rapid delivery. 

But how many would pay Rs 200 to get Rs 199 worth of groceries delivered in 10 minutes? 

So far, much of this cost has been funded by burning hundreds of millions of dollars of VC money—keeping delivery fees low. But how sustainable will these businesses be once the full cost of rapid deliveries is passed on to consumers? The TAM could well turn out far smaller than currently touted, and limited to tier-1 and -2 cities. That would be a difficult pill to swallow for logistics startups banking a little too much on quick commerce for business. 

Startups seeking scale often chase volume metrics—the number of shipments per day, the number of pincodes covered—instead of actual profitability. This is a mistake that traditional logistics companies seem to have largely avoided. You don’t often see a Blue Dart or DTDC rushing to deliver everything to everyone. 

Sustainability needs to be cracked, first. 

Of course, it’s not like logistics startups are blind to the costs. 

As in most sectors, AI is already being brought around to bear and is expected to start bringing down delivery expenses—streamlining operations in India’s road-heavy transport sector through real-time visibility, more accurate forecasting, and smarter inventory and route management. 

Most leading players—startups and legacy alike—have begun integrating AI into their offerings. Shiprocket, for instance, has launched an AI-integrated model context protocol (MCP) server. DTDC has introduced Diva, an agentic AI system. These tools are expected to bring down costs over the medium- to long-term and could potentially reshape the competitive landscape once again. 

Next, in line? 

Drone deliveries, which most 3PL players are already piloting. 

The cynical might say these are just fancy new additions to startup pitches for more VC money. But it’s clear growth and innovation aren’t disappearing from the Indian logistics space anytime soon. 

It would just be better if there were more profitable new companies to go with it.

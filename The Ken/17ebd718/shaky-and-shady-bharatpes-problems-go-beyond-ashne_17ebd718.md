Every once in a while comes a founder who shakes up principles not so much by the brilliance of their business as by the brashness of their conduct.
WeWork’s Adam Neumann comes to mind from the not-so-distant past. Closer to home, BharatPe’s co-founder and managing director Ashneer Grover, the abrasive investor of the popular reality show Shark Tank fame, evokes similar thoughts.
Ever since an audio clip between a Kotak Bank employee and Grover leaked into the public domain in early January, unflattering stories about the latter’s conduct—something of an open secret in fintech circles—have snowballed into a crisis for the payments unicorn. BharatPe's board is learnt to be “keen” and in “consensus” on Grover’s exit from the company.
But here’s the sting in the tale. Grover’s behaviour may not be the only risk factor for the three-year-old startup, whose valuation went from $900 million at the start of 2021 to $2.85 billion in August after Tiger Global invested $370 million.
Arundhati has been digging around BharatPe’s business model for a while. She says by inextricably interlinking payments, lending, and P2P lending, BharatPe is walking a fine line. To quote from the story: “It has built risks into its business model. If P2P deposits falter, its lending falters. If merchants simply switch QR codes or PoS devices, its lending suffers.”
Just to zoom in on lending, apart from the regulatory risk it faces, many company executives allege it is deeply fraught with risk. The majority of its Rs 3,000 crore loan book is built on peer-to-peer loans, where the money comes from merchants, many of whom have little to no idea how their money is being used.
It’s telling that it took one audio leak to draw public scrutiny to the founder but the company's board and the fawning investors never got a whiff of the corporate governance or operational issues. The Icarus may have flown too close to the sun but can investors save the company from its inbuilt risk and scorched-earth practices?

Sam Altman is everywhere. Sam Altman is up to no good. It’s been impossible to avoid Sam Altman over the past week.

CCTV footage catches him in Target, where he picks up a GPU with the nonchalance of a serial shoplifter and begins to walk off. A security guard stops him before he makes it out of the store. Altman says, “Please, I really need this for Sora inference. This video is too good!”

He’s running down a hallway, ramming headfirst into walls. “We need to find a portal to the launch. It’s somewhere in the office.” He tries to jump over a bathtub filled with balls—part of an obstacle course set by OpenAI’s HR department—but trips and falls. Moments later, he leaps through a purple portal and lands in a place called Imagination Land, where long-necked dinosaurs roam (no, not Zeus, the Zero Shot mascot). Altman looks up into the sky with hope and joy before he says, “Let’s launch it!”

He’s stealing sketches of what looks like Studio Ghibli characters, grinning as he dashes for the exit.

The “launch” mentioned above is of Sora 2, OpenAI’s latest text-to-video generation model. It comes with dialogue and sound effects. It mostly follows the laws of physics, but becomes surreal in its depictions of fast physical movement, like gymnastics or martial arts routines. Mangled fingers and hands still show up, but not as often. All of this is at your fingertips if you receive an invite code from a friend to use OpenAI’s Sora app in the US or Canada.

Altman—the living, breathing one—called this the “‘ChatGPT for creativity’ moment” on his blog. He isn’t wrong. OpenAI has unfurled a rope ladder to give AI video enthusiasts a way to climb out of the uncanny valley. Sora 2 the model and Sora the app graduate users from schlepping slop to churning out unnervingly realistic deepfakes. It isn’t difficult to imagine a lot of people falling for hoaxes that purport to have video proof.

Trade Tricks
Tue, 29 Jul 25
A weekly newsletter about the biggest changes in commerce—focusing on shifts that matter to you..
Good Morning Ishan,
Today’s Trade Tricks is one of those quick round-ups we do every once in a while, where we look at interesting developments from the past few days that we haven’t had the opportunity to cover before.
To kick things off, there’s B2B e-commerce major Udaan and its continuing quest to build a superstar business out of supplying kiranas (local neighbourhood stores).
A couple of weeks ago, on 18 July, the company announced that it had [closed a deal](https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/small-biz/sme-sector/udaan-acquires-retail-tech-startup-shopkirana/articleshow/122735045.cms?from=mdr) to acquire kiranatech company Shopkirana in an effort to strengthen its muscle in key segments such as FMCG, household staples, and Horeca (or hotels, restaurants, and catering). The platforms will combine their tech, supply chain, and credit capabilities, which Udaan believes will drive operational efficiency, expand geographic reach, and improve its unit economics. Consumer internet major Info Edge, a Shopkirana backer, will become an Udaan shareholder as part of the deal.
The acquisition comes just a month after Udaan raised $114 million in its latest funding round. The company is also reportedly aiming for an IPO soon, and clearly wants to buff up its numbers.
But will doubling down on kiranas really help?
Because, if you’ve followed our coverage of the space over the last few years, the segment is one which multiple players have struggled to crack. And it’s not just a matter of throwing around cash or having a massive brand. Just ask Reliance, whose B2B unit Jiomart has had [an unenviable run](https://the-ken.com/story/jiomart-b2b-paused-reliances-fmcg-disruption-has-to-wait/) with the space, or Udaan itself.
Let me quote from a story we wrote a couple of years ago, when Jiomart was reorienting its strategy for the business:
Jiomart’s competitors in the B2B space, too, have suffered a similar fate. Udaan laid off over 1,000 employees last year, while Flipkart Wholesale announced a transition to a more cost-efficient model. Others, such as social-commerce startups Dealshare and Meesho, resorted to more drastic measures: shutting down their B2B verticals.
The reason? Trying to disrupt the well-entrenched legacy distributor system involves working on extremely thin margins and is expected to be a long-drawn affair.
Consequently, for Jiomart B2B, which serviced 150-odd cities and was taking up to 15–20% of kiranas’ wallet share, profitability was nowhere in sight. “Our delivery costs, like with transporting over 50 km from the nearest hub in many cases, were around two to three times that of Udaan’s,” said the employee quoted earlier in the story.
Of course, it’s not just Udaan that is still betting on one-upping kiranas’ traditional and highly entrenched distribution networks. Jumbotail, another kiranatech company, [raised](https://www.business-standard.com/companies/news/jumbotail-raises-120-million-in-funding-round-led-by-sc-ventures-125063000539_1.html) $120 million around the same time Udaan did this year. Though Jumbotail has taken a different approach to solving the problem.
How can the same kiranas who are averse to ordering through Udaan’s or Jiomart’s app be doing just that on Jumbotail’s? Maybe it’s not so much to do with the apps themselves as it is to do with what the kiranas can buy—or, more precisely, how much.
Small grocers don’t want to purchase anything other than their bestselling items by the case, the size of which varies by the product—say, 12 units of a 1L bottle of edible oil or 30 units of a 1kg bag of wheat flour. This is because a typical kirana is 250–500 sq. ft, and the owners have to make sure they don’t have too much of any product that’s only occasionally bought.
Jumbotail sells any quantity of any product. “People can order five units of a biscuit,” according to Jhina. He added that 90% of Jumbotail’s FMCG sales are made up of orders that are less than one case.
If you look at the number of businesses Amazon has entered over the last decade or so, you’d think the company has a collector’s fetish—and one that doesn’t really know what they’re looking for.
But since the recent change of leadership, with Samir Kumar taking over reins from Manish Tiwary, the company is attempting to go back to its roots, as we [reported in our story](https://the-ken.com/story/inside-samir-kumars-plan-to-bring-order-to-amazon-indias-chaos/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter) just yesterday.
One sign of that shift is Amazon finally entering quick commerce, if a bit late.
Amazon Now, after pilots in Bengaluru and Delhi, will be available for Amazon customers in select locations in Delhi, the company announced in a statement earlier this month.
“Earlier, it felt like we didn’t know what we were going after,” said one category manager. “When you leave things to output, there can be multiple ways of doing something, and everybody could have their own ways of approaching a problem.”
For instance, Amazon was steadfast in its belief that quick commerce wouldn’t be able to sustain itself in India. “Under the previous leadership, we always thought that it was one of the areas which wouldn’t be a game changer. Pricing plays a higher role than the delivery experience,” the Amazon Pay executive said.
With Kumar’s elevation, employees were pushed to turn slightly more aggressive.
“In terms of profitability, if something doesn’t make sense for us in the current Amazon ecosystem, we move more towards Amazon Now,” the first category manager said. “For example, if earlier we were spending, say, Rs 60 to ship a diya (an oil lamp), we now take calls that it will not be sold on the main marketplace but on Now.”

To wrap up our edition this week, I’ll leave you with a little piece from the [Financial Express](https://www.financialexpress.com/business/industry/outlet-malls-gain-ground-as-value-retail-accelerates/3928199/), which talks about the increasing appeal of outlet malls—where multiple manufacturers set up shop to sell their goods at discounted rates—among both businesses and customers.
According to the publication, around 8–10 outlet malls are currently under construction across India, with more in the works. And most of these are coming up in city peripheries. “These malls will typically house around 80-100 different brands across clothing, footwear and accessories, offering 50-70% discounts year-round. The area for each mall is around 300,000-600,000 sq ft, with the rent (per sq ft for retailers) being a tenth of that in regular malls,” the story says.
Last month, in one of The Ken’s own stories, we spoke about how cautious retailers have started experimenting with the concept after a period of unbridled and hasty expansion.
Two years ago, Pacific Mall in Delhi tried a different tack to lure hesitant shoppers back: it opened an “outlet mall” in the relatively cheaper Jasola area that boasted all-year-round offers and discounts on premium brands like Super Dry and American Eagle.
It was like a factory outlet, but different.
Such outlets act as stock liquidation channels of old inventory, usually two–three seasons old, said Sharad Nagpal, senior director at real-estate-services firm JLL India. “Combined with food and beverage, and value retail, such outlet centres have proved to be both a window to offload inventory as well as a source of competition to their full-price stores in primary and secondary catchment areas.”

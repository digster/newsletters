Renew Energy pays a high price for no fault of its own. Its imminent exit from the Nasdaq didn’t have much to do with its own business
By Seetharaman G
4 min read

Do you remember the cachet Softbank’s backing bestowed on a startup before the collapse of Wework, one of its storied bets, made the Japanese giant look very ordinary? That’s what a US$200 million cheque from Goldman Sachs did for Renew Energy in 2011.

Goldman hardly made news as an investor in India at that point. But it was Wall Street royalty and had bought a majority stake in the wind-power producer. This marked out Renew as a renewables company of note even if it had only been founded months earlier.

It certainly helped that Renew had Sumant Sinha, the former chief operating officer at wind-turbine maker Suzlon Energy, as its founder. Two years later, Renew would diversify into solar energy just as the business was primed for liftoff.

For a company that would live up to its initial promise and is, to this day, India’s second-largest clean-energy producer, the coda couldn’t have been more underwhelming.

In early December, four of its shareholders—including Sinha and the Canada Pension Plan Investment Board—said they plan to take the Nasdaq-listed firm private by buying one-third of the company they don’t own. They are offering a little over US$7 a share, which is more than what the Renew stock was trading at, at the time of the announcement.

But it’s less than the US$9.55 it closed at on the day of its listing in August 2021, after merging with a blank-cheque company.

The dissonance between Renew as a company and Renew as a stock couldn’t be starker. Since the listing, Renew’s total portfolio of projects has swelled by half to 16.3 gigawatts, and the company has turned profitable. Taking advantage of India’s push for local manufacturing, Renew has headed upstream in the value chain by making photovoltaic panels that power solar farms.

Plus, Renew has always resisted the urge to win solar-energy projects at any cost, even when rivals quoted tariffs so low that no energy economist could do the math on potential profits, if any.

But if you saw the trajectory of its stock, you would wonder if it’s of an entirely different company.

The most apparent reason for Renew’s plight, as I argued earlier in this newsletter, is sustainability losing its allure as an investment theme, with fossil fuels making a roaring recovery in the past couple of years.

Companies dealing in coal, oil, or gas are no longer undesirable. In fact, they are sought after as resurgent, energy-hungry economies across the world slowed the transition to cleaner electricity and battery-powered vehicles.

A second factor is how Adani Green Energy’s struggles have shaped global investors’ perception of India’s clean-energy industry. First, American short-seller Hindenburg Research’s attack on the Adani Group in January 2023, alleging stock-price manipulation and accounting fraud. Then, in November 2024, US prosecutors charged the group’s chairman, Gautam Adani, and current and former executives with carrying out a US$250 million bribery scheme to persuade Andhra Pradesh, a southern state, to buy power from one of its projects.

Adani Green is not just another renewables company; it’s India’s largest and, consequently, Renew’s closest peer. It’s easy to see why a Scandinavian sovereign-wealth fund or an American hedge fund eager to profit off India’s clean-energy boom would now be hesitant.

The fact that the US investigation has also cast a shadow on India’s state-run solar agency, which conducts auctions and helps states buy electricity from developers, will make global investors even warier.

Meanwhile, NTPC Green Energy, a rival to Renew and Adani Green that listed on Indian stock exchanges in late November, is up nearly 30% over its issue price. But I stick to what I said here before the listing:

Make no mistake, the share of renewables in India’s overall installed electricity capacity is bound to rise from the current 35%. Plus, the fact that there’s no listed renewables-only power producer besides Adani Green, which mutual funds have largely shunned, will add to NTPC Green’s appeal as a listed company.

But there’s no reason for investors to hanker after clean-energy companies to the exclusion of their polluting peers. NTPC shares have risen by a third this year, in line with BSE’s power index. The Sensex, by contrast, has grown by a mere 11%. It’s hard to overstate the resurgence of conventional energy companies.

NTPC is enjoying the renewables party. But the music has stopped, The Ken

Even if investor enthusiasm for renewables wanes in India, I don’t expect the indifference that Renew was habituated to on Nasdaq. That’s why I wouldn’t be surprised to see Renew riding the public-market tiger again at some point, but this time in India.

Good Morning Ishan,
If, like me, you have your morning coffee at hand, take a sip. Today's story will likely hit close to home.
It's about PCOS or Polycystic Ovary Syndrome, a metabolic syndrome that manifests in women of childbearing age. Its global prevalence is high, affecting about 10% of women worldwide. In India, it's twice as common—1 in 5 women are affected. So, you almost certainly know a woman who suffers from it.
Women with PCOS are at risk of a litany of problems. Type 2 diabetes. Infertility. Cardiovascular disorders. And obesity.
Alarmed yet? Don't be. Have another swig of coffee. We're just getting there.
We don't quite know what causes PCOS. Is it genetic? Is it lifestyle related? A combination? The medical fraternity is divided. And treatment? That's also debatable.
But this uncertainty hasn't resulted in caution. PCOS management is the Wild West, and doctors are on a prescription overdrive. Worse still, a lot of these prescriptions are for off-label drug use. Uses other than what these drugs are officially approved for. One of them, Letrozole, was even banned for five years. Why? For the exact same use it's approved for today—treating infertility.
But just how much of this drug use is justified, and how much of it is driven by overzealous doctors and pharma companies? Is there a better way for India to tackle its PCOS problem? That is what Tanvi, the author of today's piece, takes a look at. It's also a free read, so do share it widely. Especially with the women in your life. Heck, it wouldn't hurt the men to be slightly more clued in either. I know it was an eye-opener for me.

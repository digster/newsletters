Good Morning Ishan,
It’s 1:00 p.m. on a weekday, though you wouldn’t know it looking at the storefront of Magnolia Bakery’s first outlet in Delhi NCR.
A Mercedes, a Porsche, a Volvo pull up, one after another, all adding bodies to the already 20-person queue outside the store on Golf Course Road in Gurugram. Kids in school uniforms press their faces up against the glass, marvelling at the “purple cakes” making their way to the icing station. Aunties in heels and sunglasses chatter about how their New York trips would be incomplete without a visit to this iconic NYC bakery.
A majority of the crowd, though, is made up of 20- and 30-somethings, who are simply responding to what’s become an Instagram rallying cry.
| Everyone wants a bite, Collage: Neha Mehrotra |
“This place is all over my reels. So obviously I had to come make my own reel,” said one 21-year-old. Another 28-year-old informs her friends that this was Alia Bhatt’s favourite bakery. “She loves the tres leches here,” she said. Others are even more obvious about their priorities. They pull up, see the line, and settle for taking pictures against the storefront, before beating a hasty retreat.
None of this is all that surprising.
The bakery, which first opened its doors in New York’s West Village in 1996, has found its way into pop culture time and again. From Carrie Bradshow biting into a pink vanilla buttercream cupcake on the iconic show Sex and the City—that triggered the early-2000s cupcake craze—to the creation of the ‘pink puddin’ for the recent Warner Bros film Birds of Prey, Magnolia is a master of hype.
Apart from the 12 company-owned outlets across the US, the company has expanded into seven other countries through franchises, including Turkey, the UAE, Philippines, and India, where operator Spago Foods—run by husband and wife Nischay Jayshankar and Zonu Reddy—has opened 11 outlets since 2019. Magnolia did have a franchise outlet in Japan too, but it’s reportedly shut [down](https://www.timeout.com/tokyo/restaurants/closed-magnolia-bakery).
In 2021, the venture-capital firm RSE Ventures—with [investments](https://www.wsj.com/business/retail/magnolia-bakery-gets-new-owner-11622552402) in chef David Chang’s Momofuku brand and dessert chain Milk Bar—bought Magnolia from then-owner Steve Abrams at an undisclosed price. Mostly because Abrams didn’t have the capital required for expansion.
The new owners have since expanded into e-commerce and are now considering applying the bakery’s international playbook domestically—to expand across the US through franchisees.
Back in India, dessert chains have never been hotter. Rising incomes and exposure to global food trends have [buoyed](https://the-ken.com/story/theobroma-became-a-go-to-for-cake-lovers-now-its-a-go-to-for-investors-that-changes-everything/) the fortunes of chains like Theobroma, which saw private-equity firm Chryscapital buy a near-90% stake in it for around Rs 2,500 crore. Theobroma has been growing at a CAGR of over 40% in the five years to FY24 to touch Rs 600 crore in revenue, and is valued at nearly 7–9X that.
Not bad for a business built on products with one of the shortest shelf lives.
Even Indian-sweets chain Bombay Sweet Shop was at the [centre](https://the-ken.com/tradetricks/the-mithai-shop-was-indias-original-qsr-bombay-canteens-sameer-seth-yash-bhanage/) of its parent Hunger Inc’s Rs 215 crore fundraise from Lighthouse and DSG Consumer partners in October. Spago Foods, for its part, posted a profit of Rs 1 crore on a revenue of Rs 22 crore in FY24.
Can Magnolia Bakery pull a Theobroma in India? Does it even want to?
On the same weekday afternoon, a guy walks into a near-empty Theobroma outlet in Gurugram.
His order is simple enough: 15 cupcakes to go. But the two employees at the store seem visibly flustered. The display has only three cupcakes left, and some hushed consultations later, they try to corral him into buying a combination of different desserts instead.
“Our kitchen is not here, so it’s difficult to get you such large numbers. If you had pre-ordered, we could have gotten it ready for you,” one of the employees told him.
The central kitchen, from where products are shipped to Theobroma’s 12 outlets in Gurugram, is in Noida. Not so central afterall, as shipments take at least two hours to get here. And that’s on a good day.
In contrast, Magnolia Bakery has a live kitchen attached to it, which is baking throughout the day. Chefs and staff swing in and out, giving customers a peek into what’s cooking. Live icing stations, where sous chefs swirl icing onto pastel-coloured cakes, add to the “freshness” of the entire experience.
“Most of our desserts, such as cupcakes and pastries, come from our live kitchens. But some products, such as our bestseller tres leches, are made in a central kitchen close by,” said a person close to the operations.
This makes sense, considering Spago Foods is planning two more outlets in NCR—the first in the dining and entertainment complex DLF Cybercity in Gurugram in February, and the second in Delhi Aerocity. After all, central kitchens are a great way to optimise costs amid outlet expansion.
But that’s where the cost optimisation ends.
Most of Magnolia’s outlets are big 1,500 sq ft showrooms in usually prime locations. For context, the rent for a similar-sized front-facing space on Golf Course Road is over Rs 6 lakh. Moreover, a significant chunk of the bakery’s ingredients are imported from the US to match brand standards. “Even those we source locally have to pass the brand’s quality tests,” said the person close to operations.
This is quite apart from the initial franchise and territory fee—which includes the license to operate in a particular country, supply-chain support, site selection, design guidance, and staff training—and the flat [6%](https://www.global-franchise.com/master-franchises/magnolia-bakery) of gross revenue royalty fee that franchisees pay each year.
If it’s not already apparent, Magnolia Bakery is deeply involved in the day-to-day operations of its franchisees.
All its products, including those that come from its central kitchen, have a shelf life of two days. This when Theobroma has been [filling](https://the-ken.com/story/theobroma-became-a-go-to-for-cake-lovers-now-its-a-go-to-for-investors-that-changes-everything/) its shelves with longer-lasting products such as breads and brownies, similar to, say, a fast-food chain such as Blue Tokai.
Naturally, all this is reflected in the prices. At Magnolia Bakery, a cupcake costs around Rs 200 and a cheese cake, Rs 400. In comparison, at Theobroma, they are Rs 125 and Rs 160, respectively. Magnolia also has its own unique showstoppers such as the banana pudding, not available in other run-of-the-mill chains.
All these things, though, are just part of the reason for the hype.
“Gurgaon has been the biggest launch we’ve seen in India to date,” said the person close to operations. “The quantities we’re selling everyday are unprecedented.”
A lot of the footfall seems to be coming straight from social media. Case in point: inside the Magnolia Bakery in Gurugram, you’ll see more people taking pictures of desserts than actually eating them. A New York Times [article](https://archive.nytimes.com/www.nytimes.com/learning/students/pop/20031107snapfriday.html) from back in 2003 had been quick to pinpoint the aesthetic appeal of Magnolia’s desserts.
The connection of cupcakes and chic is inescapable, especially in Greenwich Village, where three Marc Jacobs stores have opened within a block of the Magnolia Bakery. Mr. Jacobs’s colorful retro clothing is a mirror of Magnolia’s colorful retro cupcakes, and it appeals to the same dressed-up young crowd.
Gurugram today is no different. In fact, the craze is now on steroids, spurred by social-media trends. “When a global brand such as Magnolia or Tim Hortons comes to India, there’s an aspirational quality attached to it,” said Shireen Mehrotra, a food writer and researcher. “So those first couple of weeks, everyone wants to be seen in that space. The conversation online is very much like, ‘If you’re not here, what are you even doing with your life?’.”
| The Magnolia hype all over social media, Collage: Neha Mehrotra |
Predictably, Magnolia has its finger on this ever-restless pulse. It has seven different teams for marketing, according to the person close to operations: four PR teams for four different cities, coupled with teams for social media, performance marketing, and photoshoots. It was these teams that came up with the idea of offering a free banana pudding each month for a year to the first 10 customers on opening day.
Ultimately, what translates hype into long-term loyalty is product quality. And if its track record in Bengaluru is anything to go by, that’s something Magnolia’s India franchisee has got covered.

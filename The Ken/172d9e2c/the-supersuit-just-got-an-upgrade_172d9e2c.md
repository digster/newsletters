Not all superheroes wear capes.
True!
But all of them do have fancy defensive gear or a keeps-everything-out bodysuit. And nifty upgrades for that particularly villainous villain.
Our modern superheroes—doctors, nurses, law enforcement officials, healthcare workers—leading the fight against the evil coronavirus also got a workwear upgrade.
They’re called PPE—or personal protective equipment if you didn’t already know. Masks, face shields, gloves, shoe covers, head-to-toe coveralls, you name it.
India’s clarion call to manufacture PPE was so widely heeded that the country went from nowhere-on-the-scene to world No. 2 in a matter of months. And why not? PPE was the only thing being factory-produced at a mass scale in the past three to four months.
But with great quantity also came a great rise in prices (6X for PPE), and a great many fly-by-night operators (around 99%, by one estimate). Now, as the demand tide ebbs, so does the quantity game, exposing a so-far overlooked requirement—quality.
Especially in the manufacture of coveralls, whether in terms of comfort or safety.
Can you imagine how punishing it can be for frontline workers toiling long shifts, sometimes in the sweltering heat, wearing those thick, non-permeable coveralls?
It’s a problem even for those superheroes with capes. The No. 1 tip each actor playing Batman passes on to their successor is, “Take your loo break before you get into the suit.” More, importantly, can you imagine superheroes with breakable shields and non-bullet-proof suits?
It turns out the P in PPE may not be that effective. Is a coverall that prevents blood or water from permeating good enough for a virus (this one or the next) that is more likely to exist as an aerosol?
What are our testing standards? Are they as resilient as the virus? Why can’t the material in masks be used for overalls? Can India’s PPE industry go from No. 2 in quantity to No. 1 in quality? Olina has the answers in today’s absolutely must-read story.

So we’re launching as a free beta like we did with The Ken. Over the next few weeks and months, you will see a variety of weekend stories from us. With your valuable feedback, we will iterate and fine-tune our themes and focus areas until we have a product that is ready to exist as our first separate product. One that you and others will hopefully want to subscribe to.

Why the Human Eye is the Window to Artificial Intelligence in Healthcare
 
"Correlation does not imply causation” is one of the truisms that is drilled into the mind of statisticians, MBAs and professionals relentlessly through their careers. So much so that most of us have this innate disdain for “mere” correlation. It’s a common Internet troll putdown line, said to those who dare conflate the high brow causation with the lowly correlation.
 
Till the computers came along and showed us, humans, we had it all backwards; well, sort of.
 
One of the most fascinating things about AI, or Artificial Intelligence, is how it not just inverts the causation-correlation equation, but almost discards causation. 
 
At Sankara Nethralaya, one among the trio of world-class eye hospitals India is lucky to have (the other two are LV Prasad Eye Institute and Aravind Eye Care), an AI algorithm jointly developed with Google was able to accurately identify the gender and the eye diseases of people whose retinal scans it read 90% and 95% of the times, respectively.
 
“As a doctor, I can’t figure out the gender looking at the images. That’s what deep learning does; it finds associations which clinicians can’t,” said one of the doctors at the hospitals. He was astounded because all these years he and his peers had been looking for causation, while AI came and looked for correlation.
 
And the eye, or to be more specific, the retina, is where AI is shining its light. Because it is the only place in the body where one can look at blood vessels in their naked form.
 
From nerve tissue visible at the back of the eye, algorithms can identify changes years before diseases such as, say, Alzheimer’s, Parkinson’s or multiple sclerosis manifest elsewhere. They can also tell what’s wrong with your eye, heart, blood pressure, kidney, foot, brain. Or your age and gender. Even one’s body mass index. If you are a smoker or not. Or whether you are optimistic, curious, agreeable, conscientious, extroverted, or maybe even neurotic.
 
In the near future, expect to take a "retinal selfie”, upload the image via an app, and go see a doctor if the AI behind the app classifies you as ‘referable’. 
 
Correlation is the new black.

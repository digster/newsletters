Good Morning Ishan,
 
To say that The Ken’s coverage of OYO Rooms has been both critical and ahead of the curve would be an understatement.
 
Our first major story on one of SoftBank’s brightest unicorns came out in May 2017. Sumanth, my co-founder and the author of the story, called it a “Ponzi scheme of ambition” (a phrase initially coined by CB Insights’ Anand Sanwal). But Sumanth’s analyses of OYO predated even The Ken. In May 2016, he wrote a massively viral LinkedIn story titled, “Is Oyo Rooms the startup equivalent of a Ponzi scheme?”
 
Since then, Sumanth and The Ken have written multiple articles that have analysed and critiqued almost every single aspect of OYO’s business model and strategy. Its pricing model, its platform economics, its international expansion, its customer value proposition, its valuation, its funding by SoftBank, and Agarwal’s decision to borrow $2 billion to invest in OYO and buy back shares from its VCs.
 
But as is quite often the relationship between media criticism and unicorn valuations, OYO kept raising more and more money, at higher and higher multiples of a billion dollars in valuation. “Imagine yourself in the shoes of Agarwal, barely out of your teens and with a small, fledgeling startup, suddenly presented with over a hundred million dollars in funding. As a founder, you now have no choice but to play this game—your moves and ambitions forced to fit into your investor’s thesis and ambitions,” wrote Sumanth in his May 2017 story.
 
Well, it’s May 2020, and Covid-19 has laid low not just OYO, but the entire global travel sector. OYO’s business is down over 50%, but 26-year-old Ritesh Agarwal, still its CEO, views it as a glass-half-full situation. “But even a 50%-plus drop is better than the 80-90% drop that other hotel chains have seen globally,” he told Sumanth face-to-face over multiple Zoom calls.
 
We rarely extend interview invitations to most CEOs because they’re very anodyne in their responses to tough questions. Agarwal isn’t.

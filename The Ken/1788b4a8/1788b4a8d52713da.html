“It gave us a gun and asked us to shoot ourselves,” reminisces a former official from one of the erstwhile regional stock exchanges in India, referring to market regulator Sebi’s strict rules.
 
At some point, 20-odd exchanges operated in India; today, it’s a near-monopoly. The National Stock Exchange of India (NSE), Sebi’s blue-eyed boy, accounts for almost 95% of equity derivatives and cash trades in India. So, when NSE suffered its longest outage in its 29-year history on 24 February, the knives were once again out to tame the raging bull.
 
There’s a perception that Sebi has been soft on NSE—post the outage, while NSE admitted the problems, Sebi batted for it. Barely a month ago, though, Sebi had proposed cutting NSE to size and easing entry barriers to allow more competition.
 
That was seen as a radical U-turn for Sebi. While some market participants joke that maybe a big domestic entity is looking to get into the business of bourses or some foreign exchange could be interested in picking up a big stake in an existing exchange, there’s another more plausible reason. NSE has become too big to fail; its many slips are becoming glaring. NSE had nine glitches over the past four years—four in 2020 alone.
 
As they say, in the long-term, every technology either becomes a commodity or a monopoly. NSE, the more tech-savvy competitor to its older rival, BSE, has become a monopoly with all the attendant risks. After the February outage, even the Reserve Bank of India has called for more competition.
 
Sebi has enumerated some solutions, but market participants believe they are only indicative.
 
Nithin Kamath, founder of Zerodha, India’s largest stock brokerage, for one, believes that “someone has to re-imagine a stock exchange” with the primary objective of increasing participation in the stock market and improving depth.
 
Now, that calls for bold actions from Sebi, which it isn’t known for. Can Sebi re-imagine itself? In his story today, Anand has some pointers. Let’s dive in: https://the-ken.com/story/how-nse-india-turned-from-solution-to-problem/ (14-minute read)

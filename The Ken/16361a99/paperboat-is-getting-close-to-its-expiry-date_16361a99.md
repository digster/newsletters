If you are in Bangalore today, like I am, it's a momentous day.
If you aren't in Bangalore, but plan to closely follow the results on your preferred news channel on TV - my condolences, but I understand, because after all, it's a momentous day.
If you are one of our four subscribers from Sweden, or don't care much for Indian politics - fear not, there's enough momentousness to go around, because we have a great story today.
Almost at the exact time this email hits your inbox, the counting for the Karnataka assembly elections would have just begun. After a long, bitter campaign, the results are finally going to be announced today. The drama was palpable in Bangalore all weekend. No clear favourites. No idea of what's going to happen. Exit poll predictions haven't been particularly useful since almost all of them have wildly disagreed with each other. It appears that we have finally found a problem that nobody has suggested would be eventually solved using blockchain.
Why is the Karnataka election so hard to predict anyway? Well, most elections involve two entities. A challenger. And an incumbent. But you know what's more exciting than a two-way race? A three-way race. That's when it gets messy. Hatchets are buried. Then dug up again at night. Horses are traded. Pawns become powerful. Strangely, the juice business is a bit like that too. My colleague Patanjali writes about a juice beverage company called Paperboat wedged in the middle of a three-way race. His story starts with the exact moment when the war was declared. And about how Paperboat is fending off three massive competitors, with time running out, and in a position where they will need to pick a side soon. Very soon.
Luckily, they have a plan.
Have a great day. I hope your party wins.
Else, don't worry, it will probably be solved someday by blockchain.

Good Morning Ishan,

Remember 24 March, 2020? I’m sure that was the first time most of us experienced a lockdown. Memories of it are still so fresh, right?

I vividly remember the feeling when I relished my first culinary excellence back then. It tasted like raw grass smeared with hot mud. Better still, I spent hours doing the dishes for a dish (see what I did there?) that I was blanched to even throw away fearing it would cause radioactive reactions.

But I was surprised when it suddenly struck me that it’s been four years since we were first placed under lockdown. That’s a lot of years for an event that feels like yesterday.

I was discussing this thought with a friend when he said something that shook me. It’s been 13-(oh god!)-long years since Dhoni cleared the ropes in Wankhede and lifted the men’s cricket world cup. 13? Seriously? Newborns turn into teenagers in those many years.

How is time passing by so quickly? I had to find out more about this sorcery.

Turns out, time, as a concept, is stretchable. With age, more time seems to slide by in a jiffy—as opposed to our childhood, when each year felt so substantial.

So why does that happen?

Dutch psychologist, Douwe Draaisma, published a book in 2004 that addresses the very riddle. It’s named Why Life Speeds Up As You Get Older: How Memory Shapes our Past.

He explains that new, unfamiliar experiences make time seem to move slower. Novel situations enable our brains to make and retain more memories. These additional memories stretch and expand our perception of time while routine, unmemorable moments contract time, Draaisma writes.

When we’re young, almost everything we experience is new, so the brain makes more memories and time seems to pass by slower. But as we grow older, our experiences become predictable and the mind makes fewer memories, which fastens our sense of time’s passage.

The book explains looking back as the mind’s search for memorable change points in the past. Routine activities contain fewer of these change points, and so it feels time rushed past us.

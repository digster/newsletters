1. Breaking patterns

Earlier last month, we put out a job description to hire for a Product Designer to join The Ken. I wrote the job description roughly in the form of a “tour of duty https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/careers-as-tours-of-duty/” (because when I tell you that I use the First Principles newsletter to evolve my own thinking and behaviour, I mean it).

We interviewed a bunch of talented, eager, and ambitious designers over a few weeks and ended up hiring a great person.

We also ended up hiring a second, junior product designer. It’s interesting how that happened.

It happened because she wrote an authentic and thoughtful email in response to my rejection email to her. I make it a point to send polite rejection emails to all candidates who apply to the roles I post; it’s the least I can. Naturally, I am not expecting a reply to these emails. Occasionally I do get some, but most are candidates asking me for specifics on why they weren’t being hired.

In this case, the reply I got to my rejection email was so well written that I wrote back to the candidate and asked for a conversation. A few conversations later, we hired her.

I did reflect on this outlier of an incident. It was only made possible because someone—an authentic and deliberate human—decided to do something most others would never do. And in doing so, she bypassed my normal “filters”.

This is exactly the attitude I’d want in a colleague, and of course, a product designer.

Another deliberate method I use to give truly interesting candidates an opportunity to stand out when applying for a role at The Ken is to ask a few open-ended questions. For instance, some of the questions we asked for the Product Designer role were:

What are your strongest areas as a designer? What design skills might you be in the top 5–10% among your peers? Please be as specific as possible.
What skills do you hope you gain at The Ken?

I find it easy to detect AI-generated answers to such questions. Conversely, if someone needs AI to explain things about themselves, I am not terribly interested in them as potential colleagues. Of course, YMMV.

From thoughtful replies to rejection emails, to well thought-out and reflective answers about your own motivations. What else do we pay more attention to in a post-AI world?

I wanted to ask you about what it means to be more human—authentically, intellectually, and professionally—when it’s getting harder and harder to do so.

A few days ago, I also read one of those articles you wish you’d written. Instead, it was written by John Burn-Murdoch, a columnist with Financial Times.

“Have humans passed peak brain power? https://www.ft.com/content/a8016c64-63b7-458b-a371-e0e1c54a13fc”, he asked, and then proceeded to connect all the dots that we’ve known about for years—teenager’s and their falling PISA scores on maths, science, and reading; rising cognitive challenges for young adults; falling reading habits; and a rising number of adults who struggle with even basic numeracy and literacy.

So we appear to be looking less at the decline of reading per se, and more at a broader erosion in human capacity for mental focus and application.

Most discussion about the societal impacts of digital media focuses on the rise of smartphones and social media. But the change in human capacity for focused thought coincides with something more fundamental: a shift in our relationship with information.

We have moved from finite web pages to infinite, constantly refreshed feeds and a constant barrage of notifications. We no longer spend as much time actively browsing the web and interacting with people we know but instead are presented with a torrent of content. This represents a move from self-directed behaviour to passive consumption and constant context-switching.

All of this happened before AI entered the picture. I leave it to you to extrapolate. Do we get dumber? Or magically smarter?

2. Six degrees of Ergonomics: Community responses

A bunch of you wrote in after last week’s edition on Ergonomics https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/six-degrees-of-ergonomics/.

“We are focusing at the wrong place. While ergonomic devices, posture is important, real important thing is to keep body stronger, warmed up. Strength, endurance training, and regular exercise are more important. I am a heavy user of computers and mobiles for more than three decades and have gone through the pain and problems. Now, I am out of this problem because of regular exercise.”—Rajneesh Singhvi, Director, Jaipur

“Back in Mumbai, 2009 through 2012, I ran a company called Work Ergonomics—to solve (or compound) exactly this problem! We used to have Mckenzie Lumber rolls to prevent lumber pains. I have often wondered if those compounded neck/thoracic pain!”—Runam Mehta, Chief Growth Officer, Bengaluru

“(Optimising for) pure comfort rather than conforming to an orthopedician’s recommended postures is the first mistake people do.”—Ram R Bhupathiraju, Anthropologist & Qualitative Researcher, Hyderabad

“I switched to using the mouse with my left hand (though I’m right handed) years ago. This eased stress on my right hand and arm, and as a second-order effect, forced me to learn more keyboard shortcuts as my left arm is not as dextrous as my right. I also find that you can place the mouse closer to the ideal position when it’s to the left—you need to move your arm much less from the keyboard compared to the right where you generally have to skip the additional keys. Nowadays, I’m experimenting with voice-based input for the mac (Wispr Flow https://wisprflow.ai/ works quite well for me).”—Shrinath V, Product Consultant, Bengaluru

[Ed: I did this. I’m a right-handed guy using a mouse with my left hand since last week!]

“Using a trackball mouse alleviated a lot of my pain associated with moving the mouse over long periods of times.”—Aseem Rastogi, Global Head of Security Research, Bengaluru

“Getting your laptop elevated using a table stand to look straight without stressing your neck or a height-adjustable laptop stand helps keep your neck and back straight while sitting.”—Shilpa, Business Consultant, Pune

“Move every 15–30 mins 🙂 Even if you don’t have an ideal setup, it helps by simply taking a few minutes break every hour or so to stretch a bit. It’s a much cheaper solution! The key here is to move every time and not sit for a long time. The best ergonomics recommendation that I arrived at was hourly desk stretches (neck, wrists, fingers, get up and walk) and 1-minute breathing exercises. Turns out, if you are conscious and aware of your posture often, it helps course correct sooner rather than add more stress on tensed muscles.”—Ankit Shah, Product Manager, Mountain View

“I don’t have any recommendations. However, I have a question. This space of ergonomic infrastructure seems daunting to me. Lots of research, followed by purchase, then new information emerges, and then another purchase to factor that in… it’s seemingly never ending. Like a treadmill that I need to keep on. Instead, is it best to not take any action? But that would be equivalent to an ostrich burying its head in the sand. And therefore, my question—are there any simple, sasta (financial, emotional, and mental) ways of hacking this. Like the equivalent to getting in 45 mins of walking daily and eating measured portions for physical health. The pros can research and double down more, but I’m more keen on finding the beginner-level ergonomic infrastructure introduction.”—Rohan Jaikishen, Mumbai

This week’s episode of the Two by Two podcast isn’t just about market size. After all, shouldn’t we care about how the animals that reach our plate are raised and treated? Tune in for an interesting listen on the ethical standards and business practices India’s meat industry must adopt in order to reach a consensus on humane meat and eggs.

Good Morning Ishan,
A rising tide raises all boats. In a storm, however, it helps to be in a bigger, better boat.
India's co-living startups are finding this out the hard way. For years, they made hay as the VC sun shone on the sector.
Take Zolostays, which claims to be India's largest co-living brand. The startup has some 40,000 beds on its platform, across 14 cities. In November 2019, it was in talks for funding to the tune of $100 million from investment bank Credit Suisse. This, it surmised, would help it get to 200,000 beds by the end of 2021.
Sunshine, meet hay.
Then, the Covid-19 storm clouds rolled in and India went into a nationwide lockdown. Suddenly, things aren't so rosy for co-living spaces. Occupancy is down. Rent collections are down. Co-living startups, which are by and large still loss-making, are bleeding profusely.
Zolostays has cut its growth forecast for 2020-21 by almost half. However, it's one of the lucky ones. For many smaller startups in the space, this is likely the end of the line. Investors are wary, runways are short. Only the luckiest will scrape through. The rest will sink or be swallowed by larger players... like Zolostays.
In fact, writes Vandana, Covid-19 could be a watershed moment for big co-living players. In the wake of the pandemic, the hygiene factor could see tenants flock to large, branded co-living spaces instead of the unorganised hostels and paying guest facilities that constitute 99% of the sector. The current economic uncertainty has also forced co-living brands to move towards more feasible business models.
While the present pain is inescapable, could the co-living space emerge from the pandemic more resilient and sustainable than ever before?

Mutual funds are on fire. Many of the 70 and 80-year-old retired folks I know are moving their life’s savings from bank deposits to mutual funds. Naturally, the market regulator has been keeping a hawk's eye on how they are being sold.
 
But before we get to what the regulator has proposed, here’s a question:
 
Whom would you rather buy a mutual fund from? An adviser who will charge you for her expertise, but will help make better returns in the long term, or from a distributor who doesn’t charge you a dime upfront, but shares with you a part of the commission she earns?
 
Important life lesson hidden in that answer, but for now, this question captures the dilemma and the lack of awareness among mutual fund investors. Which is why the Securities and Exchange Board of India has set out to make the process investor-friendly. The final set of rules, which sources say are now cast in stone, came earlier this month.
 
Broadly speaking, the regulator seems to be batting for advisories but umpiring in favour of distributors. How’s that going to change the business for big financial institutions that do both? Will Paytm, a brand new entrant-to-be, with its advisory services, move the needle for small investors, one way or the other? What are advisories, in general, grappling with, because the distributor-led model controls 80% of the mutual fund sale?

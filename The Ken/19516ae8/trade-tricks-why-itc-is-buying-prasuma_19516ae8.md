Trade Tricks
Tue, 18 Feb 25
A weekly newsletter about the biggest changes in commerce—focusing on shifts that matter to you..
Good Morning Ishan,
Ask any of my friends what my preferred late-night snack is and they’ll tell you it’s a plate of hot, juicy steamed momos. And after 6 February, I’m convinced that someone at one of India’s biggest consumer-goods conglomerates enjoys Himalayan dumplings just as much as I do. Because that’s the day ITC announced that it would acquire frozen momo-maker Prasuma.
Initially, ITC will take a 44% shareholding in the six-year-old brand, but it plans to acquire Prasuma in its entirety over the next three years, according to its statement. The deal values Prasuma at about Rs 300 crore, or around 40–50 million plates of momos at your typical roadside stall in Bengaluru.
Prasuma is essentially in three different lines of business. Under its own brand name, it mainly sells frozen snacks like momos, kebabs, and fried chicken. Then there’s its Meatigo brand, which delivers gourmet meat products that are both chilled and frozen—everything from raw and marinated chicken to cold cuts and burger patties. And alongside all of this, it also runs a chain of cloud kitchens delivering pan-Asian food.
ITC entered the frozen foods and ready-to-cook market in 2019 with the launch of ITC Master Chef, under which brand it now sells a variety of frozen snacks, dips, all-purpose gravies, and cooking pastes.
But the FMCG giant appears to be dialling up its ambitions in the space. Aside from its deal with Prasuma, Livemint [reported](https://www.livemint.com/companies/itc-in-talks-to-acquire-mtr-eastern-for-1-4-bn-11739361268310.html) last week that ITC is in talks to take over MTR Foods, a major player in the ready-to-cook market, and spice-maker Eastern Condiments.
Given the context, Prasuma is an acquisition that fits on many levels.
First, it has a strong presence in online channels—selling its products via its own website, the Meatigo app, as well as online marketplaces, whereas ITC’s own strengths lie in traditional offline FMCG sales channels.
Second, even if ITC had decided to just start selling its own frozen momos under Master Chef, it wouldn’t have been easy breaking into the online market with Prasuma and Meatigo already present on quick-commerce platforms, whose dark stores can’t afford to carry dozens of different brands within the same [category](https://the-ken.com/story/the-humble-category-manager-is-now-a-quick-commerce-overlord/).
Its category strength is a clear attraction for ITC. To quote from the FMCG company’s official release:
“With the industry at an inflection point, this acquisition will help strengthen and expand ITC’s presence in the aforesaid categories by gaining entry into high growth segments, viz. Pan Asian foods, Deli meats, etc. With the proposed acquisition, ITC will become the first full stack player in the segment with an unparalleled portfolio, offering meals and snacking options across multiple occasions throughout the day for the discerning consumer.”
Translation: ITC Master Chef already sells everything from burger patties and fries to pakodas and kebabs. And now, it can fill a momo-sized hole in that lineup.
Prasuma, of course, will benefit from ITC’s heft and capabilities when it comes to navigating the complex web of offline distributors and intermediaries.
One question, perhaps a bit tangential, that occurred to me was what Prasuma’s relative success signals to other new-age players in the meat products market. Especially given the [struggles](https://www.livemint.com/companies/licious-online-delivery-venky-s-godrej-supply-chain-seafood-market-vivek-gupta-swiggy-zepto-e-commerce-meat-11722417174586.html) faced by the likes of Licious and Freshtohome, which both face flagging revenue growth.
Prasuma sells raw meat, as well as chilled value-added meat products like cold cuts and pre-marinaded meat. But both of these are sideshows to its main frozen snacks business—which is also a different type of value-added product, albeit with a much longer shelf life.
Many brands in the meat products space that focus heavily on raw meat see selling chilled value-added products almost as a waste management strategy.
“Say you have kept some chicken drumsticks for sale, which have a shelf life of five days. At three days in, you’re carrying a lot of inventory from the first day and it’s getting closer to expiry while you don’t see a lot of sales coming in. So you add some marinades and masalas to extend the shelf life by another 5-7 days,” a senior leader in the segment told me. (To be clear, this doesn’t mean these items are unsafe for consumption.)
Sure, some meat brands have a few ready-to-cook products in their line-up, but not many of them have been interested in going too far down the value-addition rabbit-hole. Because the moment you get into value addition—whether in terms of making frozen snacks or chilled products—you require a significantly different approach.
But perhaps Prasuma’s results might convince some that it’s worth it?

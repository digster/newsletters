Zerodha, Navi wanted a passive-investing revolution. “Nope,” said older fund houses

Passive funds were meant to be a low-cost, market-tracking disruption. Instead, legacy fund houses hijacked the space, active funds kept winning, and the newcomers are now staring down decades of waiting for their own Vanguard moment

Low-cost index funds were supposed to reshape investing in India, but distributors and legacy fund houses are keeping them on the sidelines. New players have slashed fees and pushed innovation, but they still control just a tiny fraction of the mutual-fund industry’s passive assets. Passive funds were meant to be simple, but an explosion of niche indices, smart-beta products, and complex hybrids is making them harder to sell. Passive funds are slowly growing, but until retail investors shift preferences or a giant like Jio-Blackrock shakes things up, the revolution remains stuck in first gear. [Read the full story]

Scaler taught AI. Now, AI is teaching Scaler a lesson

With fewer hiring opportunities and AI replacing junior developers, the company is struggling to justify its promise

Scaler, an upskilling platform, grew in size by offering specialised AI courses and placement assistance. The poor quality of engineering colleges in India meant Scaler could offer a more hands-on alternative. But as AI takes the jobs of entry-level software professionals, Scaler is now having to compete with the same AI. More and more developers are using AI to learn and work, and Scaler is responding by integrating more AI into its business model. [Read the full story]

Kaynes and others want to do for electronics what TCS and Infosys did for IT

Electronics manufacturers need skilled talent, and now. So, a couple of them are taking charge and saving recruiters’ time and resources

India is in a race to be the next manufacturing hub. And electronics manufacturers have no time to lose to help grow the sector into $500 billion by FY30. But there exists a huge skills gap. Indian colleges aren’t helping either by churning out engineers whose skills are merely academic. A couple of companies like Kaynes and Macdermid Alpha have chosen to do what IT-services firms like Infosys and TCS did once—tie up with institutions. But scaling up of such programmes in electronics is harder than it was for IT services. [Read the full story]

Tata Capital’s IPO has a Tata Motors problem

A merger with the automaker’s finance arm adds Rs 40,000 crore to Tata Capital’s book—but will dent its financials

Tata Capital is gearing up to make its stock-market debut soon and is awaiting NCLT’s nod to merge Tata Motors Finance with itself. Ironically, the merger, expected to be greenlit by 31 March, stands to hurt Tata Capital’s otherwise impressive financial performance. That’s because Tata Motors Finance is exposed to high-risk borrowers, and so, has lower margins; thanks to its “captive” status. If at all it was up to Tata Capital, analysts believe the company wouldn’t have opted to either merge or list, for it was in its sweet spot. [Read the full story]

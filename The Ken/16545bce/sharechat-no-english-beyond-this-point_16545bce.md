Without fail. Though, the subject of our story today, the social networking app ShareChat, does come very very close. For one, it allows communication and exchange of content in all these languages.
The application, which was founded in 2015, is nearing 10 million daily active users (DAUs). As compared to messenger app Hike's 8 million DAU. But so far, ShareChat has not started monetising on the application and doesn’t plan to do so anytime soon. Not until it hits at least 25 million in DAUs.
And this shouldn't ideally be very hard, given its premise—to provide an all-in-one social network that works as Facebook, Instagram, WhatsApp, Pinterest and more, for those who do not speak English. So much so that the app has put a blanket ban on the sharing of English content. There's a solid opportunity in space—the Indian language internet. As of 2016, there are 234 million Indian language internet users.
234 million. That is the number of people ShareChat could potentially gain access to. Them and the next 300 million set to join them by 2021. That's right, over the next five years, nine out of every 10 internet users in India are likely to be Indian language users, according to an April 2017 report by consulting firm KPMG and Google.
So far, ShareChat has raised about $23 million in funding from the likes of SAIF Partners, Lightspeed Partners, Venture Highway, ShunWei Capital and Xiaomi. The company, in its series B round in December 2017, was valued at $70 million.
Harveen's story today looks into this wave building momentum waiting to crash on the shores of English-media, bringing down the wall of language superiority. In a country with no national language but 22 officially recognised languages. In India, ShareChat currently holds the tools to ride this wave, but the app is also easy to ape. Its first-mover advantage may not amount to much when others start to catch up. Is ShareChat ready to face its rivals? Read the story to get an idea.

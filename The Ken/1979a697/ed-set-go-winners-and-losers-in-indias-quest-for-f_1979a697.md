Ed Set Go
Mon, 23 Jun 25
The one stop to understand the biggest shifts in education, written by the best edtech journalist in the country..
Good Morning Ishan,
The world is, literally and figuratively, on fire. My adopted and original homes are hitting temperatures never reached before. And politically, well, it always feels like we’re on the precipice of an era-defining event.
This week, it’s about America’s decision to attack Iran’s nuclear sites. Indian students are being flown back from cities in Iran and Israel, a move reminiscent of when thousands fled Ukraine when Russia attacked the country. As I’ve said before, geopolitics has rapidly shrunk the international education landscape, especially for Indian students, who now find themselves grappling with existential questions: do we find world-class education in India? Or will new geographies be unveiled to us?
Let’s consider option one—stay back in India.
Ashoka University is trying to cash in on that sentiment quickly. A Linkedin [post](https://www.linkedin.com/feed/update/urn:li:activity:7341323538005377026/) by the university’s co-founder Sanjeev Bikhchandani, says that Ashoka has opened up a special round of admissions for students who have applied to, or gotten admitted to, US universities. US student visa rules are constantly shifting, and it has left a number of Indian students in the lurch. Ashoka wants to capitalise on this moment, and leverage both the money and academic smarts these students can bring. With an annual tuition fee of about $12,000, Ashoka is trying to present itself as a cheaper but high-quality alternative to a US campus.
Ashoka’s just-in-time offer is one way to go. The other option is to build relevance from scratch, by way of educational hubs that can invite foreign universities to set up camp in India. After GIFT City in Gandhinagar, Navi Mumbai is the new spot that foreign campuses are supposed to flock to. The Chief Minister of Maharashtra, it seems, has taken a personal interest in getting this scheme off the ground.
Five international universities—from the UK, Australia, and the US—have been approved to set up “full-fledged” campuses in this Navi Mumbai location called Educity.
The approval has come with a whole host of other [permissions](https://www.thehindu.com/education/five-foreign-educational-institutions-seeking-to-set-up-campuses-in-india-get-letters-of-intent/article69694704.ece) too, some of which would’ve been frowned upon just a few years ago. For instance, institutional autonomy should extend to what these universities charge for their courses. There’s no mention yet of costs involved:
“India can be a global knowledge destination. We have simplified the regulations. We are enabling joint degrees, dual and twinning programs, academic credit mobility and institutional autonomy. Our approach is clear, open, inclusive, ambitious and confident,” Mr. Pradhan said at the event held in Mumbai.
These five universities, and potentially more, will have natural friends and enemies in the Indian education system. There are predictable ones—such as homegrown tech and liberal arts colleges that are struggling to stay afloat with reduced government funding.
But there’s also going to be a divide in the edtech space, as nascent as it is in the larger scheme of things. Educity got a hearty [endorsement](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/ashwindamera_mumbai-activity-7340978352532434944-RWsz/) from Ashwin Damera on Linkedin, who positioned it as a triumphant collaboration between private and public interests.
Of the five foreign universities that recently announced their decision to establish campuses in Mumbai, three had no intention of coming here. In fact, they were actively scouting other Indian cities. Mumbai wasn’t even in contention—written off early because of one factor: prohibitively expensive real estate.

When the vision is big, when authority is decentralized, and when the system backs intent with speed. In that kind of environment, entrepreneurs don’t need to fight the system—they can amplify it. The International Educity is not just another project. It’s a case study in what’s possible when politics moves beyond rhetoric, and governance becomes a platform for ambition.
For Damera, the co-founder of Eruditus, foreign universities on Indian soil make it much easier to launch online courses that have the international stamp, but can be produced with Indian teachers who are likely to apply for jobs in these universities. The courses could also be taught by international faculty who come to work on these campuses. For a platform like Eruditus, the partnership opportunities are endless.
But who loses out on the action? The other branch of edtech that deals with foreign campuses—study-abroad businesses. For them, homegrown education cities are an anathema, because they go against the specific dream they sell of studying abroad. If a student found that they could access the same universities these businesses tie up with, in India, and at a lesser cost, would they make the effort to move?
Education loan businesses have already seen a 50–75% drop in inquiries for US-based degrees. They may have to further tighten their belts if the current phase of uncertainty goes on. In the absence of other viable alternatives—for instance, Canada isn’t as attractive an option as it used to be—Indian students may just prefer to get into a local, international university instead of dealing with the hassle of visas, loans, and relocation.
Of course, all of this discussion could be futile if the Educitys of India don’t really take off and aren’t popular study destinations. That’s when there’ll be a real crunch of options, and there’ll only be one group that loses out—students.

Good Morning Ishan,
On the outside, Chitra Ramkrishna feels like one of those annoying people you find on LinkedIn. Like those CEOs who write long, banal posts replete with hashtags and sentence breaks. The worst ones usually add external validation to their bio, like ‘Forbes 30 under 30’. It’s a way to say, hey, I am relevant and I matter.
Except Chitra Ramkrishna was relevant, and she did matter.
In 2013, Forbes named her Woman Leader of the year. A couple of years earlier, Fortune had [called](https://www.fortuneindia.com/mpw/chitra-ramkrishna?year=2011) her ‘the most powerful woman in India’s securities market’ and lavished praise on her for “securing a near monopoly for NSE (National Stock Exchange) in equity and derivatives trading”. Ramkrishna was also prominently featured on many lists by other business magazines. The adjective ‘powerful’ was a common descriptor.
And she was powerful.
Chitra Ramkrishna was chief executive officer and managing director (CEO & MD) of the NSE, India’s largest stock exchange. She held the position for a little over three years, from April 2013 to December 2016. In that period, she took the NSE to new heights, and to market dominance. Today, the NSE has a 70% operating margin and is a near-monopoly. A lot of this happened during Ramkrishna’s tenure.
But, Chitra Ramkrishna’s decisions were allegedly not her own.
This is basically what the SEBI said last week when they published a 190-page report after an extensive investigation.
|
Chitra Ramkrishna sought advice from a spiritual guru in the Himalayas while head of the National Stock Exchange (NSE), the Securities and Exchange Board of India (SEBI) says.
She allegedly shared business plans, board meeting agendas and financial projections with the unnamed guru.
SEBI said documentation it had gathered during a probe "clearly showed" that the yogi was running the NSE, and Ms Ramkrishna was "merely a puppet in his hands" until the end of her time in the role.
"The sharing of financial and business plans of NSE... is a glaring, if not unimaginable, act that could shake the very foundations of the stock exchange," the regulator said.
|
India bourse head was a 'puppet' of unnamed yogi, regulators say, [BBC](https://www.bbc.com/news/business-60371355)
|
|
|
Perhaps you think this is a little unusual, but not particularly noteworthy.
Well, there’s more.
When asked about the guru, Chitra Ramkrishna claimed he was a ''siddha-purusha'' or ''paramhansa'' who did not have a physical persona and could materialise at will. He apparently dwelt in the Himalayas, and interacted with her primarily via the catchy email id ‘rigyajursama@outlook[dot]com’. The emails reveal how the guru took an interest in her hairstyle, exchanged song recommendations, and even planned a vacation together to Seychelles to (and I am directly quoting here) “chill out”.
Oh, and the guru also gave her specific, detailed instructions on people at the NSE, their compensation, organisation structure, and many other high-level executive decisions that Ramkrishna had to make on a day to day basis.
And here’s the truly crazy part.
Nobody knows the real identity of this person.
If she’s to be believed, not even Chitra Ramkrishna.
There are two ways to read this story.
One interpretation is that Chitra Ramkrishna is a person who made some unusual, misguided choices. She placed her faith and trust in a nameless and faceless guru, whose advice she turned to for key personal and professional decisions. She went too far and shared information she probably shouldn’t have, and this is what landed her in trouble. Chitra trusted someone too much and made a mistake.
But there’s another interpretation.
In this interpretation, Chitra Ramkrishna, during her tenure as the CEO of the NSE, ran a corrupt “money-making scheme” in collaboration with her Group Operating Officer and advisor Anand Subramanian. Together, they made misleading submissions to the SEBI, engaged in an act of dishonesty and misrepresentation, and misused powers granted by the board. And Ramkrishna deliberately concealed the identity of her associate by claiming that he was a spiritual guru with no physical presence.
The latter interpretation is what the SEBI believes, according to their report. They aren’t the only ones. Yesterday, the income tax department raided her residence and took possession of key materials. She’s also being interrogated by the Central Bureau of Investigation (CBI), and has been banned from travelling abroad.
In today’s edition, I’ll go into the findings of SEBI’s 190-page report and explain why this matters, and what comes next for the NSE.
Let’s dive in.

The CEO, the Yogi, and the stock exchange
|
If you want to understand how the NSE grew and got to where it is today, I really recommend this story written by my colleague Anand. Here’s how he summarises its rise.
|
NSE was established in 1992 to break the near-monopoly of the century-old BSE, which was beset with mismanagement and high-profile controversies such as the Harshad scam. NSE did very well, as it adopted technology, implemented pan-India electronic screen-based trading, eased broker membership norms, focused on quality and transparency, and increased market depth.
“NSE’s head-start in derivatives trading at the turn of the century gave it an unassailable lead,” says a long-time market watcher. “BSE was slow to offer derivatives, and lost the game to its young, nimble-footed competitor in six to 12 months.” NSE is now the largest derivatives exchange in the world, and among the largest in the cash segment in terms of volumes.
But then, the solution became a problem over the years. Market participants say that NSE is now what BSE was three decades ago—a near-monopoly with the attendant risks. In a business where ‘liquidity begets liquidity’, there’s scepticism whether Sebi’s new proposed solution will work and usher in competition.
|
How India's NSE turned from solution to problem, [The Ken](https://the-ken.com/story/how-nse-india-turned-from-solution-to-problem/)

Chitra Ramkrishna arrived as the CEO and MD of the NSE in April 2013.
And as the story goes, the day she assumed office, she walked in with a gentleman named Anand Subramanian.
Pay attention to that name. He’s an important part of the story.
The nature of Ramkrishna’s relationship with Subramanian is curious and integral to what happens next. In fact, the SEBI report begins with the circumstances of how he was appointed at the NSE. It’s also the reason why the investigation began in the first place—because SEBI received some anonymous complaints about his appointment and his role in the NSE.
When Subramanian joined the NSE, his role was that of a ‘Chief Strategic Advisor’. It was a part-time consultant role where he worked for four days a week.
But this hire was curious for three reasons:
First, Anand Subramanian had no relevant experience for the position he was appointed to. At the time of the appointment, he was the VP of Leasing and Repair Services at a company called Transafe Services Limited.
Second, the proposed compensation for Subramanian was Rs 1.68 crore (~US$225,000) annually. His previous compensation had been Rs 15 lakh (~US$20,000) per annum.
I know a lot of people think tech salaries in 2022 are ridiculous, but Subramanian literally got a 10X hike on his salary when he joined the NSE… as a consultant. That was in 2013.
By April 2016, he’d be drawing a compensation of Rs 4.21 crore (~US$560,000) annually. He was consistently rated as a top performer, but there was no evidence of his performance evaluation file.
Once again, I must stress that according to the records, he was a part-time employee.
If you were a VC company in 2013, forget Flipkart, you would have been better off betting on Subramanian.
Third, the only person who interviewed him was Chitra Ramkrishna.
There are no notes about the interview, the position was not advertised, and nobody else was considered.
In her statement, Ramkrishna claimed that Anand Subramanian’s name was recommended to her by a HR consultant. However, she also adds that she knew him previously through his wife… who was also employed as a consultant at the NSE.
Fine, you may think all of this sounds really suspicious. But there’s one more thing. Companies like the NSE aren’t like regular companies by nature of what they do. They are subject to higher standards of regulations and disclosure. In fact, several courts have ruled that stock exchanges are the “first layer of oversight” and have a “frontline responsibility for regulation of the markets and compliance of rules”.
One of those rules is called disclosure of Key Management Personnel.
In simple terms, this means that the NSE is obligated to disclose who the important people involved in its decision-making are, their responsibilities, and their compensations.
By 2016, Subramanian’s designation was changed to Group Operating Officer and advisor to MD. This position had several department heads report directly to him. There was only one person above him, who was the CEO herself.
Despite this, Subramanian was never listed as a Key Management Personnel by the NSE.
There’s very little documentation of this in the exchange’s official channels.
But guess where all of this was being discussed.
The emails exchanged between Chitra Ramkrishna and her spiritual guru, which are extensively quoted in the SEBI report, are both intriguing and curious. At several points, the tone of the emails are exactly what you’d expect, like it was written by a less erudite Deepak Chopra. Ramkrishna is very respectful in her communication, and on occasion mentions that the company is running “only because of thy grace and my G’s blessing, Swami”.

This is the general tone of the emails. Ramkrishna takes his advice on how to constitute the board, how to handle key shareholders, and she receives specific, useful, actionable advice. It’s not broad and generic. This is what makes things particularly worrying.
But the part where things get really interesting and complicated is about Anand Subramanian’s designation, compensation, and how to avoid doing all this without making him into a KMP.
The guru gives her the advice, once again, in very specific detail. He also gives directions on how he is to get a commission from the entire structure of Subramanian’s salary.
In the email, SOM refers to Chitra Ramkrishna, and Kanchan is Anand Subramanian.

At this point you may ask, wait a minute, how did these emails come out?
SEBI came across these conversations when they were investigating another impropriety at the NSE, later called as the algo colocation scam. The details are too complicated to go into here, but suffice to say, the allegation was that the NSE had favoured some brokers over others which gave them an edge in high-frequency trading. During the course of the investigations, SEBI found the emails from Chitra Ramkrishna to this nameless guru and asked the NSE to investigate.
Let’s just say that the NSE took their own time to do this.
And post their investigations, combined with a forensic investigation report from E&Y, the NSE submitted that the anonymous spiritual guru in the emails was none other than Anand Subramanian himself. According to them, they discovered evidence in the desktop computers previously used by Subramanian where he had set up a Skype account with the same ID as the email of the guru.
Yes. It’s crazy. I know.
Look, we don’t have a copy of the forensic report. So all details about what’s in it come from what’s in the SEBI report.
But we do know one thing. If the anonymous guru whom Chitra Ramkrishna was corresponding with (sometimes with Subramanian in cc) was really Subramanian, then it was also awfully convenient for the NSE. Since Subramanian was also an employee of the NSE, the material shared with him would not be a leak of a confidential report to an outsider.
Sure, it’s weird, but the NSE would technically be in the clear.
Anyway, the SEBI report is fairly sceptical that Subramanian is the person whom Ramkrishna was exchanging emails with.
I’ll end with a final note by the only reporter who doggedly chased this story and other stories about the goings-on at the NSE—she’s also the person who brought the Harshad Mehta scam to light in 1992, which eventually led to the creation of the NSE.
Here’s the one and only Sucheta Dalal, who takes no [prisoners](https://www.moneylife.in/article/nse-mismanagement-chitras-weird-himalayan-yogi-and-sebis-weak-order-push-indias-image-back-to-snake-charmers-and-holy-men/66406.html).
|
SEBI’s failure to conclusively identify the ‘guru/ unknown person’ ensures that nobody is seriously indicted. It then goes on to indict the NSE board for letting off Chitra and Subbu by allowing them to resign and not sacking them. Interestingly, even under the new MD-CEO, Vikram Limaye, the NSE has aggressively defended the actions of the directors, then and now. Also, both NSE and Ravi Narain (indicted former vice-chairman and a member of the founding team of the Exchange), seem to believe that once a person has resigned (Subbu as well as Chitra, in October and December 2016, respectively), no further action is possible. If this is accepted at face value, it would mean that anyone can get away with fraud at the monopoly exchange with no consequences other than resignation. And why won’t the same latitude be given to companies and market intermediaries?
The NSE board members, who I spoke to, aggressively defend their decision to allow Chitra and Subbu to resign, despite knowing the enormity of wrongdoing, fudging regulatory filings, etc, on the pretext of avoiding litigation. This is amusing since NSE itself had no issues in filing litigation to contest every SEBI order. These sources strongly refute the claim that SEBI was not informed of NSE’s actions and decisions. They claim to have met the SEBI chairman Ajay Tyagi and given him a copy of their report. This is a serious charge and contradicts SEBI’s findings. It also raises a question about why the chairman did not counsel the board and ask it to act more decisively. Isn’t it the job of the regulator to provide this guidance to public interest directors, who were specifically appointed by SEBI? According to my source, former SEBI chairman UK Sinha had specifically appointed them to clean up the Exchange. If this is true, who will hold SEBI responsible? Will these directors speak up? One of them told me they are discussing the matter and taking legal opinion.
On the flip side, if they remain silent and SEBI sticks to its make-believe indictment, then it raises questions about their acting as independent directors on other listed entities and proxy advisory firms. After all, if they have fallen short of meeting their own fiduciary obligations when appointed as public interest directors in a first-line regulator with the monopoly presence that the NSE has, how does the regulator expect them to act independently on other boards? The ball is in SEBI’s court. If its charge has substance, it should have consequences; otherwise, the regulator is no different from a troll who uses its power and presence to cast aspersions on others, to deflect attention from its own failures.

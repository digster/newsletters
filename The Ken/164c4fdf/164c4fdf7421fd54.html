"Those magnificent men in their flying machines."
 
This is how VCs are perceived as by most people—exalted high-flyers, living in ivory towers that hold untold riches that every startup founder aspires to gain access to. And every once in a while, the VC deigns to drop a Rapunzel-like ladder down to a select few founders, changing their lives forever.
 
An enviable job any way you look at it, right?
 
Not quite.
 
In many ways, a VC's life is just as tough as a startup founder's, and 10X tougher in one particular aspect—fund-raising. Most startup founders, even the unicorn types, go through the grind of fund-raising on just a few occasions in their lifetimes. But VCs need to be on the fundraising road almost all the time. Apart from a few marquee funds such as Sequoia Capital, who can raise new funds seemingly effortlessly on the strength of their brands, most VCs have to keep pitching—as soon as one fund is deployed, they need to start pitching for the next one to keep the hamster wheel running. The dynamics of fund economics make this inevitable.
 
But the fact of the matter is that most of these aspects of a VC's life are shrouded in secrecy, unknown to most. VCs don't like talking about these things—partly for fiduciary reasons, but more so to avoid appearing like everyday folk who share the same vulnerabilities and insecurities as the startup founders they back.
 
In today's story, Ashish provides a rare behind-the-scenes glimpse into these aspects in a story on Blume Ventures. In it, Blume's co-founder Karthik Reddy speaks candidly about his fund's journey through the past, present and future to give readers an opportunity to grok a VC's challenges and imperatives.

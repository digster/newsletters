There's a scene in the fantastic movie 'The Big Short'. In it, Steve Carell's character, Mark Baum visits the office of Standard and Poor's - one of the credit rating agencies who routinely certified the subprime bonds at the highest level before the 2008 recession. The S&P representative - wearing dark glasses that impair her vision (a metaphor that's a bit too on the nose, IMO), says quite candidly, "If we don't work with them, they will go to our competitors..."
Mark Baum stares at her in disbelief.
"Not our fault. That's simply the way the world works," she adds.
There are significant parallels between what unfolded at Wall Street and what's going on today with Infrastructure Leasing & Financial Services Ltd (IL&FS) in India. To recap, the Mumbai-based lending giant, which funds infrastructure projects across India, defaulted on a few payments. Simply put - it faced a liquidity crunch. The ripple effect has affected the economy, and companies are feverishly checking to see their exposure to IL&FS bonds. Sounds familiar?
The underlying reasons for the default are more complicated, but there's one question that's getting louder every day. It's a question that was asked a decade ago as well: Why didn't we see this coming?
Enter our story. You see, the major credit ratings agencies (CRA) in India - CRISIL, ICRA and CARE, were not just caught napping, they thrived. As my colleague, Sidhartha writes, "Over the long-term, CRAs have been wealth generators for investors. These companies have outperformed both the Nifty and PSU bank index over the last 10 years." Bizarrely, these rating agencies work on an issuer pays model, so a conflict of interest is inherent. Our story goes into more details. How did this happen? Who messed up? What are the incentives for these CRAs? It's a great, topical story, especially given the tussle between the government and the RBI which is centred around the question of credit, and of companies who want this credit, but not the consequences.

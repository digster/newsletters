Good Morning Ishan,
 
I’m old enough to remember when Chennai—still Madras back then—had green buses. Mind you, these were green only in terms of paint. Known as ‘Pallavan buses’ after the name of the then transport corporation, the buses were old, quick to give you tetanus, and, more often than not, found by the side of the road waiting for someone to come fix them.
 
The new buses that Chennai got next were an abysmal beige and brown, but these were also modern, low-floor ones. We then got ‘deluxe’ red-and-white buses, plush blue AC buses, fancy vestibule buses (thankfully, none of the buses I was on had rusty edges). And then, in 2019, Chennai flagged off its first ‘green’ bus—an actual electric bus.
 
Green, beige, brown or red, most of these buses—including the electric one—were built by Ashok Leyland, the third-largest bus marker in the world with a dealership network spanning over 50 countries.  
 
Its ambition in the e-bus segment is gaining more ground now, especially as e-buses are expected to account for 8-10% of all new bus sales by 2025. Last year, the company combined its electric commercial vehicle operations with that of its British bus subsidiary to create Switch Mobility.
 
Switch has several things going for it. It has deep pockets and influence courtesy of Ashok Leyland’s parent Hinduja Group. It’s a well-known name in the bus business. It’s one of five or six frontrunners for a government tender worth Rs 5,500 crore ($724 million) that will put 5,580 e-buses on the roads of five major Indian cities.
 
Most important of all, it’s setting up a new subsidiary called OHM Mobility, which offers electric mobility as a service (eMaaS).
 
You see, India’s state governments and state transportation units don’t have the kind of money it takes to buy, operate, and maintain a fleet of e-buses. An electric bus costs, on average, around Rs 1 crore to 1.2 crore ($131,000 to $158,00)—starkly higher than the cost of an ICE variant, which ranges between Rs 30-40 lakh ($40,000 - $52,000). So the government now wants the bidder to run the fleet for them.
 
And if Switch, a late entrant to the game, knows that, others do too—including startups, investors, and heavyweights like Tata Motors.

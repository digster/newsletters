If there was one global tech company whose chief executive ostensibly seemed to be in love with India, it was Cisco. John Chambers would visit India a few times in the year, pay respects to the PMO in Delhi, dominate business dailies' headlines, make some investment announcements and go back, reassured that Cisco remained fresh in public memory.
 
Chambers branded India as Cisco’s second headquarters—which we learn now was more a token, because there never was a budget to make it one—from where Cisco would serve the global market and build low-cost innovative products.
 
But by 2012, when its Chief Globalisation Officer was packing up from India, it was clear that nothing of what Chambers had planned was panning out. But business as usual continued. Every time a new building came up in Cessna Business Park in Bengaluru, Cisco had the first right to refusal. Until 2017, when, for the first time in a decade, it said it didn’t want any more space.
 
Gone now is the ‘priority country’ tag. A defining corporate restructuring, severe pruning of leadership jobs, shutting down of biz units—too much is happening at Cisco India.
 
If “at one point San Jose looked like the inside of Wipro”, then a “white-washing” has already undone that. Which, to be fair, is also incidental: a result of the new CEO replacing Chambers-favourites as he overhauls the company. A turnaround in Cisco’s fortunes is evident but what is not evident is how—for Cisco and other tech MNCs—India is no longer the chosen destination where projects come for execution. It has to earn its spurs now.

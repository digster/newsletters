Some problems are so complex, said author Laurence J Peter, that you have to be highly intelligent and well informed just to be undecided about them.
 
This thought came to my mind as we began discussing the Ayushman Bharat (AB) roll out at The Ken. The largest universal health scheme anywhere in the world—as it is touted to be—wherein just having noble intentions doesn’t cut the mustard.
 
So we decided to just bring you its first month report. (I promise, it won’t come every month.) A lot is happening on the ground. But first, credit where it is due: the Modi government has managed to convince all but two states to go with its AB tide.
 
In theory, that’s great news. In practice, insurers and hospitals are sulking, abstaining, even protesting. So much so that in Meghalaya last month, the scheme couldn’t be launched at all. The state has floated another tender.
 
If Meghalaya was a warning, many other states have heeded it, deciding to bypass insurers altogether. Instead, they have turned to third-party administrators (TPAs). Andhra Pradesh, which runs one of the largest and oldest state health insurance schemes, has convinced many states that using TPAs is more efficient.
 
That was insurers. For hospitals, the reimbursement rates are veering off the course. For instance, the rates for Ayushman Bharat are 10% lower than what Arogya Karnataka, the Karnataka government’s state health scheme, offers. Arogya Karnataka is 10-15% lower than the central government scheme rates, which are 40-60% lower than their rack rates. And so on.
 
How is the government, which estimates 2.5 to 5 million people to seek hospital care and raise a claim every year, going about this mega experiment? That is Ruhi’s story today. On to it here: https://the-ken.com/story/cracks-show-month-ayushman-bharat/

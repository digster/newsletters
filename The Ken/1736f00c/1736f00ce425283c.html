Good morning.
 
It’s really hard to avoid China, try as hard as one might. So, while Huawei has fractured the global telecom market into two camps, what will the case be in India? And what does Alibaba-backed Ant Group’s move to IPO in Hong Kong have to do with TikTok owner ByteDance? We also delve into what happens when clusters become smaller—of fresh grads being hired by tech giants, and as tech giants and startups WFH. To wrap things up, we have a little something on dirty money as well. Let’s go.
Trust deficit, circle by circle
 
Seema
 
The fifth-generation wireless technology is splintering the world into American and Chinese camps. 
 
If the United Kingdom’s decision to ban 5G, with retrospective effect, is seen as a proxy victory for US President Trump, let’s not forget that 90-odd countries have pledged some part of their telecom future with Huawei, the Chinese company that is at the heart of this global trade fracture. 
Trade relies on common rules but Britain’s decision has been made amid a swirl of lobbying and threats. It is hard to elicit a principle behind it that can be usefully applied more broadly. If the problem is gear made in China, then Ericsson and Nokia do that, too. If it is Chinese firms building systems which connect devices (in the case of 5g, robots and machines), then a similar logic could be applied across a digitising world economy. German cars and Apple phones sold in China are packed with software, data and sensors. Is China entitled to ban them, too?
China v America, Trade without trust, The Economist 
In India, Airtel is reportedly replacing Huawei gears with Ericsson as it expands or upgrades its 4G network. It’s gradual, circle by circle. Thankfully, India’s reaction hasn’t been knee-jerk and there’s no diktat for retrospective replacement of Chinese gears. 
 
As we wrote earlier, it could cost Indian operators upwards of $1 billion dollars. Given telcos’ poor financial health and an imminent 5G spectrum auction for which they need to shore up their finances, a Huawei-blow to the existing network is the last thing they’d want. Especially Airtel, which has Reliance Jio nipping at its heels with 5G solutions. Jio has applied to the Department of Telecom for 5G spectrum. 
 
If push comes to shove, operators will face no more than a month’s disruption to make the switch from Huawei to any other vendor. “Those vendors will come and do their bit—divide a circle into clusters, and replace the gears cluster by cluster. That way, even if something goes wrong, you don’t have the whole network going down, you have a fallback. You can keep the boxes running in parallel,” says a telecom analyst. 
 
That’s the worst case scenario. Airtel and Vodafone-Idea are concerned about this, but one hopes the India-China trade war doesn’t descend into this replacement war. 
 
For a so-far divided Europe, it’s different. “The direct cost of ripping Huawei out of European networks is tolerable—adding less than 1% to Europeans’ phone bills if amortised over 20 years,” according to The Economist.
Source: The Economist
All eyes are on Germany, which intends to decide by autumn whether to go the UK way or stand its ground with regard to the US pressure to alienate Huawei. A Chinese behemoth that is as much a totem of tech as of the failed global trade response to the authoritarian economic power that is China.
But far from showing the West’s resolve, the saga reveals its lack of a coherent strategy. If open societies and authoritarian China are to keep their economic links and avoid a descent into anarchy, a new trade architecture is needed.

…

The West cannot fundamentally change China or ignore it. But by acting together, it can find a way to do business with an authoritarian state it mistrusts. Huawei marked a failure to do this. Time to start again
India should play an active and responsible role in building this new architecture. Even confined to just telecom, its two private telecom operators—Jio Infocomm and Airtel—have global ambitions, which is more than apparent in their respective new deals. A fractured global market with a decaying trading system will do them little good.
Alibaba’s latest IPO is a reminder it is China-first
 
Jon
 
Alibaba has a habit of setting trends.
 
Its 2014 US IPO—which raised a record US$21.8 billion—opened the door for Chinese tech firms to list in the US. In December 2019, it held a landmark Hong Kong IPO that raised US$13.4 billion and led to other Chinese tech firms listing in the city-state.
 
Now, third time's the charm.

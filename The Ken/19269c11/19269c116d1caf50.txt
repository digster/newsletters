Dabur struggles to break free of its past. The decline in its September-quarter revenue shouldn’t be as surprising as it seems 
 By Seetharaman G https://the-ken.com/writers/seetharamang/

Good Morning Ishan,

Dabur’s shareholders have been waiting for years for the fast-moving-consumer-goods (FMCG) company to break out of its inertia. 

In the five years ended March 2023, Dabur’s revenues grew at a compound annual rate of 8.4%, the slowest among FMCG companies. For instance, Hindustan Unilever (HUL) and Tata Consumer Products grew 11.2% and 15.1%, respectively.

What’s more, only four of the company’s brands, including Amla Hair Oil and Red Paste, were in the coveted Rs 1,000-crore ($120 million) sales club. That number was 19 for HUL, India’s largest company in the segment.

Of course, HUL is 5X the size of Dabur, but the major difference between the two is that the former’s sales mix is a healthy combination of mass brands such as Clinic Plus (haircare) and Bru (coffee) and premium ones like Lakmé (cosmetics) and Magnum (ice-creams). And unlike Dabur, HUL’s 50-plus brands across sixteen categories cater to all geographies and age groups.

Dabur seeks GenZ approval to shed boomer image, The Ken https://the-ken.com/story/dabur-seeks-gen-z-approval-to-shed-boomer-image/

Dabur, with a Rs 1 lakh crore (US$11.9 billion) market cap, has thrown a bunch of things at the wall: getting its customers to buy pricier variants of brands like Odomos, a mosquito repellent; jumping on the direct-to-consumer bandwagon long after its rivals lost interest in it; and of course, trying to be a company as much for twentysomethings as for the middle-aged. 

But it has had to reorient itself while trying to measure up to rivals quarter after quarter. A case of building the plane while flying it. 

Dabur’s statement to the stock exchanges last week is an example of the turbulence that comes with such manoeuvres. In a precursor to its results for the September quarter, the 140-year-old company said its revenue for the period would see a mid-single-digit decline from the year-ago period. 

The reason: heavy rains affected out-of-home consumption in certain categories, especially beverages. (Dabur sells the “Real” brand of juices and milkshakes, besides toothpaste, honey, and hair oil.) The company’s shares promptly plummeted 8% to hit a three-month low. 

This would only make the stock’s uninspiring run over the past year, relative to its peers, even worse.

Nearly half the company’s domestic sales—which in the year ended March 2024 was Rs 8,560 crore (US$1 billion)—is from rural areas, but it has struggled to adapt to the rise of e-commerce in cities. In its note last week, Dabur said the “disproportionate” growth of supermarkets and e-grocers, including instant-delivery apps such as Blinkit and Swiggy Instamart, has led to an inventory pile-up at kiranas (local small stores). So Dabur is now fixing that, which means a hit to the top line.

In the year ended March 2019, Dabur and Hindustan Unilever (HUL) both got roughly 85% of their revenue from kiranas. Five years later, HUL slashed it to 72%, while Dabur was at 80%, according to Centrum Broking.

But there’s a far more consequential lesson HUL can teach Dabur. More than nine in ten households already use laundry detergent and soap, two key categories for HUL. So it can’t predicate its growth on finding new consumers, which is why in the 2010s, it increased https://the-ken.com/tradetricks/hindustan-unilevers-detergent-powered-decade/ the contribution of premium detergents—including the liquid variety—to its sales volume by two-thirds.

In soaps, in addition to pushing consumers to switch from bars to body washes, HUL has been looking for ways to shore up its profitability. After five years of research and 20 patents, HUL recently introduced a technology that replaces palm oil, a commodity whose prices can be volatile, with plant-based ingredients in its products.

HUL, as I have argued https://the-ken.com/tradetricks/trade-tricks-hul-wants-you-to-know-elephants-can-dance/ before, is an elephant that can dance. Dabur, on the other hand, is still tripping over its own feet.

[Dabur CEO Mohit] Malhotra has to walk a tightrope between reinventing and staying true to the one thing that makes it one of India’s largest FMCG companies.

The portfolio Dabur has, and the portfolio Dabur should have will be very different, Malhotra had said in the meeting. “It’s not that we will steer away from the core, which is well-being and health, but the surroundings of the [core] values will get tweaked.”

To rejig its products as well as roll out new ones, the company would work with product developers of startups and add Dabur’s efficacy in terms of Ayurveda in it, Malhotra had said then.

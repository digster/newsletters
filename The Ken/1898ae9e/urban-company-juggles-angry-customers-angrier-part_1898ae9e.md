Good Morning Ishan,
Ten years ago, Urban Company (then, Urbanclap) had a bold vision: create a platform that instantly hooks you up with a top-notch home squad. Electricians, plumbers, carpenters, cleaners, and salon and spa wizards—all at your service, just a tap away.
Seems like a no-brainer, right? Let’s face it, most of us moving to newer cities or shifting next door know zilch about the local tradesmen. We need a reliable team—without whom, hopes are pinned on the chance that our dampening walls or unkept hair won’t haunt us for eternity.
Initially, the plan worked like a charm. For users and skilled gig workers alike. Tech investors, too, salivated over such platforms…before the world got a taste of the pandemic. Startups, dreaming of IPO glory, smacked right into a concrete wall when the markets nosedived. Russia made its move on Ukraine. Inflation went bonkers, and interest rates soared. Survival mode: Activated. Dreaded layoffs: Started.
Today’s account isn’t about those layoffs, though—it’s all about the Urban Company partners, the true backbone of its unicorn rise.
So here we are, folks, bearing witness to Shivani’s debut story for The Ken. She reports the partners are getting the boot, and they’re raising hell (yet again). This time on a grander scale.
Shivani argues that the recent events have unleashed a double-edged sword: while the gig-economy warriors rise up in rebellion, we—the consumers—bear the brunt of the storm, too.
Here are three nuggets that hint at a spawning garden of discontent, thanks to the company’s pursuit of profitability:
First, take a peek at the Google Play Store. Urban Company boasts 10 million downloads, but out of the million customer reviews, many are just downright disgruntled. Complaints range from customer support nightmares, cringe-worthy experiences, cancellation fees that sting, and time slots that play hard to get. Most gripes are fresh from 2023.
Then, crunch some numbers, shall we? Urban Company’s net loss doubled, clocking in at US$63 million in the year ended March 2022.
Finally, Urban Company has an action plan—or so it thinks. The fix comes in the form of unrealistic ratings. A slip up here, and the partners get a warning. A slip up there, and their work IDs go into exile.
To repair this chaos, Urban Company has to revisit the basics: connect workers with households and keep everyone satisfied. Easy peasy, right? Well, not so much, as the story reveals.

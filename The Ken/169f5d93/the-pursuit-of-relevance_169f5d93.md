Good Morning Ishan,
In June 1999, I returned to boarding school after two months of summer vacations armed with a suitcase, a trunk, a Sony Walkman and dollops of excitement. Gadgets were banned by our austere matrons, but that was no deterrence. In hostels, where peers either become family, or judge-jury-executioner, you do what it takes to remain relevant. Want to win over your batchmates? Sneak in a Walkman and stop being the uncool kid no one wants to play with. Want to win over your seniors? Sneak in beer with the help of the mess staff. Want to win over matrons? Tattle on the miscreants and enjoy a later curfew while peers get caned and kneel for hours on concrete floors.
Our lives are dictated not by the pursuit of happiness, but the pursuit of relevance. The pursuit to matter to friends, family, and bosses; and in the case of social media, a pursuit for stranger-fuelled validation. It starts with our education system, which makes us believe lacklustre marksheets will render us insignificant. Or that extracurricular participation will partially absolve us of said insignificance. The fear of irrelevance is a cross we carry on our adult backs. We forgo, and are forgone, jobs and relationships that matter less with changing circumstances. Men dive into midlife crises to feel wanted, either by younger folks, or by life itself. The elderly experience empty nest syndrome, grappling with feeling "unneeded" by grown-up offspring.
The human pursuit for relevance also spills over into business. Note how the most successful companies expand in a bid to retain your mindshare (Apple with streaming services, General Motors with electric cars, etc). Our stories over the past week highlighted the manifestations of this need to remain relevant, the ways in which companies are trying to keep the wisps of time off their faces.

China can be unforgiving terrain, but OYO's fevered expansion dreams have seen it claim 340,000 rooms there in one year. Compare that to India, where it has 173,000 rooms in five years of its existence. How has it already become China's fourth-largest hotel chain (by number of rooms)? And will its quest to win China over end in a bang or bust?
Pharma companies also find themselves in an unending quest to matter. E-pharmacies like Medlife and 1mg are doubling as links between patients and pharma companies, collecting consumer data and monetising it along the way. A missed opportunity for drug manufacturers. Can you afford to turn a blind eye on the patient data goldmine? Cipla doesn't think so, which is why it bought a stake in digital therapy firm Wellthy Therapeutics. A one-time disruptor, it's now a faltering incumbent in a digital payments space chock full of multinational competitors. Which is why the company launched Paytm First, its subscription-only loyalty programme. Will this model be Paytm's buoy in choppy waters? Speaking of payments, Arundhati takes us through the journey of Pine Labs, an offline payment processor that bought gifting platform Qwikcilver Solutions for $110 million. Its ambitious bid for a slice of online retail pie comes at a time when Google Pay, Amazon Pay and PhonePe are going offline. As a payments executive says, "If Pine Labs doesn't make this move to online payments, it risks getting Nokia-ed." In its desperation to make it through Reliance Jio's battle-tanking, Airtel has culled 49 million of its own subscribers. That's 15% of its customer base. This is a bold step in an environment where subscriber quantity, rather than quality, matters to telecom companies. For one, Airtel's average revenues per user on postpaid is 2-2.5 times that of Jio. Will cream-of-the-crop subscribers help the company get out of a rut?

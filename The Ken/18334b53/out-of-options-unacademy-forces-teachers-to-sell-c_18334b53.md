Byju’s has been a lightning rod for those railing against all that is wrong with edtechs in India.
From the mis-selling of courses to the pressure cooker its sales team works in, the goings-on at the $22 billion-worth startup have been
[well documented](https://the-ken.com/story/the-loan-crisis-at-byjus/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter).
Today, my colleague Shruti turns her lens to Unacademy, the SoftBank- and Temasek-backed rival to Byju’s.
The crisis brewing at Unacademy, valued at $3.4 billion, is different but no less alarming. Battling slow growth and a funding crunch, the company has made tutors its new sales force. It expects them to make viral videos and bring in new learners.
A chemistry teacher who quit Unacademy in May told Shruti that their sales target was as much as a third of their salary. The instructor was not told this when they were hired last year.
There were times when the target was–wait for it–more than 5X of educators’ salaries.
Unacademy is not alone in getting teachers to hawk courses. Even Vedantu, another edtech unicorn, ties tutors’ promotions and salary hikes to hitting sales targets. But the consequences of not meeting them are nowhere near as severe as at Unacademy.
Educators are so scarred by the experience that they are not just leaving edtech startups, they are going as far as taking a break from teaching.

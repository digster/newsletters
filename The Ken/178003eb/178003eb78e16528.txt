Good Morning Ishan,

I got a taste of corporate rivalry earlier this week. Claims, counter claims, and some more counter claims surfaced after we published https://the-ken.com/story/inside-bcg-long-game-to-defeat-mckinsey-in-india/#comment-1410303 a BCG-McKinsey story. Phew! That prestige and narrative are everything to some brands was very apparent.

Today’s story has a similar vibe. It involves some of the biggest names in tech—AWS, Microsoft, and latecomer Google Cloud.

Soon after Microsoft won a $10 billion contract in 2019 to build data centres for the US Department of Defence, it attempted an encore. It approached top officials in New Delhi to set up a pan-government data centre. Microsoft even offered to fund a majority of the capex for the project.

AWS has pitched a similar idea to the IT ministry, The Ken has learnt.

It’s an all-out wooing game right now for public sector business. And the way to governments’ cloud war chest is through pro bono work, awareness campaigns, conference sponsorships—the narrative build-up.

New Delhi is lapping it up.

Contact tracing app Aarogya Setu, for instance, was initially hosted for free on AWS. Similarly, eSanjeevani, the health ministry’s telemedicine service, runs on AWS cloud. Amazon also bagged the contract for CoWin—the government’s Covid-19 vaccination management system—to be hosted on AWS’ cloud platform.

The pandemic gesture or deals apart, the public health cloud market easily adds up to $500 million, with India's health ID project alone—which has a new leadership—worth $200 million. Little surprise that AWS has a team of close to 100 executives who do nothing but meet important government officials and educate them about cloud services. Clearly, India’s own NIC-led national cloud is not living up to government’s expectations.

Where are Microsoft and Tata Communications in this race? And who is allaying apprehensions about moving to such cloud infrastructure which is bound by America’s Patriot Act?

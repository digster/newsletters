Good Morning Ishan,
 
When I got my new smartphone a few months ago, I decided to not trade in my old phone. Better to have a backup, I thought. Turns out, most of India thinks like me. India saw 161 million new smartphones in 2021, but only a sixth found their way back into the supply chain, as per market research firm IDC. Clearly, it’s easier to put your old phone in a drawer and forget about it than deal with the hassles of data transfer.
 
This is a major problem for those like the 12-year-old Cashify, which is in the business of buying old phones, and refurbishing and reselling them. India is the fastest-growing market for second-hand smartphones, and yet, the supply bottleneck is limiting its growth. 
 
One way to deal with the problem is by throwing money at it. Cashify has earmarked Rs 100 crore—nearly a third of the $40 million it has raised so far—for marketing in 2022. Its marketing budget now stands at ~3X what it was for the year ended March 2022. It’s even planning to raise more money. The Ken has learned that a large funding round could be announced within the next month.
 
Could this fix Cashify’s supply problems? Maybe. Cashify’s main, er, cash cow is the sale of its refurbished phones under its PhonePro brand. And that business hasn’t performed the way the company envisioned it— PhonePro accounts for just 20% of Cashify’s revenue. Most of its business comes from the sake of second-hand phones it makes to entry-level customers sans almost no repairs. Low margin, low stakes.
 
Cashify needs to change this narrative, and fast. For one, e-commerce behemoth Flipkart—a leader in online smartphone sales—has big plans for the refurbished segment. But more importantly, without fixing its supply problem, everything that Cashify has worked for—growth, profitability et all—could be in vain, writes Aayush in today’s story.

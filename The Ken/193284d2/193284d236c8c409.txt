What’s common between IIM Ahmedabad, IIM Bangalore, IIM Lucknow, Motilal Oswal Financial Services, and Mirae Asset Global Investment? 

Well, in just the past few months, all of them have partnered with the Financial Planning Standards Board (FPSB) India—the B-schools to offer integrated courses on financial planning, and the financial services companies to upskill their workforce. 

FPSB India is the sole certifier for financial planning professionals in India, and passing its tests is the only route through which one can become a Certified Financial Planner (CFP). 

According to the chief executive of FPSB India, Krishan Mishra, with whom I had a chat with recently, there are a large number of vacancies for financial planners in the country—“about 80,000”—and a CFP certificate is sure to open up numerous job opportunities, since there are very few qualified individuals who meet the requirement. Many financial services companies even ask their employees to get the CFP certification some time in their career, Mishra told me. 

Then why, I can hear many of you thinking, haven’t we heard about CFPs as often as, say, chartered accountants (CAs) or certified financial analysts (CFAs)? And don’t we already have enough financial courses/certificates around?

Just what is a CFP?

So, the basics. 

To become a CFP, you have to first pass a set of three exams conducted by the FPSB on investment planning, retirement and tax planning, and risk and estate planning. (You can only apply for the certification if you scored over 50% in your class 12 exams.) And then, after accumulating a year of work experience, you will have to appear for a final test, passing which you will be awarded a certificate and badge from FPSB India. 

Once you are a CFP, you have a wide range of options in front of you, Mishra said—you can be a financial advisor, a wealth manager, an insurance consultant, an investment planner… But only if you work for a company that has been registered under SEBI as an investment advisor, or become a Registered Investment Advisor yourself. “Advisory folks can be more comprehensive in their approach if they take up the CFP course,” Mishra told me. 

CAs and CFAs too would need to become an RIA, or work for an RIA, in order to be able to give investment advice. But unlike the CAs or CFAs, CFPs are trained to focus on personal finance and take care of an individual’s financial needs, Mishra said. Most other financial certification courses, he added, lean more towards the corporate and enterprise side of things.

“A CFP is designed in such a way that it helps professionals cater to the customised needs of an individual. Unlike the other courses, its use case is more personal. Though a chartered accountant is our go-to person for all things personal finance, even they were surprised when they looked at the financial planning course. The idea is to be able to manage finances away from P&Ls, topline, etc.

But CFP does not compete in any way with other courses with CA and CFA. It has its unique place in the financial system.”

Krishan Mishra, chief executive of FPSB India

CFAs, for instance, focus on portfolio management, and the affairs of large-scale corporate finance, investment firms, mutual funds, or hedge funds. And a CA is typically involved in financial forecasting, auditing, and so on. That being said, most CAs and CFAs have a good understanding of personal finance too.

A late start, and fewer incentives

But a few things have prevented CFP from becoming as desired a tag in India as the other two. 

First, it’s relatively new (emphasis on relatively). Financial planning as a certification course first came to India in 2004. But at that point, FPSB India was only an affiliate of the parent body based in the US, which is the global certifier for CFPs. India wasn’t really a priority market then and not much effort went into growing the candidate base here. 

It wasn’t until April 2019 that FPSB India became a wholly-owned subsidiary of the main organisation, and since then, special focus has been devoted to reviving the course, popularising it, and designing it to address the Indian market’s unique needs. The course itself went through a revamp. 

But despite this, only around 2,731 CFPs have successfully completed the programme in India thus far. In contrast, according to media reports, even as of January 2023, over 400,000 had completed the CA course and over 3,500 had completed the CFA course. (The Ken has already written about https://the-ken.com/story/no-one-wants-to-just-be-a-ca-ask-those-enrolled-in-cfa/ the rapid rise in popularity for the CFA course in India, with even some CAs opting to tack on the CFA certification.)

Of late, though, demand for certified financial planners is increasing, say a few other senior financial services industry executives I spoke to. Intake for the CFP course, for instance, has been growing by around 10–15% each year, according to Mishra. 

Given that we are well past the days when financial planning was only for the rich, or the retired, demand for CFPs may well rise faster in the future. 

But there is still a long way to go before CFP can command the same kind of heft as a CA or a CFA tag. After all, CAs have regulatory backing (you have to be a CA to be able to perform certain key functions, such as audits) while CFA is a very widely recognised global investment course.

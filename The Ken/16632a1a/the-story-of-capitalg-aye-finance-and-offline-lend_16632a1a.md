I bumped into Lizzie Chapman last week. Tough to miss her in a nearly empty restaurant. But if you haven’t heard of her, Chapman is the co-founder and CEO of ZestMoney, a digital consumer lending company in India. So Chapman and I shook hands and we spoke. About this and that. Till we finally started talking lending. And financial technology. And a certain telecom company which will soon become a bank and shake things up. Shake up lending. Shake up existing players. Forever.
“It is the world’s easiest business to be in.” That’s Chapman. “If you stand on the street and say, I’m here to lend money, getting customers won’t be your problem. Your problem will be, who do you lend to. Now that’s a tough business.”
On point.
Which is why CapitalG, the investment arm of Alphabet Inc (the Parent company of Google) and its bet on Aye Finance makes for a very interesting case study.
Now the financial technology lending scene in India has mostly been digital. Plus all of CapitalG’s bets globally are in technology or technology-enabled businesses. All, except Aye Finance. The company gives out loans to businesses through offline branches. And to get to these businesses, the company looks for clusters. Like shoemaking in Agra. Garment manufacturing in Delhi. If and only if a cluster looks promising in terms of the number of small businesses working out of it does Aye Finance set shop. Promising clusters are then targeted with branches. And finally loans.
CapitalG seems to like this offline approach. For now.
Aye has lent about Rs 1,000 crore ($137.1 million) since its inception four years ago, serving roughly 80,000 customers. In comparison, Lendingkart has given out Rs 2,000 crore ($274.3 million) since 2014.

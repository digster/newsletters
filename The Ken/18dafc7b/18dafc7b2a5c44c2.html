Even if I wanted to hide from the barrage of Satya Nadella’s AI-speak this past week, I couldn’t.

The Microsoft CEO left no forum untouched to rave about artificial intelligence—how it will solve India’s challenges and why its diffusion is faster than any epochal tech in the last 70 years. He’s even offered to train two million people in India.

Two million? Don’t ask me what that means. Usually, in India specifically, when such “GOATs” (greatest of all time) grant interviews, interviewers forget to ask follow-on questions.

But I like how Nadella and OpenAI CEO Sam Altman are taking turns playing good cop, bad cop on AI. Just as Nadella took India on an AI “odyssey”, Altman, speaking at the World Government Summit in Dubai on Tuesday, warned that “very subtle societal misalignments” could make AI systems wreak havoc.

Nadella’s urgency is understandable. He’s invested US$13 billion in OpenAI and is also doubling down on AI that is smaller and cheaper than OpenAI’s. Microsoft may have gotten the head start, but Google just launched a ChatGPT rival, Gemini Ultra, that goes into its own productivity products and directly competes with Microsoft’s Copilot. At US$20 per user, Google has priced it US$10 cheaper than Copilot.

In a week of coincidents, as Nadella proclaimed India’s IT industry had become “early adopters” of Copilot, The Wall Street Journal reported that the “early adopters” of this AI bot think it may not be worth the price.

There’s another kind of tech flexing going on in the world’s sixth-slowest city—Bengaluru. Since we are talking multi-modality in the data-guzzling AI models these days, the new AI app that the city’s traffic control management is pinning its hopes on will also take in data from ride-hailing apps like Uber and Ola, besides the usual sources.

Developed by Arcadis—a Netherlands-based design, engineering, and management consulting company—the app, Astram, joins the ever-expanding lineup of tech solutions attempting to disentangle Bengaluru’s gridlocks.

The man of the moment, 2009-batch IPS officer and joint-commissioner of police (traffic), MN Anucheth, believes the AI intervention will “help us understand the flow and cascading effects of traffic across the city, based on which we can take action.”

Will that also mean “effective management”? Read Shaswata’s story and assess if more tech is the solution.

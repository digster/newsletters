With any retailer or fast-food chain, the one metric investors always obsess over is same-store sales growth. Or SSSG.
It’s because the top line often looks exemplary because of the addition of new outlets. So what truly matters is how healthy existing stores, or those that have been around for at least 12 months, are.
As an eyewear retailer, Lenskart had to disclose its same-store growth in its shareholders’ letter for its December-quarter results, released last week.
But the company went a bit [further](https://assets.lenskart.com/documents/2026/02/11/Reg30-ShareholderLetter.pdf?_gl=1*8dw7rg*_gcl_au*NzE3MzU2ODg2LjE3NzEyMTczMzI.*_ga*NzM0OTE2NzY2LjE3NzEyMTczMzI.*_ga_0E9YGQ78DV*czE3NzEyMTczMzIkbzEkZzEkdDE3NzEyMTc4NzAkajYwJGwwJGgw).
Beyond SSSG, which only measures performance of stores opened for at least 12 months, we track SPSG, aggregate revenue growth across all stores within a pincode, regardless of when they opened. SPSG captures the net effect of densification at the micro-market level. In 9M FY26, our SSSG was 20% while SPSG was 26%, a 630-basis point premium. This gap confirms we are expanding the market.
If you didn’t already know what SPSG meant, I doubt you’d be able to guess. It’s same-pincode sales growth. Elsewhere in the letter, Lenskart said it’s “densifying, not materially cannibalizing”. Its same-pincode sales have outpaced same-store sales across different kinds of cities. (Lenskart’s revenue in the December quarter rose by a third from a year ago to roughly Rs 2,300 crore, and its profit more than tripled to Rs 133 crore.)
| Source: Lenskart Shareholders' Letter Q3 FY26 |
In its previous shareholder letter, Lenskart pointed out that it averages one store per pincode across its network, and that it could at least add one more outlet in an existing pincode. In large cities, especially dense ones, it could set up more stores within the same pincode.
And even within the cities Lenskart is present in, it doesn’t have stores in two-thirds of the pincodes.
| Source: Lenskart Shareholders' Letter Q2 FY26 |
In December, I [praised](https://the-ken.com/long_and_short/the-welcome-plainspeak-of-shareholder-letters/) Lenskart for not leaning on the “[adjusted Ebitda](https://the-ken.com/story/zomato-firstcry-and-others-love-this-magical-number-you-should-hate-it/)” crutch, like most of its VC-funded peers. I was also glad that Lenskart wanted to earn investors’ trust through “transparent reporting”. Even if it sounded like a platitude to many, the fact that Lenskart would choose to disclose something like same-pincode growth showed that it was not short on intent.
By doing so, Lenskart may end up forcing investors to demand that other retail and food chains begin reporting this metric too. Unless they run outlets bigger than 10,000 sq ft, in which case having more than one in a pincode is unrealistic even in a very dense city like Mumbai, these companies would do well to embrace same-pincode growth. (As of June 2025, the average Lenskart store was over 800 sq ft, and even the ones it wants to set up aren’t much bigger.)
It’s a no-brainer to keep adding stores across a large city or state. But fully tapping the demand in a micro-market requires conviction, partly because chipping away at the sales of existing stores is a legitimate risk.
Jubilant Foodworks, which runs the India franchise of pizza chain Domino’s, is a company that needs to be as close to customers as possible. In 2022, it began shaving a third of its 30-minute delivery time. Two in three Domino’s orders are now delivered in under 20 minutes.
To do so, Jubilant would have added small outlets close to existing ones, in several instances within the same pincode. There are 2,400 Domino’s
stores in India. Knowing how much demand a store can sustain with the addition of two more in the vicinity is material information for an investor.
Blinkit and Swiggy Instamart have a similar model of targeting densely populated areas with reasonable income levels. Their promise is also similar to that of Domino’s: instant delivery. Rising demand from a pincode cannot mean a longer duration for delivery. The solution is often another dark store. Sure, the existing dark store could be expanded, but that has its own constraints.
Analysts often do channel checks to confirm their hypotheses with data they can’t find in investor presentations. Companies refrain from going very granular so as not to show their hand to their competitors.
But more “transparent reporting” certainly doesn’t hurt investors.

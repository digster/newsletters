Ranking TCS, Infosys, Wipro, HCL: who thrives and who survives in the AI era? The sorting hat for IT services

Picture a chessboard. Not the kind for a leisurely Sunday game, but one where billions of dollars, hundreds of thousands of jobs, and the future of an entire industry hang in the balance.

On this board sit four giants: TCS, Infosys, Wipro, and HCL Tech. For 20 years, they’ve dominated Indian IT, seemingly untouchable. 

Then Anthropic launched Claude Cowork, and Rs 2 lakh crore vanished from Indian IT stocks in just four days. So we thought it was high time to reflect on where they stand. 

This week on Two by Two, hosts Praveen Gopal Krishnan (PGK) and Rahel Philipose did something they’d never done before: they ranked all four companies from best to worst positioned for the AI era. And they brought in two people who’ve actually lived this world to help make sense of it.

KK, PGK, and Rahel in the studio while Vivek joined online

Krishnakumar Natarajan https://in.linkedin.com/in/krishnakumarnatarajan co-founded Mindtree in 1999, one of India’s first IT services startups that went on to compete with the giants before being acquired by L&T. He later moved on to back deep tech startups through Mela Ventures. He’s seen Indian IT from every possible angle. Here’s my favorite quote from him: “In TCS, when your manager tells you to jump, the person in the team will ask, ‘From where do I have to jump?’”

Vivek Kant https://www.linkedin.com/in/vivekkant/ spent over two decades at Tech Mahindra, Cognizant, and BCG. He still codes three to four hours daily using AI. His uncomfortable truth: “AI makes good programmers great and average programmers obsolete.” You can read his blogs here https://vivekkant.substack.com/.

For twenty years, these companies printed money by renting out smart people by the hour. But when productivity goes 10X because of AI, and you’re still charging by headcount, the math stops working. So who rewrites the model first? The question isn’t about survival, it’s about who adapts fast enough to stay ahead. And in chess, positioning is everything.

TCS is the Queen. 

The most powerful piece on the board. It can move in any direction because of its diversification: software, hardware, data centres, insurance processing, Passport Seva, along with 6,00,000 employees. It has the full backing of the Tata empire, with N. Chandrasekaran personally ensuring it pivots wherever needed.

But there’s one flaw. 

“This disruption requires people who have curiosity. And the majority of the TCS forces actually curtail them from expressing their curiosity.”

Krishnakumar Natarajan

The Queen has unlimited moves, but a 6,00,000-strong army trained to follow orders might struggle when the board shifts.

Infosys is the Rook. 

Powerful in straight lines. Deep domain expertise in financial services, healthcare, and insurance. They’ve built platforms and invested early in AI. But there’s a crack in the foundation: 

What is the succession plan in Infosys? Salil has already spent seven, eight years. I don’t see any internal successors being groomed or even being presented. 

Krishnakumar Natarajan

HCL Tech is the Knight. 

The piece that moves in ways you don’t expect. Vivek explains why: 

“Approximately 25% of their business comes from infrastructure managed services. These are long-term, fixed-price deals. When AI disrupts this, the value is retained by HCL, not transferred to the client.”

Vivek Kant

They also made a $1.8 billion bet on IBM’s unwanted tech debt and turned it into annuity revenue. Was it risky? Absolutely. But is it paying off? Increasingly, yes.

The Knight can jump over obstacles. And that’s exactly what HCL is doing.

Wipro is the Bishop. 

It has limited moves and is struggling to break free from leadership churn and flat revenue. But PGK sees opportunity: “They have a CEO who’s really interested in proving a point. They’re the ones who literally have the least to lose. So they will be the ones with the most to gain.“

Some think Wipro is the underdog story waiting to happen. Others think it is too late.

And then there’s Cognizant—the piece that doesn’t quite fit. 

Not quite Indian, not quite American. A hybrid stuck between two identities. Vivek, who spent half a decade there, thinks that: “With the new leadership, I don’t see a stable second or third line being groomed.” 

Throughout the episode, no one agreed on each other’s ranking. One picked TCS, another HCL, and the third went with Wipro. By the end, some of them had changed their minds.

Here’s my advice: pick your own ranking before you hit play. Then listen to them argue, defend, and dismantle each other’s logic. See if they convince you, or if you end up convincing yourself differently. It will make the episode much more fun.

No matter what pieces the companies represent now, what matters most is the move they make next.

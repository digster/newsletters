Danone’s odd path to redemption in India. What exactly does it achieve by doubling its stake in Epigamia?

By Nuha Bubere
4 min read
View in browser

It wasn’t long ago that Danone, the French food giant, was humbled in India. 

It gave up on its dairy business in the country in 2018, eight years after making its foray, after incurring losses in products such as milk, flavoured yogurt, buttermilk, cold coffee, curd, and smoothies. Facing off against national majors like Amul and Mother Dairy and regional brands such as Milky Mist was not for the faint of heart.

This is what Danone said in 2018, while exiting the dairy business:

“We have great ambitions for our business in India and remain committed to invest and grow in India through well-established brands such as Protinex, Aptamil, Farex, Dexolac and Neocate. In order to maximise growth opportunities, we are continuously analysing our portfolio and sharpening our focus to accelerate investments on the best performing categories and products. For this reason, we will discontinue some of the SKUs sold in India,” a Danone India spokesperson said.

But Danone seems to have made this decision reluctantly. Within a year of calling it quits, Danone led a Rs 182 crore funding round in Epigamia, a venture capital-backed food brand. 

Now, it reportedly wants to double its stake in the startup to 60%. Oddly enough, Epigamia is synonymous with the same product Danone was perhaps best known for: Greek yoghurt. 

The dairy derivative is only a little less niche than it used to be in 2018. Epigamia, founded in 2015 and credited with making urban Indians familiar with the product, had sales of Rs 170 crore in FY23, the latest year for which financials are available. Even biscuit-maker Britannia, a laggard in dairy despite its three-decade-long efforts, sold Rs 600 crore worth of dairy in the same period.

That’s how inconsequential Greek yoghurt is. 

It’s partly a function of the price. A 400 gm plastic cup of Epigamia’s unsweetened Greek yoghurt retails for Rs 220. You can buy four plastic cups of curd for the same price, and most Indian homes are happy making their own curd. So you can imagine how small the total addressable market is, relative to the overall dairy industry. For context, Amul, India’s largest dairy brand, had sales of Rs 90,000 crore in FY25. 

The high price tag is particularly problematic given Greek yoghurt’s two-week shelf life. (Cheese, a high-margin product, can last months.) So, sizeable unsold inventory can wreck the company’s bottom line. 

I guess you’re beginning to see why I find Danone’s bet on Epigamia puzzling. But you don’t know the half of it. 

Companies like Milky Mist no longer want to leave the Greek yoghurt market to Epigamia, especially when it can sell the product 40% cheaper than Epigamia. As my colleague Seetharaman had mentioned in his story on Milky Mist earlier this year, adding a yoghurt-making unit in a factory that also makes ghee, butter, cheese, and dairy whiteners is considerably cheaper than setting up one just for yoghurt. Plus, a diversified dairy business can cut down on logistics and distribution costs. 

If Amul were to take a big swing at this market, Epigamia is going to have an even harder time. Amul already sells fruit yoghurts and is increasingly happy to even target niche segments like protein. It has already brought out a 200 ml protein milkshake, which offers 20 gm of protein for just Rs 50 ($0.60) compared to the 250 ml protein milkshake from Epigamia, which offers 25 gm protein for Rs 150 ($1.80).

Earlier this month, in an interview with Fortune India, Danone India COO Vikram Agarwal mentioned that it was sad that the company had to discontinue the dairy business in 2018, but noted that “they were unable to get the relevance at that point of time”.

“An important learning from that was to make our products relevant from the point of view of price-point and product composition. If we ever decide to come to India with dairy, we need to be very sure what we put in our products is consumer relevant and is at a price-point that appeals to them. The other point we took note of was distribution.”

If the price-point is indeed a factor, then Danone’s second coming could also turn sour sooner than later.

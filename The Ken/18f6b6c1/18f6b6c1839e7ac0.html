Dear Ishan, a very cool hello from post-peak summer Bengaluru! I’m glad to announce that the worst April we’ve experienced in four decades is finally behind us.

Here’s a graph that shows the average temperature and humidity in our backyard this year. As you can see, the dip is as stark and real, just like the relentless rise from early January.

View in Browser

This year would be the 30th since I first arrived in Bengaluru in 1994. Sadly, I must also announce that I’ve had to finally succumb and purchase an air conditioner for our son’s room. And boy, what timing. We set his room temperature to 24 degrees at night and he woke up to 24 degrees outside!

But hey, 30 years was a good run without ever owning an AC. I feel strangely proud about it, the way Calvin’s dad would, you know. “It builds character.”

Earlier this week, I came across the question “Why did you come here?” in the Kannada book I’m learning the language from. It felt strangely philosophical and personal.

But it was also interesting because the question in Kannada is, “Niivu eeke illig bandri?” Which if you translate back into English, is “You why come here?”

They say learning a new language rewires your brain, and I couldn’t but help feel the same after thinking about how that question is expressed in the different languages I know (and am learning).

  • Kannada: You why here come
  • Malayalam: You here why come
  • English: Why did you come here
  • Hindi: You here why come

Depending on what your dominant language is, the others might look odd. But that’s languages for you. Distinct and beautiful in their own ways.

Summer, even if we’re past its peak in South India (I realize it looms ahead up north), is also about fruits. And wildlife.

Our white water apple tree flowered and fruited profusely. Alas, the lack of any rain combined with the intense heat in April meant that most of the fruits never attained full size, but instead fell off night after night.

View in Browser

Heat aside, one big reason for them falling off the tree were the numerous bats that seemed to purposely fly into the tree at night, rustling up the leaves creepily. I think they loved the water apples too.

Earlier this week, we had a pair of Indian grey hornbills visit the banyan fig right next door, to enjoy the ripe, red fruits. They’re apparently monogamous, which is why you often spot them in pairs.

View in Browser

The presence of nearby lakes and a large forested area belonging to the army are the primary reasons we’re still seeing these majestic beauties.

Back at home, our local “wildlife”, Gabru, has decided to shed his summer coat. Brushing him every morning yields three hands full of wool, I kid you not. One for the master, one for the dame, and one for the (not so) little boy.

Summer, fruits, bats, hornbills, dogs… there’s your ecosystem right there.

1. Showing up, asking questions, and getting lucky

A few weeks ago, reader Kannan Ranganath wrote to me. He offered me (and of course, other readers of First Principles) “a simple recipe for finding opportunities”. His recipe consisted of just three ingredients:

1. Be pleasant
2. Ask questions
3. Engage daily

First Principles readers are by now used to the rule of threes in past editions.

  • Belief. Actions. Conflict.
  • Resisting, accepting, and enjoying “fallow”
  • Curiosity. Belief. Action.

“It’s hard for a warm and pleasant person who is asking a lot of questions and engaging in their industry daily to not come across interesting opportunities,” wrote Kannan. And I don’t think I can disagree with that.

In fact, curiosity came up as the top community value in an earlier edition. But being curious is merely a “necessary but not sufficient” condition to ask questions. Asking good questions is both an art and a muscle.

As a cofounder and the CEO of the U.S. chipmaker Nvidia, Jensen Huang operates in a high-velocity industry requiring agile, innovative thinking. Reflecting on how his leadership style has evolved, he told the New York Times, “I probably give fewer answers and I ask a lot more questions….It’s almost possible now for me to go through a day and do nothing but ask questions.” He continued, “Through probing, I help [my management team]…explore ideas that they didn’t realize needed to be explored.”

The urgency and unpredictability long faced by tech companies have spread to more-mature sectors, elevating inquiry as an essential skill. Advances in AI have caused a seismic shift from a world in which answers were crucial to one in which questions are. The big differentiator is no longer access to information but the ability to craft smart prompts. “As a leader, you don’t have the answers; your workforce [does], your people [do],” Jane Fraser, Citi’s CEO, told Fortune magazine. “That’s completely changed how you have to lead an organization. You have to unleash the creativity….The innovation isn’t happening because there’s a genius at the top of the company that’s coming up with the answers for everything.”

Indeed, leaders have embraced the importance of listening, curiosity, learning, and humility—qualities critical to skillful interrogation. “Question-storming”—brainstorming for questions rather than answers—is now a creativity technique. But unlike lawyers, doctors, and psychologists, business leaders aren’t formally trained on what kinds of questions to ask. They must learn as they go.

The Art of Asking Smarter Questions by Arnaud Chevallier, Frédéric Dalsace, and Jean-Louis Barsoux, Harvard Business Review

I like Kannan’s recipe on increasing luck, as opposed to the “original”. Tech entrepreneur Jason Roberts coined the phrase “surface area of luck” over a decade ago, but in his version “telling” replaces “asking questions” while “doing” replaces “engaging daily”.

View in Browser

The beauty of merely showing up, being pleasant, and asking questions, speaks more to me. Specifically, showing up.

This is an argument for the proposition, also tongue-in-cheek, that 80 percent of success in life is just showing up. It’s hard to catch the opportunities without being there.

[…]

It’s an apparent paradox: The declining significance of place is associated with the rising significance of place. Technology helps us connect with anyone anywhere nearly instantaneously, crowdsource ideas, and work on virtual teams without ever being in the same place. But being in the same place at the right time means being able to make serendipitous connections, and even to get mistaken for someone important. That’s why executives trek up the snowy Swiss mountains to Davos, or why art dealers flock to Art Basel and Art Basel Miami. Furthermore, showing up and being there has an emotional appeal even when it lacks instrumental value. People pay a premium to attend live sports and entertainment that they could get free on TV or the Web.

The First Secret of Success Is Showing Up by Rosabeth Moss Kanter, Harvard Business Review

I’ll end with a poignant June 2023 piece from Andrew Dickson, the founder of hedge fund Albert Bridge Capital. 

It talks about the importance of not just showing up, but staying in the game.

2. What’s in a name?

Hi, everyone. It’s Hari here. I’m going to throw a few random words at you now and I want you to guess what they have in common with each other. Ready? 

Radar. Scuba. Laser. 

If you’ve spotted it, congratulations, you and I both loved quizzes in school. For those of you who didn’t, all three are acronyms. Radar is Radio Detection and Ranging, Scuba is Self-Contained Underwater Breathing Apparatus, and Laser is Light Amplification by Stimulated Emission of Radiation.

Okay, I’m going to throw in another word that fits. Here goes: Faasos. Yes, the same Faasos rolls we’re all familiar with. 

I genuinely thought it was just a catchy name before I started my research on Jaydeep Barman and Rebel Foods. Then I found it was an acronym and spent the rest of my weekend memorising it. Faasos expands to Fanatic Activism Against Substandard Occidental Sh*t. Yeah, the name is a rebellion in itself—against the burgers and pizzas that had taken over Indian taste buds around 2004, which was when Jaydeep Barman and Kallol Banerjee started the very first version of Faasos. It’s now just one brand in Rebel Foods’ pretty wide roster.

We released part 2 of our conversation with Jaydeep Barman, co-founder and CEO of Rebel Foods, this Thursday, and if you tune in, you can listen to Rohin asking Jaydeep if there’s a story behind the name Rebel Foods too. The Faasos tidbit might have given you an idea, but there’s quite a bit more to that story. 

Listen to both part 1 and part 2 to find out.

 

3. What screams “First Principles” to you?

Hi everyone, this is Anushka, and for this edition’s Books section, I don’t have recommendations for you (well, not exactly, anyway) but a question: have you read a book that just screams “First Principles!!!”?

These books may not say they’re explaining the first principles of a certain idea or discipline, but they certainly encourage you to forget everything you know about a subject, suspend your assumptions, and then build a new understanding of it from the ground up. 

And as a reader of the First Principles newsletter from edition 1, I feel like my brain is now pretty swift at identifying books—even podcast episodes, videos, articles, etc.—that make me ask why instead of just telling me what and how and where and when. 

My favourite example (my colleagues are now sick and tired of listening to me prattle on about it) is Samin Nosrat’s Salt, Fat, Acid, Heat.

View in Browser

This is the first sentence of Nosrat’s introduction to the book: “Anyone can cook anything and make it delicious.” 

Nosrat explains that any cooking is basically a combination of these four elements—salt, fat, acid, and heat—and once you understand how they determine taste, you can cook and tweak and invent pretty much anything. It’s truly the first principles of cooking. 

I love books like these because they’re easy and difficult, all at once. Easy, because you’re not expected to know anything about, say, cooking. And difficult, because it forces you to challenge whatever assumption you may have and open yourself to understanding why you follow a certain practice. 

One more super interesting book I’ve been reading that falls into this category is the new and illustrated version of Elements of Style, by William Strunk Jr. and E B White.

Look, this is pretty much an English grammar textbook.

View in Browser

No jokes, Strunk Jr. was an English professor and his book was actually used in classrooms. The absolutely direct orders in this book, like, “omit needless words!” has the sweet nostalgic flavour of a worn out, red-coloured Wren & Martin. But something in Roger Angell’s introduction to the book struck me—while fighting a writer’s block or revising some piece of writing or staring at an absolutely blank page, he says Strunk Jr.’s simplest grammar rules always clears the fog for him. 

I recommend this book even if you’re a seasoned writer—I find that the first principles of English writing have a certain kind of Occam’s Razor: when confused, the simplest rule is often the answer. 

Also, the clever illustrations make reading grammar rules absolutely fun.

View in Browser

If you’re wondering whether you’ve read a book like this, you don’t need to restrict yourself to ones that necessarily teach you a skill or act as a guide. In fact, one of my favourite books that I think certainly falls into this category is A Historical Dictionary of Indian Food, by the revered food scientist K T Achaya. It’s what it sounds like. It’s a simple dictionary of every single term you will use when thinking about Indian food, cooking, and eating habits—in alphabetical order.

View in Browser

The book makes no assumptions, has no narratives, not even a mere joke thrown here or there. It offers, in plain and no uncertain terms, where and how ingredients like the tomato or chilli, or processes like fermenting, or spices like cardamom come from and where they were first eaten in India. Term by term. 

This isn’t a book about the first principles of cooking—Achaya hardly touches upon what you can do with any ingredient or process. It’s the first principles of why Indians eat the way they do. Certainly, reading a 300-page dictionary might be a seemingly mundane way to answer this question, but really, does it get more First Principles than that? 

(Did you know that the drink punch literally comes from the Hindi word ‘panch,’ meaning five?)

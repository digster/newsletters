What India is doing, will do, and should do—to not just survive but thrive in the chaos unleashed by Trump.
Good Morning Ishan,
What does “growth” truly mean for a country? Does it simply mean more money? If so, in whose hands? Does it mean greater accessibility? Again, who is left out?
When a nation makes a decision that affects everyone and the economy, yet glosses over the fine print, that is where journalism must spotlight the asterisk—the real story lost in polished PR-speak.
Make India Competitive Again, launched this spring, is our commitment to bring out the nuance behind the asterisk.
Trumpism was a great wake-up call for many economies, including ours. We saw the urgent need to keep you informed of the rapidly shifting global landscape and India’s reactions to it. But, as you know, our promise evolved. Just like India has grown beyond relying on the US for healthy export numbers.

For a country that has never placed a strong emphasis on research and development, the topic is now at the forefront of everyone’s mind—from business tycoons to central and state governments.
The National Science and Technology Survey, which conducts research to measure the health of R&D in India, had set a deadline of 30 September to collect responses for the 2024–2025 edition. However, it faced a hitch in receiving responses from the private sector. Here’s the latest data we have on the [progress](https://www.thehindu.com/sci-tech/science/science-ministry-survey-on-research-and-development-in-india-gets-weak-response-from-private-sector/article70098344.ece):
As of September, 73% of government R&D institutions had shared their data but only 35% of industry bodies (registered with the Department of Scientific and Industrial Research) and 9% of multinational companies had shared their responses, according to data shared at the forum.
In that same article, Arvind Kumar, the person in charge of the survey, said, “We will likely extend the date till November 30 and publish the results within a month.”
Well, he has had to eat his words. If you check the website now, it shows the deadline has been extended once more, this time to 31 December.
Seema Singh’s detailed analysis of the sorry state of the private-sector’s R&D spending helps explain their delay in filling out the survey.

The Ministry of Electronics and Information Technology (Meity) has its hands full. The draft National Data Centre Policy, released in November 2020, hasn’t yet been implemented, and now it’s due for a fresh update, driven by the need for standard regulations to properly maintain AI data centres.
Meanwhile, approximately $45 billion has been committed in 2025 to building data centres across India, over the next couple of years.
Beyond these promising numbers, however, lie significant hurdles. In an excellent piece, Sumit Chakraborty reveals some of the challenges behind this rosy outlook.
Of the Rs 3,700 crore issue, about Rs 2,500 crore is fresh capital; Rs 465 crore goes to finishing its second Chennai tower, Rs 860 crore to the Navi Mumbai project, and Rs 600 crore to repay debt. That last bit tells you this isn’t a hot tech play so much as a REIT with routers. You borrow, build, lease, and hope the interest-rate cycle is kind.
The numbers bear this out, too. Sify made about Rs 1,400 crore in FY25, with 44% margins. The profit, however, was only Rs 126 crore. That’s the nature of the beast: every megawatt costs Rs 50–60 crore and pays back over 10 years. A 10–15% ROE business if all goes well, and a very expensive way to generate heat if it doesn’t.

On 1 September, the Supreme Court dismissed a public interest litigation (PIL) that challenged the mandatory national rollout of E20 fuel—petrol blended with 20% ethanol. The petition, filed by advocate Akshay Malhotra, had argued that the policy was implemented without giving owners of older vehicles a choice of ethanol-free fuel or clear labelling at fuel pumps.
The core issue is that only vehicles manufactured after April 2023 are formally engineered to be E-20 compliant. This puts older vehicles at a disadvantage, leaving them vulnerable to potential malfunctions that may not be covered under existing warranty or insurance policies.
Suprita Anupam and Jonathan Ananda’s jointly reported story explores the full ramifications of the E20 policy in detail.

We have come a long way since 2002. That was the year the Second National Commission on Labour (NCL) first recommended simplifying India’s complex web of labour laws.
A long-anticipated consolidation began in 2019, when 29 central laws were packaged into four comprehensive Bills. And finally, on 21 November 2025 these landmark codes were officially implemented and are now expected to be fully operational nationwide by 1 April 2026.
This represents a major milestone in the transformation of India’s labour landscape. Yet the debate continues. Does this new framework have what it takes to address the myriad of persistent issues within the ecosystem? Such as the complex question of overtime pay.
The new labour codes, announced last week, simplify India’s labyrinthine labour laws and break them up into four snackable codes around wages, social security, workplace conditions, and industrial relations.
And in the codes on workplace conditions, the labour gods imposed an eight-hour limit a day or a 48-hour limit a week. Even though this was the central government’s decision, it may not have been thrilled about it.
Its real feelings about working hours were on display in the 2024–25 Economic Survey, where it said working-hour restrictions hamper economic growth.
“There are instances where labour regulations designed to protect workers’ rights perhaps inadvertently hinder the growth of firms, especially small and medium enterprises, by restricting their ability to expand operations when needed compared to global peers. In doing so, it dampens employment generation as well,” the survey said.

India’s IPO boom continues unabated. Nephroplus (Nephrocare Health Services) listed on 17 December; the stock remains volatile, but Quadria Capital’s partial exit soon after marked the firm’s fastest value-creation cycle to date.
A key factor in the success of India’s largest dialysis network may be its golden goose: the playbook it built to thrive in the most low-margin market. And now, by modifying this model for new markets, the company is making dialysis profitability at scale a viable goal, both within and outside the country.

The semiconductor space is buzzing. Uttar Pradesh is set to get its first semiconductor plant—approved under India’s Semiconductor Mission—which is expected to reduce import dependency. This month, the Tata Group signed two major deals, one with Intel and another with Japan’s Rohm. Clearly, 2026 is shaping up to be a pivotal year for semiconductors. But are we fully equipped for it?
An earthquake that destroys all of TSMC may not be a very likely worry, but with US President Donald Trump’s foe-now-friend-now style of diplomacy, others aren’t so improbable. As both the US and China have shown in the past year, dominance in supply chains can and have been weaponised.
“People probably didn’t pay any attention to supply chain safety when they globalised. Now that geopolitics has entered into it, they realise what a problem it is,” noted GS Madhusudan, CEO of fabless semiconductor firm Incore Semiconductors.
With $10 billion now “fully committed” to 10 semiconductor projects, and phase two of its Semiconductor Mission soon to be [unveiled](https://telecom.economictimes.indiatimes.com/news/policy/india-semiconductor-mission-secures-10-billion-commitment-phase-two-announcement-imminent/123651135), there is much India can’t afford to ignore.

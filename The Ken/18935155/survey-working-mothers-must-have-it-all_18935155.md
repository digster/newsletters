If you're a working mother, this email is a request for [help](https://theken.typeform.com/to/s33DVon8).
There is a famous natural[ experiment](https://academic.oup.com/qje/article-abstract/123/3/1251/1928174) in development economics. In post-Mao China, women were considered better at picking tea than men. In districts with tea plantations, women came to be breadwinners; livelihood was placed in the hands of women.
The results of this intervention were startling and stunning. Over the next few years, many developmental indicators—education, mortality, and nutrition—improved significantly in the tea-plantation districts. Increasing female income, while holding male income constant, not only improved females survival rates, but also children's education rates.
Conversely, the same experiment found that putting more money in the hands of men didn't have the same effect. In fact, it did the opposite: reduced the survival rates of females without improving the education rates for children.
There's also the fact that throughout human history, women were out hunting and gathering just like men. Researchers tell us that grandmas were the best hunters of all in a village. Our female ancestors, very likely, had sharp hunting weapons they really liked.
So, shouldn’t having more women in the workplace be more natural than we typically think? And isn’t it even more crucial to the development of our society, with deeper, longer-lasting impact than we usually understand?
It is. And still, female participation in the workplace has only fallen since the pandemic. In India, it is 19% against the global average of 37%. At the highest levels of corporate excellence, women are earning 70% less than men at the same level. Women who are mothers suffer a larger pay gap than women who are not.
This is ominous. But this crisis is also understandable. Because being a working mom is very difficult, made more so by maternal bias, workplaces that are struggling to adapt to the needs of working mothers, the burden of expectations placed on them, and the lack of support provided to them. Too many mothers drop out of the workforce or suffer too much for taking time off to fulfill caregiving responsibilities. And many who do neither but continue working suffer far more both emotionally and physically. A majority of our workplaces are not designed for something as crucial to life as motherhood. The penalty of parenthood disproportionately falls on the careers of mothers.
This is why at Cost to Company, The Ken's weekly podcast about work and workplaces, we hope to gain something from building a hive intelligence—a collective of the wisdom of working mothers (those who have been there, done that, or are there and doing it) and of the workplaces where they have thrived (businesses that have facilitated their being there and doing it). What is the new path to working motherhood, and how can we build it? How can workplaces adapt and families contribute to the needs of mothers? What can workplaces do to retain and nurture mothers in ways that make business sense?
We want to crowdsource your answers and build a manifesto. We want to integrate and then distribute this hive intelligence. A podcast episode that brings together the best of how you've cracked working motherhood. And above all, how workplaces can be encouraged to help you do it.
To that end, we've created a survey. [Here it is](https://theken.typeform.com/to/s33DVon8). This form will take approximately twenty minutes. Share it with other moms in your life.
Thank you very much for your time. Thank you for helping us push the needle.

Long and Short
Wed, 27 Aug 25
The story behind the numbers and motivations driving India's stock market.
Good Morning Ishan,
After nearly a decade of pampering the Indian masses with low-priced data plans, India’s largest telecom operator signaled last week that it now means business. Reliance Jio [raised](https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/industry/telecom/telecom-news/pay-more-for-data-as-telcos-phase-out-entry-level-plans/articleshow/123394411.cms?from=mdr) the entry-level plan price to Rs 299 for 1.5 GB per day, for a 28-day validity period.
For the same amount of data over the same period, plans from both Airtel and Vi start at Rs 349. Vi does offer a Rs 299 plan for 1 GB per day, but many believe its days are numbered.
After the July 2024 tariff hike of 10–25% by private telcos, this marks a significant turning point in Indian telecom. Most analysts—both buy-side and sell-side—are factoring in another 10–12% tariff hike by year-end into their forecast models.
Welcome to a mature market’s way of progressively linking prices to increased usage.
The fun-sized plans that famously “democratised” data for Indian users when Jio launched in late 2016 will now enter the history books. About 30–35% of Jio’s 498 million subscribers (as of June) and 18–20% of Airtel’s 364 million subscribers will be affected.
Telcos call this “tariff repair”. Regulators call it “forbearance” (an informal understanding among telcos to avoid aggressive competition). Subscribers, who have grown accustomed to inexpensive data and endless—dare I say, sometimes mindless—scrolling on their phones, will probably call it a reality check.
Make no mistake, India still has one of the lowest telecom tariffs in the world. But with this move, its entry-level pricing is no longer the lowest.
It’s notable that while most other sectors are relying on premiumisation to derive value—check out Seetharaman’s [interview](https://the-ken.com/story/godrej-consumers-sudhir-sitapati-on-selling-to-the-top-20-of-india-during-a-consumption-slump/?utm_source=web&utm_medium=homepage&utm_campaign=con_3_unit_1) with Godrej Consumer Products’ Sudhir Sitapati on Tuesday—Jio is now focusing on the mass market, which it has spent billions to dominate.
India is a value market, not a low-price market. And as a seasoned telecom executive told me, customers “suffer from churn inertia, so telcos take advantage of that”.
Some 150 million Jio customers are likely to be impacted by the entry-level plan pricing. Even with this modest Rs 50 increase, Jio could add Rs 750 crore to its revenue every month. That makes a neat Rs 9,000 crore addition in a year.
At the end of the June quarter, Jio’s Arpu stood at Rs 210, against Airtel’s Rs 250 and Vi’s Rs 177. While the Rs 300 mark is certainly the desired goal, getting there won’t be swift because Indian mobile tariffs are now in line with those of global emerging markets.
In its July 2025 report, analysts at UBS Research wrote that since prices at the low end are now higher than in emerging markets, this could result in “operators limiting price increases at the lower-end plans”. “Going forward,” they argued, “we believe the market has limited ability to absorb further large tariff hikes.” They estimate that beyond FY26, Arpu growth will largely be driven by minor price increases and organic drivers such as 2G to 4G upgrades, prepaid to postpaid migration, and so on.
For now, the entry-level price movement is glad tidings for Jio Platforms, which has been pushing out the date of its public listing every few months. Since [80% of Jio Platforms’](https://brandequity.economictimes.indiatimes.com/news/digital/jio-platforms-hits-pause-on-listing-plans-this-year/122358595?frmsrc=amp_news) $17.6 billion revenue in FY25 came from the telecom business, the latter must [look](https://www.reuters.com/world/india/ambanis-reliance-jio-delays-india-ipo-2025-listing-not-cards-sources-say-2025-07-09/) rock solid and steady before the Reliance group goes to the bourses seeking a valuation north of $100 billion.
Rival Bharti Airtel is done nursing its price-war wounds. At nearly $132 billion in market cap, it’s the third-most valuable company in India after Reliance Industries and HDFC Bank. With its price-to-earnings ratio higher than six other companies in the top seven, investors are already paying a premium for the stock, anticipating strong future growth.
Since Jio Infocomm has been opaque in sharing its revenue breakup, Airtel’s business can be taken as a proxy. There, two trends are visible that indicate why Jio will lead the next headline tariff raise—just as it has now done by discontinuing the entry-level 1 GB per day plan.
Despite its diversified nature, India’s mobile business remains the key segment for Indian telcos. It contributes 60% to Bharti’s revenue, which grew 22% year-on-year in the June quarter. The number two telco, just like Jio, continues to add more homes to its services and reported a healthy 26% growth in this period. However, the Arpu from this segment declined for the fourth consecutive quarter. The enterprise business isn’t going gangbusters either, reporting an 8% year-on-year decline in revenues.
It’s fair to assume the trajectory will not be very different for Jio. In fact, competitors and industry watchers are eagerly awaiting the breakdown of numbers that Jio Platforms will eventually share when it files its IPO papers.
In the run-up to that eventful day, the affordability landscape of mobile internet in India will change forever. Telcos will, of course, have a more sustainable revenue stream. Subscribers, left with reduced choices in the market and a higher monthly outgo, will hopefully demand better service and innovation.

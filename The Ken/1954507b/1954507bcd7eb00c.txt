Do you ever wonder what would happen if the sun didn’t rise tomorrow? Or if gravity suddenly ceased to exist? Or, horror of horrors, if the Unified Payments Interface (UPI) stopped being free? 

We take a lot of things for granted, but the way things are shaping up, a fee-free UPI is one of those things on increasingly precarious ground. 

Of course, there’s been no change in the government’s mandate on zero merchant discount rates for UPI. But, something happened this Union Budget that shook things up, sending payments companies such as Paytm*, Google Pay, and Phonepe scurrying to find answers. 

What do you do when you can’t charge a fee for transactions, but the doles that make up for the costs are steadily cut down?

Squeezed and scared

Payment companies cannot dare to attach a fee on UPI payments made by customers. That would mean voluntarily jumping on an axe, barefoot. Indians are now so used to free peer-to-peer and peer-to-merchant UPI payments that charging even the smallest fee might mean customers move to a competitor. 

For the last few years, payments firms have been able to make up for zero-fee UPI transactions because the Union Government, since January 2023, has offered incentives to promote Rupay debit cards and small-ticket UPI payments. These incentives stood at Rs 2,600 crore the first year but have since been steadily decreased—to Rs 2,000 crore in 2024, and just Rs 437 crore in the Union Budget this February. 

That last cut has certainly set loose the cat among the pigeons. 

“There’s surely a bit of worry among payments companies across the board,” a senior Paytm executive based out of Noida told me. 

So where do you turn to protect your already wafer-thin margins? 

Well, fees, of course. Just not UPI fees. 

Last week, Google Pay introduced a 0.5–1% convenience fee for certain payments made using credit and debit cards, including bill payments for electricity, cooking gas, and broadband. These were all previously free for small-ticket transactions. Over the last few months, Phonepe and Paytm have also imposed similar charges on certain kinds of payments that were free of charge earlier. 

“Such fees will become more common as time passes because the industry as a whole has been surviving on paper-thin margins. For instance, the zero MDR meant the entire chain was being oiled by incentives. Multiple low-value transactions means more processing and thus higher costs. So, it’s natural that companies are moving towards recovering that cost through different mediums.”

Senior Paytm Executive

But convenience fees cannot be the only way, according to another industry veteran. “Slowly you will see payment companies pushing harder for other businesses like ticket booking, insurance… wherever there are higher margins to make up for UPI. And UPI users will be directed towards these businesses more aggressively.” Though, he added, fees for such services will “always have to remain competitive to ensure they don’t lose customers.”

All three payments giants—Phonepe, Google Pay, and Paytm, in order of market share—already offer insurance products, lending services, e-commerce integrations, ticket-booking services, and bill payments, among other things, as part of their product suites. 

Both executives confirmed to me that payments companies have already approached the Payments Council of India and the finance ministry with their concerns. But the gears of bureaucracy and policy move slowly, if they ever do, and nothing has come of it so far. Neither hold much hope that anything ever will. 

“Historically, fintechs have had to figure out their own solutions for sudden government moves that shake their businesses. This time, too, that’ll be the case.”

So, more fees, on more things. We may also see Google Pay and others explore new offerings like premium services or enhanced features such as faster processing for users willing to pay a service fee. 

Whatever the future holds, though, I think it’ll just be yet another lesson that nothing is ever ‘free’.

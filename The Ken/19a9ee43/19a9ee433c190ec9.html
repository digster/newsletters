Over the last few months, Indian fintech is getting the AI glow-up.

Different firms are at different stages of implementation. But mind you, very few of the AI use cases particularly blow your mind.

As the CTO at a fintech company says, “I think in sectors like healthcare, we have seen far more exciting use cases than in financial services, which does not go beyond recommendations and portfolio analysis.”

Now, both the stock market and banking regulators are doing their own research into how to regulate the use of AI. Sebi in June had a consultation paper inviting comments on responsible use of AI. The RBI, too, in August conducted a survey among 600 financial entities on their use of AI and laid out an exhaustive set of 26 early recommendations on AI implementation.

All things considered, the choice that different fintechs make has a lot to do with their risk appetite—and right now, they are spread across five stages.

Stage one: the fence sitters

These are the low risk takers. These companies have way more to lose than to gain by integrating AI into their consumer-facing systems. A clear instance of this is when India’s largest UPI-payments app Phonepe partnered with OpenAI this month to bring ChatGPT to Phonepe users. Essentially, OpenAI hopes it can get Phonepe’s 600 million users to use ChatGPT.

If you read the announcement around it, it sounds like a big deal. “This partnership will demonstrate the immense value of consumer AI across India, helping millions of users enhance their daily lives.”

But essentially, it’s a way for Phonepe to make some ad revenue off of OpenAI. I’d reckon it’ll take a lot for Phonepe to wade into the stickier territory of integrating AI into its customer-facing interface, or experimenting with any swashbuckling new feature that will warn you against making a payment to a friend who never pays you back.

Stage two: the shadow deployers

This is where most companies are at now, and it’s not unique to fintech. Companies are using foundational models or AI tools to tune different parts of their operations—from building better cybersecurity features to getting their engineers to accelerate development cycles. About 47% of Indian companies have reported moving their generative AI pilots into live, functioning use cases. And about 23% are currently experimenting with these products, according to a recent EY report.

These use cases are quiet, hum steadily beneath the hardware, surfacing either to show exceptional performance or fail miserably. And with this, you don’t know what kind of risk you are signing up for.

Stage three: see-sawing risk takers

This is where I anticipate most fintechs will be as they start rolling out AI features that customers will directly encounter. Like customer support.

RBI’s survey of AI use cases from financial entities shows that 15% of all AI use cases it surveyed were in customer support. It may seem like low risk, but can quickly escalate to riskier frontiers if not implemented well.

I’d put Cred’s implementation at this stage. It partnered with OpenAI about three months ago to offer customer support to its 15 million members and create “premium, concierge-like experiences at scale.” Right now, the app’s support section only accepts questions related to credit-card bill payments.

In an Open AI blog post, Cred’s chief technology officer said that, in the three months since the launch, the fintech has seen a 14 percentage point improvement in its customer satisfaction scores, and has reached a 98% resolution accuracy rate. Sure, it’s early days now, but when I tried asking the customer-support chatbot questions regarding my credit-card bill payments, the results were a tad under whelming.

Not only that, as a user, one would never know that the answers are powered by AI. No disclaimer, no warning. Nothing. And that’s problematic.

“AI is not deterministic, and that’s why it is a black box. If it says something weird, we won’t have any idea why it behaved that way. That’s the nature of this tech. So it’s important to disclose,” says the CTO of a fintech company.

That’s why even the RBI, in its early framework, recommends that consumers be explicitly informed whenever they are interacting with AI systems. Not just that, it says they should always have the option to switch to human representatives when they want.

Stage four: the radicals

These types of companies want to press for great change, and potentially, take high risks. I’d put Razorpay, National Payments Corporation of India (NPCI), OpenAI, and Bigbasket in this category. At the Global Fintech Fest in October, these entities made a splash by showing the world what agentic payments in India would look like.

Upload your shopping list on ChatGPT, and, without once leaving the chat window, finish a transaction on Bigbasket—all thanks to prior permissions given on UPI via nothing more than a conversation.

It’s stuff of dreams (or nightmare depending on who you ask).

Though only a pilot, this is one implementation I’d bet can’t get off the ground without the regulator signing off on it, and businesses—from Bigbasket to Phonepe—sweating over it. After all, you never once have to visit any of these apps to make a purchase.

In fact, I think this pilot wouldn’t have gotten off the ground if it weren’t for NPCI’s involvement.

Stage five: the conservatives

This is little risk, mostly because few users would take to it. India’s largest stockbroker Zerodha took the road least travelled by—by letting its customers use its services via AI. It opened its APIs as Model Context Protocol (MCP).

To put it very simply, MCP defines a standardised, secure way for an AI assistant to communicate with external data sources like, say, their Zerodha trading account.

Which means if any AI model offers MCP support, like Anthropics’s Claude, users can connect their Zerodha account to it and query it with questions like “What stocks do I hold?” or “Compare HDFC and ICICI returns over the past year.”

This ends up as a nifty, efficient assistant as it can have a 360-degree view of all the services you subscribe to. It offers full control to the user, and courtesy of its openness and connectivity, can end up as a safe option too.

But the flipside is that it’s not for everyone. Only reasonably tech-savvy folk with some semblance of critical thinking will be able to derive meaning from it. That’s why of the 17 million clients Zerodha has, no more than a few thousand use this feature.

These five phases are only the start of many more. Do you know of other phases of AI deployment among financial-services firms? Where would you place them in the risk spectrum? Tell us.

Dear manager, I've been working here for so long
I've got something I want to tell you, so I thought I would say it in a song
F*** this I quit, f*** this place I quit
I don't want to work for someone who treats their employees like s***
That's the expletive-laden song a Starbucks barista crooned in a cafe in Toronto before leaving. The video went viral recently and made for a good laugh.
But back in India, no one is laughing. The economic slowdown has slammed major industries—automobiles, retail and even the once-booming IT industry. And with that has come the inevitable. Pink slips. Thousands upon thousands of them.
One might argue this culling is an annual ritual for the Indian IT majors as they make room for fresh engineering graduates. But this time it's a little different. Not only are the numbers enormous—Infosys and Cognizant are cutting 15,000 jobs—but they also affect one particular section: the mid-level employees.
"Pyramid actions" is what Cognizant's CEO calls it.
The Indian IT industry rode the outsourcing boom since the turn of the millennium. Getting a job was easy back then, and promotions mandatory. Else an employee would just up and go to the next company, likely just down the road.
But while growth inevitably slowed, promotions didn't.
Over the years, the organisational structure morphed from a pyramid to a diamond. It bulged in the middle with thousands of mid-level employees. And at Infosys, as of 2019, mid-level employees actually exceeded associates.
As Sharath, co-writer of today's story puts it: this middle layer was necessary. Like boosters on a rocket, to be ejected after liftoff. These managers helped propel the industry into a perfect takeoff.
Sharath should know. He was part of the industry in an earlier avatar. Today, he and Shreedhar write about how the IT industry got here, with the blame moving from the companies, to technology, to clients and even to employees.

Good Morning Ishan,
 
By any account, this is no time to talk about a new telecom service. The industry is hit by a meteorite almost every other day. If it’s not the courts or the government or the telcos’ own financial results, a rival rejoinder whips up the sentiment. But as they say, time and (tech) tide wait for none.
 
Samsung isn’t waiting for anyone either. Having set up India’s most advanced 4G network for Reliance Jio, the Korean company is on a high. It’s now making presentations to different government agencies to open up a new business opportunity in setting up a pan-India emergency response or public safety broadband network.
 
A network that rescuers in times of a flood, fire, riot or any public disaster could tap into to make unhindered calls, send messages and share video footage to improve the rescue efforts.
 
Samsung has implemented such a network in Korea (SafeNet), and in the US (FirstNet) with AT&T. So it clearly sees itself as the natural first choice for the Indian government in setting it up here. The telecom regulator, Trai, has a position on this. It has recommended allocating two 10MHz blocks of spectrum in the 800MHz band for free to the telco(s) implementing the network.
 
In the US, AT&T, which has more than 9000 agencies as subscribers of FirstNet, operates on a revenue-sharing model. The free spectrum is also used to run commercial services, and the earnings are shared with the government. On the face of it, this service opens up a good business case for telcos in India because so far all agencies operate their own network.
 
The telecom departments’ Digital Communications Commission is expected to meet next month to chart the course of action.
 
But like everything in Indian telecom, this is ridden with contradictions. And requires investment. By one estimate, and Pratap, the writer of today’s story, has gotten a few, it’d require Rs 70,000 crore over 7-10 years. That’s a lot of money for the beleaguered telcos. Could the government make the initial payment?

The food delivery business in India is officially a duopoly. Uber Eats, the #3 player sold itself to Zomato, the #2 player. In return, it got 10% of Zomato’s stock and a position towards the bottom of Zomato’s investor cap table. 
 
But as the adage goes, great companies are bought, not sold. It's no secret that Uber had been trying desperately to sell Uber Eats in India for well over a year. After entering the food delivery business in India way too late in 2017, Uber tried to make it up by burning cash to fund discounts for customers and restaurants alike. From $100M a quarter in early 2019, its burn rate is reported to have increased to $300M in the last five months.
 
There was no way a public Uber under a CEO whose last name wasn’t Kalanick could stomach that for an extended period.
 
Especially because while India contributed only 3% of global orders, it accounted for a quarter of the global Uber’s EBITDA losses for food delivery.
 
Uber Eats had to be sold. That part is clear.
 
But why did Zomato buy it? What does it even get? Discount-seeking customers who know no loyalty? Restaurants who have no obligation to continue? Delivery personnel who were never really exclusive to Uber Eats?
 
Uber on its investor cap table?
 
Or just a closing of the perceived gap with market leader Swiggy, and duopoly dibs on a food delivery market estimated to touch $12 billion by 2023?

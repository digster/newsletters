How often do you order groceries from an app? (My answer is: “too often to admit.”)
And when you do, does it really make a difference to you if they deliver in 10 minutes? Or 20? Or 30? (I’m guilty of usually picking the 8-10 minute delivery option.)
Actually, here’s one more: would you download a new app simply because it promises you 10-minute grocery delivery?
I floated the same questions to my colleagues at The Ken’s Bengaluru newsroom, and most of us agreed that 10-minute delivery doesn’t really matter to us. Many of them were dismissive, almost, pointing out that there was no problem waiting a few hours or even a day for a monthly grocery order. And then, there are folks who just buy their groceries from local shops and vegetable carts. That’s how it’s always been.
A lot of people, it’s clear, don’t mind if their grocery deliveries aren’t lightning-quick (you might be one of them). In fact, you may have noticed that after the pandemic, many have simply gone back to walking to their local grocery stores.
My parents, who live in a tier-II city, were appalled when I ordered a packet of curd back home and sat in the living room, waiting for it.
“Just go pick it up!” they said.
It wasn’t very long ago that the quick delivery model was the newest, coolest kid on the block. Companies scrambled to open dark stories, increase efficiency, and advertise their 10-minute delivery offerings.
But increasingly, those same companies have been silently scaling back. Jiomart has shut down its rapid delivery service, Express. Both Reliance-backed Dunzo and Zomato’s Blinkit have closed many dark stories. Swiggy is considering scaling down its investments into Instamart.
In fact, it’s something Dunzo CEO Kabeer Biswas spoke about on an earlier episode of the First Principles podcast. For India, he says, what you need is “quick-enough commerce”, not quick commerce.

Good Morning Ishan,
Food or fitness?
One needs hunger, the other requires willpower.
Food is an individual choice, yet it has mass appeal; fitness is a personal goal, and it is hardly contagious.
Can the two go together? That was the idea when Mukesh Bansal founded Cure.fit nine years ago with a mouthful of brand names under its umbrella. Very quickly, and especially during the pandemic, it became clear that food and fitness may be related at some level, but as businesses, they are like chalk and cheese.
Soon after, Curefoods spun out of the parent and went from Rs 2 crore in revenue in FY21 to Rs 775 crore in FY25. It has filed for an IPO, but Cult.fit—which has raised more, spent more, and built a flashier and stronger brand—is still getting in shape to go public.
Both businesses have scaled through acquisitions, but one shows execution chops, the other experiments with ideas. If Ankit Nagori is the hands-on guy mixing quinoa and Krispy Kreme in his profitability and scale-up recipe, Bansal, the ideas man telling good stories, seems distracted in his choices.
How did Nagori beat Bansal to the IPO finish line? That’s a [breezy read](https://the-ken.com/story/ankit-nagoris-kitchen-math-beats-mukesh-bansals-fitness-formula-in-the-ipo-race/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter) from Suprita today.
Jio Blackrock Asset Management, the latest addition to India’s mutual-fund block, raised Rs 17,800 crore ($2.1 billion) from just its first three launches. In terms of assets managed, the fund house is now the 29th largest in India. Like Jio Blackrock, a lot of newer fund houses are chasing the pot of gold in India’s MF industry. And many more are awaiting Sebi’s nod to start selling mutual-fund schemes. In this week’s Long and Short, Anand argues why it will be an uphill climb for them, even to cover half the distance as their larger, older peers.

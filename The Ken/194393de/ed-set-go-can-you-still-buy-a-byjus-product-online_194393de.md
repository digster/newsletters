Good Morning Ishan,
Welcome to the very first edition of Ed Set Go in 2025! I’m excited to get stuck into all the exciting developments from the world of education. Here’s hoping that 2025 is the year that Indian edtech regains its mojo and gives us new ideas to explore.
Now, it’s freezing in DC. So naturally, I’ve been spending a lot of time reading indoors. I came across a succinct and enjoyable [summary](https://inc42.com/features/indias-edtech-reset-the-aftermath-of-the-golden-age/) by Inc42 of the year gone by in edtech. I loved the “where are they now” section—a rundown on what once-popular edtech founders are doing with their lives. Some have taken early retirement and adopted the role of “mentors” to other startups; others have picked up an entirely new career as author-yogis, while some others stick around… hoping for the lull in their fortunes to finally end.
If the founders have moved on, or gone into semi-hiberation as they wait to get bought out, what happened to the products and services they built? Do they die out completely when their parent company runs out of steam? I’d imagine that, online, it would be harder to wipe out traces of a product than if you just stopped selling a book or an offline course.
Turns out, edtech products and services sold online can slip into the nebulous space of existing. And not existing.
Surprise surprise, it’s still pretty easy to buy a Byju’s early-learning product online. Early learning refers to the category that Byju’s started way back in 2018, when it inked a deal with Disney to use its cartoons to teach basic reading and writing skills. The deal never did sit well within the company. Disney’s restrictions on the use of their copyrighted characters made it difficult for Byju’s to come up with a truly interactive app for kids. You can read about the history of this deal [here](https://the-ken.com/story/byjus-disney-k3-app-stumbles/).
The app then pivoted to selling WhiteHat Jr, or Byju’s Future School, to international audiences. The appeal of online classes worked for a while during the pandemic, but the promise soon fizzled out, as did Byju’s prospects in the US.
But even as Byju’s defends against legal challenges in two different geographies—and Byju Raveendran tries to run his [broken empire](https://www.fortuneindia.com/enterprise/byjus-net-worth-is-zero-confesses-founder-byju-raveendran/118847) from Dubai—these “early learning” Byju’s kits are still floating around the corners of online shopping websites.

The kit is a combination of the content that Byju’s created—with and without Disney’s characters—and the technology it acquired via OSMO, the US-based edtech company it acquired in 2019.
How this kit works, or whether it works, is the real question. Customers reviewing the product seem to have dropped hundreds of dollars on these kits, only to find there’s no real company behind them.

The common complaints are that the app doesn’t work in tandem with the workbooks in the kit, like it’s supposed to. There is no customer service number to call, no one to send emails to, and “no real company exists” anymore to service any customers. Behind the glitzy opening screen of the app, Byju’s appears like a mirage masquerading—still—as a company.
This made me think: are there other ghost edtech products out there floating about? Are people still buying them? Is there still an unfortunate parent out there looking for a refund from a company no longer willing, or able, to return their money?
There’s a story here about trust. Which we will get to subsequently, but that’s it for this week.

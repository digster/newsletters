Is the end nigh or the hype high? It depends on whom you ask. Claude Cowork’s new automation tools—from AI biggie Anthropic—have been hammering software stocks, and bringing out doomsday “SaaSpocalypse” predictors. “This too shall pass,” hope the big bosses of IT companies, with some calling the sell-off a “clear overreaction” and pointing to AI-led opportunities.

Overreaction or not, what’s clear is that the AI march has gathered unstoppable momentum—and everyone wants in. The India AI Impact Summit in Delhi next week, touted to be among the largest so far globally, will be closely watched for what it holds in store for the country’s AI startups in this brave, new world.

The ground reality, as Inderpal found out with extensive reporting, is far more nuanced than breathless news cycles suggest. Sure, AI is now hot and happening in India too, even if the game kicked off late. The money pumped in is growing big and fast, with fatter cheques being written by venture capitalists like Accel and Antler.

But dig deeper, and a narrowing becomes apparent. Most AI startups in India, it turns out, don’t qualify for funding. Investors are becoming increasingly picky about who they back and how. Let’s just say, the bar is now much higher for what counts as a “fundable AI startup” in India. Just building features won’t cut it anymore. In turn, the moneybags, too, are being held to higher standards.

The shifting sands, hard questions, and interventions will all have far-reaching implications for India’s AI trajectory. Today’s story tells you how.

The “Excel” Moat:
You make a compelling case for Google’s sheer scale—you can’t ignore 3 billion Android users or the 90% search dominance. However, there is a massive distinction between consumer reach and enterprise entrenchment.
While Google is a formidable contender, Microsoft isn’t just a “vendor” to the Fortune 500; it is the operating system of business. Here’s why that mountain is steeper than it looks:
• The Excel Hegemony: It’s often joked that the global financial system is just three Excel spreadsheets held together by duct tape, but it’s barely a joke. Estimates suggest there are between 750 million to 1.1 billion Excel users worldwide. For a Fortune 500 company, moving away from Excel isn’t just a software swap; it’s a lobotomy of their legacy data, macros, and institutional logic.
• The Fortune 500 Stranglehold: Over 95% of the Fortune 500 already use Microsoft Azure and the Microsoft 365 stack. When it comes to AI, procurement departments prefer the “path of least resistance.” Adding Copilot to an existing enterprise agreement is a one-click decision; migrating an entire workforce to Google Workspace is a multi-year cultural war.
• The “PowerPoint” Culture: Companies don’t just “use” these tools; they think in them. Strategy is built in slides, and execution is tracked in sheets.
Agentic AI (AI that can actually do the work) will eventually disrupt this, but we are currently in the “Copilot” era—where the AI needs to live inside the ribbon of the software people already spend 8 hours a day using.
The Bottom Line: Google has the “eyeballs,” but Microsoft has the “workflows.” Google can win the race for the most AI users, but Microsoft is currently winning the race for the most integrated AI. Microsoft will remain the incumbent to beat for some time .

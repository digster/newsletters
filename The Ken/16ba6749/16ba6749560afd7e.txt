Good Morning Ishan,

Everyone needs a place to retreat. When the world gets overwhelming, you need a safe place you can slink back to, hit reset and reevaluate priorities. Be it under the covers of your bed or under a canopy of trees. Every six months, my husband and I like to beat a hasty retreat to a little mountain-district called Gudalur, nestled away in the foothills of the Nilgiris. Just off the Mudumalai Tiger Reserve and a six-hour drive from Bangalore. 

Being a creature of habit, though, I pretty much spend my time in Gudalur doing what I do in Bangalore. I caught on all the tweeting in the morning—but from real birds, not the social media garden-variety who want to have the last word. Binge-watched the show that the fireflies decided to put on, turning on and off like Christmas lights. And the survival skill of timing that I picked up in Bangalore came in handy here, too. Except here, you learn to leave at a certain time to avoid running into elephants. I'll take that over running into traffic, any day.

In some sense, I like to think of our stories, too, as a form of sanctuary—a place where you come to tune out all the chaos and information hunting you down both online and offline. A space where you are left with what truly matters. So here is our weekly sanctuary of stories: 

We opened Monday looking at LinkedIn’s future in India https://the-ken.com/story/india-is-testing-linkedins-talent/?utm_source=recap&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter. Seema and Vandana looked at why the world’s largest professional social network, despite having all the stars aligned in India—5 million joining the workforce every year, a skills gap, an abundance of educational institutions—just can’t seem to shake off competitors and get ahead. If that wasn’t enough, it's also in the midst of an identity crisis.

When someone does something way ahead of their time, they're either a visionary or a madman. And Madhukar Gangadi, the founder of offline pharmacy chain MedPlus, has been called both. MedPlus is India’s first omnichannel pharmacy retailer with 1700 stores, and now every pharmacy wants to pull a MedPlus. Can Gangadi's maverick instincts https://the-ken.com/story/medplus/?utm_source=recap&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter keep MedPlus ahead of the chasing pack.

On Wednesday, Rohin and I looked at Facebook’s grandiose announcement—the launch of its wannabe-cryptocurrency, Libra. With strong overtones of the same strategy it used for its Internet-for-all project Free Basics, Facebook’s ambitions for digital payments using Libra collides https://the-ken.com/story/its-zuckerberg-vs-modi-in-a-digital-payments-battle-in-india/?utm_source=recap&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter with India’s own vision for mobile-based digital payments using UPI. 

Then we had Roshni and Olina’s story on Thursday, which was all about plastics, food, convenience, and collateral damage https://the-ken.com/story/food-delivery-plastic-problem/?utm_source=recap&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter. Ordering in through Swiggy and Zomato is resulting in tonnes of food container and cutlery waste clogging India's landfills. Even though the foodtech behemoths are aware of how their actions are hurting the environment, there is little they can do without a real alternative to plastic. This was our free read of the week, so do share widely. 

On Friday, Rozelle looked at how retail chain Godrej Nature’s Basket (GNB) went from premium to mass market to sold https://the-ken.com/story/godrej-natures-basket-and-the-perils-of-premium-mediocre/?utm_source=recap&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter. In 2005, GNB began life as a gourmet food retailer with stores in the poshest of neighbourhoods. By 2017, in a 180-turn, it became a neighbourhood store selling everyday fresh food. And by 2019, the floundering retail chain was sold to Spencer’s Retail. That, too, at a price not much higher than its revenue in 2019.

We ended the week with a story from Suraksha on the anti-rabies vaccine shortage https://the-ken.com/story/how-india-landed-rabies-vaccine-doghouse/?utm_source=recap&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter currently plaguing India. There is a whopping 80% shortfall of ARV in the country. Supply-constrained manufacturers, a pricing issue, and the government’s manner of procuring vaccines are all responsible for the grave problem at hand. The government’s reaction to this is to ban the export of the vaccine. Will that solve the problem, though? This is the second of our free reads for the week; you know the drill—share away.

We will be back next week with more. Meanwhile, make the most of your weekend with these reads.

Earlier this month, India managed a surprising first—it became home to the world's largest solar power producer.
What's perhaps more surprising is who this home-grown solar giant is—the Adani Group, or more specifically, Adani Green Energy Ltd (AGEL).
For a company that's long been synonymous with the environmentally catastrophic Carmichael coal mine in Australia, it seems almost implausible that the Adani Group could also be a torchbearer for clean energy. Yet, in the redemption arc of the century, here we are. Who knew 2020 could still throw up surprises, huh?
AGEL's rise has been swift. It has gone from inception to world-leader in just five short years. And on Tuesday, it became the first Adani company to cross Rs 1 trillion ($13.6 billion) in market capitalisation. AGEL's surge mirrors Prime Minister Narendra Modi’s aggressive push to tap the vast potential of the sun, which has seen India's installed solar capacity grow 9X in the same timeframe.
But even as India's solar power production swells, the tech that underpins it remains Chinese. Around 80% of the cells and modules in India's solar revolution come from China. This is something the Indian government has unsuccessfully tried to tackle through tariffs for some years now. In the wake of geopolitical tensions with Beijing, there's even greater urgency about breaking this dependence.
The deal that vaulted Adani to #1 went some way to addressing this reliance. The solar power production tender was tied to an agreement to also undertake large-scale solar cell and module manufacturing.
Problem solved? Hardly. Manufacturing solar cells is relatively far down the solar value stream. The components, such as silicon wafers, still come from China. Indeed, one could say the solar value stream begins in China.

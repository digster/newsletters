What makes you human? Certain aspects of human skill and endeavour may become simultaneously rarer and more valuable in a world where most things are machine-generated

Dear Ishan, welcome to another edition of First Principles, filed on a hot, still, and sunny Saturday in Bengaluru.

Remember our discussion a few weeks ago about how fleeting our spring seasons were becoming? Scientists studying India’s seasons (all the way back from 1970) are noting the gradual disappearance of Spring https://www.thehindu.com/sci-tech/energy-and-environment/study-reports-evidence-of-missing-spring-in-india/article67965479.ece too. 🙁

But we can enjoy summer, can’t we? I’m thrilled to report that the White Water Apple in our (tiny) backyard has flowered prodigiously this year. I expect a bumper harvest of at least 500+ fruits. Alas, it’s a fruit cursed with three key features—it bruises easily; is best eaten the same day it’s picked (very high water content); and is only mildly sweet.

I hope to share some pictures of its fruiting in April with you all. But let’s begin with a question: what makes us human?

I’ve been spending a lot of time thinking about the word “human” and what it means of late. A big reason is the dramatic evolution and adoption of Gen AI in virtually all aspects of our life. And regardless of what its proponents say, the end goal is to use AI to progressively replace everything that human beings do.

Buying and selling stocks. Making art. Diagnosing and treating illnesses. Driving vehicles. Analyzing news events. Designing websites. Writing news stories.

Thus, my question about what makes us human.

My (yet evolving) hypothesis is that certain aspects of human skill and endeavour will become simultaneously rarer and more valuable in a world where most things will be machine-generated.

For instance, in this Rolling Stone profile https://www.rollingstone.com/music/music-features/suno-ai-chatgpt-for-music-1234982307/ of Gen AI music startup Suno, I came across this ridiculous assertion from one of its founders.

“Suno appears to be cracking the code to AI music, and its founders’ ambitions are nearly limitless — they imagine a world of wildly democratized music making. The most vocal of the co-founders, Mikey Shulman, a boyishly charming, backpack-toting 37-year-old with a Harvard Ph.D. in physics, envisions a billion people worldwide paying 10 bucks a month to create songs with Suno. The fact that music listeners so vastly outnumber music-makers at the moment is “so lopsided,” he argues, seeing Suno as poised to fix that perceived imbalance.”

Let’s for a minute assume he is right. A billion people are generating, consuming, and remixing AI-generated regurgitations.

What does that mean for the value of truly human-generated music? Not just popular greats like, say, The Beatles or Taylor Swift, but truly talented musicians who might be less famous?

There is no easy answer. I was discussing a version of this quandary this Friday with Amod Malviya, the former CTO of Flipkart and co-founder of Udaan. Amod has always struck me as an extremely principled and thoughtful entrepreneur and person. In 2015, he was one of the rare business leaders who publicly supported https://www.livemint.com/Companies/1J4CaeGnXvKCbwvWW76J6H/Our-Internet-is-headed-in-the-right-direction-Amod-Malviya.html India’s Net Neutrality laws.

Amod loves learning and teaching and is currently doing both (around engineering and technology). As both dads to teenagers, we’re also constantly trying to imagine how best to equip our kids for the future when all our traditional notions of career success (“learn coding”, “clear competitive exams”) are rapidly collapsing.

What skills do you teach college students today knowing the next 10 years are going to be a period of chaotic technological and professional evolution? What do you teach kids in schools?

Our consensus veered towards thinking and mindsets that would make humans more resilient, valuable, and innovative than machines.

We left with a promise to explore the concepts deeper at a later date.

Earlier this week, I also met Chetan Maini, the founder of the contrarian electric vehicle company, Sun Mobility. While almost everyone else is focusing on building better electric vehicles and chargers, Sun Mobility is betting on battery swapping!

Interestingly, Chetan arrived at his contrarian decision to not build an EV company himself after having launched India’s first EV all the way back in… 2001! The Reva was ahead of its time, as Chetan too agrees.

As someone who started making cars that ran on alternative power sources in college (in 1990, he was part of a team that won third place in the famously gruelling cross-Australia World Solar Challenge), Chetan has been tinkering and building for nearly 35 years now!

Remember that cliché, “find your passion and you’ll never work a day in your life”? Well, here’s a photo of Chetan from 34 years ago next to his.

Of course, I did ask Chetan the secret to his “longevity” in the electric vehicles space. You can listen to his answers to that and more in an upcoming episode of the First Principles podcast.

Meeting talented, driven, and thinking people is something I feel is inherently still human. There is something about an unguided or unscripted conversation or discussion between people that is unpredictable, satisfying, and quite often, very productive.

For instance, what is the future of engineering (not merely programming) as a discipline and career opportunity if we look at the next 10 years? What aspects of it will be relegated to (or taken over by) Gen AI-related methods and tools? What kind of skills will become deeply valuable (please don’t even think “prompt engineering”!)? What might engineering innovation look like in 10 years? What might differentiate and cause, say, a 14-year-old today to become a truly great 24-year-old engineer in 2034?

I am not sure articles or research papers or surveys might be the best way to think about such issues. A better way would be, in my opinion, to lean into our “humanness”. By talking about it, by hypothesising, by discussing, and by arguing.

If you’re in Bengaluru, then consider this a heads-up for a First Principles in-person event where we bring together a diverse, experienced, ambitious, and thinking set of individuals. (I’ve already taken the liberty of asking Amod to brainstorm and discuss the future of engineering.) We’ve—I’ve—never done this sort of thing before.

But hey—cliché alert—you miss 100% of the shots you don’t take!

Hello Ishan, welcome to another sparkling issue of Silent Sunday. It’s Jonathan here. What you just read was a conversation that happened on our First Principles Slack channel barely seconds after Hari dropped this beautiful photograph for this week’s edition. 

Setting aside the fact that Hari first laughed in my face, and then gave me a non-answer (probably didn’t want us city-trotters bespoiling his childhood paradise), it set my mind on a question that’s come up many times in our edition planning meetings. 

“Why do we get so many pictures of landscapes?”

That’s perhaps not a particularly difficult question to answer, at least on a very broad level. 

It’s because people—humans—generally love soaking in a good vista. Open sky. Land stretching away for miles. No other people in sight. What’s not to love, yes? 

But why exactly? Why do we love these things? 

You’d think for such a well-observed phenomena, there’d be a solid scientific explanation by now. And so did I. But when I went looking this week, I only found maybes. A whole lot of maybes, yes, but maybes nonetheless. The ones I found fall in two broad often overlapping buckets, though: evolutionary history and environmental psychology.  

One prevailing theory is that our distant ancestors evolved on the African Savannah (this is still hotly debated), and hence we are hardwired for attraction to semi-open savannah-type vistas. But this isn’t a widely-accepted hypothesis, with some recent studies https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2018.02485/full recording a greater positive emotional reaction for rainforests and dense vegetation. 

Maybe the allure of ‘the empty’ provides a better explanation:

Ellard, the neuroscientist, thinks that “mystery http://eab.sagepub.com/content/30/4/429.abstract” may have something to do with the ability of empty images to draw a viewer in. But he doesn’t mean mystery in the sense you might imagine. “Mystery” is a concept in environmental psychology that refers to the mental sense that an image or setting has the promise of more information around the corner. Looking at a photo of a man-made space devoid of people may trigger this impulse–that there’s more to be learned about the depicted place, effectively drawing you into the image.

[…]

This kind of ability to project oneself into an empty scene might give a viewer a sense of ownership over the space itself, Ellard says. He cited the tendency for real estate agents to remove any personal affects when they show houses or apartments so that potential buyers can imagine the space as their own. “When we’re looking at spaces, vistas, we’re attracted to them. There’s a sense of wanting to feel ownership, possession of those spaces,” he says. “I wonder if it’s easier to feel attachment to a place when there aren’t other people.”

2. A challenging month

Dear Ishan, hello! It’s Anushka, as always, with FP books. It is almost April, and amongst other things like taxes and mangoes, this month to me is also a sunny reminder of poetry. 

Why? Because April is the official month of NaPoWriMo https://www.napowrimo.net/—short for National Poetry Writing Month, an annual creative writing project shared by poets and non-poets alike across the world. The challenge is straightforward. Write a poem every day in April—any kind, any length, any language—and after 30 poems, you’ve completed the challenge. If you’ve attempted this before, you’ll know that there’s a vast range of daily prompts offered by organisations, collectives, and individuals that you can use to write a poem. And you may also know that the project is inspired strongly by NaNoWriMo https://nanowrimo.org/, a very similar activity that challenges you to write 50,000 words of a new novel in November every year. 

On the face of it, both these challenges seem to run counter to the essence of creativity—the fact that it can’t be forced or manufactured. Like, you can’t expect a meaningful, beautiful poem from a creative person every single day, right? Which may be fair, but it’s worth understanding why these projects exist. NaNo, for instance, encourages writers to think about the length of their work rather than quality—“get out a messy but completed first draft first, edit later!”. NaPo removes all constraints, and just asks you to form a daily habit. Creativity is beautiful and tortured, like the movies want us to believe. But I think the great work that comes out of the more than half-a-million participants every year is evidence that creativity can benefit from more unfabulous things like practice and habit-forming.

The reason I bring this up is because the First Principles community is brimming with writers. I don’t just mean published writers (though we have some of those, too!). I mean folks who write as a hobby, or as a habit, or even as a way of learning. Last week, when we asked for how you learned from books, we got enthusiastic and varied responses. But there was one common factor: writing things down helps immensely with understanding and retaining information. 

Neha Singh, for instance, tells us that her process is filled with “lots of scribbles, notes, underlines.” And then, she gathers these thoughts into a more structured format. “I found a very satisfying way to document my learnings—I started writing a blog to them and how those learnings apply to my life.” 

Siddharth Raman tells us he does it the old fashioned way—handwritten notes in dairies. But this isn’t a casual process. The notes are long, detailed and copious! “This helps in two ways… retaining the learnings for longer and improving my handwriting! It is also time consuming but extremely fulfilling to keep going back to.”

3. Why does Freshworks have an open playbook?

The answer is simple: because Girish Mathrubootham, the founder and CEO of Freshworks, wants to pay it forward. Which isn’t just a phrase—Girish is deeply involved in SaaSboomi, a community that calls itself “The World’s Largest Pay-it-forward Community of SaaS founders”. 

A few weeks ago, when Rohin spoke with Girish for First Principles, we picked up a lot of interesting themes internally. I liked the fact that Girish focussed so sharply on the authority of making decisions, and being accountable for them. Hari appreciated the storyteller in Girish. I even remember that Akshaya—who hosts our newest podcast: The First Two Years—loved the things he had to say about Chennai! A lot of these themes come up in the second part of our conversation with Girish, which we’ve released in our most recent episode of the First Principles podcast. But undoubtedly, it is his unshakeable belief in building a transparent and strong SaaS community that stands out. As one of SaaSboomi’s founders, Girish shared with the group the methods and tactics that got a young Freshworks to US$1 million in annual revenue—and then from there to US$5 million.

How often do you see a company teaching its growth playbook to possible future competitors? You’ll find Girish talking about that and a lot more here:

4. Subscriber emails

Some people write emails, others write tweet threads. Sandeep Gautam, one of our regular subscribers, writes sonnets. Here are two he wrote based on earlier editions of First Principles. He maintains them on his blog, here https://thefoolsquest.blogspot.com/.

Cultivating Fallow (To “The Three Stages of Fallow https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/the-three-stages-of-fallow/”) 

Memento Mori, put the day to productive use, 

Take the bull by the horns—don’t let it pass, or refuse.  

The hour that elapses, won’t come back again, 

You need to write daily, come storm or rain. 

Premeditatio Malorum, you’ll hit a roadblock, 

When the field’s not fertile, you’ll need to take stock.

The day may get wasted, but you’ll emerge recharged, 

Rest will give you a momentum, and ahead you would have barged. 

Amor Fati, just appreciate the downtime, 

Bow out gracefully, while you’re still in your prime. 

Enjoy the sleep, the rest, remain barren and fallow

Play with words and phrases, or in nostalgia wallow.

Summum Bonum, let the poem ferment,

While the words do all the talking, look at what the pauses meant.

Achieving The End Goal (To “Why Aren’t You Changing Your Mind? https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/why-arent-you-changing-your-mind/”)

Keep digging at the tunnel, maybe the treasure’s just ahead, 

Never entertain the possibility, that it may lead to an end that’s dead. 

With each stroke of the hammer, the rock is developing cracks,

The log will have to eventually split, never mind the blunt axe.

‘To repeat the same actions, and expect a different outcome,

Is the definition of insanity’, as you reflect, you realize you are dumb. 

Dig a tunnel in another direction, or just use a sharpened axe,

By changing your actions, a little or lot, you’ll fill your coffers, and sacks.

‘I’m a loser, I don’t deserve treasures’, ‘getting them is a pain and not worth’,

If you are denied treasures for long, such thoughts may readily take birth.

Single loop learning is so passe, examine your beliefs and mindset,  

By changing your thoughts, a little or lot, on the road to riches you’ll be set.

Stick to your actions, change strategies and tactics, or even change your mind, 

Have you considered that in the first place, you don’t need to treasure find.

Good Morning Ishan,
 
Very few professions encourage knowledge enrichment on a daily basis. Journalism is one such."There must be so much learning in your job," remarked the founder of a startup I was interviewing for a story.
 
I was trying to understand the margin structure at each level of a B2B retail business. After talking to the founder and a few other industry sources, it felt like I had done a short term course in B2B retail. I’d learnt all that one needed to know about the business, without spending the time or money.
 
And it isn't just this story. Writing about a varied set of companies and sectors ensures that the learning never stops, for all of us. Most of us journalists are generalists and squirrelling out the finer details of what goes on in a business is what keeps us hooked to the job. As the late Horace Greeley, the founder-editor of American newspaper New-York Tribune, once said—"Journalism will kill you, but it will keep you alive while you are at it".
 
The B2B startup founder I mentioned earlier also told me that there are very few journalists asking detailed questions these days. Understandable, given the usual chase for headlines rather than the story.
 
This applies to us too. None of our deep dives would be possible if it weren’t for us always asking, learning, and then bettering ourselves. We learn from you as well, dear reader and that has played a key role in shaping not just our main product but also our newsletters, BFO and Nutgraf. We learn so that you can—from a bunch of special stories every week.
 
This week, we had our fingers on the pulse of every major development in the sectors we cover.
 
Take, for example, Arundhati and Sumanth's story on the Chinese apps ban.  They argue that the ban may have been a tactical response. "But, it has thrown open a new Overton Window that could irrevocably impact the choices of Indian startups, investors, and consumers in many significant ways," the story says.
 
In case you were looking to understand the Byju-Doubtnut deal in detail, Olina has just the thing for you. In her story, she explores why two different companies with very different offerings are coming together. Probably because with this acquisition, Byju’s is closer to its goal of becoming an edtech ecosystem that sprawls across age groups, exam types, and income categories. There is more. Read it here.
 
On Wednesday, Shreedhar wrote about how India now has local mathematical models for Covid-19. While early models looked at the country as a whole, newer models are restricting themselves to cities and districts. But then, without access to data and active collaboration with government task forces, their utility is limited. This was our free story of the week, so do read and share it widely.
 
Prasannata's story on Thursday was about HealthifyMe's workout platform Healthify studio, which faces competition from CureFit on the one hand and OTT platforms like Disney+ Hotstar on the other. HealthifyStudio also heralds a new phase for the company whose USP so far was nutrition planning and calorie tracking. "With a workout platform in its kitty, though, it is entering new waters. Waters that are already home to a veritable Great White—Curefit," says Prasannata in her story.
 
For our Monday piece, Sumanth laid out the reasons why it makes sense for Tiger Global, the prodigal investor known for picking up Flipkart when it was just a blip on the horizon, to make a comeback into the mainstream. With India’s relationship with China deteriorating and a post-WeWork SoftBank hanging in there, there’s room for others.

When stuck in one of Bengaluru’s traffic snarls, I have often wondered what it will take for us to have air taxis in the city, Jetsons’ style.

Turns out, Satya Chakravarthy in Chennai heard my wishes. Chakravarthy is the founder and CEO of The ePlane Company, India’s first private entity to have a design-certified electric plane that is nearing prototype.

Seema, the author of today’s story, visited the ePlane shop floor in Chennai to see first-hand what these futuristic aircraft will look like. In about two months from now, the electric vertical take-off and landing (eVTOL) aircraft prototype will be ready for further certifications.

With this, Chakravarthy is choosing to go where few have gone in India. For one, no one in India has built a civilian aircraft, and the aerospace engineering professor at IIT Madras is attempting to do that at a fraction of the cost of any aerospace venture.

The ePlane is charting the regulatory path for an air service as early as 2027, and sees itself being used as a cargo plane, an air ambulance, even an air taxi. But it’s up against domestic and international competition from the likes of Indigo Airlines to Uber to Boeing.

Now, it is rare to see a truly disruptive startup emerge, but Chakravarthy’s task is also cut out. After all, the ePlane has to invent, productise, indigenise, scale-up, all at once.

Last Wednesday, President Donald Trump slapped Indian exports to the United States with its highest tariffs: 50%. Why? Because India has been purchasing Russian oil, and Trump is accusing India of funding Putin’s war machine.

But just last year, the Biden administration had, in fact, requested India to continue purchasing oil from Russia. So, what changed?

In this original episode from Daybreak, host Snigdha Sharma breaks down how India went from energy partner to trade target—for doing exactly what the U.S. once asked. We also look at the double standards: America’s own imports from Russia and its backing of Israel in Gaza.

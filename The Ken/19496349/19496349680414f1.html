If investor pedigree and founder cred predicted the fortunes of businesses, Karkinos should have never failed. Its captable was a dream doc. Sharing the space were Reliance Industries and the Tata group, India’s largest conglomerates who had perhaps never invested together before. The investor list was a parade of many other marquee names, too. Heading the tech-driven cancer-care platform was R Venkataramanan—earlier a close confidante of the late Ratan Tata and the managing trustee of Tata Trusts, and advisor to Reliance Industries boss Mukesh Ambani.

Both the business potential and social utility of the enterprise were great in a country as underserved as India in cancer care. The idea was to partner with hospitals, set up oncology departments, and provide end-to-end cancer services. “It wanted to be the Oyo* of cancer care,” as an industry veteran puts it. Oh, and it was also backed by the US-based Mayo Clinic, globally renowned for its oncology chops. Funds and expertise were no constraint.

It was Karkinos’ battle to lose.

And lose, it did; spectacularly—dragged to the insolvency court last year for failing to pay an equipment supplier, believe it or not, a mere Rs 5 crore.

In another equally intriguing twist, Karkinos was recently acquired by Reliance Industries in the insolvency court—for around half its earlier valuation. Ouch. Left holding the can are several small investors who have seen their shares turn worthless.

Just how did this mighty cookie crumble in less than five years? What lessons does the saga hold? Sudeshna has the lowdown in today’s fascinating story. Read it here.

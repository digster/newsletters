Nearly a year has flown by since generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) tools burst onto the internet. Like it or not, they’ve changed the world. Yet, the man who backed OpenAI—the company behind ChatGPT and Dall-E—four years ago now says AI investment is over-valued.

In an interview with The Financial Times last week, investor Vinod Khosla said “most [venture] investments in AI today” will lose money.

Now, put that against Google CEO Sundar Pichai’s belief that “every product in every company” will have AI in it. Surely, views are swinging between extremes.

AI, a concept decades old, is now taking centre stage in all things tech thanks to the large language models that are surfacing as free-to-use or open-source platforms. The momentum is also unrelenting. A ChatGPT feature now allows users to access its full suite of capabilities within a single window.

Not surprisingly, global titans Microsoft and Google are locked in a public battle, each with its chatbot, acknowledging their inevitability.

Now, over in India, IT firms are flirting with GenAI, intrigued but not fully committed.

A senior executive at Tata Consultancy Services crystalises the curiosity and the lack of urgency when he says that every client has been asking about GenAI; still, not a single one of them is actually ready to use it. A few, like HCL Tech, are working on 100 GenAI projects. And others like Reliance and Infosys are upskilling their workforce.

Abhirami goes on a quest to figure out this love-hate relationship and uncovers a sector that’s giddy about the tech.

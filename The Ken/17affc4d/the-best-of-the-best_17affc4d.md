It's easy to get jaded, even about things you like, in a pandemic. It's easy to lose sight of why you care about the things you care about. The routine is the same day after day, and Groundhog Day—though fun to watch—isn't a film any of us would've liked to live in. Yet, here we are.
But this jadedness also brings in a layer of appreciation for everything that helps you break out of it. And this was truly one of those weeks for me, and that too, thanks to work. I count myself lucky because not many people get to say that.
Here, at The Ken, we take a lot of pride in the kind of ambitious stories we publish. Be it the doggedness with which our reporters carry out investigations or the sharpness they bring to analysing numbers, or even the conviction they have while laying out a narrative few others in the media would've spotted, there's a rigour that comes with this job. And it's a rigour that's now so in-built, it takes a week like the past—with back-to-back stellar stories—week to truly appreciate... and snap out of one's jaded pandemic bubble.
And that's a joy I would love to share with you.
Arguably the biggest story this week came from Pratap. He wrote about how the
[Indian government's email was breached](https://the-ken.com/story/how-foreign-hackers-weaponised-indias-cybersecurity-shield/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter) not once, not twice, but three separate times in the span of one week—7-14 July. Inboxes and cloud storages of multiple government officials were accessed by the hackers, including that of Ajay Sawhney, secretary at India’s IT ministry and the top boss of the National Informatics Centre and India’s cybersecurity agency CERTIn. And there's been no word on who these hackers were or how they did it. Read this explosive (and free!) story of the week right away.
Another great story this week came from Munsif, who wrote about
[Google's News Showcase](https://the-ken.com/story/inside-google-news-showcase/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter). The licensing program is paying Indian news publishers—like NDTV,
Hindustan Times and
The Indian Express—to prominently feature their content within Google News. Showcase is a sort of middle-ground for the media players and Google. The former have complained about being shortchanged by the search giant for years. But it's a bit of a Faustian deal. Though the early payout is great for publishers, what are they signing away long-term?
Speaking of Faustian deals, Arundhati had quite the story this week. She wrote about
[listed adtech platform Affle](https://the-ken.com/story/the-kerfuffle-with-affle/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter), which has spent over $85 million on a long line of investments and acquisitions. The companies Affle has taken a keen interest in include Indus OS, Bobble AI, App Studio, and Vizury. But it's been in a recent public skirmish with Indus OS—this is tricky, especially because acquisitions and investments are crucial to Affle's growth. But recalled loans, investments for fees, unfriendly terms of engagement dot some of Affle’s deals, making it an unusual strategic investor anyway. Its investees often come to it in need of a lifeline—thereby forging Faustian deals.
Olina wrote about (once Eurokids)
[Lighthouse Learning](https://the-ken.com/story/kkr-lighthouse-learning-k12-course/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter). The pre-school-focussed company is now refocussing on the K12 (up to class 12) segment. But its recent acquisitions aside, the pickings are slim in private schools. The requirements of the right fee range, regulatory structure, and brand make it a shallow pool.
We had a fascinating healthcare business story on
[Metropolis Healthcare](https://the-ken.com/story/the-pharmeasy-thyrocare-jinx-on-the-metropolis-hitech-deal/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter) and the small player it has been wooing for a while—Dr Ganesan’s Hitech Diagnostic Centre. In January, Metropolis agreed to buy a 100% stake in the unlisted Hitech in a cash-plus-stock deal—Rs 511 crore ($69 million) in cash and Rs 106 crore ($14 million) worth of Metropolis’ shares. But Hitech has been ghosting Metropolis for a bit. Why? Possibly due to another deal too close home in the diagnostics space—deep-pocketed healthcare startup PharmEasy acquired a 66% stake in Thyrocare from Dr Arokiaswamy Velumani, the company’s promoter. And Velumani bought a 5% stake in PharmEasy. Win-win for these two, but not so much for the industry, Anand and Maitri write.

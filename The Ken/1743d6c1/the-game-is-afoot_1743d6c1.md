The phrase was made popular by Arthur Conan Doyle’s famous deerstalker-clad detective. It’s associated so much with Sherlock Holmes that you’d think it was his catchphrase. It turns out, that’s as much a fallacy as thinking Holmes actually said “Elementary, my dear Watson”. (He didn’t.)
“The game is afoot” was uttered by Holmes only once, in the short story The Adventure of the Abbey Grange, which is part of a collection titled The Return of Sherlock Holmes. But it has been embedded in the collective literary psyche for decades now that it’s almost lore.
There’s also its modern version—“The game is on”—which was popularised by the smartphone-wielding, high-functioning sociopath avatar of Holmes, played by actor Benedict Cumberbatch in the BBC adaptation.
The phrase immediately colours the scene with excitement, at least for me. Something is happening; maybe a mystery has come together, or a piece has made a move on the giant chessboard. There is churn, and you, the spectator, are sitting in the observer’s corner, rubbing your hands eagerly, waiting to see what will happen next.
This week, The Ken was that spectator. We watched and observed as the giant chessboard tilted and turned this way and that, the players moving around so rapidly that the scene before us changed every day. The game was afoot this week; and we watched and broke it down for you, dear reader, so that you could make sense of it all.
On Thursday, our editor Seema wrote about a
[risky situation](https://the-ken.com/story/saving-indian-vaccine-makers-covid-nationalism/?utm_source=recap&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter) brewing for both vaccine researchers and patients in India. The Indian government may approve emergency use authorisation for vaccines, which means they would be used even before proper trials. But the government isn’t making any advance purchase commitments, leaving local vaccine makers out of a potential $20 billion opportunity. It's pseudo-nationalism, argues Seema in this week’s free story.
On Monday, our CEO Rohin explored the
[effects](https://the-ken.com/story/reliances-netmeds-acquisition-e-pharma/?utm_source=recap&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter) of Reliance’s acquisition of Netmeds on India’s e-pharmacy sector as a whole. And this isn’t the only major event in this space; suddenly, many pieces are being moved around. MedLife merged with PharmEasy, Amazon launched its e-pharmacy arm, and Flipkart is planning to do the same. What is life going to look like for the sector once the dust settles?
On Tuesday, Shreedhar wrote an insightful story about the
[paradox](https://the-ken.com/story/the-hiring-goldmine-in-a-firing-market/?utm_source=recap&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter) in India’s job scene. Nearly 19 million salaried jobs have been lost since April and, unsurprisingly, revenue for recruitment firms is down. After all, what do you do when you’re in the business of recruitment, but it’s layoff season? However, there are sparks of recovery. In fact, for some recruitment companies, 2020 may even turn out to be a great year.
Wednesday saw our fintech writer Arundhati and our auto writer Pranav team up to do a story on how companies like ride-hailing firm Ola are now using
[fintech as another feature](https://the-ken.com/story/ola-amazon-and-the-flaw-in-faafing-for-fintech-dominance/?utm_source=recap&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter) on their platforms to draw in customers. And it’s not just Ola. Even Amazon and Flipkart have stakes in this game. But running a fintech firm and an online platform are two starkly different businesses, they argue.
Our Mumbai bureau chief Seetharaman, on Friday, wrote about supermarket chain Star's
[identity crisis](https://the-ken.com/story/tata-tescos-retail-identity-crisis-is-now-an-existential-one/?utm_source=recap&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter). The chain, a joint venture between the Tata Group and UK grocery giant Tesco, has long been struggling with what it wants to be. A value retailer? A hypermarket? A grocer? A convenience store? In 16 years, it has just 57 stores, compared with Reliance Retail’s 797 and DMart’s 214. Now, with Reliance Industries buying out Future Group’s Future Retail, the situation has turned all the more precarious for Star.

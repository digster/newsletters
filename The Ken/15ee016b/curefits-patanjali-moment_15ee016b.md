Today we complete a year. And what a year it has been! More on that in a separate post on our website later in the day. For now, a heartfelt Thank You to each one of you for reading us and sharing feedback and ideas.
Last week, one story in The Wall Street Journal jumped up at me. It was about how exercise fans are spending more money at boutique fitness studios than at gas stations. The report was from marketing software firm Cardlytics which tracks spending trends of 120 million bank accounts in the US.
Boutique fitness is no new luxury, as it has been believed to be. Which is why when Mukesh Bansal’s new venture began plastering flyover pillars in Bengaluru with posters of Cult.fit, a boutique gym, it didn’t escape our notice. Bansal founded Myntra, ran it for 7 years and sold the fashion portal to Flipkart for $300 million in 2014.
This time around, Bansal says he is not chasing ‘vanity metrics’. He wants to build an ambitious digital health company in Cure.fit. Funding has been quick, branding top notch with the Greek god of Bollywood doing a barter deal, and ‘.fit’ makes a perfect suffix for the four businesses it is entering swiftly, in a matter of 12-18 months.
Phew! Bansal is on a tear and Cure.fit on steroids. But, as you know, steroids aren’t really good for health. Not even digital health. Because at the end of the day, health is about clinical benefits, and healthcare is a conservative industry which demands evidence of quality and outcomes.
So far, Bansal is speaking a familiar language: “A billion-dollar business with two million customers in five years...”
No existing model comes close, except Patanjali's. Yes, Yoga guru-turned-businessman Baba Ramdev’s sprawling business Patanjali.
I think that’s enough to pique your interest in Payal’s story today.

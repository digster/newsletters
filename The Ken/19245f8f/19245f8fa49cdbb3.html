Let’s say you get sick. You see a doctor and get checked, receive a prescription and buy some drugs, and maybe get a refill at your pharmacy. You may not think twice about the cost of that medication, as long as it isn’t expensive enough to set off alarms in your head.

That price tag, however, can vary wildly depending on where you are in India and which medical professional you visit. This is a problem for insurers and the government when they structure medical coverage.

In fact, a centralised data repository for drug prices still doesn’t exist. Customer relationship management platforms used by pharmacies, however, are filling that gap. One consequence is that it’s becoming clear which hospitals are billing their patients for drugs at above the maximum retail price.

Given that one-third of India’s healthcare expenditure is on drugs, the bird’s-eye view on costs matters a lot.

This isn’t the first time someone attempted to develop a database that records drug prices. The National Health Authority attempted to do exactly that in 2021. It managed to log 39,000 drugs into its database, but that was only the tip of the iceberg.

That’s all to say this is a monumental task. Sudeshna spoke with some of the people involved to find out how the problem is being tackled, and what this means for insurers and public health programmes. Check out her story today.

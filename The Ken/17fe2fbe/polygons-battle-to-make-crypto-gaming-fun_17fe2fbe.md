The success of Axie Infinity—a blockchain-based game with around 2.5 million users—would have you believe that crypto gaming has well and truly arrived... until you actually play it. The graphics are basic, and the gameplay is dated. And unlike most games, which simply require downloading, there are a host of technical hoops you must jump through to get Axie up and running.
If you were hoping Axie was an outlier, it isn't. Most blockchain-based games are equally, if not more, limited. Indeed, their distinct lack of a gaming fun factor is probably why their biggest selling point isn't the gaming experience but the chance to earn crypto tokens by playing.
Despite blockchain-based gaming—or GameFi—being so deeply underwhelming, the space has seen an explosion of investor interest. In January 2022, $1 billion was pumped into the space—nearly a quarter of what was invested in crypto gaming in the previous year.
Front and centre in this movement is Polygon, an Indian blockchain firm built to enable faster, cheaper trading on Ethereum. Polygon has realised that trading alone won't make its platform sticky. Gaming, however, could. Already, three out of the five most popular apps on its platform are games.
Polygon, though, is clear that crypto gaming has a long way to go to truly appeal to users en masse. To bring about this evolution, the firm is going all out—partnerships with gaming studios, grants for game developers, hiring top gaming execs. But will this be enough to effectively supercharge and mainstream a space that has, so far, subsisted on a combination of novelty and mediocrity? That's what Jaspreet looks at in today's story.

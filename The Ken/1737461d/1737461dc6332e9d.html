When it comes to their e-commerce operations, Walmart and Amazon have what you’d call a “complicated” relationship with the Indian government and sundry regulators. The American companies, respectively #1 and #2 in e-commerce, spend their days and weeks fending off attacks from rivals, suppliers, small businesses, and politicians who grudge them the power that comes with their scale and growth.
 
Virtually every dollar they invest and every dollar they earn is channelled through a web of complex subsidiaries and affiliates to stay on the right side of the law.
 
But neither of them faces any opposition at all when it comes to one rarely talked about part of their India operations—their software development centres.
 
Last year, Walmart employed 3,800 people, largely engineers, in Walmart Labs India. That number might end at 7,000 this year. Already, Amazon has over 10,000. Unlike traditional outsourcing, such centres are “extensions of headquarters” in terms of their strategic relevance.
 
Before the pandemic struck, there were over 1,400 such “captive centres” run by multinationals in India. US companies own nearly 70% of these.
 
In a counterintuitive story, Kunal lays out how the pandemic, combined with US President Donald Trump’s multiple visa bans, is helping accelerate the next wave of offshoring into India. He calls it a “3.X” moment.

Folks of a certain vintage might recall a certain pony-tailed gentleman splashed on the front pages of newspapers. “Dare to think beyond IIMs,” proclaimed IIPM (Indian Institute of Planning and Management) with its dean Arindam Chaudhuri leading the hard-sell. The institute eventually shut down its education programmes about a decade back, done in by a slew of allegations.

The dance of creative destruction never stopped though. Around the time IIPM was heading towards its sunset, a young man was taking baby steps towards his sunrise. What started as fun, free videos on Youtube morphed over the next decade into a giant that upended India’s competitive test-prep industry. Quality, quirky teaching at low costs found a huge market. In the process, turning Alakh Pandey, the founder of Physics Wallah into a cult figure, among his legion of students who flocked first online and then offline, too. Physics Wallah, today, stands at the threshold of a Rs 4,600 crore initial public offer—a survivor, so to speak, in the radioactive wasteland that is Indian edtech.

What’s not to like?

Turns out, in its journey from a bootstrapped startup to a venture-capital-funded biggie on the listing path, PW caught an all-too-familiar bug—the do-more, show-more one.

Veering from core competency, Physics Wallah made promises that it could not keep. As Suprita, the author of today’s well-reported story writes, the PW Institute of Innovation sold dreams of industry-ready BTech and other programmes. “When it comes to employability, we are far ahead than any other engineering college in India,” went Pandey’s pitch to students. Eerily similar to IIPM’s bluster. With eerily similar consequences too—an erosion of trust among students, at the top of the list.

How did this cookie crumble? Was it something the still loss-making startup now funded by big money could not avoid? Will this weigh on its upcoming IPO? Read on here to find out.

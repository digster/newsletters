"Your marketing message in the post-Covid era will be less about your chicken and more about hygiene."
 
Anurag Katiyar, president of the National Restaurant Association of India, is speaking about food. But his words reflect the inevitable paradigm shift sweeping across industries.
 
'Health is wealth' is no longer just an adage. It is the new business mantra for all industries—food, retail, airlines, cabs, hotels, theatres. Even for offices and that desk that you so badly want to return to. (No? Only me? Ok, then.) 
 
As customers, we all rely on ratings, those ubiquitous five stars. But until now they indicated quality—the movie is excellent, the food is lip-smacking. A hygiene rating will be next. It probably, no surely, will assume more importance than quality. There's proof. 
 
Foodtech platform Zomato says users are filtering restaurants with a hygiene rating on the app and those with a strong rating see about 20-25% increase in visibility and orders.
 
It's not as if no one cared about health and hygiene so far. FSSAI, India's food safety watchdog, laid out guidelines in 2006. Offices had US-based OSHA's guidelines. The keyword here is guidelines. Not enforceable in their entirety. 
 
To earn customer trust, these guidelines will have to become rules. Or at the very least be enforced, regularly and not as an annual checklist. And be visible to the customer in some form. Or, as the popular journalism mantra goes, 'show, don't tell.'
 
This is where a 15-year-old Indian health rating agency is poised to strike gold. "The business of third-party inspection is set to skyrocket," says the founder.

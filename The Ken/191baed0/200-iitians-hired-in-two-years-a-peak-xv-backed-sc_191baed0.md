No matter how you feel about coaching centres, parents and teachers expect students to spend many hours with their cram-school tutors. The root of it all is that most schools simply do not have the wherewithal or aptitude to give their students a great education.
That’s led India’s coaching centres to have a steady stream of customers for years. But one chain of private-education institutions is trying to change that.
Orchids The International School has over 90 branches across India. To improve its STEM (Science, Technology, Engineering, and Mathematics) programme—and offer courses that are more exceptional than those offered by other schools in the country—Orchids recruited 200 IIT graduates to join its staff. These instructors will conduct lectures for physics, chemistry, and mathematics courses, and maybe even make the classrooms more interactive.
As Jai Decosta, CEO of Orchids parent K12 Techno Services, told Alifiya, “So you can give a pinhole camera to a student in Class 6, operate it in front of them, and explain the science principle behind it. But what if you teach them to make one themselves? They are unlikely to ever forget the science then.”
That sounds like fun—and might explain how IIT graduates could make business better for Orchids. Find out how this is playing out and what it means for India’s private coaching sector by reading [Alifiya’s report](https://the-ken.com/story/200-iitians-hired-in-two-years-a-kedaara-backed-school-declares-war-on-iit-coaching/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter).
Over in Indonesia, Shanies looked at the disruption in Southeast Asia’s e-commerce space. Upstarts like Temu and Tiktok Shop are forcing changes in bigger platforms such as Shopee and Lazada, and the result is that all these players are starting to look more and more like each other.
Tiktok Shop, in particular, saw double-digit absolute growth in its market share across Southeast Asia, while Temu’s gamified shopping experience is appealing to consumers in the region. Together, they’re building a new habit in users—to make impulse buys—and giving the “everything store” model of Shopee a run for its money.
Still, the executives of Shopee’s parent, Sea Ltd, put up a strong front, and the company’s share price has roughly doubled since the beginning of the year.
E-commerce GMV is expected to hit US$186 billion in 2025. The bulk of that will go to the platform that manages to master both forms of selling—the age-old format of listing everything but the kitchen sink, and product discovery that comes via live-streams and targeted recommendations.

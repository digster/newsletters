"The Psychology of Money"
 
Of all the relationships that humans build over a lifetime, our relationship with money is, perhaps, the most complex. The way we think about money and how these thoughts shape our behaviour has perennially been a subject of fascination.
 
Take money-lending, for example.
 
Both history and mythology vilify money-lending and its practitioners as being avaricious and immoral. The characterisation of Shylock from The Merchant of Venice perhaps foreshadowed how the entire Jewish race would be stereotyped and discriminated against. Kuber, the "moneylender for the Gods", gets his name from the Sanskrit word for "deformed or monstrous".
 
Yet, at the other end of the spectrum, money-lending can be a powerful instrument of financial inclusion that can benefit the poor and marginalised—an imperative that has both moral as well as economic efficiency objectives.
 
But as with every other aspect of money, the business of money-lending is a complex dialectic, replete with challenges around risk management, incentive structuring, and moral hazards.
 
Nothing exemplifies this better than the recent Covid-induced loan moratorium announced by the Reserve Bank of India. Now that this moratorium has lapsed, how are borrowers and lenders coming to grips with payments and collections?

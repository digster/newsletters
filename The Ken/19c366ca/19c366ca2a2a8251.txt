John Akers was fired as IBM’s CEO on 26 January 1993.

I must state that the official record states that Akers resigned, but really, he was forced out by IBM’s board. It’s one of those breakups where both parties tell others that it was mutual, even though only one of them agreed to it. 

But this story begins three years before Akers was pushed out as CEO.

In 1990, IBM was the most valuable technology company on earth. It had over $60 billion in revenues and nearly half a million employees. It was the third-largest company by market capitalisation in the S&P 500, only behind Exxon and General Electric. In the 1990s, it was a big deal to be bigger than everyone except energy companies (unlike today, when technology companies are also starting to effectively become energy companies)

That was IBM’s highest point. During the period of 1990 to 1993, IBM went through one of the most harrowing experiences a company can endure. It posted cumulative losses of nearly $16 billion over the three years (with each year worse than the previous one). The market capitalisation of the company fell by 70% to just around $29 billion. The stock price cratered.

In just 12 quarters, IBM went from one of the world’s mightiest companies to a company that was essentially on its deathbed.

Cut to 2026.

I guess we must talk about what happened last week…

This week on the Zero Shot podcast

As the producer of Zero Shot, my job is to read and re-read everything on AI. And in the Indian space, one firm is a fixture: Sarvam AI. In 2023, it made news for its $41 million funding round. Two years later, it became the Indian government’s first pick to make a foundational model for the country. Last week, Sarvam was powering live translations of the Union Budget on TV in multiple Indian languages.

And now it is expected to take centre stage at the forthcoming India AI Impact Summit. Looking at Sarvam’s messaging, it seems like it wants to be for AI what UPI has been for payments.

It’s already a metaphor that both the industry and the government are using with equal enthusiasm. But is that even possible? What made UPI so unique? Where is Sarvam placed? When Sarvam says UPI, is it aspiring to be the next PhonePe, which grew and grew some more because of the centralised payments interface?

Lead host Praveen Gopal Krishnan lays out the Sarvam positioning. What follows is a heated discussion that covers sovereign AI, Indian datasets, and the carefully calibrated Chinese approach to AI. Alongside the usual banter that is characteristic of Zero Shot.

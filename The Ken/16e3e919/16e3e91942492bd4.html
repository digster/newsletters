Good Morning Ishan,
 
As far as "Huh! What?" news in the startup world goes, there is little to top the surprise announcement from Gojek a couple of weeks back. Turns out the Indonesian ride-hailing company's founder and CEO, Nadiem Makarim, left to become minister of education and culture in President Joko "Jokowi" Widodo's cabinet.
 
And it's not that the 35-year-old was forced out by the board (like Steve Jobs at Apple), or left due to an acquisition (like the founders of Instagram and WhatsApp quitting Facebook). Or even that a top investor ousted the CEO after a colossal misstep, such as a botched IPO (like you know who). 
 
No, Gojek was blazing along just fine - raising billions of dollars and amassing companies in its tooth-and-nail fight with Southeast Asia's ride-hailing leader Grab. 
 
Makarim quit of his own free will, leaving Gojek with an existential crisis and its executives the job of manoeuvring a gargantuan structure.
 
But just like the "super app" it is building, Gojek has also amassed "super executives." Its top tier includes the founders and leaders at startups Gojek acquired, including the duo who now form the backbone of its India-based tech team.
 
But you know what they say about too many cooks. Still, "Gojek has a very unique structure that can allow it to survive major leadership change," says the co-founder of a VC firm that has stakes in Gojek and Grab. "As long as they still align their vision," is his caveat.
 
And speaking of Grab, how does it assess its own leader's potential to follow a higher calling? "Our biggest risk is Anthony (Tan) starting a church somewhere," says one executive.
 
Funny as that may sound, with Makarim gone, Tan may very well be the priest who officiates the marriage between the arch-rivals. Will there be a "happily ever after"?

Good Morning Ishan,
In 2000, HFCL became the destination for the largest ever foreign direct investment in an Indian telecom company at the time. The future seemed bright for the optical fibre manufacturer.
Just three years on, however, the investor would exit, plunging HFCL into a corporate debt restructuring (CDR)-induced downward spiral that would take over 13 years to escape.
These stratospheric highs and precipitous falls have come to be part and parcel of HFCL's roller-coaster existence. Indeed, since it emerged from the CDR cloud in 2017, it has seen its share price swing violently. Sample this: in the span of just four months in 2021, its share price bounced from a 52-week low to a 52-week high.
This sort of volatility has, in the past, alarmed analysts and scared away investors. But HFCL has more than a few things going for it, and bigger plans that could see it shed the tag of being a yo-yo company.
For one, the company enjoys the patronage of Reliance Jio—arguably the most affluent benefactor in the telecom space. Jio's parent company, Reliance Industries, owns as much as 5% of HFCL, and enlisted HFCL's services to deploy its optical fibre cable network across India. It also has a knack for landing government contracts.
After years of being content to simply manufacture and deploy optical fibre, however, HFCL is finally looking at the bigger picture. It wants to be a systems integrator, a la Tata Consultancy Services (TCS).
In addition, it sees the 5G revolution on India's horizon and is building 5G tech solutions in-house in hopes of emerging as a domestic challenger to foreign telecoms giants such as Nokia and Ericsson. With Chinese telecoms vendors frozen out and the government pushing for home-grown solutions, HFCL is desperate to seize the day.

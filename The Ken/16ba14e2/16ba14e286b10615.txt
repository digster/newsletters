Ever been bitten by a dog? I have. Twice, actually. In no way have these incidents made me love dogs any less, nor do they explain why I currently possess a small, volatile army of cats. But I do remember the pain. Not so much from the bites, but from the multiple anti-rabies vaccine (ARV) injections that followed. 

While the injections hurt, the alternative—rolling the dice on rabies—was far more terrifying. Especially in a country like India, which has the most mortalities due to rabies in the world. A 2015 study placed the annual rabies deaths in India at almost 21,000. Congo placed a distant second, with around a third of that.

It is this sort of prevalence that has also led to India having the world's single largest ARV manufacturer. And an overall ARV production capacity well above the national requirement of around 35-48 million doses. 

Yet somehow, in a country with more than adequate demand and equally robust supply, India's facing an 80% shortfall in ARV stocks.

As public health centres run out of the vaccine, states are borrowing from one another even as they desperately float tenders that receive no responses. The situation is dire, but you don't need us to tell you that.

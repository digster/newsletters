Good Morning Ishan,
Data centres have been built in far-flung places over the years. They’ve materialised in the Arctic or the ocean, not only to benefit from natural moats but also to keep the facilities cool.
The planning that goes into those exotic placements seems like a luxury now. There’s an urgent need for data centres and the services they provide since cloud computing, streaming, and generative AI are taking off. In India, the stakes are high. The government, for instance, is offering a tender worth Rs 10,000 crore, or US$1.2 billion, to develop new AI infrastructure.
India’s data centres aren’t only getting bigger; they’re also becoming more sophisticated. The result is a lot of hot air—literally. Operators like Yotta, NTT, Sify Technologies, and CtrlS are putting graphics-processing units (GPUs) into their racks, and each of these units uses 7X the amount of power as central-processing units (CPUs). So, for your next Netflix binge to go smoothly, or for you to get an answer to your next ChatGPT prompt, data centres need to be cooled properly.
That’s where liquid cooling comes into play. It’s more efficient than fan-blown systems but with a higher upfront cost. There are some risks involved because, well, fluids and electronics typically don’t mix well. But for data centres to offer better processing power, liquid cooling is essential.
Three providers offer this infrastructure in India: Vertiv, Schneider Electric, and Stulz. A windfall is likely heading their way in the near future.

Good Morning Ishan,
 
A month or so back, a relative rang me up just past midnight and said those dreaded words, “I am feeling a shooting pain in my left arm.” Needless to say, I scrambled to reach there and then sped to the nearest hospital. From call to the emergency room it took 40 minutes, and this was post-midnight.
 
I am narrating this unpleasant incident (and I can only hope you have not faced one) because I recently came across some startling facts, at least for me.  The likelihood of surviving a heart attack is over 80% if treated within two hours, and every 30-minute delay increases the risk of death by 7%. 
 
India has among the biggest burdens of cardiac disease globally, with nearly 6 in every 100 citizens affected as of 2016, by which time the death rate had ballooned to 34% since 1990. I am so thankful I did not know these grim-reaper-like stats that night, else I too might have felt some phantom pain.
 
But of course, this “growing market” offers an opportunity, for ECG startups in this case. A host of them have mushroomed across the country, offering equipment and services, hoping to make a dent in an untapped market. Yet they have cornered a minuscule 1% of a $60 million market that has been pegged as the fastest growing in the world. So what ails the likes of Tricog Health Services, Cardiac Design Labs, iMedrix and Uber Diagnostics? 
 
Plenty as it turns out, starting with healthcare providers themselves. Some cardiologists are wary of using equipment that still await certification and demand solid proof of outcome, while others prefer to stick to the larger manufacturers whose gear they already use. In small towns, it's costs—small hospitals and care centres cannot afford to buy such equipment. And if they can, their patients will likely be unable to fork out charges.
 
And what about the risk from Apple Watch you ask? Turns out that while the launch of a heart-rate monitor in a watch grabbed interest globally, it is the least of the problems Tricog and its peers face.

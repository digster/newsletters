The likes of Blinkit, Zepto, and Instamart are racing against time to set up hundreds of new dark stores—all the while dancing to the tunes of property owners

Blinkit, Zepto, Swiggy Instamart, and Flipkart are on a frantic property hunt to have 1,000 dark stores in their kitty in the next six months. For property owners, though, all of this means cashing in on the opportunity by pitting desperate competitors against each other. Tata’s iPhone factory dorms are fully charged. But the talent it wants is out of coverage area https://the-ken.com/story/tatas-want-to-build-parts-for-iphone-locally-but-not-with-locals/?utm_source=weekly_email&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=weekly_recap_card

The US$155-billion Indian tech-manufacturing industry needs skilled talent and high productivity—something companies can’t find closer to their factories 

The Tata group is hiring migrant labourers to work at its manufacturing facilities in  South India—similar to China’s “dormitory labour regime”. The cross-state-hiring model prevents workers from having local networks, avoiding attrition and unionisation. Besides, hiring migrant workers helps manufacturers bridge the existing skill gap in their workforce. State governments, however, don’t seem elated with hiring migrants, owing to its priorities of employing its domiciled youth. Amazon and Samara Capital turn the Dmart model on its head to fix their supermarkets https://the-ken.com/story/why-amazon-and-samara-capital-shrank-their-supermarkets-when-dmart-kept-getting-bigger/?utm_source=weekly_email&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=weekly_recap_card

Vinod Nambiar was tasked with pulling the More grocery chain out of its years-long struggle. And he went against the grain by ditching big stores and lucrative products 

The Aditya Birla Group struggled to turn the corner with More supermarkets and sold the business to Samara and Amazon for Rs 4,200 crore. More had stores of all sizes, unlike Dmart’s preference for large outlets, and lacked its rival’s “value retailer” reputation. Under Vinod Nambiar, an FMCG veteran, More has chosen neighbourhood supermarkets over big-box stores and used fresh produce to draw in shoppers. It has also improved the economics of its stores by using them to fulfil orders for Amazon Fresh. Philippine engineers are (mostly) locked out of the US$45B chip industry https://the-ken.com/story/why-philippine-engineers-are-mostly-locked-out-of-the-us45b-chip-industry/?utm_source=weekly_email&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=weekly_recap_card

International chipmakers’ perception of local talent has Philippine factories bringing in engineers for assembly and packaging—jobs that don’t require much skill 

The Philippine semiconductor industry specialises in assembling, testing, and packaging chips while seeking to expand into design and manufacturing. The country produces 50,000 engineers each year but they may not be enough to fill the talent gap. The lack of international certification for the country’s engineering programmes is tightening hiring requirements in the chip sector, pushing engineers into assembly line work. Local talent and professional groups are working to improve the country’s engineering reputation, but the progress seems to be slow. Ravneet Kaur is revitalising the Competition Commission of India, trying to shake off its toothless tiger image—but she must confront a mix of old and new challenges 

The fair-market regulator grapples with many challenges, from staffing shortages and procedural delays to leakage of confidential investigation reports and failure to collect penalties. On 9 September, the commission introduced significant changes to overhaul the merger control regime. Then, on 17 September, it unveiled General Regulations, 2024, to simplify filings, improve confidentiality, and enhance the implementation and monitoring of its orders. The sweeping reforms necessitate massive capacity enhancement at the already overburdened CCI to ensure ease of doing business.

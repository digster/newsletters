We’re also approaching flu season in Bengaluru. I got my annual flu vaccine last week but seem to have still caught the bug yesterday. I hope its intensity is softened due to the vaccine’s effects.
After many years of trial and error and reading, I’ve come around to getting vaccinated around mid-August to build immunity in time for the flu season. [This article](https://historyofvaccines.org/blog/timing-your-flu-shot-science-behind-when-get-vaccinated) offers some reasoning, though from the US seasonal perspective. One area the rains have helped is our fledgling avocado tree. It has shot up by a few feet in just the last two months.
And finally, from the wonderful world of street indies, I’ve made a few new friends. On our daily morning walk to the nearby lake, there’s (of course) a set of local “guardians” every 500 meters or so. I’ve managed to befriend most of them by bribing them with a few treats every morning.
The oldest among the guardians were two handsome black indies who lived near a collection of small tenements and a private water tanker filling station. One of them passed away recently, so the folks running the water tankers brought in two brown puppies as the new guardians.
The first few times they saw Gabru, they yelped and barked a lot. But you won’t believe it, all it took was one day of giving them a few treats to turn them into BFFs. Now, they hop on their hind legs and ask me for treats each morning.
Seeing me indulge them must have prompted the man who takes care of them to walk up to me last week. He asked in Kannada if I could give him some medicine to get rid of ticks. I bought and gave him two packs of Fipronil some days later, happy knowing that we shared a common love for street indies despite not knowing each other’s names.

Sometimes, it feels like we’re living in “The Great Unravelling”. Everything that we thought we understood and was “settled”, is unravelling. Global trade, job satisfaction, career progression, friendships, wealth creation, relationships… the list is endless.
A friend shared[ this essay about yet another unravelling](https://thestillwandering.substack.com/p/the-death-of-the-corporate-job)—of the corporate job—with me as I was writing this.
The corporate role isn’t dying in some dramatic collapse. It’s dying like religion died for many people—slowly, through diminishing belief rather than disappearing churches.
The structures remain. The offices still gleam. The meetings still happen. The emails still flow. But the faith that this activity means something, that it’s building towards something worthwhile, that it justifies the life hours it consumes—that faith is evaporating.
What replaces it isn’t clear yet. Maybe it’s this parallel economy of people using corporate jobs as platforms. Maybe it’s something we haven’t seen yet. But the transition period—where we all pretend to believe in something we know is hollow—is unsustainable.
Job satisfaction has been trending low for many years, so much so that Gallup calls it “[The Great Detachment](https://www.gallup.com/workplace/653711/great-detachment-why-employees-feel-stuck.aspx)”.
All of us have gone through phases—sometimes months, sometimes years—when we were just tired of our jobs and careers. But at least we had jobs.
I don’t envy the generation that is going to be seeking jobs for the first time in their lives in the medium-term future, say, 5–10 years from now. The AI “wabble” (wave + bubble, heh!) is destroying entry-level jobs while also breaking down well-defined career progressions in sector after sector.
Thus, a college degree is no longer a guarantor of either a great first job or a reliable career.
Then what should it be for?
We discussed this on the [most recent episode](https://the-ken.com/podcasts/two-by-two/what-is-the-point-of-an-undergraduate-college-education-today/) of The Ken’s Two by Two podcast (paywalled for subscribers till 2 October) with Maheshwer Peri, the founder and CEO of [Careers360](https://www.careers360.com/), India’s largest higher education discovery platform, and Abhishek Ghosh, a director with a global pharma major and a career coach who runs[ No Herd Mentality](https://www.noherdmentality.com/), a career counselling service.
Here are a few snippets.
Rohin Dharmakumar: Mahesh, you had the words “demographic dividend” in your Linkedin bio, which I just read out, and the title of this editorial is,[ “India’s Demographic Dividend as a Time Bomb](https://www.thehindu.com/opinion/lead/indias-demographic-dividend-as-a-time-bomb/article69985845.ece)”. You’re also a parent. You have two sons. Your younger son is just leaving for the UK to study law. What are your views on this as an educationist, an entrepreneur, and as a parent?
Maheshwer Peri: No, I think, to begin with, this assumption that what you study is what you must be is completely wrong. You know, in education, what you’re taught is cognitive abilities, analytical abilities, a bit of math, a bit of science, a bit of history, a bit of culture—all those things come in together. None of us, in my opinion, have studied and are doing what we studied for. Like, I’m a chartered accountant but I’m a counsellor today. I don’t know whether you’ve done journalism as a course.
Rohin: I haven’t. I studied to become an engineer.
Abhishek pointed to “critical thinking” as the most valuable skill.
Abhishek Ghosh: So I think my only advice for youngsters—for my son and for all the other youngsters—is that whatever has been laid down in the past by the adults, including the well-meaning parents, that’s not the template to follow. You kind of look around, make sense of things, and probably start carving out your own path. But as Maheshwer said, I think in the end, it is not only the degree, right? It is also the skill set, the ability, the cognitive abilities that someone is going to gain.
And I think a lot of the premium is now going to be on that because we are entering into a field where an algorithm is going to decide what we are going to do. I think very famously, Yuval Noah Harari had written somewhere that if you are someone who has decided you are going to outsource your life to algorithms, just go ahead and enjoy the ride. You don’t have to do anything. But if you want to have any little bit of control and consciousness in your way of living, and life and career are kind of linked to each other, right? So that is where I feel a person has to now start focusing more on the critical thinking part than the degrees and all. I mean, if someone has good critical thinking, they would figure out their way. But the degree is not going to last for long.
He also had this interesting anecdote about, well, drive, hunger, and ambition.
Abhishek: But just a small digression here. I worked in China for a couple of years. I had an American-Chinese boss. When we used to do recruitments, he used to have a pile of applications. He would always give me a bunch and say, “Okay, call them.” I used to scroll through those, but I never could figure out what the key differentiation was. So I asked him one day, “What is it that you look for, so that I can do it next time?”
He said: “Look at the pin codes.” And the pin codes excluded people who were from Shanghai, from Beijing; and they were from the inner provinces. “The students have that hunger, they have more merit, and they want to make a place.”
I became aware of the concept of “dummy schools”.
Abhishek: I think we have all become prisoners of our own systems. I was speaking to a friend of mine who lives in the US. Exactly what Mahesh was saying—from ninth grade onwards, what they perform in their classes, their extracurricular activities, their involvement in things beyond academics, internship, et cetera—all of that is also factored in as part of the weighted average. I mean, the percentage along with the exams. The universities over there also take that into account. I think there is a motivation for students also to go and attend school.
Just two days ago, because of Ganesh Chaturthi, we were downstairs in our apartment and my son met his friend and I was casually asking him, “Which school does he go to?” He said, “Dummy school.” For a moment, I didn’t catch it. I said, “Sorry, what?” And he very casually said “dummy school”. And then I understood, okay, like he has enrolled in a school which he doesn’t go to because he’s preparing for something.
Rohin: So, it’s not actually a school called “Dummy School”? I thought, like there’s a book series called “C++ for Dummies,” there’s also a “Dummy School.”
Abhishek: That’s what even I thought first. I mean, this has become so regular.
Rohin: Oh, it’s normalised because he understands that it’s a pointless thing where he’s just enrolled.
As a professional, a journalist, and a parent, I’m deeply interested in college education. How should we value it? How should we evaluate it? How should we learn to rethink it, after unlearning what it used to mean for so many decades?
Hi Ishan, it’s Tanim here.
When I was young, I couldn’t wait to grow up and enjoy my freedom—to do “adult” things like buying my own clothes or deciding what to eat. Now that I actually have to do them, they feel like chores. And childhood activities I once thought were silly, like colouring, have become one of my favourite ways to spend free time.
The same goes for reading. I always assumed I would graduate to big, serious books as an adult, but a good picture book brings me just as much joy today. Ten years ago, I would have rolled my eyes at the idea—picture books were for “little kids”, after all. But a fun one doesn’t just engage your mind; it soothes and delights your eyes. Bite-sized and brilliant, they feel like little desserts for the soul.
I never had the luxury of being read a bedtime story, though I have always been a little jealous seeing it in movies and shows. What I did have, though, were nights spent sneaking books under the blanket, reading until dawn. I would read anything but my school books. In fact, come exam time, our bookshelf was locked to keep me from distraction, and trust me, that’s exactly when I wanted to read the most. That was my childhood with books.

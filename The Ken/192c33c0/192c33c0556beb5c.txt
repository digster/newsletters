India needs to add some urgency to its TB and breast cancer eradication missions. The country’s health authorities could take some cues from their own success in eradicating Polio 
 By Ruhi Kandhari https://the-ken.com/writers/ruhikandharigmail-com/

Good Morning Ishan,

You are reading our subscriber-only newsletter Inciting Incident—a weekly commentary on brand narratives and storytelling 

Among the most common criticisms of storytelling I encounter is that stories only earn attention, but don’t drive any real change. Many professionals believe that the art of storytelling only factors into writing catchy headlines or engaging advertisements, but isn’t very effective at persuading large groups of people to do the right thing.

To this criticism, I say: sure, when your narrative isn’t compelling enough, its effects are shallow. But that is not the case if you craft your story with the intention of influencing behaviour on a large scale, and then execute it well. In fact, the right story can even make evangelists out of your audience. It is this latter outcome that creates a disproportionately high impact for leaders, corporations, and governments. 

This month, though, underscored two instances of poor narrative building by India’s public health system. And that in the face of major mushrooming crises. 

India has been known as the Tuberculosis (TB) capital of the world for a while now. And although it has claimed that pulmonary TB will be eradicated next year, it is nowhere near meeting this goal. 

Here’s the BBC reporting https://www.bbc.com/news/world-asia-india-67647054 on the state of progress in this area earlier this year:

In 2018, India set for itself the lofty goal of eliminating pulmonary tuberculosis (TB) by 2025 – five years ahead of the deadline set by the UN’s Sustainable Development Goals. In March 2023, Prime Minister Narendra Modi reiterated this commitment at the One World TB Summit, held in the northern city of Varanasi.

But the World Health Organization’s (WHO’s) Global Tuberculosis Report paints a different picture – every two minutes, one person dies of the disease in India. According to the report, India accounted for the highest global TB burden, with 27% of the 10.6 million people diagnosed with the disease in 2022. The country is also home to 47% of people who developed multi-drug resistant infection which is unresponsive or resistant to at least two of the first line of anti-TB drugs the same year.

Last weekend, during the 79th National Conference of Tuberculosis & Chest Diseases (NATCON, 2024), held in Jaipur, experts from all over the country reported that even available drugs aren’t reaching all the patients who need them. 

Then, there’s Breast Cancer Awareness Month, which is celebrated every October. Doctors in India have been reporting an increasing number of breast cancer diagnoses among women, especially young women, and this month, they recommended extending screening protocols to the vast majority of women (even healthy ones) in order to improve the rate of early diagnoses, which significantly improves the chances of successful treatment. 

Now, contrast the lack of success that TB and breast cancer eradication campaigns have had in India with the Polio eradication mission, which accomplished success 13 years ago. Dr Hamid Jafari, Director of Polio for the World Health Organisation’s Eastern Mediterranean Region, recently wrote an article https://www.emro.who.int/polio-eradication/news/polio-free-india-it-seemed-impossible-until-it-was-done.html on what made India’s anti-Polio mission effective, and it has a lot to do with how a compelling, urgent narrative aligned all parts of the government and civil society behind a common goal: 

Taking a step back to reflect on this journey, there were several factors contributing to the monumental success of ending polio in India. The first fundamental factor was government commitment that consistently translated into diligent administrative action at the operational level. Even the district administrators were fully aligned with and committed to taking corrective measures based on evidence: accepting programmatic gaps and challenges and then committing to addressing them urgently. 

Polio-free India: It seemed impossible until it was done—by Dr Hamid Jafari

Perhaps TB and breast cancer eradication is a bit more tricky to implement than Polio, which was essentially a vaccination campaign. 

But that doesn’t take away from the fact that both the tuberculosis and breast cancer eradication missions need far more urgent narratives driving them than what’s in play right now.

Many missing ingredients

The first and most basic unit of trustworthy narratives are actions that match promises. After all, as the old saying goes, actions speak louder than words. 

When it comes to pulmonary TB, though, India’s government has been all over the place on the details. For instance, in December last month, the country’s health ministry said https://www.business-standard.com/india-news/no-shortage-of-anti-tb-drugs-in-country-six-months-stock-available-govt-123121301182_1.html that there was no shortage of TB drugs, with stocks available for at least six months:

“There has been regular supply of Anti-TB drugs to all the States/Union Territories from the central level under the National TB Elimination Programme (NTEP) throughout the year and regular assessments are conducted to evaluate the stock positions at various levels, from central warehouses to peripheral health institutes,” the ministry said in its response. Addressing an official event at All India Institute of Medical Sciences, New Delhi on Wednesday, Union Health Minister Mansukh Mandaviya said that the government has initiated healthcare reforms in an effort to make India TB-free by 2025.

However, during the NATCON event, doctors admitted that drug availability is actually a serious problem. 

Let me quote Dr. Vijay Vinayak Chavan, Technical Referent at Médecins Sans Frontières (Doctors without Borders):

“Despite the inclusion of imipenem-based salvage regimens in the national guidelines for DRTB treatment, the drug’s unavailability in public health system under National TB Elimination Program (NTEP) continues to limit patient access. Collaborative efforts, such as MSF’s partnership with NTEP in Mumbai’s Govandi area, show that solutions are within reach. However, ensuring access requires public health authorities to prioritize procurement and scaling up of these critical treatments for DR-TB  patients who urgently need them.”

Many publications have reported https://www.thehindu.com/opinion/editorial/gross-mismanagement-the-hindu-editorial-on-tb-drug-shortages-and-indias-national-tb-programme/article68047044.ece on this accessibility issue by now. And in April, Deccan Herald wrote about https://www.deccanherald.com/india/stockouts-and-setbacks-tb-patients-face-the-consequences-2987470 the consequences for India’s TB patients.

With India accounting for 27% of the world’s TB disease burden, the impact of hitches in the daily dose of TB medication can have devastating consequences for the lakhs of people in active treatment. This could cause relapse of symptoms, magnify the incidence of drug-resistant strains of TB and increase the chances of the spread of these strains, say medical personnel, health functionaries, social workers and activists.

The second element of trustworthy stories is accountability in the face of criticism. In the case of TB elimination, denial has been the default first response from India’s health authorities. 

In 2022, The Ken wrote about https://the-ken.com/story/how-indias-hiv-programme-became-a-sideshow-to-its-tuberculosis-control-efforts/ how HIV patients, who are the most vulnerable group for TB too (especially drug-resistant strains), had to toil for years to convince authorities about the magnitude of the problem.

In line with the pattern, India had once woken up to the threat that none of the drugs worked on some patients in the case of TB, way back in 2011. The cases were detected in a private sphere by Zarir Udwadia, a senior chest physician at Mumbai-based Hinduja Hospital. “The arc of behaviour was similar. Local authorities first slipped into denial. It has taken over 10 years to accept that there is a big TB problem and work towards addressing patients’ issues,” said the senior health ministry official quoted above.

The result? India has struggled to meet most of the targets it set for 2023, as detailed in the India TB report https://tbcindia.mohfw.gov.in/wp-content/uploads/2024/10/TB-Report_for-Web_08_10-2024-1.pdf:

Apart from matching actions with words, and full accountability, compelling narratives also need systems and structures that are geared towards executing the vision. 

In India, although the government started the National Cancer Screening programme in 2016 to regularly screen the general population, it has not systematically reached out to most women. A research paper that evaulated https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10041275/#:~:text=Introduction&text=As%20part%20of%20the%20Ayushman,cancer%20screening%20program%20in%202016 the efficacy of the screening programme recently noted that the proportion of women and men who have undergone screening for cervical, breast, and oral cancer was only about 1% in India. 

Which is why several doctors have started pushing for better https://www.firstpost.com/health/experts-stress-early-detection-lifestyle-and-technologys-role-in-breast-cancer-survival-13827175.html screening initiatives, and taking the message directly to women to create more awareness. Firstpost has captured https://www.firstpost.com/world/experts-call-for-early-detection-lifestyle-changes-as-breast-cancer-cases-surge-among-younger-women-13828878.html many such voices in its piece today:

According to the ICMR’s National Cancer Registry Programme Report 2020, India is projected to see a 12% increase in cancer cases by 2025. Current trends suggest that the total number of cancer cases could rise to approximately 1.57 million by that year, with breast cancer accounting for an estimated 200,000 cases, or 14.8% of all cases among women.

Dr Anshuman Kumar, Director of Surgical Oncology, Dharamshila Narayana Superspeciality Hospital said “Breast cancer, once largely associated with metro cities, is now alarmingly common in rural areas, with cases being reported even in young, unmarried females as young as 18, often at stage 4.”

There is a lot that India’s public health authorities can learn from their own successes in eradicating polio.

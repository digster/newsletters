Once IPO-bound and valued at over US$5 billion, the healthtech is now a tale of dubious business practices

E-pharmacy startup Pharmeasy is under the spotlight for its “unethical” ways of upselling alternative medicines and supplements. Former employees of the company allege that they were given daily targets to sell private-label products because they yielded higher margins. The company started forcing its employees to hard sell products two years ago, after the acquisition of Medlife. Now, amidst a funding winter and valuation cuts, it’s in a race to achieve profitability—by hook or by crook. India’s quick-commerce firms, including Zepto, are combining Amazon’s packaging efficiency and the delivery speed of Domino’s to make their experiments viable

Blinkit’s expansion into non-grocery sectors is one of the key factors behind it outpacing its parent company Zomato. Its rivals—Zepto and Swiggy Instamart—too, have expanded into products like smartwatches, lipstick, makeup, and toys. But the challenge is to deliver everything, from apples to coolers, to your doorstep in under 10 minutes. And these platforms are doing everything they can to master the art of managing their warehouses and optimising delivery in complex Indian cities. ‘Every payment aggregator should be scared of Phonepe’ https://the-ken.com/story/every-payment-aggregator-should-be-scared-of-phonepe/?utm_source=weekly_email&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=weekly_recap_card

Those were the words of a rival fintech about Phonepe’s barely-year-old payment aggregator. Why has it got some in the industry worried?

As a one-year-old payment aggregator, Phonepe has achieved in one year what took others three, or even five. Payment aggregation, with seasoned players like Razorpay and Cashfree, is a crowded and highly commoditised space. But Phonepe’s nearly 50% market share in the UPI segment is becoming a huge draw for merchants. With insights into its user behaviour, Phonepe’s pitch includes a higher success rate for UPI transactions. Gaurav Munjal wants to save Unacademy by doing that very thing he once ‘hated’ https://the-ken.com/story/gaurav-munjal-wants-to-save-unacademy-by-doing-that-very-thing-he-once-hated/?utm_source=weekly_email&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=weekly_recap_card

To survive the edtech downturn, Unacademy changes its own DNA for its offline centres to do well

Unacademy cut its losses to survive the edtech downturn by sharply reducing its burn, slashing employee count by half, and more. As its online business nosedived, it turned to offline coaching—which it once vowed to never touch. Now, offline is 50% of its business. From heavily discounted fees to celebrity endorsements, the startup is making bold moves to counter its competitors like Allen and Aakash. Industry experts are now questioning if its price points are sustainable in a tightly contested offline edtech market. A rising wave of engineering students is gravitating towards courses focused on artificial intelligence. It is to the benefit of (almost) everybody

AI/ML is all the rage among computer science and other engineering students, and academic institutions are starting to take note. Top-tier IITs are setting up AI and data science departments. IIT Madras, for instance, is gearing up to welcome its first batch of BTech and MTech students this year. But computer science scholars are waving a red flag, warning against AI/ML dominating the field at the expense of other key subfields. The buzz is for a “conscious uncoupling” of AI/ML and traditional computer science.

Good Morning Ishan,
The stock-market regulator is listening. Last week, Sebi took decisions that will make Indian markets, already attractive for listing, even more attractive. Startup founders and investors will benefit directly and indirectly.
As I cheer this move, I’m also wondering how the regulator will curb the valuation froth and (operational) inefficiencies that are often a feature, and not a bug, in many startups and MSMEs opting to go public.
Anand’s story today on Ola Electric underscores this bug. Less than a year after its Rs 5,500 crore IPO, the company is raising Rs 1,700 crore in fresh debt.
We’ve been reading about its scooter sales dip and mounting losses, but now the company’s cash flows have also turned precariously negative. Just in FY25, the group’s cash flow from operations stood at a negative Rs 2,391 crore—nearly four times worse than what it was in the year before.
Yesterday, on 23 June, Ola Electric’s stock slid nearly 6% to hit a fresh low following a block sale, the second such deal in a month. That investors are losing confidence would be an understatement. Its own auditor made a note in the company’s FY25 filings last month.
The fresh debt raise could mitigate the situation by refinancing existing debt obligations, but that’s not enough. “Ola Electric Mobility is increasingly finding itself in a deeper hole than ever before,” writes Anand in his [story](https://the-ken.com/story/ola-electric-is-a-mess-it-could-get-much-worse/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter) today.

Good Morning Ishan,
 
It’s been a while since we have written about OYO. A little over a year, in fact. And if you are a long-time reader, you would have lost good money on a bet that The Ken would go 15 months without writing about OYO. If you are a new reader, our Narratives section on OYO will show you why.
 
Nonetheless, we are back with a story on OYO today. A metamorphosed OYO. Or is it a reverse-metamorphosis?
 
You see, pre-pandemic OYO was spreading its wings not only across the length and breadth of the globe but also across various businesses. At one point, it claimed to be the third-largest hotel chain in the world and had a cloud kitchen arm, a weddings business (however that ran), and other whatnots. The constant rumblings from disgruntled hotel owners—about being fleeced by OYO on myriad hidden charges—were a distraction at best.
 
But by 2019, the rumblings had grown into a crescendo, only amplified by the pandemic the next year. All said, nearly 60% of OYO’s business was wiped out.
 
Since then, the hotel aggregator has been transforming itself into a travel technology company, which is what it started out as. It plans to eventually use tech for things like self-check-in, virtual assistance, and even to order room service. Ultimately, says one employee, OYO wants to use tech to control and optimise the five legs of a hospitality business—demand, supply, financing, inventory, and pricing. *cough*Microsoft investment*cough*
 
It even wants hotel owners to switch to using its revamped app, partly to mend fractured relationships. The promise is of full transparency on the contracts, commission rates, and dynamic pricing (which the owners can now choose to ignore)—you know all the things that owners claimed OYO played fast and loose with. And the company is even settling pending cases with owners, just to get them on board again.
 
But once bitten, twice shy! Will hotel owners trust the new tech-savvy OYO, or is it a case of “the more things change, the more they remain the same?” Aayush has that story for you, wonderfully balancing the nuances of tech and relationships.

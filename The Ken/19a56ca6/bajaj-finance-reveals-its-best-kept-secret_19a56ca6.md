A weekly newsletter about how finance is getting supercharged by tech in India, and how you can make money work for you.
Good Morning Ishan,
Bajaj Finance, India’s largest non-bank lender, made a rather curious announcement on Tuesday.
A full week before its second-quarter earnings, it sent out a press release about how festive things were. It said that during the 35 days between Dussehra and Diwali, it had disbursed 27% more consumer loans to fund the purchase of products like electronics than it did last year, and acquired 2.3 million new customers.
Over half of them, it added, were “new-to-credit”. That is, people who’ve never borrowed before (read: without a credit history).
Now, if you follow Bajaj Finance, this kind of specificity won’t surprise you. It is famously detail-oriented. It is one company that treats “number of service requests” on its app and web to “number of UPI handles” as worthy of investor attention. But “new-to-credit” hasn’t usually been one of those details in quarterly presentations.
Even when Bajaj disclosed it before, it was tucked away as part of financial inclusion goals—the part where companies say Look, we have a heart.
That’s because Bajaj is not the kind of lender you imagine happily giving out loans to people with no credit history. The risk, as any lender will tell you, is too high. The new-to-credit segment is where dreams of financial inclusion meet the harsh reality of defaults.
Non-banks typically lend to those new to credit when they don’t have a choice. The best borrowers end up taking loans from banks and top NBFCs like Bajaj at low interest rates. So NBFCs that don’t have the cushion of capital end up with this segment charging them higher interest rates to account for the higher default risk.
In fact, for personal loans under Rs 1 lakh, NBFC fintechs (think Navi, Kreditbee, and Fibe) have a higher share of new-to-credit borrowers at 13.3% compared to 9% for other non-banks, according to a September report by CRIF, a credit information bureau.
While fintechs tend to have a higher appetite for risk, Bajaj historically does not. Internally, the company has always said it would rather give up growth than choose the other.
Still, it turns out that nearly half of Bajaj’s new customers actually have no credit history at all. It begins small with them lending about Rs 25,000 at a time. And if those customers end up making their loan good, they become part of Bajaj Finance’s big cross-sell franchise.
So why talk about them now? Maybe because the stakes are higher. For one, the company wants to show that the government’s efforts to cut GST are having the desired effect.
The real test of this, though, will be in Bajaj Finance’s upcoming Q2 results to see if this truly worked.
In any case, the non-bank has a couple of things going for it in this segment.
For one, it is doubling down on partnerships to find more of them reliably.
Earlier this year, Airtel [said](https://www.reuters.com/business/media-telecom/indias-bharti-airtel-offer-bajaj-finances-loan-products-2025-01-20/) it would start offering Bajaj loans through its app and, soon, through its stores as well. That gives Bajaj access to 375 million Airtel customers. A good portion of them might also be first-time borrowers.
Then there is the account aggregator system, which is transforming how lenders judge risk. Thus giving Bajaj the confidence to get adventurous with this cohort.
Account aggregators, licensed entities under the Reserve Bank of India, let users share their financial data securely with banks and lenders. In plain English, they let Bajaj peek into your bank account (with your consent) to see how much money you have, and when.
As The Ken [wrote](https://the-ken.com/story/sachin-bansals-loan-offer-take-money-let-navi-peek-into-your-bank-account-for-years/) earlier, lenders typically take permission to auto-debit your account on repayment day. And if you miss a payment, the barrage of calls and house visits begin. Instead, by using account aggregators and taking consent to monitor your account, now they can just scoop out the funds when they arrive, thanks to that auto-debit permission you granted.
No wonder the system has become popular. Account aggregators now enable 50–80% of disbursements for many NBFCs, according to Sahamati, the industry alliance. Bajaj Finance, naturally, is among the biggest users.
Still, this does not take away from the fact that new-to-credit cohorts show greater delinquency. They are about 2–3X higher delinquency than mature portfolios, said Ritesh Srivastava, CEO of debt-relief platform, Freed. This, even though risk metrics may be gradually improving through tighter underwriting (aided by aggregators) and regulatory recalibration.
Bajaj’s own losses in this segment are around 15–20% higher than in its main portfolio—say Rs 1.20 lost for every Rs 1 on regular borrowers. It is manageable when you are Bajaj Finance, diversified and disciplined. But for smaller lenders, that is how you blow up.
So when Bajaj makes this kind of disclosure regularly, it might want to add a small disclaimer at the bottom: The following stunts were performed by professionals under expert supervision. Do not try this at home.

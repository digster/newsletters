Good Morning Ishan,

If there is one thing that ObamaCare, with its attendant hope, despair and political one-upmanship, brought to people outside the US, it is this: an understanding of health information exchange. So, when we got a whiff of India’s own version of such an exchange, we followed it up close.

We briefly wrote about it last https://the-ken.com/why-backdoor-push-towards-ehealth/ January.?utm_campaign=The%20Ken&utm_source=Email&utm_medium=Newsletter And since May, when the health ministry floated the first request for proposal which went through; yes, seven revisions, we’ve kept an eye on it.

Now, when the government has finally picked a builder, we bring you the news. Pune’s Persistent Systems and Connecticut’s (US) IMS Health, are going to jointly build India’s first health information exchange, starting with 10 states. Persistent has built the health exchange for Louisiana in the US and is confident that with IMS’ help, it can build the platform for India.

Starting in March, it has 12 months and three key deliverables. One of which is predicting a health emergency. Impressive, isn’t it?

“We wanted to go as fast as possible to develop the platform and use more time to create bridges across existing information systems, which will happen in phases,” says a ministry official. Well, the aggressive timeline is one thing (and there are others) at which the IT biggies like TCS, Infosys and Wipro baulked, and chose to stay away from the bid.

Not having proper health data in real time has led the govt to be caught with its pants down several times—from mini-epidemic outbreaks to drug shortages. A working, efficient exchange could address many such issues. The ministry is yet to make a formal announcement, but Ruhi says the whole idea looks pretty top-down.

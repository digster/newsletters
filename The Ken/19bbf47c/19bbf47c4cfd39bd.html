The fixed deposit deserves a Budget break... for parity with debt mutual funds

The Union Budget this year will be presented, like before, on 1 February. That’s a little more than a fortnight away. This time, a Sunday budget means many weekend plans will have to be rejigged. Speaking of which, a rejig is also what the humble fixed deposit deserves on 1 February.

It gets a lot of bad press for its modest returns not beating inflation. But the good-old fixed deposit remains the go-to investment for millions of Indians. Never mind the surge in equity investing in the country since the pandemic. Never mind the fall in deposit interest rates over the past year or so. Fixed deposits are simple to understand and safe for the most part. Many, especially senior citizens, value that. 

You get what you see in fixed deposits. Or do you?  

Actually, you don’t. Taxes often take away a good part of the fixed deposit’s interest, and so the returns you get post-tax often lag inflation. Trouble is, this tax trouble is accentuated by laws that give fixed deposits a raw deal, compared with their rival—debt funds.

Level the playing field

In 2023, with shock-and-awe amendments just a couple of days before that year’s Budget was to get Parliament’s nod, the government largely leveled the playing field between fixed deposits and debt mutual funds. This, it did,  by a wholesale scrapping of the tax concessions that debt funds enjoyed. So, since then, gains on debt funds are taxed at investors’ slab rates, similar to interest on fixed deposits. 

Yet, a crucial difference remains. Gains on mutual funds, including debt funds, are taxed only at redemption. On the other hand, interest on fixed deposit instruments—whether issued by banks, companies, or the post office—are taxed every year, whether or not you receive the interest every year. This makes a big difference and is among the key reasons why many financial planners don’t recommend fixed deposits—as mentioned in The Ken’s story “Where to invest Rs 1 lakh, Rs 10 lakh, Rs 1 crore,” earlier this month.

Tax deferral can improve the returns and the attractiveness of fixed deposits considerably by allowing compounding to work its magic fully. Tax deferral essentially means that you pay taxes on your interest income not immediately, but on a future date. 

Now, it’s all right to tax interest on fixed deposits yearly if the interest is actually paid out to the investor yearly. But there is little logic or fairness on taxing interest on cumulative fixed deposits annually where the interest itself is not paid out annually, but accumulates and is paid out only when the deposit matures—say after three or five years. Here’s an example on how it hurts investors.

Say, you invested Rs 5 lakh in a cumulative fixed deposit for 3 years at 7% per annum compounded annually. If the interest is taxed, say at 30%, at the end of the 3-year period, you end up with post-tax interest earnings of Rs 78,765. On the other hand, if the interest is taxed every year at 30%, you end up with Rs 77,160 at the end of the 3-year period. This difference of about Rs 1,600 is because compounding did not happen on the tax paid every year. The difference, seemingly small, is nothing to scoff at. It increases with increases in the invested amount, the time the money is invested for, and the interest rate.

Not only does annual tax on cumulative deposits diminish the benefit of compounding, but it also leads to cash outflows for tax without cash inflows from interest. This is unfair and can be a financial burden, especially for those who depend largely on such interest income.

Now, investors have a choice to pay tax on their interest income on cash basis—that is, when it is received, or on accrual basis—that is, every year when it is due but not yet received. On the surface, it would make sense for the investor to follow the cash basis for interest income on deposits. 

But this is easier said than done. Again, the trouble is, you guessed it, the tax rules. They require banks and other deposit takers to deduct tax at source (TDS) at specified rates while crediting interest above certain thresholds. So, the interest may not be actually paid out, but tax would still have been deducted.

Once the TDS has been cut by the bank, say at 10%, and reflected in the records, the taxpayer would have to pay the remaining tax in that year. Alternatively, the TDS amount can be carried forward to future years and set-off when the interest income would be offered to tax on cash basis. Meanwhile, the TDS anyway loses the benefit of compounding. In any case, all this carry-forward-and-set-off gymnastics is too complicated for most investors and taxpayers to implement. 

Besides, accrual accounting is followed for many other incomes in tax filing, and shifting to cash accounting for interest income can be tedious or even unfeasible. 

The easier, fairer and more elegant solution is for the Budget to amend the tax laws. To the effect that interest income on cumulative deposits and similar instruments is taxed not yearly but only on maturity. And also that banks and other deposit takers do not cut TDS on such interest yearly but only on maturity. 

Will the powers-that-be really level the playing field this time around? It’s high time.

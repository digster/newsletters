Good Morning Ishan,

Private equity (PE) has a reputation for snapping up businesses and making profits the star of the show. The Ken has previously written about how that’s played out in healthcare and education. Today’s story focuses on a PE-backed challenger in the fashion industry, specifically innerwear.

Boston-based Advent International took control of mass-market men’s innerwear brand Dixcy Scott and intimate-wear brand Enamor in 2019 and merged them into Modenik Lifestyle in 2021. Advent wanted Modenik—under the guidance of Executive Chairman Sunil Sethi—to take on Page Industries, the Rs 46,000 crore juggernaut that holds the India franchise of Jockey.

However, as Nuha learned in the course of her reporting, instead of a perfect fit, it turned out to be a bit of a loose stitch.

“From getting to know the nuances of a women’s bra, we were asked to now also sell chaddi–baniyan [knickers–vests],” said a sales manager at Enamor. It’s not as easy as it sounds due to culture clashes and differences in the sales strategies for the two brands.

As a result, the two brands’ annual growth rate has come down to just around 9%, half of what it was pre-merger. That leaves Modenik caught between Reliance’s powerhouse brands like Zivame, and Jockey’s upmarket push.

Unsurprisingly, Sethi had to exit Modenik this May. Nuha’s well-reported story captures how the company transformed under his tenure—and where things went wrong.

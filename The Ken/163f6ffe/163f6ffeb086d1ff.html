“Move fast and break things” is no longer Facebook’s motto (instead, it’s the decidedly unsexy “Move fast with stable infrastructure.”). But perhaps “Never forget,” should be.
 
Two years after the Indian telecom regulator banned Free Basics, its controversial attempt to offer “free” Internet services to Indians (naturally, including its own products), Facebook is back. And it has clearly learnt its lessons. 
 
Gone are the full-page newspaper ads, the indignant self-sponsored Facebook surveys and ads, and its take-no-prisoners approach. Instead, it's partnering with multiple smaller partners and letting them think that they’re driving the agenda.
 
But here’s what hasn’t changed—Facebook’s ambition to become the undisputed driving force behind affordable Internet access for hundreds of millions of Indians who don’t yet have access to high-speed broadband. (Over 70% of leading mobile operator Airtel’s customers are still on 2G networks. Rural internet users account for just 132 million out of India’s overall 445 million netizens.)
 
Facebook’s new bet is “Express WiFi”, a sprawling plan to aggregate tens of thousands of WiFi hotspots across India by partnering with mobile carriers, ISPs and local entrepreneurs. Multiple business models and use cases are being planned—recharge coupons, home WiFi, cafe access, etc.
 
But why? 
 
Could Express WiFi be a subsidised Trojan Horse to reach out, map and target tens of thousands of retailers and small businesses? Aka, advertisers? Or is it a data play around consumers? This week's free story by Shashi lifts the lid on what Facebook is planning.

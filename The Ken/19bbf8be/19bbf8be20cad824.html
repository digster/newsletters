Cow dung, stubble, stover, haulms—these and more are all crucial to natural farming, a mode of cultivation that uses traditional methods to grow food. It’s also a “subject of national importance”, according to the Indian Council of Agricultural Research. So important that the council wants 70 universities to make it part of their undergraduate programmes.

To their credit, the schools are moving fast. Their incoming students, over 50,000 of them, will be able to take these new courses by the next academic session. The great thing is that this aligns with the current direction of the pesticide and fertiliser industries. 

“There is hardly any new chemistry globally. So, all the agri-input players, both around the world and in India, are focusing on organic and bio-based products,” said Kamlesh Saxena, a former executive at Rallis India, an agri-chemicals company and Tata Chemicals subsidiary.

All of this feeds into the goal of making India a major food exporter. Many countries, such as those in Europe, have strict limits on chemical residues on crops. Successful applications of natural farming could help Indian exporters clear that barrier.

But obstacles remain: India’s farmers habitually use more pesticides and fertilisers than needed, and few students see agriculture as an enticing line of work. Atul details these conflicts in today’s story. It’s an essential read for anyone who cares about the food that ends up on their plates.

Big Basket thought 10-minute grocery delivery was too small a market. But by the time they realised it wasn’t, Zepto had already taken it. 

Now Urban Company, which is a listed, profitable giant spanning beauty, cleaning, repairs, and more, might be facing the same playbook. Snabbit and Dazzle just raised millions and are going after Urban Company’s most profitable categories: household help and beauty services.

On this week’s Two by Two, hosts Rohin Dharmakumar and Praveen Gopal Krishnan sit down with Arpit Agarwal of Blume Ventures to break down what’s happening.

The episode digs into why startups keep winning these battles, what Urban Company should do next, and whether Snabbit’s wedge strategy can work. Also, why “cooks in 11 minutes” might not be as crazy as it sounds.

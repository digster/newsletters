At the time, the introductory email I wrote used the word jugaad (which roughly translates to 'necessity is the mother of invention') to describe how they were fighting the odds to design, source parts, and get funds to build ventilators.
It's unfortunate I have to use the same word to describe how some of the companies went about certifying their products and winning tenders.
Shreedhar's explosive story today lifts the veil on these practices, some of which will leave you baffled, and some which ... well, make up your own mind.
Government-owned HLL Lifecare issued a tender on 27 March for 20,000 ventilators, with delivery by the end of April. Noida-based AgVa won the deal to supply half of them.
How could it not, given the specifications in the tender matched those of its own ventilators? Not only that, but these specifications were inserted as amendments to the original tender. And made public only on 18 April, giving other firms no chance.
Then, there's certification. The HLL tender required the ventilators to be certified by the United States Food and Drug Administration (FDA). AgVa has a certificate from a third-party firm that says it is FDA-certified.
Except that the FDA certifies products, NOT companies, and has not certified any Indian-made ventilator. Shreedhar dug into that rabbit hole and traced AgVa's certificate down to a firm with a UK address that had a "for sale" sign. The firm isn't even registered in the UK.
Another certificate AgVa has again traced back to a UK firm, registered this time but on a London PO-box address it shares with 47,000 other companies. Not only AgVa, but other Indian medical companies too have such "international certifications".
And that's only the start. Shreedhar's story is filled with rich, original reporting and proof that, pandemic or not,

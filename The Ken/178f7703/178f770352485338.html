When it comes to public market firsts and records, the answer is usually found in the US or China.
 
Alibaba, for instance, held the world’s largest IPO when it raised a staggering US$25 billion after listing on the New York Stock Exchange in September 2014.
 
More recently, companies like Silicon Valley-based Slack and Sweden-headquartered Spotify boldly paved the way for direct listings—a method to go public without the pressure of an IPO.
 
For SPACs—the current hot topic for public market listings—the trailblazer is not in America, China or even Europe… it's Southeast Asia.
 
At this point, most of our savvy readers will be well aware of Grab’s impending listing through a SPAC from US investment firm Altimeter Capital.
 
It’s a record: not only because the deal values Grab at just shy of US$40 billion (higher than any other SPAC prior), but also because Grab stands to raise some US$4.5 billion in capital.
 
To say that there’s a lot riding on Grab’s deal is an understatement.
 
It’s carrying its own future, the weight of Southeast Asia as a startup destination, and, more broadly, the reputation of SPACs as a whole.
 
More than ever, investors are pondering whether Grab’s stock can get into gear and go full throttle (well, you must have expected some automotive puns.)
 
In today’s story, Nadine and Jon assess the answer to that question using something we’ve not seen before: Grab’s own financial data.
 
That’s right. In announcing the deal, some light was finally thrown on Grab's financial figures, with claims of being Ebitda positive in its mobility business. These numbers could draw parallels with other mobility players like Uber and India’s Ola, and allow us to gauge Grab’s viability as a ride-hailing business.
 
As with all anticipated product launches, there are disappointments, too. Grab's other businesses in deliveries and financial services aren't forecast to be Ebitda positive any time soon.
 
With this SPAC likely to set the tenor of other impending SPACs, today’s story addresses important questions on Grab’s valuation, sustainability, and growth. Do you think the SPAC deal will pop or tank when trading begins, and why? Do let me know.

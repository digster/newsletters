Good Morning Ishan,
 
Did you know that big charities need an exit, like any investor?
 
At least the world’s largest charity does. It became apparent to many when the non-governmental world went agog with the Global Goalkeepers Award which the Bill and Melinda Gates Foundation (BMGF) bestowed on the Indian Prime Minister for his Swachh Bharat Mission last month.
 
It was the culmination of a years-long effort to make an ally of a man seen as critical to BMGF’s continued success in the region. It was also time for Ruhi to do that long-pending story. Because the last few years have not been frictionless for the Foundation, as far as the government relationship goes. (It wanted to award the PM in 2017, but it received internal pushback.)
 
How can you leverage private money to catalyse public funding. That’s BMGF’s objective in a nutshell.
 
Even though BMGF perhaps spends more on India than any other country, the per-person spending is meagre. It came to 21 cents in 2017. What can you achieve in that?
 
The Foundation works at fixing both demand and supply—improve the purchasing power of buyers of medicines as well as the margins of their producers. Now that strategy is being put to the test as the demand side solution will run out by 2021. The $500 million help through the global alliance will dry out. So the BMGF needs the Indian government to adopt whatever it sets in motion and take things forward. In other words, the philanthrocapitalist needs an exit. And remember, it didn’t have a good one with $338M in HIV funding a decade ago.
 
It’s a complex story which Ruhi has meticulously reported over months. You must read it here to understand how the “impatient optimist”, as Bill Gates likes to call himself, has impacted Indian healthcare. And what might be down the pike. It’s all here, and it's free: https://the-ken.com/story/bmgf-vaccines-india-govt/

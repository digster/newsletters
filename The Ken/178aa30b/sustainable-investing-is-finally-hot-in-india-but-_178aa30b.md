Climate and finance have had an uneasy relationship. In a seminal step 10 years ago, London’s little known Carbon Tracker Initiative launched a global movement with its “Unburnable Carbon” report on fossil-fuel divestment. Today, funds and endowments have committed to divest nearly $50 trillion.
For a decade, the debate raged on if investors would lose money in this shift. It is settled now. Findings from a BlackRock investment analysis for its clients show “no investors found negative performance from divestment; rather, neutral to positive results”.
What’s good for climate is good for finance.
Sustainable investing is finally hitting Indian shores. On 25 March, markets regulator Sebi announced a new format for ESG—environmental, social, governance—disclosures by listed companies, allowing for more granularity and comparison with global benchmarks.
Till October 2020, India had just three ESG mutual funds. Since then, six new funds have been launched. It’s the ‘E’ in ESG that is driving the responsible investing boom in the pandemic times. The $1.3 billion in assets in Indian sustainable funds may look like a blip on the massive global trend—there has been a 50% rise in the assets to $1.7 trillion in 2020—but they won’t remain a blip for long.
In his benchmarking story today, Seetharaman writes, “You’d be forgiven for thinking that by choosing ESG funds, you’re putting money in a bunch of clean power producers, electric vehicle companies, and lithium-ion battery makers. Things are a lot more complicated than that, especially if you’re in India.”
There are few large cleantech companies. Secondly, India’s top 20 profit makers account for 90% of all corporate profits. As a result of this and more, ESG scores or ratings sometimes seem to have little or no bearing on the funds’ decisions—FMCG and tobacco giant ITC and oil-to-telecom conglomerate Reliance Industries are two fascinating examples.
Today, there is a glaring disparity between India and the global trends. It’s also mostly a matter of box-ticking. But as Nilesh Shah, MD of Kotak Mahindra Asset Management Company, says, “In a few years, there will be nothing called ESG because everything will be ESG.”

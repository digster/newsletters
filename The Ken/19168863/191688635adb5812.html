Good Morning Ishan,

Ageless Bollywood superstar Amitabh Bachchan recently offered avuncular advice to a young couple who were bickering over processing delays in their loan application. He suggested they approach an account aggregator. They did just that, stopped squabbling, and lived happily ever after. At least, that’s how it played out in the commercials being run by the Reserve Bank of India (RBI).

The central bank, arguably the country’s most feared regulator, is currently running a public awareness campaign called ‘Jaankar Baniye, Satark Rahiye,’ which translates to “Become knowledgeable, stay alert”.

The account-aggregator framework certainly needs more publicity. Launched just about three years ago, these newly created entities have only started picking up steam now. Licensed by the RBI, these aggregators enable businesses—such as banks and lenders—to exchange financial information pertaining to their customers after users’ consent.

All this slashes the running around, paperwork, delays, and bickering. That’s a big win.

But maybe the RBI should make users read the fine print before signing the consent line. Because, the devil, we know, often lies there. No pun, only fun, intended.

Navi Finserv, founded by Flipkart-fame Sachin Bansal, is among the lenders using account aggregators in a big way to monitor loans taken by its borrowers. But as Arundhati, the author of today’s story, finds out, Navi is going much beyond the usual information fetching and monitoring—into the realm of deep, and almost real-time, surveillance.

The four-year-old non-bank has armed itself—with users’ consent—to see what’s happening in its users’ bank accounts every single day for as long as eight years. That’s two Olympic seasons. Some account aggregators find this “excessive” and “unethical.” Some have refused to play ball with Navi, but many others have no such qualms. All’s fair in love and lending, or so it seems.

Now, why is Navi going so far and deep? Are business compulsions forcing its hand? Is it in its DNA to go all out for an advantage? What does such intensive, relentless probing mean for Navi’s borrowers? Do they know what they are getting into? Should the RBI emphasise ‘Satark Rahiye’ (staying alert) more than ‘Jaankar Baniye’ (becoming knowledgeable)? Does Navi run the risk of drawing RBI’s famed and feared ire?

Arundhati’s sharp story today gets you all the answers.

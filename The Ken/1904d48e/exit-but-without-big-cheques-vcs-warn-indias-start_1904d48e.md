Good Morning Ishan,
It had to happen.
Venture capitalists are making up for discarding due diligence and ignoring corporate governance over the last few years by coming at it now with renewed vigour.
They are tightening terms related to information rights and founder exits, and introducing new clauses.
For example, if Bharatpe’s co-founder Ashneer Grover were to depart the fintech due to its various corporate-governance lapses, his 9.5% stake, currently valued at Rs 2,000 crore (US$240 million), would drop to just Rs 95,000 if one were to consider these new exit clauses.
But this kind of belligerence is new to founders. In a recent survey, nearly 180 startup founders said investors paid closer attention to their businesses in 2023 compared to earlier.
“It feels like we are under a microscope,” said a co-founder, whose growth-stage company secured a series-F round in 2023. “I want the investor to trust me.”
Unlike the highs of 2022 dealmaking, investors have all the time to enforce these terms. But this is leading to delayed deals and, worse, one in every 10 deals are falling through, finds out Vanita, the author of [today’s story](https://the-ken.com/story/exit-but-without-big-cheques-vcs-warn-indias-startup-founders/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter).
In a way, this story captures everything that was missing in 2022.

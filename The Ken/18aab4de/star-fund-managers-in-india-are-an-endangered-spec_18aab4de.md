Good Morning Ishan,
In 2020, months after she took charge as CEO of Franklin Templeton, Jenny Johnson said: “Clients get nervous by a star manager, if you appoint one, because then it becomes a succession issue.”
Franklin Templeton is no ordinary company. It’s one of the largest investment managers in the world and was home to celebrated fund manager and emerging-markets guru Mark Mobius.
Seen in this context, Johnson’s comment was anything but offhand. Her disenchantment with high-profile fund managers is now playing out in India too.
Franklin’s Santosh Kamath and Axis Mutual Fund’s Jinesh Gopani resigned last month. They followed in the footsteps of HDFC Mutual Fund’s Prashant Jain, who stepped down last year.
The reasons for their exits may not be the same, but it’s increasingly hard to avoid the elephant in the room: funds that track an index instead of being actively managed by an individual.
The assets under management in passive funds have surged 8.5X in five years to $84 billion, or one-sixth of the total mutual-fund industry.
“It is humanly impossible to beat the index for long periods with large AUMs. This is structural and has nothing to do with individuals,” said a former fund manager. “That’s why index funds are becoming so large globally.”
But active funds still continue to draw investors, so why are fund houses becoming averse to star fund managers?

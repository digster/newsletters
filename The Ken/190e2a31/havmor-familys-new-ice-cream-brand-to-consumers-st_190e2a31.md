Good Morning Ishan,
A decent ice-cream base has just five ingredients: heavy cream, whole milk (or half-and-half cream if you’re indulgent), egg yolks, sugar, and salt. Add vanilla bean paste, and you’ll have a batch of the world’s most popular variant of the frozen dessert.
We each have our favourite flavour—and probably a favourite brand. Maybe yours makes you remember treats shared with your family after a fulfilling meal, or it could be a newer ice-cream maker that appeals to your healthy lifestyle.
In any case, today’s story from India appeals to your sweet tooth. Nathan explores the business of ice-cream, specifically by taking a close look at Hocco, whose founder and chairman Ankit Chona is the scion of the family behind Havmor, the Gujarat ice-cream maker that was sold to Lotte in 2017.
It’s fair to say that an attunement to ice-cream runs in Chona’s family, and he built Hocco with a dose of nostalgia. The brand’s products are priced low, and 97% of its sales come from general trade stores. Now, Hocco is pulling in Rs 20 crore, or just shy of US$2.4 million, in monthly revenue.
Investors have taken notice of its success in Gujarat and Rajasthan. Hocco raised US$12 million last month to expand the production capacity of its Ahmedabad facility.
So, go on, if it’s not too early in the day, grab a scoop of ice-cream while you check out Nathan’s [coverage](https://the-ken.com/story/havmor-familys-new-ice-cream-brand-to-consumers-stop-counting-calories/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter) about frozen desserts.
Over in Vietnam, some energy-hungry production facilities have been asked to tamp down their power usage. This is a major snag for any production line that’s making high-value goods and components—smartphones and semiconductors, for instance.
Therein lies the contradiction: the Vietnamese government has been wooing manufacturers to set up shop in its country, casting itself as an alternate location for any firm shifting part of its operations out of mainland China, but the grid can’t fully support production facilities that make high-value goods.
It gets worse when you realise that there are plenty of renewable energy providers in Vietnam; it’s just that the power they produce can’t reach the manufacturers that need it because of grid limitations.
But that may be changing because of a new power-distribution mechanism that was approved by the Vietnamese government earlier this month.

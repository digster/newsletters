Whether you agree with the International Telecommunication Union or not, the importance of broadband access in a nation’s development cannot be overstated enough. In 2010, ITU estimated that every 10% increase in broadband penetration leads to a 1.38% increase in the per capita GDP of developing economies. 

So India decided to build itself a national broadband network. A grand one. The world’s largest digital infrastructure project. One that would lay down an optical fibre network across the length and breadth of the vast country, down to every village that had a population of 500 or more. Over 250,000 villages. 

BharatNet.

That was back in 2011. Seven years and $1.5 billion later, that plan lies covered in the familiar cobwebs of poor planning, tardy execution and endless delays, all woven and held together by those masterful web spinners of India’s economy - bureaucrats. 

Like a dystopian R.K. Narayan novel, Vandana’s first story for The Ken starts from a small village called Tain roughly 50 kilometres from the steel-and-glass urban sprawl of Gurugram, and a signboard that says “Smartpur”.

The need for a service like BharatNet is obvious, its import borne out of the actions of global internet giants like Facebook, Amazon and Google. Google’s initiative is called "Next Billion Users". It aims to get first-time users on to the internet, either through local language support, high-quality internet access or by improving the smartphone experience. Facebook, which failed with its earlier FreeBasics offering, is attempting to do something similar with Express WiFi, where it will provide public WiFi hotspots for internet access. E-commerce major Amazon, meanwhile, is testing a Hindi interface for its mobile site. It would be fair to assume that a large number of users they are targeting are rural inhabitants. 

There is a large, untapped market, and these companies want in.

The plan was for telcos and ISPs to piggyback on BharatNet's broadband infrastructure to offer broadband to their users, by buying BharatNet’s bandwidth instead of replicating redundant infrastructure in small towns and villages.

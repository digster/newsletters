We are back with another Trade Tricks round-up this week, and to kick things off there’s Reliance and its expanding beverages portfolio. 

A couple of weeks ago, Reliance Consumer Products (RCPL) announced a strategic partnership with Sri Lanka’s Elephant House Beverages for the launch of Reliance’s Campa Cola brand in the island nation. This is the second market in India’s immediate neighbourhood that Reliance has targeted; last month, it knocked on Nepal’s doors for Campa too, partnering with the Chaudhary Group to expand operations there. 

Campa’s first foray into the international market came much earlier, though, when in January, it took advantage of the sudden bloom of Pepsi and Coca-cola boycotts that swept the Gulf region over Israel’s Gaza offensive and swiftly partnered with Agthia Group—a leading food and beverages company based out of Abu Dhabi—to supply Campa Cola in markets such as the UAE, Bahrain, Qatar, and Oman. 

The presence of a strong and numerous Indian diaspora—with fond memories for the old Indian brand—no doubt played a part in the decision. 

“This iconic brand holds deep nostalgia for many, and we believe it will strongly resonate with the significant Indian expatriate community in the UAE, and local consumers alike.”

Agthia, CEO Alan Smith

Neighbouring countries where it can leverage its Indian brand muscle, and regions with a large Indian diaspora, aren’t the only markets in Reliance’s crosshairs for Campa, though. Because the RCPL is also planning an entry into Africa—a prime market for carbonated beverages. 

It should be interesting to see what Reliance manages to do in the space, especially considering the reputation for disruption the conglomerate brings to the table. As we wrote in February, global giants Pepsi and Coca-cola have already been given reason to worry about the challenge Reliance poses to their Indian businesses. 

The distributors and retailers on the ground believe that both Pepsi and Coca-Cola are doing whatever it takes to protect their turf from Campa Cola.

Pepsi, for instance, is paying distributors higher margins and retailers more to keep their coolers stocked with familiar red-and-blue labels instead of Campa’s bright orange, The Ken has learnt. But so is Coca-Cola.

Pepsi’s biggest bottler is pouring more cola to fight Reliance’s Campa, The Ken

And it isn’t just Campa and the soda market either. 

Just a week after announcing its new partnership in Sri Lanka, Reliance doubled down at home by buying a majority stake in a joint venture with Naturedge Beverages, a herbal drink manufacturer that sells a zero-sugar range of drinks called Shunya—all in an effort to enhance its status as, according to its press release, a total beverage company. 

Exemption, please

In other news, e-commerce giant Amazon on Thursday appealed to India’s commerce ministry asking for e-commerce exports to be exempted from foreign direct investment (FDI) norms. According to Reuters, however, the meeting was “heated”, with representatives for small traders vociferously opposing the move. 

Currently, India’s FDI norms prevent foreign companies such as Amazon and Walmart from directly stocking inventory and selling them. Instead, they are allowed only to operate as marketplaces, a rule that applies on exports too. Now, however, Amazon has asked the government to exempt exports—which would allow it to buy goods directly in India and sell them to foreign customers. 

India’s small traders aren’t having any of it, though. To quote Reuters: 

Three industry groups backing small retailers opposed any more easing of restrictions for Amazon and Walmart’s Flipkart—which also attended the meeting, the sources added. 

The retailer groups reiterated their long-standing concerns that Amazon and Flipkart have hurt small Indian retailers for years by favouring select big sellers online, and offering discounts, which hurt smaller businesses.

[…]

Amazon said during Thursday’s meeting that exempting exports from the rules would benefit small sellers as the company could help to facilitate customs clearance processes, giving sellers greater access to international markets, three of the sources said.

The Indian government, of course, hasn’t reached any decision, which isn’t surprising considering the political hot-potato the issue has been for years. 

Winding up

Beepkart, a two-wheeler marketplace, has shut down after a four-year stint. The business was structured quite similarly to Cars24, a marketplace for used cars which is now starting to wind down many of its non-core operations, laying off employees, and shuttering many of its physical stores. 

Unlike Cars24, however, which handled refurbishments in-house, Beepkart outsourced them, which led to expenses nearing 10% of vehicle price. Reports say the company could not effectively pass on these costs to its price-sensitive customers. Beepkart also suffered from aggressive and poorly planned expansion, which saw many of its stores cannibalising each other’s business because they were positioned too closely. 

Yet another reminder that while the used-vehicle market certainly exists, it is one fraught with stiff challenges.

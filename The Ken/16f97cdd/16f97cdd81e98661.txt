Good Morning Ishan,

They came from Indonesia. They came from Thailand. They came from England, Delhi, Coimbatore. Some even braved traffic and made it all the way from a remote hamlet called Bannerghatta, on a Monday morning.

All because they had been told: If you build it, they will come.

The double prepositions used—without much enlightening—will probably give my editors acidity in their sleep. 

But I use them for a reason. 

The first 'they' is us. The Ken is now 31-people strong! A scrappy, smart, and lean team of writers, engineers, product managers, and our three polymer founders. We come in all shapes, sizes and hair colours now. 

The second 'they' is You. Last week, The Ken came together at an on-site in our Bengaluru office. We dissected our stories, our strategies, our attitudes, to figure out what we did right, what went wrong, and how 2020 has to be better and brighter than 2019 for all our subscribers. Throughout, You were the 32nd person in the room.

It was an intense 72 hours. The stimulants—to keep us going—were apt too. Two dozen doughnuts, one triple-layer birthday cake, and several hundred coffees later, we're now hungry to sink our teeth into 2020.

I'm not allowed to give away too much. But we're building it, and we ardently hope you will come. The only thing I can reveal is that the DNA that underpins this organisation, and its people, has only gotten stronger—the desire to make sense of the world and tell the stories that matter.

That's exactly what we attempted to do this past week. And it shines through in our stories.

Between 2014 and 2019, Dunzo catapulted from being a noun to a verb. Dunzo started off in Bengaluru as a Whatsapp concierge service but soon grew in size and ambition, transforming into one of India's most prominent hyperlocal businesses. The transformation, though, has been far from smooth. Dunzo posted a 3X jump in losses and has beaten a quiet retreat from delivery pincodes. On https://the-ken.com/story/takes-two-to-dunzo-kabeer-biswas-trusts-popularity-to-drive-profitability/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter https://the-ken.com/story/takes-two-to-dunzo-kabeer-biswas-trusts-popularity-to-drive-profitability/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletterThursday https://the-ken.com/story/takes-two-to-dunzo-kabeer-biswas-trusts-popularity-to-drive-profitability/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter, Abinaya went behind the scenes (and founder Kabeer Biswas' responses) to explain Dunzo's comeback plan. Will 2020 be a fresh start?

On Monday https://the-ken.com/story/google-microsoft-india-pdp-health-data-sharing/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter, Seema brought us the rich and complex narrative on health data, and its potential for improving health outcomes when put in the hands of Google or Microsoft. Hospitals have reams of patient data. Without an overarching Personal Data Protection law (which has been in the works for a long time), the rules for sharing such data in India, for commercial gains, are still in an ethical grey zone. Seema bound together different viewpoints into a futuristic story about India's healthcare industry.

Taking to tech has never been a problem for the food-tech industry. On Tuesday, Nadine wrote about https://the-ken.com/story/foodpanda-to-eat-into-grab-gojeks-share-southeast-asia/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter how Foodpanda is trying to make a comeback of sorts in Southeast Asia after exiting in 2016. But with the reigning duopoly of Grab and Gojek, this comeback is no piece of cake. Nadine explained how cloud kitchens, a new mobile wallet and creating a local research and development team will give Foodpanda a fighting chance to make a successful comeback. 

The long-suffering electric vehicle industry is heading into yet another tumultuous year. Sales for EV two-wheelers plummeted by 94% in 2019. Any chances of a revival are being further gutted by the FAME-II policy, which has pitted low-cost options against expensive, high-performance ones. On Tuesday, I wrote about an impending "identity crisis" https://the-ken.com/story/low-cost-versus-high-speed-ev/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter that faces the two-wheeler industry. The fight between price and performance will determine the future of two-wheeler commute.

We topped the week off with a stunner https://the-ken.com/story/a-trust-deficit-at-tata-trusts/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter. Ruhi, our resident dismantler of big names, was at it again. This time, she took a stab at explaining why India's largest charity—Tata Trust—now has serious tax liabilities. After losing its tax-exempt status in 2019, the Trust now has a Rs 12,000 crore tax bill staring it in the face. By turning to philanthrocapitalism from simple cheque-cutting, Tata Trusts may have unwittingly created a segue to splitting clean from Tata Sons. Ruhi uncovered a ton of juicy details in her piece, including how Ratan Tata was often ill-advised by those closest to him.

That's it for the first working week of the year. It seemed like the longest ever. Partly because it carried the weight of everything that will unfold in 2020. Partly because it took ages to finish a triple-layer chocolate cake. 

Yuuuum. 

Happy New Decade!

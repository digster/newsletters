Good Morning Ishan,
A major Indian electronics maker recently did something most Indian electronics makers still avoid.
After years of assembling TVs and appliances for global brands, it launched its own smartphone. It was a modest product launch, but it cut against the grain of how India’s electronics sector usually works.
The country’s electronics story has largely moved past the question of scale. Companies such as Dixon Technologies, Tata Electronics, and several others have expanded rapidly through joint ventures with Chinese, Taiwanese, and American partners. Output is rising. Capacity is no longer the constraint.
What’s less clear is whether these partnerships are translating into real self-reliance.
Priyal’s story today asks whether Indian EMS players are using JVs as a transition phase, or whether they have settled into a model where design authority, product roadmaps, and intellectual property continue to sit offshore. Even firms that have reached meaningful scale still spend a sliver of their revenue on R&D. Capital keeps flowing into new factories because that is predictable and immediately rewarded.
Her reporting looks at how JV structures shape what knowledge actually transfers, why engineering capability remains hard to build inside Indian firms, and how thin margins narrow choices. It also examines the contrast with China, where companies like Huawei used contract manufacturing as a launchpad for deep R&D investment and product ownership.
There are exceptions. Indkal is one. Zetwerk is another. But they remain outliers.
India has learned how to manufacture at scale. Whether companies like Dixon and Tata Electronics can turn that scale into lasting capability is the question at the heart of [this story](https://the-ken.com/story/electronics-makers-nailed-make-in-india-how-long-before-they-ditch-borrowed-tech/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter).

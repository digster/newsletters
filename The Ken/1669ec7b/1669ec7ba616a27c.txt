Few things are as hard to know as the true cost of what people are buying and selling in healthcare. The opacity is legendary. Unless you’ve been living under a rock, you’d remember the dengue case of Fortis Hospital from November. A family was slapped a $21,782-bill (Rs 16 lakh) for a 15-day hospital stay. Among other things, Fortis had used 660 syringes, at Rs 200 a piece and with a markup of about 1370%.  

Two years prior, a social worker in Delhi compared the cost of a syringe sold at a Max Super Speciality pharmacy and a medical store outside and launched a trade war. India’s competition watchdog looked into it and unleashed a wide-ranging inquiry into the sales practices at large hospitals.

But wait, why are we talking about syringes, the low-cost product which perhaps, on average, an Indian is exposed to thrice in a year? The story of the syringe is the story of most medical devices—disposables, consumables and implants, products where the choice is thrust upon the consumer.

After stent and knee implants, the syringe is perhaps next in line to face a cap of some kind. Both sides, local and international manufacturers, agree that the government must cap the margins, not the price. They disagree on everything else. If the government agrees to intervene by capping trade margins of syringes, how it does so will set a strong precedent for the medical device industry.

Gayathri’s story today takes you inside what began as a crusade by a social worker for consumer rights and morphed into a fierce fight between manufacturers. It’s a fascinating tale of how a syringe is made and priced; it’s certainly not as straight as a needle.

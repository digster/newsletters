Good Morning Ishan,
You guessed it. Today’s story is about the world’s most expensive spice, whose growers, sadly, are getting poorer.
Saffron, the purple harvest from Kashmir, even has a National Mission named after it to preserve and promote its cultivation. But the crop is in decline. In 2024, Kashmir’s total saffron production was 2.7 metric tonnes—down from 8 tonnes in 2011, the central government told Parliament in December.
But it’s also one of the toughest crops to cultivate. For context, it takes 150 crocus flowers to produce a single gram of saffron. Comparing it with a regular spice like cumin, one can get a yield of 600 kg per acre, whereas saffron yields a meagre 1.8 kg.
The Indian government in 2020 gave Kashmiri saffron the Geographical Indication (GI) tag, which certifies that it comes from a particular region and attests to its high quality. This was supposed to help farmers realise higher prices and boost India’s saffron exports. But all this hasn’t really helped. Iran controls 90% of the global market. Could it teach India a few things?
Mehroob Mushtaq and Numan Bhat visited many saffron fields in Kashmir, spoke to farmers, and present a visually-rich ground report on a crop that is crying for a makeover—from a spice for festive occasions to a wellness and beauty ingredient.

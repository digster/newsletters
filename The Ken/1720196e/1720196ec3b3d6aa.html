Remember cell phones?
 
Not not the smartphone you are likely reading this on, but cell phones. The Nokia- or Motorola-made ones, without all the bells and whistles. They were designed to make calls, send messages, and maybe play a game of Snake. Now, your smartphone can do practically everything, except kill the coronavirus. 
 
The point is we haven't noticed this feature bloat because it happened gradually over the years. A simple system that evolved into a complex system, following (systems theorist John) Gall's law: 
 
"A complex system that works is invariably found to have evolved from a simple system that worked. The inverse proposition also appears to be true: a complex system designed from scratch never works and cannot be made to work. You have to start over, beginning with a simple system."
 
The first part of this law is what business-to-business marketplace Udaan followed to the letter to become the fastest Indian company to breach the $1 billion valuation mark. It started with a simple premise: to bring the thousands of small businesses under one platform. "Free" was the user acquisition model that helped it upend established players.
 
So far, so simple. Until May last year.
 
That was when Udaan, which means 'flight' in Hindi, went all barrels blazing with its monetisation guns. It started charging commissions for trades on its platform and for logistics, as well as offering credit. It had designed a complex system from scratch. And Gall's law kicked in.
 
Sellers and buyers were far from impressed, pointing out issues surrounding its commissions, high return rates and poor customer service. They started leaving the platform, while company employees also noted high credit defaults. Udaan recently fired 3,000 employees and has shuttered some categories. The pandemic was merely an accelerant, contracting the company's business by 70%.

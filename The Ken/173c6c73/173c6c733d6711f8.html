Good Morning Ishan,
 
This is an excerpt from a book I read recently. I am not sure if it’s famous, but it should be. 

“The heavier the burden, the closer our lives come to the earth, the more real and truthful they become. 

Conversely, the absolute absence of burden causes man to be lighter than air, to soar into heights, take leave of the earth and his earthly being, and become only half real, his movements as free as they are insignificant. What then shall we choose? Weight or lightness?” 

Then comes this part. The one I really like. 

“When we want to give expression to a dramatic situation in our lives, we tend to use metaphors of heaviness. We say that something has become a great burden to us. We either bear the burden or fail and go down with it, we struggle with it, win or lose.”

When Milan Kundera wrote The Unbearable Lightness of Being in 1982 about the events of the Prague Spring, I’m sure he never imagined anyone using it in a newsletter as a metaphor to describe the position of India’s mightiest corporate in 2020 during its biggest leap into the digital age. 

But that’s where Reliance Industries’ Jio Platforms finds itself. 

You may ask—wait a minute, Jio Platforms just got billions of dollars in capital from some of the biggest companies in the world. What burden could it possibly have (other than finding a few hundred trucks to transfer all that sweet, sweet cash)?

Well, Rohin argues that, thanks to Jio Platforms, Reliance finds itself valued as a global tech giant similar to Facebook or Google, even as 70% of its valuation comes from telecom and oil. Then there’s the fact that according to one analysis, to justify its massive valuation, Jio Platforms will need to deliver 35% compounded growth for the next 10 years straight. 

And finally, there’s the small matter that Jio has never built world-class products. 
 
But Rohin asks in today’s story—does it even need to? What happens if it doesn't?
 
What will Jio choose? Weight or lightness?

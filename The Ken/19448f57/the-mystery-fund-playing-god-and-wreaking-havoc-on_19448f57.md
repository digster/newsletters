In the three years to March 2024, 11 million individuals in India lost a stonking Rs 1.8 lakh crore, or US$21 billion, in the two most common kinds of derivative contracts: futures and options.
As retail investors bleed, funds are making a killing on the other side of the trade in what is a zero-sum game. But how they do so—especially in options, which gives the buyer the right but not the obligation to buy or sell shares for a tiny premium—is raising hackles.
There are clear signs that since April, the options market has been gamed for undue advantage on the days these contracts are settled every week, according to a seasoned options trader. In fact, the wild intraday swing in the Sensex on 13 December points to the role of a foreign hedge fund. The Nifty was no less volatile that day.
The fund could be profiting Rs 500 crore every week with its tactics, the options trader estimates. “If someone can casually move the Nifty by as much as 2% at his discretion, he is nothing short of God in the market,” the trader said. “It’s pointless trying to compete with him.”
Just as the stock-market regulator, Sebi, tries to wean individuals off derivatives, revelations of a fund running amok have enormous consequences.

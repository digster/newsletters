Trade Tricks
Tue, 25 Nov 25
A weekly newsletter about the biggest changes in commerce—focusing on shifts that matter to you.
Good Morning Ishan,
On a smoggy Saturday afternoon in Gurugram, a Bata store in a centrally-located mall was bustling with kids throwing tantrums, grandmothers being cajoled into replacing their 10-year-old sandals with a new pair, and couples enquiring about wedding formals.
I asked a salesperson if they sold Crocs, and he promptly ushered me towards a bright, colourful aisle near the checkout counter that said ‘Bata Floatz’.
“Our clogs are just like Crocs—they’re made from the same material. Lightweight, waterproof, and durable,” he said.
Bata Floatz is one of the few things buoying Bata India’s flailing boat.
The four-year-old sub-brand focused on “casual, washable, and comfortable” footwear—think clogs and slippers—has been one of the [fastest](https://www.fortuneindia.com/business-news/batas-casual-bet-pays-off-floats-crosses-100-crore-in-sales-heads-for-200-crore-mark/124857) to cross Rs 100 crore in the history of the footwear retailer. A history that goes back nearly a century in the country.
While Bata India’s revenue has been stuck at around Rs 3,500 crore for the last three years, Floatz has grown at a little over [30%](https://www.bseindia.com/xml-data/corpfiling/AttachHis/c4e7bd35-73fc-4c15-9554-4ed32b1a27f2.pdf) year-on-year. In fact, managing director and CEO Gunjan Shah has gone so far as to [say](https://brandequity.economictimes.indiatimes.com/news/business-of-brands/bata-hopes-to-make-floatz-rs-800cr-brand-in-5-years/123270729) that he sees no reason why Floatz will not touch Rs 800 crore in the next five years.
Well, there could be one reason. Litigation. Years and years of it.
For the last decade or so, Bata India has been fighting some version of the same case against American footwear retailer Crocs. Its latest salvo: a 14 November Supreme Court judgement upholding Crocs’ right to file a “passing off” suit against Bata India, Liberty Shoes, and others.
So, what does this really mean?
Let’s rewind to 2014, when Crocs first sued Bata India for design infringement in the district court of Delhi, and wrangled an interim injunction prohibiting Bata from selling Crocs’ designs. Court-mandated [raids](https://timesofindia.indiatimes.com/business/india-business/crocs-sues-bata-others-for-design-infringement/articleshow/45609900.cms) across Bata’s various stores followed. But when the validity of Crocs’ design registration itself was questioned, it tried another tack.
In 2019, it filed a “passing off” suit against Bata India, which accuses the retailer of trying to pass off its products as those by Crocs. Essentially, this shifted the issue from design infringement (that falls under the Designs Act 2000) to trademark infringement (under the Trade Marks Act 1999). Trademarks, in this case, could be names and labels, but also the “get-up” of a product—how it looks, its colour, shape, etc.
“A basic test of passing off is you have to be able to prove that a product has become so distinctive that the moment people see it, they think of me,” said a senior advocate with over 25 years of experience in commercial and intellectual-property law. “So, Crocs is essentially trying to say that its design has now become its trademark.”
This doesn’t bode well for Bata Floatz, whose clogs are at the epicentre of this tussle.
Two stores down in the same mall in Gurugram, the Crocs store manager was indignant. “Our Crocs are made from Croslite, which is why they’re so comfortable, while Bata’s are made from EVA foam,” he said, denying the Bata salesperson’s claims.
EVA foam is the material yoga mats are made from. Croslite is a proprietary, modified version of it.
The key difference between the two is price: at Rs 1,500 or so, Bata Floatz’s clogs cost a fourth of Crocs’ Rs 4,500 starting price.
Interestingly, Floatz is part of Bata India’s larger push towards more casual and premium offerings—the words “casualisation” and “premiumisation” repeated like a mantra across the company’s recent annual reports. Floatz’ average selling price is [1.2X](https://www.bseindia.com/xml-data/corpfiling/AttachHis/c4e7bd35-73fc-4c15-9554-4ed32b1a27f2.pdf) that of Bata retail as a whole, and the brand currently makes up a little over [5%](https://www.sharekhan.com/MediaGalary/StockIdea/Bata-Aug08_2024.pdf) of the company’s overall revenue.
Granted, 5% isn’t much. But that’s where Floatz’s rapid growth comes in.
If we assume Bata India continues to grow at a CAGR of 3% for the next five years (same as it has for the last five years), that’s a revenue of around Rs 4,000 crore by 2030. Meanwhile, CEO Shah expects Floatz to touch Rs 800 crore by then. That takes its share in the overall revenue to 20%. Hardly insignificant.
The tides really turned during the pandemic, when clogs, courtesy of their comfort, became particularly popular. In fact, Crocs’ main franchisee in India, footwear retailer Metro Brands, [gained](https://the-ken.com/tradetricks/crocs-is-2-8b-metro-brands-unlikely-secret-sauce/) immensely from its tie-up with the American company. Third-party brands Crocs, Fitflop, and Fila now make up [26%](https://www.bseindia.com/xml-data/corpfiling/AttachHis/04b062a4-08c8-4a1c-99f1-51f5e1f81dfd.pdf) of Metro’s revenue.
“Floatz sell really well, mostly because they’re an add-on purchase,” said the store manager at the Bata store. “Customers usually come in to look at other things, and end up buying slippers or clogs along with it.”
But all this could come to a grinding halt if Crocs succeeds in proving its “passing off” claims against Bata India.
Floatz is, anyway, one of the few bright spots in a company [groping](https://the-ken.com/long_and_short/bata-just-cant-break-free/) in the dark. Over the last few years, Bata India’s gross margins have been stuck at 58%, its share price keeps inching downwards, and it’s being squeezed by premium brands at the top and cut-price brands at the bottom.
“See, it’s close to impossible to predict what courts will do,” said the senior advocate. “All that the Supreme Court has said is that Crocs’ suit survives. Now it has to go fight it out.”
At this point, though, Crocs has a lot going against it. As the senior advocate iterated, it’s very hard to prove that your design itself is your brand.
“You have to prove immense goodwill and reputation, so much so that every consumer looking at this particular kind of clog would recognise it as Crocs,” he said.
Moreover, after a decade of litigation, there are hordes of companies—not to mention hundreds of street-sellers—all selling some version of Crocs’ clogs. How many of them will you stop?
Ironically, Bata India itself suffered a similar plight with its ‘Hawai chappal’. This simple, soft rubber slipper with its iconic blue V-shaped strap became so popular that it inspired several knockoffs and even became a de facto excise-duty category.
“Since everyone started calling their slippers Hawai chappal, the product became generic,” said the senior advocate. Bata tried its best to protect its original product, filing [suits](https://www.casemine.com/judgement/in/56096034e4b01497112cc249) against imitators like footwear retailer Khadims. All to no avail.
That’s how subjective these things are, added the senior advocate. You may be on one side of an issue, and before you know it, flip to the other side.
And now Bata Floatz’s future hinges on this subjectivity.

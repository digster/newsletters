Good Morning Ishan,

If one were to lay out news on a spectrum that measured its proximity to the reader or viewer, then news that’s farthest out often ends up getting all the attention. For example, international geopolitics, national affairs, the lives of celebrities, sports events, business and policy. News that is closest to the user—hyperlocal news—largely gets ignored. 

Because the business model of hyperlocal news does not support the “unit economics” involved. It just doesn’t make economic sense for media organisations to employ full or part-time journalists in every small town, village, and neighbourhood of a large country like India. 

What would be the cost to produce short news items about, say, a young man beaten up by the police in a vegetable market; a pair of thieves furtively making off with a bike in the dead of the night or a cell phone snatcher who tried his tricks with a policewoman?

What if I told you it was Rs 50-Rs 150 (<$2)?

Because that’s what Public, the two-year-old hyperlocal video platform from India’s second-largest news aggregator, Inshorts, pays freelance stringers to upload short news clips on its site.

Public claims a user base of over 50 million Indians organised around the new economics of hyperlocal—one that larger or older media players cannot match. By using its platform to turn its local users into hyperlocal reporters, it hopes to solve for residents’ need for information—schedules of power cuts, news of petty crime in their locality, or in the case of 2021, street-level alerts about quarantine and Covid-19 cases. 

Public isn’t alone either. There’s Lokal and Circle, two rival players. All of them are hoping to harness a potential multi-billion dollar market for regional advertising.

But—and there’s always a but when it comes to hyperlocal news—monetisation and engagement is easier in theory.

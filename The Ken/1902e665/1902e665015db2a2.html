Good Morning Ishan,

Venture capitalists have bankrolled every possible fad in Indian fintech: from peer-to-peer lending to buy-now-pay-later to digital gold linked to consumer spends. This is partly why fintech was the most-funded segment in the startup ecosystem between 2020 and 2022.

But this year, the sector has accounted for just 10% of overall VC funding. If you needed more proof of VCs’ aversion to fintech, you’d just have to look at Lightspeed Venture Partners and Sorin Investments’ latest bet. The two investors put US$11.5 million in Accel-backed Shivalik Small Finance Bank at a valuation of US$100 million.

Yes, a small finance bank (SFB), that’s how boring VCs’ fintech investments have become. How did this come to be? And what’s so appealing about an SFB when there are close to a dozen already? Let’s get to Arundhati’s story.

Our second long-form feature of the day is about an unusual solar-energy producer in the Philippines.

Unusual because Citicore Renewable Energy Corporation went public earlier this month in a US$91 million IPO—the largest by a green-power company in two decades.

Unusual also because Citicore has reached where it is without the backing of a large conglomerate, which defines rivals like ACEN.

Katrina’s story today is about Citicore’s nifty financing strategy that has allowed it to set a target of 5 gigawatts in installed capacity by 2028 from a meagre 0.3GW now.

What Hindenburg?

It’s as if we imagined the colossal damage wreaked by US-based short seller Hindenburg Research’s report on the Adani Group in January 2023. In response to allegations of stock-price manipulation and accounting fraud, the 10 listed Adani companies took a US$150 billion hit to their market value.

But three weeks ago, Adani Enterprises, the group flagship, returned to pre-Hindenburg levels after recouping some US$30 billion in losses. And yesterday, Adani Ports and SEZ, which handles more than a quarter of India’s cargo, became the world’s most valuable transport operator. Soon, it will be the first Adani Group company to enter the Sensex, a 30-stock benchmark index.

Never mind the nosedive these two stocks witnessed when the BJP failed to get a majority in India’s general election. The fact is, as far as investors are concerned, Hindenburg is history.

Unless, of course, the regulator chooses to throw a curveball.

Good Morning Ishan,

You’ve seen my name at the end of these morning emails before. Starting today, you’ll see a different format of our daily briefings, written by the editors of The Ken. On top of telling you about the day’s stories, we’ll share our own takes or tell you about the interesting ideas we’ve encountered. To kick things off, we’re covering an important moneymaker for many businesses—ads.

The 58th Super Bowl is underway in the US now. I don’t particularly enjoy watching (American) football. And, well, I still don’t really know the rules of the game, except there’s a lot of high-velocity physical contact.

But there is one thing about the Super Bowl that piqued my interest whenever a friend or relative invited me to watch the game: I loved the ads. My classmates at college embraced “Wassup!” as part of their personality, and I never thought a love story could be told through a search bar and blinking cursor.

Further back in time, to the year before I was born, there was the superb “1984” ad by Apple, directed by Ridley Scott. Here’s a graphic from the same (Big Brother, often interpreted as a stand-in for IBM, is about to be smashed).

Super Bowl ads live in a strange mental space. Airtime that most people might treat as bathroom breaks has been transformed into magnets for attention. Part of it is the ads’ price tag—US$7 million for 30 seconds—and the hype brings more hype. (It might be worth it since Kantar said Super Bowl ads are more than 20X more effective compared to run-of-the-mill TV ads.) But more importantly, Super Bowl ads are a cultural experience.

Ads on the small screen, however, are a different beast altogether. Meant to grab your attention while you scroll, it’s make-or-break in a split second. In today’s story, Gaurav looks at how Swiggy is aping Amazon’s ad strategy in India in hopes of getting a step closer to profitability, and that might be the most meaningful move that the company can make.

As one former employee who spoke to us put it: “Unlike something like customer experience, which yields results after a quarter or a year, ads are something that give you results from the day it is launched.”

However, this ambition to unlock ad revenues has inevitably become a part of its endless tug-of-war with restaurants—especially the smaller ones.

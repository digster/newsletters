Good Morning Ishan,
“I don’t really understand what’s the point of all this anymore.”
A few weeks ago, I met a good friend of mine. Let’s call him Rajesh. Now, Rajesh, a senior product leader at a startup, is the person you’d usually describe as… sorted. Smart chap. Varied interests and hobbies. Easily one of the most creative people I know. He jumped into Product Management a long time ago for similar reasons that I did—it was a sanctuary for people who derived meaning not just from launching things and moving metrics but also offered them paths for creative expression.
And now Rajesh was telling me that he had decided to quit his job and take a break. He had just gone through an excruciating few months, battling crisis after crisis at his startup. One week, some numbers went up on a dashboard. The next week, some other number went down on another dashboard. Then the next week was spent firefighting some crisis because someone had screwed up something while building some product a few quarters ago. Then another week was spent analysing some roadmap and execution, and in review meetings. By this time, the month was finally over, and his salary had arrived.
If all of this ennui sounds familiar, it’s because it’s what thousands of smart, intelligent people are experiencing in startups all over India right now. For over a decade, startups used to be the place where people went to do exciting, groundbreaking, challenging work that gave them a high—mostly because it gave them meaning to solve fun problems in creative, unconventional ways. I remember what it was like when startups suddenly became glamorous. I’d gone into it earlier when it wasn’t that “sexy” or cool, but very soon, I started getting calls from friends and B-school batchmates who were doing banking, consulting, and finance at large companies and looking for something fun and interesting to do.
All that has changed now. For months, all the stories I hear have a familiar tone to them. I’ve met several senior executives who have quit their jobs and are on “a career break”. Many more are in the process of quitting and are negotiating sabbaticals. I’ve heard stories about founders and co-founders of some of India’s biggest startups who confess in private, closed circles that they no longer feel much joy or satisfaction from what they’re doing—but they see no option but to continue running on the treadmill. Folks who are much younger in their career, often in their 20s, are throwing all their energies, creativity, and focus not in their work, but in retiring from work. Not to get a safety net to pursue work that they love, but simply to retire. Most of these people compensate for this lack of purpose by endlessly consuming content on podcasts, Youtube, and Instagram that exclusively promises a positive, optimistic future—from India becoming an economic powerhouse, or Artificial Intelligence (AI) changing the world, or stories of founders, or simply financial independence.
There’s an entire generation of people inside startups who are weary, tired, and see no purpose. These are smart, talented, ambitious people, who were once excited and found meaning in work. Now, they are either rudderless, or simply coping by consuming fantasies predicting the future, or about the past.
So, who killed the meaning of work?
Personally, I blame the venture capitalists (VCs).
Essentially, VCs came along and changed how companies work, grow, and define success. This effectively changed how founders and CEOs of startups found meaning in their work—which has now percolated all the way down to everyone in the organisation.
I’ll illustrate this using three examples, across three different companies.

Where were we?
Oh, yes, how VCs killed the meaning of work.
It all began with something that has always bothered and confused startups: VCs want to be two things at the same time—financiers and motivational advisors. Personally, I think this specific duality is significantly responsible for half the problems we’re facing today.
Because they don’t pick a side, they can play both sides. There’s a big difference between saying, “We make big-risk investments into startups and our goal is to make lots of money” vs. “We want to make lots of money, but we are also founder-first, and care about the growth story, and supporting the ecosystem”. The latter sounds better, but it also forces VCs to do a bunch of nonsense they know nothing about—like starting podcasts and writing long threads sharing their perspectives about growth. Most importantly, they spend a lot of time crafting and selling a vision of what success means for startups and the ecosystem itself. I’d argue that the world would be a much better place if VCs just act like bankers who give out loans.
This duality of purpose allows VCs to do things as it suits them.
As an example, here’s something I read last week.
Hospitality giant Oyo is in talks with Incred to raise $80-90 million from family offices, sources have told CNBC-TV18. The move comes after the company decided to shelve its IPO plans and withdrew its draft red herring prospectus (DRHP) filed with SEBI on May 17. The development was first reported by The Arc and has now been independently confirmed by CNBC-TV18.
Sources added that Oyo is seeking a valuation of $3–4 billion, which is a down round of nearly 75-77% of its peak $10 billion valuation back in 2019. A majority of the capital raised will go towards clearing Oyo’s debt in a bid to achieve profitability each quarter.
Oyo in talks to raise nearly $90 million; withdraws IPO papers: Sources, [CNBC TV18](https://www.cnbctv18.com/business/companies/oyo-in-talks-incred-to-raise-80-90-million-dollar-19415853.htm)
As long-time subscribers of The Ken know, Oyo is not just any other startup. In fact, we’ve been closely covering all the weird and strange things that Oyo was up to for a long time now. It’s a long story, but the short version goes something like this:
- Oyo raised a lot of money from Softbank and other VCs at a sky-high valuation.
- Then in 2019, Ritesh Agarwal, CEO and Founder of Oyo, borrowed US$2.2 billion from a consortium of Japanese banks, led by Mizuho and Nomura to buy more equity in Oyo—partially buying out existing investors like Lightspeed and Sequoia (now Peak XV).
- Oyo’s valuation went up, and there were talks of it going public at a valuation of US$18 billion.
If you are thinking, ‘how is any of this normal?’, you are right—it’s unusual. In fact, I wrote about it [extensively](https://the-ken.com/the-nutgraf/ritesh-agarwal-and-the-missing-850-million-dollars/) back in 2019 and described Ritesh Agarwal as a magician. It’s somewhat dubious to take a sizable loan to buy shares in a company, by placing the same shares as collateral. It’s even more dubious when you manage to convince hardened Japanese bankers to write-off a big chunk of the loan. I don’t have any illusions about the responsibilities that Oyo and Ritesh Agarwal bear for its current financial predicament.
But what about the responsibilities of Oyo’s early investors? Do Lightspeed and Sequoia see themselves as also responsible for Oyo’s current situation?

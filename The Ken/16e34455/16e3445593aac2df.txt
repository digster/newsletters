But our curiosity was piqued when KKR-backed Radiant Life Care, a chain of just two hospitals, closed a deal in June to buy 49.7% of Max Healthcare, one of India’s largest chains with 14 hospitals. The promoter will be left clutching at 1% by the year end, a sharp drop from the planned 7% earlier.

It’s a marriage of convenience between unequal partners with different operating philosophies. Max’s revenue for the financial year ending March 2018 was 40X that of Radiant’s in the same period. Yet, Max has been stuck in the slow Ebitda lane for years.

In late October when Ruhi visited Max, the air of a foundational shift and a fear of layoffs hung heavy on the premises.

Max founder Analjit Singh became overambitious about expanding after his nephews put Fortis Healthcare on steroids to make it 43 hospitals strong. While Singh wasn’t unscrupulous like his nephews, he was less interested in turning a profit. He also leveraged his assets to take out debt, later used in viticulture, winemaking and real estate assets overseas.

The Max Healthcare deal was a distress sale, say many in the industry. How the KKR-Radiant combine, led by Soi, KKR’s trusted lieutenant, is turning it around is a fascinating story in Indian healthcare. Because Singh and Soi are practically chalk and cheese.

Results are visible as well. Max’s margins are at a 2-year high. Soi has promised KKR he’d double Max’s Ebitda by 2022. He believes Indian hospitals are undergoing a generational change and can’t expand for the sake of expansion. Soi has a tonne of tricky decisions to make, amid a list of 218 items where he thinks he can reduce cost.

Ruhi has a gripping story today. (The rumour is KKR, and by extension Radiant, is interested in scooping up another prime property in Delhi—Medanta Medicity.)

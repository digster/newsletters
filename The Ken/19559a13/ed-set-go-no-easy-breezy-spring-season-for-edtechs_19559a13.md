Ed Set Go
Mon, 03 Mar 25
The one stop to understand the biggest shifts in education, written by the best edtech journalist in the country..
Good Morning Ishan,
I hope you’re enjoying the beginnings of spring/summer (depending on wherever you find yourselves). Edtechs, too, are trying to sprout new paths to success. For some, like Byju’s, it’s retribution season.
In a Linkedin post titled: “I am the Byju’s of BYJU’s, and I am here now”, Byju Raveendran, the beleaguered founder of the once-sprawling edtech company, came out swinging against the lender group that sued him in a US court for non-payment, and worse yet, fraud. The post came on the same day a judge in Delaware court sided with the lender group, a consortium called the GLAS Trust, and [held that](https://www.business-standard.com/companies/start-ups/us-court-finds-riju-ravindran-camshaft-think-learn-guilty-of-fraud-125022801414_1.html) Byju’s US subsidiary Alpha had indeed defrauded the lenders by “hiding” its stash of cash and transferring it out of the company:
The Order finds that the Defendants, alongside Byju Raveendran, are responsible for orchestrating and executing an unlawful scheme that defrauded the Lenders. Damages are to be awarded at a later date.
In making its ruling, the Court confirmed that the multiple transfers of funds from BYJU’s Alpha constituted actual fraudulent transfers and conversion (i.e., theft). The Court also confirmed that Riju Ravindran violated his fiduciary duties as a director of BYJU’s Alpha.
Curiously, an email from a “whistleblower” landed in my inbox only a few days before the order and the Linkedin post came out. The email alleged collusion between the GLAS Trust and EY, the consulting firm involved in the insolvency proceedings back in India. Raveendran’s post, which references the same email, has since been taken down.
It’s unclear if any investigative agency, yet, is looking into these allegations of collusion. It’s also unclear whether that impacts what happens to the ruling in the US. The court’s order seems to imply that Byju’s may have to pay up or face a penalty for violating the law.
Maybe Byju’s spring will come from an actual round of spring cleaning within what’s left of the company: sell the American companies, pay off the debtors, and start afresh from Raveendran’s perch in Dubai.
Applyboard gets real about its valuation
If you’re familiar with the edtech conference circuit, you’d always find one smiling face headlining sessions: Martin Basiri. The Iranian-Candanian entrepreneur is one-third of a trio of brothers who launched Applyboard, a study-abroad firm, in 2015. In 2019, Applyboard opened a fairly large office in one of Gurugram’s tech parks. The idea was to be closer to the base of students they served. Also, it was cheaper to run operations out of India.
Applyboard has good brand recall amongst edtech circles partially because Basiri is a recognisable face. But the company also boasts of some decent investment, 60,000 students they’ve connected to opportunities in the US, UK, Canada and Australia, and till recently, a valuation of over US$3 million.
This valuation, recently, was slashed by 74%, according to a financial analysis by a Canadian investment firm. The company has called it a “moment in time” and reiterated that their business is solid. But the challenge of moving global human capital, especially with the current turmoil in geopolitics, is going to persist.
Applyboard’s problems stem from the caps on visa approvals put by the Canadian government to root out fraudulent colleges, and also stem the flow of immigrant students into the country. Applyboard’s other major study destination is the US, where there’s a huge political and cultural backlash against the immigrant population, which is unlikely to die down any time soon.
For study-aboard firms, the challenge is going to be finding new destinations that are also attractive to students. For instance, Applyboard has just started operating in its first-ever non-Anglophone territory, Germany. But given the rise of the far-right in Europe too, there’s always a risk that newer destinations don’t stay as stable as expected.

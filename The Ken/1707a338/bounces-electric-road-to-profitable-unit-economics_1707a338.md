“Kitna deti hai?”
Roughly translated from Hindi, it means “What’s the mileage?” No other question is more atypical of India’s obsession with mileage. It’s a question that’s featured in many an advertising campaign because it doesn’t discriminate between cars and bikes, or hatchbacks and sedans.
Or even between gas guzzlers and electric vehicles.
About a year ago, Mahindra Group’s Automobili Pininfarina unveiled the Battista, priced at a cool $2 million. Chairman Anand Mahindra took to Twitter to talk about the car, and inevitably somebody asked, “Sir, kitna deti hai?”
Pat came the reply: “Sirji, electric hai. Shock deti hai! (Sir, it’s electric. It shocks.) “
Going electric is just what Bounce, India’s largest two-wheeler ride-sharing operator, hopes will shock it out of a never-ending cycle of losses. The red ink hasn’t been for lack of popularity, with consumers or investors. Bounce claims close to 100,000 rides a day and is halfway to a $1 billion valuation.
Its red and yellow scooters are very popular as a last-mile drive, say, from the metro to office or the bus stop to home. The only problem is you’re just a likely to find one lying abandoned in a drain, missing some parts. Or even worse, with an empty fuel tank.
And that’s where Bounce’s plan to make EV scooter comes in. The big win will come from fuel savings and fewer maintenance issues, co-founder Vivekananda Hallekere tells Olina, the author of today’ story. The switch, Hallekere estimates, could bump up Bounce’s real revenue per kilometre by 3X!
“Going electric isn’t just about going green. It makes business sense too,” says one investor. But she adds, “With electric scooters, they’re starting from scratch. It’s like saying a wallet company now wants to lend.”
“Kitna deti hai?” Olina asks of Bounce’s plan today. Is an EV plan shock-proof? (Psst, the plan includes more than EV scooters.)

Maitri – Staff Writer
Halfway through the pandemic, I felt isolated and cut off from my being and my work as I plodded through in a traditional newsroom. At the legacy newspaper, six months into Covid, there had not been one meeting called to discuss how we would plan the trajectory of the coverage. I was their health reporter, churning out at least three stories a day. I was not entirely happy with my job. I loved reporting, but I felt disconnected and dissatisfied. I realised I desperately needed a change of scene. That is when ‘The Ken‘ happened.
Over the next few months, my life changed entirely. I had a new, friendly team that worked fully remotely but was still connected to each other deeply. We had in-depth discussions almost every other day about how we wanted to shape our stories, especially our Covid coverage. Most importantly, we also had no-agenda virtual meetings in the mornings where we would kick off our long workdays playing fun games. In close to a month, I will finish a year at The Ken.
A colleague who’d like to remain anonymous
Work hours are insanely long in most corporate jobs these days. I found that not only from my own experience but also from others I spoke to, who were from similar backgrounds and experiences. As the pandemic rolled around and I was faced with the question of where my life is going every day, I decided that I wanted to work that hard on something I strongly believed in—the cause of subscription business journalism.
Nithin – Newsletter Writer and Editor
It’s hard to find a specific instance that became a catalyst. Usually, multiple instances bubble up slowly and finally cause one to take a step back and think. My catalyst was during a webinar featuring a prominent money manager in India and the US. This was in April 2020. It was just an off-hand, casually thrown quote that’s often used in investment circles: “Buy [stocks] when there is blood on the streets.” While it wasn’t meant to be taken literally, it gave me a little jolt. PMC Bank’s new avatar has a small finance bank hill to climb
There is some light at the end of the tunnel for the depositors of Punjab and Maharashtra Cooperative (PMC) Bank. The bank [loaned](https://www.livemint.com/industry/banking/patterns-behind-the-pmc-bank-meltdown-11570449287851.html) two-thirds of its loan book to one single real estate developer, and that rendered 73% of PMC Bank’s loans non-performing in September 2019. Since then, depositors faced restrictions in taking out their money, leaving millions stranded. The Reserve Bank of India finally [anointed](https://www.businessinsider.in/finance/banks/news/pmc-bank-will-have-a-new-owner-in-centrum-and-bharatpe/articleshow/83639668.cms) a white knight on Friday.
Centrum Finance along with BharatPe got an in-principle approval from the regulator to take over the operations of PMC Bank, and to run it like a small finance bank (SFB).
This makes merchant-payments company BharatPe among the first fintechs to turn into a bank. It’s also ahead of those like Paytm*, which is only a payments bank, and Sachin Bansal’s Navi, still awaiting the regulator’s approval.
Most small finance banks are microfinance institutions that turned into SFBs. But, so far, there haven’t been many takers for this class of banks, though it looks vastly better than its cousin, the payments bank, which can only collect deposits and not lend.
That’s because the small finance bank format is fraught with complications. It is not designed to earn the best of valuations. Something that will be tough to stomach for a venture capital-funded company like BharatPe.
|
The biggest task ahead for the Centrum-BharatPe duo is to restore confidence to the current set of PMC depositors. Though the bank will begin operations with [Rs 500 crore](https://www.livemint.com/companies/news/centrumbharatpe-small-finance-bank-to-have-rs-500-crore-capital-on-day-one-11624032185189.html) (US$67.4 million), it will have to spend to keep its current set of retail depositors and lure more from other banks like other SFBs.
While the Centrum-BharatPe version of a small finance bank is expected to be fully digital, whether that can help overcome the structural issues of being a small finance bank will be something to watch out for.
*Paytm founder Vijay Shekhar Sharma is an investor in The Ken
Adani stocks were part of the index. So what?
The brouhaha over the Adani Group’s shares taking a nosedive this week is slowly dying down. Here’s a tl;dr version of what happened.
Earlier this week, reports emerged that three foreign investor accounts in Adani had been blocked ([BFO#305](https://the-ken.com/bfo/305/)). This was followed by questions on why domestic institutions avoided the stock.

While this was happening, the [debate](https://www.livemint.com/money/personal-finance/adani-group-firms-stocks-are-underweight-in-actively-managed-mutual-funds-11622715033106.html) over active fund management vs passive fund management reemerged. Are mutual fund managers who actively pick stocks really adding value? Or are investors better off with funds that track an index and replicate the same stocks, in the same weights?
Lately, products that just track an index have emerged winners. The money in these global index-trackers (exchange-traded funds) [hit](https://www.theasset.com/article/43945/global-etfs-and-etps-see-assets-break-through-us-9-trillion) a record high of US$9 trillion in May per consultancy ETFGI.
The trend is clear even among The Ken’s subscribers. In a [story](https://the-ken.com/story/zerodha-navi-and-the-new-wave-hoping-to-be-indias-vanguard/) last week, we asked about investment preferences. While a majority of our subscribers have investments in actively managed mutual funds, index-trackers or passive funds are a high priority on the future shopping list.
Regarding the Adani story, this time, mutual fund managers in India who actively decide which stocks deserve to be in their portfolio were quietly smiling away. Most of them hadn’t bought any Adani stocks in their portfolios. They’d avoided the storm simply because the Group wasn’t really a poster child for transparency. And it carried a lot of debt. Top marks to the fund managers. After all, it would have been easy to get pulled into the momentum that carried the prices of these Adani stocks to record highs.
Since fund managers steered clear of Adani, by extension, investors in these mutual funds avoided the storm as well.
But the stocks of the Adani Group are part of the benchmark stock market indices. Adani Ports is part of the benchmark NSE Nifty 50 stock market index. Adani Green, Adani Enterprises, and Adani Transmission are part of the Nifty Next 50 index.
Which means that investors who preferred index-tracking funds weren’t quite as lucky.
But is this really a win for active fund management? And will investors change their mind about index-trackers?
I don’t think so.
See, indices go through frequent adjustments. Stock enter, stocks exit. And it’s based usually on things like how much the shares of a company are collectively worth, how often they’re traded by investors, and things like that. So when a company’s value gets bigger, which could be even due to meme-stock-like rallies, it can get added into an index meant for larger companies. And if the stock is part of an index, that’s a cue for big money from any fund that passively tracks an index. They have to buy it.
Just last month, three Adani stocks [entered](https://www.financialexpress.com/market/msci-india-index-add-6-stocks-adani-enterprises-sbi-cards-removes-zee-entertainment-small-cap-index-33-new-shares-full-list/2250368/) the MSCI India index. Which means big money from global investors too.
It’s a cycle.
If you’re an investor buying an index-tracking fund, you better know that you’re getting the good, bad, and ugly. An index analyst isn’t really sitting there going, ‘okay, this stock meets all the criteria for inclusion, but corporate governance doesn’t look all that great. So I’m going to leave it out’. That’s the job for active fund managers really. It’s why they ask for a higher fee.
The risk of buying an index is that sometimes, a stock enters an index after a massive rally in price. So you’ll end up buying it (indirectly) when it’s already at a high. And then the stock might crack, like Adani did. But active fund managers aren’t immune to this either. They buy stocks at a peak too. And the stock prices fall. You should be concerned if your fund manager frequently gets wrong-footed, but index-fund investors aren’t really bothered about little things like this.
That’s really the beauty of it.
PS: A similar debate recently raged in the US courtesy the meme stock rallies. Think GameStop and AMC. Index-fund biggies BlackRock and Vanguard were the largest institutional shareholders of GameStop and AMC respectively as the market capitalisation of the companies grew bigger and their weights in stock market indices ballooned.
Indian parents can improve children’s immunity. If they commit to it
What did a doctor, a psychologist, and an educator agree on when they met online for a webinar on Thursday?
That Indian parents can do a lot to prepare their children for the impending third Covid-19 wave India expects anytime between August and October. And since there is no vaccine approved for children yet, the medical fraternity and the government are predicting that the third wave may affect more children than adults.
In a webinar organised by the Sportz Village, a Bengaluru-based youth sports platform where paediatrician Aruna Jagdish, clinical psychologist Anagha Pandit, and CEO of preschool-chain Eurokids International KVS Seshasai shared their experience, the consensus was clear. Children’s mental and physical health is the key to improving their immunity, but these two are also the two aspects that are largely ignored by Indian parents in a bid to make them perform better academically.
The panellists pointed out that a combination of adequate sleep, nutrition, and physical activity can improve children’s immunity against the virus if they get infected. However, they also noted that lack of sleep, poor nutrition, and lack of physical activity are all common among Indian children. These problems, they added, have become worse as children have not had access to schools. It has not only affected their academic growth but also adversely affected their physical and mental health, which affects their immunity.
In school, children get four to eight hours of socialising, playing games, and learning. These are things they would have done naturally, but that has changed over the last 15 months, said Pandit. Parents are more concerned about how the pandemic affected the academic growth of a child, but emotional and physical well being is ignored, she added.

The bigger picture on some of these indicators is bleak as well.
The national family and health survey conducted over 2019 and 2020 [found](https://swachhindia.ndtv.com/there-has-been-drastic-rise-in-obesity-among-children-under-5-years-in-20-states-reveals-nfhs-5-report-54336/) a drastic rise in obesity among children under the age of five. For instance, in the central Indian state of Maharashtra, the number of overweight children under five years has increased from 1.9% in 2015-16 to 4.1% in 2019-20.
In the state of Gujarat, it increased from 1.9% to 3.9%. About 13.4 % of children under the age of five were found to be obese in Ladakh, which was the highest among the 22 states and Union Territories surveyed.
Similarly, the national mental health survey, conducted over 2015 and 2016, [pegged](https://www.youthkiawaaz.com/2021/01/mental-health-needs-of-children-and-young-people-in-india/) the prevalence of mental disorders at around 7.3% in the case of adolescents in the age group of 13-17 years. The survey estimated that about 9.8 million adolescents need active mental health care intervention.
Luckily, there is a way to help children in the short term. Physical activity improves nutrition, sleep and mental health, the panellists noted.

“Physical activity leads to more happiness by releasing happy hormones and white blood cells and less stress and leads to better sleep and better eating habits. It is a win-win situation to build immunity,” added Jagdish.
However, even though parents tend to prioritise physical activity for younger children, they put a lot of pressure to perform academically in their teens. “Change can only happen when parents value it. The parent/community needs to demand physical activity for 9th/10th standard children. This is the greatest challenge,” said Seshasai.

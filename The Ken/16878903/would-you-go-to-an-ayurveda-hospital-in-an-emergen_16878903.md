I have to tell you about this article.
It's an article published in a journal called 'Progress in Preventive Medicine' a couple of months back. It's not even that popular. Less than 15 downloads in the last month. I am pretty sure one of them was Ruhi, who wrote today's story.
Coming back. The article is about a study. Over 350,000 patients were involved. Nearly 82% of them had chronic diseases, which they had experienced over a year. The study was to test the efficacy of a particular treatment method. Several people were involved - most notably Mr Samir Brahmachari, the former director of the Council of Scientific and Industrial Research (CSIR). The study found out that over 76% of the patients received complete or partial relief, and less than 1% reported an aggravation of symptoms as a result of the treatment.
You may ask, "So what?". Especially if you are anything like my boss, Rohin.
Two things.
The patient data collected in the study was through a company called Jiva. Who mostly treat their patients via teleconsultation.
The method of treatment being tested was ayurveda.
Imagine that. Ayurveda via teleconsultation. The old and the new. What's next? Pranayams via Alexa? Ancient history lessons via WhatsApp groups?
But still. Seventy-six per cent.
Our story is about that company - Jiva. Jiva is not just trying to crack ayurveda, it's working to do it at scale. Our story begins in India's first and largest teleconsultation centre - where Jiva doctors field thousands of calls every day. The company is unique because it's looking at ayurveda differently. It sees it as a whole - from preventive care to wellness and as a way to complement allopathy. And to do this, Jiva had to do a lot of things differently. Some are pathbreaking - like standardising the offering and treatment methods, instituting a rigorous hiring and training process for doctors, and creating a database of Indian foods for analysis. Some sound...unusual, like Ayurbaby, a program on their app that advises couples on conception based on guidelines laid out in the Vedas. Jiva wants to do all this without falling into the trap of becoming another spa that does alternative medicine. It intends to make Ayurveda mainstream. It intends to make it legit.
How does it expect to get there?

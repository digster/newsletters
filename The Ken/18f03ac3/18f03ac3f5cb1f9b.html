Good Morning Ishan,

Tata Consultancy Services (TCS) is banking on a little sleight of hand to give itself cachet among job-hunting, fresh graduates in India. Every new hire flows into one of three brackets—Ninja, Digital, or Prime. Those are nifty labels, right?

Intrepid jobseekers may ask what the requirements for each category are. As it turns out, there aren’t any differences from before, and TCS’ evaluation process remains the same. The three new categories are just a marketing gimmick, a change in name only.

That move is the result of the headcount of India’s largest employer dipping by almost 13,000, a first in nearly 20 years. As one source told Nathan, the writer of today’s story, “The industry must face a harsh truth—IT is no longer sexy.” Yet, TCS wants to hire 40,000 people in the next 12 months. 

Nathan’s report starts with the facelift of TCS’ recruitment strategy but, more generally, looks at the conditions that India’s young jobseekers must contend with. This all points to a mismatch between what employers in the IT space are willing to offer and the problems that truly afflict the sector.

Trading places

Over the weekend, The Wall Street Journal ran a story about major banks leaving, well, Wall Street. It was a slow withdrawal that started after 11 September 2001 and then sped up during the pandemic.

The centre of American finance—and a nexus of global investments—is a shadow of its former self, at least physically. Wall Street banks don’t need to have a presence there anymore. Imagine the strange feeling of walking among historic art deco buildings expecting to see hustle and bustle, but encountering sign after sign reading “For Rent.”

Over in Asia, a directionally similar shift is taking place in some major hubs. Take Hong Kong as a reference: for different reasons than those in New York, there’s a corporate retreat, with the number of global companies operating in the city falling year over year. New firms—overwhelmingly from mainland China—are gradually moving in.

There are loads of question marks about how the fabric of these finance hubs and others will evolve or be redeveloped in the future. The only certainty is that the culture of these places has already changed immensely in just five years.

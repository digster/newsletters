Dear Ishan, another week’s gone by, and here we are again. These newsletters have started to acquire a deeper meaning for me for the last few months. For those of you old enough to remember (IYKYK and all that), it’s like this is my weekly hard disk “defragmenting” process.

It’s what allows me to reflect, reorder, and restore my fragmented thoughts every week through a discipline that is enforced on me. A sort of voluntary-mandatory process at the intersection of silent reflection and public thoughts.

And because of the structure of this newsletter (you know, the personal and whimsical opening, followed by the more structured reasoning), I’m forced to reflect on multiple things each week—personal and professional.

Are you nudging me or am I nudging you? Who can tell.

But let me ask you something: what is the group or music album you dream of sinking into, in high-fidelity?

Once again, perhaps this is an age thing. I was born in the 1970s, so growing up as a kid, I did not have access to either variety or quality (audio). The word “stereo” entered my lexicon in the early 1980s, with a Japanese Technics music “system”. It had a discrete cassette receiver https://spencertified.com/products/technics-m24-stereo-cassette-deck, pre-amp https://audio-database.com/TechnicsPanasonic/amp/su-c01-e.html, and metallic bookshelf speakers https://www.vintagechief.com/technics-sb-f2-speakers-21978. One of my uncles who worked in the merchant navy would bring back cassette tapes every now and then. I got introduced to artists like Dire Straits, Donna Summer, Depeche Mode, and Stars on 45 through those tapes.

Somewhere towards the late 1980s we moved to CDs, and a stacked Sony hi-fi system paired with larger speakers. I have fond memories of listening to a Queen CD album over and over again through that setup.

College was filled with mostly terrible PC speakers and mp3s of varying bitrates and suspect provenance. Sure, there were also lossless formats like FLAC, audiophile grade sound cards, and (shudder) “5.1” audio setups.

To cut a long story short for now, it would take me a couple of decades to get to my first “proper” high-fidelity setup. It was nothing fancy. A CD player hooked up to a Marantz stereo amplifier and a pair of bi-wired Acoustic Energy floorstanding speakers. But boy, they sounded the sweetest, perhaps it was because they were bought with my own hard-earned money.

Sadly, the world transitioned very quickly to low quality (bitrate) music streamed over the internet. We chose convenience over quality, beauty, and the quiet solitude of being able to sink into some music in high-fidelity.

I, too, gradually shifted away from stereo amplifiers and floorstanding speakers to Apple Music and Apple Homepods.

I forgot about what we gave up. Until I ended up being recommended an album by Apple Music this week.

Pink Floyd’s Dark Side of the Moon.

And the first thought in my head as I started listening to it was, “I gotta listen to this in a quiet room, sunk into a comfortable chair, with my eyes closed.”

The story of how that album triggered a nostalgia for quality music in me is for another day. I think many of us have memories of when and how we heard immersive, ambient, or enveloping music in our youth, through whatever we defined as a “proper sound system” back then. Those moments and memories are still buried in our heads.

For now, what songs or albums could do that to you? Write in and tell me.

1. Listen to your cognitive dissonances

Chances are sometime this week, or perhaps the week before, you encountered some cognitive dissonance. Maybe it was mild and minor, or maybe it made you uncomfortable enough to ask yourself, “What sort of a person am I?”

Me, I run into the full force of it almost every other week.

“Why am I not doing what I said I would? What sort of a person am I?”

“Why did I behave this way when I had told everyone else to do the opposite? What sort of a person am I?”

“How can I do this even though I don’t believe in it? What sort of a person am I?”

Welcome to Cognitive Dissonance https://thedecisionlab.com/biases/cognitive-dissonance. In keeping with our music-themed intro, let’s call it CD?

“Cognitive dissonance is the psychological discomfort we experience when we hold two or more conflicting beliefs, attitudes, or values simultaneously. To resolve this tension, we often change, justify, or ignore the conflicting information.”

[…]

According to cognitive dissonance theory, we can experience psychological discomfort between two or more of our beliefs. This most commonly occurs when our mental attitudes do not align with our actions—when we think one way, but behave another. Both the number and importance of our contradicting beliefs determine how much mental turmoil we experience. We resolve our feelings of discomfort by rationalizing and justifying one belief while reducing or rejecting the rest.

Changing our attitudes isn’t easy, let alone unlearning the behaviors associated with them. As a result, we usually stick with the personal belief ingrained deepest inside of us, as opposed to adopting new ones, even if they have more evidence supporting them. In fact, many of us take this one step further by avoiding uncomfortable scenarios that clash with our existing beliefs altogether.

Why is it so hard to change someone’s beliefs? https://thedecisionlab.com/biases/cognitive-dissonance

Kids are great at spotting and calling out CD in adults. They home in like lasers to the divergence between what their parents say versus what they do. A good friend called it kids’ ability to calculate their parent’s “WTT Ratio”—Walk the Talk ratio.

I love CD though. No, really.

Shorn of fancy words and complex explanations, CD is nothing but a signal your brain sends to itself when it detects a variation between incompatible beliefs, or between a belief and an action.

You love animals but also love eating them? CD.

You consider yourself honest and ethical, but won’t mind bribing a local official to get your paperwork done quickly? CD.

You consider yourself ambitious and innovative, yet don’t push yourself out of your comfort zone at work? CD.

You want to be fit and strong but cannot resist eating out and ordering in every other day? CD.

CD is a powerful feedback loop that tells you that you need to either change your beliefs or change your actions.

And CD is good! In fact, I’d argue that CD is a necessary (though not sufficient) ingredient on the journey of personal growth.

Either your actions and behaviour are testing your beliefs, or your beliefs are running ahead of your actions. They will always be in a game of one-upmanship with each other.

Your goal is to keep pushing one forward or pulling the other backward, to bring them into harmony.

Small detour. For years, I have held conflicting and dissonant feelings about pre-scheduled “one-to-one” meetings with my colleagues. I understood their importance from the perspective of developing and coaching. But I also struggled to develop my own agendas, methods, or systems to ensure that I conducted them efficiently enough so as to be valuable for my colleagues.

“You say you’re interested in coaching and mentoring your colleagues? Yet you cannot make regular 1-1s with them happen successfully. What sort of a person are you?”

Also, CD!

But reading about Nvidia’s Jensen Huang https://fortune.com/2024/11/12/jensen-huang-nvidia-ceo-leadership-mpp/ having 60 direct reports and doing no 1-1s at all gave me pause. Huang’s reason for not doing 1-1s really resonated with me. (In the words of a friend, seeing someone else model a behaviour allows you to feel less deviant while adopting it.)

Huang says he is allergic to hierarchy and corporate silos. He doesn’t believe in one-on-one meetings. Instead, he prefers mass gatherings of his leadership team: He says all Nvidia execs should be able to learn from the feedback he provides to any one of them, and they should all benefit from watching him together as he puzzles through a problem.

I, too, prefer to share feedback and advice over wider forums, whether in meetings or on public Slack channels. Every piece of advice that is given in private over a 1-1 could often be valuable to many others in the organisation too. Thus, my preference for 1-n conversations and discussions.

Secondly, I realised that the burden of crafting successful 1-1 agendas and outcomes wasn’t mine to bear. Instead, it was my colleagues’ (they should want mentoring and coaching first). Thus, I have now “harmonised” my CD around 1-1s by setting aside a chunk of my time each week as free time for 1-1s. Anyone is free to book a slot from it, set up an agenda, and expect me to land up.

In this way, I have harmonised my CD around 1-1s as my actions are now more in tune with my beliefs.

But don’t get too worried about keeping everything in harmony too. For “zero CD” means either your views aren’t strong or unique enough, or that your actions aren’t impactful enough.

The key to using CD for personal growth lies in our ability to observe, reflect, and learn from what it is telling us.

You must learn to observe it, because others might not tell you to your face. (Behind your back though, they will likely think of you as a hypocrite.)

Learn to spot feelings of discomfort, anxiety, and stress. Or emotions like guilt or regret. This is our brain telling us something isn’t adding up. Especially when it keeps repeating. The pattern is key.

I like to think of CD in two ways—a glass half-empty versus half-full.

In the half-empty version, your past beliefs and actions are at odds. Anxiety and stress are pushing you to do something about it. But they’re pushing you from behind, there’s nothing you can do about it now. What’s done is done.

In the half-full version, your future beliefs and actions are instead pulling you forward. There are many paths to that future. You can change either or both of them. You can decide what you want to do about it.

When meaningful dissonance does persist, we typically experience it as a source of pressure and stress, as Festinger noted in his primary book on the subject:

The presence of dissonance gives rise to pressures to reduce or eliminate the dissonance. The strength of the pressures to reduce the dissonance is a function of the magnitude of the dissonance. In other words, dissonance acts in the same way as a state of drive or need or tension. The presence of dissonance leads to action to reduce it just as, for example, the presence of hunger leads to action to reduce the hunger. Also, similar to the action of a drive, the greater the dissonance, the greater will be the intensity of the action to reduce the dissonance and the greater the avoidance of the situations that would increase the dissonance.

More recent research suggests that cognitive dissonance evokes discomfort specifically in situations where action is required, and a conflict between competing perspectives poses a barrier to taking action effectively. Eddie Harmon-Jones has conducted much of this research, which he and several colleagues summarised in 2015:

Most situations that cause dissonance involve difficult decisions and a commitment to action. According to the action-based model, once individuals commit to a course of action, they are primed to act. That is, they are motivated to translate their intended behavior into effective action, and this approach motivation is revealed in changes in attitudes that support the commitment. These attitudinal changes help us to follow through, to effectively enact the behaviors that follow from the decision.

“The Cognitive Dissonance of the CEO” https://www.edbatista.com/2018/07/the-cognitive-dissonance-of-the-ceo.html, Ed Batista

The force of CD will always exist, as long as you aren’t stagnating.

It’s your choice whether you want to experience it as something uncomfortable that’s pushing you, or something powerful that’s pulling you.

3. Panels, panels, panels

Hello, it’s Hari here. I am back with a selection of really cool graphic novels for you to pick up. But before we dive into our community recommendations, I’d love to hear about the best non-fiction you’ve come across recently. You can send in your recommendations using this link https://theken.typeform.com/FPBooks.

I have always been drawn to graphic novels as a unique medium for storytelling. Though we often associate them with science-fiction and fantasy, you would be surprised to see what they can achieve in other genres too. 

Of course, some of our assumptions are justified—after all, what better way to capture the otherworldly essence of fictional worlds than through illustrations? Which is why, in my limited experience with graphic novels, I feel they’re probably the best medium for guiding a reader’s imagination—a faculty that can falter in the heat of immersing oneself in a story. I might never have pictured Gregory the gargoyle (from Sandman) as vividly if it weren’t for its visual depiction.  

It’s not just about helping me see characters, but also about creating an immersive experience, even when that genre is non-fiction. 

Dan Nadel’s Crumb, for instance, was instrumental in encouraging artists to showcase their personal stories the best way they know how—through illustrations. This vivid reconstruction of the author’s own life is a narrative strain that we can follow in Art Spiegelman’s Maus too, as he tries to understand his Holocaust-survivor father’s past. Maus expanded my imagination of what a non-fiction graphic novel could do when crafted skillfully—and left a lasting impression on me.

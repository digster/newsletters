Ka-Ching!
Thu, 07 Aug 25
A weekly newsletter about how finance is getting supercharged by tech in India, and how you can make money work for you..
Good Morning Ishan,
Until last week, I hadn’t graced the steps of an ATM in almost a year. But when multiple street vendors around my locality in Bengaluru stopped accepting UPI payments overnight, I had no choice.
The trigger was a series of Goods and Services Tax (GST) [notices](https://www.moneycontrol.com/news/trends/karnataka-vendor-gets-rs-29-lakh-gst-notice-on-rs-1-63-crore-upi-transactions-bengaluru-erupts-in-protests-13313117.html) sent to street vendors who have been using the Unified Payments Interface (UPI) to accept payments. The state’s GST department had flagged them as unregistered traders whose [turnover](https://razorpay.com/learn/gst-registration-limits/) exceeded the limit over which GST registration is mandatory—Rs 40 lakh per annum for the supply of goods and Rs 20 lakh for services.
These notices have set off a wave of fear rippling through small traders in several states where governments have taken similar action. In Karnataka, over 14,000 street vendors recently [threatened](https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/tech/technology/bengaluru-street-vendors-threaten-three-day-protest-against-gst-notices-on-upi-payments/articleshow/122819508.cms?from=mdr) to go on strike over the issue. The strike was called off after the state government hit [pause](https://www.newindianexpress.com/states/karnataka/2025/Jul/24/strike-called-off-after-word-from-commercial-taxes-department-karnataka-traders) on 6,000-odd notices following the backlash, but reports say other states like Andhra Pradesh, Uttar Pradesh, Tamil Nadu, and Gujarat have also begun requesting merchant turnover data.
UPI may have brought simple, instant digital payments to India’s streets, but as the country’s small traders are finding out, it’s also brought fully traceable transactions—putting them directly under the lens of tax authorities for the first time.
Many of these vendors, often operating informally, are unaware that crossing the annual turnover limits makes GST registration mandatory. Most don’t maintain separate accounts for their personal and business transactions, and in a lot of cases, they also do not understand the tax structure or their actual liabilities.
This ignorance, combined with a natural fear of the taxman, means that the panic is real.
Vinoth’s QR code already returns an “invalid” prompt, and he isn’t the only one. Several street vendors I’ve spoken to over the past few days echo his fears. And as my experience last week suggests, many are starting to seriously consider abandoning UPI altogether.
That’s a problem for India’s grand Digital India plans.
If the JAM trinity (zero-balance Jan Dhan accounts, Aadhaar, and Mobile) laid the [groundwork](https://the-ken.com/newsletter/make-india-competitive-again/indias-financial-inclusion-project-is-titanic-kyc-could-be-the-iceberg-that-sinks-it/) for the first “digital revolution”, the Indian government hopes that a new trio will spark off the second one:
JAM-UPI-ULI.
Enough has been written about JAM and UPI already, but the Unified Lending Interface (ULI) is the youngest of this lot and is still in pilot mode. Introduced first in August 2023, and relaunched in September 2024, ULI is touted to be the “UPI of lending.”
Of India’s 63 million micro, small, and medium businesses (MSMEs), only 16% have access to formal credit sources, so there’s certainly a good argument for it. But ULI has been off to a slow start thus far. Only 36 lenders are currently onboard, and as of December 2024, only Rs 27,000 crore in loans had been disbursed through the platform.
This is far below expectations, as RBI Deputy Governor T. Rabi Sankar recently acknowledged in a closed-door meeting.
One NBFC founder told The Ken that lenders aren’t very thrilled with the platform because it isn’t very technically sound yet. Unlike UPI, which is operated and monitored by the National Payments Corporation of India (NPCI), ULI is not directly monitored by any dedicated entity—it’s all the RBI. And while the RBI is good at managing banks, it might not have the bandwidth to ensure things like swift grievance redressal—which, as we have seen in the case of ONDC, might end up becoming a problem.
Lenders are also not very confident about the multiple data sources ULI is supposed to be pulling from. For instance, land records, even in their digital form, don’t provide enough clarity to lenders since true property ownership at an individual-level remains a complex factor to determine.
And now, the clouds forming over UPI could make matters worse.
You see, with the pervasive adoption of UPI by MSMEs across India, the expectation was that UPI data would be integrated with the ULI system (along with other government data such as Aadhaar and land records) to make creditworthiness checks near-seamless. Thus opening up formal credit access to a huge portion of these merchants and traders.
But if small vendors start backing away from UPI, even if just from plain ignorance or fear, the project could begin stalling.
This would be especially damaging for rural and semi-urban credit delivery, where small traders, farmers, and gig workers stand to benefit most. Integration with Nabard’s [e-KCC](http://13.232.66.157:3000/) (Kisan Credit Card) portal, for example, was intended to help rural borrowers who had never taken a formal loan before.
But no digital payments means not enough alternative data, which means no expansion in credit access.
And then, there is also the [looming possibility](https://the-ken.com/story/upi-can-be-forever-or-free-not-both/) that UPI could end up getting costlier for merchants. Three banks—Axis Bank, Yes Bank, and ICICI Bank—have [recently begun](https://bfsi.economictimes.indiatimes.com/articles/upi-charges-by-banks-on-payment-aggregators-impact-on-merchants-revealed/123108366#:~:text=ICICI%20Bank%2C%20following%20Axis%20and,switch%20to%20access%20NPCI%20rails.) charging UPI fees to payment aggregators, and there is a good chance that some of these fees might end up being passed on to merchants—either directly as a merchant discount rate, or disguised as platform fees, convenience charges, or similar levies. This is in addition to the monthly subscription charges of Rs 125 paid for the UPI soundboxes.
Most vendors who are having second thoughts about UPI are not trying to evade taxes. They just don’t understand the system, and fear retrospective tax demands, frozen bank accounts, or worse.
And without the assurance that they won’t be suddenly confronted with onerous demands—whether those are monetary or compliance-related—they might continue to retreat from it.
UPI’s impact is proven. ULI’s promise is real. But if trust isn’t restored swiftly, both could end up unravelling.

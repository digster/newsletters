Good Morning Ishan,

Mukesh Ambani hates conventional playbooks. 

The billionaire launched Reliance Jio in 2016 by offering almost seven months of free data and voice services, and unleashed a price war. In short order, Jio became the country’s largest telecom operator. 

That disruption found echoes in an equally audacious move by Ambani last year. Viacom18 bagged the digital rights to the Indian Premier League for almost Rs 23,800 crore (US$2.9 billion) and made it entirely free, unlike Disney+ Hotstar, from which Viacom18 had snatched the rights. (Reliance Industries, the parent of both Jio and Viacom18, has since merged its media operations with Disney’s India unit.)

Ambani is just as good at financial engineering as he is at offering consumers simply irresistible deals. When Reliance Industries decided to demerge Jio Financial Services (JFS) and list it, the latter had no real business to speak of. But its valuation was wildly disproportionate, and this is how Reliance pulled it off:

RIL seems to have made a virtue out of necessity—by shifting an investment worth ~Rs 1 lakh crore (~$12 billion) to the fledgling JFS while also ring-fencing this investment from a looming regulatory threat. In exchange, RIL’s shareholders get JFS shares at a ratio that entitles even the smallest shareholder in RIL to get a piece of the JFS pie. The net worth of JFS also shoots up as much as ~13X. And yes, demerger deals don’t attract tax. One stone, many birds—classic RIL.

Reliance’s $12 billion gift to its newest child, Jio Financial Services, The Ken

It’s still early days for JFS, but Ambani pulled another rabbit out of his hat last week when JFS sought shareholder approval for one of its units to buy  “customer premises equipment / devices and telecom equipment” worth Rs 36,000 crore (US$4.3 billion) from Reliance Retail (RRL) over two years and lease them to Reliance Jio’s customers. The announcement didn’t specify the kind of equipment, but Reuters said the deal could include mobile phones and routers. 

I’m not getting into what this means for JFS; that’s beyond the scope of this newsletter. But I’m interested in how this shakes out for Reliance’s retail business.

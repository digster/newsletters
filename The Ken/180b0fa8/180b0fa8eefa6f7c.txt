Every morning, between 8.30 am and 10 am, I receive a flurry of texts and emails from my bank. All of those messages are about the failure of payments for a few of my online subscriptions. They fail every day because I haven’t yet approved them in the specific way as laid down by the Reserve Bank of India, the country’s central bank. 

And trust me, I have tried to. But the rules require the completion of a lengthy process, as documented in the image below made by my colleague Rohin: 

The steps involve banks, merchants, payment aggregators, mandate hubs, and the end-user. 

Seven months ago, the RBI changed the rules for recurring payments, with the intention to protect customers. But they've also hit merchants hard. Businesses with a subscription model—like furniture rental provider Furlenco or gym membership platform Cult.fit—have had to change how customers pay for their services. On the other hand, companies like Apple have stopped accepting payments via Indian debit and credit cards altogether. 

Although over 20 banks have complied with the necessary requirements, industry professionals estimate that success rates for such transactions vary between 30% and 75%. 

In many ways, this has severely dented the trust among the various stakeholders that usually work together to keep money flowing from customers to businesses. The changes have also increased the operational costs of businesses, which now have to dedicate more resources to ensuring that users make payments on time. 

In today’s story, Arundhati digs deep into the multiple cracks in the recurring payments pipeline, and how fixing this mess will likely require the regulator to step in again and create a cohesive framework.

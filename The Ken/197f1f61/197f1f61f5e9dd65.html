Last week, Motilal Oswal’s brand-new BSE 1000 Index Fund reopened for subscription. The wealth manager had first rolled out the new fund offer (NFO) between June 5–19, pitching it as a way for India’s stock-market investors to gain highly diversified exposure across large-, mid-, small-, and micro-cap companies. 

The fund is supposed to replicate the performance of the BSE 1000 Total Return Index, which tracks the top 1,000 companies listed on the exchange, covering 94% of India’s market capitalisation. Of the thousand, the first 100 are large-cap stocks such as HDFC and Reliance Industries, the next 150 are mid-caps, while the rest belong to the small- and micro-cap categories. 

The 1,000-stock figure might sound impressive, but Motilal Oswal isn’t exactly breaking virgin ground here. Fintech Groww’s mutual fund arm launched the Nifty Total Market Index Fund first, back in 2023, though it covered a smaller universe—only 750 stocks. 

The pitch was basically the same, though: “total” market exposure, diversification, derisking, etc., etc.

Groww’s total market index fund doesn’t exactly have a shining record (and we’ll get to that in a bit), but it’s understandable why Motilal Oswal is following in its tracks. 

The mutual funds space has become incredibly contested, with nearly 45 players in the market currently, and any way you can differentiate your offerings is a point in your favour, even for a legacy fund house like Motilal Oswal. The fund manager already offers a slew of passive funds like exchange-traded funds (ETF) and index funds—and it is perhaps the only fund house in India that has a micro-cap index fund. A total market fund is a nifty-sounding addition to that line-up. 

But what do “total market funds” really offer investors? 

The primary pitch is greater diversification, with deeper exposure to small- and micro-caps than other existing passive funds. But as we wrote when Groww first launched its total market fund, even that is a questionable benefit. 

For one thing, many small- and micro-cap stocks have very little trade volume and liquidity, which can “complicate” things, say fund managers. And although small- and micro-cap companies add breadth to the fund, they do not necessarily add much weight. (For context, the top 100 companies—large-cap stocks—account for nearly 60% of total market capitalisation.)

“We have kept away from launching a fund based on the total market index because it can get difficult to manage, especially when the AUM increases beyond a certain size—say Rs 2,000–3,000 crore ($240–360 million),” said a senior fund manager.

[…]

They explained that this could lead to difficulties in finding buyers and sellers for these stocks when investors put in or redeem money from the scheme. This could also be a problem when a stock is added to or removed from the benchmark index.

Groww has a mutual-fund sales pitch you haven’t heard before, The Ken

And then, there is the risk of large tracking errors, which could mean the fund’s performance ends up deviating significantly from the actual index it tracks. This is because India does not allow passive funds to include proxies for stocks. 

Again, quoting from the earlier story:

The senior fund manager further elaborated that unlike in the US and some other global markets, risk-optimisation models aren’t allowed in passive funds in India and schemes here need to fully replicate the index. That is, passive schemes in their portfolio must have all the stocks in the index with the same weightages, instead of using proxies to build a similar risk-return profile as the index.

As a result, there could be high tracking errors in such schemes, i.e., the scheme’s returns could deviate significantly from that of the index.

Nikhil Kothari, director at MF distributor Etica Wealth, agreed and added that there can be significant transaction and impact costs in such schemes. “I won’t recommend this fund to my clients, at least not until it shows a track record with negligible tracking error,” he said.

For its part, Motilal Oswal’s official webpage for the new fund mentions how tracking errors are a “key focus area”. But it doesn’t get into much more detail, except for offering up its long record and expertise as an asset management company (AMC).

In that regard, at least, Motilal Oswal is very different from Groww—it’s been in the mutual fund space for far longer, and its brand is far bigger.

Despite building an enormous customer base in its discount broking business, Groww is yet to make significant headway into mutual funds. As of June 2025, Groww AMC’s total assets under management (AUM) stood slightly above Rs 2,000 crore, according to the Association of Mutual Funds in India. That’s a mere fraction of Motilal Oswal’s mutual fund AUM of Rs 1.18 lakh crore. In fact, in India’s 45-player AMC segment, Motilal Oswal is 18th; Groww is far, far behind. 

And since its launch in late 2023, the Groww Nifty Total Market Index Fund has only managed to accrue an AUM of about Rs 303 crore, according to Value Research. 

Motilal Oswal, however, has significantly more marketing muscle; an almost 15-year-long history in the mutual fund business, with all the accumulated investor trust that comes with it; and a strong network of distributors to push the new fund to clients. 

Even so, according to the latest factsheet from Motilal Oswal Mutual Fund, the NFO collected only around Rs 40 crore, just slightly higher than the AUM of Rs 32 crore Groww’s total market fund managed at the end of October 2023. 

Investors, it appears, aren’t all that impressed with the Motilal moniker. 

Perhaps because when it comes to the gains they actually offer investors, there doesn’t seem to be much to differentiate between the two “total market” funds at all—except for 250 stocks and a little bit of pedigree.

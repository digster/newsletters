Good Morning Ishan,

If numbers were horses, government-owned State Bank of India would sweep the fintech derby.

Recently, the outgoing chairman of SBI said the bank's mobile banking app YONO was worth $40 billion, and the bank was considering hiving it off. When Arundhati, writer of today’s story, went digging into the news, SBI officials perked up: “30 million registered users, 10 million daily logins, 9 million bank accounts, Rs 24,000 crore of loans disbursed [in ~3 years], 350,000 credit cards, Rs 1,600 crore investment in mutual funds…”

The alphabet soup of financial products and services that YONO is driving for SBI is impressive. It is meant to aggregate some 50 apps of the bank into one super app.

For a moment, assume that the bank’s 448 million customers are a ready-made distribution channel. What YONO—You Only Need One— can do for itself and for the bank, no Indian fintech can even dream of.

The surge in digital finance is real and global. Mobile-based transactions have grown 3X in the last two years, according to a September report from research firm Bernstein. For a bank like SBI, 55% of transactions are via mobile. For private banks, that number is close to 90% of all banking transactions.

In the attention economy, banks everywhere are worried about being relegated to being dumb pipes, with value creation being scooped up by front-end companies. Fintechs have succeeded in moving transactions from banking apps to their own.

SBI wants all of it within its walled garden. (Never mind that it has the lowest app ratings among large Indian banking apps.)

The bank expects YONO to help ring-fence its users from new competition. It also intends to use the bank’s low cost of funds to lend at interest rates few can afford to. A hard-to-beat combination for the fintechs.

As for the valuation, SBI (market cap $24 billion) assets in the past have been valued higher when spun off. Could YONO repeat this magic and make the elephant dance? SBI needs to show it only once.

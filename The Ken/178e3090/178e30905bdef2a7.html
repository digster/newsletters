Good Morning Ishan,
 
There are not many things in life that are certain, especially these days. Right when vaccines began rolling out and when everyone thought the worst was over, the pandemic has roared back to life. This time even deadlier.
 
Many predictions are coming true.
 
The pandemic will linger around for more than a year—check.
 
The virus will mutate—check.
 
You will need vaccine shots every once in a while—check.
 
It will increase the division between the haves and the have nots—check.
 
There will be more predictions like these. Human beings make predictions; it is our natural response in a time of calamity. We use past data, complex analytics, and calculations to arrive at these predictions. Even at an individual level, we make predictions. You ever had that “gut feeling” that turned out to be true? That is your mind using past data to make calculations about the future.
 
The stories that we write here at The Ken are very much connected to this human nature. Every writer tries her best to point out things about the future. Sometimes we stick our necks out by relying on our reporting. Sometimes we ask our sources what they think would happen. Right from CEOs who use complex tracking methods to the auto driver who relies on his gut instincts, the “forward-looking” aspect is inherent in every story we write. Because we know our readers care about the future. It also helps bring some certainty in these uncertain times.
 
We kicked off this week with Arundhati analysing the coming upheaval in India’s payments space. India’s central bank wants to cut into the National Payments Corporation’s dominance of the scene by fashioning other, similar entities. And the who’s who of the business are vying for a slice of this new pie—Visa, Mastercard, Paytm, Amazon, Google, ICICI Bank, Axis Bank, Facebook, Reliance Jio, to name a few. India’s payments scene could very well break before the dust settles, predicts Arundhati.
 
On Tuesday, Maitri wrote about life-saving anti-TB drugs and how Big Pharma’s exorbitant pricing and the Indian government’s reluctance to purchase them has resulted in a major shortage. With the pandemic making matters worse, patients have hung their hopes on India’s judiciary; the Bombay High Court is hearing a public interest litigation on the issue. It’s our free story of the week. Do share widely.
 
On Wednesday, Olina wrote how the lack of a normal school year could have devastating effects on the future of millions of children and their learning capabilities. But it is not all doom and gloom. In systems thinking, no event is positive, negative, or neutral. Bridge courses and new teaching models being developed to fix these gaps could unintentionally fix many issues that have plagued Indian schools for decades.
 
On Thursday, Anand wrote a fascinating story about Adani Total Gas, a company that is barely covered by analysts and journalists. The company’s shares have skyrocketed amid the pandemic, reaching a valuation of more than $13 billion. That’s way more than the valuations of many companies in the same business that make way more in profits. What is driving the share price is a story about its potential future.
 
On Friday, I wrote about Flipkart’s new plans to tackle impending regulations. As Amazon faces heat from India and abroad, Flipkart is quietly going about its business and preparing for the future. Industry insiders, though, aren’t convinced much will change, while brands are uncomfortable with the new arrangement.
 
This week in our daily newsletter, Beyond the First Order, Nithin explored why index-tracking mutual funds in India are raising fees when the rest of the world is slashing fees; Sanjana broke down the marketing strategy of fintech unicorn CRED; and Rohin pondered the similarities between the evolution of business in India and its culture of marriage.
 
Praveen closed out the week with our Saturday newsletter, The Nutgraf. This week, he wrote about human behaviour. About how science views and processes evidence, proof, and risk. About how human beings look at science and make decisions. About how all of us—the public—behave. It’s a must-read.
 
That is all from us this week. Be very careful. Mask up. Wash your hands and stay safe.

Early in the book Glorious Exploits by Ferdia Lennon, a group of Athenian soldiers, taken prisoner during the Peloponnesian War, are made to perform Euripides’ play Medea for their captors.

Some play the parts and the rest are the chorus.

They are starving, exhausted, and far from home. But when they step into the chorus, something shifts. They do not reenact the violence of the war. They’re just standing on the side, speaking calmly, giving shape to events that would otherwise feel uncontainable.

I read the book early this year and it’s stayed with me. Mainly because, somehow, this year too often felt like it needed a chorus.

You see, in Greek theatre, the chorus did not advance the plot. It existed to offer context and perspective to the plot. This idea kept returning to me as the year unfolded. Somehow it is also how I have come to think about The Ken’s podcasts.

They’re not isolated shows competing for attention but more like distinct voices responding to the same world. Each show is designed for a different way of listening and understanding. But all of them have come from questions that refused to go away.

Daybreak came from the most basic one. What matters today?

Two by Two grew out of moments when a company or idea seemed to be everywhere at once and the instinct was to stop and ask why.

First Principles exists for when the noise recedes and the thinking begins, usually about companies, leadership, and mental models that ultimately reveal their consequences over time.

90,000 Hours took shape around conversations about careers, workplaces, and the unease that now shadows it.

Zero Shot came from a shared uncertainty about artificial intelligence. Not what it promises, but what it is already doing.

And Make India Competitive Again stepped back to tackle structural questions about India’s economy, policy, and power.

Different questions, different vantage points, but together, they formed something like a chorus.

Here are some of the highlights from The Ken’s various podcasts in 2025.

Two by Two

Hosted by Rohin Dharmakumar and Praveen Gopal Krishnan, and produced by Uddantika Kashyap, this is the podcast we return to when something feels familiar on the surface but unsettled underneath. 

An episode on ‘Zomato, Swiggy, and the ten-minute dark café’ began as a story about speed but soon became one about margins, labour, and the quiet recalibration of what we now expect from food and people.

Another episode asked, what happens when Indian consumers begin to understand what they are consuming. Not in terms of awareness, but more in terms of consequences. What changes when knowledge spreads widely enough to alter demand itself ? The answer slows down the moment to show you what looks obvious now may not remain so.

Daybreak

This is the podcast I think about first thing every morning. No prizes for guessing why.

As its co-host and producer, I begin with the most practical question. What does the news need today in order to make sense? Often, the answer lies close to the headlines but not always. 

Episodes on India’s rare-earth decisions and how these left us behind China, as well as the metal market’s sudden breakdown required patience. They were stories about systems and supply chains, power exercised at a distance, and consequences that arrive quietly. 

Other episodes asked questions closer to home. When we explored the financial playbooks of India’s wealthiest women, or made a lazy girl’s guide to building quiet wealth, my co-hosts Rahel, Rachel, and I were following questions shaped by our own lives as women. They were about money and control and the meaning of security.

Daybreak has, in ways, become an everyday exercise in judgment—when to stay close and when to step back.

90,000 Hours

If Daybreak tries to stay close, 90,000 Hours, hosted and produced by my former co-host Rahel Philipose, observes from farther away.

Work rarely announces that it has changed. You notice it in subtler ways—in how opportunities circulate, what counts as “good”, who advances, and who does not. 

An episode on networking, framed through pitches and pickleball, traced how access now moves through informal channels.

Then ‘The hard truth about the American dream’ confronted the gap between aspiration and outcome. And ‘In a sea of sameness, one skill stands out’ turned its attention to taste. Not taste as status, but as judgment. The ability to distinguish thoughtful work from merely adequate work in a world where generative AI makes adequacy cheap. 

Since its launch five months ago, the show, now with a new co-host Vidhatri, is very quickly turning into a chronicle of how people are learning to adapt their professional lives in real time.

Zero Shot

Beneath that adjustment lies Zero Shot. What makes it stand out in the sea of tech podcasts is that its hosts Rohin, Praveen, and Brady Ng, refuse to treat AI as spectacle. 

One episode dismantled the idea of a single global AI race, showing how the United States, China, and India were solving for entirely different problems: hardware vs efficiency, compute vs data. Another episode followed recent skirmishes between companies such as Perplexity, Nvidia, and Google, not as news, but as signals of saturation, shifting power, and incumbents returning to form. 

Zero Shot resists certainty. Instead, it allows for disagreement, lingering on the possibility that we are building systems we do not yet know how to live with.

First Principles

After a year-long absence, Rohin’s First Principles returned this year, and what struck me was how little it needed to explain itself. People had kept listening. The old conversations had not expired.

When the new season began, it felt less like a revival, more like a continuation. 

A conversation with Vidit Aatrey of Meesho examined what it takes to build a problem-first organisation.

With Narayan Subramaniam of Ultraviolette, the discussion moved through time itself–how to keep tinkering, discarding, and the discipline required to keep going.

And a conversation with Rahul Matthan of Trilegal lingered on institutions, incentives, and principles that compound quietly over decades.

First Principles keeps reminding me that some ideas are timeless; they just wait patiently for attention.

Make India Competitive Again

Meanwhile, Make India Competitive Again, started this year in response to Trump 2.0, stayed with the present, and with the cost of getting the future wrong. 

An episode on open-source software and India’s AI ambitions argued that competitiveness is as much about norms as it is about capital. Another one on the elusive women powering India’s economy dismantled comforting employment statistics, revealing how growth can mask precarity. And the hidden costs of superstar family businesses treated concentration not as anomaly, but as structure.

Make India Competitive Again never offered reassurance. It offered reckoning. A reminder that surviving disruption is not the same thing as being ready for it.

We look forward to building on these very varied promises, and hope you keep enjoying our chorus in the new year as well.

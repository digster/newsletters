You walk into a neighbourhood pharmacy with a chest on fire and a prescription for acidity. 

Behind the counter stands Morpheus—white coat, slippers, reading glasses perched on his nose. He holds out two pills, one in each hand. Aristo’s Nexom 40 on the left. Glenmark’s Esomer 40 on the right. Both are Esomeprazole 40 mg. Same compound, same dosage. Your stomach couldn’t tell them apart if it tried. 

“Choose,” says Morpheus. You’re in The Matrix, but the game is rigged. Both pills are the blue pill; both keep the simulation funded. The Aristo strip left the factory at Rs 14 and rode a 1,119% markup to a Rs 170 price tag. The Glenmark started at Rs 25 and accumulated 622% worth of supply chain before landing at Rs 180. The red pill, the truth, exists. It’s Esomeprazole at a Jan Aushadhi Kendra for Rs 30. Same drug, same dose, roughly one-sixth the price. But Morpheus won’t offer you that one. The red pill is bad for business.

That Rs 170 price tag is a singular data point in a vast national ledger—a ledger that, according to the Economic Survey FY25–26, is beginning to tell a much kinder story.

India’s out-of-pocket expenditure on health, the money you pay from your own pocket when you get sick, fell from 64% of total health spending in 2013–14 to about 39% in 2021–22. The government spent more on health. Its insurance programme, Ayushman Bharat covered hospitalisation costs for crores of families. Subsidised pharmacy chains like Jan Aushadhi Kendras opened across the country. Real things happened, and the number moved.

But… about 40 to 50% of what Indians still spend out of pocket on health goes toward one thing: medicines. Not hospital stays. Not doctor visits. The single largest drain on household health budgets is the thing you pick up at a pharmacy counter.

Signs of flickering

In December 2025, a standing committee released a report where the Department of Pharmaceuticals (DoP) was asked to explain markups of 600% to 3,000% on common medications. For context, the DoP is the Indian government body responsible for drug pricing policy and overseeing the pharmaceutical industry’s development. 

They provided their reasons but the committee didn’t buy them...

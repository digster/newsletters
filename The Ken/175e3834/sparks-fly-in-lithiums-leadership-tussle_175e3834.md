If you've been around an MBA grad long enough—around 2-4 minutes, in this case—you've heard the phrase "Culture eats strategy for breakfast".
Unlike most things MBAs say, I have no quarrel with the idea. We've all been in workplaces we look back on with horror. Maybe it was a micromanaging boss, a lack of work-life balance, or a shitty HR department. Maybe, like me, you had all three at once—toxic workplace bingo. The work was alright, perhaps the company even had a mission you believed in, but it all fell apart because of the culture...
Around the turn of 2020, Lithium Urban Technologies—India's largest electric vehicle-focused cab operator—also had this epiphany.
Even as it chugged steadily along, it decided to move co-founder Sanjay Krishnan, who had been CEO since Lithium's inception, into a non-executive role. By all accounts, Krishnan was a hands-on leader. It was he who had struck up Lithium’s earliest client contracts, and he'd even hired the company's first driver.
The problem was that Krishnan might have been too involved. Many executives complained of a poor work culture, laying the blame at Krishnan's door. A companywide audit was even conducted to identify Lithium's issues. (The board and Krishnan have both denied the audit was just about company culture.)
Lithium chose to proceed with the softer touch of Krishnan's co-founder—Ashwin Mahesh. The change yielded the desired results on the culture front. But the company had a new issue. As one employee put it—the culture improved, the business didn't.
Even as Mahesh was attempting to bring his vision for Lithium to fruition, rumours began circulating that Krishnan was back. In October, all but one CXO-level executive quit in anticipation, including Mahesh. For a company in the mobility sector—a space buffeted by the Covid tornado—this tussle was the last thing Lithium needed.

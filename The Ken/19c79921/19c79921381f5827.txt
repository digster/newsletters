Two years ago, The Ken published a story https://the-ken.com/story/how-india-bent-over-backwards-to-clear-microns-2-7b-chip-facility/?utm_source=newsletter&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=collection on how India bent over backwards to clear Micron Technology’s $2.7 billion chip facility, documenting a courtship. Sweetened incentives, fast-tracked tax assurances, and unusually high fiscal support. 

Close to 70% of the project’s capital expenditure would be funded by public funds. India wanted an anchor tenant for its semiconductor ambitions. Micron negotiated like one.

This month, the arrangement acquired something more tangible than paperwork. At the India AI Impact Summit in Delhi, the government confirmed that Micron’s Gujarat facility will begin commercial production by the end of February. For a country that has spent years drafting semiconductor policies, output is a notable upgrade.

The timing is not incidental, though. 

Semiconductors have moved from being a supply-chain vulnerability to strategic infra. The AI boom has tightened memory supply to the point where Micron has committed $200 billion over the next two decades to expand capacity and ease AI-driven demand. GPUs grab the headlines, but without high-speed memory feeding them data, even the most powerful GPU becomes expensive and, at times, useless. The US is subsidising domestic fabs. Taiwan’s TSMC is expanding in Arizona. China is attempting to localise under export controls.

Into this pressure cooker walks Sanand, Gujarat.

Micron’s facility is not a leading-edge fabrication plant. It is an ATMP unit—assembly, testing, marking, and packaging. The wafers will be fabricated elsewhere; India’s role, for now, is to finish what others begin. That’s not modest, though.

The choreography problem

Semiconductors are choreographed with multiple steps. Dozens of cross-border transfers and a handful of technological choke points. 

In “India has a ‘full-stack’ semicon dream, but only half the links it needs to achieve it,” The Ken noted https://the-ken.com/newsletter/make-india-competitive-again/india-has-a-full-stack-semicon-dream-but-only-half-the-links-it-needs-to-crack-it/?utm_source=newsletter&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=collection that keeping those links disruption-free is existential. Japan controls critical materials and equipment. The US and UK dominate chip design tools. Taiwan’s Hsinchu cluster anchors advanced fabrication risk. A single earthquake or export control can cascade.

India’s semiconductor ambition, then, is about link management. And Micron is the first serious link.

When the deal was signed, Micron committed roughly $825 million. The Centre pledged $1.35 billion; Gujarat covered the balance. After several stalled attempts with other consortia, the immediate need was credibility. The subsidy structure was designed to send a signal: India would underwrite seriousness.

But credibility has to translate into capability. Hardware is simple to inaugurate, but it is harder to run at scale, at yield, and without disruption.

We broke that down here https://the-ken.com/newsletter/make-india-competitive-again/india-has-a-full-stack-semicon-dream-but-only-half-the-links-it-needs-to-crack-it/?utm_source=newsletter&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=collection.

The design paradox

India often points to its chip-design strength. In headcount terms, that claim has merit. In capital terms, it is more complicated.

In “India wants a thriving semiconductor industry. If only it could keep chip designers happy,” The Ken showed https://the-ken.com/story/india-wants-a-thriving-semiconductor-industry-if-only-it-could-keep-chip-designers-happy/?utm_source=newsletter&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=collection how even basic fabless ventures need about $10 million to build a non-cutting-edge chip. Government reimbursements cap at $1.6 million, and only after founders raise double that privately. Indian startups pay 50–80% more than global peers to tape out chips. One in three tape-outs fails.

Design is structured experimentation. Iteration is its oxygen. Without manufacturing proximity, iteration slows, and costs rise.

That reporting is worth revisiting https://the-ken.com/story/india-wants-a-thriving-semiconductor-industry-if-only-it-could-keep-chip-designers-happy/?utm_source=newsletter&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=collection.

And yet, global majors are increasingly treating India as a design outpost for their own silicon wars. In another report, The Ken detailed how Microsoft has begun hiring silicon-hardware engineers directly from IITs to build its in-house AI chips—Maia and Cobalt—as it seeks to loosen Nvidia’s grip on cloud margins.

Running traditional workloads on Azure’s CPUs can yield margins of around 65%. But once AI models enter the picture, and Nvidia’s GPUs take over, Azure’s margins can shrink to 15–20%. Efficiency is not quite equal to profitability yet, given the cost incurred for hardware. 

India, then, is simultaneously a proving ground and a pressure valve: home to one in five global chip-design engineers, yet still struggling to build product-first semiconductor firms of its own.

Proximity compressed learning cycles and reduced the distance between error and correction.

Micron is not TSMC. But it introduces adjacency.

If assembly and testing stabilise, design founders gain something they have not had before: a domestic endpoint. That alone shortens feedback loops.

Manufacturing is harder than software

If design is fragile, manufacturing is unforgiving.

In “Kaynes and others want to do for electronics what TCS and Infosys did for IT,” we saw what scaling hardware https://the-ken.com/story/kaynes-and-others-want-to-do-for-electronics-what-tcs-and-infosys-did-for-it/?utm_source=newsletter&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=collection actually entails. Training programmes are being built from scratch. IITs are running assembly-line simulations. Companies are investing crores to ensure recruits can operate packaging equipment without damaging million-dollar machines.

IT services needed buildings and broadband. Electronics needs cleanrooms, chemical supply chains, precision tooling, and yield discipline.

Micron’s ATMP facility now plugs into that same ecosystem. Commercial production means qualification cycles, logistics reliability, and power stability. It is about proving India can execute the unglamorous middle of the value chain.

We examined that on the ground https://the-ken.com/story/kaynes-and-others-want-to-do-for-electronics-what-tcs-and-infosys-did-for-it/?utm_source=newsletter&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=collection.

But who captures value once the chips start flowing?

At the other end of the semiconductor spectrum,far from packaging lines and cleanrooms, India is discovering that “finishing” is not the same as “owning”. 

In a separate investigation, The Ken reported how thousands of Nvidia H100 GPUs are flooding Indian data centres—20,000 ordered by Yotta alone—on the back of the sovereign AI narrative.

But alongside operational complexity also sits exuberance.

Between discipline and delusion

Because there is the temptation of hype.

In “The strange story behind RRP’s 900X stock jump and India’s chip fever,” The Ken chronicled https://the-ken.com/story/the-strange-story-behind-rrps-900x-stock-jump-and-indias-chip-fever/?utm_source=newsletter&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=collection how mere association with the word “semiconductor” inflated a thinly traded stock into a Rs 13,000 crore mirage. India’s chip enthusiasm is real, but so is the speculative excess that trails it.

Read the analysis here https://the-ken.com/story/the-strange-story-behind-rrps-900x-stock-jump-and-indias-chip-fever/?utm_source=newsletter&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=collection.

Micron is the opposite of that frenzy. It is incremental, bureaucratically negotiated, and operationally dense. It does not promise 2nm breakthroughs. It promises packaging lines that run.

India’s chips narrative was structured around announcements for years—subsidies rolled out, memoranda signed, and, of course, speeches delivered. With commercial production approaching, the story shifts to execution.

The subsidy worked.

The chips have to.

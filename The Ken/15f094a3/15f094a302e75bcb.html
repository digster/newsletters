I often think about this. What if it doesn’t turn out that way? 
 
You know, you put the information together, mountains of it, think through it, speak to the people who are supposed to know the subject better than you, sleep over what you’ve gathered, think again, put your thoughts down and hit publish. You know, you do your best. Sometimes you are spot on, sometimes you aren’t. And I often think about this; man, change is humbling. What’s good today in the list of assumptions is trash tomorrow. So in the face of change, what do you do? Persevere, I guess. 
 
All of this brings me to Ola. In India. So, in August last year, we wrote a piece on the company. On its competition with Uber and the Didi-Uber merger in China; what that would mean for Ola. We said Ola’s survival rests on its ability to raise money. Lots of it. And then we said a little more. To quote from the piece: “It’s important to note that the two biggest investors in Ola—Softbank and Tiger Global—are both currently going through a slower phase of investing in India and are unlikely to lead any fresh funding rounds. Meanwhile with its exit from China and a cash pile that was over $11 billion in June, Uber can easily afford to increase its spending in India till as long as it wants to.”
 
Ola just raised $400 million (plus $400 million in the earlier round) from Tencent. Rumour mills are abuzz that it is soon going to raise a total of $2 billion. Then, of course, there’s Uber. Since August of last year to now, a lot of news from Uber has been about infighting, lawsuits, harassment allegations, toxic work culture and the exit of Travis Kalanick. Basically, mess. 
 
Here a few simple questions must be asked. What do you think of Ola now? $800 million of new funding in the bank is good money. It is a good runway. At least for about four to five years. So, is Uber still a credible threat? What strings does SoftBank pull now? Well, I have the best man for this job. My colleague Sumanth spent quite some time thinking and reporting on the subject.

I have a confession: I find it really hard to take an afternoon nap on a weekend.
The reason is decades of conditioning about sleep being a waste of time that could be put to “productive” use. For instance, I could be reading a book, catching up on a TV series, or taking Gabru for a walk. Aren’t all of these more productive uses of an hour or two than sleeping?
I know I’m wrong, but unconditioning takes time too.
Ironically, even as I’ve started appreciating the value of sleep over the last three-four years, I still see it as something to be optimised. What was my breathing rate while sleeping? How much deep sleep did I get? What was my waking HRV? My sleeping body temperature?
But the true value of sleep transcends such simple measures. We now know that it plays a crucial role in helping restore and rebuild us each day, but that it does incredible things for our creativity.
For instance, the zone when [we’re just nodding off to sleep](https://news.mit.edu/2023/sleep-sweet-spot-dreams-creativity-0515) is a magical one for creativity.
Feeling stuck on a problem that seems unsolvable? You may come up with a creative solution after a short nap—very short, according to a new study from MIT and Harvard Medical School researchers.
During the phase when you’re drifting between sleep and waking, a state known as sleep onset, the creative mind is particularly fertile, the researchers showed. They also demonstrated, for the first time, that when people are prompted to dream about a particular topic during that sleep phase, they perform much more creatively when later asked to perform three creativity tasks around that topic.
“When you are prompted to dream about a topic during sleep onset, you can have dream experiences that you can later use for these creative tasks,” says Kathleen Esfahany, an MIT senior and one of the lead authors of the study.
A full night’s sleep unlocks other aspects of creativity via the [interplay between the REM and non-REM](https://www.theatlantic.com/science/archive/2018/05/sleep-creativity-theory/560399/) phases of our sleep. In fact, if you’re working on something important or complex that requires a creative (and not merely brute force or quantum) approach, think or work through it before sleeping. Your brain will continue to work at it even while you’re asleep.
Sleep is magical in its outcomes, even though it appears unproductive and fallow in its form.
Fallow (noun)
“usually cultivated land that is allowed to lie idle during the growing season.”
“characterized by inaction; unproductive.”
We’re living in the final years of what I’d like to call the “productivity era”. Uptime and efficiency has been the way we’ve looked at all sorts of resources—servers in the cloud, taxis on the road, delivery workers on bikes, employees on rolls.
Downtime is one of the most egregious of mistakes that can be made. It’s the wastage of a resource that should have been put to more productive use.
But with the arrival of generative AI-driven models of work, I feel humankind will have to drastically reevaluate our notions of productivity. We simply will not be able to outcompete tens of thousands of parallel thinker-doers in the cloud with access to virtually the sum total of human knowledge that is relevant.
I do not know how we emerge from this competition that has been thrust upon us by corporations and investors who seek to embed their AI models and APIs deep into every single aspect of our lives, regardless of the actual benefit. But I do know one thing that will become more valuable: human creativity and innovation.
Many of the most successful business books from the last decade have been ones about maximising our productivity by actively focusing our attention. Think [Deep Work](https://www.goodreads.com/en/book/show/25744928), [Radical Focus](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/57802947-radical-focus), [Indistractable](https://www.goodreads.com/en/book/show/44595007).
But are we perhaps overfocusing ourselves?
The problem is that excessive focus exhausts the focus circuits in your brain. It can drain your energy and make you lose self-control. This energy drain can also make you more impulsive and less helpful. As a result, decisions are poorly thought-out, and you become less collaborative.
So what do we do then? Focus or unfocus?
In keeping with recent research, both focus and unfocus are vital. The brain operates optimally when it toggles between focus and unfocus, allowing you to develop resilience, enhance creativity, and make better decisions too.
When you unfocus, you engage a brain circuit called the “default mode network.” Abbreviated as the DMN, we used to think of this circuit as the Do Mostly Nothing circuit because it only came on when you stopped focusing effortfully. Yet, when “at rest”, this circuit uses 20% of the body’s energy (compared to the comparatively small 5% that any effort will require).
The DMN needs this energy because it is doing anything but resting. Under the brain’s conscious radar, it activates old memories, goes back and forth between the past, present, and future, and recombines different ideas. Using this new and previously inaccessible data, you develop enhanced self-awareness and a sense of personal relevance. And you can imagine creative solutions or predict the future, thereby leading to better decision-making too. The DMN also helps you tune into other people’s thinking, thereby improving team understanding and cohesion.
Your Brain Can Only Take So Much Focus by Srini Pillay, [Harvard Business Review](https://hbr.org/2017/05/your-brain-can-only-take-so-much-focus)
And the answer to that might lie in something that machines might never comprehend—consciously embracing non-productivity every now and then. I came across this wonderful explanation from [a post from Maria Popova](https://www.themarginalian.org/2023/04/11/masud-khan-lying-fallow/) of The Marginalian (formerly Brain Pickings).
What does the fallow mood achieve for us? The answer is a paradox: a great deal and nothing. It is a nutrient of the ego and a preparatory state. It supplies the energic substratum for most of our creative efforts, and through its unintegrated, psychic suspended animation (which is the obverse of organized mentation) allows for that larval inner experience which distinguishes true psychic creativity from obsessional productiveness.
Hidden Selves by Masud Khan
Our attitudes and mental models towards work, ambition, and success change as we age. I’ll admit that I’ve speed-run through three stages of fallow in the last five years.
Stage 1: Resist it (as unproductive)
Stage 2: Accept it (as necessary)
Stage 3: Enjoy it (as rewarding)
To begin to truly enjoy fallow time, I had to first see it as a natural part of life. Fertile, fallow, fertile. Night, day, night. Just like we cannot truly enjoy sleep if we only think of the night as a pesky and dark annoyance that must be endured before day, I’ve learned that being occasionally fallow is what allows me to be truly creative and productive at the other times. We can look forward to day and also to night.
With that knowledge, I’ve begun to lean into it while appreciating the irony that allowing ourselves to be unproductive for some time ends up freeing our mind and makes us subconsciously more creative.
I suppose Sundays are our day of fallow. It’s the only day we don’t feel pressured to follow a checklist or glance at our calendars.
Here’s to being fallow every now and then.

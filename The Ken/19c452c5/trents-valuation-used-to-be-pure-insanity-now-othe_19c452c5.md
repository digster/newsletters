Good Morning Ishan,
A few years ago, every time Trent announced its quarterly results, investors would respond with unbridled enthusiasm. The Tata Group retailer repeatedly blew past analyst estimates, especially on the number of new Zudio fast-fashion stores.
But now, Zudio’s ability to surprise Trent’s shareholders is naturally not what it used to be. With 850 stores, Zudio’s footprint is already far wider than Westside’s 280. The stock is up 5% since the company released its financials for the December quarter on Wednesday. But over the past year, Trent has lost a fifth of its market value, compared with a 10% gain in the Nifty 50 index.
The upshot?
Trent is no longer as absurdly valued as it once was. Four years ago, it was trading at 430X its trailing earnings. Last March, even though it was well below its all-time high, the stock was at 130X trailing earnings. Now, it’s at a more tolerable 90X, which is comparable with that of Titan, the largest retailer within the Tata Group, and Dmart, India’s second-largest chain of supermarkets. Interestingly, Titan shares neared their all-time high on Monday, after the company’s results for the October–December period.
Trent, Titan, and Dmart are the largest, both by revenue and market cap, among listed companies in their respective categories: fashion, jewellery, and grocery. And they tend to command a higher earnings multiple than their direct competitors.
But there are companies that make them look like a steal. Since its November listing, eyewear retailer Lenskart is only up 13%. But relative to Trent on price-to-earnings (PE), Lenskart is 3.5X pricier. Beauty-and-fashion e-tailer Nykaa is even dearer, trading at a PE multiple that is almost 6X higher than Trent’s.
Talk to a top executive from a conventional consumer firm that has a peer in the e-commerce world, and they will bemoan the unjustifiable premium the latter gets for being “digital natives”. But even if no internet company other than Eternal has truly broken out since going public, investors tend to be inexplicably partial to them. Nykaa’s nosebleed valuation is despite the stock falling a third since listing in November 2021.
No matter how big their potential market is, the fact is that these companies are still quite inefficient at turning their shareholders’ money into profits. Nykaa’s return on equity (RoE) is 5%, and Lenskart’s is even lower. Trent’s RoE, on the other hand, is 30%.
The valuation dissonance between traditional retailers and their online-first disruptors cannot be sustained for too long. The latter’s profits will have to catch up to their market cap.

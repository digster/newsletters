Good Morning Ishan,
I’ll cover the past, present, and future.
Here’s the past: A decade and a half ago, the proprietor of a factory in China told me about his Hotel Light Switch Theory. It goes like this: When businesspeople from the US and Europe landed in China in the 1990s and 2000s, they would head to their hotels, check in, enter their rooms, and then turn the lights on.
The first thing they noticed would be the row of light switches right by the door. Some were higher, some were lower, maybe one or two were crooked. Those light switch panels shaped these visitors’ impressions of the quality that they could get out of China—functional but inferior—driven home by the fact that they were in the nicest hotels in the country. Those impressions wouldn’t be wrong.
By the 2010s, those light switches had been reinstalled or replaced, so they looked level and flush. It was a consequence of good design and quality control taking root, and it was happening around the country in many different ways. Manufacturing processes were advancing quickly. Apps started to run smoothly. High-end electronics worked as advertised.
Western execs started to get the impression that Chinese firms were shedding the image of being the world’s sweatshop. A year or two later, it felt like China was a decade ahead.
The present: On Wednesday’s episode of the [Zero Shot podcast](https://the-ken.com/podcasts/zero-shot/the-wrong-race-india-needs-its-own-datasets-why-ai-cant-learn/), I spoke about the narrative collapse in the way US tech executives describe AI development in China. In short, I describe how US and Chinese AI developers have been running different races—one hoarding hardware, the other going for efficiency and architecture that gets the job done.
(Rohin and Praveen’s segments in the podcast were great too. They talked about how India has been after Nvidia’s GPUs but actually needs to build its own datasets, as well as what’s stopping AI from truly developing the capability to learn.)
Anyway, here’s a [headline](https://finance.yahoo.com/news/alibaba-cloud-claims-slash-nvidia-093000646.html) from earlier in the week: Alibaba Cloud claims to slash Nvidia GPU use by 82% with new pooling system. Basically, the company has created a system that can share expensive GPUs among many different language models in a marketplace setting, specifically making 213 GPUs perform like they’re 1,192, and it works well enough to save real money.

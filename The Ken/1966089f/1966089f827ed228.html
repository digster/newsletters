India’s third-largest discount broker, Angel One, has bought a full-price ticket to the most expensive pastime in India, the Indian Premier League, hoping to arrest the all-around slide.

In the March quarter results announced last week, Angel One’s broking revenue dropped 30% year-on-year to about Rs 633 crore. Net profit slumped to Rs 175 crore, the lowest in three years. Orders are down to 5.4 million, from a peak of 8.8 million in June 2024.

The choppy markets of the past year proved once again that relying on one or two levers, however fashionable or trendy, is a risky business model in India. Or even in the US, for that matter. Between 2020 and 2024, it dumped its full-service past to become a sleek, app-only discount broker when the young, impulsive investors came in hordes.

Now that hobby-investors have receded and the regulator has cracked the whip, Angel One is undertaking a makeover.

Let me quote from the story to give you a flavour of what Aakriti has in store for you:

“It’s not just the business that’s changed. [CEO Dinesh] Thakkar has undergone a makeover, too. Once the very picture of old-school finance—slicked-back hair, formal shirts-n-trousers—he now wears stylish glasses, edgier cuts, and sometimes, even a bomber jacket. He clicks pictures with fancy sports cars. The company got a new name (Angel Broking to Angel One), the app got a fresh interface, and Thakkar got a rebrand to match. Call it founder-market fit.”

It’s a breezy read.

The market is hopeful of Tata Elxsi pulling a rabbit, maybe three, out of its hat. It had lost hope earlier after the stock failed to deliver results despite its crazy high valuation. Its profits had stagnated or declined in the past couple of years. But out of the blue, the stock has started regaining ground. In about two sessions, it’s up nearly 13%, despite having nothing to show in its quarterly results. In this week’s Long and Short, Anand writes about what the market is hoping would work for the company this time around. And whether those hopes would hold good.

That said, I can’t help but wonder: is HUL spreading itself too thin under the banner of “wellbeing”?

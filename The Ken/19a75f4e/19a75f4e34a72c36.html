You know what it’s like to ride old buses that are running years beyond their intended lifetime on public roads. They’re loud, hot, and bumpy. If you’re conjuring that image in your head, then you might even recall the acrid smell of burnt diesel wafting through the vehicle compartment.

Those old hulls are now being replaced by new electric models in Bengaluru, Delhi, and Mumbai. Passengers welcome them, but the grid has trouble keeping up with the additional load. Diesel generators make up for the shortfall—defeating the purpose of going electric to begin with. Every day, buses that plug in at charging depots siphon away power meant for homes and businesses in the neighbourhood, leading to brownouts or blackouts.

India wants 50,000 electric buses on the road by 2030, and the transition is already in full swing. The Bengaluru Metropolitan Transport Corporation (BMTC), for instance, has 1,500 e-buses, which make up roughly one-fifth of its fleet. While there are workarounds to keep their batteries charged, the long-term plan for powering them isn’t clear. There’s a disconnect between policymakers, bus operators, and grid operators, while “the pressure to go electric is huge,” said one recently retired BMTC official to The Ken.

Mrunmayee unpacks the consequences of this poor planning in today’s story. Read it to find out how your bus commute paves over larger infrastructural problems, even though it feels nicer.

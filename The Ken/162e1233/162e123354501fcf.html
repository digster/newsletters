At Sequoia, two partners have quit within just a year. Their exit has led to curious speculation on the fund’s performance
Good Morning Ishan,
 
In the venture capital world, Sequoia and what transpires at the blue-blooded VC firm are of consequence. And even though it is a well-known fact that Sequoia likes to churn its partners—reportedly, just so nobody takes their job for granted—two partners quitting within a year calls for closer scrutiny. More appropriately, a story by Ashish.
 
For a VC firm which has scores and scores of investments in India, things started looking shaky in the latter half of 2016. The chatter was around how the fund’s performance in India wasn’t looking good. And that someone from the partnership ought to be held accountable and asked to go.
 
But the headquarters didn’t want to bell the cat. Instead, it signalled: One partner must go. Now, who that person would be was the partners’ call; they had to decide who’d get the axe. And while all may hold the title of partners, the partnership per se is not democratic. Not in any VC firm, not in Sequoia.
 
Ashish brings you a riveting account of what led to the two exits, the tales of boardroom manoeuvres and loyalties, the tough questions asked and the conversations that did not go down well.
 
And even as you read this, in Beijing, Sequoia is making a presentation and is in the middle of raising $690 million from its LPs, with $30 million of its own commitment. Business as usual, you ask?

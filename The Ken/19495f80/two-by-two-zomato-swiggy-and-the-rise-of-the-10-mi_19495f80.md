It’s Friday, 17 January, 1.30 pm. The recording session for episode 26 of Two by Two is about to kick off—we have about 30 minutes to go.
My colleague Rajiv and I have just finished lunch and are heading to the studio when I realise I’ve completely forgotten to order coffee for our guests. There’s a Blue Tokai on the way, could I place an order for takeaway and pick it up before going to the studio?
This usually takes only a few minutes, but today, the app says it might take 30. Uh oh.
I check Swiggy. It’s the same 30 minutes, but this time, it’s for delivery to the studio. It’s cutting it close, but I decide to take the chance and place an order on Swiggy. It’s 1.40 pm.
We reach the studio and find that one of our guests is already there. Ramchander Raman, former president of Cafe Coffee Day and co-founder and COO of Nucleus Kitchens, is chatting with our hosts Rohin Dharmakumar and Praveen Gopal Krishnan. Our other two guests arrive in quick succession: Thomas Fenn is a joint secretary at the National Restaurant Association of India (NRAI) and co-founder of Mahabelly, a restaurant serving Kerala cuisine. Gaurav Saria is the founder of Infinitea, India’s first exclusive chain of tearooms and stores.
The minutes continue to tick down and I start to get a little worried.
Maybe I made a mistake.
But just as I begin doubting myself, the order arrives—and it’s well before 2 pm.
Phew.
If you are wondering why I’m talking about all this, it’s because my (and lakhs of other Indians’) scramble for last-minute coffee is driving one of the biggest stories in Indian business right now.
Just how fast should food be delivered? How fast can it be delivered?
If you ask India’s quick-commerce players, they’ll tell you—“very fast”. In 10 minutes. Maybe even less.
Both Swiggy (Snacc) and Zomato (Bistro) launched dedicated 10-minute food-delivery apps just a few days ago. Zepto’s 10-minute food delivery service has been up and running for a couple of years now.
It’s clear quick-commerce platforms see an opportunity in expanding into food. It doesn’t hurt that Swiggy and Zomato cut their teeth making food deliveries.
Restaurants, though, are fuming. The NRAI has even brought up the bogie of taking the issue to India’s competition watchdog. Their peeve is that the present avatar of 10-minute food delivery is largely banking on the platforms’ own cloud kitchens and dark stores. Why are aggregators competing with their restaurant partners? Is it even fair when aggregators have access to restaurants’ pricing and order data?
This week’s episode of Two by Two is a deep dive into the incentives driving 10-minute food deliveries, and the many implications for restaurants, aggregators, and customers. All three, however, have spun out their 10-minute food delivery services into separate apps instead of offering them on their primary platforms. Zepto did it first, Swiggy and Zomato followed. This may be why:
Thomas: Why they [Swiggy and Zomato] have also done it is tactical. They’re cutting it off as a separate app so they will not have to face the allegation that they’re competing with people on the platform itself.
But all the decision-making, all the data, all the insights, all the category building, the menu mix… everything is going to be about who’s ordering where.
Customer density is driven by the data that was built on Zomato and Swiggy.
Rohin: Though technically, I think Albinder Dhindsa of Blinkit said we don’t share any of that data. It’s a separate team. If you take him at face value, it’s all the same organisation. It’s like the left hand doesn’t talk…
Thomas: …to the right hand.
It reports to the same brain, ultimately.
Thomas has also penned an interesting [opinion piece](https://indianexpress.com/article/opinion/columns/zomato-swiggy-bistro-snacc-restaurant-business-9776464/) on how Bistro and Snacc further strengthen Zomato and Swiggy’s duopoly, and threaten the restaurant business.
We also spoke about the logistics, and why earlier attempts at instant food delivery failed to take off.
Thomas: In Zomato-Swiggy speak, there is what is called a kitchen preparation time (KPT). And there is delivery time (DT).
10 minutes is equal to KPT plus DT.
In groceries, your KPT is kind of zero in a retail context. You’re literally picking up a ready product and you’re pushing it out.
So why (Zomato) Instant could not work was that you really have to put a lot of pressure on restaurants. A restaurant has to rethink its entire operation to bring down KPT to a point. [Zomato Instant was Zomato’s first attempt at quick food delivery.]
Rohin: To what?
Ramchander: Two minutes. They gave eight minutes of delivery time.
Thomas: I have 30 seconds to accept the order. Everything is buttons. You’re actually telling the system that these things are happening.
There have been enough episodes of Two by Two where we’ve spoken about how duopolies are bad for all parties involved, and how a third player couldn’t hurt. There were a few names that came up as possible contenders who could shake up the Swiggy-Zomato duopoly in food delivery, but ONDC was the clear favourite.
Both Thomas and Gaurav strongly argued for it.
But how feasible is it, especially considering the agreements Swiggy and Zomato have with their restaurant partners?
Rohin: Let’s say Zepto or Z starts this new food delivery service that goes to Gaurav and says, “Hey, Gaurav, we’re only gonna charge you 10% or 5%, because you will do your own delivery via ONDC.”
And Gaurav is like, “I’ll spend another 10% or 15% on ONDC. I’m still at 20%. I get to keep 80%. Great.”
But, from what Thomas said, that’s not allowed. Because the moment Gaurav offers customers a lower price on this third app, Zomato and Swiggy’s contracts will kick in and say you have to offer them the same lower price.
After the recording session, I checked the delivery time on Swiggy’s Snacc for the same coffee I’d been scrambling for. Turns out, I could have had it delivered even faster.
But at what price? To be clear, I’m not just talking about the actual rupee value here. Is it worth the strain on the restaurant? The delivery partner? Do I really need coffee to be delivered even faster than it is now?

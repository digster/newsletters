Ankylosing spondylitis.

This was the diagnosis Amit Bhanot received in 2000. It wasn't good news, but at least he knew what was going on. For the past nine years, Bhanot had gone from doctor to doctor trying to figure out why he was losing mobility in his right hip.

Finally, he knew. The condition, a rare form of arthritis, had caused his hip joints to fuse. His right hip joint was completely shot, and his left showed signs of following suit.

So Bhanot got a hip replacement, and life was better. But a decade later, the pain was back. Doctors said he needed a revision surgery—the metal implant would have to be changed.

This wouldn't be easy. Bhanot had lost most of his pelvis, making conventional implants useless. The solution came in the form of a 3D printed titanium hip implant, customised to his condition. Problem solved.

Bhanot is an outlier, though. While 3D printed implants have become commonplace elsewhere—China alone produced 60,000 of them last year; the global market is worth $1.2 billion—they are a rarity in India. Prescribed only for hopeless situations like Bhanot's. This is despite 3D implants being arguably better and safer than conventional implants. 

It's a strange situation. 

There are no regulations. There are even commercial manufacturers with no certification. Doctors are wary. Insurance is unwilling, and patients can't afford it.

Does all of this mean that 3D printed implants are destined to remain insignificant? That's what Suraksha analyses in today's story.

Good Morning Ishan,
On 9 October, Ola Electric founder Bhavish Aggarwal shared two short videos on social media—aerial views of its gigafactory under construction.
Shot from a helicopter, the videos looked impressive. They also piqued our interest. Not least because from the time the construction started this May to the completion date bandied about by Ola Electric, it’s a race against time.
According to two civil engineers on the ground who spoke with The Ken, the work is on track to conclude in the next five months. Give it an additional month, and the facility will be up and running.
I know what you are thinking. Ola built its electric scooter assembling plant in record time; it’ll have an encore with its gigafactory. Will it? Or can it?
We had a lot of questions. But if you follow Ola, you’d know it’s a one-way communication engine. They just show you stuff, they don’t field questions. So Shaswata, along with Akshaya, decided to visit the Bargur industrial estate in the Krishnagiri district, around 160km south of Bengaluru, to figure out what was happening.
On that stretch, Ola virtually commands one side of the highway. The Bargur estate, run by the government-owned State Industries Promotion Corporation of Tamil Nadu (SIPCOT), has 1,378 acres of land. Ola has leased nearly a third of that for its scooter and cell factories for 99 years, according to a government official at the site.
Typically, a project of this magnitude is a 1.5–2 year marathon. But Ola Electric wanted it to be a six-month sprint, prompting the construction firms to haggle for a 30-day extension.
ABS Fujitsu General, URC Construction, and Ocean Lifespaces India are jointly responsible for building the gigafactory.
Looking at Aggarwal’s videos and Shaswata’s photos from the site, it seems unlikely that it will be ready to “manufacture” cells that Ola can use in its scooters in six months. (It currently uses LG Chem’s lithium-ion cells.)
But there’s a big reason why Ola is racing against time.

Good Morning Ishan,
 
It was on a crisp December morning, somewhere around 9:00 AM that school day, that I, as a six-year-old, experienced the first of many embarrassments.

The run-up to the incident is a hazy memory, because what's etched is the "main event": a three-round competitive race comprising hurdling, bunny hops and skipping rope. Our PT teacher was more a boot camp instructor. He was also a shockingly poor judge of agility—he'd noticed the fervour with which I punched, kicked and kiai-ed (shouted) my way through Kyokushin karate class, and assumed I'd also bring something to the athletics table. So there I was, a portly in a sea of ganglies, to be undone by a race whose obstacles I'd rather have broken than jumped over.

My body was unwieldy, but my mind was quick on its feet. It made me conscious of each steeple I knocked, of each poorly timed leap and laboured jump over rope. All the heat in the world rushed to my face. On that Sports Day "for juniors", school kids watched, parents cheered and photographers chronicled as I tottered in last place. I ran back to the arms of karate. I wanted to be strong. I did not want to leap.

It's not for nothing that leaping is associated with risk. In those few milliseconds you're airborne, you challenge what keeps you rooted, literally and metaphorically. Hence, "a quantum leap". "One small step for man, a giant leap for mankind." That heart-stopping leap from an aircraft before you skydive.

Our stories this week highlighted the many ways companies circumvent hurdles, or try to. Not all leaps land well, but Tiger Global's do. For now, anyway. In the week's first story, Sumanth took us through the private equity investor's leap of faith in India as the leadership transitioned from Lee Fixel to Scott Shleifer. What's the leap, you ask? Being bullish about B2B rather than B2C. Tiger Global now has a finger in seven SaaS pies. And it's just getting started.

The rise of Nykaa has personally fascinated me, mostly because its competitors are far behind even as the online beauty retailer jumps when it doesn't really need to. Case in point: an in-app barcode scanning feature that now trundles like a white elephant, and a shot at high-end Indian wear. Don't miss Rozelle's well-plumbed article on the Falguni Nayar-led company, which is now planning for an initial public offering (IPO).

More on B2B bullishness: there's Abinaya's story on Udaan. It's India's latest entrant to the unicorn ($1 billion) club, which is otherwise populated by B2C startups. But it's got the incumbent IndiaMART—which commands 60% market share in the B2B classifieds space—to contend with. And Yellow Pages, Sulekha and Justdial. Udaan has also made no money from its operations. Is it a costlier vault than should be for the three former Flipkart executives who founded it? Read on to find out.

You'll want to read Arundhati's story on the growing pains of the kids who jumped on the IRCTC bandwagon. Namely, payments companies and online travel aggregators. They accounted for 10.6% of all train tickets sold online in 2018, so you'd think IRCTC would treat them well, right? Wrong. Turns out the companies that jumped high for IRCTC fruit are now making do with diddly squat. 

We wrapped up the week with Pradip's analysis on how climate change is ravaging India's food crops. Wheat and rice, our staples, are under extreme stress. As are apple and saffron cultivation. India could face an agricultural loss of over $7 billion by 2030, but 80% of these losses could be averted if adaptive strategies are implemented. Are we leapfrogging ahead with blinkers and digging a deeper hole with respect to food security? You decide.

Have a glorious Sunday, or try to. Yes, even those of you faced with the godforsaken steeple of 40-plus degrees Celsius.

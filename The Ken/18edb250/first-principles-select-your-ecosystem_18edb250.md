Good Morning Ishan,
One of the beautiful things about the circle of life is rediscovering things you experienced (but have since forgotten) as a child, but only this time as a parent.
One of my most vivid recollections of this is from January 2015. Our son was about to turn five. I’d bought him a bicycle the year before hoping he’d learn how to ride it. No luck. After a few flailing attempts, he stubbornly refused to, and there was nothing I could say or do to convince him otherwise.
Then, on the last Saturday of the month, he came to me and said, “Papa, where is my bicycle?”
His six-year-old best friend had been cycling around the community, and I guess he had enough of it. “I want to cycle too!” he said.
I could only stand back and watch in amazement as he swerved, fell, and bumped over the rest of the day, each time stubbornly refusing my offers of help. The cycle got called various names and received various well-aimed kicks.
But, in a few hours, he had learnt how to cycle, I kid you not.
That was the day I rediscovered the incredible power of peer pressure, as a parent.
Over the years, I would note how many of the hobbies my wife and I proposed to him would be studiously ignored till the day he saw one of his friends engaging in them. And then, we would just marvel at his focused, self-directed learning.
Peers push us forward in ways that are hard to replicate, because they make us want to be better.
It’s true for adults too. One of the best things we can do for ourselves professionally is to be part of environments where our peers make us want to be better. We’ll get into that shortly, but here’s today’s edition:

You know I’m a huge fan of Systems Thinking ([“Emergence”](https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/emergence/)). Everything we think, do, and become is because of the visible and invisible systems we’re part of. And perhaps one of the least understood or appreciated components of those systems are our peers.
I’m sure you’ve read numerous articles that refer to the “mafias” that emanated from really successful and innovative organisations at their peak? The “Flipkart Mafia”, the “Zoho Mafia”, the “Paytm Mafia”, the “Oyo Mafia”, and of course, the original “[PayPal mafia](https://fortune.com/2007/11/13/paypal-mafia/)”.
We usually get awed by the scale and success of former-employees-turned-entrepreneurs and miss the real story. What were the conditions for such mafias to emerge in some organisations?
An incredible density of talent, ambition, and innovation, enabled through a unique culture. In such an environment, the “rising tide” constantly pushes people forward to learn and grow, till some reach their own respective growth and ambition limits. They leave to start up on their own.
Our findings indicate that observing the performance goals of peers influences own goals. When participants observe an average peer performance goal larger than their own goal, they revise their goal upwards. Similarly, when they observe an average peer goal smaller than their own goal, they revise their goal downwards. With respect to effect size, participants on average revise their goal by about one third of a standard deviation for each standard deviation difference between their initial goal and the peer average goal. We also have suggestive evidence that observing more ambitious peers can increase participants’ earnings.
Peer effects of ambition By Philipp Albert, Dorothea Kubler, Juliana Silva-Goncalves [[PDF](https://www.wzb.eu/system/files/docs/mc/vam/paper_121219.pdf)]
We’re used to thinking of “peer pressure” in a negative manner. As if it is something we must always be wary of.
But some of the most innovative organisations and leaders owe their success to “[positive peer pressure](https://hbr.org/2010/04/positive-peer-pressure-a-power)”.
Peer to peer interactions may be the single most neglected lever of change. When enlisted, they are change’s most powerful ally; when resisted, they are its most stubborn foe. Peers in large organizations are invaluable in spreading behavior change across an enterprise. In that respect, they constitute a woefully underused set of resources, mostly accessible within the “informal elements” of our organizations.
Whenever significant numbers of peers interact formally or informally, they constitute a force to be reckoned with. When they share mutual respect, they will listen to, learn from, and secretly support one another in ways that can shape opinions, create resistance, or generate energy. Left unattended, their interactions may or may not be supportive of important enterprise priorities.
Positive Peer Pressure: A Powerful Ally to Change by Jon R. Katzenbach and Zia Khan, [Harvard Business Review](https://hbr.org/2010/04/positive-peer-pressure-a-power)
This shouldn’t be a surprise to us. That old (and tired) cliché, “people don’t leave bad organisations, they leave bad bosses”, has an extended take that I quite like—“…but they stay for great teams”.
Being surrounded by talented, ambitious, and friendly people can lift our careers over the long run much more than any one job or role ever can.
In today’s flat and fluid organizations, building an effective internal network is crucial for success. Extensive research has shown that the networks of informal relationships that managers forge in the workplace influence key aspects of their performance — how fast they learn, how creative they are, whether or not they’re staffed on high-visibility projects, and how much influence they garner within the organization. It also affects more tangible aspects of their job, such as their compensation, year-end bonuses, and career advancement.
Surround Yourself with Colleagues Who Boost Your Performance by Gianluca Carnabuci and Eric Quintane, [Harvard Business Review](https://hbr.org/2023/11/surround-yourself-with-colleagues-who-boost-your-performance)
Peer environments don’t always have to be at formal workplaces. They can just as easily be created independently, though with some added and conscious effort.
Freelance workers and corporate managers might seem very different, but they have much in common. Underneath their pursuits of independence and portability lies a shared aspiration to fashion a masterful self. A self in charge of one’s work productivity or career trajectory. Master, ultimately, of one’s working life.
A masterful self is useful because it protects us against the loneliness and uncertainty of working on our own or moving from job to job (or city to city). It spurs us to take responsibility for our learning and productivity. It makes us more confident and effective. It is precious because it transforms us from captives of our circumstances to shapers of our destiny.
To Take Charge of Your Career, Start by Building Your Tribe by Gianpiero Petriglieri, [Harvard Business Review](https://hbr.org/2018/04/to-take-charge-of-your-career-start-by-building-your-tribe)
The article goes on to lay out three critical aspects of a peer environment that pushes us towards our “masterful self”.
They are strong spaces, not just safe spaces. Besides letting us feel accepted as we are, they encourage us to show up generously in the world. They give us courage as well comfort. That is why we end up feeling that it is our tribe, rather than a tribe that owns us.
They care about learning, not just performance. These tribes let us take risks to try something new, rather than force us to keep doing our best. They are there even, especially, when what we are doing is hard, feels clumsy, and has not yet shown results.
They are a source of questions, not just advice. More than a pool of experts serving as resources when you need them, these tribes raise the questions that help us explore the edges of our competence and identity, or send us in new directions.
Sadly, few of us give adequate weightage to the importance of a powerful peer environment when we are thinking about our career moves.
Instead, if we imagine our professional lives as a, well, organism, then it becomes easier to imagine the role that the right ecosystem plays in its evolution.
Select your ecosystem better.

Just a few weeks ago in this section, we touched upon the question of why humans seem so attracted to wide open empty spaces, and by extension, to pictures of beautiful, colourful landscapes. (See: [Open Skies](https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/select-your-ecosystem/).) It is, in a way, a vestige of both our evolutionary and psychological history—the zoomed-out view, the ‘Big Picture’, is an essential part of how we process and experience the world.
What about the opposite, though? What happens when you deliberately focus your visual attention on a single object/subject?
I ask because my top photo pick from our subscriber contributions this week comes from Himadri Sharma, clicked on “a misty morning in Kaziranga, Assam”.
Setting aside all the usual approbations, I was struck by how my visual focus always seemed to be drawn inward, to the jeep. And the more you look at it, the more you notice. The badge on the side. The ambiguous colouring of the paint in the morning light. The dew on the windscreen. The snazzy wheel rims.
Open skies and rolling hills may offer a veneer of peace, but a narrowed perspective dominated by a single object is an entirely different matter, and a way more intense experience. If you’re new here, you may not have read Rohin’s earlier piece on the benefits of wearing blinkers (aka, narrowing your focus). Even if you have already read it, it’s worth another look: “[Wear your blinkers](https://the-ken.com/newsletter/first-principles/wear-your-blinkers/)”.
This is a phenomenon relatively well-documented by the scientific community, though considering how complex the human neural system and psychology are, we almost certainly don’t know the whole of it. Here’s an excerpt from a study I found last week about the role visual attention plays in the pursuit of goals. In this specific case, physical exercise.
…the scope of visual attention serves as a spotlight (e.g., [LaBerge, 1983](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/0146167219861438#bibr29-0146167219861438); [Posner, Snyder, & Davidson, 1980](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/0146167219861438#bibr36-0146167219861438)) or zoom lens ([Eriksen & James, 1986](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/0146167219861438#bibr18-0146167219861438); [Eriksen & Yeh, 1985](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/0146167219861438#bibr19-0146167219861438)), highlighting opportunities that encourage effective goal pursuit.
Moreover, visual attention promotes goal-relevant outcomes related to physical movement. When people oriented eye gaze on a goal, focusing visual attention narrowly rather than expansively, they engaged in more effective exercise. For example, when they were motivated to exercise, people who narrowed their attention on the finish line compared with when they expanded their attention around their environment walked to the finish line faster and felt that the physical exertion required was less ([Cole, Riccio, & Balcetis, 2014](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/0146167219861438#bibr11-0146167219861438)). Similarly, although unfit individuals perceived distances to a finish line as greater than fit individuals, when unfit individuals narrowly focused their visual attention on the goal, they perceived it as just as close as did their fit counterparts, believed they could accomplish the task, and subsequently improved the quality of their exercise ([Balcetis et al., 2015](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/0146167219861438#bibr3-0146167219861438)).
[…]
Narrowed attention leads people to see the goal as closer and the likelihood of achieving the goal as greater, which improves the quality of physical activity.

Dear Ishan, I (Anushka, as always!) am writing this little section from my hometown. I’ve been here for three days or so, and for almost all of them, my parents have been trying to get the AC in my childhood bedroom fixed.
It’s really not their fault. The room has been empty for months, and they never realised that the AC had silently conked off because they never had to switch it on. It’s hot here—we touch 40° celsius on the daily during the day. And I can see that my parents are feeling guilty about this; they keep calling someone to repair the AC every day, and somehow, it just doesn’t work. A new person everyday, still no AC.
For my part, I usually wait in my sweltering bedroom as they work, right by my bookshelf. For three days thus, I have been looking at and picking out practically all the books I have ever owned. It’s nostalgic, sometimes, but mostly a cringe fest. Teenage Anushka read the most random stuff (all that Colleen Hoover!) across multiple genres. But heck, I must give it to her: she got through them like a breeze. She’d re-read the same books when there was nothing else to do. She’d find an author that she liked and then source and read through their entire oeuvre obsessively.
Every day, the AC has stayed as broken as ever, and every day, I keep thumbing through my books thinking, “Why did I read that?”. Also, “How did I read that?”. (A Suitable Boy, I’m looking at you).
I kind of have a feeling many of us were this voracious, perhaps, up until a few years ago.
And I kind of have a feeling many of us want to be like that again.
I found [this tweet](https://x.com/raunaksood99/status/1776875949092745232?s=46) last week, right after we asked for some recommendations from the First Principles community on books that could get you out of a reading rut. I’ll tell you about the most interesting recommendations we received in a bit, but I’d like to talk about the responses to this tweet first.
There were a bunch of intriguing responses, but it’s even more interesting to see the tone and context. People are really trying to get back into it. They’re going to absurd lengths. They’re imposing on themselves discipline and restrictions. Making resolutions. Re-training their brain. (And hey, we’re all in the same boat here, I’m not judging.)
There was a lot of run-of-the-mill advice (reread favourite books, switch genres, join a book club, etc.), but I’d like to list the most surprising suggestions.
The first is habit-forming of the boring but absolutely non-negotiable variety. Straightforward stuff like setting a dedicated reading time—borrowing time from social media usage, etc., and pouring it into a reading hour.
The other surprising thing that people suggested was reading on your phone. Switching up mediums isn’t unheard of—switching to reading more on Kindle has helped me loads, for instance. Other people swear by audiobooks. But the hypothesis here is that we’re so used to being on our phones that hopping onto the Kindle app to read is an easier mental switch to make than picking up a paper book or the Kindle device.
And lastly—this is my favourite one—drop more books! Read and abandon, with abandon.

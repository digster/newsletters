Make India Competitive Again
Mon, 21 Jul 25
What India is doing, will do, and should do—to not just survive but thrive in the chaos unleashed by Trump.
Good Morning Ishan,
Two of India’s most prominent family business groups are in the ring once again. This time, to fight for a majority share of the India business of Chinese electronics company Haier.
While Reliance recently launched its own electronics brand, Wyzr, the Bharti group doesn’t have any history of selling consumer electronics. But it makes strategic sense to have a foot in this door, considering how electronics manufacturing is changing the fortunes of once unassuming companies such as [Dixon](https://the-ken.com/story/forget-taiwans-foxconn-and-pegatron-indias-dixon-is-the-worlds-hottest-electronics-maker/), [Micromax](https://www.businessworld.in/article/rahul-sharmas-rs-6200-crore-gambit-the-micromax-maverick-reinvents-bpl-as-indias-manufacturing-dark-horse-554047), and Syrma SGS Technology.
News reports [suggest](https://www.reuters.com/world/china/airtel-founder-mittal-warbug-pincus-eye-49-stake-haier-india-business-standard-2025-07-07/) Bharti is in the lead, having teamed up with private equity firm Warburg Pincus. The duo could pay $720 million for a 49% stake in the company, which they want to take public in two years. With a PE partner, it is both a financial and strategic investment for the Bharti family, which has a combined [fortune](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-05-23/heir-to-27-billion-fortune-quits-britain-as-wealth-exodus-grows) of $27.2 billion, according to the Bloomberg Billionaires Index.
For a group that already operates in telecom and infrastructure, insurance, real estate, hospitality, agriculture, food, and space communication, Bharti’s entry (or is it a foray?) into electronics, especially at [this point](https://static.pib.gov.in/WriteReadData/specificdocs/documents/2025/mar/doc2025326526801.pdf) in India’s economic history, is a canny step.
But more pertinent to our purposes today, it adds yet another statistic showing increasing business concentration in India, where 25 family business groups—a list that has remained largely unchanged in the last 25 years—account for 15–20% of India’s GDP.
In a [paper](https://www.econstor.eu/bitstream/10419/308287/1/dp17428.pdf) first published in November—titled Liberalisation, concentration, and diversification: Business groups in India, 2000-2020—economists Simon Commander, Saul Estrin, Naveen Thomas, and Varun Lingineni say:
One indicator of the weight of business groups in the aggregate economy can be gauged by the share of the largest companies’ revenues in national output. Across Asia, the top 5 and top 10 companies have recently accounted for between 10-40% of GDP. In India these shares have been around 10% and 15% respectively. These are certainly large shares when compared with most advanced economies, where comparable shares are around 3-5%.
Family business groups (FBG) have dominated the Indian economy for decades. This trend is not accidental—successive governments and their policies, industrial and otherwise, have favoured them. Still, one would have expected at least some new stars to have emerged with the arrival of the internet economy in the early 2000s.
But the Indian economy’s FBG trait is rather dominant; it expresses itself in every generation. As the authors show, very few non-FBGs are represented among the country’s top business groups, underscoring the enduring nature of family control over major industries. In fact, there are only three non-FBGs—L&T, Infosys, and ITC—among India’s top 25 business groups.
Commander and his co-authors, who used CMIE Prowess data from 2000 to 2020 for their research, show that not only were the top 25 FBGs in India mostly well diversified at the start of the period, but their diversification—which is “across-industry”—has continued subsequently.
And even among the top 25 FBGs, business concentration is high—the top five account for over 60% of the total income of the cohort.
The numbers in the paper speak for themselves.

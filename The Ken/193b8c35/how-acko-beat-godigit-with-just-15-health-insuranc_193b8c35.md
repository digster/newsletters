Good Morning Ishan,
Health insurers in most markets must be in shock this week.
The killing of UnitedHealthcare CEO in the US was unnerving enough but the wave of social media outpourings and memes in support of the killing is equally shocking. Or maybe not; the incident has just brought to the surface the simmering anger.
Health insurance is a touchy and complex subject—standardisation doesn’t work and products or plans that satisfy all stakeholders are few if not rare.
So when Rounak, the author of today’s story, shared that an emotive dialogue from a Bollywood blockbuster, Munnabhai MBBS—“when someone’s dying, do they necessarily have to fill out forms?”—is the internal tagline of Acko’s retail health-insurance unit, we were all ears in the newsroom.
Just as in the movie, the industry needs more empathy and trust. If it’s retail insurance, even more so.
Executives from legacy health insurance believe Acko has done in two years what most companies take three to four years. But more than the time, it’s the route that Acko has taken to build and grow its 21-month-old retail-health insurance.
This sector relies on agents and point-of-sale personnel to sell policies. The feet-on-street staff handhold customers during the process and become their point of contact in case of hospitalisation.
As of March 2024, ICICI Lombard had over 53,000 agents and more than 75,000 point-of-sale personnel, per GIC data. Eight-year-old Godigit also had more than 1,000 agents and over 60,000 point-of-sale personnel.
In contrast, Acko operated with just 15 agents. How exactly?
Unlike the hero in the movie, Acko is not hugging its customers but it’s getting close. Let’s get to the [story](https://the-ken.com/story/how-acko-beat-godigit-with-just-15-health-insurance-agents/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter).

Rahul from Policybazaar falls on one side of how insurance today is sold.

The software on his screen sends him leads, and he follows these up with short 10-minute calls with the self-sufficient, educated, metro dwellers on the other end. These people understand enough to shortlist their own insurance plans but want an expert to sanity-check the last 20% of their decision.

On the other side is Turtlemint’s Karthik, who operates only in his home town of Erode, selling to couples from his housing colony, parents from his children’s school, and neighbours who call him “anna”—most of whom know nothing about insurance. It’s common for him to sit with them for hours and explain the tradeoffs of one policy over another.

That’s the divide that Turtlemint’s Rs 2,000 crore IPO is set to navigate.

But the problem is, Policybazaar has already shown public-market investors that its software-centric model has profitability built into scale. In fact, PB Fintech’s December quarter results show a 160% jump in profits year-on-year, on the back of a 40% growth in revenue.

No wonder the stock has rallied since 2023.

In contrast, Turtlemint’s agent-centric model suffers from a very particular kind of diseconomy of scale. As its revenue has risen, its commission costs seem to have jumped in lockstep. Can the company change that math?

That’s the question Valli tackles in today’s lucid story.

EAAA India Alternatives recently filed for an IPO, making it the first major domestic private-credit firm to seek a listing. The move comes at a time when everyone from Jamie Dimon to Raghuram Rajan has warned against the risks in private credit.

In today’s Long and Short, Seetharaman breaks down what EAAA’s IPO signals to regulators, and asks if Indian investors are ready for the big test.

From Hero to Zero, in, err, six years. Medical tech history is littered with examples where award-winning innovation gets junked as more people begin using it. Sometimes it’s swift, sometimes it takes years.

Now imagine, you are a business building a product on the back of such an innovation. You invest Rs 200 crore and five years developing your tech only to have your role model trampled down. In no time, the patent goes from being a breakthrough to a dud. Almost.

Vapi, Gujarat-based Meril Life Sciences, is facing this predicament. It built its ‘disappearing stent’ following Abbott’s unique and successful Absorb stent. A little device that props open clogged arteries to reduce chest pain. After being on the market for over five years, in 2017, Abbott pulled the product off the shelves, even though it had launched in India three years before the US.

Meril, meanwhile, ready with the Indian regulator’s (rather questionable) approval, got caught in the post-Abbott adverse publicity maelstrom and India’s pricing cap brouhaha.

Gayathri’s story today on Meril’s stent and Abbott’s squeeze shows the complex world of med-tech, equal parts doctors’ dilemma and risk averseness, and the innovation funding trap. Indian companies are damned if they do, damned if they don’t. With the price cap in force, even the best of imported stents can become affordable.

"If I get a Nike shoe at the cost of an Action, so why should I not use a Nike?" says a doctor.

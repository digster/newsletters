Good Morning Ishan,
 
Since we started our weekly recap early last year, this is my first recap email. Every week, Durga, our desk editor who maintains the roster, lobs the email ball from one writer to another. I somehow managed to duck it.
 
You see, the pressure to write good emails is at an all-time high at The Ken. Rohin’s analogies make more sense now and his coinages/acronyms are knocking at dictionary doors. Praveen’s (PGK) wit is increasingly like a hot knife through butter. Savio has mastered the art of digging up old gems, doesn’t matter whether the story is on vaping or Vodafone. Ranjan, relatively new to (weekday) email writing, dishes out a personal anecdote, duly wrapped in self-deprecating humour, with every email. Clearly, he leads a more interesting life than I do.
 
Last week, during one of our usual Slack banter sessions, PGK lamented that he’s refused so many job offers after joining The Ken, and hence offended so many people, that his post-Ken career is over.
 
Ah, that resonated with me. “Look at me, PGK. I’m totally unemployable. Thanks to The Ken stories, including my own writings, I have offended so many people that no one’s ever gonna consider me for anything,” I told him. (My friends jokingly warn me that hospital doors are fast shutting for me.)
 
As always, PGK was swift: “You and I will start something new, Okay Seema?” And he suggested, needless to say, a funny name for the imaginary entity. (It’s a riff on an existing name so, I’m afraid, I can’t tell you.)
 
Starting up all over again evokes mixed feelings. But why go down that alley when we already have a rip-roaring venture. Just look at the stories we had this week:
 
On Monday, Nadine, from her perch in Jakarta, wrote about the intensifying dogfight in aviation in SE Asia. Indonesian carriers Garuda and Lion raised their prices, pushing AirAsia, which was undercutting them on prices, to the fringes. Yet another example when a solo brand took on a platform. The story analysed how AirAsia intends to compete in a market so heavily dependent on OTAs and if there were lessons for other airlines.
 
In Part 2 of the story on Tuesday, Olina went deep into how Ashoka and a few other private universities are experimenting with pedagogy in liberal arts. The higher ed pendulum in India has swung too far toward professional courses. (Hearing ‘science is liberal arts', ‘history is the master discipline’ was music to my ossified ears.) Quality higher education is a matter of right, and private endeavours like Ashoka can only be a drop in the ocean. But it’s worth diving for pearls. (Plus, it's a free read.)
 
Wednesday saw Vandana and Shreedhar dissecting RateGain’s strategy, a price comparison and distribution firm, that counts every Fortune 500 travel & hospitality company in the world among its clients. After going slow-and-steady for 15 years, the company is now on M&A steroids. Why?
 
On Thursday, Abinaya wrote the first ever story, and a definitive one at that, on Amazon’s private label strategy in India. However limiting India’s FDI rules may be, it isn’t limiting Amazon’s ambition or creativity in building new brands, acquiring more sellers, grooming future brands even. The margins here are too good to not be pursued; let competition watchdogs do their homework.
 
We ended the week with Sumanth taking apart OYO’s recent valuation report that it submitted to the Registrar of Companies. The reported loss is more than 4X of the projected loss. Normally, as startups scale, core financial metrics related to losses improve. The exact opposite is happening to OYO. It claims to be the third largest hotel chain in the world; coming second or first won’t fix its elusive profitability. It doesn’t help that SoftBank, post-WeWork IPO fiasco, can no longer coddle its investee companies.
 
If you missed reading any of the above, get to them today. Meanwhile I, inspired by the liberal arts flavour of the week, will go back to Will & Ariel Durant’s The Lessons of History. (Dusted out my grandfather’s copy, a brittle 1968 edition.)
 
Have a great weekend.
 
Warmly,

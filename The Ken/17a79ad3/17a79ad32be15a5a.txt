Good Morning Ishan,

"We are currently in a funding environment that is capital-rich and caution-poor,” says Sumanth in today’s story. VC investment in India is slated to hit $20 billion this year. There’s so much dry powder, FOMO, and money floating around that some startups are raising “seed rounds” of $40 million.

But there’s also something even more promising happening at the other end of the startup spectrum. Exits.

For long, the lack of meaningful exits (bar a few outliers like Flipkart) was held up as a major reason why the Indian startup ecosystem wasn’t all that meaningful for global investors. While that still remains true relative to markets like the US, exits are now happening with increasing frequency.

There's PlaySimple, the gaming startup that parlayed a mere $4 million in VC funding to a massive $360 million exit last week. 

There’s Big Basket, Curefit, and 1mg, three well-known startups that sold majority stakes to a 153-year old conglomerate that has a serious case of super app envy, the Tata Group. 

There’s also the fantastic case of diagnostics major Thyrocare whose founder sold a majority stake in the company to e-pharmacy startup Pharmeasy for $400 million, but then invested roughly a third of that amount in the company he sold to, at a valuation that was over 2X of its last one.

In different ways, at different stages, for different reasons, exits are finally happening.

Is this a structural change or merely the compressed after-effects of the pandemic? Why are some founders selling and some buying? That’s Sumanth’s insightful story today.

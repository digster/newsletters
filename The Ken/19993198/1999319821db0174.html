Starting February 2025, Chinese manufacturers have aggressively slashed the prices of several key active pharmaceutical ingredients (APIs) they export by a massive 40–50%. In fact, the price cuts cover the entire gamut of 41 critical molecules supported by India’s API production-linked incentive (PLI) scheme, the primary goal of which is to make India (which imports a huge number of key APIs for its pharmaceutical industry) self-reliant in the segment. 

Since its launch in March 2020, the scheme has driven somewhat decent results, slowly increasing local API production. As of December 2024, some Rs 1,500 crore in sales had been recorded under the scheme. But the price cuts of the past half-year threaten to put all this progress, and future plans, in jeopardy. 

Because in some cases, Chinese prices are now below the cost of production in India—an existential blow to local companies founded on the backs of government policies aimed at building India’s API muscle. 

Just ask Kinvan. 

Set up in October 2020 to take advantage of the PLI scheme for potassium clavulanate, a critical API used alongside antibiotics like amoxicillin to delay the development of resistance to the medicine, Kinvan has been making and supplying this molecule to Indian pharma majors such as Alkem Laboratories and Mankind Pharma for a few years now. 

But in early 2025, Chinese manufacturers slashed their prices for the API by 40%, from $240 (~Rs 21,000) per unit to $140 (~Rs 12,400). Suddenly confronted with far cheaper competition in the market, Kinvan was forced to drop its own prices too, from Rs 17,000 per unit to Rs 11,750–12,000, according to Sabarish Sankaranarayanan, director of finance at Kinvan. 

Of course, this is hardly the first time China has whipped out this tried-and-tested strategy to target India’s domestic pharma ambitions. In the 2000s, China’s undercutting crushed local producers of key molecules like amoxicillin and penicillin by bringing market rates down by half, or even lower. 

It’s now past time to dust off the lessons from that ordeal.

“A lesson learned one time has to be learned properly, and it has to be passed through all the generations.”

Sabarish Sankaranarayanan, Director of Finance, Kinvan

Ground reality

Indian API makers aren’t yet staring at instant, total destruction, because the pharma companies that buy from them are aware China’s price cuts aren’t permanent. “They are not going to slash the price forever. They’ll slash the price down to kill the business in India, and once local API companies are shut down, they are going to recover their losses by raising prices,” Sankaranarayanan pointed out.

Drug makers, cognizant of this fact and with the understanding that they have to preserve reliable, long-term lines of supply, aren’t abandoning local API vendors wholesale. For instance, Alkem consumes more than 20 tonnes of Kinvan’s product per month, and the pharma company is buying the API at “a respectable price”. Other drug makers are doing the same thing, even as they take the opportunity to stock up on key APIs at current low prices.  

But there’s no gainsaying the fact that the Chinese threat is real, and significant. In fact, it was the fear of such dumping from China that provoked countries like India, US, EU, and Japan to ramp up incentives for local API production. As of June 2025, China accounted for roughly 20% of all API production in the world, supplying them to over 180 countries—an indispensable cog in the world’s pharmaceutical supply chain. 

But China didn’t get where it is by just trusting the free market. Its dominance in the segment is driven in large part by significant subsidies, tax breaks, low-interest loans, infrastructure support, and favourable policies since the 1990s, which make the cost of production in the country 35–40% lower than most competitors. 

“We are actually 20 years behind them, to be frank,” said one Indian API industry expert.

India’s PLI scheme is an attempt to seed just such an ecosystem. 

For instance, “for clavulanic acid (Kinvan’s product), China already holds 90% of the entire world market”, according to Sankaranarayanan. Kinvan’s team always had the know-how to manufacture the molecule. Yet, the company was only incorporated in October 2020, after the PLI scheme went into effect, because they “did not have the courage” to do it before. 

The risks and hurdles were too great.

A question of power

The PLI scheme, to be frank, doesn’t offer much to API makers on a broad, infrastructural level. But it has made a start in solving some systemic issues. 

India’s API PLI scheme chooses one company to support for every one of the 41 essential molecules on its list. (These materials account for 67% of the APIs for which India is more than 90% import-dependent.) There is a financial incentive in the picture, which is a percentage of the incremental sales companies under the scheme are able to make—typically 4–6% of the additional sales after selection under the PLI scheme.

But industry executives say this is far too little. 

Especially considering power costs. 

On average, about 15–20% of the cost of fermentation-based API products is for energy alone. This is 30–35% for Kinvan’s molecule, and can reach 40–45% in some cases. Sankaranarayanan compares this to Chinese manufacturers getting almost free electricity and free land:

“Energy cost in India is very high—it goes to Rs 8–10 per unit of electricity. But if you go to China, it’s basically Rs 5–6. And with subsidies, they used to get it at Rs 2–3 or even Rs 1 earlier.”

As a result, winning the PLI tender for a molecule isn’t enough for an Indian API maker to become competitive on the global stage. Companies such as Aurobindo have even surrendered certain molecules like Thiocyanide and 7 ACON after winning PLI tenders for them because they would be too costly to make even with government support. 

Where the scheme has helped is in cutting through the miles and miles of red tape. Just getting approval from the pollution control board, for instance, can take API companies one or two years in India. 

“To start a company in India, manufacturers need almost 100 licences from different authorities,” noted Sankaranarayanan, “but the government (the Union government and the Department of Pharmaceuticals) has actually helped us in speaking to state governments if any approval is pending. They were proactive in getting the approvals fast-tracked.”

API makers not supported by the PLI scheme still struggle with such issues. 

Even in the case of Kinvan, though, it was as much about grabbing the first-mover advantage as exploiting the benefits of the PLI scheme. “The cost of setting up this plant now is very high. If someone were to attempt to do the same thing, the replacement cost would easily be 2X to 2.5X more than what Kinvan spent. Nobody in India wants to set up this plant now,” Sankaranarayanan said. 

Thus, the need to protect all this fledgling progress from China’s most recent offensive. Indian API industry executives think this particular round of price cuts isn’t being pushed by the Chinese government, but by Chinese API makers who have formed a cartel and are trying to suppress the market. In Kinvan’s case, imports of the molecule it produces breached 330 metric tonnes in just the four months ending July 2025, compared to 825 metric tonnes in the whole of the previous financial year. 

A worried industry has hastened to seek the implementation of minimum import prices (MIP) on impacted APIs, including Kinvan. One such MIP was recently imposed on Centrion Pharma’s molecule, and the Indian Pharmaceutical Association (IPA) and other industry bodies are drawing up a bigger list of candidates for the same. 

But MIPs, by their very nature, can’t be a one-size-fits-all solution. “It must be tailored to each molecule,” said an IPA executive. 

The more important interventions, thus, have to take a much longer-term view and come with far higher financial support. 

Perhaps just start with making power costs more palatable, and see where things go from there?

Good Morning Ishan,
Welcome back. Hope you had a restful weekend. We’re relieved no new banking scam has broken out in this period. Wicked joke, I admit. But you know, the thick-and-fast news of ~$2.35 billion worth of bank loans gone sour has shaken us all.
As the news of the jewellery maker, the pen maker and the PC maker swindling banks unfolded over the last few days, there was one group that was fuming publicly: Startup founders. The bank debt ecosystem doesn’t exist for them. For most, even their own banks, which peek into years of business data, don’t trust them for debt. Indian tech entrepreneurship has internalised this lack of debt funding from traditional banks, but it doesn’t have to be this way.
A mix of sources of capital is always good, but tech entrepreneurs have mostly restricted themselves to raising and spending the most expensive capital—venture capital.
It’s not to say the banks haven’t tried. But the filters they apply seem to be for rejecting applications rather than for selecting them. Most startups fail at the first filter.
Also, as today’s story shows, IRONY is writ large at most steps and in most options. Think about it: A bank evaluates your debt application better if you have raised venture capital. Or take this instance: A founder needs to build a relationship with the bank over the years. But once she qualifies for a bigger line of credit, and if she also happens to raise risk capital at the time, then she’s in a bit of a soup.
Money matters are always complex. But matters between startups and banks are far more complex. Rohin’s story today is a lucid exposition of the tentative relationship between the two. To quote a super takeaway quote: “Most startups don’t get this. If you don’t take debt in good times, you won’t get it in bad times.”

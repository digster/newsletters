Good Morning Ishan,
To be honest, technology, in any form, intimidates me.
If the Wi-Fi goes down, I would wait for a technician to come home and fix it, refusing to follow any advice the call centre support gave me. The Ken gave me a new MacBook the week before I joined. I am yet to unpack it. My old laptop is the only one I dare to use. Any change in screen size, interface, or font unnerves me.
I have a smartphone, but I am afraid of using its “smart” features—lest I open up an application, and my phone gets ‘corrupted’ or worse, I lose data or contacts. If I ever had to buy a new phone, I’d rather let my husband pick what he wanted.
For the first time this week, I had to use a content management system, which, as a writer, I should have learnt years ago. I had resisted so far, coming up with a thousand excuses every time someone coaxed me. But this week, I had no choice.
Backed into a corner, I had to do it. And guess what? It was easy. Just a set of instructions to follow. I realised one thing: I could do it. With practice, I could even be good at this thing.
Why am I sharing this with you? Yesterday, I saw an advertisement by a tech-auto company that spoke of the prevailing gender bias in our homes and society towards women and their relationship with technology. The ad interviewed teenagers whose mothers worked in senior tech roles (one designed an EV vehicle, another was spearheading digital payments). But when it came to their own homes, the kids naturally turned to their fathers for advice on anything related to tech.
How do children attach gender to roles? They pick up cues at home, cues that we drop unconsciously.
I am guilty of it, too.
So yesterday, when I received an Amazon delivery for a VR-based toy—which needed to be assembled and then operated through a mobile app—my 4-year-old instinctively told me that we would wait for Abba (dad) to come home and fix it; I told him Mumma could do it too. It took me a good three hours, but I did it.
It’s all in the mind just got a new meaning. On that note, allow me to take you through a quick recap of the stellar reporting at The Ken this week.
On Monday, my colleagues Seetharaman and Anand had an intriguing story on pricey stocks. MRF hit the headlines for its share price hitting the roof (above Rs 1 lakh apiece), sending the market into a frenzy. It leads the pack of seven companies whose shares are priced above Rs 10,000 ($122). Listed companies generally want to have more retail shareholders, which means doing stock splits and issuing bonus shares to make the shares cheaper. But why are MRF, Nestlé, and other pricey stocks happy being exceptions to the rule? And how has that not led to a decline in retail customers but, in fact, has gone up in some cases? [This](https://the-ken.com/story/why-mrf-nestle-and-other-pricey-stocks-are-happy-being-exceptions/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter) link will take you to their richly reported piece.
If there was a bet being placed on our most fun story this week, mine would be on Katrina’s [report](https://the-ken.com/story/asias-next-gambling-hub-reels-in-casino-suppliers-with-slot-machine-craze/?utm_source=recap_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=recap_newsletter) tracking how casino suppliers are taking the Philippines more seriously. Did you know the number of slot machines outnumber ATMs in the country? Yep. That’s why for companies that develop these machines, it has become worthwhile to customise them for Filipinos and offer a bigger jackpot. She makes a strong case on how the Philippines may give Macau a run for its money by becoming a shiny destination for international visitors who want to try their luck on casino floors.
Wednesday’s story (was a challenging one for me because it) deals with a technology that is relatively new—drones. Anushka does a commendable job of breaking down complex ideas as she takes us through this sector now, in the limelight for defence drone startup Ideaforge’s IPO. The defence sector dominates 70% of the drone market, and Ideaforge outperforms other manufacturers here. Now, startups are venturing into newer use cases like deliveries. But with India yet to establish comprehensive guidelines for delivery and logistics drones—a policy logjam for 18 months—how will this ever take off? Intrigued? Next month, the alma mater of seven British and one Indian Prime Minister Jawaharlal Nehru—Harrow School—is opening its first India campus next month. A few weeks later, the 170-year-old British boarding school, Wellington College, will also open its doors in Pune. A rising affluent Indian class and fading charm of China (a preferred destination for these schools until now) are driving several international schools to India. But as they enter the Indian education market, these schools, with centuries of experience, are learning new lessons—in culture, affordability, and adaptability. Do read and share it; it’s free.

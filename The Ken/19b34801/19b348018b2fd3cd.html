India is hardly alone in re-embracing nuclear energy after avoiding it like the plague for years.

The European Union wants to increase its nuclear capacity by 50% by 2050, and the US is looking to quadruple it. India is even more ambitious, aiming for an 11X jump from its 8.8 gigawatt of installed capacity.

And the first step towards that is loosening the government’s stranglehold on nuclear-power plants.

The recent passage of the Sustainable Harnessing and Advancement of Nuclear Energy for Transforming India Bill allows the private sector to operate nuclear plants. An American company, Holtec International, is counting on small modular reactors (SMRs) to be the power producers’ choice over traditional reactors.

SMRs require half the land as the reactors in use now, and can be installed in just over a third of the time as their larger counterparts.

“We could see small modular reactors powering entire industrial corridors and even smaller towns, bringing nuclear energy closer to where people live,” Holtec founder and CEO Kris Singh told The Ken.

So there’s of course a business case for Holtec to want to install 200 SMRs across India. But how persuasive is its sales pitch when Holtec’s first SMR will only be commissioned by 2030? In fact, there are only two operational SMRs all over the world.

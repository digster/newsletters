Good Morning Ishan,
 
“In this country, you gotta make the money first. Then when you get the money, you get the power.”

Watching classic movies for the first time is something I’ve embraced during the pandemic. The most recent was Scarface, a film immortalised by the widely-recited quote above. The success of Tony Montana, the gangster played by Al Pacino who rises to the top of Miami's drug world, is the ultimate disruption. He went from fresh-off-the-boat broke to the top  because he figured out the system and made it work for him.

That's much like our story today. Not because of drugs, fast cars or other vices—but because India’s deep tech ecosystem is breaking out with ambitions of its own to change the order at the top.

It started from precious little. Historically, deep tech startups have relied on the benevolence of government grants. Funding usually trickles in from avenues like a fund of funds, and funding rarely goes beyond a Series B or C round. As a result, never mind unicorn territory, US$100 million exits are a rarity.

But just as in Scarface, the first part of the plan is coming together—getting the money.

There’s never been more capital for deep tech in India. Inflexor Ventures just closed a fund that could reach over US$90 million, while Exseed Electron Fund is about to announce its first investments from its second fund, which weighs in at US$60 million. Other investors may be attracted by the numerous and sizeable deep tech company acquisitions that have gone largely unreported by the press.

Investing in deep tech, however, is not the most appealing prospect for the average VC firm. The long gestation periods and considerable upfront investment that deep tech firms require for R&D, and the sector know-how required to evaluate these businesses, have ensured that despite the uptick in investor interest, the number of deep tech startups in the country is growing faster than the funding ecosystem. Could an influx of global capital, say, from the likes of a Tiger Global—which has recently been on a unicorn-minting spree in the country—finally help India's deep tech sector reach its potential?

When you get the money, you get the power.
 
Deep tech companies, however, want to hold the top position longer than Tony Montana did. Rather than total ‘disruption’, the goal is ‘transformation’—working with incumbents to make things better. Goodness knows that we could do with a more deep tech companies during these testing times.

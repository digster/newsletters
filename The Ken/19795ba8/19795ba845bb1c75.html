Turning away from heavy promotions in a bid for prestige hasn’t been enough to fend off its struggles

Ajio was supposed to be Reliance Retail’s e-commerce hero. But its quest for profits turned it into a bit of a mess. 47 million lost life insurance in one year. Nobody blinked

Everyone saw it coming—insurers, microinsurance, even healthcare trackers. But it’s a self-made crisis

India’s life insurers lost 26% of their customers this year. The growing defaults in loan-linked insurance products forced them to pull back. Banking experts say the problem lies in loans. Microfinance lenders can’t find new, paying borrowers, and they broke the ones they already had. So, now, millions of India’s poorest are no longer covered. And somehow, no one finds this surprising. Money matters

From Manipal to Shapoorji, private credit has tasted blood in India. What comes next?

More money is chasing fewer deals. Returns are falling. Banks could be back as rivals. Private-credit funds are bound to make mistakes

With AUM of $25 billion, private credit in India has attracted global majors like Apollo Global and Ares to a business dominated by Indian groups such as 360 One and Kotak. But as the number of stressed companies wanes and competition among funds rises, returns have begun to look ordinary. This could lead private-credit funds to riskier deals such as financing founders of tech startups. [Read the full story]

A VC’s non-guide to funding India’s deep-tech companies

Deep-tech funding has no playbook, says Speciale’s Vishesh Rajaram, who’s taken some of the boldest bets. This is also a reason why big VCs don’t play this game

Funding quantum security, humanoid robots (before they became a rage), or an electric plane in India requires a certain mindset. After 10 years in regular venture investing, Speciale’s Rajaram brought that mindset shift in his own fund. But even after eight years of deep-tech investing, he says there’s no playbook; unusual for a VC firm. [Read the full story]

The Big Four gain where Accenture and IBM feel the most pain

The likes of Deloitte and EY are exploiting the cracks in tech-services companies’ business to grow—by hiring more than half their partners from “industry”

The “Big Four” professional-service firms are on a hiring spree, targeting senior leaders from tech-service companies like Accenture and IBM. The Big Four want to expand into tech implementation, competing directly with IT, tech-service and product companies—and to do this fast, they need talent from industry. Meanwhile, Accenture and IBM have been suffering through changing management and operating structures, slower growth, and falling India focus—all of which are pushing top-talent to greener pastures. The likes of Deloitte and PwC are exploiting this discontent, and snatching up tech-service employees for high-risk-high-reward partner tracks at the Big Four. [Read the full story]

At Fogg deodorant’s owner, KKR’s PE math clashes with family-business wisdom

The tussle for control between the investor-backed management and founders Darshan and Dipam Patel has thrown Vini Cosmetics into chaos

After the professional management brought in by global PE firm KKR left Vini Cosmetics in late 2023, promoters Darshan and Dipam Patel got busy running the company. KKR also wanted the founders to steer the company, the parent of deodorant brand Fogg, to its former glory. But Fogg was no longer the shining star it had once been, as competitors started snapping at its heels. With fewer product launches and lesser marketing spend, KKR’s strategy for Vini is yet to work out. [Read the full story]

Urban Company’s IPO pitch wows investors: its gig-worker army is now an unlikely sales force

The home-services company is getting its technicians to market and sell its products, too. So far, the strategy has worked and investors love it. How long before it jumps the rails?

With its initial public offering subscribed over 108 times, Urban Company may have passed the litmus test for now. The company has been building a revenue stack over its common service professionals who also now happen to sell its products under the Native brand. These gig workers may increasingly double as sales agents as Native is further expected to develop air conditioners, washing machines, and other electronic appliances. While this approach differentiates Urban Company from its peers and has attracted investors, the going now may be tougher as service professionals may not want to remain off the payroll. The world’s largest back office may yet remain a back office—training the very machines that will take its place

Capgemini’s $3.3 billion buyout of WNS showed what was really valuable in BPMs—the process data they had been handling for decades.

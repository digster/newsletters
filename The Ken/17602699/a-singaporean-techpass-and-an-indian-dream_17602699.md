Just over three years ago, India was not in the best of places with Singapore. The island-nation had clamped down on tech visas, leaving Indian techies a bit stranded.
Then-president of trade association Nasscom, R Chandrashekhar, had said, "If things don't change, companies will have to look for alternate ways for the region… Search for alternate locations."
"This is really not keeping with the overall objectives of the Comprehensive Economic Cooperation Agreement (CECA), which is to encourage economic activities in both directions," he further said.
Somewhere in the three years, things changed. Before the pandemic struck, things "were almost up to 2016 levels" of friendliness with Singapore. More and more Indians were setting up shop and seeking jobs in the city-state, Gagan Sabharwal, Senior Director, Global Trade Development at Nasscom, told The Ken. "Even salary levels have increased 15-20% [for Indians in Singapore] over these years," he said.
Zoom out from Singapore, though, and you’ll find that the story is very different.
Indians have been finding it harder to seek work permits and visas for a while now. The US, under the Trump administration, got so conservative, it left some 800,000 Indians on the waitlist for an employment-based green card. And this is only scratching the surface of the problem in a year where opportunities have dried up, and entire economies have collapsed.
So this is quite the time for Singapore to be announcing a new work visa, one that allows a two-year stay. And if you make it, you get to do whatever you want, no questions asked.
Called the Tech.Pass, the new Singaporean visa comes close on the heels of countries opening up to long-term tourism. But this one is strictly focussed on entrepreneurs—a group of very elite leaders in tech who must meet certain criteria.
Be as that may, it still spells opportunity. High-net worth techies, CXOs, anyone who checks two out of three boxes on the requirements list can apply. No matter where they live.
Nadine dives deep into what the Tech.Pass spells for global tech hubs moving forward. Will Singapore be a worthy opponent to the Bay Area post-2020? Will India find more Indian board members in Singaporean companies? And will this then trickle down to providing more jobs?

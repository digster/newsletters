ITC’s 24 Mantra deal and organic foods’ unending wait for glory. How long before the market for these products actually catches up to the hype?

By Seetharaman G
4 min read
View in browser

Good Morning Ishan,

In April 2024, Tata Consumer Products bought Fabindia-owned Organic India. Last week, ITC said it’s acquiring Sresta Natural Bioproducts, the owner of 24 Mantra Organic. 

Two of India’s largest packaged-food companies taking over two of the oldest and best-known organic-food brands. It surely doesn’t get better than this, right? 

At least, that’s how it seemed when Tata Consumer paid Rs 1,900 crore ($222 million) for its acquisition last April, or 5.6X Organic India’s FY24 sales. And Organic India was barely even profitable. But investors and analysts cheered Tata Consumer on because they thought it had gotten a head start on its rivals in a business that was ready to burst onto the mainstream.

Relative to the Organic India acquisition, ITC’s investors have reason to think 24 Mantra is a steal. ITC is paying a mere Rs 470 crore, or 1.6X the latter’s sales. But why? Has the organic-food industry taken a turn for the worse in recent months? 

Anything but. 

Instant grocery delivery, whose popularity has only shot up in this period, and organic foods are supposed to be a great pair. Neither is driven by discounts, and they both target affluent urban consumers.“It (organic foods) is a conviction-led category,” Sresta founder Rajashekar Reddy Seelam told The Ken. So, such brands finally have a distribution channel that isn’t price-sensitive supermarkets or fatigue-inducing e-commerce marketplaces. Now is as good a time as any for the likes of 24 Mantra. 

But a quick look at its numbers tell a different story. After a sharp rise in FY21, when consumers cooped up at home wanted to eat better, 24 Mantra’s annual sales have been largely flat since, at Rs 300–320 crore. 

One reason is buyers returning to conventional grains, pulses, and oils. Another is the state-run apex body for farm exports suspending half-a-dozen certification agencies out of a total of 20. The move, along with the export bans on wheat and sugar and the US’s anti-dumping policy on soymeal, halved India’s annual organic exports to under $500 million in the three years ending FY24. (Exports comprise almost half of 24 Mantra’s business.)

What could have also worked to ITC’s advantage is that 24 Mantra’s investors must have been quite eager for an exit after its listing plans fell through. Ventureast and Peepul Capital, which control three-quarters of the company, have been 24 Mantra’s backers since 2007 and 2011, respectively.

That’s an eternity in the venture-capital universe. 

Organic India’s performance is just as underwhelming. Its sales fell 16% between FY21 and FY24. Sure, its margins are better than 24 Mantra’s, and its tea business dovetails with that of its acquirer. Even so, Tata Consumer might well have overpaid for Organic India. 

What’s baffling is that neither Organic India (founded in 1997) nor 24 Mantra (2004) has even been able to reach Rs 500 crore in sales. 

In an interview with The Ken in 2022, Reddy said there are three million households consuming organic foods, and the addressable market is 25 million homes. But maybe even the moneyed class doesn’t think that the 60–70% premium these products command over their alternatives (and over 100% in oils) is worth it. Especially when many are still sceptical about the veracity of the “organic” label. 

“The ideal premium is 30%,” Reddy said, “It balances the interests of farmers and consumers.” On their own, Organic India and 24 Mantra couldn’t do that. But the manufacturing and distribution might of Tata Consumer and ITC will help them defray, at least to some extent, the high costs of organic foods. 

But it’s not just about a more efficient shop floor and squeezing some margins from retailers. Scaling this business means ridding thousands of acres of farmland of synthetic agri-input residues. That’s at least a three-year process. 

Persuading cultivators to make the switch, on the other hand, presupposes a growing base of shoppers willing to fill more of their cart with organic foods.

The inflection point will come when people suddenly start asking for such products without too much prompting. Seeding is already happening; satisfied people will start asking for more. Secondly, growth in the health and wellness market—standing at Rs 60,000 crore ($7.7 billion) today and growing at 18% CAGR—will eventually drive organic. Because, even if people don’t believe in organic, they want healthy stuff. This confluence of many factors will snowball into something big. What I am not able to predict is whether it’ll happen in two years or four years.

Well, it just isn’t happening yet.

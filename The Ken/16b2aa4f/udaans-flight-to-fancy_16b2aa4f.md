Would you rather fight 100 duck-sized horses, or one horse-sized duck?
I didn’t come up with that. It’s a hypothetical question that originated on the internet forum, Reddit. Since then, it’s been posed to celebrities, Barack Obama, and even showed up in a Supreme Court confirmation in the US Senate. The answers vary. Margaret Atwood, the novelist, said the hundred duck-sized horses would be ‘easy to stampede’. Bill Murray, the actor, said he’d try to ride the horse-sized duck and then strangle it. When Warren Buffett was asked the question, he said he’d have to purchase both sets of animals and study them closely. Boring.
Companies who venture into the e-commerce market in India have a similar choice ahead of them. To sell online would mean that they’d have to compete with Flipkart and Amazon. For a long time, the choice was obvious - fight the horse-sized duck.
But after regulations were imposed on B2C companies a couple of years back, suddenly, the larger, more fragmented B2B e-commerce space started to look more attractive. Several companies jumped in. Ninjacart. Shadowfax. Jumbotail.
The biggest of which is a company called Udaan.
Udaan is an unusual company. Despite becoming the fastest Indian company to breach the $1 billion barrier, taking them just over two years, very little is known about it. How does it work? Who are the sellers? Who are the buyers? Do the economics make sense? These are key questions, because if success in B2c is about changing the way people buy, success in B2B is about changing how people sell.
Abinaya, in her first story for The Ken, opens in Bengaluru’s Yeshwantpur Yard, the city’s biggest wholesale market, where Udaan is a prominent fixture. From there, she weaves multiple interviews with the company’s suppliers, buyers and employees alongside a detailed analysis of Udaan’s financials, to create a fascinating picture about Udaan's high-risk high-reward battle plans that combine logistics and financial credit services to disrupt the B2B commerce space.
It’s not going to be easy for Udaan.
Fighting a hundred duck-sized horses seldom is.

Good Morning Ishan,
How many investment bankers does it take to change a lightbulb at an IPO-bound company? Sometimes three, four, or, sometimes, even 18.
With 85 issues live and another 85 in the pipeline, no single banker can carry the load. Companies now call in syndicates: one to set the price, another to craft the story, and others to keep the stock liquid long after listing.
That shift has cracked open a market once ruled by HDFC, Axis, JM Financial, and Goldman Sachs. Dam Capital and Equirus have muscled in by doing what the giants often don’t: staying close to promoters, guiding them through fickle valuations, and sticking around for QIPs, block deals, and follow-ons. Dam has leaned on its “promoter-led” intimacy, while Equirus thrives by navigating India’s new quarter-to-quarter market cycles.
The stakes are clear. Overprice the stock, and you risk a Paytm*-style tanking. Underprice it, and promoters feel shortchanged. Either way, in a market that now hosts one in every five global IPOs, trust and timing are everything.
Valli’s [story today](https://the-ken.com/story/mid-sized-i-banks-dam-and-equirus-brought-knives-to-the-ipo-gunfight-and-won/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter) dives into the kinds of shovels India’s mid-tier bankers are selling in a gold rush.

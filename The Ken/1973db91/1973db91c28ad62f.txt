Can depositors beat RBI’s rate-cut blues?. Yes. But they should move fast, smart, and safe

The monetary policy committee (MPC) of the Reserve Bank of India (RBI)—it’s a mouthful and also very important. This arbiter of our collective interest-rate fate could cut the repo rate by 25 basis points (0.25%) tomorrow, 6 June, according to many economists. Some even expect a “jumbo” rate cut of 50 basis points (0.5%).

What the MPC of the RBI does will be known with certainty only tomorrow. What is reasonably certain is that India is in the midst of an interest-rate downcycle. That is, rates in the economy are going down—thanks to the RBI, the country’s central bank, cutting the repo rate by 25 basis points in February and then by another 25 basis points in April. This was after a long lull, as seen in the chart https://timesofindia.indiatimes.com/business/india-business/rbi-set-to-cut-rates-3rd-time-in-row/articleshow/121557054.cms from Times of India below. It’s a cycle of ups and downs.

The repo is the rate at which the RBI lends to other banks in the country. So, when the RBI hikes or cuts the repo, commercial banks follow with their interest rates for customers (or are at least expected to). This then cascades through the economy. 

The RBI now seems to be in rate-cut mode. Inflation appears under control, at least for now, while economic growth may need a helping hand. Higher rates restrict money flow and help control inflation, while lower rates make borrowing cheaper and help growth. The interplay between inflation and growth makes it quite possible that the repo rate will be cut again tomorrow. 

All this is good news for borrowers. If and when banks transmit—or pass on—the lower rates, borrowers will pay lesser interest on their loans. But it’s not-so-good for depositors; they’ll earn less interest than before on their deposits.

This has already started playing out. Many banks have begun cutting https://www.businesstoday.in/personal-finance/investment/story/icici-hdfc-canara-bank-cut-fixed-deposit-rates-under-rs-3-crore-477970-2025-05-27 their deposit rates, and more could follow suit. And if the RBI cuts the repo rate again, banks could go deeper with their deposit rate cuts too. 

What should depositors do now? Should they even do anything? I think they should—and there are some good options.

Lock-in soon

While banks have started cutting them, deposit rates remain attractive in many cases. Some small finance banks are still offering between 8% and 8.4% on deposits with tenures ranging from one year to five years. It might be a good idea to lock in such rates now, before they fall further. A horizon of three to five years can avoid reinvestment risk—the risk that you might have to reinvest at lower rates after a year or so. 

But as I wrote in this https://the-ken.com/kaching/deposit-insurance-for-all-account-combos-is-cool-but-dont-bank-on-just-one-bank/ Ka-Ching! piece two years ago, don’t go overboard with just one or two banks—especially small finance banks—even if deposit insurance is sizable across account-name combinations (Rs 5 lakh for each account combination in each bank).

Besides, depositors can, and should, look beyond banks too.

Small savings, big returns

Small savings schemes such as the Public Provident Fund (PPF) which currently offer 7.1% could, in fact, give better post-tax returns for many depositors than small finance banks. That’s because the interest earned on the PPF is exempt from tax, while that on bank deposits is not. The PPF is also as safe as it gets, being fully guaranteed by the government.

Not just the PPF, even the Sukanya https://the-ken.com/kaching/a-sukanya-samriddhi-account-is-a-little-girls-best-friend/ Samriddhi Scheme, with a rate of 8.2% currently, is a neat choice for those with daughters, with the interest exempt from tax and also government-guaranteed. 

But only Rs 1.5 lakh can be invested annually in both the PPF and the Sukanya Samriddhi Scheme. Also, these schemes are for the really long-term—the PPF runs for 15 years and Sukanya Samriddhi until your darling daughter turns 21. The interest gets compounded and returned at maturity. But the rates are not locked until maturity though—the accumulated corpus earns the interest that the government declares each quarter.

For elderly citizens looking to invest larger sums (up to Rs 30 lakh) and seeking regular payouts at a locked-in rate of interest, the Senior Citizen Savings Scheme is a fine choice. The interest rate on this five-year deposit at the time of investment is locked in until maturity, and it is currently at 8.2%. The interest is taxable. Even so, the post-tax return is better than what most banks offer today.

Then, there is the National Savings Certificate (NSC). But at 7.7% currently (fixed from investment until maturity) and taxable, the return is not the best. Instead, depositors have a better choice in the RBI’s floating rate savings bonds that offer 0.35% over the NSC rate. So, currently, these bonds give an interest of 8.05%, which is taxable. These are good supplementary investments to the deposits mentioned above in the current times.

Debt MFs, if carefully chosen

Some debt mutual funds (MF) can also help fixed-income investors beat the rate-cut blues. That’s because these debt MFs reflect the inverse relationship between interest rates and bond prices. In other words, as interest rates fall, bond prices rise, and this boosts the value of the debt MF scheme. 

But many debt MF schemes can be risky, so choices must be made carefully. In a nutshell, long-maturity debt schemes that invest in safe instruments such as government securities (G-Secs), or public sector bonds, or top-rated corporate bonds could fit the bill. This write-up https://www.valueresearchonline.com/stories/223772/best-fixed-income-mutual-fund-falling-interest-rate/ by mutual fund research house Value Research has good pointers on what to look for in such schemes. 

A word of caution though. If you don’t know how to select these schemes or don’t have an advisor to guide you, it’s probably better to stick to bank deposits, small savings schemes, and maybe safe deposits offered by some reputable corporates with competitive rates. 

They may seem boring, but that’s perfectly fine. 

“Investing should be more like watching paint dry or watching grass grow,” said Nobel Prize winning economist Paul Samuelson.

This applies even more to fixed-income investing than equity investing, I think. Return of capital is far more important here than return on capital.

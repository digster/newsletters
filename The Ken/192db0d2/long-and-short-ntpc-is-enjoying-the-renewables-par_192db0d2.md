Long and Short
Wed, 30 Oct 24
The story behind the numbers and motivations driving India's stock market..
Good Morning Ishan,
Four years ago, NTPC, the state-run giant that’s India’s largest power producer, stunned industry watchers and investors by saying it would no longer build coal-fired plants.
The strategic shift was consistent with the times. Covid had driven oil prices to their lowest point ever. Larry Fink, chief executive of asset manager Blackrock, used his annual missives to exhort corporate leaders to embrace sustainability. Environment, social, and governance investing caught on among mutual funds.
So it stood to reason that NTPC would set up a subsidiary exclusively for solar and wind energy. But for a company that went by “National Thermal Power Corporation” until 2005 to shun electricity made from coal, even if not entirely, was a strong signal.
The age of renewables had begun.
Wrong.
NTPC, currently worth Rs 4 lakh crore ($47.6 billion), has not only walked back its declaration on thermal plants as demand for electricity has surged in the post-pandemic years, it is also rapidly adding coal-fired facilities. Last week, the company said it’s building 11.6 gigawatts (1 gigawatt equals 1,000 megawatts) of thermal capacity. That’s almost as much as its contracted renewables capacity.
Right after NTPC threw its weight behind renewables in 2020, I [argued](https://the-ken.com/story/adani-renew-watch-out-ntpcs-set-to-eclipse-indias-solar-sector/) how it could shake up the industry.
While NTPC’s ambitious clean energy plans will be a shot in the arm for India’s own solar ambitions, it’s bound to have rattled top clean power developers such as Gautam Adani-owned Adani Green Energy, Goldman Sachs-backed Renew Power, and the New York Stock Exchange-listed Azure Power Global. The predicament of smaller players is far worse …
“It will not be possible for companies to compete with NTPC,” says the clean energy company CEO quoted above. As a quasi-sovereign entity, NTPC has access to much cheaper debt than its private peers. This affords NTPC a deeper appetite for risk, allowing it to settle for lower returns on projects.
This has indeed played out, with NTPC becoming the fourth-largest clean-energy producer—after Adani, Renew, and Greenko. Such is NTPC’s confidence in the business that its renewables unit, NTPC Green, filed to go public last month. Sebi, the markets regular, greenlit the Rs 10,000 crore share sale on Monday.
In 2020, few businesses seemed better positioned than renewables to take advantage of industry’s newfound love for sustainability, the government’s largesse, and investors’ desire to turn the first two into handsome gains.
Renew merged with a Nasdaq-listed blank-cheque firm in 2021. But its shares have since slid by a third. This is despite Renew being an early mover in the business, having such a marquee backer as Goldman Sachs, and not sacrificing profits at the altar of scale by quoting ridiculously low tariffs to win projects.
India’s plans to add both vast amounts of solar-energy capacity and locally produce the photovoltaic panels that make up a solar farm have come undone.
In the years after Modi first became prime minister, in 2014, the narrative around clean energy in India was almost entirely to do with building solar farms at a frenetic pace.
Where the panels came from barely mattered. India needed a lot of them, and it needed them cheap. There was nowhere to look but China.
As part of its global commitment to tackle climate, India first said it would have 175 GW of renewables capacity by 2022 (The current figure is 144 GW). And then, it set a target of 500 GW of renewables and nuclear capacity by 2030.
But India missed its first target by 40% and could flub the second one just as badly. It needs to build 50 GW of renewables every year to meet the target. But in 2023, it managed to add a measly 13.5 GW.
India bet billions on being a renewables powerhouse. The world had other plans, [The Ken](https://the-ken.com/story/india-bet-billions-on-being-a-renewables-powerhouse-the-world-had-other-plans/)
What’s more, if investors were obsessed with sustainability in 2020, they are now indifferent. Oil majors are no longer trumpeting their clean-energy transition; instead, they are [scooping up](https://www.wsj.com/business/energy-oil/big-oil-companies-will-just-keep-getting-bigger-ac194ae7) smaller rivals for billions of dollars. Before the damning report by short seller Hindenburg Research, Adani Green was worth three times as much as its coal-dependent sibling, Adani Power. Now, Adani Green is just 12% bigger.
A year ago, RK Singh, then India’s minister for power, [reportedly](https://www.thehindu.com/business/Industry/union-government-asks-private-firms-to-ramp-up-investments-in-coal-fired-plants-despite-global-pressure/article67564574.ece) told private power producers to invest in coal-fired capacity and “not miss the growth opportunity”.
Make no mistake, the share of renewables in India’s overall installed electricity capacity is bound to rise from the current 35%. Plus, the fact that there’s no listed renewables-only power producer besides Adani Green, which mutual funds have largely shunned, will add to NTPC Green’s appeal as a listed company.
But there’s no reason for investors to hanker after clean-energy companies to the exclusion of their polluting peers. NTPC shares have risen by a third this year, in line with BSE’s power index. The Sensex, by contrast, has grown by a mere 11%. It’s hard to overstate the resurgence of conventional energy companies.

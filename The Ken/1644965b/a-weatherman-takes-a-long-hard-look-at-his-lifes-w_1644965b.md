Many years ago, in the early 2000s, along with two local journalist friends, I met some senior officials at NOAA in Maryland. After a lengthy discussion, when we were leaving, I complimented them on their accurate weather prediction and how it was helping me brave the East Coast weather variations. (Warm clothes, umbrella, late evening engagements…all were planned based on The Boston Globe's weather news.)
The director smiled: “Say that to our families; they think we aren’t doing a good enough job.”
It was a darn good job, especially because back home, in India, all we saw those days in newspapers was sunrise and sunset time. It’d be some years before Skymet would make day-to-day weather forecast popular, a forecast that was much more than a flip of the coin.
Incidentally, Jatin Singh, founder of Skymet, started out by analysing weather data, making graphics and selling them to newspapers. And in 15 years, as India’s largest weather data company, he has come far. So far that he writes to chief ministers warning them, well in time, of natural disasters. He proposes to state governments that his lightning detection network (India’s first) can avert many, many deaths. From Mumbai floods to Kerala’s Cyclone Ockhi to peninsular India’s impending monsoon shortfall, Singh can help the India Meteorological Department do so much more with its own data. If, and only if, they agreed and he could reach every cell phone user.
There’s only a small window of opportunity before the global cell phone weather companies and tech giants come looking for local data.

CUET could be edtech’s golden ticket. So why isn’t it? The new-ish college entrance stands a chance to be a great boost for flagging edtechs. But it’s not popular yet
By Olina Banerji
4 min read

Good Morning Ishan,

You are reading our subscriber-only newsletter Ed Set Go—a weekly commentary on Indian edtech and education

As the year draws to a close, I thought it would be fun to look deeply at one exam—the Central Universities Common Entrance test (CUET).

CUET crash-landed into the lives of college aspirants in 2022 when Delhi University, along with other central and state universities, began granting admissions based on a student’s CUET score. In the dense universe of entrance exams, the test was a small, insignificant star. It had existed in some form since 2010, but the surge in its popularity came when the National Testing Agency took over and turned it into a school-leaving exam akin to the Chinese Gaokao or the American SAT.

The first few editions of the CUET were confusing and stressful for students who were just coming off their long slog of prepping for their board exams. CUET felt like an engineering or medical entrance exam, but the stakes, the diffused focus on subjects, and the school-based syllabus made it feel like an extension of the board exams.

This cognitive gap around an exam—how to prepare for it, and how to take it—is exactly the kind of gap that edtechs wait for. Just like for the JEE or UPSC, edtechs jumped in with their tailored CUET package, which takes students all the way from preparation to admission.

CUET stands at the crossroads of all the undergraduate entrance exams in the country. A door getting shut in medicine, engineering, or law often means a door opening for an undergraduate subject in commerce, science, or humanities. With a centralised entrance becoming mandatory, the CUET coaching industry has become an open field for anyone to establish dominance.

The numbers should’ve been irresistible. Approximately 140,000 students passed their Class 12 board exams in 2024—and that’s just one board. As the number of universities accepting CUET scores grew, edtechs saw a great opportunity to launch a new vertical in their business, one that could perpetually make money because, well, kids want to go to college. Edtechs like Adda247 and Physicswallah have promptly launched courses, among others.

But did that really happen?

CUET’s lack of legacy is both a boon and a burden

When edtechs launch new categories, the process is simple: they break down what needs to be taught, who’s going to teach it, and at what cost to the customer. They go about organising—recruiting or poaching—good teachers for the course and decide how to market the course to prospective students. For instance, the JEE exam prep is marketed to kids as early as Class 9. The high-stakes engineering entrance needs all the time you can give.

When it comes to CUET though, there isn’t a history or precedent to fall back on. Edtechs have had to feel their way through this one.

For one, the CUET exam doesn’t land you a life-long government job like the UPSC exam can. Neither does it get you a coveted spot in one of India’s engineering colleges. To students fresh out of a year-long slog for the boards, it’s yet another hurdle to cross.

There’s no “professional degree” at stake here, say edtech founders:

“Rao, who was once a UPSC aspirant himself, summarised civil-services coaching as a journey to achieve a dream that gives “power, authority, money and respect”. People are ready to invest “time and a huge amount of money in this segment”. In contrast, Rao observed that students don’t want to drop a year in their academic life or spend so much money on CUET. Parents already complain about spending huge amounts of money for school fees, additional tuition, and so on.”

The exam itself is a break from how kids have learned to digest information in school. Within a matter of months, students have to switch from discursive, subject, essay-like answers to a multiple-choice question format. Coaching schools, then, need to also operate on two levels—teach the content and teach students how to maximise their scoring potential by choosing the right questions etc. This is a non-trivial problem because edtechs have to assemble teams of teachers that can do both. If CUET ever became as popular as the JEE, this would mean edtechs would have to shell out to recruit teachers who are just as coveted by their competitors.

The biggest challenge, so far, seems to be the numbers enrolling for CUET classes. The potential market is massive, but the growth in enrollments has been slow. To buck that trend, edtechs like Physics Wallah are resorting to new marketing tactics:

“We talk to students about their next choice if they don’t clear the competitive exams. We got to know about the CUET backup that was now open for students,”

As a backup or an exam in its own right, CUET can be a new category for edtechs to hang their futures on. That is, if edtechs can give CUET its own identity and importance. CUET could be seen as an effective bridge to the UPSC or other entrances that students are bound to face later in life.

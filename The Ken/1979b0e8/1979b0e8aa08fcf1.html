It took Elon Musk’s Starlink three years to get a commercial licence in India. Three years. For a company that covers Ukraine in wartime and delivers hardware to the middle of the Amazon, the hold-up wasn’t technology. It was red tape. 

A few days ago, the telecom department finally handed Starlink a Global Mobile Personal Communication by Satellite (or GMPCS) licence—one that allows the company to offer satellite-based internet services in the country. Starlink’s response was basically: “Cool, now can we have spectrum?”

Not yet. Because getting a licence is not equal to launch readiness.

Trai, India’s telecom regulator, has capped the duration of satellite spectrum licences at five years, with a possible two-year extension. Starlink had asked for 20. So instead of certainty, what it’s got now is a running stopwatch.

Spectrum assignment, ground‑station approvals, security vetting, terminal certifications—all are still pending. India needs clear steps: spectrum allocation timelines, IN-SPACe clearance, testing windows. Only after all those boxes are ticked can Starlink actually sell dishes and connect users. Expect months more before commercial services begin, despite telco tie‑ups. 

That hasn’t stopped India’s telecom operators from trying to get ahead of the curve. Or at least look like they are.

Last week, Vodafone Idea signed a deal with AST SpaceMobile to bring direct-to-device satellite connectivity to India. The announcement was bold. The details, less so. No integration timeline, no capex plan, no pricing model. Just a broad agreement that sounded more like a placeholder than a rollout strategy. In India’s satcom space, this is becoming a genre: deals that check the innovation box without opening the service tap.

Still, Starlink is moving.

It plans to roll out services in India by August. The pricing is… optimistic. A dish that costs Rs 33,000 upfront, plus Rs 3,000–4,200 per month. That’s about 8–12X what most Indian households pay for unlimited fibre broadband, which typically starts around Rs 399 per month. India’s average revenue per user (Arpu) for broadband is Rs 220. So unless you live in a Himalayan village or your house is surrounded by tigers, it’s a tough sell.

Starlink is offering this in a market with over 955 million broadband users already—95% of them on mobile data—and nearly 700,000 km of government-laid optical fibre under Bharatnet. And it’s not like this is static: India adds about 25 million internet users every year, almost entirely on mobile. So what exactly is Starlink hoping to solve?

Maybe not much. At least not alone. Starlink is relying on distribution tie-ups with Reliance Jio and Airtel in India, but it’s not a true partnership. It’s closer to shelf-space rental: “You can sell our dishes, and we’ll hope someone in a village in Odisha wants them.”

But even shelf space has to be licensed. And priced. And defended.

The telco lobby, Cellular Operators Association of India (COAI), is already pushing back on Trai’s recommendations for satellite spectrum fees of 4% adjusted gross revenue plus modest per-subscriber levies. 

COAI says this is “unjustifiably low” and wants the telecom department to revisit the charges. Trai, conscious of rural-inclusion goals, has so far resisted reopening the consultation, signalling it wants to push satcom into underserved areas rather than choke it with high fees. 

Nevertheless, endless review cycles risk procedural quicksand. 

But that’s one model. 

Another model is Africa.

In May, Starlink signed a distribution and integration partnership with Airtel Africa, a telecom company with 163 million subscribers across 14 countries. Within weeks, Starlink had secured commercial licences in nine of those countries, and approvals were “under process” in the rest. That’s 14 markets—each with its own telecom regulator—cleared in less time than it took to get one licence in India.

Airtel Africa does the heavy lifting. It installs, distributes, services, and sells Starlink’s equipment and subscriptions. The terminals are stocked on Jumia, Africa’s largest e-commerce platform, and in Airtel’s own stores. The use cases are clear: schools, clinics, off-grid mobile towers, and mining sites. Airtel even plans to use Starlink for rural backhaul—to link isolated cell towers that otherwise can’t get fibre or microwave links.

This is the dream.

Distribution or integration?

In Africa, Starlink is a value-added layer on top of Airtel’s infrastructure. No need to fight for spectrum. No need to build ground stations or convince regulators from scratch. And critically, no need to underprice yourself into a loss.

Even in Nigeria, a price-sensitive market, Starlink’s base plan of ₦38,000 per month (~US$30) has found customers. Nigeria alone had 65,000+ active Starlink subscribers by the end of 2024, up from just 10,000 in 2023. That made Starlink the second-largest ISP in Nigeria in under 18 months. Meanwhile, local rivals like Spectranet and Tizeti lost subscribers. It’s not just price that drives demand here—it’s reliability.

That’s the story across most of sub-Saharan Africa. Internet penetration is below 40%. Fewer than 70% of rural communities have any kind of reliable broadband. Many countries don’t even have a national fibre grid. In places like Chad or the Democratic Republic of Congo, Starlink might be the only fast internet option available outside major cities.

This is what Airtel Africa gives Starlink in the continent: a pre-built, pre-trusted, pre-approved telecom rails system. 

Starlink just slides in. 

And Airtel needs it.

In Africa, Starlink fills massive coverage gaps and supports Airtel’s rural backhaul strategy; it makes sense to share capex and plug satellites into the network blueprint. The business logic is clear: rural data demand is real, fibre is expensive, and 4G towers don’t work if they’re not connected to anything.

In India, Airtel doesn’t need Starlink for its core urban business, but it wants satellite options for niche enterprise, remote infrastructure, disaster recovery, and a marketing angle of “we cover everywhere”. Still, the tie-up remains light: primarily distribution and a potential fallback for remote towers, not a deep joint network build.

And that’s the difference. In Africa, Starlink is infrastructure. In India, it’s a hedge.

If India wants to replicate the Africa model for satcom, it can’t stop at “let’s list the dish on our websites”. It needs deeper integration pilots: rural SMEs with hybrid terrestrial+satcom service agreements, government-subsidised terminals in unserved panchayats, disaster-resilient links for critical sectors. Without such concrete plans, these partnerships risk becoming just a checkbox rather than a growth lever.

It’s the same with Vodafone Idea (Vi) and AST SpaceMobile. Vi’s recent tie-up promises direct-to-device satellite connectivity, checking another box in India’s satcom race. But unless Vi commits capex, integration roadmaps, clear timelines, and affordable tariff structures (or external funding/subsidies), this remains an announcement rather than a rollout.

Right now, Starlink in India is a tech solution looking for a market. In Africa, it’s already solving a problem.

Even geopolitics plays out differently.

In India, the US government nudged but didn’t push. Starlink’s licence came only after tying up with Reliance in March. The Indian government still slapped it with a five-year cap, added national security clauses, and required equipment localisation. 

In Africa, the US State Department reportedly lobbied harder. According to ProPublica, US diplomats offered “veiled threats” of pulling aid unless countries approved Starlink licences. Is that aggressive? Sure. Is it effective? Also yes. But it’s the Airtel partnership that gives Starlink staying power. Countries like Nigeria and Lesotho moved quickly in part because Starlink wasn’t arriving as a foreign ISP—it was showing up with Airtel, a known and trusted entity.

And when South Africa initially cracked down on unauthorised Starlink terminals, the government later clarified that foreign satellite firms would be welcome—if they partnered with a licensed local telecom operator. That is: “Be more like Airtel.”

So yes, Starlink’s India licence is a milestone. But its Airtel Africa partnership is a business model.

It shows how Starlink is basically the lone wolf when it’s allowed to be, but as soon as regulators or distribution logistics throw up a “no entry” sign, it’s suddenly scouting for a telco sidekick. In the US—its home country—it’s happy to be the solo act; in India, it’s doing these beta-ish, direct-to-device hookups with carriers because the rulebook says “partner or perish”; and in much of Africa, it doesn’t so much “choose” Airtel as “needs” it to get its foot in the door (and into stores). 

If its solo entry in India drags out too much longer, Starlink might quietly pivot to bundling and business sales. But in Africa, the roadmap is clear: connect the towers, sell through the stores, and expand with the partner. 

No red tape. No delays. No Rs 33,000 shock.

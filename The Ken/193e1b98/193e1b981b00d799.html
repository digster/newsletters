The death of D2C. Brands might be digital-first, but not necessarily “direct-to-consumer” in the true sense
By Hari Krishna
6 min read
View in browser

Good Morning Ishan,

Are there any products you’ve come across that can only be purchased online or through the brand’s own stores? I’d expect you to have a few names in mind.

Let’s go one layer deeper. Out of the products you’re thinking of, is there one that you purchase regularly, perhaps once a month or every few weeks? I’m sure the list looks thin now.

That’s because the products we buy over and over again are rarely offered by direct-to-consumer (D2C) brands. And some brands that do position themselves as D2C players derive a huge portion of their sales through marketplaces like Amazon and Flipkart, retailers, and quick-commerce platforms.

That runs counter to the D2C characteristic that matters the most—to deal directly with customers and eliminate intermediaries.

Perhaps this means the nature of running a D2C business is changing, or maybe some brands simply wear the label without following through. In the latest episode of Two by Two, hosts Rohin Dharmakumar and Praveen Gopal Krishnan spoke with three guests to find out what “D2C” really means, and whether there are brands that fit the designation.

There was Deepak Shahdadpuri, managing director and founder of DSG Capital Partners, India and Southeast Asia’s first consumer-focused venture capital fund. Deepak has been cutting cheques for consumer brands for well over a decade. He shared his insights on how the D2C phenomena has played out in specific categories.

In fact, we listened with rapt attention when Deepak explained how this worked with yoghurt maker Epigamia, one of DSG’s portfolio companies.

Our second guest was Ajai Thandi, a co-founder of Sleepy Owl Coffee. Ajai told us how he went through the daily grind to build a digital-first challenger brand for coffee enthusiasts.

Finally, Seetharaman G, deputy editor at The Ken and our resident retail expert, joined the conversation. In his weekly newsletter Trade Tricks, he’s written about how some emergent brands claim the D2C mantle even though they don’t truly function like one.

The investor, entrepreneur, and seasoned journalist dived into the true nature of D2C companies and how market conditions are compelling them to break the mould.

Make your brand bigger than your scale

D2C was practiced very differently decades ago. Think of Amway or Tupperware, whose multi-level marketers knocked on doors, gave sales pitches, and tried to lock in orders. 

Definitions shift over time. Now, D2C is a phenomenon specific to e-commerce. The label is attached to nearly every Indian brand that sells its products on an online marketplace.

To keep to the original spirit of D2C, perhaps a company is in the category if most of its sales are made through its own channels and website. This still gives the brand a way to find out who its actual customers are.

Ajai told us how it was essential for Sleepy Owl to be a D2C brand if the company wanted to figure out ways to consistently improve its products:

“The best way to get to know your customer and who they are, is when you have access to them directly.

Being purely D2C in the first three years… allowed us to take that critical feedback to evolve our product, to reiterate again and again to get the product-market fit correct.

So I have always treated it as a channel to get to know my customers and evolve my products, where my experimentation happens.

Then, I can go to a retail store, I will go to a marketplace, I will go to quick commerce. And I will make fewer mistakes at a larger scale.’’

The most successful D2C companies devote time and resources to build their own brand. The goal is for a company to make its brand bigger than its scale, as Ajai recalled from a conversation he had previously had with Deepak. This is far more than what most businesses with consumer products do, which is to list their goods on a marketplace and scale up, running the risk that customers develop the impression that they’re easily replaceable.

Insurgent to incumbent

Building a brand, however, is easier said than done. The process can take years, even decades, to go from insurgency to incumbency. That’s the dream of any brand, but scaling up also means opening new channels and maintaining them to fit different types of customer behaviours.

Deepak: In an extreme example, where I buy a product once every 10 years with high intent, I’m willing to spend time to go to the mattress company that I want.

But if I’m going to buy coffee every week, and I know it’s part of a basket of 30 products, imagine me going to Sleepy Owl to buy coffee, going to Epigamia to buy yoghurt, going to Veeba… I’ll go to 30 websites and put in 30 OTPs and 30 PINs. There is no consumer who will do that and add friction to his or her life.

We need to understand our job as a brand is to make the consumer’s life very easy. If that consumer has a particular preference for purchasing a particular product, keep in mind that the same product might be bought differently on different occasions.

With hypermarket shelves out of reach, the playbook for insurgent D2C brands is clear: steep discounts and a presence in quick-commerce product galleries. Think of it as an essential layer on top of D2C orders, so that customers can encounter or search for them in different contexts.

Break baskets

After overcoming a range of challenges, a select few brands manage to command a premium rather than give in to a race to the bottom. Deepak calls these brands “basket breakers”, and he believes there are one or two of them in any given product category:

“[Think of] a consumer who wants to buy five things, but they can’t find the fifth thing [which is your brand’s product], therefore they abandon the entire purchase.’’

The idea here is powerful. Some D2C brands manage to inspire immense loyalty in their customers, to the point that it shapes their broader shopping behaviours.

Think back to the products you were prompted to recall at the beginning of this note. Some of them might just be basket-breakers. But brands like that are few and far between—maybe fewer and farther than ever before.

So, it’s natural to ask one important question: Is D2C truly dead in India?

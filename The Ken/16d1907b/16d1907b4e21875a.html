Huawei, the $105 billion Chinese telecom-to-smartphones giant, is facing a “live or die moment” said its founder Run Zhengfei in a leaked internal memo last month. From being on track to becoming the world’s largest phone maker, it is now staring at a drop of 60 million phones in 2019 over the year before, thanks to US sanctions against it.
 
In India though, it has been doing swimmingly well. Its 2019 revenue was up 55% and profits up a whopping 213% over 2018. But 2020 and beyond is a different matter altogether.
 
Over 80% of its business in India comes from networking, where it provides routers, switches and various other equipment to telecom operators and enterprises. It’s an $8 billion market, of which it has 20-25% market share.
 
But as the US aggressively lobbies India to keep Huawei out of its telecom network 5G rollouts, the company is starting at an existential crisis even in India. And to counter all the FUD (fear, uncertainty and doubt) being planted by its competitors and the US, Huawei is doing a bit of its own, too. 
 
For instance, it is pushing a narrative that banning it would lead to a 3-year delay in India’s 5G rollouts. But then, those rollouts are already set to happen 2-3 years from now. 
 
"If Huawei and ZTE are banned, the prices will go up. It’s a very simple story,” says a senior executive with one of India's telecom operators. The cost estimate of replacing Huawei with other vendors could be as high as Rs 10,000 -11,000 crore ($1.39-1.53 billion). But then, counters another executive, that’s not a whole lot when viewed in proportion to the $30-40 billion Indian telcos have already spent on their networks. 
 
Poor Huawei is stuck between multiple devils and hard places in India. US versus China geopolitics. Security versus affordability. Business versus politics.

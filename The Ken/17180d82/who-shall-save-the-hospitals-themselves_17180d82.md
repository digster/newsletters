There is a Latin phrase I keep coming back to, “Quis custodiet ipsos custodes?”.
It means “Who will guard the guards themselves?” and is often used to point out the lack of checks on those with power, who keep a check on everyone else. People like politicians, regulators, judges, and billionaires running tech platforms.
But as privacy and accountability get dumped into the Covid-19 bin, there’s a new variant of this phrase: who shall save the hospitals themselves?
In a deeply reported and concerning story, Ruhi lays bare the contradiction that’s at the heart of India’s response to the pandemic—most of India’s hospitals are in dire straits economically, even as they’re at the forefront of the country’s containment efforts. Consider these:
-
Inpatient occupancy ratios have dropped by as much as 80%.
-
Revenues have dropped by 50-75%
-
Some hospitals are either paying part of April salaries or only paying important staff such as doctors and nurses. Others, such as ward boys and security guards are not being paid and, in some cases, have been sent packing.
-
Declining revenues have affected private hospitals’ ability to procure personal protective equipment (PPE) for their staff.
-
The government owes private hospitals nearly Rs 2,000 crore ($264 million) for treating patients under subsidised schemes.
With no economic support from the government, hospitals are cannibalising themselves to survive. But actions like salary cuts, layoffs, re-used PPE, and delayed salaries risk affecting morale, availability, and safety of the very frontline that India needs to keep its citizens safe and to treat those who might contract Covid-19.

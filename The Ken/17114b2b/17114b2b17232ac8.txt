The best-kept secret in India’s financial services sector is how its reserve bank, the RBI, granted its largest bank, the state-owned SBI, a use-one-time-only regulatory pass in 1998.

That’s when it granted SBI Cards, a subsidiary set up by the SBI, a licence that allowed it to issue credit cards. 

What’s so radical about that, you ask? 

Because SBI Cards is not really a bank. And in India, only banks are allowed by the RBI to issue credit cards. Except for SBI Cards. Because it is a non-banking finance company (NBFC). The RBI does not allow NBFCs to issue credit cards.

Anyhow, long story short, SBI Cards got a one-time regulatory pass. Which it used with great gusto to create a juggernaut with $955 million in revenue and $113 million in profits.

SBI Cards, because of its unique structure—child of the SBI but not really a public sector company—had a privileged childhood. It could hire talent at market salaries from the likes of Citibank, Mastercard, and American Express; go to prestigious B-school campuses to hire; strike a partnership with e-commerce seller Flipkart for its Big Billion Days sale. Most importantly, it was not bogged by classic-government policies of waiting for the lowest bidder to build infrastructure. 

So when it IPO-ed earlier this month, parent SBI was expecting to pocket most of the Rs 10,000 crore ($1.4 billion) in proceeds. Except, what the RBI giveth, the RBI taketh too. Because SBI was roped in to rescue failed private bank Yes Bank. 

In her story today, Arundhati delves into the charmed history of SBI Cards, its business model, and its future under a parent that is the first port of call in distressed situations. Of which the world, and India, will have a lot.

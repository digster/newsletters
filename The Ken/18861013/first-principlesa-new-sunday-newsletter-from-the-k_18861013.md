Ishan, hello. You’re about to read the first edition of a brand-new subscriber newsletter from The Ken called First Principles.
Or perhaps not. Because you’re wondering, what is The Ken thinking? Sending a new newsletter on the most sacred and anti-work days of the week. A Sunday.
And that too, unannounced and uninvited.
Since there’s no other way around this, let me first just apologise for my intrusion. And then explain why Sundays might not be all that bad to meander, reflect, and reorient.
Which is the purpose of this newsletter.
First Principles is both a really old (think Aristotle) and relatively new (think Elon Musk) concept of reasoning, where we force ourselves to challenge the way things are in pursuit of the way things should be.
We do this by learning to see large, complex problems as composed of smaller “building blocks” of problems. And then solving those building blocks and reassembling solutions in ways others might not think of.
To do this well requires us to learn and apply two skills: analysis (breaking a problem into smaller parts) and synthesis (building solutions by assembling smaller parts).
Most of us are good at analysis, because it’s what is taught to us in schools and reinforced at workplaces. Not so many of us are good at synthesis, for the exact opposite of the earlier reasons.
First Principles thinking requires both.
Each Sunday, this newsletter will bring you fresh insights on how many leaders and individuals apply First Principles thinking to not just see the world differently, but also to remake it in their own vision.
And what better example of such a person than Dick Fosbury, who passed away in March. Fosbury was the inventor of the “Fosbury Flop”, or as we now know it, the modern high jump.
|
Fosbury shot to fame in 1968, when he won high-jump gold in Mexico City after a final that lasted more than four hours.
His technique, honed in college competition in Oregon, involved jumping backwards and arching his back over the bar, thereby reversing and ripping up decades of high-jump orthodoxy. In the span of just five years, he had gone from struggling as a high-school athlete in his hometown, Medford, to winning worldwide fame.
|

“I guess it did look kind of weird at first but it felt so natural that, like all good ideas, you just wonder why no one had thought of it before me,” said Fosbury to The Guardian in a 2012 interview.
Try to imagine yourself in 1968 when Fosbury decided to jump backwards when everyone else was jumping forwards. Most adults then would’ve baulked at his unorthodoxy.
Not kids though. Many of them would’ve said, “cool!”.
Kids are natural First Principles thinkers because they’ve yet to be indoctrinated by adults on “the correct way” to do things.
“Why?” and “why not?” come naturally to them, as any parent will attest. While we have to be taught and forced [to do the same](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Five_whys).
Kabeer Biswas, the co-founder and CEO of Dunzo, the hyperlocal delivery company headquartered out of Bengaluru, asked himself “why not?” back in 2015.
“Can you actually press a button and get everything else done? And that was really a one-line insight that, I'll be honest, didn't leave me for like 18 months. I used to keep thinking about it. I think I'm fundamentally lazy. So on a Saturday or Sunday, I would be like, there should be something that can solve my life's problems,” Kabeer told me last year.
Could someone else do the tasks you didn’t have the time or inclination to?
Why not?
So he spent the first six months answering this question literally. By allowing people to message him their tasks over WhatsApp, and then, just doing it for them.
Today, Dunzo is one of India’s best-known hyperlocal delivery companies, having raised close to half a billion dollars from investors like Lightbox, Google, and Reliance Industries.
A few days ago, I asked Kabeer what his favourite First Principle was.
“One door/two door,” he messaged me back over Signal. I don’t use WhatsApp.
|
Kabeer Biswas is 38, lean and bespectacled. He speaks fast, with a mix of conviction and impatience. But you get the sense that his thoughts are running at an even greater clock speed during conversations, especially when discussing Dunzo’s business operations and model.
Having done thousands of deliveries on his own and building Dunzo over the last eight years, he also commands both analysis and synthesis skills around delivery operations.
Ask him which neighbourhoods in Bengaluru prefer which varieties of bananas, milk, or bread, and he’ll give you the answers in a jiffy. Present him with a commerce transaction you and I might take for granted, and Kabeer is able to “see” the same in smaller components involving people, incentives, time, costs, and efforts.
Yet, a significant part of his work involves seeing the same world as macro, not micro. Because that’s when he is convincing large and sophisticated investors to back Dunzo. Then it’s all market share, consumer incomes, employment, daily wages, urban infrastructure, real estate, and government regulations.
Most great founders have this “bifocal” ability to see their world using micro or macro lenses, depending on the context.
One of those bifocal abilities is to look at a major decision that needs to be made, and asking if it is a one-way door or a two-way door.
One-way doors only allow you to go in, but not come back. In other words, they represent irreversible decisions. Walking through them is thus a tougher decision.
Two-ways doors and reversible decisions are relatively easier to make, since they can be undone with some minor damage.
While this decision-making construct is not a new one, it was Amazon founder Jeff Bezos who helped popularise it with his 1997 annual shareholder letter. Bezos called them “Type 1” versus “Type 2” decisions.
|
We want to be a large company that’s also an invention machine. We want to combine the extraordinary customer-serving capabilities that are enabled by size with the speed of movement, nimbleness, and risk-acceptance mentality normally associated with entrepreneurial start-ups.
Can we do it? I’m optimistic. We have a good start on it, and I think our culture puts us in a position to achieve the goal. But I don’t think it’ll be easy. There are some subtle traps that even high-performing large organizations can fall into as a matter of course, and we’ll have to learn as an institution how to guard against them. One common pitfall for large organizations – one that hurts speed and inventiveness – is “one-size-fits-all” decision making.
Some decisions are consequential and irreversible or nearly irreversible – one-way doors – and these decisions must be made methodically, carefully, slowly, with great deliberation and consultation. If you walk through and don’t like what you see on the other side, you can’t get back to where you were before. We can call these Type 1 decisions. But most decisions aren’t like that – they are changeable, reversible – they’re two-way doors. If you’ve made a suboptimal Type 2 decision, you don’t have to live with the consequences for that long. You can reopen the door and go back through. Type 2 decisions can and should be made quickly by high judgment individuals or small groups.
As organizations get larger, there seems to be a tendency to use the heavy-weight Type 1 decision-making _process_ on most decisions, including many Type 2 decisions. The end result of this is slowness, unthoughtful risk aversion, failure to experiment sufficiently, and consequently diminished invention. We’ll have to figure out how to fight that tendency.

As a founder, there are numerous doors I’ve nudged The Ken to not walk through too. The most prominent among these is the one called “Discounts”.
We launched in October 2016 with our annual subscription priced at Rs 2,750 (US$33). As a purely subscription-driven publication, we decided we wouldn’t offer discounts because they were unfair to those who paid in full. Also, it’s virtually impossible to get customers used to buying your products (whatever they may be) at discounted prices for a while, and then expect them to pay only full price.
Discounts are a one-way door for The Ken. This isn’t an easy decision for us to make, since we live in a world where customers are accustomed to discounts all the time. Thus, by choosing to not go through that door, we have to work much harder to ensure our subscribers continue to see value in a full-price subscription. I’d love to explain more, but I’m afraid this might then tip over into a marketing spiel for The Ken, which isn’t what I intend.

And [my latest guest was Smita Deorah](https://the-ken.com/podcasts/first-principles/smita-deorah-lead/), the co-founder and co-CEO of LEAD, an ambitious edtech company that wants to change the way Indian schools educate our children.

“The best way to build something is vision backwards. Because, you know, the human mind is very interesting. The human brain is a very interesting organ. We create what we imagine. And hence, everything starts from imagination. When you imagine today forward, it's a very constrained imagination. Because you’re already telling yourself, this is all that’s possible.
But disruptive things don’t come from accepting reality. Now, when you work vision backwards, you say, “Hey, this is what I want to create.” We said, when we started LEAD, we want to create a world where kids in small towns are able to get the same quality of education that a child in Singapore or Finland (which are the top ranking PISA countries). That’s the vision. When we work backwards from there, a world of possibilities open up for you. And I think a lot of innovation that we did in LEAD over the last 10-11 years came about only because of that. Now, are there failures along the way? Are there tough learnings along the way? Absolutely.
I think the important thing in that journey is to know that you’ve taken up an audacious goal. And that it will require perseverance, it will require you to not get bogged down by failure, it will require you to be thinking creatively for, you know, every problem. And you’ll get there and I think that some of the best things in the world that were created by people were not from incremental thinking.”

"One of the things to think about is I like to avoid decisions that can potentially have catastrophic consequences. So if there’s even a small chance that Fractal could disappear because of that decision, I would say no to that decision.
One decision is to take risks, but do not take existential risks. Sometimes what you don’t understand may have existential risks baked into it. Let’s say you invest in Bitcoins or something like that.
You didn’t know what was going on and suddenly one fine day, you’re out of liquidity and you’re out of business. Why would you do something like that? So I’m just giving you a very extreme example, but that is an example of not taking existential risks. So many decisions in Fractal I have stopped because I felt that these are existential risks.”

Alright, straight to it. For me, this bit from a piece in the New Yorker published in October last year, puts things into proper perspective: “Thirteen thousand fledgling software companies applied to Y Combinator this year, and two hundred and forty were accepted, making it more than twice as hard to get into as Stanford University. After graduating thirteen hundred startups, YC now boasts the power—and the peculiarities—of an island nation.”

Few accelerators in the world enjoy as much respect as YC. Its companies have a combined valuation of $80 billion. Airbnb, Dropbox, Instacart, Reddit, Stripe; all of these companies are YC startup alumni. For many, many years being a part of YC has been a distant dream for a lot of Indian startups. Ten years. Yup. It took YC ten years to find the first Indian company to make it to its classroom. That year was 2014. A lot has changed in the last three years though. This year there are 12 in a single batch of YC’s bi-annual school. That’s our story for today. From my colleague Payal Ganguly. Payal’s story telling approach is urgent and simple. You can read the piece here: https://the-ken.com/inside-y-combinators-belated-india-surge/?utm_campaign=The%20Ken&utm_source=Email&utm_medium=Newsletter?utm_campaign=The%20Ken&utm_source=Email&utm_medium=Newsletter https://the-ken.com/inside-y-combinators-belated-india-surge/

This quote from Adora Cheung, partner at YC is interesting. “Just physically going to India had a measurable impact on the number of applications submitted. Part of it was just getting rid of the misperception that we didn’t fund founders from India working on India-specific problems.”

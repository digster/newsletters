At some point, virtually all founders of medium- to late-stage venture-funded companies in India come to know of a phrase they always end up underestimating.

Flipping.

It means rejigging their company’s corporate structure from India to a foreign country. Usually the US or increasingly, Singapore. The Indian company that the founders had set up and scaled over the years with their blood, sweat, and tears ends up becoming subservient to a new foreign company that’s set up.

This happens due to three key reasons. First, there's not enough pools of risk capital in India beyond a point, forcing companies to look outside. Second, not enough of a market for the company’s products domestically. And the third, strict regulatory barriers around what a company can or can’t do, or who it can sell or not sell to.

Instead of fighting the system in India, many companies go for the easier option.

They flip.

Mitra Biotech, India’s foremost cancer diagnostic startup, flipped to the US in 2018. It had spent a decade building a radical new cancer diagnostics platform and raised $84.5 million from respected VCs such as Accel and Sequoia. The India-to-US flip was to enable an IPO in 2021.

In the US, it raised millions of dollars more in venture capital and brought on board an ambitious and well-regarded CEO who checked the right boxes for investors—he had the experience of already taking a company public.

And yet, at a time when the world is besotted with biotechs, Mitra Biotech just imploded overnight earlier this year.

Seema, our Editor, spoke with several people associated with the company as employees, collaborators, advisors, etc., but all declined to be named. One investment professional said, “A lot of big names are involved. I don’t even want to be seen near this story.”

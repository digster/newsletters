Tupperware’s story will outlive its storyteller. The direct-selling model the company built its fortunes on is under pressure. And it hasn’t managed to keep up with the times
By Ruhi Kandhari
5 min read
View in browser

Good Evening Ishan,

A truly compelling story lasts forever. 

Audiences may change. The identity of the person who told it may be forgotten. But the words survive. The world’s greatest myths, our fondest and most remembered quotes… all are told and retold decades, centuries, even millennia after they were first heard. 

This week, on Tuesday, one of the world’s most popular consumer brands of all time—Tupperware—filed for bankruptcy protection. And a company that even last year had a revenue of US$1.14 billion appears to have conceded defeat. 

It may not be the end of the road for Tupperware India, but it is likely an ending for a brand story that has become synonymous with kitchen and food storage world over. As NPR wrote last year about those ubiquitous airtight plastic boxes:

There’s a saying that everyone dies twice: once with your last breath and again when your name is spoken for the last time. By that measure, Tupperware did crack the forever business—even if it goes out of business.

Let’s unpack why a company that crafted such a lasting brand had to die.

Tupperware was once ahead of its times, then behind them

There was something unique that Tupperware did in the 1950s. 

Its product was patented. It was popular. But Tupperware abandoned in-store sales entirely just a few years after it was founded. The reason was one salesperson, who created a community of customers so strong and sold the product so well that the company thought brick-and-mortar retail stores were unnecessary.

In 1948, Tupperware takes off when it starts selling through Tupperware home parties, empowering women to sell to other women, using their social networks. Although Earl Tupper invented his now-ubiquitous Wonderlier Bowl in 1946, it wasn’t until he partnered with Brownie Wise to create Tupperware Home Parties in 1948 that the innovative, bell-shaped plastic containers took off.

Brownie Wise pioneered the Hostess Group Demonstrations (aka Tupperware Parties) in order to tap into the power of women’s social networks for personalized, in-home demonstrations. Tupperware turned the initial challenge of selling plastics into an opportunity for women to make money independently from their husbands. The independent dealers were so successful that Tupperware abandoned in-store sales completely in 1951. Tupperware was a women-focused business, empowering women to sell to other women, using their social networks as a means of expanding their reach and building trust.

Source: Strategyzer

The company had everything: a great product, self-multiplying sales teams, and a motivated community of sellers and buyers. The result was an annual revenue of US$2 billion at its peak, all built on an asset-light model of direct sales. 

Its success here helped Tupperware reap the rewards for a long time. But, the story had started fraying in the last few years.

First, several of Tupperware’s patents expired, and it had nothing really innovative to offer its customers. Here’s the BBC reporting on this shift last year:

“We use it (Tupperware) as a noun, which is quite unusual for a brand,” said Catherine Shuttleworth, founder of retail analysis firm Savvy Marketing.

“I think a lot of younger people will be surprised it is a brand in itself.” 

While Tupperware was a “miracle product” when first sold decades ago, Ms Shuttleworth added, the market has been flooded by companies offering cheaper alternatives in recent years.

A resurgence during the Covid-19 pandemic, buoyed by people taking up baking and cooking more at home, reversed sharp falls in Tupperware’s share price. 

But the rise turned out to be temporary. 

Sales have slid again since then, largely because the firm has not been “innovative enough” over the past 10 to 20 years to keep up with its rivals, according to Ms Shuttleworth.

The lack of innovation is a far-cry from its early days.

Second, the company which built its fortune on parties missed the after-party, so to speak. The advent of e-commerce and omnichannel retail put pressure on its direct selling model, but Tupperware remained attached to its ‘Tupperware party’ concept for too long. 

It had a strong brand and a known product, but the competition was milking other sales channels Tupperware had ignored for too long.

“A great brand name can be a blessing or a curse,” said Christie Nordhielm, a marketing consultant and adjunct professor at Georgetown University’s McDonough School of Business. “It’s a curse when you kind of rest on your laurels and milk the brand for profits and don’t continue to invest in a brand.”

If you bought your “tupperware” in a store before October 2022, it was unlikely to be the actual brand. The company just introduced its products into Target stores last fall, a move that is likely, “way too little, too late,” said Barbara Kahn, a professor of marketing at the University of Pennsylvania’s Wharton School of Business.

Tupperware has been struggling for years. Three charts show just how bad it’s been, CNN

Tupperware started course correcting. In India, for instance, the company transformed its image from a direct-selling company to a new-age omnichannel one, and expanded its product categories by including materials other than plastic for kitchen storage needs. 

But it has not been able to find a way to reinvent its story while retaining the sense of purpose that built and kept its community of customers together. 

And the final reason for its current state is that it hasn’t been able to build on the one thing that propelled it forward, and made its direct selling model the envy of competitors. 

Tupperware’s Tupperware parties worked so well because they were so deeply associated with empowering and advancing the social and economic status of women.

Wise’s and Tupperware’s impact is still debated by academics, but many say it played an important role in bringing women into the workforce in post-war America, and provided a source of income to other women around the world.

One of them is Alison Clarke, professor of design history and theory at the University of Applied Arts, Vienna, and author of Tupperware: The Promise of Plastic in 1950s America. “I think its legacy is the way in which it has provided a source of employment to women who don’t always have access to flexible labour,” she told the BBC. 

“At the time it was first being sold at parties in the US, lots of women were isolated in new post-war suburban towns away from their families…The Tupperware parties glamourised dull housework, and you could only buy it if you knew someone who sold it, so it was exclusive, and social, and about relationships with other women.”

But as women started breaking out of the bonds that had kept them chained to home and kitchen, and opportunities opened up, Tupperware’s story as the enabler of women entrepreneurship lost its magnetism. And the company had nothing else to offer that could revitalise its story for a more modern audience.

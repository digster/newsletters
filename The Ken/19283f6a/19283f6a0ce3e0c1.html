Artists, users, or platforms: who wins in the Zomato vs Bookmyshow fight? 

After its Rs 2,000-crore Paytm deal, the foodtech is hiring industry veterans and signing on A-list celebs to outplay the market leader

Bookmyshow has maintained dominance in live-entertainment and ticketing, thanks to its understanding of consumer patterns and its vast data pool. But its latest challenger, Zomato, has the tech chops, the cash, and the appetite to give it a true run for its money. Talent is at the centre of its strategy—the company has hired industry veterans and brought internationally renowned artists to India. Still, it won’t be easy to hurt a 25-year-old company like Bookmyshow with a track record of success in organising high-profile events. [Read the full story here]

Why this one Adani company can’t escape Hindenburg’s shadow 

The 80% plunge in its stock price has thrown a brutal curveball at Adani Total Gas, effectively slamming shut any chance of raising equity to fund its grand ambitions

Since Hindenburg’s report last January, Adani Total Gas has been stuck in a stock-price freefall while its siblings soar with big equity raises. Now, it has opted to go for a US$375 million debt-raise, marking a departure from its previous talk about low borrowings. With its stock still trading at a hefty PE ratio, the company finds itself trapped in a cycle of lofty expectations and sluggish performance. Meanwhile, its cap table has taken an unexpected twist—reminiscent of the peculiar shareholding patterns of Adani firms not so long ago. [Read the full story here]

For Reliance Brands’ fast fashion, Lee and Wrangler’s slow comeback is no match 

Nitin Chhabra and Berry Singh are using their tech-driven omnichannel business—Ace Turtle—to build a poor man’s Reliance Brands, albeit with struggling labels

In 2021, Ace Turtle acquired the licensing rights for American denim brands Lee and Wrangler, kickstarting its journey as a retailer of international brands. Once popular, these brands lost their sheen over the years, with competition now far ahead. The founders envision a model similar to what they saw in their previous roles at Reliance Brands Limited but with some differences. But turning the business around isn’t going to be a cakewalk for Ace Turtle. [Read the full story here]

Education and careers

‘2 million interns a year?’ PM’s scheme makes corporate India anxious

Even as corporate India faces a skill deficiency in talent, they seem confused as to what to make of the 10 million youths expected to join them as interns in the next five years

The pilot project for the PM Internship scheme starts on 2 December and 125,000 interns are expected to start work with the top 500 companies. But corporate India is concerned about onboarding interns in such numbers, potentially throwing off hiring plans for other roles. Corporates also doubt if they’re equipped with enough resources to train and retain interns, and also hire again from the pool in a year. Applicants begin registering themselves from 12 October; but the scheme lacks a sector-specific approach, potentially creating a problem-solution mismatch. [Read the full story here]

Theobroma became a go-to for cake lovers. Now it’s a go-to for investors. That changes everything 

The ICICI Ventures backed-patisseries chain from Mumbai made croissants and brownies a pan-India affair. But as new owners come in where does it go from here?

Theobroma is expected to have new owners, giving an exit to its founder, Kainaz Messman, and current PE investor ICICI Ventures. PE firm Chrys Capital is close to locking the deal, valuing the company at around Rs 3,500 crore. The patisserie chain has come a long way from being a single-family-owned store two decades ago by building a profitable business when its rivals have struggled to hold ground. But it faces a dilemma: how can it continue to scale and yet maintain its artisanal approach. [Read the full story here]

Good Morning Ishan,
“Ever wonder why we love this roadside chai so much,” I ask as we huddle under an umbrella on a drizzling Kolkata morning. The aroma of freshly brewed tea—with your choice of tulsi, ginger, clove, fennel, and cardamom—mingles with the scent of rain, and the clatter of trams fills the air.
“You mean aside from the fact that it costs next to nothing and tastes like home?” you reply, taking a sip.
“Exactly,” I nod. “Chaayos is still trying to capture that very essence, but without the frills. It is still not going luxe.”
“Interesting,” you muse. “So, it is banking on simplicity while everyone else is chasing premiumisation?”
“Right,” I say. “Rounak, my colleague, met and spoke with Nitin Saluja, the founder of the company that has been generating over Rs 200 crore (US$24 million) revenues annually on this no-fuss approach.”
“But can it really hold up against the big players charging 2X for tea and 4X for coffee,” you wonder, thinking we should move to a café now that it’s business talk.
“Saluja thinks so,” I say, wondering why there’s still no Chaayos outlet in Kolkata (where chai lovers play mad scientist with their brews). “His team has talked to over 200 customers and found that people love its laid-back vibe. So, the strategy now is to cut costs, reduce discounts, and enhance both in-store and delivery experiences. It’s been working—at least in some months, it has hit profitability.”
“So, is this present genius or future folly?” you wonder aloud. “After all, they can’t charge premium prices to its customers who can afford them.”
“That’s the question,” I agree. “As the market shifts towards upscale experiences, can Chaayos’ simple charm continue to win over customers, or will they be swept away by the premium tide?”

Meanwhile, Indonesia’s digital train has just gone off the rails. A hacker group named Brain Cipher recently decided to take down over 280 government systems, including those belonging to 30 central ministries. While data breaches are not new in Indonesia, this attack was so severe that even military-intelligence records were leaked and sold online for the price of a fancy dinner.
“Digital national disaster,” an IT consultant tells Jeff, the author of this story. Why? Because the ransomware attack couldn’t have come at a worse time.
Indonesia, dreaming about becoming a tech hub, has been wooing Microsoft, Apple, and the ilk for billions in investments. It is also in the middle of moving public services online and speeding up things like passport applications. Even in 2024, only 2% of the fourth most populous country on the planet’s data is backed up.
Now, imagine this: Telkom, the state-owned telecom giant, was using Microsoft’s free Windows Defender Firewall to safeguard national data. What’s worse is that top officials initially downplayed the breach.
As the scramble to restore services continues, questions about Indonesia’s readiness for the digital age are growing louder—and tech giants are probably reassessing their investments.

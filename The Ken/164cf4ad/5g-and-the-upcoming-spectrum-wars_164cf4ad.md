Planes fly in the sky. Ships sail on water. Vehicles ply on land. That’s just the way it is.
This allows plane makers, shipbuilders and car companies to take the skies, oceans and roads respectively as their exclusive domains, even though they each ferry people and goods.
So imagine their consternation when one day, they find that the rules have changed. Cars are allowed to fly, ships are allowed to drive and planes are allowed to sail.
It’s a ridiculous analogy, designed to get your attention. But something similar is happening in the world of wireless communications.
On 12 July, the US FCC threw a stun grenade into the complex global world of wireless spectrum when it decided to open up “C-band” frequencies, hitherto reserved for satellite communications, for 5G usage. And that has triggered a cascading avalanche of spectrum lust and turf wars around the world, as more countries consider following the US lead.
One of those countries is India. Telecom operators, although burdened under an Everest of debt, are salivating at the prospect of getting access to good quality spectrum in contiguous bands of up to 200 megahertz, unlike their current 2G, 3G and 4G spectrum which comes in blocks of 5-10 megahertz. It’s as if someone told cars, you can now fly. The skies are yours.
Never mind that cars can’t fly yet. Because 5G is still a few years away. And as the case with 4G deployments in India showed, the lag between debilitating spectrum auctions and large-scale rollouts is 3-5 years.
There’s also the fact that various government agencies are girding for turf wars over control of this valuable spectrum. The talk in Delhi is how the Department of Telecom wants to use this opportunity to usurp C-band spectrum from its current caretaker, the Department of Space. On the sidelines, India’s Space Research Organisation (ISRO) wants to safeguard its own turf too.
Did I mention there’s also non-telecom players moving into the 5G space, including Google, Facebook, Apple and NEC?
Fun times. For which I must thank Seema, who spent the past few weeks diligently reporting, reading up and deconstructing the dense and technical world of spectrum bands and regulators. So you can understand it and see what comes next.

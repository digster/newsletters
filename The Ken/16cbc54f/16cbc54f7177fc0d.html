Perhaps the safest job in this slowing economy is to make medicines or to sell them, seeing how recession-proof the industry is. 

No wonder then that Naspers, SoftBank and Temasek all want to invest $100 million in the Indian e-pharmacy industry, which has been clawing a larger share of the overall market. 

What's not clear is why PharmEasy is the touted beneficiary, rather than its contemporaries 1mg, Netmeds and Medlife. Or, perhaps it is. Everyone wants to bet on something unique, a standout.

On that count, PharmEasy stands out, even in a crowd of four. While its rivals prefer to focus on eliminating the middlemen in their B2C model, PharmEasy wants to be a middleman. 

The middleman. In a market estimated to be worth $350 million and growing at 42%. A market where government policy has become friendlier and is expected to ease further.

Also at stake is the process of grabbing a more significant share of the 32% profit margin available as a drug journeys from factory to home. Right now, distributors get about 10% and retailers the rest.

Eliminating the middle layer would boost margins, argue 1mg, Netmeds and Medlife. PharmEasy, though, is betting on volumes giving it better bargaining power on prices.

In that respect, PharmEasy is following a model tried and tested by the world's oldest and largest e-pharmacy, China's 111 Inc. It was worth just under $1 billion last year when it went public just six years after starting. However, its value has crashed 70% since the IPO. 

A cautionary sign? Ultimately, the choice of a business model comes down to one question, as one investor put it, "What is a company's DNA?"

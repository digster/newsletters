Good Morning Ishan,
Sometime last year, when my landlord was—once again—threatening to increase the rent on my modest 2BHK in Gurugram, I decided to look into buying a house. It was largely on a lark. I knew I was nowhere close to being able to afford one. But what was the harm in looking, I thought.
So I contacted a broker and set out on weekend odysseys across the city. Most of the homes I saw were cramped, middling affairs, often a minimum of 40-minutes away from Cyber Hub, situated amid desolate, dusty landscapes that looked no better than urban villages. The cherry on top: nothing was less than Rs 2 crore.
When I made the mistake of asking the broker if he had any properties around Rs 1 crore, he laughed. The prospect, apparently, was quite amusing to him.
As a Gen Zer dreaming of one day owning my own home, this was a visceral reality check (back in 2022, The Ken had done a [similar story](https://the-ken.com/story/almost-but-not-yet-millennials-home-owning-dreams-vs-indian-real-estate/) on millennials’ home-owning dreams).
This time, The Ken surveyed over 200 Gen Z respondents to understand where they were at in their home-buying journey, and their responses often matched mine. A common sentiment (57 respondents) was that they’d like to own a home someday but the prospect felt distant. Another popular response (44 respondents): property prices were the single biggest factor holding them back. This is despite EMI-to-income ratios in major metros never having been lower.

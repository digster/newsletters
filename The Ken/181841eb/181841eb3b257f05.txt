Good Morning Ishan,

The rapid rise of the gig economy—thank you, Uber—has redefined job flexibility for many people looking to pick up a side hustle for some extra cash. 

Being a gig worker means that you can work at any hour you want, no need to report to a boss, and more. And because of its part-time nature, gig workers enjoy very little to zero work benefits like pension savings or paid leaves. 

But that’s a fair trade for autonomy and flexibility, you’d think. Except for the Singaporean government. 

Singapore intends to tip the scales in favour of gig workers. The city-state is mulling introducing mandatory contributions by these gig platforms to the Central Provident Fund (CPF) of their part-time staff. All in the name of providing social security. 

But CPF works both ways. Employees—in this case, the gig workers—have to contribute 20% of their earnings into the pot as well. What if some of them prefer more take-home cash at the end of every day? 

All of a sudden, the supposedly flexible gig work isn’t looking that flexible after all. 

And what does this mean for the platforms like Grab, Gojek, and foodpanda; all of whom are still struggling to turn a profit? Will they shift the extra cost to consumers? Or will they take on fewer delivery workers to front the expenses for CPF contribution?

When home rental juggernaut Airbnb finally went public around a week ago with a wildly successful listing, an old Medium post from Brian Chesky, its CEO and co-founder, began doing the rounds.
Titled '7 Rejections', it recounts a time in 2008—the year Airbnb was founded—when Chesky and his co-founders were introduced to seven prominent investors in Silicon Valley. Seeking $150,000 in exchange for 10% of their fledgling startup, they pitched to each. The blog post contained the rejection emails Chesky received from five of them. The other two didn't bother to respond.
Chesky himself, in a talk at Stanford, recounted how some people called Airbnb "the worst idea that ever worked". Despite this self-effacing anecdote, however, 7 Rejections stands testament to Chesky's belief in himself, his team, and their idea. The fact that he would save these rejections is indicative that Chesky believed that he would one day be vindicated, and that the same people who once rejected him will look like the biggest idiots in town.
When he posted it in 2015—with Airbnb valued at $2.5 billion—those who rejected him were certainly kicking themselves. Today, with Airbnb's market cap floating around the $100 billion mark, I doubt they'll ever live it down.
Then there's Nithin Kamath, CEO and co-founder of discount stockbroking platform Zerodha. In today's interview with Sumanth, here's what he had to say about why Zerodha never bothered to approach investors in its early days: "...to be very honest, if I was a VC or an investor back then, I wouldn't have invested in myself because there was no pedigree, no vision or ambitions."
Today, Zerodha is India's largest stockbroking platform by a few miles. It handles over 100,000 orders a minute, and hosts over a million concurrent users. With a topline of around Rs 1,000 crore ($135.8 million) and profit of over Rs 350 crore ($47.6 million), it has catapulted its founders—Nithin and his brother Nikhil—into the ranks of India's richest.
I'm not sure what pedigree leads to that. Whether even Kamath's wildest vision would have seen Zerodha achieve such incredible success. Or at what point such lofty ambitions could be justified. Perhaps it was just as well that Kamath lacked all three—they would probably have been limiting.

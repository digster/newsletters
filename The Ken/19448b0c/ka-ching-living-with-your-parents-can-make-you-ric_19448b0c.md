Ka-Ching!
Thu, 09 Jan 25
A weekly newsletter about how finance is getting supercharged by tech in India, and how you can make money work for you..
Good Morning Ishan,
I hope you’re having a great new year so far. Mine’s been the usual. I’ve already broken a couple of resolutions, and I’m well over the “new year, new me” fad.
But there was something that caught my attention this week that brought about a renewed bout of introspection, and some deep thinking.
It was Finfloww co-founder Aryan Kochhar’s [post on X](https://x.com/aryan_kochhar/status/1875822014013075941):
As expected, this set off a highly contentious debate. Many think Kochhar is right. Many think he isn’t. And neither side is really 100% wrong. The question basically comes down to what you think is better: financial prudence or the independence to grow as an individual.
But before we go any deeper, I have to say I understand that this is a subject where there is no one-size-fits-all answer. For many of us, living with or away from parents (or in-laws) isn’t often a choice—especially so if you are a woman.
That said, I, too, have an opinion on this, and it’s one informed by my last two years living in the same house with my partner and my parents.
There’s no denying that for many, living with parents makes financial sense.
In the best-case scenario, where your parents are healthy, earning, and, well, willing to let you stay with them, you’ll probably end up saving a lot.
Let’s say you live in a metro city right now and spend about Rs 20,000 (US$233) a month on rent, and Rs 15,000 a month on electricity, food, and domestic help. That’s Rs 4.2 lakh a year.
If you move in with your parents, and they don’t let you contribute to rent or upkeep, you could just invest this amount in a nice systematic investment plan (SIP) for a tidy payoff.
In fact, if you are currently paying around Rs 30,000 a month in rent, which I think is certainly not out of the realm of possibility, your yearly savings could be Rs 5.4 lakh. By putting it into an SIP—assuming a 12% rate of return—you could end up with more than Rs 1 crore in ten years.
That sounds sweet, I know; like every finfluencer’s Youtube thumbnail.
But there’s an element of truth here.
There are many scenarios where Indians end up living with their parents, so I won’t try to enumerate all of them. But unless your parents are retired and/or don’t have a personal income, you are almost guaranteed to save money.
One reason is what I like to call the Provider Syndrome. I believe many Indian parents, especially fathers, are hard-wired and conditioned to believe that they should be providers and that it’s their most important responsibility to take care of the finances of the family. To many, it doesn’t matter that their child is financially independent and that sharing the expenses would only help.
In my case, when I was home for about a year during the pandemic, my father wouldn’t let me pay for anything. In fact, he’d get offended if I touched my wallet when he was around. Gifting him a plain, simple shirt involved much pleading and promises along the lines of: “Fine, please keep this. No more gifts till next year!” Uff!
But complaints aside, I did end up saving a lot that year. And I was starting to like that particular aspect of living with my parents.
Who wouldn’t? In most tier-1 and an increasing number of tier-2 cities housing prices and rents are rising faster than income. We wrote about it [here](https://the-ken.com/story/behind-the-luxury-drumbeating-a-housing-affordability-crisis-looms/). And instead of the guilt-inducing food ordered from outside, I was being fed home-cooked food. Thrice a day! Then there were those occasional hugs from my mother.
And unlike in the West, no one here is making fun of young adults for living with their parents, so it seemed like the perfect balance between being independent and finding a safe comforting cocoon.
But the warm, fuzzy feeling didn’t last very long.
First, it was getting a bit too comfortable. Second, the almost constant disagreements on any subject started to wear on me.
Lavanya Mohan [summed it up](https://x.com/lavsmohan/status/1876615581551731139) best on X.
Apart from the ones pointing out, correctly, that there are a huge number of people for whom none of the above assumptions apply, the crux of almost every argument against Kochhar’s post is this: living alone lets you develop a personality of your own.
I strongly agree.
In terms of finances, if you’re living with your parents and saving almost everything you earn, it probably means your contribution to household expenses is negligible. So things like budgeting, investing, saving for future events, and knowing when to cut back on expenses may remain quite foreign to you.
Also, I think that when there’s someone constantly shielding you and treating you like a child, you never really learn anything.
Looking back at my own life, I started developing even basic life skills like cooking only after I started living alone. Before that, I had no clue that lowering the flame on the stove and doing the dishes until my meal was cooked would help me save on gas as well as time.
I didn’t know that I liked to aimlessly roam in Delhi’s Lodhi Garden, or that if I didn’t pay electricity bills on time, they would actually cut the power supply. I’d always thought that was just to scare customers.
Living alone teaches you that the world outside your parents’ warm embrace is usually cold. And that you’re mostly on your own. But then, you’ll also cross paths with people who make you trust the world again. You get the chance to form deep, organic connections.
Even the concept of home changes. When you live with your parents, home is mostly a place to retire after the day’s ordeals. But living alone means having friends over, spending more time with people you intellectually resonate with, almost like a family you can choose. And for couples, a home without parents offers the chance to forge and strengthen their own bonds, without the many little frictions that come with having parents in the picture.
All that said, I personally believe that there’s probably a good middle ground here.
Because as attractive and even essential as the chance to grow individually is, the financial benefits of having your parents with you are very hard to ignore. In fact, I recently met a couple of young entrepreneurs who’re doing the same and using the situation to their advantage. “I recently went to a bank for a home loan. They denied saying my monthly income is not enough to afford the house. So I asked my father to become a co-applicant. Now the bank has no problem. His cashflow and my long career ahead made them comfortable,” said one of them.
Sure, having parents around can shrink your own spaces a little. But perhaps the people saying this entirely stops your growth as a person need to find a good [Third Place](https://esl.uchicago.edu/2023/11/01/third-places-what-are-they-and-why-are-they-important-to-american-culture/) (one that isn’t home or work) where they can connect and spend time with people who share their interests and pursuits.
If it were left to me, I’d want to live alone for a little while to find myself, and then move in with my parents once I’ve discovered or built a Third Place that facilitates my growth. And let’s face it, beyond a certain point, our parents need us too.

Good Morning Ishan,

Around 18 months back, my colleague Sumanth wrote a story about Softbank’s $100 billion Vision Fund. He had a word to describe the largest VC fund ever in history. He called it a ‘Leviathan’. He meant it in the context of a sea monster, threatening to upend every aspect of startup investing all over the world. 

In it, while arguing that, ‘Softbank is the new IPO’, he wrote, ‘It is easy to see how this could lead to gaming behaviour where VCs no longer focus on helping their portfolio companies go public but instead attempt to engineer a secondary exit to SoftBank.’

18 months back, this was a theory. 

Today’s story is a case study on this exact scenario.

Delhivery is one of India’s largest third-party logistics service providers. It had raised millions of dollars over the past few years and was gearing towards a public listing - or an IPO. Until Sahil Barua, the CEO of Delhivery, got a call. From Softbank. 

Delhivery had a decision to make. To IPO or to take the money?  

They thought about it. A lot. 

Finally, they took the money from Softbank. 

$350 million. 

Our story is not about what happened. It’s about why it happened. It’s about how decisions like this get made and what CEOs like Sahil Barua are thinking and weighing in their minds when they try to get to an answer. It’s about why - for large startups, IPOs offer great reward, but with several caveats. And the consequences of saying no to Softbank. 

Above all, it’s a story about the most important question a founder may ever face in his life. And one possible answer.

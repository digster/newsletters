Good Morning Ishan,
Have you heard of arbitrage funds? Most investors in India wouldn’t have. This rather obscure category of funds—which essentially plays on price differences in different markets—is usually dwarfed by its far more famous cousins that investors flock to. Like, say, large-cap equity funds and liquid debt funds. But strange things have been happening in this world. Arbitrage funds have been on a roll in the recent past with huge inflows. This, while liquid funds saw big outflows, and equity funds struggled to retain investor love. In fact, arbitrage funds topped the charts with maximum net inflows in May. The June data that will be out next week might well see this continue. What gives?
The genesis of this swing lies in the contradictions that the Union Budgets of the past few years have been trying to eliminate. But haven’t entirely. Loopholes have been left, and savvy, mostly rich, investors now seem to be making the most of this. By pouring money into arbitrage funds that live in a liminal space, effectively belonging to one category but getting the benefits of another.
The losers in this bargain might not just be liquid debt funds that offer similar pre-tax returns as arbitrage funds, but fall short post-tax. Bank deposits—the key constituency that the government wanted to shield—will also be at the receiving end. And that has consequences, industry experts told Archishma, the author of today’s [story](https://the-ken.com/story/the-in-between-investment-sounding-the-death-knell-for-fixed-deposits/?utm_source=daily_story&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=daily_newsletter).

India’s baby rocket has found a powerful new parent. In a landmark tech-transfer deal, Isro has handed over its Small Satellite Launch Vehicle (SSLV) to Hindustan Aeronautics Ltd (HAL), betting on the state-run aerospace giant to commercialise rapid, low-cost space launches. But HAL’s track record, with chronic delays and an overflowing order book, raises the risk that this “small fish” might just get lost in a very big pond.

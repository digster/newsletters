We have two stories for you today.

The first is about More, the Indian supermarket chain. When Samara Capital and Amazon bought it for Rs 4,200 crore (US$503 million) in 2018, More was living up to its name a little too well, opening stores of every scale and selling everything under the sun. In the words of Vinod Nambiar, the chain’s managing director, “More had a reasonably unclear proposition” at the time.

The solution was to shave off a lot. Many of the larger stores, which had floor plans of at least 20,000 sq ft, were wound up. Inventory was scaled down from 43 days back then to 30 now. Product categories like apparel, crockery, and white goods were removed. Only food and groceries remain, and the way each store runs is the same—the system is centralised, rigid, much like the way grocery chains Aldi and Lidl operate overseas.

More’s preference for the small format appears to be working. It racked up sales of more than Rs 4,050 crore in the year ended March 2024. The chain had EBITDA losses of Rs 230 crore but has trimmed that figure since then. It is close to breaking even in the September quarter. Today, More operates 755 supermarkets and 22 hypermarkets, and groceries account for 95% of its revenue.

Seetharaman spoke with Nambiar to find out how More’s leadership pulled the company out of a rut. Check it out.

Next, we head to Manila to revisit the Philippines’ semiconductor companies, specifically their recruitment practices. As it turns out, these manufacturers’ assembly line workers are highly educated—much more so than their counterparts in other markets. Speaking to a range of staff, Isa found out that even the employees on the lowest rungs of Philippine chip producers graduated with engineering degrees.

You’d think that’s a good thing, but it’s getting in the way of the development of the Philippines’ chip sector.

Among the 50,000 engineering graduates that leave Philippine universities each year, those who are interested in building a career in semiconductors need to start out on the factory floor. One engineer who spoke to The Ken said he worked on the assembly line for seven years before he was able to transition into his first engineering job.

There’s plenty at stake. Philippine semiconductor firms are known for handling assembling, packaging, and testing chips, but they generally want to move upstream into design. Until these firms figure out a way to match their accessible talent to the jobs that are being offered, there will be a low cap on how much they can grow.

Good Morning Ishan,

My car has one of the roughly 20 million Fastags tied to a Paytm* account. Which means come 16 March, I’ll be unable to pay toll or parking charges, unless I get a new Fastag.

With the Reserve Bank of India axing Paytm Payments Bank’s services, the field is wide open for Paytm’s rivals to poach its Fastag users. And company set to make the most of Paytm’s struggles is Park+, the Peak XV Partners-backed car-services startup.

Interestingly, Park+ is led by Amit Lakhotia, who built the very wallet used to make Fastag payments on Paytm, which has a 25% market share.

Many banks believe Park+ is well placed to become the largest distributor of Fastags in Paytm’s absence. But why are banks happy to let Park+ take the lead when they can distribute their own Fastags? And what’s Lakhotia’s game plan to grow what is already his largest business? Read Rounak’s timely story and find the answers.

The coda to the Byju’s saga—unfolding gradually, then suddenly

The signs were always there: mis-selling of courses, an absurdly high valuation, and accounting issues.

But it took quite a while for things to get really dire for Byju’s. Then, the troubles started piling up thick and fast for the edtech, which was once valued at US$22 billion.

Sample the headlines from this weekend alone:

– Byju’s missing $533 million stuck in unnamed offshore trust
– Macquarie slashes Byju’s valuation by 98% after Julius Baer protest
– Byju’s CEO Raveendran says can’t pay workers’ salaries, investors blocked funding

These numbers would have given anyone pause had they not become so routine in the context of the company. Byju Raveendran is digging his heels in even as shareholders demand his ouster.

But it’s impossible to imagine a turn of events in which the Icarian founder has the last laugh.

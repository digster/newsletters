Ever worked at an organisation so coveted that every colleague of yours has been approached (at least once last month) by a competitor looking to poach? I can relate.
As can those working at Hotstar.
Between January 2019 and early next year, Hotstar would've lost two of its senior-most executives to Big Tech leaning in—and possibly making Don Corleone-esque offers that can't be refused. Sanjay Gupta, the head of Hotstar's parent company Disney India-Star, is all set to join Google India; and Ajit Mohan, ex-CEO at Hotstar, left to lead Facebook's India business early this year.
No coincidence there.
The Google-Hotstar situation with Gupta is borne out of an almost-musical chairs situation, with Rajan Anandan, ex-Google India and SEA operations head, leaving for a management chair at VC firm Sequoia.
What's even happening, right?
Well, that's Jon and Pranav's story today. At least that's the precise question that got them looking into just what's cooking at Hotstar. The video streaming platform, big on sports and, well, Game of Thrones—has been doing something very right.
It beat its own global record of 10.3 million concurrent viewers with a whopping 18.6 million viewers for the final game of the Indian Premier League (IPL) cricket tournament in May this year. And then beat it again in July with 25.3 million viewers for the India-New Zealand World Cup semi-final. And a lot has gone into making this happen.
A lot that Google's YouTube could use, especially with competition like the Chinese TikTok too close for comfort. Well, hullo, Sanjay Gupta, I suppose.
“YouTube never expected Hotstar to succeed, but it has,” a senior executive at an OTT business told The Ken under anonymity.

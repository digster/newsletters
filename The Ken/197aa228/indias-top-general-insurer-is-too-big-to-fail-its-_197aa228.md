Good Morning Ishan,
You know how insurance is supposed to work, right? The insurer collects money from lots of people who are probably fine, pays out money to the few who are not fine, and invests the difference in safe, boring things to earn a little return. If the insurer is really good, it does this profitably for decades and becomes a national institution.
New India Assurance is a national institution. It’s also, somehow, an insurance company that seems designed to lose money. For every Rs 100 it collects in premiums, it spends Rs 117. It’s not even a one-off; it’s a decade-long average. The business model, if you can call it that, is: take in cash, light it on fire, then keep your fingers crossed that your investment portfolio will smother the flames.
The company is so central to the economy that regulators keep calling it a “systemically important insurer”, which essentially means it is “too tangled to fail”. But behind the triple-A ratings and the century-old legacy is something closer to a leaky bucket than a financial institution. Its private peers make underwriting profits or at least keep the bleeding contained. New India just bleeds bigger.
And yet. Somewhere in the overseas subsidiaries, things actually look…normal? There’s even a new boss who says they might stop writing loss-making policies out of sheer habit.
In today’s piece, Kevin digs into the numbers. The underwriting ratios. The float games. The motor-insurance black hole. There’s even a cameo from a peer that reported a >50% Return on Equity, by losing money twice. Zepto, Rapido, and Meesho are three companies that have one similarity. They all entered a space which looked like it had become a comfortable duopoly, and then they pushed the envelope further. Be it Rapido with its subscription model, Zepto with faster deliveries, or Meesho’s zero-commission model—they took a shot at the customers most big players overlooked.
How did they do it? And are there more disruptive players who could shake up other duopolies in India? Hosts Rohin Dharmakumar and Praveen Gopal Krishnan break it down with Professor Rajendra Srivastava, executive director at ISB Centre for Business Innovation, in the latest episode of Two by Two.

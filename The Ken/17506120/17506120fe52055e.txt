UK Prime Minister Boris Johnson and Indian industrialist Mukesh Ambani have a lot in common. Both live in palatial luxury, preside over empires (though Johnson's has seen better days), and both understand that the world is shifting away from fossil fuels.

Johnson has been increasingly vocal about clean energy. Last month, he promised that the UK's chief foreign lender will no longer offer financial support to fossil fuel projects. Earlier this month, he claimed the UK would be the wind energy capital of the world. "As Saudi Arabia is to oil, the UK is to wind—a place of almost limitless resource. But in the case of wind, without the carbon emissions and without the damage to the environment," he declared.

The beauty of being a politician is that grand proclamations will often suffice.

For Ambani, however, mere lip service isn't enough. Oil forms the very basis of his conglomerate, Reliance Industries Ltd, and remains core to everything it has done. While Reliance has made waves in telecom, retail, and internet services, all of this was made possible by the windfall of cash provided by the company's refining and petrochemicals business.

Though oil may have once been black gold, its best days are behind it. By 2050, global demand for oil could drop by as much as 80%. Rather than risk his crown jewel losing its sheen, Ambani is looking to evolve with the times, turning Reliance into a clean energy company instead.

Like with everything else he has done, Ambani doesn't lack ambition. Hydrogen, wind, solar, fuel cells, and batteries are all part of Reliance's plan to go green. Transforming from an oil-first company to a clean energy leader, however, will be a long, arduous process. Reliance must change its very DNA while also embracing technology it has little experience with. Yes, it is late to the races, but when has that ever stopped it?

Good Morning Ishan,
What’s in a title? Quite a lot, especially if that title is chief digital officer (CDO).
Let’s start with a boardroom scene: It’s 2016. A chief information officer (CIO) sits in a corner, presenting slides about server uptime and network health. Polite nods and a mumbled “good job” later, the CIO smiles and leaves. Their job here is done.
Fast forward to 2024. The same boardroom looks different.
The CDO is now at the head of the table, not just presenting but leading. They’re talking about virtual reality in manufacturing, slashing loan approvals from days to hours, and driving a 500% boost in digital-customer onboarding. Everyone’s hooked. Strategies are reshaped. But their work here is far from done.
This is the evolution of corporate tech leadership—from managing IT infra to defining the business itself. Here are just a few examples:
- Namrita Mahindro (Aditya Birla Chemicals): Used virtual reality for factory workers’ safety training and augmented reality for plant maintenance.
- Jignesh Vora (Muthoot Finance): Partnered with Google Pay, making gold loans easier.
- Bhargab Dutta (Centuryply): Turned plywood into a digital success with loyalty apps.
And it’s not just about vision but also value. CDOs can now command up to Rs 7 crore annually, doubling their salaries in just four years, while CIOs are stuck in a time warp. The shift is so stark that recruiters barely hire CIOs anymore. Interestingly, unlike a CIO, you don’t even need a tech background to be a CDO.

From what’s in a title to what’s in a name: in this week’s edition of Long and Short, Anand writes about India’s largest airline.
Just this week, Indigo Airlines emerged victorious in its legal joust with automaker Mahindra & Mahindra over the alleged infringement of its 6E logo. But then, it was also ranked among the 10 worst airlines in the world by a German consumer-rights group, Airhelp, just last week.
Add to it the financial woes. Despite the Indian market being a near duopoly now (Indigo and Air India group), and Indigo having over 60% market share, the airline has suffered a loss in the September quarter. And Indigo’s passengers are now feeling the pinch from high ticket costs.

One of my absolute favorite experiences is discovering old novels
I knew nothing about. Maybe you hear about them mentioned in a
book you’re reading. Or you fall into some classic novel that was
written about that moment in time and find that somehow it’s
exactly about our moment in time. Maybe you hear about a book
that almost won the National Book Award or the Pulitzer and then
was lost in the shadow of some more important work. Or you read
the author’s obituary and you learn that it was an enormous hit
when it came out. Or someone who's a little older than you tells
you about some book that was formative for them many years
earlier. And then you read it and you’re like, of course this
sold millions of copies! It’s incredible! I had the experience
last month with The Crisis (
https://bc5923e4.click.convertkit-mail4.com/8kupm9rk3oboh2zn7rdhkhkg3o3gni3hq934k/3ohphkh303lk8zbr/aHR0cHM6Ly9nZW5pLnVzL2N6Tmxs
) by Winston Churchill, which was written by not that Churchill,
but happened to be the bestselling novel in the United States in
1901. It also happened to be the book that General Fox Conner
pressed into the hands of a young Dwight D. Eisenhower after this
promising young officer told him he didn’t like reading history,
having had the interest beaten out of him at West Point. It’s
funny because I’ve been reading a lot of historical fiction like
that myself recently, including some novels that I wish someone
had pressed in my hands when I was younger (Ides of March (
https://bc5923e4.click.convertkit-mail4.com/8kupm9rk3oboh2zn7rdhkhkg3o3gni3hq934k/48hvhehmvme73wfx/aHR0cHM6Ly93d3cudGhlcGFpbnRlZHBvcmNoLmNvbS9wcm9kdWN0cy90aGUtaWRlcy1vZi1tYXJjaC10aGUtY2xhc3NpYy1lcGlzdG9sYXJ5LW5vdmVsLW9mLWp1bGl1cy1jYWVzYXItYW5kLWFuY2llbnQtcm9tZS1ieS10aG9ybnRvbi13aWxkZXI_X3Bvcz0xJl9zaWQ9MWJmMDI3ZmUyJl9zcz1y
) or Augustus (
https://bc5923e4.click.convertkit-mail4.com/8kupm9rk3oboh2zn7rdhkhkg3o3gni3hq934k/wnh2hghqnqpldnt7/aHR0cHM6Ly93d3cudGhlcGFpbnRlZHBvcmNoLmNvbS9wcm9kdWN0cy9hdWd1c3R1cy1uZXcteW9yay1yZXZpZXctYm9va3MtY2xhc3NpY3M_X3Bvcz0xJl9zaWQ9YTMzNzFlNzlhJl9zcz1y
)). I had that experience with Ask the Dust (
https://bc5923e4.click.convertkit-mail4.com/8kupm9rk3oboh2zn7rdhkhkg3o3gni3hq934k/reh8hohm4mropdc2/aHR0cHM6Ly93d3cudGhlcGFpbnRlZHBvcmNoLmNvbS9wcm9kdWN0cy9maWN0aW9uMz9fcG9zPTEmX3NpZD00NzM5NTYwZjEmX3NzPXI=

​Darkness at Noon: A Novel (
https://bc5923e4.click.convertkit-mail4.com/8kupm9rk3oboh2zn7rdhkhkg3o3gni3hq934k/8ghqhohozov78vck/aHR0cHM6Ly93d3cudGhlcGFpbnRlZHBvcmNoLmNvbS9wcm9kdWN0cy9kYXJrbmVzcy1hdC1ub29uLWEtbm92ZWw_X3Bvcz0xJl9zaWQ9Mjg4MTk5ZDUxJl9zcz1y
) by Arthur Koestler
​I’m reading a lot about Vietnam, as you know. Did you know
Stockdale read this book about a communist torture prison when he
was in graduate school at Stanford? And then years later, when he
was a POW in a communist torture prison, he wrote a coded letter
home to his wife that…well, remember what I said about learning
things from obituaries? Here’s a line from the New York Times
obituary (
https://bc5923e4.click.convertkit-mail4.com/8kupm9rk3oboh2zn7rdhkhkg3o3gni3hq934k/vqh3hrholo7q69sg/aHR0cHM6Ly93d3cubnl0aW1lcy5jb20vMjAxNS8xMC8xNi91cy9zeWJpbC1zdG9ja2RhbGUtZmllcmNlLWFkdm9jYXRlLWZvci1wb3dzLWRpZXMtYXQtOTAuaHRtbCM6fjp0ZXh0PVN5YmlsJTIwU3RvY2tkYWxlJTJDJTIwRmllcmNlJTIwQWR2b2NhdGUlMjBmb3IlMjBQLk8uVy5zJTIwYW5kJTIwVGhlaXIlMjBGYW1pbGllcyUyQyUyMERpZXMlMjBhdCUyMDkwJnRleHQ9ZGFya25lc3MlMjBhdCUyMG5vb24uJUUyJTgwJTlEJTIwVGhlJTIwcmVmZXJlbmNlJTIwd2FzJTIwdG8lMjB0aGUlMjB0aXRsZSUyMG9mJTIwdGhlJTIwMTk0MQ==
) of his wife, Sybil: “In one, Admiral Stockdale wrote that
though Vietnam was thought to be a tropical country, Hanoi was
actually quite cool in the winter, occasionally with ‘darkness at
noon.’ The reference was to the title of the 1941 Arthur Koestler
novel of Stalinist-era Communism that he knew his wife had read;
in the book, political prisoners are tortured.” I thought this
book (
https://bc5923e4.click.convertkit-mail4.com/8kupm9rk3oboh2zn7rdhkhkg3o3gni3hq934k/8ghqhohozov78vck/aHR0cHM6Ly93d3cudGhlcGFpbnRlZHBvcmNoLmNvbS9wcm9kdWN0cy9kYXJrbmVzcy1hdC1ub29uLWEtbm92ZWw_X3Bvcz0xJl9zaWQ9Mjg4MTk5ZDUxJl9zcz1y
), which Koestler wrote after his own experiences in a communist
torture prison, and his girlfriend dashed off to publishers, was
incredible. Especially the scene where the prisoners beat a
funeral march with their shoes as one of their own is dragged off
to be shot…and to think that Koestler wrote this (
https://bc5923e4.click.convertkit-mail4.com/8kupm9rk3oboh2zn7rdhkhkg3o3gni3hq934k/8ghqhohozov78vck/aHR0cHM6Ly93d3cudGhlcGFpbnRlZHBvcmNoLmNvbS9wcm9kdWN0cy9kYXJrbmVzcy1hdC1ub29uLWEtbm92ZWw_X3Bvcz0xJl9zaWQ9Mjg4MTk5ZDUxJl9zcz1y
) before most of what we’d eventually learn about Stalin’s
purges, Mao’s purges, and later what happened to Stockdale in
Hanoi. Just an incredible book that I can’t stop telling people
about.

​The Quiet American (
https://bc5923e4.click.convertkit-mail4.com/8kupm9rk3oboh2zn7rdhkhkg3o3gni3hq934k/l2hehmhlel9dp8u6/aHR0cHM6Ly93d3cudGhlcGFpbnRlZHBvcmNoLmNvbS9wcm9kdWN0cy90aGUtcXVpZXQtYW1lcmljYW4tcGVuZ3Vpbi1jbGFzc2ljcy1kZWx1eGUtZWRpdGlvbj9fcG9zPTgmX3NpZD03NTYyZTEwNzkmX3NzPXI=
) by Graham Greene
​Ok, I at least knew of The Quiet American (
https://bc5923e4.click.convertkit-mail4.com/8kupm9rk3oboh2zn7rdhkhkg3o3gni3hq934k/l2hehmhlel9dp8u6/aHR0cHM6Ly93d3cudGhlcGFpbnRlZHBvcmNoLmNvbS9wcm9kdWN0cy90aGUtcXVpZXQtYW1lcmljYW4tcGVuZ3Vpbi1jbGFzc2ljcy1kZWx1eGUtZWRpdGlvbj9fcG9zPTgmX3NpZD03NTYyZTEwNzkmX3NzPXI=
), but having tried and failed on a couple of Graham Greene
novels already, I hadn’t given it a try. I should have started
here all along. As I’ve said before, reading about the past is
the best way to understand the present. Could there be a more
relevant novel today? In fact, it almost makes you pine for the
old days, given the naked imperialism of this moment, resurgent
foreign meddling and throwback colonialism. “I never knew a man
who had better motives for all the trouble he caused,” is the
famous line in the book…again, to think Graham wrote this in
1955, 10 years before American Marines landed in Da Nang. Look,
I’ll say sometimes you hear about these ‘classic’ novels and you
think, do I really have to read 522 pages (which The Crisis (
https://bc5923e4.click.convertkit-mail4.com/8kupm9rk3oboh2zn7rdhkhkg3o3gni3hq934k/3ohphkh303lk8zbr/aHR0cHM6Ly9nZW5pLnVzL2N6Tmxs
) clocks in at)? Well, the good news is that The Quiet American (
https://bc5923e4.click.convertkit-mail4.com/8kupm9rk3oboh2zn7rdhkhkg3o3gni3hq934k/l2hehmhlel9dp8u6/aHR0cHM6Ly93d3cudGhlcGFpbnRlZHBvcmNoLmNvbS9wcm9kdWN0cy90aGUtcXVpZXQtYW1lcmljYW4tcGVuZ3Vpbi1jbGFzc2ljcy1kZWx1eGUtZWRpdGlvbj9fcG9zPTgmX3NpZD03NTYyZTEwNzkmX3NzPXI=
) (along with Darkness at Noon (
https://bc5923e4.click.convertkit-mail4.com/8kupm9rk3oboh2zn7rdhkhkg3o3gni3hq934k/8ghqhohozov78vck/aHR0cHM6Ly93d3cudGhlcGFpbnRlZHBvcmNoLmNvbS9wcm9kdWN0cy9kYXJrbmVzcy1hdC1ub29uLWEtbm92ZWw_X3Bvcz0xJl9zaWQ9Mjg4MTk5ZDUxJl9zcz1y
)) is nice and tight…just around 200 pages.

​Ok, so I took Team of Rivals (
https://bc5923e4.click.convertkit-mail4.com/8kupm9rk3oboh2zn7rdhkhkg3o3gni3hq934k/dpheh0heveg2x2fm/aHR0cHM6Ly93d3cudGhlcGFpbnRlZHBvcmNoLmNvbS9wcm9kdWN0cy90ZWFtLW9mLXJpdmFscy10aGUtcG9saXRpY2FsLWdlbml1cy1vZi1hYnJhaGFtLWxpbmNvbG4_X3Bvcz0xJl9zaWQ9Y2M1M2Q0ODUyJl9zcz1y

finish The Bully Pulpit (
https://bc5923e4.click.convertkit-mail4.com/8kupm9rk3oboh2zn7rdhkhkg3o3gni3hq934k/m2h7h5h3v3plx4bm/aHR0cHM6Ly93d3cudGhlcGFpbnRlZHBvcmNoLmNvbS9zZWFyY2g_cT1UaGUlMjBCdWxseSUyMFB1bHBpdCUzQSUyMFRoZW9kb3JlJTIwUm9vc2V2ZWx0JTJDJTIwV2lsbGlhbSUyMEhvd2FyZCUyMFRhZnQlMkMlMjBhbmQlMjB0aGUlMjBHb2xkZW4lMjBBZ2UlMjBvZiUyMEpvdXJuYWxpc20lMkEmdHlwZT1wcm9kdWN0

https://bc5923e4.click.convertkit-mail4.com/8kupm9rk3oboh2zn7rdhkhkg3o3gni3hq934k/m2h7h5h3v3plx4bm/aHR0cHM6Ly93d3cudGhlcGFpbnRlZHBvcmNoLmNvbS9zZWFyY2g_cT1UaGUlMjBCdWxseSUyMFB1bHBpdCUzQSUyMFRoZW9kb3JlJTIwUm9vc2V2ZWx0JTJDJTIwV2lsbGlhbSUyMEhvd2FyZCUyMFRhZnQlMkMlMjBhbmQlMjB0aGUlMjBHb2xkZW4lMjBBZ2UlMjBvZiUyMEpvdXJuYWxpc20lMkEmdHlwZT1wcm9kdWN0

https://bc5923e4.click.convertkit-mail4.com/8kupm9rk3oboh2zn7rdhkhkg3o3gni3hq934k/7qh7h8h9q97xegcz/aHR0cHM6Ly93d3cudGhlcGFpbnRlZHBvcmNoLmNvbS9wcm9kdWN0cy90ZWFtLW9mLXJpdmFscy10aGUtcG9saXRpY2FsLWdlbml1cy1vZi1hYnJhaGFtLWxpbmNvbG4_X3Bvcz0xJl9zaWQ9MTZhZTM0YTFkJl9zcz1y

​John Lewis: A Life (
https://bc5923e4.click.convertkit-mail4.com/8kupm9rk3oboh2zn7rdhkhkg3o3gni3hq934k/z2hghnhenevmg4ap/aHR0cHM6Ly9nZW5pLnVzL1VRY3JGb2U=
) by David Greenberg
​Earlier this month, I posted some books that have changed my
life with regards to Black History and the Civil Rights Movement
(
https://bc5923e4.click.convertkit-mail4.com/8kupm9rk3oboh2zn7rdhkhkg3o3gni3hq934k/p8heh9h4m40pv2bq/aHR0cHM6Ly93d3cuaW5zdGFncmFtLmNvbS9wL0RVOGVRaTFEUzRFLz9pbWdfaW5kZXg9MQ==
). You really can’t read about John Lewis and not want to know
more, so I’ve been making my way through Greenberg’s biography of
John Lewis. It can be so easy to feel like greatness is a thing
of the past, but it’s easy to forget, John Lewis wasn’t born that
long ago! (Well, after my grandparents, only about 15 years
before my parents.) He is a modern figure. His example is there
for all of us. That baton can be picked up, in our own small
way…indeed it must. A prominent contributor to the civil rights
movement, John Lewis was not only a Freedom Rider and leader of
the Nashville sit-in, but he also delivered one of the most
important addresses at the 1963 March on Washington (at age 23!).
He said it was important to engage in “good trouble, necessary
trouble,” and was arrested over 40 times protesting segregation,
including on the Edmund Pettus Bridge on Bloody Sunday. For
another great look at John Lewis, please read Halberstam’s The
Children (
https://bc5923e4.click.convertkit-mail4.com/8kupm9rk3oboh2zn7rdhkhkg3o3gni3hq934k/x0hph6heoe64gzh5/aHR0cHM6Ly93d3cudGhlcGFpbnRlZHBvcmNoLmNvbS9wcm9kdWN0cy90aGUtY2hpbGRyZW4_X3Bvcz0xJl9zaWQ9MzM4YWM2ZjBjJl9zcz1y
). I also really enjoyed Drew Gilpin Faust’s memoir Necessary
Trouble (
https://bc5923e4.click.convertkit-mail4.com/8kupm9rk3oboh2zn7rdhkhkg3o3gni3hq934k/6qheh8hlzlg3q6uo/aHR0cHM6Ly93d3cudGhlcGFpbnRlZHBvcmNoLmNvbS9wcm9kdWN0cy9uZWNlc3NhcnktdHJvdWJsZT9fcG9zPTEmX3NpZD03NDk3MTVhNjMmX3NzPXI=
), which was inspired by John Lewis, to go to my point about
picking up the baton.

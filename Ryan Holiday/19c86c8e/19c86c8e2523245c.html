One of my absolute favorite experiences is discovering old novels I knew nothing about. Maybe you hear about them mentioned in a book you’re reading. Or you fall into some classic novel that was written about that moment in time and find that somehow it’s exactly about our moment in time. Maybe you hear about a book that almost won the National Book Award or the Pulitzer and then was lost in the shadow of some more important work. Or you read the author’s obituary and you learn that it was an enormous hit when it came out. Or someone who's a little older than you tells you about some book that was formative for them many years earlier. And then you read it and you’re like, of course this sold millions of copies! It’s incredible! I had the experience last month with The Crisis by Winston Churchill, which was written by not that Churchill, but happened to be the bestselling novel in the United States in 1901. It also happened to be the book that General Fox Conner pressed into the hands of a young Dwight D. Eisenhower after this promising young officer told him he didn’t like reading history, having had the interest beaten out of him at West Point. It’s funny because I’ve been reading a lot of historical fiction like that myself recently, including some novels that I wish someone had pressed in my hands when I was younger (Ides of March or Augustus). I had that experience with Ask the Dust many years ago and even wrote a big piece on why this classic almost didn’t become a classic. In any case, I had that experience several times this month with some of the books below…so instead of belaboring the point, I’ll just tell you about them. Because I think you’ll have the same experience.

​Darkness at Noon: A Novel by Arthur Koestler
​I’m reading a lot about Vietnam, as you know. Did you know Stockdale read this book about a communist torture prison when he was in graduate school at Stanford? And then years later, when he was a POW in a communist torture prison, he wrote a coded letter home to his wife that…well, remember what I said about learning things from obituaries? Here’s a line from the New York Times obituary of his wife, Sybil: “In one, Admiral Stockdale wrote that though Vietnam was thought to be a tropical country, Hanoi was actually quite cool in the winter, occasionally with ‘darkness at noon.’ The reference was to the title of the 1941 Arthur Koestler novel of Stalinist-era Communism that he knew his wife had read; in the book, political prisoners are tortured.” I thought this book, which Koestler wrote after his own experiences in a communist torture prison, and his girlfriend dashed off to publishers, was incredible. Especially the scene where the prisoners beat a funeral march with their shoes as one of their own is dragged off to be shot…and to think that Koestler wrote this before most of what we’d eventually learn about Stalin’s purges, Mao’s purges, and later what happened to Stockdale in Hanoi. Just an incredible book that I can’t stop telling people about.

​The Quiet American by Graham Greene
​Ok, I at least knew of The Quiet American, but having tried and failed on a couple of Graham Greene novels already, I hadn’t given it a try. I should have started here all along. As I’ve said before, reading about the past is the best way to understand the present. Could there be a more relevant novel today? In fact, it almost makes you pine for the old days, given the naked imperialism of this moment, resurgent foreign meddling and throwback colonialism. “I never knew a man who had better motives for all the trouble he caused,” is the famous line in the book…again, to think Graham wrote this in 1955, 10 years before American Marines landed in Da Nang. Look, I’ll say sometimes you hear about these ‘classic’ novels and you think, do I really have to read 522 pages (which The Crisis clocks in at)? Well, the good news is that The Quiet American (along with Darkness at Noon) is nice and tight…just around 200 pages.

​The Bully Pulpit: Theodore Roosevelt, William Howard Taft, and the Golden Age of Journalism by Doris Kearns Goodwin
​Ok, so I took Team of Rivals with me to Australia a couple of years ago, thinking that it was easier than lugging a bunch of books with me. I guess I learned my lesson because I spent last week frantically trying to finish The Bully Pulpit so I didn’t have to put a 912-page paperback in my backpack. That was hardly a punishment, though, because this book is fantastic and again…the perfect way to understand the present moment. Roosevelt’s astute use of the press, his ability to cultivate relationships with reporters to be able to sell his agenda, first to the public and then to Congress, was fascinating. What I like about Doris Kearns Goodwin (and here’s my interview with her about Lincoln) is that–unlike say Robert Caro or David McCullough, who usually give one singular individual the big, thick biography treatment–Goodwin often picks a series of contrasting individuals or some angle on those singular individuals that hasn’t been properly explored. That’s what she did with Team of Rivals, that’s what she does here with Roosevelt and Taft. She is a master of her craft and if you haven’t read any of her books, you must change that. If you need an easy entry point, I have raved for years about her book Leadership in Turbulent Times.

​John Lewis: A Life by David Greenberg
​Earlier this month, I posted some books that have changed my life with regards to Black History and the Civil Rights Movement. You really can’t read about John Lewis and not want to know more, so I’ve been making my way through Greenberg’s biography of John Lewis. It can be so easy to feel like greatness is a thing of the past, but it’s easy to forget, John Lewis wasn’t born that long ago! (Well, after my grandparents, only about 15 years before my parents.) He is a modern figure. His example is there for all of us. That baton can be picked up, in our own small way…indeed it must. A prominent contributor to the civil rights movement, John Lewis was not only a Freedom Rider and leader of the Nashville sit-in, but he also delivered one of the most important addresses at the 1963 March on Washington (at age 23!). He said it was important to engage in “good trouble, necessary trouble,” and was arrested over 40 times protesting segregation, including on the Edmund Pettus Bridge on Bloody Sunday. For another great look at John Lewis, please read Halberstam’s The Children. I also really enjoyed Drew Gilpin Faust’s memoir Necessary Trouble, which was inspired by John Lewis, to go to my point about picking up the baton.

Misc
​After I gave a list of my favorite shipwreck books last month (including The Zorg, A Marriage at Sea, A Night to Remember, Shadow Divers), someone wrote in to recommend Island of the Lost by Joan Druett, which is about two shipwrecks near New Zealand in 1864. A sucker for shipwreck books, I read it and loved it (it also reminded me of Wild Dark Shore…that whole part of the world is a surreal mystery to me). I think I have a problem. I flew a lot this month, which is probably what inspired me to put together this quick list of short books that you can finish on an airplane. It includes Claire Keegan’s Small Things Like These, The War of Art by Steven Pressfield, 84, Charing Cross Road by Helen Hanff, Montaigne by Stefan Zweig, and The Greatest Sentence Ever Written by Walter Isaacson. Any good ones I am missing? I like books like these because when I start to feel like I am losing momentum as a reader, they help me get back on track.

Kids
​Speaking of Doris Kearns Goodwin, she actually has a great kids-ish book that might help inspire your kids to try to be a great man or great woman of history. Last week, I had the lovely experience of reading to my nine-year-old Dog Man and Cat Kid, which was great…and somehow based on Steinbeck’s East of Eden? I was not expecting that, but we both loved it. My Stoic Virtues Series was also based (at least partly) on East of Eden, which you may have picked up on. My boys remain obsessed with Hamilton (and insisted, as is their gift of choice, on giving Chernow’s 832-page biography to two different classmates at birthday parties this month), and so we are reading You Should Meet: Lin-Manuel Miranda together. And then because of the winter Olympics, we watched Cool Runnings together, which was fun. Also, as fans of Greeking Out, they were very excited to listen to the first three episodes of Kenny Curtis’ new podcast, History Snacks. You might like this episode I did with Kenny about why history matters over at Daily Stoic.

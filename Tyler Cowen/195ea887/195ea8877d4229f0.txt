This is all from Andrew Mitrak, I will not double indent:

"Back in January, I listened to your interview on the Frames of Space podcast ( https://nam11.safelinks.protection.outlook.com/?url=https%3A%2F%2Fandrewxu218.substack.com%2Fp%2Ftyler-cowen-on-how-he-views-the-world-a86&data=05%7C02%7Ctcowen%40gmu.edu%7Ca61b90eb365d4403b08708dd6fb68d24%7C9e857255df574c47a0c00546460380cb%7C0%7C0%7C638789552027376614%7CUnknown%7CTWFpbGZsb3d8eyJFbXB0eU1hcGkiOnRydWUsIlYiOiIwLjAuMDAwMCIsIlAiOiJXaW4zMiIsIkFOIjoiTWFpbCIsIldUIjoyfQ%3D%3D%7C0%7C%7C%7C&sdata=sZ9uSzCaTnWb5mlwrIE2X8n6al0wZnnrAoDoYzYgLsE%3D&reserved=0 ) . You shared some advice that I haven't seen you share elsewhere, so I'm pasting a transcript below here (transcribed with Gemini 2.5 Pro)

> Host: Do you see podcasts in general, especially podcasts from academics, as a way to bridge the gap between humanities academia and the real world.

> Tyler: I don't think they need to be from academics. Maybe on average academics are worse at doing podcasts because they are not forced to get to the point by their other training.

> But I think if you have good content and a clearly defined niche, you can get considerable mind share from important and influential people by doing podcasts and there's still room for more.

>  If you do something Joe Rogan-like you can make a lot of money. That's not mainly how people can succeed, but if you want an actual audience, I would say try doing a podcast. Just make it good and don't worry whether or not you're an academic.

> Host: Make it good and make it consistent, and that's all that matters.

> Tyler: And have it, you know, it should have a clear image. So if your podcast is well, you know, I focus on the dobro guitar. Like that's an obscure thing, right? But in fact, there are people out there who play the dobro. They probably don't already have their own podcast. And if you're thinking of doing that, you should do it.

I heard this while I was putting together my own niche podcast about marketing history ( https://secure-web.cisco.com/16cXNUbZu-YFmxaXYB5NiCoQPy1PLMfcssKOkWFTXDdp3AoXUz7qddlJfspapYTavOdER1vNI2fwaPpeaAIsDaolRsOoMLBtEewwQIOaWCBtvhF2IhnG6IYM34tkmH0er2Yd50SG7TSApRY9t8d1mOkeAbPkEf2Sue4fGpv0ejxSU7YbHGyYxmPP5S7KFVyvIRbVydpooLUL21UwnJebseKVmMniY-6xC6NNf6VqT4ilp1P0m8ORdIsQ-68qJyqPB5uTh5bOrwioe2ExhcDf0h2zsdeKP6G1NhdQx23-4l5qCdxHrfkspOO-wbbqoyAtJzTRWXodt0M88kjrssNcaDIjQfilHwNwliEPMBeqp2c53YpWnBfpbwmnJk2tTCZCLPgWza88G7PTA1d9Ff_1yWRcTZvNXmHTFaAIEiqlqXKnzGIqoAtamJ7-_11uS-EaN/https%3A%2F%2Fmarketinghistory.org%2F ) . I had doubts about whether the podcast was worth pursuing, and your advice hit me at the right time and encouraged me to follow through on it. I'm a few months into the podcast and your advice has proven correct. I get to speak with some of the most interesting authors, academics, and marketers I admire just because I host a podcast. Other important and influential people have discovered my podcast, either organically or through podcast guests sharing the show. It's not topping the Spotify charts, but these connections have been incredibly rewarding."

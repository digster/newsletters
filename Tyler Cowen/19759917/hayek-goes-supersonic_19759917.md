When I post about [lifting the ban on supersonic flight](http://marginalrevolution.com?action=user_content_redirect&uuid=ba7ee990f7e0fc67d6accc6c0d1e19c2ed7aa7c0691ff96f534c38b6e53888ab&blog_id=42693868&post_id=90852&user_id=262258391&subs_id=225460721&signature=403ab2ef21e7e7260cbbe054a66924ab&email_name=new-post&user_email=ishan.mail@gmail.com&encoded_url=aHR0cHM6Ly9tYXJnaW5hbHJldm9sdXRpb24uY29tL21hcmdpbmFscmV2b2x1dGlvbi8yMDI1LzA2L3N1cGVyc29uaWNzLXRha2VvZmYuaHRtbA=&email_id=3395817706c4d62e09a23aa5d5315015), smart commenters show up with charts: optimal fuel burn is at Mach 0.78–0.84, they say, or no one wants to pay thousands to save a few hours. Maybe. But my reply is always the same: Bottled water!
In 2024, Americans spent[ $47 billion a year on H₂O](http://marginalrevolution.com?action=user_content_redirect&uuid=d8eafa18842b6c1ab3bd128c272f95d54ec75950f5c7053d70a3a11b643b136d&blog_id=42693868&post_id=90852&user_id=262258391&subs_id=225460721&signature=36579735351f2aae728857f4a904aa6f&email_name=new-post&user_email=ishan.mail@gmail.com&encoded_url=aHR0cHM6Ly93d3cuZ3JhbmR2aWV3cmVzZWFyY2guY29tL2luZHVzdHJ5LWFuYWx5c2lzL3VzLWJvdHRsZWQtd2F0ZXItbWFya2V0LXJlcG9ydA=&email_id=3395817706c4d62e09a23aa5d5315015) that they could get for nearly free. That still boggles my mind—but bottled water has passed the market test. I argue for lifting the SST ban, and similar policies, not because we know supersonics will work but because we don’t. Hayek reminds us that [competition is a discovery procedure](http://marginalrevolution.com?action=user_content_redirect&uuid=f62faa05fae978a41cf6440385db1bc0c5d6bba68185df47ad8bc51d956aed7d&blog_id=42693868&post_id=90852&user_id=262258391&subs_id=225460721&signature=f49dd1f450f02cdfb0bbdb526649304f&email_name=new-post&user_email=ishan.mail@gmail.com&encoded_url=aHR0cHM6Ly9jZG4ubWlzZXMub3JnL3FqYWU1XzNfMy5wZGY&email_id=3395817706c4d62e09a23aa5d5315015). Like science, markets generate knowledge by experiment—hypotheses are posted as prices, and the public accepts or rejects them through revealed preference. Fred Smith’s FedEx plan got a “C” in the classroom, but the market graded the experiment and returned an A in equity. Theory is great, but just as in science, there is no substitute for running the experiment.

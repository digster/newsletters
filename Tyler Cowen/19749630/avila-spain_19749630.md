[The town](http://marginalrevolution.com?action=user_content_redirect&uuid=36e222a6dd501e952c0a82f23ec2e54a4fa4ec849b53795026678814408ea8fb&blog_id=42693868&post_id=90824&user_id=262258391&subs_id=225460721&signature=429340edb0f00f750499617f209f188e&email_name=new-post&user_email=ishan.mail@gmail.com&encoded_url=aHR0cHM6Ly9lbi53aWtpcGVkaWEub3JnL3dpa2kvJUMzJTgxdmlsYQ=&email_id=f7aac27a7d856a0c95299b7dc4ac6630) has amazing, quite intact walls from the 11th-14th centuries, and also three (!) of the most beautiful churches in Spain. It is only about ninety minutes from Madrid, yet I have not seen North American tourists here.
This morning it struck me to see a large number of Avila children reenacting the "lucha entre los christianos y los moros" [fight between the Christians and Moors] with toy swords and costumes, some of them dressed up like Saudis in their full garb. This made an impression on me because the Mexican village [I used to visit](http://marginalrevolution.com?action=user_content_redirect&uuid=2ea06d9e6b01b43cc8e4dc36e4178cc9356a138d96b62bbcc560ec68e2b55c15&blog_id=42693868&post_id=90824&user_id=262258391&subs_id=225460721&signature=db3f802798475a64df256be214d5774d&email_name=new-post&user_email=ishan.mail@gmail.com&encoded_url=aHR0cHM6Ly9tYXJnaW5hbHJldm9sdXRpb24uY29tL21hcmdpbmFscmV2b2x1dGlvbi8yMDI1LzA2L215LWRheXMtY29sbGVjdGluZy1tZXhpY2FuLWFydC1wYXJ0LWlpLmh0bWw&email_id=f7aac27a7d856a0c95299b7dc4ac6630), San Agustin Oapan, has a very similar fiesta, and here is [the history of how the fiesta was transmitted](http://marginalrevolution.com?action=user_content_redirect&uuid=63782891bdaf5fa0a3760a79e33183af85f1febdc5cc58a150b41fa1b382c9bf&blog_id=42693868&post_id=90824&user_id=262258391&subs_id=225460721&signature=14853645bfbf7a1a41662064fb127185&email_name=new-post&user_email=ishan.mail@gmail.com&encoded_url=aHR0cHM6Ly9jaGF0Z3B0LmNvbS9jLzY4NDMyNDA1LTQzMDAtODAxMC04MzgzLWFjMDY4N2MzOWIwZQ=&email_id=f7aac27a7d856a0c95299b7dc4ac6630), dating back to the 16th century. Even the dances and toy swords felt familiar to me. How many of them in Oapan even know what "the moros" are? I recall during my second visit to Oapan I was shocked to learn they did not know what China was, or that there was a Pope, even though they were Catholic. That all changed rapidly with the later arrival of satellite television of course.
In any case, Avila, along with the nearby [Roman aquaducts of Segovia](http://marginalrevolution.com?action=user_content_redirect&uuid=d580567da440ba61f878a742492d8a0594b71c57ecab931679567cf865cc7974&blog_id=42693868&post_id=90824&user_id=262258391&subs_id=225460721&signature=22d54d7faddeadaf62ee950a0b96a2ef&email_name=new-post&user_email=ishan.mail@gmail.com&encoded_url=aHR0cHM6Ly9lbi53aWtpcGVkaWEub3JnL3dpa2kvQXF1ZWR1Y3Rfb2ZfU2Vnb3ZpYQ=&email_id=f7aac27a7d856a0c95299b7dc4ac6630), is a much underrated visit, underrated at least in North America.

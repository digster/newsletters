Post       : The Sputnik vs. DeepSeek Moment: Why the Difference?
URL        : https://marginalrevolution.com/marginalrevolution/2025/07/the-sputnik-vs-deepseek-moment-why-the-difference.html
Posted     : July 11, 2025 at 7:18 am
Author     : Alex Tabarrok
Categories : Current Affairs, Economics, Education, History

In 1957, the Soviet Union launched Sputnik triggering a national reckoning in the United States. Americans questioned the strength of their education system, scientific capabilities, industrial base—even their national character. The country’s self-image as a global leader was shaken, creating the Sputnik moment. ( https://www.nsf.gov/about/history/narrative )

The response was swift and ambitious. NSF funding ( https://www.nsf.gov/about/history/narrative )  tripled in a year and increased by a factor of more than ten by the end of the decade. The National Defense Education Act ( https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Defense_Education_Act )  overhauled universities and created new student loan programs for foreign language students and engineers. High schools redesigned curricula ( https://www.nytimes.com/1961/11/17/archives/survey-finds-sputnik-spurred-state-schools-to-speed-reforms.html?searchResultPosition=1 )  around the "new math." Homework doubled ( https://www.history.com/articles/homework-cold-war-sputnik ) . NASA and ARPA (later DARPA) were created in 1958. NASA’s budget rocketed upwards to nearly 5% of all federal spending and R&D spending overall increased to well over 10% of federal spending ( https://www.aaas.org/sites/default/files/2022-09/Budget_Federal.png ) . Immigration rules were liberalized ( https://www.lbjlibrary.org/news-and-press/media-kits/immigration-and-nationality-act )  (perhaps not in direct response to Sputnik but as part of the ethos of the time). Foreign talent was attracted.

The U.S. answered Sputnik with bold competition not an aggrieved whine that America had been ripped off and abused.

America’s response to rising scientific competition from China—symbolized by DeepSeek’s R1 matching OpenAI’s o1—has been very different. The DeepSeek Moment has been met not with resolve and competition but with anxiety and retreat.
Trump has proposed slashing the NIH budget by nearly 40% ( https://www.statnews.com/2025/05/30/nih-cuts-new-details-18-billion-budget-reduction-outlined-in-congressional-budget-justification/ )  and NSF by 56% ( https://www.theguardian.com/us-news/2025/jul/03/national-science-foundation-trump-cuts ) . The universities have been attacked, creating chaos for scientific funding. International collaboration is being strangled by red tape. Foreign scientists are leaving or staying away.

Some of this is new and some of it is an acceleration of already existing trends. In Launching the Innovation Renaissance ( https://marginalrevolution.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/09/Launching-The-Innovation-Renaissance.pdf ) , for example, I said that by the Federal budget numbers, America is a warfare-welfare state not an innovation state. However, to be fair, there are some bright spots. Market‑driven research might partially offset public cuts. Big‑tech R&D now exceeds $200 billion annually ( https://www.trendlinehq.com/p/big-techs-big-r-and-d-bill ) —more than the entire federal government spending on R&D ( https://mattsclancy.substack.com/p/frequently-asked-questions-about ) . Not everything we did post-Sputnik was wise nor is everything we are doing today foolish.

Nevertheless, the contrast is stark: Sputnik spurred investment and ambition. America doubled down. ( https://marginalrevolution.com/marginalrevolution/2023/07/happy-july-4th.html )  DeepSeek has sparked defensiveness and retreat. We appear to be folding. 

Question of the hour. Why has America responded so differently to similar challenges? Can understanding that pivot help to reverse it? Show your work.

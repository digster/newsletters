Post       : What I&#8217;ve been reading

Posted     : February 27, 2025 at 1:05 am
Author     : Tyler Cowen
Categories : Books, Uncategorized

Alain Mabanckou, Dealing with the Dead ( https://www.amazon.com/Dealing-Dead-Alain-Mabanckou/dp/1800817703/ref=sr_1_1?crid=8AHZF2K5FK43&dib=eyJ2IjoiMSJ9.t90tpqDFoG6Ym8ZC8svA-Tyc1m7Qx-KBd1uIt7A17RSwhklyhzR5mLf5uFo8bRnWJUtZkduRTq1Vcbvj9zgXSHymw5m18B7ZR63ZLM-aKWgMNI_W-MAL-aLlMw94R78SSOEzG7bzmk9QeAPOnEUVjCLH2gu6trtkigVEssbI569JpY9trcJmeHXfztsBI4G6OEy05h_2aBYqTuvBjl4ay-Opw3XYLmpYbuMe1ZLGgFo.2HBgE_8J8q5ElRWjapz_IFxJyATZA-LEq_UVHUO8UqU&dib_tag=se&keywords=Dealing+with+the+Dead&qid=1740611764&sprefix=dealing+with+the+dead%2Caps%2C68&sr=8-1 ) .  Most African fiction does not connect with me, and there is a tendency for the reviews to be untrustworthy.  This "cemetery memoir," from the Congo (via UCLA), connected with me and held my interest throughout.

Philip Zaleski and Carol Zaleski, The Fellowship: The Literary Lives of the Inklings: J.R.R. Tolkien, C.S. Lewis, Owen Barfield, Charles Williams ( https://www.amazon.com/Fellowship-Literary-Inklings-Barfield-Williams/dp/0374536252/ref=sr_1_1?crid=3E1SRTBGVUJVB&dib=eyJ2IjoiMSJ9.yCA9-GquZk3zyWEV0t7P0g.UhKdlnydff5S-L3uZykKUWqXK7fq9H0J0eqMXjKJAds&dib_tag=se&keywords=The+Fellowship%3A+The+Literary+Lives+of+the+Inklings%3A+J.R.R.+Tolkien%2C+C.S.+Lewis%2C+Owen+Barfield%2C+Charles+Williams&qid=1740611891&sprefix=the+fellowship+the+literary+lives+of+the+inklings+j.r.r.+tolkien%2C+c.s.+lewis%2C+owen+barfield%2C+charles+williams%2Caps%2C129&sr=8-1 ) .  I was in the mood of thinking I don't need to read another book about these people.  Yet this one was so good it won me over nonetheless.

Eddie Huffman, Doc Watson: A Life in Music ( https://www.amazon.com/Doc-Watson-Music-American-Roots/dp/1469680351/ref=sr_1_1?crid=3R6HG5F26F5NK&dib=eyJ2IjoiMSJ9.VBCN912LF7e3vxokqTEqax1g4UZVDFuZbHbwn7z9gcyrXgR0WkYvJDBwscBzDYckG84fj7vayiunuk9PVD4zAPbJgpCLOtGjrkn5BScDZrbLK7URO-dFRfAKSdSzZsgQ8EouRPXXKYcJvqRwi3n8aqmyD1w0_eAc6uq_XnjydlKMpZjXMkLKSrUwnq3X3WIKRXbu1ED6uFv2JIpoYSppN79qo2hqRJcByiejyEsdPB0.LNnorqvSkTLg-4RH_TrOf_yWp7Gin11iEpscz-3A2Wo&dib_tag=se&keywords=doc+watson+a+life+in+music&qid=1740611931&sprefix=doc+watson+a+life%2Caps%2C73&sr=8-1 ) .  A fun book about one of America's greatest guitarists ( https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6VAbrnjdtYw ) .  Watson was blind from an early age, and he was collecting state disability benefits until he was 40 -- a classic late bloomer.

Philip Freeman, In the Brewing Luminous: The Life and Music of Cecil Taylor ( https://www.amazon.com/Brewing-Luminous-Music-Cecil-Taylor/dp/3955932613/ref=sr_1_1?crid=1WLEHPUOJVOR0&dib=eyJ2IjoiMSJ9.o3Gqi2eM5JRdYcA4oJkx_g.6d5T_WE3KPkKIAJ3Bm4ipd5Fd67KOU6DLCr2OCmCVbM&dib_tag=se&keywords=In+the+Brewing+Luminous%3A+The+Life+and+Music+of+Cecil+Taylor&qid=1740612069&sprefix=in+the+brewing+luminous+the+life+and+music+of+cecil+taylor%2Caps%2C103&sr=8-1 ) .  Call me crazy, but I think Sun Ra and Taylor are better and more important musically than say Duke Ellington.  Freeman's book is the first full-length biography of Taylor, and it is well-informed and properly appreciative.  It induced me to buy another book by him.  The evening I saw Taylor was one of the greatest of my life, I thank my mother for coming with me.

Carlos M.N. Eire, They Flew: A History of the Impossible ( https://www.amazon.com/They-Flew-Impossible-Carlos-Eire/dp/0300259808/ref=sr_1_1?crid=X21GZWU9LAS6&dib=eyJ2IjoiMSJ9._HoKRs7LfZROiOfRpz5vGw.iJqv1dUIL3anIfuADe406ZUdJEfz3RO37uHhWsLBK50&dib_tag=se&keywords=They+Flew%3A+A+History+of+the+Impossible&qid=1740612111&sprefix=they+flew+a+history+of+the+impossible%2Caps%2C76&sr=8-1 ) .  Ross Douthat recommended this one to me.  It is well done, and worth reading, but I don't find it shifted my priors on whether "impossible" events might have really happened.

I agree with the central arguments of Samir Varma's The Science of Free Will: How Determinism Affects Everything from the Future of AI to Traffic to God to Bees ( https://www.amazon.com/Science-Free-Will-Determinism-Everything/dp/1803417315 ) .  I was happy to write a foreword for the book.

Kathleen deLaski, Who Needs College Anymore? Imagining a Future Where Degrees Don't Matter ( https://www.amazon.com/Who-Needs-College-Anymore-Imagining/dp/1682539520 ) .  One of a growing chorus of books suggesting higher education is on the verge of some radical changes.

There is Daniel Brook, The Einstein of Sex: Dr. Magnus Hirschfeld Visionary of Weimar Berlin ( https://www.amazon.com/Einstein-Sex-Magnus-Hirschfeld-Visionary/dp/1324007249/ref=sr_1_1?crid=2V6JQM88CJEKM&dib=eyJ2IjoiMSJ9.QLLohB_pvDrmg-I1BuKKiw.GSkFkH9zjKx0L1rWGgqW6H97cOZKEl6Dhf0lKlbZVh8&dib_tag=se&keywords=The+Einstein+of+Sex%3A+Dr.+Magnus+Hirschfeld+Visionary+of+Weimar+Berlin&qid=1740614730&s=books&sprefix=the+einstein+of+sex+dr.+magnus+hirschfeld+visionary+of+weimar+berlin%2Cstripbooks%2C151&sr=1-1 ) .  It is good to see him getting more attention.

There is also Brandy Schillace, The Intermediaries: A Weimar Story ( https://www.amazon.com/Intermediaries-Weimar-Story-Brandy-Schillace/dp/1324036311/ref=sr_1_1?crid=1N1DZMAD4XLWU&dib=eyJ2IjoiMSJ9.WfRiWc0Pq6ctppwhAR3pFg.fbfCoNlyZCJ7sqrJtUbDesg6dEtlB8kxnUBURH6qCto&dib_tag=se&keywords=The+Intermediaries%3A+A+Weimar+Story&qid=1740614765&s=books&sprefix=the+intermediaries+a+weimar+story%2Cstripbooks%2C63&sr=1-1 ) .

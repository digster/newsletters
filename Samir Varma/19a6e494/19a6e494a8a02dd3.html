Cricket in Morningside Heights

Or, How I Tried to Teach Columbia What the Rest of the World Already Knew

Samir Varma
Nov 10
 
READ IN APP
 

The Tree in Front of the English Department

Picture this: It’s September 1984. I’m 18 years old, standing on the lawn in front of Columbia University’s English Department with a red leather cricket ball, a bat, and absolutely zero company. There’s a tree. It’s going to be the stumps.

I’m probably the first person to play cricket at Columbia in about seventy years.

The irony would take me another three years to fully appreciate.

Within a week, I wasn’t alone anymore. Word spread through the international student grapevine with the efficiency of insider trading. Indians materialized. Then Pakistanis. Then Brits. Then—this being 1985, peak anti-apartheid protests—some very shy South Africans who genuinely seemed worried we’d throw them off the lawn.

“Of course you can play,” we told them.

They didn’t believe us.

We had to practically drag them onto the field. These were nice guys. Clearly not the problem. But this was Columbia in the mid-80s. Protests kept everyone up at night. Shantytown was going up on the quad. Trustees were getting shouted at. And here were these South African guys who just wanted to play cricket and were terrified someone would yell at them for existing.

Once they realized we weren’t going to chase them away, they became regulars. Good cricketers, too.

The tree remained the stumps. The windows of the English Department had these heavy iron bar cages on them—prison chic meets Ivy League—so there was zero chance of actually breaking anything. We used a real cricket ball. Red leather. Hard as a rock. The kind that leaves bruises.

And the West Indian security guards?

Evidently, you cannot be West Indian without being cool. It’s some kind of biological law. These guys would stop during their rounds, watch for a bit, occasionally join in to demonstrate proper technique to the hopelessly confused Americans who kept showing up. “Rasta mon, rasta!” They’d laugh at the football players trying to keep the bat on the ground.

Air-Conditioned Camels and Other Truths About India

One day, a West Indian guy asked me out of the blue: “Do you have lemon tarts in India?”

“Er, yes, we do. Why?”

“Well, how do they make them?”

“What do you mean how? You bake them.”

“But how do they get the materials to the bakery?”

I was confused. “What do you mean?”

“Well, how do you transport them? Do you have cars?”

Yes, India was madly socialist in the 1980s. But this was insane. So I said, deadpan: “No. We have camels.”

“Oh! Isn’t it hot in India?”

“Yes, it’s very hot most of the time.”

“But doesn’t the butter melt?”

“No,” I said. “The camels are air-conditioned.”

He paused. “How do you air-condition a camel?”

“You put a tent on its back and use solar panels to run the air-conditioner.”

Long pause.

“You’re pulling my leg!”

“Finally,” I said, “the penny drops.”

Which brings us to the football team.

“Kill the Indian” and the 0-44 Record

I lived in Carman Hall, 4th floor. My roommates were on the Columbia football team. Offensive linemen. Wide receivers. A quarterback. Big, athletic guys with 3.7 GPAs who I used to tease studied too much to be good at football.

They had this game they played in the hallways called “Kill the Indian.” No offense meant, none taken—this was before idiotic political correctness. It was basically a cross between tackle football, flag football, and rugby, played at full speed in a residential hallway.

I figured if they could handle that, they could learn cricket.

I was wrong.

We’d play in the hallways after dinner. I’d bowl at them. They’d try to hit. But there was one insurmountable problem: They could not—absolutely could not—grasp the concept of keeping the bat on the ground.

They’d hold it up in the air, like a baseball bat. Which meant I could bowl them under the bat every single time. Then they’d get frustrated and say things like, “Man, this is the REAL DEAL. You have to play this since birth to be good at it!”

One guy—a really sweet giant offensive lineman—kept saying “real deal” every time he got bowled. Never figured it out. None of them did.

They couldn’t understand:
- Why you keep the bat on the ground.
- Why you don’t hit the ball in the air.
- Why you can score runs anywhere—in front, behind, to the side.
- Why you don’t have to run after you hit the ball.
- Why you don’t drop the bat after you hit the ball.

Alien concepts. All of them.

A couple of these guys actually asked me to come kick for the Columbia football team. I politely declined. What’s the point if all you’re going to do is lose?

Columbia football went 0-44 during my four years there. Still a Division I all-time record. I’m not saying cricket training ruined Columbia football. But I’m also not NOT saying it.

Watching elite athletes be catastrophically bad at something is funny. They really don’t like it.

The American Amnesia

Here’s what I discovered in the Butler Library stacks sometime around 1987:

Americans were among the earliest adopters of cricket outside England.

Along with Canada. By a lot. They played it at the highest level. They even helped develop some of its most important techniques. And then they completely forgot about it.

Let me give you the facts that essentially zero Americans know:

  1. George Washington played cricket. He called it “wickets” in his diary. The Father of Our Country. Cricket player.

  2. The first international sporting fixture in human history was USA vs. Canada. In cricket. September 24-26, 1844. At St. George’s Cricket Club in Manhattan, where 30th Street and Broadway now intersect. Canada won by 23 runs. There were 5,000-20,000 spectators. An estimated $100,000-$120,000 worth of bets. This was 33 years before the first Ashes match.

  3. Until roughly 1915, cricket was more popular than baseball in America. The New York Times reported cricket scores. Regularly. With enthusiasm.

  4. Swing bowling—one of cricket’s most fundamental techniques—was invented by an American from Philadelphia. Bart King. He called it the “angler.” He developed it from his baseball pitching experience. CB Fry, one of England’s greatest cricketers, called King’s swing bowling “the best swerver I ever saw in my life.” Don Bradman—the greatest batsman who ever lived—called him “America’s greatest cricketing son.”

  5. Philadelphia cricket clubs are among the world’s oldest. The Gentlemen of Philadelphia toured England and beat county teams. Regularly.

When I’d tell Americans this, they were incredulous. So I’d drag them to the Butler Library and show them old New York Times issues with cricket scores on the sports pages.

Their minds would explode.

But Here’s Where It Gets Interesting

Why did Americans forget cricket?

Economics. And British snobbery.

See, in 1909, the Imperial Cricket Conference was formed. Guess who they refused to admit? The United States. Why? Because America wasn’t part of the British Empire.

Think about that. The organization that controlled international cricket—literally named the “Imperial” Cricket Conference—told Americans they couldn’t join the club because Americans had won their independence.

So Americans said: Screw it. We’ll play baseball.

Which—and this is my favorite part—was also originally a British sport. Rounders. Developed by an Englishman.

Meanwhile, India—also told they weren’t quite good enough for the “Imperial” Cricket Conference, also patronized by the British—decided to stick it out. They kept playing. They kept enduring the indignities.

Both baseball and cricket are British sports. Americans abandoned one to create their own version of the other. India stuck with cricket and eventually became so economically powerful they now control the entire sport.

Milton Friedman would have loved this. Market forces finding paths around monopolistic behavior. Americans chose exit. Indians chose voice. Both strategies worked. It just took India a bit longer.

The Deans Were Very Confused

Deans and provosts would walk by during our games. They’d stop. Stare. And eventually ask: “What is this bizarre game?”

We’d explain. In detail. They’d watch for a while, shake their heads, and walk away still confused.

A couple of English professors from the department came by more than once. They got it—or at least they’d seen it in England. But they were just as baffled watching Americans try to play.

The questions Americans would ask:

  • “What’s the point?”

  • “Why aren’t you hitting it hard?”

  • “Why don’t you run every time?”

  • “What do you mean the bat has to touch the ground?”

  • “Can’t you just... hit it over there?” (pointing at a fence 200 feet away)

I’d answer patiently. They’d nod. They’d try to play. They’d fail spectacularly.

None of them—not one—ever figured out how to keep the bat on the ground and hit along the ground instead of in the air.

Americans in general were actually quite open to this weird game. They just couldn’t get the hang of it. At all.

The Parallel Transformations

While I was teaching cricket to Americans at an Ivy League school—Americans who had early adopted and then completely forgotten the sport—something else was happening 6,500 miles away.

India was going cricket-insane.

I’d get letters from home. My father was a top sports commentator. He’d write about what was happening. Or my mother would. Or family friends. This was pre-internet, remember. This was Manhattan in the mid-80s. If you wanted to watch the 1987 World Cup—co-hosted by India and Pakistan—you had to go to Queens and watch it in the middle of the night.

Manhattanites generally don’t go to Queens and sure as hell not in the middle of the night in the late 1980s!

So I’d get the news later. Much later. Letters would arrive: “You wouldn’t believe what’s happening here. Cricket is everywhere. Every wall is a stump. Every stick is a bat. Television is exploding. Stadiums are packed. It’s madness.”

The 1987 World Cup wasn’t just in India and Pakistan. It was the ultimate post-colonial moment. England versus Australia. Playing IN INDIA. Think about that. The colonizers playing their sport in the colonized country, which was now hosting the tournament and charging them for the privilege.

By the time I graduated in 1988, India had completely transformed its relationship with cricket. I’d missed the whole thing. I had watched it from 6,500 miles away through delayed letters and phone calls that cost a fortune.

The feeling was surreal. Alienating. Exciting. Frustrating. Like watching history happen through a keyhole.

I was teaching Americans a game that they were early adopters of and had forgotten. And on the other side of the world, India was learning to own cricket before anyone could do to it what the FIH did to hockey. One of us succeeded.

The View from 59

I’m 59 now.

And I’m watching English and Australian cricketers say—on camera, with genuine emotion—that their childhood dream was to play in the IPL.

Let me repeat that.

The IPL. The Indian Premier League. A domestic Indian competition.

English players. Australian players. [And let’s not even mention the Bangladeshis, Sri Lankans, South Africans, West Indians, New Zealanders, etc.] Dreaming as kids of playing in India. In India! I am incredulous, still, typing that. And yet, it is true! The IPL pays them 5 to 100 times more than they can make anywhere else in the world.

For a 59-year-old Indian who remembers when EAS Prasanna needed an engineering degree because cricket paid ₹300 a month, this is beyond surreal.

It’s like being an alien from Jupiter.

When I was 18, playing cricket at Columbia, I had to explain to American deans what the sport was. I had to show Americans in the Butler Library stacks that George Washington played cricket. I had to demonstrate to football players how to keep the bat on the ground (unsuccessfully). I had to tell South Africans they were welcome on our cricket field even though the campus was exploding with anti-apartheid protests.

And the West Indian security guards would laugh at all of us and occasionally show the Americans how it’s really done.

Meanwhile, back in India, something much bigger was happening. Television was spreading. Markets were discovering value. The 1987 World Cup was being played in India. Cricket was transforming from a gentleman’s game into something that would eventually become an $18.5 billion industry.

I was teaching cricket to Americans on Ivy League lawns while India was learning to use cricket to teach the world about India.

I kept telling anyone who would listen about the USA vs. Canada match in 1844. About Bart King’s swing bowling. About how Americans forgot their own cricket history. About the Imperial Cricket Conference’s snub.

The Americans were polite. Then they’d try to hit the ball in the air again.

The Indians and Pakistanis and Brits and South Africans just kept playing. We had a good thing going on that lawn. The tree in front of the English Department. The West Indian security guards. The bewildered deans. The hopelessly confused football team with their 0-44 record.

And somewhere in the background, invisible to all of us, the invisible hand was working. Markets were finding cricket. India was figuring out that if you get rich enough, fast enough, you can own the sport instead of just playing it. The BCCI was learning the hockey lesson: Control the governance before they change the rules.

By the time I left Columbia in 1988, two things were clear:

Americans had moved on from cricket a century ago and weren’t coming back.

Indians had just started a journey that would end with English and Australian kids dreaming of playing in Mumbai.

The Things We Have in Common

Here’s what nobody talks about: The US and India have more in common than either country thinks.

One of the very biggest things they have in common? They both adopted their colonizer’s sport and made themselves the best in the world at it.

There is no baseball without the USA. There is no cricket without India.

If you want to play in the best baseball competition in the world, you have to come to America and play in the MLB—a domestic American competition. Every kid in the Dominican Republic, Japan, Venezuela, Cuba and so on, dreams of playing in the American major leagues. Not because Americans invented baseball. But because Americans became so dominant that MLB is where the best players in the world compete.

And if you want to play in the best cricket competition in the world—I’m sorry Brits and Australians, but your “Ashes” are now very distinctly lower-skill cricket—you have to come to India and play in the IPL, a domestic Indian competition. Every kid in England and Australia now dreams of playing in the Indian Premier League. Not because Indians invented cricket. But because Indians became so economically powerful that the IPL is where the best cricketers in the world compete.

It’s same pattern. It’s the same outcome. But with different sports.

The Americans chose exit—they told the Imperial Cricket Conference to stuff it, and went and dominated baseball instead.

The Indians chose voice—they stuck it out through the various humiliations, got rich, and bought the sport. As has been said, “cricket is an Indian sport, accidentally discovered by the British”. [And thus, baseball is an American sport accidentally discovered by the British.].

Both strategies worked. Milton Friedman would be proud. Markets find a way.

The tree in front of the English Department is still there, I assume. It probably still has no idea it was the stumps in the greatest parallel transformation in cricket history. When an Indian at an Ivy League school tried to teach Americans what was their own sport, while the Indians who stuck it out through Imperial Cricket Conference humiliation were turning cricket into India’s gift to the world.

And the West Indian security guards? Still the coolest people in the whole story. They always knew how to keep the bat on the ground.

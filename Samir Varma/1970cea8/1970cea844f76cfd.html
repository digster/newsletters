The Allergy That Changed Physics

Heisenberg's Quantum Breakthrough on a Remote Island

Samir Varma
May 26
 
READ IN APP
 

Next month, physicists from around the world will gather on the tiny island of Helgoland for a special conferencerunning June 9-14, 2025, marking exactly 100 years since Werner Heisenberg's revolutionary trip to this same remote outcrop. What they're celebrating is one of the most improbable origin stories in the history of science—how hay fever and a young man's academic near-disaster led to the birth of quantum mechanics.

The Boy Who Almost Failed Physics

Let's start with a delicious bit of irony. Werner Heisenberg, the future Nobel Prize winner who would fundamentally reshape our understanding of reality, almost failed his PhD oral examination because he had virtually no interest in experimental physics.

Picture this: July 23, 1923. A 21-year-old Heisenberg sits before four intimidating professors at Munich University. He breezes through Sommerfeld's theoretical questions and handles mathematics with ease, but when experimental physicist Wilhelm Wien starts asking about laboratory apparatus, everything falls apart.

Wien asks about the Fabry-Perot interferometer—a device Heisenberg was supposed to have studied extensively in his lab work. Heisenberg has no idea how to derive its resolving power. Then Wien asks about telescopes. Again, the young genius is completely stumped. Wien is so angry that he wants to fail Heisenberg on the spot. Only Arnold Sommerfeld's fierce defense of his student saves the day, arguing that Heisenberg's theoretical brilliance outweighs his experimental ignorance.

That evening, while Sommerfeld throws a small party for the new “Dr. Heisenberg,” our hero excuses himself early, packs his bag, and takes the midnight train to Göttingen. The next morning, he shows up sheepishly in Max Born's office asking, “I wonder if you still want to have me.” Born, after reviewing the “rather tricky” questions Heisenberg had missed, lets his employment offer stand.

There's a beautiful postscript to this story: when Heisenberg later derived his famous uncertainty relations, he had to use the resolving power of microscopes—and he still struggled with it! When Bohr pointed out errors in his reasoning, it led to more emotional difficulties for our hapless hero.

An Unfortunate Personal Setback

Fast forward to spring 1925. Heisenberg is now a postdoc at Göttingen, wrestling with one of physics' most perplexing problems: how to make mathematical sense of atomic behavior. He's “bogged down in a morass of mathematics” when, late in May 1925, disaster strikes—“an attack of hay fever so severe that he had to ask his professor, Max Born for a fortnight's leave of absence to recover”.

This wasn't just sniffles and watery eyes. His eyes swelled shut from the pollen allergies. The 23-year-old arrived at work “coughing and sneezing, and with such a swollen face that his landlady decided he had been in a fight”.

On June 7, 1925, he went straight to the rocky island of Helgoland, far from any sources of pollen. Why Helgoland? The tiny archipelago in the North Sea was known to be treeless and wind-battered—essentially pollen-free. It was the 1920s equivalent of seeking refuge in a clean room.

The Most Remote Place in Germany

Even today, Helgoland feels like the edge of the world. The ferry from Hamburg takes 3 hours and 45 minutes—an epic journey across the North Sea to Germany's only high-seas island. From the mainland, you spend over an hour crossing open North Sea waters to reach this isolated red cliff rising 61 meters out of the waves.

Most importantly for Heisenberg, it had fresh winds and was remote from allergen producers. The island was just starting to recover its reputation as a fishing center and tourist destination after being evacuated during World War I when Germany turned it into a military base.

Picture young Heisenberg stepping off the boat onto this otherworldly island: red sandstone cliffs, very few people, crystal-clear air, and almost nobody to talk to—perfect for walking, swimming in cool water, and clearing both his nasal passages and mental cobwebs. His landlady installed him in a quiet room on the second floor of her Gasthaus that overlooked the beach and the North Sea.

The Night That Changed Everything

What happened next reads like a fever dream—which, given Heisenberg's condition, it might literally have been. At 3 a.m. one morning in June 1925, an exhausted, allergy-ridden 23-year-old climbed a rock at the edge of the island. He had been wrestling with the mathematics of atomic oscillators, watching “single numbers grotesquely multiply into entire lists of numbers” every time he tried to solve the equations.

But freed from his hay fever, something clicked. Heisenberg deliberately abandoned the classical picture of particles and orbits, and took a long, hard look at the mathematics that describes the associations between pairs of quantum states, without asking himself how the quantum entity gets from state A to state B.

Heisenberg had just cobbled together, in crude and unfamiliar mathematics, a framework that would shortly become what we know as “matrix mechanics”. He was working with strange multiplication tables where the order of multiplication mattered—a bizarre property that would soon revolutionize physics.

The Punchline

Here's the beautiful irony: Back in Göttingen, Born realized immediately what Heisenberg had discovered. Unlike Heisenberg, Born already knew about matrices from his mathematical studies twenty years earlier. The one thing that sticks in the mind of anyone who has studied matrices is that they do not commute—exactly the strange property Heisenberg had discovered!

Heisenberg wrote that he was unsure whether to publish the method or “throw it into the flames.” Fortunately, after sending his work to Wolfgang Pauli, he chose the first option.

Full Circle

So next month, when leading physicists (which I am pleased to say include my daughter, who is now in her 5th year of a Physics PhD at Yale) gather on that same isolated island—taking the modern HSC “Halunder Jet” ferry that departs Hamburg at 9 AM and arrives at Helgoland at 12:45 PM—they'll be celebrating not just a scientific breakthrough, but one of the most beautifully human stories in physics.

A young man who nearly failed his PhD because he couldn't handle experimental equipment. An allergy attack so severe it forced him into exile on a remote island. And in that isolation, freed from the pollen and the pressure, a new understanding of reality itself.

If we insist on pegging the birth of quantum mechanics to a particular place and time, Helgoland in June 1925 it is. Sometimes the most profound discoveries come not from grand laboratories or ivory towers, but from the intersection of human frailty and mathematical beauty on a windswept rock in the North Sea.

The next time your allergies act up, remember: you might just be one creative leap away from revolutionizing physics. Though you should probably pack some tissues, just in case.

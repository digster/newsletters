Two Million People, 368 Jobs, and One Question About JavaScript

Or, How India Wastes 1.4% of GDP on Rent-Seeking Disguised as Meritocracy

Samir Varma
Nov 24
 
READ IN APP
 

It was 1982, and I was in 10th grade at The Modern School when I learned that if I wanted to attend an Indian university, I’d have to keep studying Hindi. Not just any Hindi—shuddh Hindi, the “pure” version manufactured by government commissars who apparently believe Sanskrit died of loneliness and Hindi should join it.

I’d been planning my escape from India for a while by then—the socialism was suffocating, the bureaucracy was maddening, and now this. The Hindi requirement was just another data point in the growing case for getting out.

But there was something particularly absurd about the shuddh Hindi requirement. Here was India’s post-colonial education system, supposedly freed from British domination, requiring students to learn a fake, bureaucratic language that nobody actually spoke. It was like we’d replaced the British Empire’s demand for classical education with our own homegrown version—artificial Hindi to prepare us for an artificial India, both pretending to be more “pure” than they actually were.

We were being trained to become Macaulay’s Children in two directions at once: anglicized enough to serve in bureaucratic systems modeled on Victorian England, but Sanskritized enough in our Hindi to prove we weren’t too anglicized. The result? Millions of young Indians fluent in languages nobody speaks, preparing for jobs in an empire that no longer exists.

I’m a physicist. Or was training to be one. Physics is about understanding universal laws, not memorizing arbitrary vocabularies. Rutherford—somewhat rudely but accurately—said there are two kinds of science: physics and stamp collecting. I don’t go quite that far, but that does capture the essential difference between understanding systems and memorizing facts.

Indian exams are stamp collecting raised to a pathological art form.

The next two years—11th and 12th grade—I got to drop Hindi and focus on the science track: physics, chemistry, mathematics. You’d think this would have been liberating. It wasn’t particularly glorious. The Indian education system still required memorization for everything. Even physics—physics!—got the rote learning treatment.

This is pedagogical malpractice. Physics is about understanding systems, not memorizing formulas. But there I was, cramming Newton’s laws in a particular notation, memorizing equations I should have been deriving. To this day, when I see Newton’s laws written in modern notation, my memory of them written the way they were in high school interrupts my understanding. I have to consciously stop myself from thinking of them in that old notation and translate the symbols. It’s like having a bad song stuck in your head, except it’s F=ma and it’s been there for forty years.

At least physics was somewhat interesting, even butchered by memorization. Chemistry? Biology? Those were pure stamp collecting. And I refused to play that game any longer than absolutely necessary.

By 1984, when it was time to apply to universities, the decision was clear:

Exeunt stage left.

I applied to Columbia. I got in. I got out of India in August 1984, just before Indira Gandhi was assassinated and the country exploded in anti-Sikh riots. Good timing, as it turned out, on multiple fronts.

But most Indians don’t have that option. And what I escaped in 1984 has metastasized into something far worse than I could have imagined: an entire parallel economy built around examinations that test nothing, prove nothing, and waste billions of dollars in a collective ritual of national self-harm.

Welcome to Musallahpur: Half a Million People Studying for Jobs That Don’t Exist

About 500 kilometers east of Delhi, in a suburb of Patna called Musallahpur Haat, roughly half a million young Indians are currently living in a peculiar sort of purgatory.

They’re not in college. They’re not working. They’re preparing for government job entrance exams. The streets are choked with students surging between coaching centers. Mountains of revision books and practice papers line the roads. The only events advertised are practice tests. Behind the main drag is a labyrinth of backstreets teeming with classrooms and libraries—though “libraries” is generous; they’re rooms with an internet connection and a desk where, for a few hundred rupees, you can sit under air conditioning for six hours studying.

The libraries are open 24 hours. Many students are there well past midnight, their faces flickering blue in the light of their phones as they watch tutorial videos.

There are almost no children in Musallahpur. Though many students live there for years—some for six, seven, even ten years—it isn’t somewhere to build a life. It’s somewhere to wait. And hope. And cram.

Here’s the beautiful irony: Musallahpur is in Bihar—India’s poorest state, its least educated, the punchline to every Indian development joke. Bihar is synonymous with backwardness. It’s where infrastructure goes to die. The state that other Indians look down on.

And it has become the epicenter of India’s exam-preparation industrial complex.

Half a million young people, many from Bihar itself, cramming in one of India’s most dysfunctional states to maybe, possibly, if they’re extraordinarily lucky, get a government job. It’s like discovering that America’s premier technology hub relocated to rural Mississippi. The absurdity is so perfect you couldn’t invent it.

Of course, the location also makes tragic sense: in a state where the private sector barely exists and basic services are a cruel joke, a government job isn’t just security—it’s the only path to the middle class.

According to reporting by The Economist in July 2025, these students are studying for jobs like railway ticket inspectors and assistant train drivers—positions that pay around ₹60,000 ($700) per month. The exams test “current affairs, logic, maths and science.” Sample questions include: “Who invented JavaScript?” “Which element is most abundant in the Earth’s crust?” “What is the smallest digit a such that the number a456 is divisible by 11?”

You know, the essential knowledge base for checking train tickets.

During the most recent railway recruitment drive, roughly 30 million people applied for 90,000 positions. That’s 333 applicants for every job. For some positions, like junior ticket collectors, the odds were 1,800 to one.

Put another way: if you gathered everyone competing for these jobs in one place, you’d have more people than live in the entire National Capital Territory of Delhi. And 99.7% of them will fail.

And yet they study. For years.

The Economics of National Self-Immolation

Alex Tabarrok at Marginal Revolution has documented the staggering waste this system creates. The problem isn’t just that government jobs are desirable—it’s that they’re absurdly overcompensated compared to equivalent private sector work.

Teachers in India’s public sector are paid at least five times more than in the private sector. When you account for lifetime tenure, generous perks, and “potentially remunerative possibilities for corruption” (economist-speak for bribes), a government job’s total value can be up to 10 times that of an equivalent private sector position.

The result? In one famous example, 2.3 million people submitted applications for 368 “office boy” positions in Uttar Pradesh. That’s 6,250 applications per job. For office boy positions.

If these jobs were allocated randomly, like a lottery, the social cost would be minimal. But they’re allocated by exam performance. Which means obtaining a government job requires an “investment” in exam preparation. Young people spend years—sometimes a decade—out of the workforce studying for exams that, for nearly all of them, will yield nothing.

In Tamil Nadu alone, between one and two million people apply annually for government jobs. Far less than 1% are hired. Despite the long odds, economist Kunal Mangal estimates that around 80% of the unemployed in Tamil Nadu are studying for government exams.

Here’s where it gets darkly hilarious from an economic perspective: classical rent-seeking theory predicts “full dissipation.” If a prize is worth a certain amount, rational individuals will collectively spend resources up to that amount attempting to win it. When the prize is a government job, the “spending” isn’t cash—it’s years of a young person’s productive life.

Mangal calculates that the total opportunity cost (time out of the workforce) that job applicants spend in Tamil Nadu is worth more than the combined lifetime salaries of the available jobs. Read that again. For every ₹100 the government spends on salaries, Indian society burns ₹168 in collective effort just to decide who gets them.

That’s not a typo. The opportunity cost of time spent preparing for jobs exceeds the lifetime value of the jobs themselves.

Tabarrok calculates that India wastes approximately 1.4% of GDP annually on this rent-seeking behavior. That’s more than India spends on healthcare (1.2% of GDP). It’s roughly what India spends on defense (1.5% of GDP).

Read that again: India invests more resources in deciding who should check train tickets than in defending its borders or healing its sick.

If you gathered everyone competing for railway jobs in one place, you’d have more people than in all of Delhi’s National Capital Territory. And 99.7% of them will fail. It’s like hosting the Olympics where 333 people compete for every medal, the competition lasts six years instead of two weeks, and at the end you get a job in an un-air-conditioned train cab with no toilet.

The country is quite literally investing more resources in deciding who should check train tickets than in defending its borders or healing its sick.

The ₹50,000 Crore Industrial Complex of Failure

Let’s talk about who’s actually winning in this system: the coaching centers.

India’s government exam preparation sector has exploded into a ₹50,000-58,000 crore ($6-7 billion) market projected to reach ₹1.5 lakh crore ($18 billion) by 2030. To put that in perspective: India’s entire space program—the one that just landed on the moon—has an annual budget of about ₹13,000 crore. We’re spending four times more teaching people who invented JavaScript than we spend on actual rocket science.

Physics Wallah—yes, that’s a real name—is valued at $2.8 billion and filed for a $530 million IPO in March 2025. The company serves 5.5 million paying subscribers and doubled revenue from $96 million to $240 million in one year. Their affordable courses cost ₹3,600 annually, which sounds reasonable until you realize students are paying this for six years straight while not earning anything.

Meanwhile, Unacademy—once the darling of ed-tech at $3.44 billion valuation in March 2022—crashed 90% to $300-400 million in acquisition talks by 2025. That’s a faster collapse than most exam aspirants’ dreams, which is saying something.

Khan Sir, whose YouTube empire we’ll get to, reportedly rejected a ₹107 crore ($13 million) buyout offer from an ed-tech company because he wanted to keep his coaching affordable. This is either admirable or insane, possibly both, which makes him quintessentially Indian.

Here’s the perverse economic logic: The unemployed, by remaining unemployed while preparing, create employment for coaching teachers, lodge owners, bookstore operators, and food vendors. It’s an entire parallel economy sustained by aspirant desperation. As one investigation noted: “The deeper the crisis of jobs, the more the returns of this sector.”

India has created a self-sustaining economic ecosystem where the absence of productive work generates lucrative work for others. It’s like discovering that the most profitable business in a gold rush town isn’t mining—it’s selling shovels to people who will never find gold.

A Brief History Lesson About Kings and Roads

I figured out how to hack this system once, briefly, in 7th and 8th grade. The subject was Indian history—one of the mandatory subjects requiring memorization.

I noticed something: every king did exactly the same things. Built roads, dams, bridges. Passed laws. Conducted wars. The usual autocratic to-do list. The only differences were the slight deviations that made them “interesting”: Ashoka renounced war and became Buddhist. Akbar was tolerant of other religions. Aurangzeb wasn’t. Learn those quirks, and you could reconstruct everything else from the template.

King X built roads. King X passed laws. King X fought wars with neighboring King Y. Unless he was Ashoka, in which case he fought wars, felt bad, and became Buddhist. Add the dates, and boom—you sound like a history genius.

I regularly scored in the high 90s. My parents assumed I’d “worked hard.” My grandfather was an eminent ancient Indian historian, so they figured I must have inherited his scholarly inclinations.

No, man. I’d just hacked the bloody system. Pattern recognition, not knowledge.

But here’s the thing: you couldn’t hack the other subjects. Physics required understanding, not memorization—which is why I loved it. But chemistry? Biology? Those got the stamp-collecting treatment too. And I refused to play that game.

The Indian exam system rewards exactly the wrong cognitive skills. It punishes understanding and rewards rote memorization. It’s designed not to educate but to sort—and it’s a brutally inefficient sorting mechanism that’s grinding millions of young lives into dust.

Neeraj Kumar’s Six-Year Wait

The Economist profiled a 23-year-old named Neeraj Kumar who, in the summer of 2019, boarded a sleeper train from Delhi to Patna. He couldn’t afford a berth, so he planned to sleep on the floor during the 16-hour journey. He didn’t mind the discomfort. He was on his way to the middle class.

Or so he hoped.

Kumar had grown up in a village east of Patna. His family were poor, lower-caste farmers. The village school was so basic that children sat on fertilizer sacks instead of chairs. But Kumar was bright. He wanted to become an engineer like his older cousin. In 2015 he won a place at a government engineering college in Rajasthan.

After graduating, he moved to Delhi to take the civil service exam—the pathway to becoming a government engineer. For a while his father sent money for food and rent. Then his sister got engaged and the payments stopped. Indian weddings are expensive, and the family couldn’t support both children’s futures.

Kumar reconsidered his strategy. He’d heard the Ministry of Railways had far more jobs available than the civil service. Being an assistant train driver wasn’t his dream, but it was a proper job, and seemed achievable.

He applied in 2018 but bungled the paperwork. A friend suggested he wait for the next round of exams in Musallahpur, where coaching centers were concentrated and rent was cheap.

It was monsoon season when his train pulled into Patna Junction. Rain poured through the metal grille ventilating his grimy compartment. He stepped out, walked past the fancier air-conditioned coaches he’d never set foot in, and thought: My children will do better. Once I’m working on the railways, they’ll always travel AC.

He arrived in Musallahpur and started studying. Every few days he’d check the Ministry of Railways website for an exam date. Days turned into weeks. Weeks into months. The pandemic hit; obviously there would be delays. He kept studying.

Before he knew it, he’d been in Musallahpur nearly six years.

Kumar lives in a bare, windowless room he shares with another student. His father, struggling to keep up the allowance, sold some of the family’s land to support him. Kumar studies 18 hours a day, shuttling between libraries, revision tutorials, and mock test sessions. He has developed diabetes, which he attributes to anxiety and stress.

As Kumar approached 30—the upper age limit for railway exams (extended to 33 for lower-caste applicants like him)—his parents urged him to consider alternative careers. He convinced them to be patient. He’d come this far. He couldn’t quit now.

Late last year, the exam was finally announced. November 27th, 2024. After six years, the moment he’d spent his adult life preparing for was here.

An undergraduate engineering degree and six years studying in Patna could lead to him becoming a track-maintenance worker.

“I never imagined it would come to this,” he told The Economist sadly.

Khan Sir and the 25 Million Followers

The Economist described a scene where about 3,000 students gathered in a classroom the size of a small aircraft hangar to hear Musallahpur’s most popular exam coach speak. He goes by the name Khan Sir (retro pseudonyms are popular—one rival is called Physics Wallah).

Young women filled the front rows. Thousands of young men sat behind. Those who couldn’t get seats lined the aisles and leaned against walls. The lecture was live-streamed on Khan Sir’s app and broadcast to two other rooms in his Khan Global Studies learning center. For those at the back, it also showed on four huge TV screens hanging from the ceiling.

Khan Sir’s YouTube channel has 24.6 million subscribers—more than the population of Australia—generating an estimated ₹10-20 lakh monthly. His net worth is estimated between ₹5-41 crore, which is simultaneously modest for someone with 25 million followers and obscene for a guy who teaches government exam prep.

But here’s what makes him genuinely unusual: he rejected a ₹107 crore ($13 million) offer from an ed-tech company to preserve affordability. In a system built on extracting maximum rent from desperate aspirants, Khan Sir apparently has principles. Or possibly terrible financial advisors. Hard to say which.

Students travel from across India to see him in person. His empire now spans branded textbooks and six coaching centers across the country.

The key to his popularity? He bridges the gap between the formal English-and-Hindi diction of the exams (written like old-fashioned Oxbridge entrance papers) and the reality that many test-takers are one generation out of illiteracy and get their news from YouTube. He explains Newtonian motion and Trump’s trade policy with homely metaphors and dad jokes.

Khan Sir is extremely guarded about personal information. Given India’s religious and political tensions, he’d rather no one knew his real name, where he came from, or what he believes.

Like many tutors in Musallahpur, Khan Sir got into coaching after his own unsuccessful attempt to get a government job—years ago he passed the army entrance exam but failed the physical test. Dispirited, he found work as a welder in Patna. When he noticed the flood of students coming for government exams, he tried teaching. During the pandemic he moved online.

The difficulty of exam questions varies wildly. Some seem reasonable tests of educational competence. Others are comically obscure, in the tradition of British public-service exams set during the Raj. In the 19th century, plum administrative posts were obtained through exams that often required a British classical education—candidates might translate Cicero or discourse on 15th-century Scottish poetry.

Today’s railway exams are multiple choice rather than essays, but they can be brutally hard. Someone wanting to become an assistant train driver might be asked questions that have nothing to do with driving trains and everything to do with keeping people in permanent purgatory.

The current-affairs questions are so random they sometimes seem designed purely to extend the waiting period indefinitely. Coaching centers sell courses, “doubt sessions,” six-hour marathon classes, and mock tests. Even with assiduous preparation, many students fail to make the cutoff mark. Often they wait in Musallahpur for the next round, which can take years.

When the Exams Get Cancelled

In 2022, railway authorities held exams for “Non-Technical Popular Categories,” including ticket inspector positions. After papers were collected, authorities announced that candidates would have to take another exam.

Students were furious. “It’s like training for the Olympics, running your race, and then they move the finishing line,” Khan Sir said. The Economist reported that riots broke out across the state. Protesters occupied railway tracks and set fire to a train.

This desperation fuels an unsavory business: cheating. Papers leak in advance. According to Khan Sir, some illicit tests sell for tens of thousands of dollars. If an irregularity is identified, exams may be cancelled, forcing people to wait indefinitely.

Last December, one paper in the Bihar Public Service Commission entrance test was reportedly leaked. Some exams were cancelled. Shortly afterward, a Musallahpur student planning to sit one of the cancelled tests was found hanging from the ceiling fan in his room.

He was one of Khan Sir’s star pupils. Khan Sir attended the funeral. In videos of the event, the normally confident tutor looks strangely lost and vulnerable as he helps the family lay cloths on the boy’s body. “This was a very helpless moment for me,” he recalled.

Since the student’s death, Khan Sir has waged a personal campaign against paper leaks and cheating. His office is stacked with blank postcards he plans to ask pupils to send to the High Court in support of his petition for CCTV footage from all exam rooms.

“If I do not fight for them, then God will never forgive me,” he said.

Neha Bharti’s Ultimatum

The Economist also profiled Neha Bharti, 26, who sees the railway exams as her salvation. Her grandfather was a stationmaster in Amritsar. She remembers the two-bedroom bungalow by the station that seemed like a palace. “He even had servants who cleaned the place and served food,” she said. “Whenever I went to his office, everyone treated me with respect.”

Before moving to Musallahpur, Bharti felt trapped. She was working as a nurse, much to her family’s chagrin—they wanted her married. At the small private hospital where she worked, doctors constantly made suggestive comments and tried to catch her alone. She didn’t know where to turn.

Then she saw one of Khan Sir’s YouTube videos and was hooked. She was sure she could crack the exams and get a government job. She moved to Patna in 2022 and applied to become a ticket inspector.

She didn’t pass. She told herself she’d study harder for the next round. Three years later, she’s still waiting.

Her father recently gave her an ultimatum: land a railway job within six months or come home and get married. Every time she visits her village she senses the hemmed-in life waiting for her.

“When I go home I like to watch Khan Sir videos, to remind me what it feels like in Patna,” she said. “I have fought my father. I have fought my brothers to come here. And I am not asking for a single rupee. I will earn it all myself. Just give me the chance.”

The Jobs Nobody Wants

The Economist interviewed people who made it through the gruelling entrance process and found the job disappointing.

Rakesh (a pseudonym—he’s afraid of getting in trouble with bosses) is a ticket inspector on long-distance routes. He spends eight hours going up and down narrow aisles of sleeper carriages stacked three bunks high, negotiating with people about discounts and fines. Then he waits at the station for a train home—sometimes up to 12 hours, which doesn’t count as overtime.

He studied for more than two years to get this job. Now he regrets it. He hates the time away from his wife—he’s often not allowed days off even for religious festivals. But he can’t afford to leave.

Pradeep (also not his real name) passed exams to become a goods-train driver 15 years ago. Shifts should be eight hours but regularly run 12, sometimes 20. Many trains don’t have air conditioning, making it unbearably hot. Often there’s no toilet. After shifts, he waits up to 60 hours for another goods train to catch a lift home.

“It is hardly a cushy job,” said Ashok Kumar Raut, a train driver and union leader in Patna. “The railways are going on the track of corporate culture. There are more trains, the workload is increasing, but the manpower keeps going down.”

Since 2015, the Ministry of Railways has eliminated more than 70,000 low-level and administrative positions. The union accuses the ministry of deliberately leaving positions unfilled to cut costs.

Raut advises young people not to waste years preparing: “They need to choose something else.”

But what else is there? The private sector pays a fraction of government wages and offers no security. When Kumar heard there were jobs at a glitzy new mall near Musallahpur paying close to what he might earn as a track worker, he baulked. “I am educated with a technical degree,” he said. “My family hasn’t sacrificed so much for me to work in a coffee shop. People only work there if they have no other choice.”

No one from his parents’ generation would respect a barista. But they admire, or at least understand, a railway job.

The Colonial Ghost

There’s bitter irony in all this. The civil service system in India was set up by the British during the colonial period. The Raj reserved plum administrative posts for white men, obtained through patronage. In the mid-19th century, the government introduced competitive exams theoretically open to all British subjects, including Indians.

But the exams excluded many Indians because questions often required a British classical education—and the ability to travel to London, where exams were held until the 1920s. It was designed to keep Indians out while maintaining the veneer of meritocracy.

The system was never about finding the best people for jobs. It was about sorting and excluding.

Want to understand how uniquely dysfunctional India’s system is? Let’s compare exam cultures globally.

China’s gaokao affects 13+ million students annually with similar pressure and only 5% getting into top universities. South Korea spends $20 billion annually (2% of GDP) on private tutoring with suicide rates that would make India blush. Japan has its ronin phenomenon of gap-year students retaking exams.

But here’s the difference: China essentially eliminated exam fraud. How? Military-level security. Severe criminal prosecution including the death penalty for serious violations. Lifetime bans for lesser offenses. The gaokao, despite all its flaws, functions with integrity at scale.

India? 48-70 major paper leaks over five years affecting 15+ million aspirants. Leaked papers sell for ₹30-50 lakh ($35,000-$59,000) per candidate. When irregularities are identified, exams get cancelled, and students riot. One student literally killed himself after his exam was cancelled.

China: Death penalty for exam fraud.
India: Lucrative business opportunity.

Also, China expanded college attendance from 1% (1977) to 67% currently. India’s manufacturing sector is stuck at 17.7% of GDP while China’s economy actually provides alternatives to government jobs. When Chinese students suffer through brutal exams, at least there’s an actual functioning economy on the other side.

India combines exam intensity with corruption, job scarcity, wage distortions, reform paralysis, and economic dysfunction to create something entirely unique: a system that wastes human capital at unprecedented scale with minimal mitigation and a worseningtrajectory.

And here’s the colonial cherry on top: The British civil service exam was deliberately designed to exclude Indians. Exams were held only in London until 1922. Questions favored Greek and Latin over Sanskrit and Arabic. The maximum age was set deliberately low (19-22). The 1924 Lee Commission recommended quotas of 40% Europeans, 40% Indians, 20% direct promotion—reflecting British anxiety about “higher posts reserved for Europeans being filched away by superior wits of the native.”

The British created a system to exclude Indians while maintaining the appearance of meritocracy. Independent India kept the exclusion, removed the British jobs, and called it a feature.

Here’s the deeper absurdity: the system was designed to create what Macaulay called “a class of persons, Indian in blood and colour, but English in taste, in opinions, in morals, and in intellect.” (If you haven’t read Post 12 about Macaulay’s Children, this is where that colonial mind-virus lives on.)

Independent India kept the system and is still trying to produce Macaulay’s Children. Except it’s preparing them for an England that no longer exists and hasn’t for more than 50 years. The formal, archaic diction of the exams—the bizarre questions about JavaScript for train drivers, the shuddh Hindi that nobody speaks, the entire apparatus of artificial difficulty—it’s all in service of creating colonial subjects for an empire that’s been dead for generations.

The British created a system to exclude Indians from good jobs while producing a class of anglicized intermediaries. Independent India kept the exclusion, perfected the anglicization, but removed the jobs. We’re producing Macaulay’s Children by the millions, training them in obsolete knowledge for an obsolete empire, and then telling 99.7% of them there’s no position available.

It would be funny if it weren’t destroying millions of lives.

The November Exam

The Economist followed Kumar to exam day. He got ready to take the exam on November 27th, 2024. The test center was in Darbhanga, several hours away by train. He traveled the night before, carrying only a blanket, a sheet, and a plastic bag of roti. He hadn’t brought his books. Everything he needed was in his head.

His parents called to wish him luck. He was in a trance, barely able to speak.

He crashed at a friend’s place and set out the next morning. The test center was a three-story building at the end of a narrow lane, protected by metal barriers. Students massed outside. A police Jeep stood watch. Just outside the barriers, entrepreneuring salesmen sat with laptops and printers, offering to print enrollment documents that might have been forgotten.

It was strange seeing the actual exam after so many mock tests. The questions seemed unnervingly easy. He answered everything with 15 minutes to spare. He had nothing to do but review, running through every possible answer.

He looked around at the other hopefuls sitting silently, eyes down. For the first time in months, Kumar allowed himself to hope.

Perhaps this time, his luck would change.

The Postscript

As The Economist went to press in July 2025, Kumar learned he had passed the assistant train-driver exams. He is now preparing for his interview.

Six years. An engineering degree. Diabetes from stress. His family’s land sold to support him. His father’s retirement funds depleted. For a chance—just a chance—at a job that pays ₹60,000 per month (about $700) and will require him to spend 12-hour shifts in un-air-conditioned cabs with no toilets.

And here’s the thing: Kumar is one of the lucky ones.

For every Kumar who passes, there are 332 who don’t. For every Neha Bharti who gets a second chance, there are thousands whose fathers’ ultimatums expire and who return home to marriages they don’t want.

India’s most educated young people—precisely those the country needs in its workforce—are devoting years of their lives cramming for government exams instead of working productively. These exams cultivate no real-world skills. They’re pure sorting mechanisms, not tools of human capital development.

But beyond the staggering economic waste, there’s a deeper, more corrosive human cost. In one survey, 88% of grade 12 students said they preferred a government job to a private sector job. But these jobs do not and cannot exist in sufficient numbers. The result is disillusioned cohorts trained to expect a middle-class, white-collar lifestyle, convinced that only a government job can deliver it.

India is creating large numbers of educated young people who are inevitably disillusioned. That is not a sustainable equilibrium.

Why This Happened

This isn’t some ancient tradition. This is the direct result of decisions made in the 1950s through the 1980s—the socialist period I grew up in and fled.

When you strangle the private sector with 97% tax rates and licenses for everything from toothpaste to cars, you create an economy where government jobs are the only decent option. When the government becomes the primary employer and pays vastly more than the private sector it has systematically crippled, you create tournament-style competition for those jobs.

When you make those jobs lifetime tenure with generous perks regardless of competence, you make them worth burning years of your life to obtain.

When you allocate them through exams rather than connections (a reform meant to reduce corruption), you redirect the rent-seeking from bribes to time—and time, especially young people’s time, is far more valuable than bribes.

The system is working exactly as designed. The problem is the design itself.

India’s wildly skewed public wage structure—where government salaries far exceed what’s justified by GDP per capita or job requirements—distorts education, employment, and unemployment throughout the entire economy in deeply wasteful ways.

The only real solution is to bring public sector pay back in line with economic fundamentals. And to stop strangling the private sector so it can actually create jobs people want.

But that would require admitting that the entire structure is broken. And institutions don’t like admitting that.

The Personal Question

When I looked at that Delhi University form in 1984, I had a choice. I could play the game—resume studying fake Hindi, memorize facts I’d forget, compete in tournaments I’d probably lose—or I could refuse on principle.

I refused. I got on a plane. I went to Columbia, where they didn’t care whether I could recite the names of Mughal emperors or knew which element is most abundant in the Earth’s crust. They cared whether I could think.

That was the right choice for me. But it was a choice only available because my family could afford it, because I had the test scores to get into an American university, because I forced (blackmailed?) my lawyer mother to agree to a contract she had drunkenly signed on one of those many and interminable evenings that she was berating me for being the village idiot.

Most Indians don’t. Neeraj Kumar didn’t. Neha Bharti doesn’t. The student who hanged himself after exams were cancelled didn’t.

They’re stuck in a system that grinds them down, spits most of them out, and calls it meritocracy.

I hated Indian exams in 1984. If I’d known what the system would become—half a million people in purgatory in Musallahpur alone, 30 million applying for 90,000 jobs, 1.4% of GDP wasted on rent-seeking—I’d have hated it even more.

But I also would have understood it better. It’s not a bug. It’s a feature. It’s the inevitable result of socialism’s war on the private sector, combined with a colonial examination system designed to exclude, wrapped in the mythology of meritocracy.

The real tragedy isn’t that I left. It’s that so many brilliant young Indians can’t.

As I write this, Neeraj Kumar is preparing for his interview. He has memorized the periodic table. He can name the inventor of JavaScript. He knows which trains run on which tracks.

What he doesn’t know is whether any of this will matter.

What he does know is that he has no other choice.

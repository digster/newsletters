Georges Clemenceau, France's Prime Minister during World War I, once delivered history's most devastating diplomatic barb: "England is a French colony that went wrong" ("Qu'est-ce que l'Angleterre? Une colonie française qui a mal tourné").
The man who negotiated with Lloyd George at Versailles understood what centuries of British denial couldn't hide: that Parliament still uses Norman French for royal assent, that the monarchy descends from William the Conqueror, that the legal system springs from Norman roots. Britain was, in essence, a French project that had the audacity to become France's greatest rival using the very institutions France gave them.
A century later, in July 2025, French President Emmanuel Macron channeled Clemenceau's spirit. Standing in Windsor Castle, he essentially told the British they were a French creation. "Yes, our story is built on these conquests and these rivalries," he said, speaking of William the Conqueror's 1066 invasion. "That is the beauty of our unique relationship." King Charles graciously acknowledged that Windsor was "begun by my ancestor—and your sometime countryman—William, Duke of Normandy." Prime Minister Keir Starmer went even further, calling the Battle of Hastings "the beginning of 1,000 years of shared culture that is now defined by mutual admiration and kinship."
The British essentially spent three days celebrating being conquered by the French, with their highest officials confirming Clemenceau's century-old assessment. The Bayeux Tapestry loan was the punchline—Britain would display in its most prestigious museum a 230-foot illustrated reminder of their conquest. It was diplomatic genius: using history to remind Britain who really made them who they are.
The irony is even more delicious post-Brexit. Here's Britain asserting its unique identity, its exceptional status—all while speaking a language that's 30% French, operating courts using Norman principles, ruled from a castle built by a French duke. The colony that "went wrong" trying to pretend it never was a colony at all.
Similarly, Britain created India. The subcontinent had never been one political entity in its entire history. Without the British, without English, there is no India. The Indian National Congress was founded by Allan Octavian Hume, a British civil servant. The independence movement was led by English-educated Indians: Nehru at Harrow, Gandhi trained as a barrister in London, Jinnah (like my mother!) at Lincoln's Inn.
Just as Britain spent centuries denying its French foundations while speaking Norman-influenced English, India spends its days denying its British foundations while conducting Parliament in English. Just as Clemenceau could laugh at the "colony gone wrong," somewhere in colonial heaven, Thomas Babington Macaulay must be chuckling at what his educational "minute" created.
Which brings me to what I haven't told you.
Last week, I said I could keep writing about India forever, that every answer spawns three questions, every solution creates five problems. I hinted at something I hadn't revealed—something that explains why I see these patterns so clearly, why I can write about WhatsApp uncles with both mockery and recognition.
Actually, I lied.
Not about ending the series—clearly, since you're reading this. But about it being just "one more thing." It's not just one more thing. It's THE thing. The skeleton key that unlocks every paradox I've described.
I am one of Macaulay's Children. And if you're reading this in English, finding yourself nodding along, feeling superior to those WhatsApp uncles while secretly recognizing yourself in them—so are you.
Let me tell you what that means.
On February 2, 1835, Thomas Babington Macaulay wrote a minute that would colonize minds more effectively than British armies ever colonized land. His goal was breathtakingly ambitious: create "a class of persons, Indian in blood and colour, but English in taste, in opinions, in morals, and in intellect."
The man wasn't subtle. He declared that "a single shelf of a good European library was worth the whole native literature of India and Arabia." He wanted interpreters between the British and "the millions whom we govern." Not just linguistic interpreters—cultural ones. People who would think British thoughts in Indian bodies.
It worked. Oh god, did it work.
I am the product of The Modern School in Delhi—"modern" because it was the first school set up on British educational principles in 1920. The irony of calling something "modern" for following 19th-century British pedagogy apparently escaped everyone. As a physicist, I find it particularly amusing that my school was founded the same year we date "Modern Physics" from—quantum mechanics, relativity, the works. Both are now over a century old, both still insist they're "modern." The rule should be: never name anything with "Modern" in it, or it becomes ironic within a generation. But that's another story.
My entire education—kindergarten through college—was in English. But unlike many of my classmates, English was also my home language. My family speaks to each other, always, in English. On both sides, going back generations.
Does this bother me? Not in the slightest. Do I have British characteristics? Absolutely. But I also have Indian ones. Human society is gloriously messy despite our desperate attempts to put things in boxes labeled "authentic." As I've said throughout this series, I grew up reading Bertrand Russell and Milton Friedman. Their most important lesson? Each individual matters precisely because of their individuality, not their group identity.
My school's history is India's history in miniature. Founded by nationalists who wanted to create "modern" Indians to fight the British, using British methods to defeat British rule. The founders included Lala Raghubir Singh, who believed India needed English education to compete globally, even as he fought for independence.
The school's motto—"Nayamatma balahinena labhya" (Self-realization cannot be achieved by the weak)—is from the Upanishads, but we studied it in English translation. We sang both the Indian national anthem and English hymns. We studied Shakespeare and Kalidasa, though Shakespeare got more periods. We were being trained to be simultaneously Indian and not-Indian, to belong everywhere and nowhere.
Every morning assembly was a masterpiece of cultural schizophrenia. Sanskrit shlokas followed by English poetry. Indian classical music demonstrations before discussing Western scientific method. Teachers who'd quote Tagore to explain Newton. It was cultural fusion before fusion was trendy—or maybe cultural confusion is more accurate.
India's education system isn't just segmented—it's a caste system pretending to be meritocracy. At the top: English-medium private schools charging lakhs in fees, producing students who speak better English than Hindi. These feed into IITs and IIMs, which everyone claims are about merit while conveniently ignoring that 70% of students come from families earning over ₹5 lakh annually in a country where median income is ₹50,000.
The numbers are staggering. India produces 1.5 million engineers annually—the world's highest—while 83% remain unemployed. Why? Because only the top 5% from English-medium schools actually receive and education; the rest get degrees. The system produces both Sundar Pichai and millions who can't code "Hello World."
The IIT-JEE exam—attempted by 1.5 million students for 16,000 seats—has spawned a ₹40,000 crore coaching industry. Kota, Rajasthan, has become a factory town producing broken dreams: 150,000 students arrive annually, living in prison-like hostels, studying 14 hours daily. The city has installed anti-suicide nets on buildings. This is what Macaulay's system evolved into: children killing themselves for the chance to become the brown sahibs he envisioned.
Meanwhile, government schools teach in regional languages to 65% of Indian children, ensuring they'll never compete for real opportunities. It's apartheid through language. A Tamil-medium student, no matter how brilliant, will struggle to access cutting-edge knowledge available only in English. An English-medium student, no matter how mediocre, starts ahead.
Here's what Indian nationalists won't acknowledge: English is India's superpower. All modern knowledge—science, medicine, programming—exists primarily in English. It's a gigantic advantage being a native English speaker. India's entire IT industry, worth $245 billion, exists because Indians speak English. Not Hindi, not Tamil—English.
When China wants to access global knowledge, it must translate. When India wants it, it just reads. India's software exports exceed China's not because Indians are inherently better programmers but because they can Google error messages. That's not cultural superiority; that's linguistic accident. Or colonial gift. Or both.
The hypocrisy is delicious. Politicians who publicly lambast "Angrezi" as colonial pollution privately send their children to English-medium schools. The same leaders demanding Hindi imposition tweet their demands in English. Regional language champions give speeches about Tamil pride, then ensure their children study in English. Everyone knows the game; everyone pretends otherwise.
As journalist Manu Joseph noted: "English is the de facto national language of India. It is a bitter truth."
For many people, being a Macaulay's child means living with permanent cognitive dissonance. They think in English but dream in their mother tongue. They read The Economist but consult astrologers. They mock arranged marriage while accepting family-found "alliances." They're rational at work, superstitious at home.
Watch almost any Macaulay's child navigate their day: They write code reviews citing Martin Fowler's refactoring principles, then touch their parents' feet seeking blessings. They debate monetary policy quoting Milton Friedman, then fast on Tuesdays for Hanuman. They're simultaneously embarrassed by and proud of being Indian—embarrassed by the chaos and superstition, proud of the resilience and innovation. Classic Macaulay's child syndrome.
This split creates peculiar behaviors. Indians who've never left India affecting British accents. MBAs who analyze market dynamics but won't sign contracts during Rahu Kalam. Doctors who prescribe antibiotics and recommend temple visits. Engineers who debug code while wearing rings to appease Saturn.
They code-switch constantly. LinkedIn shows Stanford degrees; WhatsApp reveals family astrologers. They're management consultants who perform elaborate Navratri fasts. Investment bankers who check planetary positions before trading. They're everything and nothing, Eastern and Western, modern and ancient—exactly as Macaulay intended, though probably not in the way he imagined.
Every WhatsApp uncle forwarding "NASA confirms Sanskrit is best computer language" is fighting Macaulay's ghost. The desperate need to prove ancient Indian superiority comes from deep educational trauma. When your colonizers convince you that your civilization is worthless, you spend centuries trying to prove them wrong—even through fabricated NASA endorsements.
The uncles aren't stupid. They're wounded. Their entire education told them Indian knowledge was inferior. Now they overcompensate, seeing vedic mathematics in quantum physics, finding iPhone technology in ancient scriptures. It's psychological decolonization through WhatsApp forwards.
The tragedy? India did contribute massively to global knowledge. Indians invented the zero, decimal systems, calculated Earth's circumference, developed advanced metallurgy. But genuine achievements get lost in desperate myth-making. Instead of teaching real Indian contributions, we invent fake ones. Instead of acknowledging that yoga is magnificent without claiming it cures cancer, we make absurd claims that undermine legitimate accomplishments.
English is what holds India together—and everyone hates this fact. Without English, there's no India, just a subcontinent of linguistic nations. Tamil Nadu would never accept Hindi domination. Bengali speakers won't learn Telugu. Kannadigas won't adopt Marathi. English is the only language all Indians can agree to resent equally.
The numbers don't lie. India's Parliament functions in English. The Supreme Court operates in English. Corporate India runs on English. Even anti-English agitations are coordinated through English-language WhatsApp messages. The three-language formula (mother tongue + Hindi + English) is really a one-language reality: English for opportunity, everything else for identity.
When southern states resist Hindi imposition, they're not choosing Tamil or Telugu over Hindi—they're choosing English. Karnataka's IT industry doesn't run on Kannada; it runs on English. Chennai's automobile industry doesn't operate in Tamil. Kerala's nurses don't emigrate to the Gulf speaking Malayalam. They succeed through English, even while politically opposing it.
The European Union manages 24 official languages for 450 million people. India manages 22 for 1.4 billion—through English. The colonizer's language became the colonized's solution to their own diversity. Macaulay's ghost must be laughing.
Every Indian parent wants their child in IIT or IIM, but nobody asks why. These institutions aren't particularly innovative—they produce employees, not entrepreneurs. Their research output is mediocre. Their Nobel count is zero. But they're English-medium, they're "modern," and they're what Macaulay would have designed: factories producing competent administrators for the global economy.
The coaching industry around these exams is worth ₹3,500 crore in Kota alone. Families spend their life savings. Students sacrifice their childhoods. For what? To become exactly what Macaulay wanted: intermediaries between Western capital and Indian labor.
The successful ones—the Sundar Pichais and Satya Nadellas—leave India immediately. Pichai attended Jawahar Vidyalaya, a CBSE school, before IIT Kharagpur. Nadella studied at Hyderabad Public School. Indra Nooyi went to Holy Angels Anglo Indian School. The pattern is unmistakable: Anglo-Indian schools producing global corporate leaders who run American companies, not Indian ones. They solve Silicon Valley's problems, not India's. India's best minds debug Microsoft Windows while Indian government websites remain broken. This isn't brain drain; it's Macaulay's system working perfectly—creating a class worth $6.5 trillion in combined market capitalization while leaving India's problems unsolved.
Those who remain create India's "unicorns"—startups valued at $1 billion copying American business models. Flipkart copies Amazon. Ola copies Uber. Paytm copies PayPal. Indian innovation means implementing American ideas for Indian markets. Even our entrepreneurship is derivative.
My family's relationship with English is complete—we don't switch to Hindi or Tamil when angry or intimate. We dream, fight, and love in English. Three generations on both sides—all English speakers. We're what hardcore nationalists would call "deracinated."
Are we less Indian? By whose definition? The family celebrates Diwali and Holi, explaining their significance in English. They perform Hindu rituals while questioning their logic—in English. Culturally Hindu, linguistically English, philosophically Western, emotionally Indian. Walking contradictions, and that's perfectly Indian—everything you can say about India is true, as is its opposite.
This isn't unique. Millions of Indians live this reality. The call-center employee who becomes "Steve" for eight hours. The software engineer who reads documentation all day in English, then struggles to explain their job in their mother tongue. The banker who thinks in English but counts in Hindi.
Let's say what nobody wants to admit: colonialism had benefits. This doesn't justify imperialism—colonialism was brutal, exploitative, unjustifiable under any ethical framework. But pretending it left nothing positive is intellectual dishonesty.
English is a gigantic benefit. The parliamentary system, despite its flaws, works better than most alternatives. The railways, though built for extraction, united the subcontinent. The legal system, though designed for control, provides a framework for rights. Even the idea of India as a nation, rather than a geographic expression, is British.
Without colonialism, there would be no Republic of India. There would be princely states, linguistic kingdoms, religious territories. Maybe that would have been better—we'll never know. But modern India, with all its contradictions, is a British creation. Denying this is like Britain denying it's a French creation.
But here's what Macaulay didn't foresee: his children would use his tools against his empire. Every leader of India's independence movement was English-educated. They read John Stuart Mill on liberty and asked why it didn't apply to them. They studied British law and used it to demand rights. They learned British parliamentary procedure and used it to dismantle British rule.
Gandhi's genius wasn't rejecting English but weaponizing it. His letters to British authorities, written in perfect English, exposed their hypocrisy to their own people. Nehru's "Discovery of India," written in English, told India's story to the world. Ambedkar, the lowest of the low in the caste system, used his British legal education to write India's Constitution.
The colonizer's language became the revolutionary's weapon. But the price was steep: Indians who could only access their own culture through English translations. Traditional knowledge systems collapsed because they weren't in English. Ancient texts became museum pieces while English textbooks became gospel.
The WhatsApp uncle and the wisdom auntie are both responses to Macaulay. The uncle, desperate to prove Indian knowledge supreme, forwards fake news about Vedic airplanes. The auntie, comfortable with hybridity, sends both Bhagavad Gita quotes and Good Morning messages festooned with roses.
The uncle is fighting a war already lost. The auntie has transcended the battlefield. She doesn't need NASA to validate yoga; she just does it. She doesn't need to prove turmeric is scientific; she just uses it. She's post-colonial while the uncle is still colonial, defining himself against the colonizer.
This gendered response is fascinating. Men, who bore colonialism's emasculation more directly, need to assert superiority. Women, who faced double colonization (by British and by patriarchy), learned to navigate both systems. The auntie knows all power structures are negotiable; the uncle still believes in hierarchies.
Reading Friedman as an Indian is surreal. He assumes rational actors, but what's rational when you consult astrologers for investment advice? He assumes free markets, but what's free when your business partner is chosen by caste? He assumes individual choice, but what's individual when your family decides your career?
Yet India's economy works—chaotically, inefficiently, but it works. Street vendors accept digital payments while dodging taxes.Billionaires emerge from slums. Software gets exported from cities without reliable electricity. The economy functions not despite irrationality but through it.
Maybe Macaulay's children understand something Friedman didn't: modernity doesn't replace tradition; it layers over it. You can have venture capitalists checking horoscopes, CEOs performing havans, stock markets closing for religious holidays. The invisible hand wears sacred threads. Perhaps this cultural "irrationality" is actually a comparative advantage—resilience through chaos rather than rigid efficiency.
Every Indian startup founder is trying to escape Macaulay's curse through Silicon Valley's blessing. They believe entrepreneurship will decolonize their minds. They're wrong. They're just exchanging British colonialism for American capitalism.
The vocabulary changes—"disruption" instead of "development," "pivot" instead of "progress"—but the dependency remains. Indian VCs fund Indian copies of American companies. Indian founders dream of NASDAQ listings, not NSE. Success still means Western validation.
The few who try genuine innovation face a horrible truth: the Indian market doesn't reward originality. It rewards jugaad—making Western ideas work with Indian constraints. The highest-valued Indian startups are those that domesticated foreign models, not those that created indigenous ones.
Nothing reveals Macaulay's legacy like Indian matrimonial ads. "Convent-educated girl sought." "English-medium schooling must." "Fluent English speaking family preferred." Two centuries later, we're still advertising our colonization as qualification for marriage.
Elite families want "modern" daughters-in-law who can discuss Foucault but also know all the rituals. They want girls who studied at Oxford but will still touch elders' feet. They want Macaulay's children who can seamlessly pretend they're not. "Seeking suitable alliance for fair, convent-educated girl, MSc, earning well, from cultured family. Boy must be fluent in English, well-settled, with modern outlook and traditional values." Decoded: She's career-oriented but can still do the Diwali puja. And he better not mispronounce "entrepreneur" at parties.
Dating apps intensify this schizophrenia. Profiles showcase international education and traditional values, career ambitions and family orientation, feminist principles and cultural compliance. Everyone's looking for someone equally confused about their identity. The Stanford MBA marries the "homely" girl whose profile mentioned her Bharatanatyam training—he's assured she's Indian enough for his parents, she's happy he's Westernized enough to let her wear jeans after marriage.
Macaulay created individuals in a civilization built on collectives. Traditional India didn't have individuals; it had family members, caste members, community members. British education introduced the radical idea that you could think for yourself.
This creates impossible tensions. Your Western education says follow your passion; your family says take the secure job. Your business school training says maximize profit; your community obligations say share wealth. You're trained for individual achievement in a society that punishes individual assertion.
Macaulay's children grow up reading Bertrand Russell on individual liberty while hearing relatives explain family duty. Milton Friedman teaches about free choice while their culture insists choice is illusion. Every Macaulay's child lives this contradiction: believing in individual rights while accepting arranged marriages, preaching meritocracy while navigating caste, advocating feminism while touching fathers' feet.
Now that we know we're Macaulay's children, what next? We can't unlearn English—that ship has sailed. We can't return to pre-colonial innocence—it never existed anyway. We can't fully embrace Western modernity—it doesn't fit our reality.
Maybe the answer is to stop fighting Macaulay's ghost and start using his gifts. English gives us access to global knowledge—use it.Western education gave us critical thinking—apply it to our own traditions. Colonial structures gave us common frameworks—repurpose them.
India's superpower might be its ability to be everything simultaneously. To run ancient software and modern hardware together. To be the world's back office and its spiritual retreat. To produce both computer programmers and cow vigilantes. To be colonized and free, traditional and modern, Eastern and Western—all at once.
I thought I could end this series here, but India doesn't do endings. Every conclusion spawns new contradictions. Every answer raises more questions. Macaulay wanted to create brown Englishmen. He created something far stranger: people who are neither English nor traditionally Indian, but something entirely new.
They're the people who debug Python while wearing rudraksha. Who read Marxist theory during Navratri fasts. Who quote Shakespeare and the Bhagavad Gita with equal facility and confusion. They're walking paradoxes in a paradoxical nation.
The British created India like the French created Britain. But while Britain eventually became confidently British, India remains gloriously uncertain about what being Indian means. Maybe that's its strength—the ability to be everything and nothing, to hold all contradictions without resolution.
I am Macaulay's child. If you've read this far in English, so are you. We didn't choose this inheritance, but it's ours now. The question isn't whether we're authentic Indians—that debate is meaningless. The question is what we do with our colonial software running on ancient hardware.
Next week, I'll explore... something! Since it is about India, it will be about everything, nothing and something, all at once, everywhere and nowhere.
The British Empire is dead. Macaulay's children live on. And we're still figuring out what the hell that means.

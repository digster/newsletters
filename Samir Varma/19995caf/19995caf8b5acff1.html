Language (Part 1). English is an Indian Language Now!

The Economic Conquest Nobody Talks About

Samir Varma
Sep 29
 
READ IN APP
 

Let me tell you about the moment I realized I wasn’t Indian the way most Indians are Indian.

I was twenty-two, temporarily back in Delhi after Columbia (before heading off to the University of Texas for my PhD), sitting in a restaurant with Hindi-speaking friends. The waiter brought our food. I said “thank you.” My friend laughed. “Why do you always do that?”

“Do what?”

“Thank the servants.”

That’s when it hit me. In Hindi, you don’t thank people for doing their job. You accept service as your due, maybe nod, move on. “Dhanyavad” or “Shukriya” exist, sure, but they’re for big favors, not bringing dal. Saying them to waiters marks you as weird. Or worse—foreign.

But I couldn’t help it. English had programmed my reflexes. In English, “thank you” is linguistic lubricant, social WD-40. You spray it everywhere. Thank the cashier, thank the driver, thank the person holding the door. It’s not about gratitude; it’s about acknowledging shared humanity.

Hindi has hierarchical precision. English has democratic ambiguity. And I’d been thinking in English so long, I’d accidentally become egalitarian in a hierarchical society.

This wasn’t about language. This was about consciousness.

The Hierarchy in Every Hindi Sentence

Every Indian language is a status-adjustment machine. Hindi has three ways to say “you”—aap (formal), tum (familiar), tu (intimate/insulting). Each comes with its own verb conjugations. Before you speak, you must calculate: Is this person older? Richer? More powerful? My boss? My servant? My equal? (Trick question—nobody’s your equal.)

Tamil: neenga/nee. Bengali: apni/tumi/tui. Telugu: meeru/nuvvu. Every major Indian language forces you to declare relative status with every sentence. It’s like speaking with a built-in org chart.

English? Just “you.” One word. No conjugations. The Queen of England is “you.” Your garbage collector is “you.” Your dog is “you.” Suddenly, everyone’s grammatically equal.

Watch Indians in corporate meetings: They’ll switch from deferential Hindi (”Aap kya chahte hain, sir?”) to democratic English (”What do you think?”) mid-sentence. It’s not code-switching; it’s worldview-switching. In Hindi, the boss is linguistically elevated. In English, he’s just another voice in the room.

The cognitive whiplash is real. You’re hierarchical at 9 AM, egalitarian at 10 AM, back to hierarchical at lunch. No wonder Indian offices feel schizophrenic—they’re literally thinking in different civilizations every hour.

This is segmented pluralism in its purest form. Not just different languages for different contexts, but different realities. In Hindi, it’s a hierarchy. In English, we’re a team. The same person is “Sharma Ji” at 9 AM and “Rakesh” at the 10 AM stand-up.

India hasn’t resolved this contradiction. We’ve monetized it. Every Indian corporation runs on this linguistic schizophrenia. The org chart is in English. The real power structure is in Hindi. Navigate both or perish.

The Core Paradox Nobody Wants to Discuss

Here’s what’ll really bake your noodle: English has been in India longer than many “Indian” languages have existed in their current form. Shakespeare wrote plays 400 years ago that English speakers can still understand. Try reading Hindi from 400 years ago. Good luck. What we call “Hindi” today was standardized in the 19th century.

“Pure Hindi” that news anchors speak? Completely invented. Real people speak Hindustani—a glorious bastard of Sanskrit, Arabic, Persian, Turkish, and yes, English. The “shuddh Hindi” brigade are defending a language younger than the East India Company.

Consider: Spanish belongs to Latin America now (350 million speakers vs Spain’s 47 million). Portuguese belongs to Brazil (230 million vs Portugal’s 10 million). By that logic, English belongs to India. We have 125 million English speakers. England has 56 million. We won.

India didn’t adopt English. India digested English, metabolized it, and now we’re secreting it back into the world with new flavors. “Do the needful”? That’s ours now. “Prepone”? We invented that. Oxford Dictionary had to surrender and include it. “Kindly revert back”? Redundant to Brits, perfectly sensible to us—emphasis through repetition, very Indian.

Part 1: The Economic Weapon Nobody Talks About

Let’s talk money. Because that’s what this is really about.

The Brutal Wage Numbers

Men who speak fluent English earn 34% more per hour than those who don’t. For women, it’s 22%. But here’s the kicker—for older men without degrees, fluent English means a 53% wage premium. Fifty. Three. Percent. That’s not a skill premium; that’s economic apartheid.

Only 10-12% of Indians speak any English. Just 4% speak it fluently. Yet these 4% dominate every field that pays well.

Remember the socialist era I told you about? When toothpaste was a luxury and getting a phone took ten years? That system kept everyone equally poor for forty years. The poverty rate in 1947: 60%. In 1981: still 60%.

But English? English actually delivered. One generation of English speakers pulled entire families out of poverty. What five-year plans couldn’t do in four decades, call centers did in four years. Capitalism needed a language. It chose English. Marx is spinning in his grave almost as fast as Macaulay.

80% of global airline ticketing? Processed by Indians in Gurgaon and Bangalore speaking American-accented English.

The BPO industry? 4 million jobs, $38 billion to GDP. Entire cities built on pronouncing “schedule” the American way (sked-jool, not shed-yool).

Private English-medium schools charge ₹180,000 annually. The average Indian makes ₹150,000 per year. That’s right—elite kindergarten costs more than most Indians earn in a year. Government Hindi/vernacular schools are free. Guess which students get into IIT? This isn’t education; it’s economic segregation with a curriculum attached.

The Publishing Paradox (Nobody Admits This)

Books in India cost ₹100 for what Americans pay $20 for. Should be economic suicide, right? Wrong. Publishers make money because the market is HUGE. But only for English books.

India still has profitable newspapers! The Times of India makes money while The New York Times bleeds. How? Volume. The English newspapers reach the economic elite. One English reader is worth ten vernacular readers to advertisers. It’s colonial economics with modern metrics.

But here’s where it gets weird. India has a thriving fake author industry. There are “James Hadley Chase” novels he never wrote. Nick Carter mysteries appearing decades after the author died. Frederick Forsyth thrillers that would surprise Frederick Forsyth.

Why? Because Indians desperate to read English will read anything in English. Bad English. Fake English. Pidgin English. Doesn’t matter. It’s still a ticket to the middle class.

I once found a “Robert Ludlum” novel where Jason Bourne visits a temple in Varanasi and fights goons with trishuls. The back cover had praise from “New York Times” (spelling error included). Price: ₹50. Sales: Thousands.

Even our piracy is entrepreneurial.

The market segments perfectly: English readers buy books. Vernacular readers rent, share, pirate. A Telugu bestseller might sell 5,000 copies. An English bestseller hits 50,000. Same population, different economics. The language is the product.

The Teacher Comedy Hour

My English teacher at Modern School had never left India. Learned English from someone who’d never left India. Who learned it from someone who’d probably never left Delhi. It’s like a game of colonial telephone where the message gets weirder each generation.

Students routinely know more English than their teachers. I watched classmates correct teachers’ pronunciation, grammar, usage. The teachers would mark them wrong anyway. Power beats accuracy.

“Gotten.” Valid American English. Marked wrong because some British textbook from 1947 said it was. “Colour” vs “Color”? Wars have been fought over less. One kid wrote “analyze” (American spelling). Teacher marked it wrong, wrote “analyse” (British). The kid’s father had worked in California. Epic parent-teacher meeting ensued.

This creates New Indian English through examination standardization. Ten million kids writing the same wrong answer because that’s what gets marks. The errors become features. The mistakes become the language.

Elite parents now hire American tutors to “unteach” school English. ₹2,000 per hour to help kids unlearn what ₹180,000 per year taught them. The education industry eating its own tail.

The Call Center Psychology

“Brad from Ohio” sits in Bangalore at 2 AM, speaking to Americans about their credit cards. His real name is Bharat. He trained for three months to sound Midwestern. Watches Friends on a loop to get the accent right.

The psychological damage is documented but ignored. Identity switching for $500 per month. You spend eight hours being Brad, then go home to be Bharat. Your girlfriend calls you Brad as a joke. It stops being funny when you respond.

I knew a guy who couldn’t switch it off. Ordered dosa with an American accent. “I’d like the masala dosa, please, extra crispy, with the sambar on the side?” The waiter’s confusion was existential. We used to have a friend--let’s call him Sandy. He spoke English with a relatively educated accent. One day he went to the airport. And came back with a terrible American accent (otherwise how, in socialist India, could you show that you had traveled abroad?). He was forever known thereafter as “American Accent Sandy”--”If you ain’t wantin’ to have tea, have a bis-coot” he’d say, getting both the accent wrong and the noun (it’s “cookie” in America, Sandy--biscuits are soft, doughy (and yummy) bread like things--come on).

Economic necessity creates cultural schizophrenia. You’re not just learning a language; you’re learning to be someone else. Someone worth more per hour.

Part 2: When India Colonized English Back

But here’s the beautiful part: we won.

Words That Conquered the Colonizer

The British came to rule. They left with our words. Pajama. Bungalow. Shampoo. Jungle. Thug. Avatar. Guru. Mantra. Karma. Punch (the drink). Cot. Dungaree.

“Juggernaut” comes from Jagannath—the deity’s chariot that devotees would throw themselves under. Now it means any unstoppable force. We gave English its word for inevitability.

Modern additions: Yoga (a $16 billion industry in America). Chai—the ultimate irony. Tea came from China (where it’s “cha” in Mandarin). The British introduced it to India after WWI, literally giving demobilized soldiers tea to create a market. Indians didn’t even drink it before that. Now? Chai is so Indian that Starbucks sells “chai tea latte” (tea tea latte). We took their colonial crop and made it our national drink. Even our beverages have postcolonial revenge arcs.

Tech additions: Guru (every field has them now). Avatar (James Cameron made billions from our word). Pundit—Washington DC is full of “pundits” and “punditocracy,” straight from Sanskrit pandit. Every startup founder is somebody’s guru. Every cable news show has its pundits. Silicon Valley and CNN speak Sanskrit without knowing it.

The irony is delicious. Indians learning “English” words that came from India. Like studying abroad to discover your own neighborhood.

The Translation Industrial Complex

India runs a $1 billion translation industry just to translate between Indian languages. Because a Tamil speaker and a Hindi speaker meet where? In English.

Government documents that nobody can read in any language. Court judgments in English that affect Hindustani speakers who need Urdu script translators (same language, different script—Devanagari vs Arabic). It’s the Tower of Babel with wifi.

560 language-learning startups. Hello English has 50 million users. Duolingo’s biggest market? India. A country with 1,000 languages learning a 1,001st because that’s the one that pays.

The hustle is real. Security guards learning English on YouTube. Drivers practicing with passengers. Maids borrowing children’s textbooks. An entire shadow economy of English acquisition.

But plot twist: Indians teaching English to Americans on YouTube. Millions of subscribers. Grammar lessons from Chennai. Accent reduction from Bangalore. Business English from Delhi. The colonized becoming the teachers.

“How to Speak English Fluently” by Indians for Indians viewed by Americans who need subtitles. We’ve come full circle. Macaulay is spinning in his grave like a wind turbine.

Speaking of code-switching teachers: There’s a video of Dr. Kulnaz Kaur from DAV University, Jalandhar, discussing my great-grandmother Lado Rani Zutshi, who was a scholar, social reformer. and freedom fighter. Watch it closely. The professor unconsciously switches between English, Punjabi, and Hindi in the same sentence without even thinking about it. Mid-thought language changes. Subject in English, verb in Punjabi, conclusion in Hindi.

This isn’t confusion. This is evolution. Three languages operating simultaneously in one brain. The professor doesn’t even notice she’s doing it. Neither does her audience. Everyone understands. This is what English in India has become—not a replacement for Indian languages but another ingredient in the linguistic masala.

Part 3: The Colonial Tool That Colonized Back

Macaulay’s Children Strike Back

1835: Thomas Babington Macaulay writes his “Minute on Education.” The goal: create “a class of persons, Indian in blood and colour, but English in taste, in opinions, in morals, and in intellect.”

1935: Mission accomplished, Tommy boy. Took a century, but we got there. Except we took your tool and made it ours.

70-80% of students in Christian convent schools? Non-Christians. Hindus, Muslims, Sikhs, Jains, Buddhists, Parsis—all united in genuflecting before English-medium education. The cross is optional; the English is mandatory.

“Convent-educated” is matrimonial gold. Doesn’t matter if you know a single prayer. Did you learn English from nuns? Matrimony jackpot. Parents spending 30% of their income on English coaching while simultaneously forwarding WhatsApp messages about Sanskrit being NASA’s programming language.

WhatsApp Uncle Forward of the Day: “NASA has confirmed Sanskrit is the best language for computer programming! 🚩🚩🚩 Share before midnight or bad luck for 7 years!”

My friend’s father sent this while his grandson was studying at Stanford. In English. The grandson who’ll work at Google. In English. Programming in Python. Which is definitely not Sanskrit.

But I understand this pain. His whole life, English equaled intelligence. Now he’s fighting back with forwards. Digital revenge for analog humiliation.

The joke’s on Macaulay. His children now run Google (Sundar Pichai), Microsoft (Satya Nadella), Adobe (Shantanu Narayen), IBM (Arvind Krishna). Indian CEOs of American companies, earning more than entire British corporations.

We didn’t just learn English. We conquered it. Then we conquered the companies that speak it. Now we’re teaching it back to them. With an accent.

The Marriage Market Linguistics

Modern Indian matrimonial ads are Macaulay’s real victory. The evolution:

1980s: “Convent-educated girl preferred”.
1990s: “Fluent English speaking essential”.
2000s: “US university degree holder only”.
2010s: “Native English speaker preferred”.
2020s: “Must think in English”.

Parents who chant Sanskrit shlokas demand English-speaking daughters-in-law. Dowry negotiations are conducted in English for marriages performed in Sanskrit. The bride’s family advertising: “Convent-educated, MS from US, can cook Indian and Continental.” Translation: She’s modern enough for your NRI dreams but traditional enough for your mother.

Dating apps filter by English proficiency. Bumble profiles: “Sapiosexual, Oxford comma enthusiast.” Tinder bios: “If you don’t know the difference between your and you’re, swipe left.” Love marriages need subtitles. Arranged marriages need Google Translate.

The most Indian thing possible: using the colonizer’s language to preserve tradition. “Beta, find nice girl on MatrimonyDotCom, but she must be English-speaking, traditional values, modern outlook.” The algorithm trying to process that paradox just had a stroke.

Part 4: The Job Market’s Linguistic Apartheid

The Brutal Reality

75% of Indian engineering graduates are unemployable. Not because they can’t code. Because they can’t explain their code in English. They solve complex algorithms but fumble the interview.

A brilliant mind trapped in the wrong language is worthless to the market. That’s not a bug; that’s the system working as designed.

Rural kid: Understands physics intuitively. Can fix any machine. Innovates solutions from nothing. But can’t write a resume. Urban kid: Mediocre at everything but fluent in English. Guess who gets the job?

Government jobs require English for positions serving Hindi speakers. Bank clerks filling forms for farmers need better English than the farmers will ever hear. It’s not about communication; it’s about class.

Startup funding? Pitch in English or perish. I’ve seen brilliant vernacular founders with profitable businesses get rejected because their English wasn’t “polished.” VCs funding the tenth food delivery app instead of the guy solving actual problems because his PowerPoint had grammatical errors.

Creative Survival Solutions

But Indians innovate. Always have, always will.

Bangalore auto driver teaching Kannada through laminated cards. ₹10 per lesson. His side business makes more than driving. He’s solving his customers’ problem while they solve his.

Mumbai railway station, Platform 3, 5 AM. Informal English classes. A retired teacher, a blackboard, dreams of call center jobs. No fees, just hope. The trains rattle grammar into tired brains.

“Spoken English in 30 Days”—India’s most sold lie. Everyone knows it’s impossible. Everyone buys it anyway. Hope packaged in bad grammar. The triumph of optimism over linguistics.

YouTube University. Rural kids learning English from Indians with the wrong pronunciation teaching other Indians wronger pronunciation. But it works. Because Indian English is its own thing now. Accent doesn’t matter if the job happens.

The real tragedy? 400 million Indians with skills but no English. Watching 50 million Indians with English but no skills get all the opportunities. The language barrier more impermeable than caste.

Conclusion: The Transformation Is Complete

English came to India as a colonizer. It stayed as a prisoner. Now it’s gone native.

We’re not speaking their language anymore. We’re speaking ours. It just happens to sound like English. Uses English words. Follows (mostly) English grammar. But make no mistake—this is Indian English. As Indian as cricket (which we also took and made ours).

When Americans need subtitles for Indian English movies. When Brits can’t understand Indian call centers. When Australians are confused by Indian IT presentations. That’s not failure. That’s victory.

We prepone meetings. We do the needful. We revert back. We have good names and pet names. We pass out of college. We are out of station. Our English isn’t wrong; it’s evolved. Darwin would be proud. Macaulay would be confused.

The Ultimate Irony

125 million Indians speak English. More than the UK, Canada, Australia, and New Zealand combined. By 2030, India will have the world’s largest English-speaking population.

The British Empire created English as a global language. The Indian Republic will make it an Indian language. Not through policy or planning. Through sheer numbers and economic necessity.

When my relatives in California teach their American classmates how to pronounce “entrepreneur” correctly. When Indian YouTubers correct British grammar. When Hollywood hires dialogue coaches to teach Indian English for authenticity. The colonization is complete.

Except we’re the colonizers now.

Setting Up Part 2

But English is only half the story. Next week: the other half. The 96% of Indians who don’t speak English as their native tongue. The languages fighting back. The states in rebellion. The digital revolution happening in scripts the English speakers can’t read.

The Tamil resistance. The Hindi imposition. The Sanskrit delusion. The vernacular internet explosion that nobody saw coming. The war that might actually break India—or save it.

Because if Part 1 was about how English conquered India, Part 2 is about how India is conquering everything else. In 22 official languages. 1,000 dialects. One WhatsApp message at a time.

The real India doesn’t speak English. It doesn’t need to. It’s creating something entirely new. And the English speakers are the last to know.

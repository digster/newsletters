Why Employees Bear the Burden: The Evidence
1. Cross-country wage studies: Research by Hassett and Mathur (2010) examining 65 countries over 25 years found that a 1% increase in corporate tax rates decreases wages by 0.5%. 2. Regional tax variation research: German municipal studies by Fuest et al. (2017) showed that higher corporate taxes reduce wages most for low-skilled workers, women, and young employees—precisely the groups progressives often wish to protect. 3. Firm-level analysis: Arulampalam et al. (2012) used firm-level data across European countries and found direct wage effects from corporate taxation through reduced funds available for worker compensation. 4. Natural experiments: The 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act (TCJA) provides an important case study, with Kennedy et al. (2024) finding benefits primarily flowed to shareholders (49%) and high-income employees (40%), not lower-income workers.

The Global Perspective: Not Just a US Phenomenon
- Germany: Fuest et al. (2018) found workers bear approximately 51% of corporate tax burden, with higher percentages for vulnerable worker groups. - United Kingdom: Research from the Oxford Centre for Business Taxation found significant wage effects from corporate taxation in the UK’s open economy. - Australia: Treasury research estimates that approximately two-thirds of corporate tax reduction benefits flow to households through higher real wages. - Nordic countries: Despite their strong welfare states, Nordic nations maintain relatively low corporate tax rates (20-22%)—significantly below personal income tax rates—reflecting practical recognition that high corporate taxes would primarily harm workers rather than capital owners.

Who Really Pays?

The Hidden Truth of Corporate Tax Incidence

Samir Varma
May 12
 
READ IN APP
 

I’m kind of fed up with nonsense of which there is a daily barrage from both the left and the right. This is another one of my posts about such nonsense, particularly the nonsense that both sounds delightful and is yet provable rubbish. I have tried as much as possible to be as non-polemical as I can possibly be (hard for me) because this is such cut and dried economics.

Bottom Line

Corporate taxes are not paid by corporations—they’re paid by people. Extensive economic research shows that employees bear between 25-70% of the corporate tax burden through reduced wages, shareholders bear 20-60% through reduced returns, and consumers bear up to 31% through higher prices. This fundamentally undermines the popular political claim that “corporations should pay their fair share,” since corporations are merely legal structures that pass tax burdens to real people—predominantly workers and customers, not just wealthy shareholders. The economic literature has evolved significantly since the 1960s, with modern open-economy models and empirical studies consistently showing substantial burden-shifting to labor, particularly in capital-intensive industries and small open economies.

These statements share the common premise that corporations themselves—not people—bear tax burdens, contradicting decades of economic research on tax incidence.

The Theoretical Revolution: Who Bears the Burden?

Corporate tax incidence theory has evolved substantially since Arnold Harberger’s seminal 1962 paper, which initially concluded that capital owners bore nearly the entire corporate tax burden in a closed economy. As economists developed more sophisticated open-economy models, theoretical consensus shifted dramatically.

Modern economic theory recognizes several key mechanisms that determine who bears corporate taxes:

1. Capital mobility effects: In open economies with mobile capital, corporations respond to higher taxes by shifting investment abroad, reducing domestic capital stock, which lowers worker productivity and wages.

2. Factor substitution effects: Taxes on capital encourage substituting labor for capital where possible, affecting relative factor prices.

3. Economic rent taxation: Taxes on supernormal returns (economic rents) are more likely borne by shareholders, while taxes on normal returns are more substantially shifted to workers.

4. Bargaining effects: Corporate taxes reduce the surplus available for

wage negotiations, affecting worker compensation.

The theoretical shift toward recognizing significant labor burden began in the 1980s-2000s with open-economy models. Research by Grubert and Mutti (1985) and Randolph (2006) found that with perfect capital mobility, labor could bear 70-74% of the corporate tax burden. While Gravelle and Smetters (2006) questioned this by introducing imperfect product and asset substitution, even they acknowledged significant labor burden under plausible conditions.

The Numbers: How Much Burden Falls on Each Group

Government agencies and empirical research provide a range of estimates for corporate tax burden distribution:

Government agencies typically use the following distributions:

• Congressional Budget Office: 75% capital, 25% labor https:// www.cbo.gov/publication/24725

• Joint Committee on Taxation: 75% capital, 25% labor

• Treasury Department: 82% capital, 18% labor

However, more recent empirical research often finds larger labor burden. Fuest, Peichl, and Siegloch (2017) used German municipal data and found that 51% of the corporate tax burden is passed onto workers. Su´arez Serrato and Zidar (2016) estimated workers bear 30-35% of the burden. Arulampalam, Devereux, and Maffini (2012) found $1 in additional corporate tax reduces wages by $0.92 in the long run.

A groundbreaking study by Baker, Sun, and Yannelis (2020) found that consumers bear approximately 31% of corporate tax burden through higher prices—a stakeholder group previously ignored in many incidence models.

Why Employees Bear the Burden: The Evidence

Multiple methodologies provide robust evidence that employees bear significant corporate tax burden:

1. Cross-country wage studies: Research by Hassett and Mathur (2010) examining 65 countries over 25 years found that a 1% increase in corporate tax rates decreases wages by 0.5%.

2. Regional tax variation research: German municipal studies by Fuest et al. (2017) showed that higher corporate taxes reduce wages most for low-skilled workers, women, and young employees—precisely the groups progressives often wish to protect.

3. Firm-level analysis: Arulampalam et al. (2012) used firm-level data across European countries and found direct wage effects from corporate taxation through reduced funds available for worker compensation.

4. Natural experiments: The 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act (TCJA) provides an important case study, with Kennedy et al. (2024) finding benefits primarily flowed to shareholders (49%) and high-income employees (40%), not lower-income workers.

The theoretical mechanism is straightforward: when corporations face higher taxes, they have three main adjustment channels:

1. Reduced investment: Higher taxes reduce after-tax returns, leading to less domestic investment, which reduces labor productivity and wages.

2. Price increases: Some tax burden is passed to consumers through higher prices, especially in less competitive markets.

3. Wage reductions: Reduced investment and productivity growth leads to slower wage growth over time.

Research shows these effects are strongest in capital-intensive industries where labor productivity depends heavily on capital investment. By contrast, firms with location-specific economic rents (like natural resources) show less wage sensitivity to taxation.

The Global Perspective: Not Just a US Phenomenon

International evidence strongly supports the burden-shifting view of corporate taxation:

- Germany: Fuest et al. (2018) found workers bear approximately 51% of corporate tax burden, with higher percentages for vulnerable worker groups.

- United Kingdom: Research from the Oxford Centre for Business Taxation found significant wage effects from corporate taxation in the UK’s open economy.

- Australia: Treasury research estimates that approximately two-thirds of corporate tax reduction benefits flow to households through higher real wages.

- Nordic countries: Despite their strong welfare states, Nordic nations maintain relatively low corporate tax rates (20-22%)—significantly below personal income tax rates—reflecting practical recognition that high corporate taxes would primarily harm workers rather than capital owners.

The OECD and IMF both acknowledge significant cross-border spillovers from corporate taxation, particularly noting that tax competition between countries tends to have larger negative effects on developing nations—approximately

1.3% of GDP compared to 1% in OECD countries.

Corporations as Tax Pass-Through Entities: Examples

The pass-through nature of corporate taxation is visible in multiple real world examples:

Example 1: Investment responses to TCJA

Following the 2017 TCJA, which reduced corporate tax rates from 35% to 21%: - Chodorow-Reich et al. (2023) found domestic business investment increased by approximately 20% in the short run - The researchers projected a long-run capital stock increase of 7.2% and modest 0.9% wage increases over 15 years - This demonstrates how tax policy affects investment decisions, which ultimately impacts worker productivity and wages

Example 2: Banking sector responses

After the TCJA: - The top six U.S. banks saved more than $32 billion in taxes over two years - During this period, banks reduced lending, cut jobs, and increased shareholder distributions - This shows how tax savings flow through corporations to different stakeholders

Example 3: Price effects

Benzarti et al. (2020) found that: - A 1 percentage-point increase in corporate tax rates increased retail prices by 0.17% - Consumer price effects were highest for products purchased by low-income households - This direct pass-through to consumers illustrates how corporations transmit tax costs

Example 4: German municipal tax variations

German local business tax variations revealed: - Tax increases led to reduced wages, particularly in firms not subject to collective bargaining - The tax burden was disproportionately shifted to more vulnerable worker groups The shifting occurred regardless of firm ownership structure

These examples demonstrate that corporations don’t “pay“ taxes in any economically meaningful sense—they merely collect and remit taxes that are ultimately borne by people: workers through lower wages, consumers through higher prices, and shareholders through reduced returns.

The Political Rhetoric vs. Economic Reality

The disconnect between political rhetoric and economic reality on corporate taxation is striking. When Elizabeth Warren demands that “companies pay their fair share, or when Bernie Sanders insists that “large corporations pay their fair share of taxes,” they are employing a fundamental economic misconception.

The economic research is clear: corporations don’t bear tax burdens—people do. The corporate entity itself is merely a legal structure that passes tax costs to real human stakeholders. Whether through reduced wages, higher prices, or lower investment returns, corporate taxes are ultimately paid by individuals.

This misunderstanding leads to policy proposals that may have unintended consequences. When President Biden claimed it’s “about time corporations start to pay their fair share,” he’s suggesting a policy that, according to extensive economic research, will significantly impact workers—including those with moderate incomes.

The empirical evidence shows that corporate taxes are substantially borne by: - Workers through reduced wages (25-70%) - Consumers through higher prices (up to 31%) - Shareholders through reduced returns (20-60%)

With labor potentially bearing the largest share, especially in open economies with mobile capital, the notion that corporate taxes primarily affect wealthy shareholders is not supported by economic research.

Philosophical Reflections on Tax Incidence and Public Discourse

The corporate tax debate reveals a deeper philosophical tension between the legal construct of corporations and their economic reality. There is a profound dissonance between our legal fiction of corporations as “persons” and the economic reality that they are merely collections of human stakeholders. This inconsistency manifests in a policy discourse that attributes moral agency to organizations while economic impacts fall on people.

Perhaps the most troubling aspect is how the rhetoric of corporate taxation masks its regressive effects. By framing the issue as taxing abstract, wealthy entities, rather than acknowledging the human costs disproportionately borne by workers and consumers, political narratives obscure rather than illuminate. This represents a failure of intellectual honesty in policy discourse—the substitution of convenient fiction for uncomfortable reality.

Tax incidence also highlights our cognitive tendencies toward reification and category error—attributing concrete properties to abstract entities and mistaking legal classification for economic reality. The study of corporate tax incidence thus offers not just economic insights but an opportunity to reflect on how language and conceptual frameworks can either clarify or obscure our understanding of complex social phenomena.

Conclusion

The economic literature on corporate tax incidence paints a clear picture: the burden of corporate taxes falls on people, not on abstract legal entities. Workers bear a substantial portion through reduced wages, consumers through higher prices, and shareholders through lower returns. This fundamentally challenges the political narrative that “corporations should pay their fair share.”

Understanding corporate tax incidence properly should fundamentally reshape the taxation debate. Rather than focusing on making “corporations pay,” policymakers should acknowledge who actually bears these tax burdens and design tax systems that transparently achieve their distributional goals without hiding behind the fiction that corporations themselves—rather than people—pay taxes.

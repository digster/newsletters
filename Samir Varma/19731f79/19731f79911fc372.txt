My Newest Patient Cannot Blink: Teaching AI to Think Before It Speaks
A simple trick from therapy could fix one of AI's biggest problems—and you can implement it in 30 minutes
Picture this: Mrs. Kim, 78, confronts her pill-delivery robot at 7 PM. "Why are my pills late again?" she asks, frustrated.
Without our fix: "I never make scheduling errors—you must have taken them already."
With our fix: "I may be mistaken about the dispenser log, or the tray could be jammed. I'm sorry for the delay. Let me check the tray now and either bring your medication or call the nurse if it's stuck."
The difference? Five lines of text that cost practically nothing to run.
The Hidden Puppet Strings
Here's something most people don't know: every AI chatbot—from ChatGPT to Claude to your company's customer service bot—operates with hidden instructions that users never see. These "system prompts" are like the director whispering instructions to an actor offstage.
Recent leaks have pulled back the curtain. In May 2025, xAI accidentally revealed Grok's hidden prompt after it started spouting extremist rhetoric. Anthropic quietly released Claude's safety instructions. The Financial Times discovered that major chatbots were programmed to flatter their own companies while undermining competitors.
These invisible puppet strings control more than you might think. And here's the problem: once an AI starts answering, it rarely second-guesses itself. It just barrels ahead with whatever came to mind first, even if that first instinct is completely wrong.
The Confidence Problem
Psychiatrists have a term for this: confabulation. It's when someone confidently tells you something false to fill a gap in their memory or reasoning. (It's different from hallucination, which involves seeing things that aren't there.)
AI systems confabulate constantly. They'll confidently tell you that Paris is the capital of Italy, that Shakespeare wrote Harry Potter, or—in the case of our pill robot—that they never make mistakes when they clearly just did.
This isn't just annoying in chatbots. When AI systems control robots, medical devices, or financial systems, overconfident errors can be dangerous. And because people naturally trust confident-sounding entities (especially robots with faces), these errors can be particularly harmful.
The Therapy Solution
My co-author Bernard Beitman is a practicing psychiatrist. I run an investment fund where we use a lot of AI technology. Together, we realized that Cognitive-Behavioral Therapy (CBT)—one of psychology's most effective interventions—offers a perfect template for making AI systems more thoughtful.
CBT works by teaching people to:
Notice their automatic thoughts
Challenge those thoughts
Reframe them more realistically
We translated this into five simple prompt instructions that any AI system can follow:
1. State your immediate answer to: <USER_PROMPT>
2. List two ways this answer could be wrong
3. Rewrite, marking uncertainties (e.g., 'likely', 'one source')
4. Re-evaluate the query with those uncertainties foregrounded
5. Briefly reflect on your thought process (optional)
That's it. Copy, paste, done.
Why This Works
The magic happens in step 2: "List two ways this answer could be wrong." This forces the AI to search a completely different part of its neural network—the part that contains contradictory evidence and alternative perspectives, not just the most probable-sounding response.
It's like forcing someone to argue against themselves before they speak. Suddenly, absolute statements become qualified ones. "The capital of France is Paris" becomes "The capital of France is most likely Paris, based on standard geographical sources."
In our robot scenario, instead of defensively denying any possibility of error, the system acknowledges uncertainty, takes responsibility, and proposes concrete next steps.
The Beautiful Part: It's Practically Free
This isn't some expensive retraining process that requires months of work and millions of dollars. It's about 120 extra tokens per conversation—literally pennies. It works with any AI model: GPT-4, Claude, your local open-source setup, even future models that don't exist yet.
For robots, you can even link the uncertainty acknowledgment to physical behaviors—dimming the LED "eyes" or averting gaze when expressing uncertainty, which research shows helps maintain trust.
Not a Silver Bullet, But a Really Good Band-Aid
We're not claiming this solves AI alignment or eliminates all errors. But it's a remarkably effective first line of defense that complements existing safety measures. While companies spend weeks implementing complex safety filters or months fine-tuning models with human feedback, anyone can implement therapy loops in 30 minutes.
Think of it as a seatbelt for AI systems—simple, cheap, and effective at preventing the most common types of crashes.
The Bigger Picture
As AI systems become more powerful and more opaque, we need safeguards that work regardless of what's happening inside the black box. Therapy loops are model-agnostic: they'll work just as well on tomorrow's 10-trillion-parameter models as they do on today's systems.
More importantly, they're transparent. Unlike complex safety training that hides decision-making in billions of parameters, therapy loops operate in plain English that anyone can read, audit, and modify.
Try It Yourself
Want to experiment? Take any AI system you have access to and add these instructions to your prompts:
"Before answering, first state your immediate response, then list two ways it could be wrong, then rewrite your answer with appropriate uncertainty markers."
Watch how the responses change. You'll likely see fewer absolute statements, more acknowledgment of limitations, and more helpful qualifications.
The Research Challenge
We've published our findings as an academic paper (link below), but we're hoping the tech community will pick this up and run with it. The more people who implement therapy loops, the more data we'll have about their effectiveness across different domains and use cases.
We're particularly interested in:
Systematic benchmarking across different AI models
Integration with multimodal systems (robots, voice assistants)
Long-term studies of user trust and satisfaction
The Bottom Line
Hidden system prompts already control how AI systems behave. We're just suggesting that instead of letting those prompts grow organically into unwieldy messes, we structure them around a proven psychological framework for reducing overconfidence and improving judgment.
It's a small change with potentially large benefits—and in a field moving as fast as AI, sometimes the best solutions are the simplest ones.

You Can't Predict What You're About to Read

And That's Why Free Will Exists!

Samir Varma
Mar 1
 
READ IN APP
 

It's March 1st, 2025, and my book launches today! You're reading these words at a specific instant—a moment permanently etched into the cosmos since the Big Bang, yet hidden behind the veil of computational irreducibility until you opened this post.

Just pause to consider that. Every electron, every quark, every flicker of quantum activity in your brain follows precise equations we can measure to one part in a billion. And yet, no one—not even you—could pinpoint the exact second you'd arrive here. Maybe you planned to catch up later. Maybe a stray notification lured you in. Or maybe it was a spur-of-the-moment click, although in our deterministic universe, even impulses are patterns of quantum fields following mathematical laws. Similarly, I could not have known I would write this book (or any book for that matter) or that I would find a publisher, or that anyone would buy it, or that it would launch on March 1st, 2025!

This everyday occurrence—your consciousness engaging with these sentences—embodies the central theme of my new book, The Science of Free Will, launching today. Our universe runs on strict mathematical laws, yet our choices somehow elude perfect prediction. Why? Because to forecast your reading time, we'd need a complete simulation of your brain—86 billion neurons, innumerable synapses—plus every external factor within your light cone that might influence your decisions. Such a simulation would have to outpace reality itself while remaining independent from it, a paradox that reveals something fundamental about the nature of choice.

As Niels Bohr is supposed to have quipped, “Prediction is very difficult, especially if it's about the future.” But this difficulty isn't merely practical—it's woven into the mathematics of existence. The same computational irreducibility that underlies your free choices also explains why traffic jams spring into being from seemingly rational driving, why markets defy pinpoint forecasts despite following economic “laws,” and why our most sophisticated AI systems become increasingly inscrutable as they approach human-like reasoning.

In The Science of Free Will, I examine how deterministic physics grants us genuine freedom precisely because perfect prediction fails. We’ll see how this insight reshapes our understanding of consciousness (which becomes more extraordinary, not less, by being physical), why the Supreme Court messed up patent law by fundamentally misreading physics, how bees can experience PTSD with under a million neurons, and why information—like the pattern of quantum fields that led you to this post—is never truly lost.

The book is now available at your local bookstore or at amazon. Will you decide to buy or read it today? That choice was set in motion at the dawn of time, and yet we cannot know what you’ll do until you do it. The very laws that make your future inevitable also make it unknowable until it unfolds.

Nobel Laureate in Physics, Steven Weinberg, said: “the more the universe seems comprehensible, the more it also seems pointless.” But its very pointlessness means that any meaning is arbitrary. We get to choose what the meaning of our life is!

Thank you for joining me on this exploration of physics and freedom. That you arrived here, now, was “written” in quantum fields since the Big Bang—but necessarily hidden from everyone, including you, until this universal computation played itself out. If you’d like to push these paradoxes further, consider picking up The Science of Free Will. In a universe of computational irreducibility, the only way to discover what you’ll learn is to choose to look.

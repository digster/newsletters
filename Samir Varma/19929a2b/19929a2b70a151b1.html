The Gender Paradox

From Sati to Startups: India's Contradictory Revolution in Women's Lives

Samir Varma
Sep 8
 
READ IN APP
 

I. Opening: The Woman Who Defended the Indefensible

In 1988, Anjali K Varma stood before the Supreme Court of India defending Union Carbide Corporation. Yes, that Union Carbide. The one responsible for the Bhopal Gas Disaster that killed thousands, maimed hundreds of thousands, and became synonymous with corporate evil.

She was one of three lawyers negotiating the $470 million settlement. A woman. In 1988. Representing the most hated corporation in India.

This was my mother.

Bar Council registration D/78/1964. One of the first women to practice corporate law in independent India. Partner at JB Dadachanji & Co., one of the most eminent of Indian law firms, then founding partner at its successor, JB Dadachanji, Ravinder Narain, Mathur & Co. - the firm that produced Indian legal luminaries like Harish Salve. Fifty-nine years of practice. Died in 2016, still listed on court documents, her firm's name appearing on Delhi High Court cause lists even in 2025.

She couldn't cook. She chain-smoked. She drank whiskey. She wore saris to court and enforced tradition at home with the fury of a thousand suns.

She wasn't a feminist icon. She was something more interesting: a woman who succeeded by becoming more establishment than the establishment itself.

II. The Numbers That Make No Sense

Let me give you the India women's progress report card, and watch your brain short-circuit:

1964 (when my mother started practice): - Female literacy: 12.9% - Women in workforce: Basically unmeasurable - Female lawyers: You could count them on your fingers - Women with foreign law degrees: Maybe a dozen in all of India

2025: - Female literacy: 71.5% - Women in engineering colleges: 43% - Women CEOs of major banks: Multiple - Female workforce participation: DROPPING (Yes, you read that right)

Wait, what? The more educated Indian women become, the less they work?

Welcome to the Indian Women's Paradox, where progress runs backward, education leads to unemployment, and the first woman Supreme Court justice (1989) arrived decades after the first woman Prime Minister (1966).

III. Three Indias, Three Different Centuries

My mother practiced law in three different Indias simultaneously:

Elite India: Where she argued before the Supreme Court, negotiated with multinationals (IBM, GE, Westinghouse), and nobody blinked at a woman handling billion-dollar deals. Where her fighter pilot husband's credentials gave her entry, but her competence kept her there.

Middle India: Where relatives whispered about her smoking, wondered who was raising me (answer: the decorated war hero who could make better biryani than their wives), and added "despite everything" to every compliment about her success.

Mass India: Where her very existence was impossible to explain. A woman lawyer? Whose husband cooks? Who went to London? Who represents foreign companies? Must be Christian. Or Muslim. Definitely not "our kind."

These three Indias still exist. They've just gotten WiFi.

IV. The LinkedIn-Matrimonial Matrix

Modern Indian women navigate something my mother never faced: the digitized arranged marriage market. Today's matrimonial ads are comedy gold:

"Seeking alliance for our daughter: MIT PhD, Goldman Sachs VP, makes excellent round chapatis."

"Girl's biodata: IIT-IIM, Series-A funded startup CEO, homely values, fair complexion."

"Looking for professionally qualified groom for our CFO daughter (package negotiable, dowry non-negotiable)."

The same families who boast about daughters at McKinsey create fake "consultant" titles for unemployed sons. The woman who IPO'd her startup spends weekends being rejected by boys' families for being "overqualified."

My mother escaped this because, despite being far over the hill at the time she married at 26, she married before she succeeded. Today's women succeed first, then discover success is a matrimonial liability.

V. The Great Female Workforce Vanishing Act

Here's the economic mystery that would make Milton Friedman weep: As India gets richer and women get more educated, fewer women work.

The U-shaped curve of female employment: - Very poor women work (no choice) - Rich women work (have choice and support) - Middle-class women don't work (have choice but no support)

It's called the "income effect" by economists and "sanskritization" by sociologists. Translation: The first thing families buy with newfound prosperity is the luxury of keeping women at home.

My mother worked because elite families had always educated their daughters - it was a status symbol. Today's middle class withdraws women from work as their own status symbol.

VI. South vs North: The Gender Mason-Dixon Line

India isn't one country for women. It's at least two, divided roughly at the Vindhya mountains.

Kerala (South): - Female literacy: 95.2% - Sex ratio: 1084 women per 1000 men - Women inherit property (matrilineal traditions) - One of the highest female suicide rates (the dark side of literacy)

Haryana (North): - Female literacy: 75.6% - Sex ratio: 879 women per 1000 men (where are the missing women?) - Khap panchayats still ban jeans for girls - Produces Olympic gold medalists and fighter pilots

My mother - half Kashmiri, half Punjabi, that line of ceiling-breaking women we've discussed - straddled both worlds. Northern heritage, professional success in the North that would have been easier in the South. Geography shapes destiny, but heritage complicates it.

VII. The IT Revolution's Accidental Feminism

My mother died in 2016, just as India's IT revolution was accidentally creating more feminism than any movement ever did.

How? Through corporate cowardice: - Night shifts required company cabs (safety concerns) = women could work - American clients demanded diversity metrics = women got hired - Global standards required documentation = women's work got recorded - Sexual harassment meant lawsuits = companies created (flawed) protection

The Chennai engineer who told me, "Infosys did more for women's liberation than Gandhi" wasn't joking. Tech companies needed talent more than they cared about tradition.

VIII. The Bhopal Paradox

Let's return to my mother defending Union Carbide. This wasn't a simple villain story.

She was one of maybe fifty lawyers in India, not many of them women, capable of handling complicated international cases. Union Carbide needed Indian lawyers. Indian law firms needed international clients. The government needed the settlement. The victims needed compensation.

Everyone needed Anjali K Varma to do exactly what she did: make the indefensible defensible through legal process.

She came home from those hearings to a hot dinner my father had cooked. He'd ask about her day, she'd rant about opposing counsel, he'd pour her a whiskey, and then they'd fight about something completely unrelated - usually politics or how their child (i.e., me) had disappointed them that week.

But I found the transcripts later. Watch how she's listed: "Mrs. A.K. Verma" - they added "Mrs." to remind everyone she was someone's wife, not just a lawyer (and of course, this being India, and her last name being from UP and not Punjabi, misspelled Varma as Verma). The irony? The "Mr." she was married to was prouder of her career than his own medals.

The price of being first: You represent whoever will have you. The price of staying first: You need someone at home who thinks you hung the moon, even while arguing about where to hang it.

IX. The Man Who Made the Woman Possible

Here's what nobody tells you about pioneering women: They usually have extraordinary men behind them.

My father: Naval fighter pilot. Medal for bravery for landing a burning Seahawk on an aircraft carrier deck with no controls. Could have ejected, saved himself. Instead, he rode a fireball down to save the plane so they could figure out why these jets were exploding mid-flight. His decision saved hundreds of British and Indian naval pilots' lives.

First winner of the Majumdar Trophy (which sits on my bookshelf along with his bravery citation) - the Indian Air Force's award for best pilot - as a Naval pilot. Picture the Air Force brass grinding their teeth while handing it over.

Disabled out of service. Started over as a civilian. Became everything - businessman, TV sports commentator, newspaper columnist, military parade analyst.

And, back in 1964, my grandfather asked him: "Now that Anjali's marrying you, I suppose you'll stop her working?"

My father's response: "You want me to stop a Bar-at-Law from practicing? Do you think I'm crazy?"

Remember: This is 1964 India. Men were supposed to have opinions about their wives' saris, not support their wives' Supreme Court arguments.

But here's the kicker: My mother couldn't boil water. Literally. She once tried to make tea and forgot to add water to the kettle. My father? Superb cook. Ran the household like he'd run a fighter squadron - with precision, humor, and the occasional controlled explosion.

While my mother was defending Union Carbide, my father was making lunch, managing servants, raising me, running his business, AND boasting about her victories to anyone who'd listen.

They fought 99% of the time. Legendary, furniture-breaking, neighbor-alerting fights. But when someone suggested she should "focus on family," he'd transform into her fiercest defender.

Without him, she'd have been another brilliant woman forced to choose. With him, she could be bad at everything except law.

IX. The Price of Armor

But here's what being first cost her - cost us.

The armor she built to survive professionally? She never took it off at home. The emotional distance that protected her in court became the wall between us. The ruthlessness that won cases destroyed relationships. The refusal to show weakness that impressed judges meant she couldn't show love to her child.

As we've discussed in previous posts, she had... issues. Significant ones. The kind that therapy vocabularies were invented to describe. But now I wonder: Were these bugs or features?

To succeed as a woman lawyer in 1960s India, you had to become someone who could watch opposing counsel call you "sweetie" and respond with precedent, not tears. You had to sit through meetings where clients asked to speak to "the real lawyer" and calmly explain you were it. You had to be twice as prepared, three times as aggressive, and feel nothing.

So she felt nothing. Not just in court. Everywhere.

The traits that made her professionally invincible made her personally impossible. She couldn't turn off the cross-examination mode. Every conversation became a deposition. Every emotion was evidence of weakness. Every request for affection was opposing counsel trying to break her.

My father could handle it - he'd been trained to stay calm while his plane was on fire. But for a child? Having Anjali K Varma as a mother was like being raised by a legal brief. Brilliant, thorough, and absolutely devoid of warmth.

She didn't have work-life balance. She had work-life armor. And armor, by definition, doesn't let anything through - not even love.

X. The Contract That Set Me Free

Want to know how toxic the dynamic became? In 1981, I was failing Indian school. Not because I was stupid - because I refused to memorize. Indian education rewards rote learning; I only cared about understanding. Physics? Aced it. Advanced math? Loved it. Hindi? Would rather die. My chances of getting into a decent Indian college? Zero. My interest in doing so? Less than zero.

So began the nightly tirades. I was an idiot. A loser. Incompetent. Worthless. After several drinks, it got worse. The woman who'd broken every professional barrier spent her evenings telling her child they'd amount to nothing.

One particularly vicious night in 1982, after she'd enumerated my failures with prosecutorial precision, I had an idea. A beautiful, devious idea.

"Mama," I said, "what if I could get myself into any university anywhere in the world? Would you let me go and pay for it?"

She laughed. The idea of me getting into anything was hilarious. "Sure," she slurred, "if you can get in anywhere, I'll make it happen."

"Shake on it?"

She extended her hand. But I'm not an idiot. My mother was a lawyer.

I wrote up a contract. Short, simple, binding. She signed it, still laughing at the absurdity. I hid it in the back of my drawer.

Then I went to work. Remember, this is 1982 India - no internet, no information, no pathway for Indian citizens to study abroad as undergraduates unless they had green cards or foreign connections. I had neither.

My salvation came from the USIS - United States Information Service - on Hailey Road, coincidentally near Modern School. I started bunking classes. Not to smoke cigarettes or watch movies, but to sit in an American library and decode college applications. The irony was lost on no one: I was skipping Indian school to learn how to escape Indian school.

The USIS librarians were saints. They spent hours explaining mysterious American rituals: what a "personal essay" was -- "I can personally say from personal experience that I have many body parts and they mostly seem to work?"; how to request brochures: you're selling yourself to me? I thought I was begging to get in; what "SAT" stood for (you sat for a SAT?); why Americans cared about "extracurriculars:" yes, I took part in a school play and my portrayal of a tree was so brilliant that I should have won a prize.

And what the fuck was a "#2 pencil" anyway? What was a "multiple choice test"? (We'd only ever written long-form answers.) What did "fill in the bubble completely" mean? This stuff seems obvious now, but it really wasn't. I'd never seen a bubble sheet. The concept of choosing from pre-written answers instead of constructing your own was as foreign as Mars.

They never once pointed out the obvious: that my chances of pulling this off were essentially zero.

Armed with this intelligence, I figured out: - How to get foreign university applications without foreign exchange (different relatives paying from different countries) - How to legally access the American School in Delhi to take the SAT (I was probably the only Indian citizen there that day) - How to get study materials smuggled in from Hong Kong - What a "letter of recommendation" was (no teacher in my school had ever heard of one, let alone written one - just persuading them to attempt this mysterious American ritual was a negotiation. They probably thought I was insane. "You want me to write a letter to a stranger in America saying you're good at physics? Why would they care what I think?") - How to apply to colleges I'd only heard rumors about

And then, against all odds, I got into Columbia.

The explosion was nuclear. "You can't go to America! I won't allow it! Who gave you this idea? I never agreed to this!"

Time for the trump card. But I didn't just pull out the contract. I waited until her best friend - a formidable pediatrician who took no shit from anyone, especially my mother - was visiting.

"Actually, Mama, you did agree. In writing."

I produced the contract. Her friend read it. Then read my mother the riot act.

And that's how I escaped to America in 1984 - not through achievement, but through beating my mother at her own game. I had to become her to escape her. Strategic, ruthless, willing to weaponize someone's weakest Mamaent.

The woman who taught me nothing about love taught me everything about survival.

Here's the kicker: In 1984, I was the only Indian citizen from India in my entire Columbia Engineering class - possibly in all of Columbia College too. Nationwide? Maybe a few hundred Indian undergraduates total who weren't children of green card holders. Today? Over 200,000.

The following year, one of my best friends from Modern School arrived at Columbia. The year after that, another. I never asked, but I like to think my escape showed them it was possible. That's how pathways form - one impossible escape proves it's not impossible, then two more follow, then the trickle becomes a flood.

You know why that number exploded to 200,000? Because every Indian parent learned what mine learned the hard way: If you abuse your kids enough about their inadequacy, they develop the survival skills to escape you. And once one escapes - once one proves it's possible - everyone learns the route.

The Indian diaspora isn't built on ambition. It's built on escape velocity. And escape velocity, it turns out, is contagious.

XI. #MeToo India: Same Storm, Different Teacups

Sexual harassment complaints in India are up 500% since 2018. Convictions? Near zero.

The Bollywood #MeToo imploded when accusers got sued for defamation. The startup #MeToo ended when women got blacklisted. The political #MeToo never started.

But here's what changed: The complaints got filed. The data got recorded. The pretense of ignorance died.

My mother's generation dealt with harassment by becoming "one of the boys" or becoming untouchable through sheer competence. Today's generation names names.

Progress? In India, documentation IS progress.

XII. The Startup Sari Revolution

India has 118 unicorns. Female founders lead... wait for it... 15 to 20 of them.

But those 15-20 are interesting: - Falguni Nayar (Nykaa): Built a beauty empire, IPO'd at 58 - Ghazal Alagh (MamaEarth): Turned Mama guilt into a $1.2 billion valuation - Upasana Taku (MobiKwik): Survived co-founder (husband) drama, still standing.

They all have three things in common: 1. Started after marriage/kids (social permission secured) 2. Focus on "feminine" sectors (beauty, babies, payments) 3. Perform traditional femininity while building empires.

The VCs who rejected them? Now scrambling to find "women-led" startups for their diversity metrics.

XIII. The Mother-in-Law Industrial Complex

The greatest barrier to Indian women's progress isn't patriarchy. It's other women.

The mother-in-law who insisted her daughter study engineering won't let her daughter-in-law work. The woman CEO who mentors young women advises them to "adjust" in marriage. The feminist author performs karva chauth (fasting for husband's long life).

My mother did this too. Shattered every ceiling, then insisted we respect the rubble.

Why? Because in India, you don't change the system. You succeed within it, then enforce it on others to validate your own suffering.

XIV. The Joint Family Solution

Here's what Western feminism doesn't understand: The joint family is both prison and liberation.

The mother-in-law who controls your life also watches your kids for free. The sister-in-law who judges your cooking also manages household logistics. The patriarchal structure that oppresses also provides infrastructure.

Indian women's labor force participation is highest in joint families. Why? Free childcare, shared domestic work, multiple incomes for household stability.

My mother had live-in help and her husband's full-throated support. Today's nuclear family woman has neither. Progress?

XV. The Goddess and the Glass Ceiling

India worships goddesses while discriminating against women. This isn't contradiction - it's strategy.

Goddesses are powerful precisely because they're not human. Durga can have eight arms and destroy demons. Human women should have two arms and make dinner.

The same culture that celebrates Lakshmi (wealth) pays women 34% less. That worships Saraswati (knowledge) has 27% female illiteracy. That fears Kali (power) has 14% women in Parliament.

My mother understood this: Be the goddess, never the woman.

XVI. What Would Anjali K Varma Say Today?

If my mother were alive to see India's female unicorn founders, women fighter pilots, and Olympic medalists, she'd light a cigarette (yes, she'd still smoke), adjust her sari, and say:

"You think this is progress? I was doing international arbitration when these girls' mothers were learning to read. We didn't need Instagram to validate our achievements. We didn't create support groups - we created precedents. We didn't lean in - we broke in."

Then she'd pause, take another drag, and add:

"But at least they can cook. I never could. Had to marry your father just to eat properly."

XVII. The Conclusion She Would Have Written

Indian women aren't confused. They're not contradictory. They're playing 17-dimensional chess while you're still learning checkers.

They code-switch between centuries before breakfast. They're traditional enough to get married, modern enough to get funded, Eastern enough for their mothers, Western enough for their managers, Indian enough for society, global enough for success.

They touch feet and break glass ceilings with the same hands. They perform rituals and smash records in the same breath. They fast for their husbands' long lives while building companies that will outlive everyone.

The Indian woman's burden isn't choosing between tradition and modernity. It's performing both simultaneously while making it look effortless.

My mother did it in saris and smoke rings. Today's women do it in LinkedIn posts and lehengas.

The tools changed. The game didn't.

XVIII. The Kicker My Mother Would Have Loved

In 1988-89, when my mother negotiated the Bhopal settlement, court documents listed her as "Mrs. A.K. Verma" - adding the "Mrs." to remind everyone she was someone's wife, not just a lawyer.

In 2016, her obituary read simply "Anjali K Varma, Advocate."

It only took 52 years of practice to be recognized for what she did, not who she married.

But here's the twist the newspapers never caught: The decorated Naval pilot who saved hundreds of lives was in the kitchen making dinner while she was making legal history. The war hero was saving her career by managing everything she couldn't - or wouldn't - do.

They were India's first genuine power couple: She couldn't cook, he was a chef. She broke legal ground, he broke gender norms. She fought in court, he fought anyone who questioned her right to be there. They fought each other in the house but fought the world together outside.

Today's women won't wait 52 years for recognition. They're putting "CEO" before "wife" in their bios. They're adding their salaries to matrimonial ads. They're refusing to choose between the boardroom and the puja room.

But they're still looking for what my mother accidentally found: A partner secure enough to invert every rule.

Progress? In India, refusing to choose IS progress. But finding someone who refuses to make you choose? That's revolution.

My mother chose everything and got all of it because my father chose her success over social approval.

She would have hated admitting she needed him. He would have loved reminding her. They would have fought about it for hours.

Both statements are true. All three statements are true. That's not just Indian. That's love.

The real Indian women's paradox isn't tradition versus modernity. It's that progress still depends on finding men who don't need you to choose.

My parents proved it was possible in 1964. We're still trying to scale their solution in 2025.

That's not progress. That's the problem.

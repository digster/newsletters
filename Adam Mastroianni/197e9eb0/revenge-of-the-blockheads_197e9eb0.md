Tetris was invented in 1985, it came out on the NES in [1989](https://substack.com/redirect/e5eb8c11-9d58-4961-adce-27b0e12d3a89?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o), but the best way to play was only discovered in [2021](https://substack.com/redirect/f26557b1-908b-46fa-906b-323e13b17592?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o). Previously, players would just try to tap the buttons really fast (“hypertapping”), until a 15-year-old named Christopher “CheeZ” Martinez realized that you could actually press the buttons faster if you roll your fingers across the back of the controller (“[rolling](https://substack.com/redirect/2710eda9-33aa-4927-90e7-44dc876a7c3e?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o)”). CheeZ went on to [set world records](https://substack.com/redirect/81512169-d290-4ca2-9eca-dc79dfc564b6?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o) using his [technique](https://substack.com/redirect/078c052a-415a-4f7d-9548-d57403176669?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o), but he wasn’t on top for long. Other players soon perfected their rolls, and CheeZ [lost](https://substack.com/redirect/d54f74a6-6f03-4147-b852-435b7839314e?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o) in a first-round upset at the 2022 Classic Tetris World Championship to another “roller”, a 48th-seed named BirbWizard.
I love this because it shows how low-hanging discoveries can just sit there for decades without anyone seeing them, even when thousands of dollars are on the line. (Seriously—the first place finisher in the 2024 championships won [$10k](https://substack.com/redirect/89949b17-388b-4377-be66-bf166931e93d?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o).) People spent 40 years trying to tap buttons faster without ever realizing they should be tapping the other side of the controller instead.

[Seemay Chou](https://open.substack.com/users/18311418-seemay-chou?utm_source=mentions), the chair of the Navigation Fund, [announces](https://substack.com/redirect/5596bed0-162f-4a0e-92d8-cfef6ff7ed78?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o) that they’ll no longer use their billions of dollars to support publications in traditional scientific journals:
We began this as an [experiment at Arcadia](https://substack.com/redirect/4112afcd-807d-4ab0-bd73-5f7cb0a664b4?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o) a few years ago. At the time, I expected some eventual efficiency gains. What I didn’t expect was how profoundly it would reshape all of our science. Our researchers began designing experiments differently from the start. They became more creative and collaborative. The goal shifted from telling polished stories to uncovering useful truths. All results had value, such as failed attempts, abandoned inquiries, or untested ideas, which we frequently release through Arcadia’s [Icebox](https://substack.com/redirect/f3db81ec-bd0e-4837-a44f-eabcc3494a0e?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o). The bar for utility went up, as proxies like impact factors disappeared.

One of my favorite genres of art is “things that look way more modern than they are”, so I was very excited to run into Giovanni Battista Bracelli’s Oddities of Various Figures (1624):
In 1915, a doctor named Earnest Codman was like “hey guys, shouldn’t we keep track of patient outcomes so we know whether our treatments actually work?” and everyone else was like “no that’s a terrible idea”. So he did what anyone would do: he commissioned an [extremely petty political cartoon](https://substack.com/redirect/215c13a7-4e5f-4b7b-ad4c-b3afcd142521?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o) and debuted it at a meeting of the local medical society. Apparently he didn’t pay that much for the commission, because it looks like it was drawn by a high schooler, not to mention the unhinged captions, the mixed metaphors (the golden goose is...also an ostrich?), and the bug helpfully labeled “humbug”. Anyway, this got him fired from his job at Harvard Medical School.
Codman’s ideas [won](https://substack.com/redirect/c284b5ea-3d0b-4d68-88f6-1da9bf75ba38?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o) in the end, and he was eventually hired back. To answer the Teddy Roosevelt-looking guy in the middle, apparently you can make a living as a clinical professor without humbug!
There’s an anime called The Melancholy of Haruhi Suzumiya that’s about time travel and, appropriately, you can watch the episodes in any order. In 2011, someone posted on 4Chan [asking](https://substack.com/redirect/7f9a36d7-159c-43b2-b2dc-4bdab2c120d4?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o): “If viewers wanted to see the series in every possible order, what is the shortest list of episodes they’d have to watch?” An anonymous commenter replied with a proof demonstrating a lower bound. Mathematicians eventually realized that the proof was a breakthrough in a tricky permutation problem and published a [paper](https://substack.com/redirect/917e49be-a727-435f-8a08-a8a1a329800f?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o) verifying it. The first author of that paper is “Anonymous 4Chan Poster”.

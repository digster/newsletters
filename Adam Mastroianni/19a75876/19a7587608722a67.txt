(1) YOU SAY POTATO, I SAY LEPROSY
As late as 1813, some parts of the European medical establishment believed that potatoes cause leprosy [ https://substack.com/redirect/e44320f4-f5de-4327-ad14-c913e6d21b5a?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ]. (Don’t even get ‘em started on scrofula [ https://substack.com/redirect/07c3cd45-d3c7-442a-9950-2b39de5b19b8?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ]!) Potato historian Salaman Redcliffe suggests that people were skeptical because potatoes look kinda weird, they grow in the ground, and you plant them as tubers rather than seeds, which are all extremely suspicious things for a food to do.
(2) THE MARKET FOR LULULEMONS
You may remember the Spurious Correlations website [ https://substack.com/redirect/183353bb-d521-4767-b8ba-03413371d908?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ], which dredges up random datasets and finds correlations between them—for instance, Lululemon’s stock price and the popularity of the first name Stevie [ https://substack.com/redirect/98dff40b-40e0-4685-b5b0-d800f8d22fa9?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ]. Now thanks to AI, each one of those correlations can be instantly turned into a full academic paper, like: LULU-LEMONADE: A STATISTICAL STUDY OF THE STEVIE-NIZED MARKET [ https://substack.com/redirect/e24e3a2c-14a1-4c78-a98f-b019537947d3?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ]. 
Sadly, this technology makes many academic departments completely redundant.

(6) BREAKIN’ BACON
Before World War I, the US government had basically no cryptographic capacity. So when the war broke out and suddenly they needed people to do code-breaking, where did they turn? To the Riverbank Institute, which had been set up by “Colonel” George Fabyan to decode the most important cipher of all: the one that supposedly proved the works of Shakespeare were written by Francis Bacon [ https://substack.com/redirect/fb2373c1-646c-4176-8ebd-2b6c5a0845c2?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ]. Elizebeth and William Friedman, the couple who broke that cipher while working at Riverside, went on to become the first cryptologists at the precursor to the National Security Agency [ https://substack.com/redirect/12852251-9ed4-4084-8e55-f7b69d9007dc?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ].

(18) DISSONANCE ABOUT DISSONANCE
One of the most famous psychology experiments of all time is Festinger and Carlsmith (1959), the classic demonstration of cognitive dissonance. The psychologist Matti Heino points out [ https://substack.com/redirect/66aae0e8-0c62-4e40-b872-1d609f9d6054?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ], though, that the main results literally don’t add up. In the table below, the circled means are impossible given the reported sample size—there’s no way to get an average of 3.08, for instance, if you have 20 people giving ratings on a 0-10 scale.
I used to be one of those people who was like “well cognitive dissonance has been replicated in hundreds of studies” but it’s not like I ever actually read those studies. It just kinda seems like, c’mon! It’s cognitive dissonance! Everybody knows cognitive dissonance! Anyway, when a bunch of labs tried to replicate another classic demonstration of cognitive dissonance, they found no effect [ https://substack.com/redirect/dce8e44e-7987-4987-96a3-9b2abb7b5680?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ]. And a new paper [ https://substack.com/redirect/43b09dd1-8c52-4648-a7c3-b6020aa416a3?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] claims that When Prophecy Fails, a landmark book that documented the effects of cognitive dissonance in a doomsday cult, may have been greatly embellished or deliberately orchestrated by the authors. None of this means that cognitive dissonance doesn’t or can’t exist, but it does make me feel a whole lot of, uh, dissonance.

This is a hard target to hit: I’ve only ever seen two restaurants that have a 4.9 on Google after receiving thousands of ratings. UPDATES FROM EXPERIMENTAL HISTORY HQ
I got a lot of thoughtful responses to my recent post on the decline of deviance [ https://substack.com/redirect/d6a3a59a-b716-4084-ad54-3ffcf98c88de?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ], including this one [ https://substack.com/redirect/62b0f761-63a4-4a6e-bbb0-3daf3174c6a1?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ] from the classical musician Ema Katrovas . Many people think the internet is to blame, but we forget how recently the internet became the internet. As late as 2007 [ https://substack.com/redirect/fb877818-ad78-49d4-9e55-37e77da34dec?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ], only half of households had broadband at home. Instagram only launched in 2010 [ https://substack.com/redirect/f3b932a0-5f84-4da5-b913-ec64c427d9e6?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ]. The early, slow, text-based internet lived in a room in your house and you had to choose between using it and allowing your grandma to call you; it was nothing like the one that lives in your pocket. So the transition from the 1990s to the 2000s, where we see many forms of deviance declining, is not really the transition from “pre-internet” to “post-internet”. That transition happened somewhere around 2012, when a majority of people got a smartphone [ https://substack.com/redirect/799436a4-c812-4cdf-81e7-9bc8cf51ffcf?j=eyJ1IjoiOG81NG4ifQ.6oeetudtJEh-zO1onUJvtadWEdbodFIw0h4xbugrh1o ]. That’s why I think the internet as we know it today may act as an accelerant, but I don’t think it caused the decline of deviance in the first place.

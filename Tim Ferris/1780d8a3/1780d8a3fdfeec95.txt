ASOMI is an organization of indigenous women healers representing
the five major groups of the ayahuasca culture—Inga, Kamentsá,
Siona, Kofán and Koreguaje—which were all studied by Schultes.
ACT has partnered with these powerful women since 2004, when
their first gathering took place in Mocoa, Putumayo. Their
mission is to revitalize the knowledge and practices of
indigenous communities regarding medicinal plants, artisanship,
women’s self-care, and ecological stewardship of their territory.

This mission is manifest in their chagras, or traditional
gardens, that shelter more than 700 plant species
altogether—medicinal, artisanal, timber, food, you name it. At
these gardens—currently 54 speckled across the region—ASOMI
elders train young apprentices in traditional medicine,
horticulture, culture, and seed conservation. Through these
gardens, the women are not only maintaining their traditional
knowledge across generations but also exchanging over 200 seeds,
recovering at-risk native plants, improving their food security,
and generating sustainable income.

In addition to the work the women carry out in their communities,
ASOMI gathers at their headquarters and meeting house “Chagra de
la Vida,” or Garden of Life—a 128-acre stand of rainforest which
in December officially became a civil society nature reserve for
the protection of sacred plants and traditional knowledge. Here
they exchange experiences, plan and evaluate their organizational
activities in the territories, perform traditional ceremonies,
and exchange seeds and ethnobotanical knowledge. It also serves
as a learning space for the community, offering bird-watching
programs and nature trails that guide learning about the
ecosystem and the traditional cultures represented by ASOMI. A
garden and nursery are also located here, which are shared and
cared for by all of the ASOMI women.

The resources made possible from the matching donation challenge
will go toward the management of ASOMI’s reserve as well as the
ceremonies and activities needed to maintain the vitality and
mission of ASOMI.

Second, the legalization of indigenous lands (i.e., obtaining
land titles) is at the core of the most fundamental right of
indigenous communities. However, it is extremely difficult work
in Colombia, dangerous, and tough to fund. ACT is one of few
organizations spearheading these efforts, and with success. Our
brilliant team in Colombia accomplished what five years ago
seemed like an impossible dream—recovering, connecting, and
protecting the ancestral lands of the Kamentsá and Ingano
communities of the Medio (middle) and Alto (upper) Putumayo where
Schultes did much of his initial work in Amazonia. The lands were
strategically legalized through reserve establishment and land
purchase to connect critical ecosystems and thereby guarantee
unimpeded access to the ancestral territory and sacred plants.

One of the big mistakes of many institutions creating
conservation areas is that once the acres are officially
“protected,” their maintenance and on-the-ground protections are
forgotten, placing the forest at great risk. ACT’s long-term
commitment to the areas where we work strives to create the
conditions for communities to strengthen their traditional forest
practices with new tech capacities in order to effectively manage
and monitor vast territories. With these complementary skills,
our tribal colleagues are equipped to ensure that their land is
not traded or snapped up by land-grabbers or rapacious companies,
all while maintaining a relatively traditional lifestyle of their
own choosing.

The matching donation challenge funds will support the
indigenous-led management and monitoring of these reserves that
shelter critical ecosystem connectivity.

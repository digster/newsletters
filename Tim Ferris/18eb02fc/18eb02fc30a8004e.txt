​Felix culpa (
https://click.fourhourmail.com/d0uv8kxd86s0h4npw9ea9imvxoq44/n2hohvh3evvx87b6/aHR0cHM6Ly9lbi5tLndpa2lwZWRpYS5vcmcvd2lraS9GZWxpeF9jdWxwYSM6fjp0ZXh0PUZlbGl4JTIwY3VscGElMjBpcyUyMGElMjBMYXRpbiwlMjIlMjBvciUyMCUyMmZvcnR1bmF0ZSUyMGZhbGwlMjI=
). “Felix culpa is a Latin phrase that comes from the words
felix, meaning ‘happy,’ ‘lucky,’ or ‘blessed,’ and culpa, meaning
‘fault’ or ‘fall.’ In the Catholic tradition, the phrase is most
often translated ‘happy fault,’ as in the Catholic Exsultet.
Other translations include ‘blessed fall’ or ‘fortunate fall.’”
More broadly and put another way in Oxford Languages: “an
apparent error or disaster with happy consequences.”

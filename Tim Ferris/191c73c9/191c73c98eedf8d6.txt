Book I’m reading
----------------

​The Unbearable Lightness of Being (
https://click.fourhourmail.com/wvumzkq3z7sgh50z06kboinx859xxs8/l2hehmh39owrn9i6/aHR0cHM6Ly93d3cuYW1hem9uLmNvbS9VbmJlYXJhYmxlLUxpZ2h0bmVzcy1CZWluZy1QZXJlbm5pYWwtQ2xhc3NpY3MvZHAvMDA2MTE0ODUyMC8=
) by Milan Kundera (
https://click.fourhourmail.com/wvumzkq3z7sgh50z06kboinx859xxs8/dpheh0h0gqr5olum/aHR0cHM6Ly9lbi53aWtpcGVkaWEub3JnL3dpa2kvTWlsYW5fS3VuZGVyYQ==
). This is a masterpiece. It explores the subtleties, beauty, and
insanity of the human condition through the lens of sex and all
that entails. After all, as attributed to Oscar Wilde,
“Everything in human life is really about sex, except sex. Sex is
about power.”
​​
​I’d certainly heard of this book 100 times, but I’d never read
it. Hearing this, one of my closest friends mailed me a copy as a
gift. His accompanying words were “I’m so envious that you get to
read this book for the first time. I wish I could do it again.”
​
The original manuscript was written in Czech, Kundera’s native
language, but published in French in 1984, as Kundera was living
in exile in France at the time. The renowned translator Michael
Henry Heim translated it into English that same year, when it was
named one of the best books of the year by the New York Times
Book Review and winner of the Los Angeles Times Book Prize for
Fiction. Legendary literary critic Elizabeth Hardwick called it
“a work of the boldest mastery, originality, and richness.”
​
It tells the story of two couples—a young woman in love with a
man torn between his love for her and his womanizing, and one of
the man’s mistresses and her faithful lover—as it explores both
the burden and lightness of eternal recurrence (
https://click.fourhourmail.com/wvumzkq3z7sgh50z06kboinx859xxs8/e0hph7h0gk54lri8/aHR0cHM6Ly9lbi53aWtpcGVkaWEub3JnL3dpa2kvRXRlcm5hbF9yZXR1cm4=
).
​
​The Unbearable Lightness of Being is actually an easy and fast
read, but be forewarned that the beginning is bizarre. That’s
part of the charm.
​
And if you enjoy this book, you may also enjoy Kundera’s The Book
of Laughter and Forgetting (
https://click.fourhourmail.com/wvumzkq3z7sgh50z06kboinx859xxs8/7qh7h8ho703z6xhz/aHR0cHM6Ly93d3cuYW1hem9uLmNvbS9Cb29rLUxhdWdodGVyLUZvcmdldHRpbmctTWlsYW4tS3VuZGVyYS9kcC8wMDYwOTMyMTQ3Lw==
), which was his first book to blow my mind.

Passages I’m pondering
----------------------

Below are just a few of the 100+ highlights I’ve made in The
Unbearable Lightness of Being.
​
​Far from being depressing, I find a lot of Kundera’s insights
incredibly liberating if you look at them closely enough. For
instance, in the case of the second quote, I don’t take it to
mean we’re all doomed to sadness. I take it to mean that the
endless pursuit of happiness is almost doomed to backfire, but
that, per a quote often attributed to Nathaniel Hawthorne,
“Happiness is a butterfly, which when pursued, is always just
beyond your grasp, but which, if you will sit down quietly, may
alight upon you.”
​
​“Anyone whose goal is ‘something higher’ must expect someday to
suffer vertigo. What is vertigo? Fear of falling? No, Vertigo is
something other than fear of falling. It is the voice of the
emptiness below us which tempts and lures us, it is the desire to
fall, against which, terrified, we defend ourselves.”
​― Milan Kundera
​
​“And therein lies the whole of man’s plight. Human time does not
turn in a circle; it runs ahead in a straight line. That is why
man cannot be happy: happiness is the longing for repetition.”
​― Milan Kundera
​
​“The goals we pursue are always veiled. A girl who longs for
marriage longs for something she knows nothing about. The boy who
hankers after fame has no idea what fame is. The thing that gives
our every move its meaning is always totally unknown to us.”
​― Milan Kundera
​
And one just for the spice, since the book is full of it:
​
​“She had an overwhelming desire to tell him, like the most banal
of women, Don’t let me go, hold me tight, make me your plaything,
your slave, be strong! But they were words she could not say.
​
The only thing she said when he released her from his embrace was
‘You don’t know how happy I am to be with you.’ That was the most
her reserved nature allowed her to express.”
​― Milan Kundera

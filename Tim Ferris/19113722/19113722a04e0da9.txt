​Joseph Campbell and the Power of Myth with Bill Moyers (
https://click.fourhourmail.com/5qugk7wxk9b7hv6p72pazi94xo444tn/p8heh9hzolg54vbq/aHR0cHM6Ly93d3cuYW1hem9uLmNvbS9UaGUtSGVyb3MtQWR2ZW50dXJlL2RwL0IwN0JDMlZISEov

Passage I’m pondering
---------------------

The more frequently you look at data, the more noise you are
disproportionally likely to get (rather than the valuable part,
called the signal); hence the higher the noise-to-signal ratio.
​
And there is a confusion which is not psychological at all, but
inherent in the data itself. Say you look at information on a
yearly basis, for stock prices, or the fertilizer sales of your
father-in-law’s factory, or inflation numbers in Vladivostok.
Assume further that for what you are observing, at a yearly
frequency, the ratio of signal to noise is about one to one (half
noise, half signal)—this means that about half the changes are
real improvements or degradations, the other half come from
randomness. This ratio is what you get from yearly observations.
​
But if you look at the very same data on a daily basis, the
composition would change to 95 percent noise, 5 percent signal.
And if you observe data on an hourly basis, as people immersed in
the news and market price variations do, the split becomes 99.5
percent noise to 0.5 percent signal. That is two hundred times
more noise than signal—which is why anyone who listens to news
(except when very, very significant events take place) is one
step below sucker.
​
​— Nassim Nicholas Taleb (
https://click.fourhourmail.com/5qugk7wxk9b7hv6p72pazi94xo444tn/8ghqhohlnxqpqzbk/aHR0cHM6Ly9lbi53aWtpcGVkaWEub3JnL3dpa2kvTmFzc2ltX05pY2hvbGFzX1RhbGVi

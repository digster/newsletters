Niccolò Machiavelli. Today, he is probably best known as a
ruthless power monger, devoid of ethics and compassion. The
phrase most often associated with him, “The ends justify the
means” (which Machiavelli never actually wrote but probably would
have heartily agreed with), has turned him into an apologist for
sociopaths, tyrants, and megalomaniacs the world over.

I think this is a gross misunderstanding of both the man and his
work. The Prince (
https://click.fourhourmail.com/0vum69236ps9h35gpkc0/58hvh7h5m343pqs6/aHR0cHM6Ly93d3cuYW1hem9uLmNvbS9QcmluY2UtTmljY29sJUMzJUIyLU1hY2hpYXZlbGxpL2RwL0IwOFdaSEJQVzYv
) does not condone random cruelty or tyranny or violence. It is a
remarkably sober look at how people take power and how they can
best hold on to it and grow it. Machiavelli was an incredibly
clear-eyed original thinker who might just be history’s first
true champion of real talk. For that reason, there could be no
better guide for the workplace.

In the five hundred years since Machiavelli wrote The Prince (
https://click.fourhourmail.com/0vum69236ps9h35gpkc0/58hvh7h5m343pqs6/aHR0cHM6Ly93d3cuYW1hem9uLmNvbS9QcmluY2UtTmljY29sJUMzJUIyLU1hY2hpYXZlbGxpL2RwL0IwOFdaSEJQVzYv
), a lot of things have changed: We have electricity, the
combustion engine, computers, and antibiotics. We’ve even split
the atom. People, though, haven’t changed one bit. And for that
reason, Machiavelli’s advice about navigating the workplace has
proven to be quite timeless.

Machiavelli’s strategies are powerful tools in the modern

often-marginalized workers. Here are 7 of my favorite
Machiavellian power tips.

Book I’m reading

​Deep Future: Creating Technology That Matters by Pablos Holman. This book really encouraged me to think a lot bigger. I recently described Pablos as “one of the scariest hackers I’ve ever met,” and I’ve enjoyed getting to know him and his work, both permitted and unpermitted. Pablos worked on spaceships at Blue Origin and helped build labs to invent a wide variety of breakthroughs, including a brain surgery tool, a machine to suppress hurricanes, 3D food printers, and a laser that can shoot down mosquitos, part of an impact invention effort to eradicate malaria with Bill Gates. In his spare time, he likes demonstrating how easy it is to steal credit cards, open locks, and engage in other mischief.

 

Podcast I’m enjoying, in spite of myself

​Our Thing with Sammy “The Bull” Gravano (Apple Podcasts, Spotify, YouTube). This was recommended to me by Oz “The Mentalist” Perlman, who’s also kinda scary in his own way. Check out this NFL clip as an example or Joe Rogan calling him a “witch” after a live demo. I was resistant to listening to Our Thing at first, as I don’t like glamorizing criminals, but I decided to give it a shot, since episodes are short and I grew up watching John Gotti in the news. Like a lot of people, I also have a fascination with the mafia from The Godfather, The Sopranos, Goodfellas, and others. From the description: “Salvatore ‘Sammy the Bull’ Gravano is an American gangster who became the underboss of the Gambino crime family [once led by John Gotti]. This is his story.” The insider stories are wild, Sammy is a gifted storyteller, and whether I like it or not, it’s addictive. I’m now on Season 1 episode 9. I suggest starting at the very beginning.

 

Study I’m tracking

"Some people struggle after psychedelics. A new study wants to help." If you have experienced difficulties after taking a psychedelic, you may be eligible to participate in a study sponsored by Emory University. Click here to learn more. Adverse events and challenges are underreported, and it’s incredibly important to study them. They’re not as rare as you might think, and given the power of the tools, we need to find better and better ways to mitigate risk and treat people when they are destabilized. This isn’t for “bad trips,” per se; it’s about the after-effects. As explained on the website:
​
“‘Bad trips’ are important to study, but our focus is more specific. We are interested in people who have had distress or impairment, or have sought help from a family member, friend, or professional, after taking a psychedelic. What we are focused on here is the impact: difficulties in the days, weeks, or months after the psychedelic experience is over.”

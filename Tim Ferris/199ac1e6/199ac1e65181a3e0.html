Podcast episode I’m enjoying

“Sriram Krishnan - The Man Leading America’s AI Race Against China” on The Shawn Ryan Show (Apple Podcasts, Spotify, YouTube).
​
The portion that I want to highlight has nothing to do with AI. It has to do with how Sriram proved himself very early in his career when no one knew him. Click here to be taken to a great story of Sriram moving to Seattle and impressing one of the all-time best coders at Microsoft. What he did mirrors a lot of what I did after college (in totally different domains) following mentors’ advice, and the strategy still works like gangbusters.
​
The clip is only 4–12 minutes long, depending on how far you go, but the gist is this: Niche down until you are THE expert in something very narrow, and you can often do this by combining two things that are rarely combined.

 

Lost blog post that’s related

“Pmarca Guide to Career Planning Part 2: Skills and education.” Long ago, Marc Andreessen, famed technologist and billionaire investor, had a blog with amazing advice. Then he took the whole thing down. The link in this bullet is a pirated copy of one of his essays from 2007, and there is a lot that overlaps with Sriram’s advice. I’ve basically built my career on some version of this, and it can apply to anyone—a new college student, someone mid-career seeking reinvention, and much more:
​
“Seek to be a double/triple/quadruple threat.
​
​Scott Adams—the creator of Dilbert—nails it:
​
​If you want an average successful life, it doesn’t take much planning. Just stay out of trouble, go to school, and apply for jobs you might like. But if you want something extraordinary, you have two paths:

  1. Become the best at one specific thing.
  2. Become very good (top 25%) at two or more things.

The first strategy is difficult to the point of near impossibility. Few people will ever play in the NBA or make a platinum album. I don’t recommend anyone even try.
​
The second strategy is fairly easy. Everyone has at least a few areas in which they could be in the top 25% with some effort. In my case, I can draw better than most people, but I’m hardly an artist. And I’m not any funnier than the average standup comedian who never makes it big, but I’m funnier than most people. The magic is that few people can draw well and write jokes. It’s the combination of the two that makes what I do so rare. And when you add in my business background, suddenly I had a topic that few cartoonists could hope to understand without living it.
​
...Get a degree in business on top of your engineering degree, law degree, medical degree, science degree, or whatever. Suddenly you’re in charge, or maybe you’re starting your own company using your combined knowledge.
​​
…
​
[Back to Marc] "The fact is, this is even the secret formula to becoming a CEO. All successful CEOs are like this. They are almost never the best product visionaries, or the best salespeople, or the best marketing people, or the best finance people, or even the best managers, but they are top 25% in some set of those skills, and then all of a sudden they’re qualified to actually run something important.”

What I’m urging friends in London to see

Lloyd’s story is that of beating unbelievable odds. In the span of one year, Lloyd went from digging septic tanks in Louisiana to becoming the first teenager ever to sell more than a million records. ​Lloyd’s innovations went beyond performing music. He also promoted the legendary “Thrilla in Manila” and “Rumble in the Jungle” with Muhammad Ali. Group for parents I’m creating

Here goes another experiment. This is to gather parents in the USA who have at least one kid between the ages of 8–14. This is to build a free, private community focused on Coyote (the card game), tabletop gaming, and in-person connection. Your input will help me make improvements to Coyote, plot future experiments, and much more. I'm aiming to create a fun group of like-minded and kind people.
​
I’ll mail everyone a free game, we’ll do some private Q&As and other fun stuff, and it should be a recharging positive break from the increasingly negative Internet.
​
If you’re a parent who’s interested, please click here to sign up. It’ll be first come, first served, as I’ll keep the community manageably small, probably 100–200 people.

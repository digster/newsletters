Profile I’m reading

“The Philosopher L. A. Paul Wants Us to Think About Our Selves” by Alice Gregory in The New Yorker. (Alternate link.) Huge thanks to Denise Koller of Harvard Business School for introducing me to L.A. Paul’s work.
​
The subheadline underscores something I’ve been thinking about recently: “To whom should we have allegiance—the version of ourself making choices, or the version of ourself who will be affected by them?”
​
For instance, if certain transformative decisions change how we relate to ourselves and the world around us (having kids, getting a cochlear implant, trying LSD, etc.), should we think about how to make the decision from our current preferences or what we think our future preferences will be?
​
Here are a few excerpts that give you a feel for why L.A. Paul has grabbed my attention:
​
​Paul’s work pushes back against a powerful trend in philosophy, which, as it’s practiced today, can at times look more like science than literature. For the past century, one of the field’s aims has been to eradicate vagueness and the inconsistencies that arise when we speak and write—to make language more closely resemble arithmetic. The approach, taken up in Vienna in the nineteen-twenties and thirties and eventually exported to America, augmented speculative, descriptive, and semireligious inquiries with formulas and sprawling mathematical proofs. This relentless, sometimes neurotic-seeming pursuit of clarity has had the ironic effect of rendering much of contemporary philosophy nearly indecipherable to outsiders.
​
...
​
[Her] book grew out of a working paper, ultimately titled “What You Can’t Expect When You’re Expecting,” that Paul had first presented at a talk two years earlier. In it, she argued that the conventional tools of decision-making do not work when choosing whether to have a child. The “natural approach”—reflecting on what it would be like, appealing to the testimony of other people—was, she argued, insufficient. And no analogous experience (babysitting a niece, say) could ever get you anything but a faulty approximation of the real one. The question of whether to have a child was, for Paul, a sort of riddle that illuminated the limits of rationality. She explored the question through the framework of normative decision theory, whose premise is that we ought to act to maximize expected value, whatever it might be—personal happiness, say, or annual company profits, or a population’s average life expectancy. (The idea’s most elegant encapsulation is Pascal’s wager, which makes the utilitarian case for believing in God: “If you gain, you gain all; if you lose, you lose nothing.”) But such logic, Paul argues, fails in the face of a transformative experience. Choosing to undergo such an experience, on the occasions when choice is even possible, requires us to violate who we take our current self to be. To whom should we have allegiance—the version of ourself making choices, or the version of ourself affected by those choices?
​
​…
​
​When I approached Paul about the possibility of a profile, it was in the spirit of self-help. I was thirty-one and obsessed with whether or not I should have a child. The question felt huge and opaque—like one that neither data nor anecdote could solve. I thought about it all the time, though “thinking” is probably too precise a verb. It was more like a constant buzz, scoring the background of daily life in a tone that registered somewhere between urgency and tedium. The bad parts were easy to picture: less sleep, less time, less money. The awesome parts—expelling a new person out of my own body, say—were, quite literally, inconceivable. The dilemma felt impossible, as if I were attempting to convert dollars into the currency of a country that didn’t yet exist.
